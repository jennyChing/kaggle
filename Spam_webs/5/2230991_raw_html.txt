         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Social Media and Privacy Norms
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="sep">
           Posted on
          </span>
          <a href="http://dmti.in/blog/social-media-and-privacy-norms/" rel="bookmark" title="6:16 am">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-07-01T06:16:30+00:00">
            July 1, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="by-author">
           <span class="sep">
            by
           </span>
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://dmti.in/blog/author/arnab-mitra/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Arnab Mitra">
             Arnab Mitra
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Social Media: One of several offshoots of the internet  has completely changed the way people interact and socialize in cyber space. With a large number of people spending a good part of their time on various social media websites each day; these multi-million dollar corporations have developed huge databases containing personal bio of millions of users. This essentially means that these particular websites, by means of their terms of service, have the liberty to modify, ban or completely remove a users content as they see fit
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          The tech blogs recently have been flooded with stories about social media websites doing exactly that. Their proprietary terms and conditions written in fine print matter less to a user who just wants to share pictures or videos of a vacation he took with his friends and family. A closer look at the rules and regulations defined by social media giants like
          <b>
           Facebook, YouTube, and Flickr will reveal that these rules completely ignore the US law
          </b>
          , a fact that is surprising since all three companies are headquartered in the US, and in some cases go beyond the law mentioned in Article 19 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Even though these behemoths often face litigation from their foreign clientele or competitors, it does little to affect their stance on the matter. Instead of the law by which they are bound, it is these companies who have the final say in the end about online privacy and speech.Even though the movement against privacy issues and speech has gained momentum in the past few months, the supporters of zero anonymity are in no small numbers.The matter to control online privacy and speech is a real one and as of today continues to grow with little being done to fully address and resolve the issue in a meaningful way. As of today we are bound to ask the question: Will control of online speech and privacy lead to a change in traditional practices?In her book titled Consent of the Networked prominent writer and media activist Rebecca Mackinnon closely examines the issues related to freedom of expression, privacy and their defiance of the countrys(USA) law.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Where are we on the same? Internet Censorship in India still remains to be a grey area. Blasphemy? eh? If theres a segment that indicates how poorly thought out Indias finalized Internet control rules are, it is sub-rule 2 and 4 of the segment pertaining to Intermediaries in the countrys finalized Information Technology rules: the first of which defines what actions by a user can result in the removal of content (via intermediaries like ISPs, hosting service providers and social networks), and the latter, which defines who can get the content removed, and how. You can find a copy of the final rules
          <a href="http://www.mit.gov.in/sites/upload_files/dit/files/RNUS_CyberLaw_15411.pdf">
           <b>
            here
           </b>
          </a>
          (pdf, specifically between page 11 and 14), and an annotated version of the Intermediary guidelines
          <a href="http://www.scribd.com/doc/53233863/DVComparison-Information-Technology-Due-diligence-observed-by-intermediaries-guidelines-Rules-2011-rules-79">
           <b>
            here
           </b>
          </a>
          , and there are significant changes. (as reports
          <a href="http://www.medianama.com/2011/04/223-indias-internet-control-rules-finalized-blasphemy/">
           Medianama
          </a>
          )
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Less hope as I can think of it. While our so called political leaders and career gurus continue to reap advantages though a revere towards their horrendous movements using this confusing times. Opinionated Indian consumers lose voice at a cost of helplessness. Sad but thats the truth.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          I am stating facts here. Feel free to join/share your opinion about the same.
         </p>
        </div>
