          <p class="heading">
           By
           <a data-component="byLine" data-ordinal="1" data-source="Bio" data-type="authorName" href="http://atheism.about.com/bio/Austin-Cline-5577.htm" rel="author">
            Austin Cline
           </a>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <b>
           Fallacy Name
          </b>
          :
          <br/>
          Illicit Observation
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Alternative Names
          </b>
          :
          <br/>
          None
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Category
          </b>
          :
          <br/>
          <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/library/FAQs/skepticism/blfaq_fall_ambiguity.htm">
           Fallacy of Ambiguity
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h3>
          Explanation of the Fallacy of Illicit Observation
         </h3>
         <p>
          Illicit Observation is a special type of the
          <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/logicalfallacies/a/Fallacy-Equivocation-Equivocation.htm">
           Fallacy of Equivocation
          </a>
          . An Illicit Observation fallacy occurs when someone uses two terms in an argument as if they were negations or opposites of each other when they really aren't.
         </p>
         <p>
          Usually this
          <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/logicalfallacies/a/overview.htm">
           fallacy
          </a>
          stems from not knowing or understanding the difference between
          <i>
           contradictory
          </i>
          terms and
          <i>
           contrary
          </i>
          terms.
         </p>
         <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-inf col col-4" id="adsense1">
         </div>
         <div class="radlinks ads-inf col col-4" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">
         </div>
         <p class="cb-split">
          Contradictory terms are negations of each other: one or the other must apply, but they can't both apply at the same time. Two terms are contraries if they can't both apply at the same time, but it's not required that either apply.
         </p>
         <p>
          An example of two
          <b>
           contradictory
          </b>
          terms are "alive" and "lifeless." Everything is alive or lifeless; nothing is both alive and lifeless and nothing is neither.
         </p>
         <p>
          An example of two
          <b>
           contrary
          </b>
          terms are "alive" and "dead." Nothing can be both alive and dead, but some things are neither.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <h3>
          Examples and Discussion of the Fallacy of Illicit Observation
         </h3>
         <p>
          Here are some ways in which the fallacy of Illicit Observation can be used in
          <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="4" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/logicalarguments/a/argument.htm">
           arguments
          </a>
          :
         </p>
         <blockquote class="yes">
          1. Child molesters are inhuman, therefore no one who molests children is a human.
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          One would think that this is supposed to be an observation, but it fails because the predicate (inhuman) has not actually been negated. The term
          <i>
           human
          </i>
          is not the negation (or contradictory) of the term inhuman. A correct formulation would be:
         </p>
         <blockquote class="yes">
          2. Child molesters are inhuman, therefore no one who molests children is noninhuman.
         </blockquote>
         <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="2" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-inf col col-4" id="adsense2">
         </div>
         <p>
          This is because the term
          <i>
           human
          </i>
          is being used in more the one way. As a part of inhuman, it has to do with being moral or kind because inhuman means cruel. But when it appears alone, it simply refers to a member of the species homo sapiens.
         </p>
         <p>
          A similar error can be seen in this statement:
         </p>
         <blockquote class="yes">
          3. No rocks are alive, therefore all rocks are dead.
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          As explained above, the terms alive and dead are
          <b>
           contraries
          </b>
          , not
          <b>
           contradictories
          </b>
          . A failure to distinguish between the two can lead to any number of logical errors in ideas or beliefs with the Illicit Observation fallacy being the easiest to recognize.
         </p>
         <p>
          If two statements are contraries, then it is impossible for both of them to be true, but it is possible for both to be false. However, if two statements are contradictories, it is impossible for them to both be true or for them to both be false.
         </p>
         <p>
          The terms alive and lifeless are contradictories because everything either has life (is alive) or lacks life (is lifeless), but nothing is both alive and lifeless. The terms alive and dead are contraries because nothing can be both alive and dead, but some things might be neither (dead implies that it was once alive, but now is not).
         </p>
         <p>
          Thus, the correct formulation of #3 would be:
         </p>
         <blockquote class="yes">
          4. No rocks are alive, therefore all rocks are lifeless.
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Because of the technical and specialized nature of the fallacy, it is unlikely that you will encounter it too often... if at all, actually. Confusion over the difference between contradictories and contraries is easy to find, but arguments the derive from this confusion are another matter entirely.
         </p>
         <p>
          The closest would probably be something like example #2 above, as part of an attempt to deny that some sort of crime places a person outside the sphere of protection of human rights. There might be some rhetorical force behind such a statement, but no logical validity whatsoever.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you do find this fallacy being used in a real-world argument, please send it in to this site as an example.
         </p>
