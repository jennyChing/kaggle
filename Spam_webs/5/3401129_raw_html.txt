   <h1>
    Unique Book Goes On Display
   </h1>
   <h3>
    <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/europe/2939362.stm" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
     <strong>
      BBC News
     </strong>
    </a>
    <br/>
    Monday, 26 May, 2003
    <br/>
    <br/>
    The book dates back to 600BC.
   </h3>
   <p>
   </p>
   <p class="subhead">
    The world's oldest multiple-page book - in the lost Etruscan language - has
    <br/>
    gone on display in Bulgaria's National History Museum in Sofia.
   </p>
   <p class="body_text">
    It contains six bound sheets of 24 carat gold, with illustrations of a 
  horse-rider, a mermaid, a harp and soldiers.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    The small manuscript, which is more than two-and-a-half millennia old, was discovered 60 years 
  ago in a tomb uncovered during digging for a canal along the Strouma river 
  in south-western Bulgaria.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    It has now been donated to the museum by its finder, on condition of anonymity.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    Reports say the unidentified donor is now 87 years old and lives in Macedonia.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    The authenticity of the book has been confirmed by two experts in Sofia and London, museum 
  director Bojidar Dimitrov said quoted by AFP.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    The six sheets are believed to be the oldest comprehensive work involving multiple pages, 
  said Elka Penkova, who heads the museum's archaeological department.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    There are around 30 similar pages known in the world, Ms Penkova said, 
  "but they are not linked together in a book".
    <br/>
    <br/>
    The Etruscans - one of Europe's most mysterious ancient peoples - are believed to have migrated 
  from Lydia, in modern western Turkey, settling in northern and central 
  Italy nearly 3,000 years ago.
    <br/>
    <br/>
    They were wiped out by the conquering Romans in the fourth century BC, leaving few written records.
   </p>
