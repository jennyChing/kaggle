         <p>
          <strong>
           Ive been living in the USA for a little over a year now
          </strong>
          , so its a good time to reflect on being a foreigner in this great land. Im a true blue Aussie, so please bear that in mind while reading through my observations.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span class="pibfi_pinterest ">
           <img alt="usa flags" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9399" height="498" src="http://www.inspiringtravellers.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/american_flags.jpg?2bb97f" width="750"/>
           <span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://inspiringtravellers.com/living-working-usa-outsiders-view/&amp;media=http://www.inspiringtravellers.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/american_flags.jpg&amp;description=InspiringTravellers.com | Living And Working In The United States: An Outsiders View | http://inspiringtravellers.com')">
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          The first thing I noticed was the
          <a href="http://inspiringtravellers.com/2013/08/02/big-texas-monster-trucks/" target="_blank" title="Big In Texas: Little Monster Trucks">
           trucks
          </a>
          that barely fit in car lanes.
          <strong>
           The highways are chaotic
          </strong>
          too and although I got my drivers license early on, Ive only recently been willing to take them on.
         </p>
         <p>
          Being Australian,
          <strong>
           you have to get used to people assuming that youre from England
          </strong>
          , which isnt particularly new as it often happens when Im in Europe. Its always fun to figure out the differences in the language too. Im looking for a carton of beer? you might ask at the
          <a href="http://inspiringtravellers.com/2014/02/19/texas-bold-beer/" target="_blank" title="Big In Texas: Bold Beer">
           beer
          </a>
          store. When met with a blank stare, you adjust your question to Pardon me, case of beer.
         </p>
         <p>
          I knew that the US is a
          <a href="http://inspiringtravellers.com/2013/07/25/big-texas-megachurches/" target="_blank" title="Big In Texas: Megachurches">
           religious place
          </a>
          , but
          <strong>
           I didnt realize how in your face religion would be
          </strong>
          . Im not going to go into details, because Im not political at all and stay away from the news channels.
         </p>
         <p>
          As for friendliness,
          <strong>
           people in the US are usually willing to have a chat and help you out
          </strong>
          . However,
          <a href="http://inspiringtravellers.com/2013/06/20/sir-south/" target="_blank" title="Yes, Sir In the South">
           Ill never get used to being called Sir
          </a>
          or calling others Sir or Maam. Its just too weird. When we bought our car, the salesman called me that at the end of every sentence. Heres an example of the cultural differences:
         </p>
         <p>
          Aussie phrase: Hey mate, theres a bloke over there who needs something.
         </p>
         <p>
          US translation: Excuse me Maam, that gentleman over there is asking for help.
         </p>
         <p>
          Gentleman, really? I used it once and felt dirty straightaway.
          <strong>
           These formalities are just a form of politeness and its actually kind of nice.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           In some countries, the customer isnt always right and is treated with disdain.
          </strong>
          At a gift shop once in
          <a href="http://inspiringtravellers.com/2011/10/15/5-reasons-love-switzerland-damn-expensive/" target="_blank" title="5 Reasons to Love Switzerland Even Though its so Damn Expensive!">
           Switzerland
          </a>
          , we bought a fragile souvenir and asked the cashier if they had any wrapping paper. He looked at us with such utter contempt and bewilderment, before casually reaching under the counter for said wrapping paper. Thanks for the effort, buddy!
         </p>
         <p>
          Anyway,
          <strong>
           in the US the customer IS ALWAYS right
          </strong>
          . Broadly speaking, if you buy something and want to return it, no questions asked. If something you bought a year ago is faulty, you can still return it and get a replacement. Now thats service indeed!
         </p>
         <p>
          Real cable television. For most of my life, I never had any such thing in Australia. Cable (or satellite) TV and fast internet in the US leaves Australias pale imitation in the dirt.
          <strong>
           And my beloved sportsoh so many sports.
          </strong>
          It sure helps for conversation around the office and football season is crazy. Not just the NFL, but college football too. Why doesnt the NFL schedule Friday night or Saturday games, I hear you ask. Well Friday night is reserved for high school football and you guessed it, Saturday is college day. In fact, every other car you see driving around will have their college emblem on the back windscreen or bumper. Around our neighborhood, college flags with appear on front lawns during game time. Speaking of flags, when some public holidays roll around, US flags also appear in front of houses and commercial areas.
         </p>
         <p>
          All in all,
          <strong>
           the USA is not that different to other places Ive lived. Its just a lot bigger and more diverse
          </strong>
          . My work life here is pretty good. Compared to
          <a href="http://inspiringtravellers.com/2013/03/14/moving-to-norway-know-30-things/" target="_blank" title="30 Things You Should Know Before Moving To Norway">
           Norway
          </a>
          , people work longer hours and are definitely more helpful. And its always fun being the token Australian in the office. You may hear others as you walk the aisles and hallways, but dare not speak because there can be only one!
         </p>
