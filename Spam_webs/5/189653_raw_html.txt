        <h2 class="date-header">
         Friday, December 13, 2013
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-stories_about_me entry-author-liz entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b6fc69e2019b02c5257b970b">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           Asimbonanga
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             So I heard this,
            </p>
            <p>
             <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="253" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/MHHjP7XrBq0" width="450">
             </iframe>
            </p>
            <p>
             And I was moved; it was hair-raisingly beautiful, and the people of many races standing side-by-side in peace and also in sorrow....
            </p>
            <p>
             It turns out it was arranged by Woolworths, South Africa (which has no real connection between the US Woolworths chain). As a tribute to Nelson Mandela, the performance above by the
             <a href="http://www.sowetogospelchoir.com/">
              Soweto Gospel Choir
             </a>
             was sponsored by Woolworths SA.
            </p>
            <p>
             I believe the lyrics sung are:
             <br/>
             Asimbonanga [we have not seen him]
             <br/>
             Asimbonang uMandela thina [we have not seen Mandela]
             <br/>
             Laphekhona [in the place where he is]
             <br/>
             Laphehleli khona [in the place where he is kept]
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Asimbonanga
             <br/>
             Asimbonang umfowethu thina [we have not seen our brother]
             <br/>
             Laphekhona [in the place where he is]
             <br/>
             Laphwafela khona [in the place where he died]
             <br/>
             Sithi: Hey, wena [We say: hey, you]
             <br/>
             Hey, wena nawe [Hey, you and you]
             <br/>
             Siyofika nini la siyakhona [when will we arrive at our destination]
            </p>
            <p>
             Then I got curious about this song. It turns out it was written by
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnny_Clegg">
              Johnny Clegg
             </a>
             , a UK-born South African of European ancestry, while Mandela was still in prison (1962-1990); the English lyrics include mention of
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Biko">
              Steve Biko
             </a>
             (1946-1977),
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victoria_Mxenge">
              Victoria Mxenge
             </a>
             (1942-1985), and
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neil_Aggett">
              Neil Aggett
             </a>
             (1953-1983).
            </p>
            <p>
             In 1999, at a concert in France, Johnny Clegg and his band sang Asimbonanga as a tribute to Nelson Mandela, who then appeared on stage. Mandela said,
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             It is music and dancing that makes me at peace with the world.
             <br/>
             And at peace with myself.
             <br/>
             But I do not see much movement up there [in the audience], you know?
             <br/>
             I would like us to join, let us repeat it, [hands microphone to Johnny Clegg]
             <br/>
             [Singing resumes, with Madiba dancing, with joy]
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             [and the clip ends, with Clegg and Madiba walking off stage, holding hands]
            </p>
            <p>
             <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/BGS7SpI7obY" width="420">
             </iframe>
            </p>
