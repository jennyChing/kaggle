           <h1 itemprop="headline">
            Measuring SEO: Beyond the Keyword
           </h1>
          </div>
         </header>
         <div class="postMeta columns small-3">
          <img alt="Adair Cameron" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/adair-cameron-lrg.jpg"/>
          <div class="postInformation">
           <p class="author">
            <span itemprop="author">
             <em>
              by
             </em>
             Adair Cameron
            </span>
           </p>
           <div class="post-info">
            <p class="time-stamp" itemprop="datePublished">
             21 Jul 2014
            </p>
            <nav class="tag-categories">

             </h6>

         <div class="narrowColumn columns small-9">
          <div itemprop="description">
           <p>
            <strong>
             For years Search Engine Optimisation (SEO) was measured through rankings. You would identify the highest volume keywords and focus in on them like a sniper scoping out their target. But in an industry that is unrecognisable to the day I first started in SEO over seven years ago, why do we still obsess with rankings?
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
             Google have long confirmed to make hundreds of changes to their algorithm each year, some of which are more public than others. The last couple of years alone have seen the industry turned on its head, not only in terms of how we do SEO but also how its measured.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            The announcement back in October 2011 by Google to
            <a href="http://analytics.blogspot.co.uk/2011/10/making-search-more-secure-accessing.html">
             make search more secure
            </a>
            and therefore hide keyword data behind the label of (not provided) within Google Analytics was certainly not welcomed in the industry.
           </p>
           <p>
            The days of measuring SEO at such a granular level were limited. As each month past wed lose a few more percent of keyword data until  almost two years after the first announcement  Google started to roll out encrypted search to all users.
           </p>
           <p>
            Statistics from June 2014 of our top-five highest volume clients show 90.8% of all Google organic searches traced as (not provided).
           </p>
           <p>
            With all the changes in the industry its more important than ever to understand how to best measure SEO in line with your companys overall objectives.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Stop thinking rankings. Start thinking visibility
           </h2>
           <p>
            First things first  stop focusing on head keyword rankings  a good SEO campaign will help improve such rankings but you cannot ignore the wider spectrum of search.
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            Since 2003 Google has answered 450 billion new unique queries, searches we have never seen before  Google.com
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            Google handles billions of searches per day of which
            <strong>
             16% of keywords are completely new and never been searched for
            </strong>
            ! Attempting to put things into perspective can be difficult, but as a general rule we say that 30% of searches are classed as head terms (or in other words, high volume keywords) meanwhile the remaining 70% are long-tail which means keywords made up of more than two words.
           </p>
           <hr/>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="The Search Demand Curve" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/searchdemandcurve.gif" style="width: 550px; height: 477px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            Following the (not provided) era, some have adopted landing page reports as a way of measuring success. But theres yet another method that helps you gain an indicative perspective of how your website performs at a granular level.
           </p>
           <p>
            Google Webmaster Tools search query report enables access to keyword-level data. It may be restricted to a 90-day history and have a two-to-three day lag on the data showing but it certainly comes in handy when calculating your websites visibility.
           </p>
           <p>
            Downloading the CSV and importing into Excel we are able to  with a few handy formulas  start to segment the data to produce a Pivot Table that can be manipulated to tell a story and help towards measuring the success of an SEO campaign.
           </p>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="SEO Visibility Table" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/seovisibilitytable.JPG" style="width: 508px; height: 247px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            The table above has allowed us to segment by brand / non-brand as well as particular products. Rather than look at the single head keyword we can see in fact our client actually ranks for thousands of keywords.
           </p>
           <p>
            Such a report allows us to calculate visibility which in fact is just one of a number of KPIs we look at within our Campaign Measurement Model.
           </p>
           <p>
            So lets not stop at visibility
           </p>
           <h2>
            Designing a Measurement Model to Drive Success
           </h2>
           <p>
            Before we can begin to design a Measurement Model for a campaign weve got to understand the relationship between the areas of marketing involved e.g. SEO, Social, Paid, the KPIs, Goals and Business Objectives.
           </p>
           <p>
            Heres a breakdown of the Measurement Model with questions to thatll help fill in your model.
           </p>
           <h4>
            1. Define your Objectives
           </h4>
           <p>
            Ask yourself what is the primary purpose of your website? Why do you need this campaign?
           </p>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="Measurement Model Table" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/measurementmodeltable.png" style="width: 550px; height: 264px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <h4>
            2. Define your Goals for each Objective
           </h4>
           <p>
            How are you going to achieve these objectives? What strategies are in place to accomplish your objectives?
           </p>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="Measurement Model Table 2" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/measurementmodeltable2.png" style="width: 550px; height: 264px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <h4>
            3. Define your KPIs for each Goal
           </h4>
           <p>
            How do you plan to measure your goals? What makes each strategy a success?
           </p>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="Measurement Model Table 3" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/measurementmodeltable3.png" style="width: 550px; height: 264px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <h4>
            4. Define how youre going to benchmark each KPI
           </h4>
           <p>
            How do you define what is a successful goal? What are you going to benchmark it against? These could also be used as your targets.
           </p>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="Measurement Model Table 4" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/measurementmodeltable4.png" style="width: 550px; height: 264px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <h4>
            5. Define your segments for each KPI
           </h4>
           <p>
            What contributing data do you need to capture to measure a KPI? For example, Social visits can be segmented by Twitter, Facebook, LinkedIn and Google+
           </p>
           <div align="center">
            <p>
             <img alt="Measurement Model Table 5" src="https://v3.myevolve.co.uk/_includes/uploads/VennDigitalV3/measurementmodeltable5.png" style="width: 550px; height: 264px;"/>
            </p>
           </div>
           <h2>
            Conclusions
           </h2>
           <p>
            Designing a Measurement Model bespoke to your campaigns will not only help you better understand performance but also helps define relationships as youll often find cross-over from different parts of Marketing.
           </p>
           <p>
            This allows us to make better decisions as to what is required to deliver a successful campaign.
           </p>
           <p>
            It also allows us to move on from measuring rankings. Whats most important at the end of the day? A single ranking keyword or an increase in revenue?
           </p>
          </div>
