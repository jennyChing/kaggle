      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-30904 comment-1 clearfix" data-nid="30904" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="image-article-inline-half">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="Chua Chin Hon, Straits Times, Singapore" height="153" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half-caption/public/field_blog_entry_images/30904-77662.jpg?itok=YBl_h95L" title="Chua Chin Hon, Straits Times, Singapore" width="230"/>
              </div>
              <div class="caption">
               (Chua Chin Hon/Straits Times)
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            The
            <em>
             Associated Press
            </em>
            is reporting a breaking story about the use of electric shock
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/therapy" title="Psychology Today looks at therapy">
             therapy
            </a>
            on 3,000 Chinese youths. The article appears below and, for obvious reasons, it is lighting up the webno irony intended among web users, I'm sure.
           </p>
           <p>
            I wrote about overuse of the Internet
            <a href="http://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/side-effects/200903/should-overuse-the-internet-become-mental-disorder" target="_blank">
             in an earlier post
            </a>
            , voicing dissent with the American
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/psychiatry" title="Psychology Today looks at Psychiatric">
             Psychiatric
            </a>
            Association's
            <a href="http://ajp.psychiatryonline.org/cgi/content/full/165/3/306" target="_blank">
             apparent interest
            </a>
            in representing the behavior as a mental illness. The piece drew on an
            <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/11/18/technology/18rehab.html?scp=1&amp;sq=Martin%20Fackler%20Internet%20Addiction&amp;st=cse" target="_blank">
             excellent article
            </a>
            in the
            <em>
             New York Times
            </em>
            , which focused on treatment centers in South Korea that address this phenomenon without
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/psychopharmacology" title="Psychology Today looks at medication">
             medication
            </a>
            or, let it be said, electric shock treatment. Certainly, South Korea's approach does seem better-informed, not to say more ... humane.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.christopherlane.org" target="_blank">
             www.christopherlane.org
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5g0fTUUMSN98L3XxqH5bwAytMyx6gD99E2UUG3" target="_blank">
             By HENRY SANDERSON - 9 hours ago
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            BEIJING (AP) - China's
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/health" title="Psychology Today looks at Health">
             Health
            </a>
            Ministry has ordered a hospital to stop using electric shock therapy to cure youths of Internet
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/addiction" title="Psychology Today looks at addiction">
             addiction
            </a>
            , saying there was no scientific evidence it worked.
           </p>
           <p>
            Linyi Mental Health Hospital in eastern Shandong province used the treatment as part of a four-month program that has so far treated nearly 3,000 youths, the China Youth Daily newspaper has reported, citing the psychiatrist who runs it, Yang Yongxin.
           </p>
           <p>
            The ministry said in a statement posted on its Web site late Monday there is no domestic or international clinical evidence that electric shock therapy helps cure Internet addiction. Electric shock therapy is most often used to treat severe
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/depression/depression-and-society" title="Psychology Today looks at depression">
             depression
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            Chinese psychologists say symptoms of Internet addiction include being online more than six hours a day - playing games and looking at
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/pornography" title="Psychology Today looks at pornography">
             pornography
            </a>
            rather than working or studying - and getting angry when unable to get online.
           </p>
           <p>
            The hospital could not be reached for comment Tuesday, but spokesman Yang Shuyun told the Beijing News newspaper had stopped administering the shock therapy after seeing the ministry's comments.
           </p>
           <p>
            Shuyun said it was only part of the overall program to treat patients, which also included medicine and psychological counseling. Patients are charged 5,500 yuan ($805) a month.
           </p>
           <p>
            Computer and Internet use has risen dramatically as China's economy has boomed in recent years, and according to an estimate by China's National People's Congress about 10 percent of the country's under-18 users are addicted to the Internet, although it is not recognized as a clinical condition.
           </p>
           <p>
            Copyright  2009 The Associated Press. All rights reserved.
           </p>
           <p>
            The London
            <em>
             Guardian
            </em>
            describes the ordeal of the treatment
            <a href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/world/2009/jul/14/china-internet-electric-shock-treatment" target="_blank">
             here
            </a>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
