    </i>

     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Theotokos
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// secondary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'th' in 'thigh'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">
           i
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
           t
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'o' in 'body'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
       
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">
       transliterated (Greek)
      </a>
      <i>
       Theotkos
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">
       translation (Syriac-Aramaic):
      </a>
      <i>
       <span dir="rtl" style="font-family:'Serto Urhoy'; font-size:125%;">
         
       </span>
       
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">
       transliterated (Syriac):
      </a>
      <i>
       Yoldath Alloho
      </i>
      ) is the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Titles_of_Mary" title="Titles of Mary">
       title
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mary,_the_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, the mother of Jesus">
       Mary, the mother of Jesus
      </a>
      used especially in the
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">
       Eastern Orthodox
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox" title="Oriental Orthodox">
       Oriental Orthodox
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Church" title="Eastern Catholic Church">
       Eastern Catholic Churches
      </a>
      . Its literal English translations include "God-bearer", "Birth-Giver of God" and "the one who gives birth to God." Less literal translations include "Mother of God" (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
       Latin
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
       <i>
        Mater Dei
       </i>
      </span>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      The ancient use of this term is emphasised in Churches of the Syriac Tradition, which have been using this title in their ancient liturgies for centuries: the
      <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_Addai_and_Mari" title="Liturgy of Addai and Mari">
       Anaphora of Mari and Addai (3rd Century)
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODCC_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ODCC-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_St_James" title="Liturgy of St James">
       Liturgy of St James the Just (60 AD)
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Ephesus" title="First Council of Ephesus">
       Council of Ephesus
      </a>
      decreed in 431 that Mary is
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      because her son Jesus is both God and man: one Divine Person with two natures (Divine and human) intimately,
      <a href="/wiki/Hypostatic_union" title="Hypostatic union">
       hypostatically united
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ephesus_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ephesus-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccel.org_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ccel.org-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" title="Roman Catholics">
       Roman Catholics
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicans" title="Anglicans">
       Anglicans
      </a>
      use the title "Mother of God" more often than "
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      ".
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutherans" title="Lutherans">
       Lutherans
      </a>
      also venerated and honored Mary as "Mother of God" as expressed by its founder,
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">
       Martin Luther
      </a>
      ;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the title was officially confessed in their
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_of_Concord" title="Formula of Concord">
       Formula of Concord
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      accepted by
      <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_World_Federation" title="Lutheran World Federation">
       Lutheran World Federation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">
       Etymology and usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theotokos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kazan_moscow.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="371" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Kazan_moscow.jpg/180px-Kazan_moscow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Kazan_moscow.jpg/270px-Kazan_moscow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Kazan_moscow.jpg/360px-Kazan_moscow.jpg 2x" width="180"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kazan_moscow.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Kazan" title="Our Lady of Kazan">
          Theotokos of Kazan
         </a>
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Theotokos is a compound of two Greek words, 
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
        God
       </a>
      </i>
      and 
      <i>
       parturition, childbirth
      </i>
      . Literally, this translates as
      <i>
       God-bearer
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       the one who gives birth to God
      </i>
      ; historian
      <a href="/wiki/Jaroslav_Pelikan" title="Jaroslav Pelikan">
       Jaroslav Pelikan
      </a>
      translated it more precisely as "the one who gives birth to the one who is God".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelikan_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pelikan-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, since many
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English-speaking
      </a>
      Orthodox find this literal translation awkward, in
      <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">
       liturgical
      </a>
      use,
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      is often left untranslated, or paraphrased as
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      . The latter title is the literal translation of a distinct title in Greek,    (translit.
      <i>
       Mtr tou Theou
      </i>
      ).
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      also accurately translates the Greek words  (translit.
      <i>
       Theomtor
      </i>
      ; also spelled , translit.
      <i>
       Theomtr
      </i>
      ) and  (translit.
      <i>
       Mtrotheos
      </i>
      ), which are found in patristic and liturgical texts, e.g.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       ... [80]   
       <b>
        
       </b>
        ... [109]   
       <b>
        
       </b>
        ...
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The English term
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      is mostly used as an imprecise translation of
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      , but with a note that Mary did not create the divine person of Jesus, who existed with the Father from all eternity,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      she is not the source of her Sons divinity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The other principal use of
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      has been as the precise and literal translation of  , a Greek term which has an established usage of its own in traditional Orthodox and Catholic theological writing, hymnography, and iconography. In an abbreviated form,  , it often is found on Eastern
      <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">
       icons
      </a>
      (see illustration above), where it is used to identify Mary.
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the Orthodox and Catholic tradition,
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      has not been understood, nor been intended to be understood, as referring to Mary as Mother of God
      <i>
       from eternity
      </i>
       that is, as Mother of God the Father  but only with reference to the birth of
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      , that is, the
      <a href="/wiki/Incarnation" title="Incarnation">
       Incarnation
      </a>
      . This limitation in the meaning of
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      must be understood by the person employing the term. To make it explicit, it is sometimes translated
      <i>
       Mother of God Incarnate
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, those reading or hearing the English phrase
      <i>
       Mother of God
      </i>
      as a translation of a Greek text cannot  unless they know the Greek text in question, or obtain additional information  know whether the phrase is a literal translation of  , or an imprecise rendering of , or one its Latin equivalents or equivalents in other languages. On the other hand,
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      and its precise translations explicitly relate Mary's motherhood to Jesus' birth in time and exclude any reference to Mary as Mother of God
      <i>
       from eternity
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      Theotokos specifically excludes the understanding of Mary as Mother of God in the eternal sense. Christians believe that God is the cause of all, with neither origin nor source, and is therefore without a mother or father, or any relation except for what is
      <a href="/wiki/Homoousian" title="Homoousian">
       homoousian
      </a>
      to Him: only the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(philosophy)" title="Hypostasis (philosophy)">
       persons
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" title="Holy Trinity">
       Holy Trinity
      </a>
      . He is
      <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">
       ontologically
      </a>
      separate from all other beings, as Creator to creation. This stands in contrast to classical
      <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">
       Greco-Roman religion
      </a>
      in particular, where a number of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goddesses" title="Goddesses">
       goddesses
      </a>
      appear as the physical mothers of other divinities which were considered gods in their own right (cf.
      <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">
       polytheism
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      On the other hand, most Christians believe
      <a href="/wiki/God_the_Son" title="God the Son">
       God the Son
      </a>
      is begotten of
      <a href="/wiki/God_the_Father" title="God the Father">
       God the Father
      </a>
      "from all
      <a href="/wiki/Eternity" title="Eternity">
       eternity
      </a>
      " (see
      <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">
       Trinity
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">
       Nicene Creed
      </a>
      ), but is born "in time" of Mary.
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      thus refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/Incarnation_(Christianity)" title="Incarnation (Christianity)">
       Incarnation
      </a>
      , when the Second Person of the Holy Trinity took on human nature in addition to his pre-existing divine nature, this being made possible through the cooperation of Mary.
     </p>
     <p>
      Though most Christians understand Jesus Christ as both fully God and fully human, only Orthodox Christians (in the East), and Roman Catholics, Anglicans, Lutherans, and schismatic Old Catholics (in the West), call Mary
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Ephesus" title="First Council of Ephesus">
       Council of Ephesus
      </a>
      decreed, in opposition to those who denied Mary the title
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      ("the one who gives birth to God") but called her
      <i>
       Christotokos
      </i>
      ("the one who gives birth to Christ"), that Mary
      <i>
       is
      </i>
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      because her son Jesus is one person who is both God and man, divine and human.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ephesus_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ephesus-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccel.org_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ccel.org-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Cyril_of_Alexandria" title="Cyril of Alexandria">
       Cyril of Alexandria
      </a>
      wrote, "I am amazed that there are some who are entirely in doubt as to whether the holy Virgin should be called
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      or not. For if our Lord Jesus Christ is God, how is the holy Virgin who gave [Him] birth, not [
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      ]?" (Epistle 1, to the monks of Egypt;
      <a href="/wiki/Patrologia_Graeca" title="Patrologia Graeca">
       PG
      </a>
      77:13B). Thus the significance of
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      lies more in what it says about Jesus than any declaration about Mary, according to this Catholic doctrine.
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the Orthodox doctrinal teaching on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economy_(Eastern_Orthodox_Church)" title="Economy (Eastern Orthodox Church)">
       economy
      </a>
      of salvation, Mary's identity, role, and status as
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      is acknowledged as indispensable. For this reason, it is formally defined as official
      <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">
       dogma
      </a>
      . The only other
      <a href="/wiki/Mariology" title="Mariology">
       Mariological
      </a>
      teaching so defined is that of her virginity. Both of these teachings have a bearing on the identity of Jesus Christ. By contrast, certain other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary#Marian_theology" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">
       Marian
      </a>
      beliefs which do not bear directly on the doctrine concerning the person of Jesus (for example, her sinlessness, the circumstances surrounding her
      <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Immaculate_Conception#Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Feast of the Immaculate Conception">
       conception
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_the_Theotokos" title="Nativity of the Theotokos">
       birth
      </a>
      , her
      <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_Mary" title="Presentation of Mary">
       Presentation in the Temple
      </a>
      , her continuing
      <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_virginity_of_Mary" title="Perpetual virginity of Mary">
       virginity
      </a>
      following the birth of Jesus, and her
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dormition_of_the_Virgin" title="Dormition of the Virgin">
       death
      </a>
      ), which are taught and believed by the Orthodox Church (being expressed in the Church's liturgy and patristic writings), are not formally defined by the Church.
     </p>

     </p>

       Gregory the Theologian

      <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">

      <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">

      <i>

      </i>

     <p>
      The use of
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      was formally affirmed at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Ecumenical_Council" title="Third Ecumenical Council">
       Third Ecumenical Council
      </a>
      held at
      <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">
       Ephesus
      </a>
      in 431. The competing view, advocated by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarch of Constantinople">
       Patriarch
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Nestorius" title="Nestorius">
       Nestorius
      </a>
      of Constantinople, was that Mary should be called
      <i>
       Christotokos
      </i>
      , meaning "Birth-giver of Christ," to restrict her role to the mother of Christ's humanity only and not his divine nature.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nestorius' opponents, led by
      <a href="/wiki/Cyril_of_Alexandria" title="Cyril of Alexandria">
       Cyril of Alexandria
      </a>
      , viewed this as dividing Jesus into two distinct persons, the human who was Son of Mary, and the divine who was not. To them, this was unacceptable since by destroying the perfect union of the divine and human natures in Christ, it sabotaged the fullness of the Incarnation and, by extension, the salvation of humanity. The council accepted Cyril's reasoning, affirmed the title
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      for Mary, and
      <a href="/wiki/Anathema" title="Anathema">
       anathematised
      </a>
      Nestorius' view as
      <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">
       heresy
      </a>
      . (See
      <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">
       Nestorianism
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      In letters to Nestorius which were afterwards included among the council documents, Cyril explained his doctrine. He noted that "the holy fathers... have ventured to call the holy Virgin
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      , not as though the nature of the
      <a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">
       Word
      </a>
      or his divinity received the beginning of their existence from the holy Virgin, but because from her was born his holy body, rationally endowed with a soul, with which [body] the Word was united according to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(religion)" title="Hypostasis (religion)">
       hypostasis
      </a>
      , and is said to have been begotten according to the flesh" (Cyril's second letter to Nestorius).
     </p>
     <p>
      Explaining his rejection of Nestorius' preferred title for Mary (
      <i>
       Christotokos
      </i>
      ), Cyril wrote:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "Confessing the Word to be united with the flesh according to the hypostasis, we worship one Son and Lord, Jesus Christ. We do not divide him into parts and separate man and God as though they were united with each other [only] through a unity of dignity and authority... nor do we name separately Christ the Word from God, and in similar fashion, separately, another Christ from the woman, but we know only one Christ, the Word from God the Father with his own flesh... But we do not say that the Word from God dwelt as in an ordinary human born of the holy virgin... we understand that, when he became flesh, not in the same way as he is said to dwell among the saints do we distinguish the manner of the indwelling; but he was united by nature and not turned into flesh... There is, then, one Christ and Son and Lord, not with the sort of conjunction that a human being might have with God as in a unity of dignity or authority; for equality of honor does not unite natures. For Peter and John were equal to each other in honor, both of them being apostles and holy disciples, but the two were not one. Nor do we understand the manner of conjunction to be one of juxtaposition, for this is insufficient in regard to natural union.... Rather we reject the term 'conjunction' as being inadequate to express the union... [T]he holy virgin gave birth in the flesh to God united with the flesh according to hypostasis, for that reason we call her
       <i>
        Theotokos
       </i>
       ... If anyone does not confess that Emmanuel is, in truth, God, and therefore that the holy virgin is
       <i>
        Theotokos
       </i>
       (for she bore in a fleshly manner the Word from God become flesh), let him be anathema." (Cyril's third letter to Nestorius)
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hymns">
       Hymns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theotokos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hymns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Hymns_to_Mary" title="Hymns to Mary">
       Hymns to Mary
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Theotokos
      </i>
      is often used in
      <a href="/wiki/Hymns_to_Mary" title="Hymns to Mary">
       hymns to Mary
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">
       Eastern Orthodox
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic" title="Eastern Catholic">
       Eastern Catholic
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox" title="Oriental Orthodox">
       Oriental Orthodox
      </a>
      churches. The most common is
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Axion_Estin" title="Axion Estin">
        Axion Estin
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       It is truly meet
      </i>
      ), which is used in nearly every service.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other examples include
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sub_tuum_praesidium" title="Sub tuum praesidium">
        Beneath thy compassion
       </a>
      </i>
      dating from the third century, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hail_Mary" title="Hail Mary">
        Hail Mary
       </a>
      </i>
      in its Eastern form, and
      <i>
       All creation rejoices
      </i>
      , which replaces
      <i>
       Axion Estin
      </i>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">
       Divine Liturgy
      </a>
      on the Sundays of
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Lent" title="Great Lent">
       Great Lent
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solemnity">
       Solemnity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theotokos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solemnity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Solemnity_of_Mary,_Mother_of_God" title="Solemnity of Mary, Mother of God">
       Solemnity of Mary, Mother of God
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the Roman Catholic Church, the solemnity of Mary as Mother of God (Theotokos) is celebrated on 1 January, on the same day as the
      <a href="/wiki/Octave_(liturgical)" title="Octave (liturgical)">
       Octave
      </a>
      of Christmas. Her maternity was celebrated on 11 October in pre-1970 versions of the
      <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">
       General Roman Calendar
      </a>
      , which some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_Catholics" title="Traditional Catholics">
       traditional Catholics
      </a>
      still observe.
     </p>
     <p>
      This solemnity comes from around 500 AD and was originally celebrated in the Eastern Churches.
     </p>
