     <p>
      <b>
       Leet
      </b>
      (or "
      <b>
       1337
      </b>
      "), also known as
      <b>
       eleet
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       leetspeak
      </b>
      , is an alternative
      <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
       alphabet
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English language
      </a>
      that is used primarily on the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
       Internet
      </a>
      . It uses various combinations of
      <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">
       ASCII
      </a>
      characters to replace
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">
       Latinate
      </a>
      letters. For example, leet spellings of the word
      <i>
       leet
      </i>
      include
      <i>
       1337
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       l33t
      </i>
      ;
      <i>
       eleet
      </i>
      may be spelled
      <i>
       31337
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       3l33t
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The term leet is derived from the word
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Elite" title="Elite">
        elite
       </a>
      </i>
      . The leet alphabet is a specialized form of
      <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">
       symbolic
      </a>
      writing. Leet may also be considered a
      <a href="/wiki/Substitution_cipher" title="Substitution cipher">
       substitution cipher
      </a>
      , although many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialects" title="Dialects">
       dialects
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
       linguistic varieties
      </a>
      exist in different online communities. The term
      <b>
       leet
      </b>
      is also used as an
      <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
       adjective
      </a>
      to describe formidable prowess or accomplishment, especially in the fields of
      <a href="/wiki/Online_game" title="Online game">
       online gaming
      </a>
      and in its original usage 
      <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(hobbyist)" title="Hacker (hobbyist)">
       computer hacking
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Leet originated within
      <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_board_system" title="Bulletin board system">
       bulletin board systems
      </a>
      (BBS) in the 1980s,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where having "elite" status on a BBS allowed a user access to file folders, games, and special chat rooms. The
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_the_Dead_Cow" title="Cult of the Dead Cow">
       Cult of the Dead Cow
      </a>
      hacker collective has been credited with the original coining of the term, in their text-files of that era.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One theory is that it was developed to defeat text filters created by BBS or
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_Relay_Chat" title="Internet Relay Chat">
       Internet Relay Chat
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_operators" title="System operators">
       system operators
      </a>
      for message boards to discourage the discussion of forbidden topics, like
      <a href="/wiki/Software_cracking" title="Software cracking">
       cracking
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">
       hacking
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Creative misspellings and ASCII-art-derived words were also a way to attempt to indicate one was knowledgeable about the culture of computer users. Once the reserve of
      <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">
       hackers
      </a>
      , crackers, and
      <a href="/wiki/Script_kiddie" title="Script kiddie">
       script kiddies
      </a>
      , leet has since entered the mainstream.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is now also used to mock
      <a href="/wiki/Newbie" title="Newbie">
       newbies
      </a>
      , or newcomers, on web sites, or in gaming communities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rome_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rome-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some consider
      <a href="/wiki/Emoticon" title="Emoticon">
       emoticons
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/ASCII_art" title="ASCII art">
       ASCII art
      </a>
      , like smiley faces, to be leet, while others maintain that leet consists of only symbolic word encryption. More obscure forms of leet, involving the use of symbol combinations and almost no letters or numbers, continue to be used for its original purpose of encrypted communication. It is also sometimes used as a script language. Variants of leet have been used for censorship purposes for many years; for instance "@$$" (ass) and "$#!+" (shit) are frequently seen to make a word appear censored to the untrained eye but obvious to a person familiar with leet.
     </p>
     <p>
      Leet symbols, especially the number 1337, are
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">
       Internet memes
      </a>
      that have spilled over into popular culture. Signs that show the numbers "1337" are popular motifs for pictures and shared widely across the Internet.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
       Orthography
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      One of the hallmarks of leet is its unique approach to
      <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
       orthography
      </a>
      , using substitutions of other characters, letters or otherwise, to represent a letter or letters in a word.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sterling_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sterling-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goss_80_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-goss_80-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For more casual use of leet, the primary strategy is to use
      <a href="/wiki/Homoglyph" title="Homoglyph">
       homoglyphs
      </a>
      , symbols that closely resemble (to varying degrees) the letters for which they stand. The symbol chosen is flexibleanything that the reader can make sense of is valid. However, this practice is not extensively used in regular Leet; more often it is seen in situations where the
      <a href="/wiki/Argot" title="Argot">
       argot
      </a>
      (i.e., "secret language") characteristics of the system are required, either to exclude newbies or outsiders in general, i.e., anything that the
      <i>
       average
      </i>
      reader
      <i>
       cannot
      </i>
      make sense of is valid; a valid reader should himself try to make sense, if deserving of the underlying message. Another use for Leet orthographic substitutions is the creation of paraphrased passwords.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Limitations imposed by websites on password length (usually no more than 36) and the characters permitted (usually alphanumeric and underscore) require less extensive forms of Leet when used in this application.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some examples of leet include
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B1ff" title="B1ff">
        B1ff
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Newbie" title="Newbie">
        n00b
       </a>
      </i>
      , a term for the stereotypical
      <a href="/wiki/Newbie" title="Newbie">
       newbie
      </a>
      ; the l33t programming language; and the web-comic
      <a href="/wiki/Megatokyo" title="Megatokyo">
       Megatokyo
      </a>
      , which contains characters who speak leet.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
       Morphology
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Text rendered in leet is often characterized by distinctive, recurring forms.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       The
       <i>
        -xor
       </i>
       suffix
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The meaning of this suffix is parallel with the English
      <i>
       -er
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       -r
      </i>
      suffixes (seen in
      <i>
       hacker
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       lesser
      </i>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in that it derives
      <a href="/wiki/Agent_noun" title="Agent noun">
       agent nouns
      </a>
      from a verb
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stem_(linguistics)" title="Stem (linguistics)">
       stem
      </a>
      . It is realized in two different forms:
      <i>
       -xor
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       -zor
      </i>
      ,
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="/-/ affix">
           -
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'or' in 'born'">
           r
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      and
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="/-/ affix">
           -
          </span>
          <span title="'z' in 'Zion'">
           z
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'or' in 'born'">
           r
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      , respectively. For example, the first may be seen in the word
      <i>
       hax(x)or
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       H4x0r
      </i>
      in leet)
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
           h
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'a' in 'bad'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
           s
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'or' in 'born'">
           r
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      and the second in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pwn" title="Pwn">
        pwnzor
       </a>
      </i>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/o/ long 'o' in 'code'">
           o
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="'z' in 'Zion'">
           z
          </span>
          <span title="/r/ 'or' in 'born'">
           r
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      . Additionally, this
      <a href="/wiki/Nominalization" title="Nominalization">
       nominalization
      </a>
      may also be
      <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
       inflected
      </a>
      with all of the suffixes of regular
      <a href="/wiki/English_verbs" title="English verbs">
       English verbs
      </a>
      . The letter 'o' is often replaced with the numeral 0.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       The
       <i>
        -age
       </i>
       suffix
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Derivation of a noun from a verb stem is possible by attaching
      <i>
       -age
      </i>
      to the base form of any verb. Attested derivations are
      <i>
       pwnage
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       skillage
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       speakage
      </i>
      . However, Leet provides exceptions; the word
      <i>
       leetage
      </i>
      is acceptable, referring to actively being
      <i>
       leet
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goss_79_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-goss_79-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These nouns are often used with a form of "to be" rather than "to have," e.g., "that was pwnage" rather than "he has pwnage". Either is a more emphatic way of expressing the simpler "he pwns," but the former implies that the person is
      <i>
       embodying
      </i>
      the trait rather than merely possessing it.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       The
       <i>
        -ness
       </i>
       suffix
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Derivation of a noun from an adjective stem is done by attaching
      <i>
       -ness
      </i>
      to any adjective. This is entirely the same as the English form, except it is used much more often in Leet. Nouns such as
      <i>
       lulzness
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       leetness
      </i>
      are derivations using this suffix.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Words ending in
       <i>
        -ed
       </i>
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      When forming a past participle ending in
      <i>
       -ed
      </i>
      , the Leet user may replace the
      <i>
       -e
      </i>
      with an apostrophe, as was common in
      <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">
       poetry
      </a>
      of previous centuries, (e.g. "pwned" becomes "pwn'd"). Sometimes, the apostrophe is removed as well (e.g. "pwned" becomes "pwnd"). The word ending may also be substituted by
      <i>
       -t
      </i>
      (e.g.
      <i>
       pwned
      </i>
      becomes
      <i>
       pwnt
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blanc33_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-blanc33-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Use of the
       <i>
        -&amp;
       </i>
       suffix
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Words ending in
      <i>
       -and
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       -anned
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       -ant
      </i>
      , or a similar sound can sometimes be spelled with an
      <a href="/wiki/Ampersand" title="Ampersand">
       ampersand
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       &amp;
      </i>
      ) to express the ending sound (e.g. "This is the
      <a href="/wiki/Sandbox_(software_development)" title="Sandbox (software development)">
       s&amp;box
      </a>
      ," "I'm sorry, you've been b&amp;", "&amp;hill/&amp;farm"). This is most commonly used with the word
      <i>
       banned.
      </i>
      An alternate form of "B&amp;" is "B7", as the ampersand is typed with the "7" key in the standard US keyboard layout. It is often seen in the phrase "IBB7" (in before banned), which indicates that the poster believes that a previous poster will soon be banned from the site, channel, or board on which they are both posting.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Use of the "-zorz" suffix
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Verbs that are generated on the Internet (such as pwn) can be inflected by putting "zorz" on the end (generating the word pwnzorz in this example). The -zorz suffix can also be used to strengthen the meaning of the word (pwn means to defeat or to make a fool of; pwnzorz means to really beat or to make a fool of in a large way). The term is often used sarcastically, e.g. "Wow, is your team full of haxzorz?"
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
       Grammar
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Leet can be pronounced as a single syllable,
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /lit/
      </span>
      , rhyming with
      <i>
       eat,
      </i>
      by way of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphesis" title="Aphesis">
       aphesis
      </a>
      of the initial vowel of "elite". It may also be pronounced as two syllables,
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /lit/
      </span>
      . Like
      <a href="/wiki/Jargon_File" title="Jargon File">
       hacker slang
      </a>
      , Leet enjoys a looser grammar than standard English.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rome_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rome-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The loose grammar, just like loose spelling, encodes some level of emphasis, ironic or otherwise. A reader must rely more on intuitive
      <a href="/wiki/Parsing" title="Parsing">
       parsing
      </a>
      of Leet to determine the meaning of a sentence rather than the actual sentence structure. In particular, speakers of Leet are fond of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbing" title="Verbing">
       verbing
      </a>
      nouns, turning verbs into nouns (and back again) as forms of emphasis, e.g. "Austin rocks" is weaker than "Austin roxxorz" (note spelling), which is weaker than "Au5t1N is t3h r0xx0rz" (note grammar), which is weaker than something like "0MFG D00D /\571N 15 T3H l_l83 1337 0XX0Z" (
      <a href="/wiki/SMS_language#Common_abbreviations" title="SMS language">
       OMG
      </a>
      , dude, Austin is the
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cber" title="ber">
       ber
      </a>
      -elite rocks-er!). In essence, all of these mean "Austin rocks," not necessarily the other options. Added words and misspellings add to the speaker's enjoyment. Leet, like hacker slang, employs
      <a href="/wiki/Analogy" title="Analogy">
       analogy
      </a>
      in construction of new words. For example, if
      <i>
       haxored
      </i>
      is the past tense of the verb "to hack" (hack  haxor  haxored), then
      <i>
       winzored
      </i>
      would be easily understood to be the past tense conjugation of "to win," even if the reader had not seen that particular word before.
     </p>
     <p>
      Leet has its own colloquialisms, many of which originated as jokes based on common typing errors, habits of new computer users, or knowledge of
      <a href="/wiki/Cyberculture" title="Cyberculture">
       cyberculture
      </a>
      and history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goss_81_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-goss_81-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Leet is not solely based upon one language or character set.
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      , and other languages have Leet forms, and Leet in one language may use characters from another where they are available. As such, while it may be referred to as a "
      <a href="/wiki/Cipher" title="Cipher">
       cipher
      </a>
      ", a "dialect", or a "language", Leet does not fit squarely into any of these categories. The term
      <i>
       leet
      </i>
      itself is often written
      <i>
       31337
      </i>
      , or
      <i>
       1337
      </i>
      , and many other variations. After the meaning of these became widely familiar,
      <i>
       10100111001
      </i>
      came to be used in its place, because it is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_numeral_system" title="Binary numeral system">
       binary
      </a>
      form of
      <i>
       1337
      </i>
      decimal, making it more of a puzzle to interpret.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        Notes 1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.google.com/search?q=%2210100111001%22+%221337%22+leet&amp;num=100" rel="nofollow">
       This Google search
      </a>
      finds examples of the two number forms used together on the Web with the name
      <i>
       Leet
      </i>
      . An increasingly common characteristic of Leet is changing its grammatical usage to be deliberately incorrect. The widespread popularity of deliberate misspelling is similar to the cult following of the "
      <a href="/wiki/All_your_base_are_belong_to_us" title="All your base are belong to us">
       All your base are belong to us
      </a>
      " phrase. Indeed, the online and computer communities have been international from their inception, so spellings and phrases typical of non-native speakers are quite common.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
       Vocabulary
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many words originally derived from Leet slang have now become part of the modern
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_slang" title="Internet slang">
       Internet slang
      </a>
      , such as "
      <a href="/wiki/Pwn" title="Pwn">
       pwned
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The original driving force of new vocabulary in Leet were common misspellings and typing errors such as "
      <a href="/wiki/Teh" title="Teh">
       teh
      </a>
      " (generally considered lolspeak), and intentional misspellings,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goss_83_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-goss_83-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      especially the "z" at the end of words ("skillz").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another prominent example of a surviving Leet expression is
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/W00t" title="W00t">
        w00t
       </a>
      </i>
      , an exclamation of joy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      w00t is sometimes used as a
      <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">
       backronym
      </a>
      for "We owned the other team."
     </p>
     <p>
      New words (or corruptions thereof) may arise from a need to make one's username unique. As any given Internet service reaches more people, the number of names available to a given user is drastically reduced. While many users may wish to have the username "CatLover," for example, in many cases it is only possible for one user to have the moniker. As such, degradations of the name may evolve, such as "C@7L0vr." As the Leet cipher is highly dynamic, there is a wider possibility for multiple users to share the "same" name, through combinations of spelling and transliterations.
     </p>
     <p>
      Additionally,
      <i>
       leet
      </i>
      the word itselfcan be found in the
      <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">
       screen-names
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamertags" title="Gamertags">
       gamertags
      </a>
      of many Internet and video games. Use of the term in such a manner announces a high level of skill, though such an announcement may be seen as baseless
      <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">
       hubris
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hope_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hope-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>

      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Haxor
      </i>
      , and derivations thereof, is Leet for "hacker",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blanc30_32_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-blanc30_32-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and it is one of the most commonplace examples of the use of the
      <i>
       -xor
      </i>
      suffix.
      <i>
       Suxxor
      </i>
      (pronounced suck-zor) is a derogatory term which originated in
      <a href="/wiki/Warez" title="Warez">
       warez
      </a>
      culture and is currently used in multi-user environments such as multiplayer video games and
      <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">
       instant messaging
      </a>
      ; it, like
      <i>
       haxor
      </i>
      , is one of the early Leet words to use the
      <i>
       -xor
      </i>
      suffix.
      <i>
       Suxxor
      </i>
      is a modified version of "sucks" (the phrase "to suck"), and the meaning is the same as the English slang. Its negative definition essentially makes it the opposite of
      <i>
       roxxor
      </i>
      , and both can be used as a verb or a noun. The letters
      <i>
       ck
      </i>
      are often replaced with the Greek  (chi) in other words as well.
     </p>

       <a href="#cite_note-blanc32_33-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As is a common characteristic of Leet, the terms have also been adapted into noun and adjective forms,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutch_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-dutch-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       ownage
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       pwnage
      </i>
      , which can refer to the situation of
      <i>
       pwning
      </i>
      or to the superiority of its subject (e.g., "He is a very good player. He is pwnage.").
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pr0n">
       Pr0n
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Pr0n
      </i>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">
       slang
      </a>
      for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">
        pornography
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchell_1-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-mitchell-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is a deliberately inaccurate spelling/pronunciation for
      <i>
       porn
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acronym_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-acronym-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where a zero is often used to replace the letter O. It is sometimes used in legitimate communications (such as email discussion groups,
      <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">
       Usenet
      </a>
      , chat rooms, and Internet web pages) to circumvent language and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Content_filter" title="Content filter">
       content filters
      </a>
      , which may reject messages as offensive or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" title="Spam (electronic)">
       spam
      </a>
      . The word also helps prevent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_engines" title="Search engines">
       search engines
      </a>
      from associating commercial sites with pornography, which might result in unwelcome traffic.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <i>
       Pr0n
      </i>
      is also sometimes spelled backwards (n0rp) to further obscure the meaning to potentially uninformed readers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        Notes 2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It can also refer to
      <a href="/wiki/ASCII_art" title="ASCII art">
       ASCII art
      </a>
      depicting pornographic images, or to photos of the internals of consumer and industrial hardware.
      <i>
       Prawn
      </i>
      , a spoof of the misspelling, has started to come into use, as well; in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto:_Vice_City" title="Grand Theft Auto: Vice City">
        Grand Theft Auto: Vice City
       </a>
      </i>
      , a pornographer films his movies on "Prawn Island". Conversely, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">
       RPG
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Loathing" title="Kingdom of Loathing">
        Kingdom of Loathing
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prawn" title="Prawn">
        prawn
       </a>
      </i>
      , referring to a kind of
      <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">
       crustacean
      </a>
      , is spelled
      <i>
       pr0n
      </i>
      , leading to the creation of food items such as "pr0n chow mein".
     </p>
