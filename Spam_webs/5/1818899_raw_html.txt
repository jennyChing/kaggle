          Next, Next, Next at Hessel Museum of Art
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
        <!--
-->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img src="http://www.evan-roth.com/photos/data/multi-touch-finger-paintings/web/DSC_1487ed.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Next, Next, Next
         <br/>
         Vinyl print
         <br/>
         2014
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.bard.edu/ccs/signal-from-noise/">
          Signal from Noise
         </a>
         , curated by Elizabeth Larison. Hessel Museum of Art, Bard College, NY
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          Roths work is from his series entitled Multi-Touch Paintings (2012-2014)enlarged prints of various fingerprint patterns, which are derived from routine tasks performed upon a touch-screen device. The image selected for this exhibition, Next, Next, Next (2014) speaks directly to the inundation of visual information that requires the simplest and most general of movements in order to encourage prolonged viewing of new content.
         </p>
         <p>
          Oversized and at eye-level, the fingerprint pattern of Next, Next, Next draws attention to an emergent language of both communication and gestural movements that are unique to the 21st century. Exploring how popular forms of passive, mundane engagement operate within contemporary networked culture, Roths Multi-Touch Paintings make visual the conflation of obscure personal information (epitomized by the fingerprint) and idle activity. Such gestures are are often made in successive, yet seemingly inconsequential ways that are nonetheless valuable elsewherewhether as an unwitting contribution to page views or to a personal data profile. Online engagement as Roths works so deftly remind us, are reduceable to a few casual taps or swipes of the finger, many of which are made because they seemingly have no consequence, yet offer some semblance of connection or engagement.
         </p>
         <p>
          Recognizing that the rapid exchange of information on the web is a new historical experience, Roth responds to this contemporary media climate by generating a body of work that is intended to be understood rapidly, if also at varying levels of depth. Intended to be experienced in at least three temporalities (online, in a gallery, and within a historical context), Roths oeuvre is reflects the signposts of a moment that demands multiplicity in order to have social and historical relevance.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         ~ Elizabeth Larison
        </p>

          Dances for Mobile Phones
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
        <!--
-->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.evan-roth.com/exhibitions/voicesoverthehorizon/dancesformobilephones/">
          <img src="http://evan-roth.com/exhibitions/voicesoverthehorizon/dances-for-mobiles.gif"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.evan-roth.com/exhibitions/voicesoverthehorizon/dancesformobilephones/">
          Dances for Mobile Phones series
         </a>
         <br/>
         single-channel videos
         <br/>
         2015
        </p>
        <p>
         <img src="http://www.evan-roth.com/photos/data/voices-over-the-horizon/carrollfletcher/web/evan-roth-sml-2.jpg"/>
         <br/>
         (image courtesy Carroll/Fletcher)
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          Our mobile touchscreens are perhaps our most intimate connection to the Internet, rarely leaving our sides. Here, video recordings show everyday people performing everyday tasks on their mobile devices. Shot with a full-spectrum infrared and ultraviolet camera built by the Cleveland Paranormal Society to document disembodied human energy, pixels ordinarily visible to the human eye become invisible, and those signals normally invisible are made visible. In obscuring the digital interface, the new and unnatural movements we have adopted become all the more apparent. By contrast, the only illumination visible from the phones is the ordinarily invisible infrared beam, emitted from LEDs in the top section of the phone near the earpiece and used primarily for facial recognition. This illumination is controlled not by the user, but by the device, and displayed together these works prompt the question of who or what is controlling our gestures as well.
         </p>
         <p>
          ~
          <a href="http://www.carrollfletcher.com/exhibitions/39/overview/">
           Carroll / Fletcher
          </a>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.carrollfletcher.com/exhibitions/39/overview/">
          Voices over the Horizon
         </a>
         is on view through April 11th at Carroll / Fletcher in London.
        </p>

          Voices Over the Horizon @ Carroll / Fletcher
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
        <!--
-->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img src="http://www.evan-roth.com/photos/data/ghost-hunting/web/P1200997.JPG"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.carrollfletcher.com/exhibitions/39/overview/">
          Voices Over the Horizon
         </a>
         (solo exhibition)
         <br/>
         Carroll / Fletcher, London, UK
         <br/>
         March 5  April 11, 2015
         <br/>
         Private View: 5 March | 6.30-8.30 pm
        </p>
        <p>
         Press release:
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Carroll / Fletcher is pleased to present a solo exhibition by Paris-based American artist Evan Roth.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         In Voices over the Horizon, Roth presents the findings of his paranormal investigations into the Internet. Using ghost-hunting technologies and rituals, Roth ventures into the Internets physical landscape to reconnect with a network changed by monetisation, centralisation and surveillance.
        </p>
        <p>
         Often described as the search for disembodied human energy, ghost hunting for Roth becomes an apt way to describe our daily interactions with the Internet  a world seemingly invisible yet swarming all around us.
        </p>
        <p>
         Like his 19th century counterparts whose urgent enquiries into the supernatural were often conducted on sites of assumed paranormal activity, Roth made a recent pilgrimage to Cornwall on the south-westerly tip of the UK, one of the worlds most important telecommunications hubs dating back to the very first trans-Atlantic telegraph cables laid there in 1870. Today, fibre-optic Internet cabling connecting the United States to Europe ascends from the depths of the Atlantic basin onto the Cornish coast carrying 25% of the worlds Internet traffic. Zigzagging and disappearing through several small beach towns, here the physical Internet meets a picturesque, untamed landscape, long steeped in tales of both communication technologies and the paranormal.
        </p>
        <p>
         Voices Over the Horizon reveals loss and an unconventional search to seek out optimism and inspiration in an increasingly dark Internet landscape.
        </p>
        <p>
         56  57 Eastcastle St
         <br/>
         London W1W 8EQ
        </p>

         <a href="http://www.blog.ni9e.com/no-original-research/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to No Original Research @ Snel Hest ">
          No Original Research @ Snel Hest 
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
        <!--
-->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://doroknot-on-gainsboro.com/">
          <img alt="" src="http://www.evan-roth.com/photos/data/no-original-research/web/doroknot-on-gainsboro.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <br/>
         <a href="http://doroknot-on-gainsboro.com">
          http://doroknot-on-gainsboro.com
         </a>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://rhombicdodecahedron-on-lightyellow.com/">
          <img alt="" src="http://www.evan-roth.com/photos/data/no-original-research/web/rhombicdodecahedron-on-lightyellow.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <br/>
         <a href="http://rhombicdodecahedron-on-lightyellow.com">
          http://rhombicdodecahedron-on-lightyellow.com
         </a>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://lotru-on-hotpink.com/">
          <img alt="" src="http://www.evan-roth.com/photos/data/no-original-research/web/lotru-on-hotpink.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <br/>
         <a href="http://lotru-on-hotpink.com">
          http://lotru-on-hotpink.com
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://no-original-research.com/">
          No Original Research
         </a>
         is a new series of 11 art websites which are premiering today as part of the
         <a href="http://skunk.cc/profile.php?id=3908">
          Snel Hest
         </a>
         exhibition at the Alingsas Konsthallen (Sweden). For a complete list of pieces included in the series go to:
         <a href="http://no-original-research.com">
          http://no-original-research.com
         </a>
         (more info
         <a href="http://www.evan-roth.com/work/no-original-research/">
          here
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          <a href="http://no-original-research.com/">
           No Original Research
          </a>
          is a series of art websites, each created from a single animation and audio file found on wikipedia.org. The title originates from one of
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Core_content_policies">
           Wikipedias core content policies
          </a>
          , which states that all material must cite reliable, published sources that directly support the material being presented. The URL of each composition serves as its title, describing the repeated animation and the background color. The use of these gifs and
          <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_colors">
           HTML color names
          </a>
          are a celebration of content driven by function and necessity. Compositions are created by copying a found animated gif file dozens of times and embedding them into a single HTML page. When the browser tries (and fails) to load all of the files simultaneously they become out of synch, creating an animation cycle that visualizes the latencies specific to the viewer. Each viewing is a unique experience dictated by the speed of the network, the browser used and the speed of the computer.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Information on the Wikipedia content used to create the pieces can be found by viewing the
         <a href="view-source:http://doroknot-on-gainsboro.com/">
          page source
         </a>
         of the individual pieces.
        </p>
        <p>
         Commissioned by the Alingsas Konsthallen.
        </p>
