   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Temple of Montu (Medamud)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Temple of Montu
      </b>
      (also Temple of Monthu, Montju, or Menthu) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_temple" title="Egyptian temple">
       Egyptian temple
      </a>
      dedicated to the worship of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monthu" title="Monthu">
       Monthu
      </a>
      . The site is located 5 kilometres (3.1mi) northeast of
      <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">
       Karnak
      </a>
      , which is located within
      <a href="/wiki/Luxor" title="Luxor">
       Luxor
      </a>
      . Medamud was excavated by French archaeologist
      <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Bisson_de_la_Roque" title="Fernand Bisson de la Roque">
       Fernand Bisson de la Roque
      </a>
      from 1925 to the post-war, and revealed many buildings including a temple dedicated to Montu. This temple replaced an ancient sanctuary and consists of an open forum with a tower and enclosing two mounds that housed the chapels of worship. It is thought that original sanctuary dates to the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Old Kingdom of Egypt">
       Old Kingdom
      </a>
      . The ruins of the last structure date to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_VIII" title="Ptolemy VIII">
       Ptolemy VIII
      </a>
      period of the 2nd century BC, although decorations and additions continued to be added centuries later by the Romans. Because of Montu's strong association with raging bulls, the temple was a major centre of worship for bulls, containing many statues of bulls for worship and reliefs. Most of these statues are now located in various museums around the world.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Montu">
       Montu
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Montu">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Menthu_in_Medamud.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="678" data-file-width="310" height="481" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Menthu_in_Medamud.jpg/220px-Menthu_in_Medamud.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/Menthu_in_Medamud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/Menthu_in_Medamud.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Menthu_in_Medamud.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Relief of Montu in Medamud.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Montu was a
      <a href="/wiki/Falcon" title="Falcon">
       falcon
      </a>
      -headed,
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_war_deities" title="List of war deities">
       god of war
      </a>
      . He was the
      <a href="/wiki/Tutelary_deity" title="Tutelary deity">
       patron god
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Egypt" title="Thebes, Egypt">
       Thebes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D.27AuriaFazzini2008_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-D.27AuriaFazzini2008-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His consorts were Tjenenyet (or Tanen-t) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raettawy" title="Raettawy">
       Raettawy
      </a>
      (or Rattaui); his son was Harpora.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bard1999_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bard1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is also associated with a sacred bull named
      <a href="/wiki/Buchis" title="Buchis">
       Buchis
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hart2005_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hart2005-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="476" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg/220px-Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg/330px-Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg/440px-Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fernandbissondelaroque.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The temple's excavator,
        <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Bisson_de_la_Roque" title="Fernand Bisson de la Roque">
         Fernand Bisson de la Roque
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In addition to the temple at Medamud, temples dedicated to Montu were built in Karnak, Armant, and Tod.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Remler2010_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Remler2010-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From 1925-1932, the Medamud Temple of Montu was excavated by French archaeologist, Fernand Bisson de la Roque, of the
      <a href="/wiki/Institut_Fran%C3%A7ais_d%27Arch%C3%A9ologie_Orientale" title="Institut Franais d'Archologie Orientale">
       Institut Franais d'Archologie Orientale
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redford2001_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Redford2001-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in conjunction with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louvre_Museum" title="Louvre Museum">
       Louvre Museum
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bard1999_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bard1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
       Geography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Medamud (or Madu) was an outpost of Thebes, located 3 miles (4.8km) away.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fletcher2011_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fletcher2011-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Little is known about the town of Medamud or its other structures. Prior to construction of the Middle Kingdom temple, the site had been burned and earlier mounds were razed. The
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">
       Middle Kingdom
      </a>
      temple is situated on a circular mound. Its orientation bears east-west.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bard1999_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bard1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nearby are the
      <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_temple" title="Egyptian temple">
       Egyptian temple
      </a>
      complexes at
      <a href="/wiki/Luxor_Temple" title="Luxor Temple">
       Luxor
      </a>
      and Karnak. The Karnak Temple Complex contains three precincts, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Precinct_of_Montu" title="Precinct of Montu">
       Precinct of Montu
      </a>
      , which contains another Temple of Montu.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray2010_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gray2010-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thanjan2011_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Thanjan2011-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Kingdom_site">
       Old Kingdom site
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Old Kingdom site">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Old Kingdom temple site, dedicated to Montu, was walled and had a sacred grove. It had a tunnel system, mounds and chambers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunson2002_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bunson2002-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Kingdom_temple">
       Middle Kingdom temple
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Kingdom temple">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Middle Kingdom
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelfth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twelfth dynasty of Egypt">
       12th dynasty
      </a>
      building is a rare example of the foundation of religion in the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">
       Middle Kingdom of Egypt
      </a>
      era. Others of this period are the temple of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sesostris_III" title="Sesostris III">
       Sesostris III
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Abydos,_Egypt" title="Abydos, Egypt">
       Abydos
      </a>
      , the Temple of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medinet_Madi" title="Medinet Madi">
       Medinet Madi
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Qasr_el-Sagha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Qasr el-Sagha (page does not exist)">
       Qasr el-Sagha
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fayoum" title="Fayoum">
       Fayoum
      </a>
      dating from approximately the same time, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Mortuary_temple" title="Mortuary temple">
       funerary temple
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Mentuhotep_II" title="Mentuhotep II">
       Mentuhotep II
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleventh_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eleventh dynasty of Egypt">
       11th dynasty
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Deir_el-Bahari" title="Deir el-Bahari">
       Deir el-Bahari
      </a>
      . The sanctuary was remodeled in later periods..
     </p>
     <p>
      The temple's features included a tribune platform, a canal, a
      <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#D" title="Glossary of architecture">
       dromos
      </a>
      , main gate, portico, hall, and sanctuary. There was also a courtyard for the living sacred bull.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bard1999_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bard1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It consisted of a first chamber, 200 by 180 meters. The main access was by the east and north. A sacred lake was probably on the west side of the temple. The temple consisted of two distinct, contiguous sections that have been interpreted as a temple to the north and one to the south where the priests would have had their quarters. Typical of the period, the temple was built of brick; it included raw elements for the doors and frames, and column or carved stone, the bases of which were decorated. Carved stone would have been present in the most intimate parts of the sanctuary, but no trace of it has been discovered. No doubt these parts were considered sacred and reused in later buildings. The plan of the sanctuary of the Middle Kingdom is still subject to discussion. But it was to have at least one
      <a href="/wiki/Hypostyle" title="Hypostyle">
       hypostyle
      </a>
      antechamber, giving access to chapels of worship or shrine. A large courtyard surrounded by portico columns where a sacred bull would have lived would have been revered as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypostasis_(philosophy)" title="Hypostasis (philosophy)">
       hypostasis
      </a>
      of the living Montu.
     </p>
     <p>
      This temple has provided many examples of royal statuary and
      <a href="/wiki/Lithic_technology" title="Lithic technology">
       lithic
      </a>
      elements of
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_architecture" title="Ancient Egyptian architecture">
       Ancient Egyptian architecture
      </a>
      . One of the extravagant doors, dated to Sesostris III, is in the
      <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">
       Louvre
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desroches-Noblecourt1962_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Desroches-Noblecourt1962-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VernesR.C3.A9ville1944_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VernesR.C3.A9ville1944-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The temple continued to be decorated in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Thirteenth dynasty of Egypt">
       13th dynasty of Egypt
      </a>
      , including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobekhotep_II" title="Sobekhotep II">
       Sobekhotep II
      </a>
      , and included some reliefs of his predecessors and of his ancestor Sesostris III,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valbelle1998_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Valbelle1998-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      dressed in the fashion of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sed_festival" title="Sed festival">
       Sed festival
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="New_Kingdom_temple">
       New Kingdom temple
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New Kingdom temple">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The temple was revised later by the kings of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eighteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eighteenth dynasty of Egypt">
       18th dynasty of Egypt
      </a>
      and was rebuilt by
      <a href="/wiki/Thutmose_III" title="Thutmose III">
       Thutmose III
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.C3.89lisabethBelgique1943_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-.C3.89lisabethBelgique1943-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who rebuilt the stone sanctuary and adorned it with statues in his likeness.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Graeco-Roman_temple">
       Graeco-Roman temple
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Graeco-Roman temple">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montu-medamud.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="464" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Montu-medamud.png/250px-Montu-medamud.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Montu-medamud.png/375px-Montu-medamud.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/29/Montu-medamud.png 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montu-medamud.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Plan of the Temple of Montu at Medamud
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The site is currently closed to the public as a team of archaeologists and restorers work to protect against the modern city encroachment on the ancient remains of the former city.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3694" data-file-width="1796" height="452" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG/220px-Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG/330px-Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG/440px-Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montu_Ptolemaic_Period_Louvre.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Montu depicted with a bull's head, from the Ptolemaic period (332  30 BC). On display in the Louvre, the statue was found in Medamud in the cell of the goddess Rattaui.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Composed of twelve columns, the structure was built under
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_VIII" title="Ptolemy VIII">
       Ptolemy VIII
      </a>
      , who was then one of the great rebuilders of Medamud. Four of the six columns of the facade are still connected by walls. The elements of the inner temple are dated them from the beginning of the Ptolemaic period. The enclosure of the temple included a sacred lake and a deep well but was blocked off because it represented a danger to children playing in the area. The temple continued to be enlarged and decorated for centuries under the Roman emperors, including
      <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">
       Tiberius
      </a>
      (14-37),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEG_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SEG-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      up until the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">
       Antoninus Pius
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.28France.291984_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-.28France.291984-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A wharf, which was reached by a canal, connected the temple with a line of sphinxes; it is in a state of ruin. Although grass grows throughout the site, there is still find evidence of ancient pilgrims in the form of graffiti or engraved footprints. Following the processional aisle, it was possible to access a large door whose decoration dates from the reign of Tiberius. This monumental gateway was a large opening within the walls of the temple axis. It was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_VIII" title="Ptolemy VIII">
       Ptolemy VIII
      </a>
      , however who built the towered portico around the door, its decoration not completed until the reign of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_XII" title="Ptolemy XII">
       Ptolemy XII
      </a>
      . Another door dates to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_II" title="Ptolemy II">
       Ptolemy II
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of the artifacts found in the temple date to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_III" title="Ptolemy III">
       Ptolemy III
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_IV" title="Ptolemy IV">
       Ptolemy IV
      </a>
      . Many of these artifacts are statues of bulls which were used for worship and reliefs. The interior of this monument is interesting because it and its doors describes the traditional scenes of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sed_festival" title="Sed festival">
       Sed festival
      </a>
      when the king in traditional costume receives offerings or during an important step in the inauguration ceremony of royal power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchoorsWillems1998_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SchoorsWillems1998-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d.27.C3.89gyptologie1946_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-d.27.C3.89gyptologie1946-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Temple of Montu is also an important insight into how very popular bull cults were in ancient Egypt, Montu being strongly associated with raging bulls. A long corridor retained the scenes of the cult of the bull
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bukhis" title="Bukhis">
       Bukhis
      </a>
      which date mostly from the emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Domitian" title="Domitian">
       Domitian
      </a>
      (81-96). There is also a wall relief of a procession of musicians who came to visit
      <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">
       Trajan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Nothing remains of the earlier or later temples, and the remains are dated mainly from the Greco-Roman period. The temple at Medamud was probably founded as the place of worship for the living god Montu while the temple Bouchum (or Bucheum) at
      <a href="/wiki/Hermonthis" title="Hermonthis">
       Ermant
      </a>
      included a necropolis of sacred bulls, and was probably dedicated to his death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hari1964_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hari1964-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are
      <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">
       Coptic
      </a>
      remains on the temple site.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bard1999_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bard1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_exhibits">
       Museum exhibits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Temple_of_Montu_(Medamud)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Museum exhibits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      What could be removed from the original structure, parts of the building and
      <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">
       artifacts
      </a>
      , were taken to museums including the
      <a href="/wiki/Karnak_Open_Air_Museum" title="Karnak Open Air Museum">
       Karnak Open Air Museum
      </a>
      . Most of the temples statues are now located in various museums, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts_of_Lyon" title="Museum of Fine Arts of Lyon">
       Museum of Fine Arts of Lyon
      </a>
      and the Louvre.
     </p>
