             <section class="article-content fullwidth">
              <p>
               There are countless songs that reference New York City, from its neighborhoods to familiar streets  and now you can see them all on a map.
              </p>
              <p>
               The specific geography of NYC references in songs is detailed in this
               <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_songs_about_New_York_City" target="_blank">
                long list on Wikipedia
               </a>
               , but that's not very fun or user friendly. Constantine Valhouli, a real estate developer with a knack for viral side projects, got the idea to present the reference in map form.
              </p>
              <p>
               <iframe height="800" src="https://mapsengine.google.com/map/u/0/embed?mid=zFkoYJWAIngI.k-Bo7T8hk040" width="900">
               </iframe>
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                See also:
                <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2012/02/26/google-street-view-sightings/">
                 20 Strange and Hilarious Google Street View Sightings
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               This new project, made on Google Maps, pinpoints some 200 musical references and links users to the songs so they can listen for themselves. Valhouli crowdsourced from friends and email submissions to compile the map. (You can submit more to musicmapnyc@gmail.com.)
              </p>
              <p>
               "For me, New York has always been about music, tech, media, and mischief  and this little map seems like a way to combine those," Valhouli told
               <em>
                Mashable
               </em>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               The interactive visualization also allows you to explore and discover how certain neighborhoods embody certain sounds or present distinct eras. You can also see which artists call themselves New Yorkers or at least have New York on the brain.
              </p>
              <p>
               "Music is such a powerful, evocative way to connect with others' stories of the city." Valhouli added. "This list [is] like a collective version of
               <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Here-New-York-E-B-White/dp/1892145022" target="_blank">
                E. B. White's
                <em>
                 Here is New York
                </em>
               </a>
               , except with a better bass line and back beat."
              </p>
              <p>
               Valhouli was also the trickster behind "
               <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2013/08/facebook-fauxlore-kerouac-burroughs-and-a-fight-over-the-oxford-comma-that-never-was/278452/" target="_blank">
                Fauxlore
               </a>
               ," a viral sensation in which he engineered a bogus plaque commemorating the site of a scuffle between Jack Kerouac and William S. Burroughs over the Oxford Comma to hype up a building in Lowell, Massachusetts.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                <div class="bonus-content">
                 BONUS:
                 <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2014/04/29/google-street-view-artists/">
                  5 Evocative Artists Inspired by Google Street View
                 </a>
                </div>
               </strong>
              </p>
