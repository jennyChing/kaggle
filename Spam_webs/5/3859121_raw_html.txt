     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Purple Noon
       </b>
      </i>
      (Italian:
      <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
       <i>
        <b>
         Delitto in pieno sole
        </b>
       </i>
      </span>
      , French:
      <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
       <i>
        <b>
         Plein Soleil
        </b>
       </i>
      </span>
      , aka
      <i>
       Full Sun
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Blazing Sun
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Lust for Evil
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Talented Mr. Ripley
      </i>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PS_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PS-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a 1960 film directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Cl%C3%A9ment" title="Ren Clment">
       Ren Clment
      </a>
      , based on the 1955 novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Talented_Mr._Ripley" title="The Talented Mr. Ripley">
        The Talented Mr. Ripley
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Highsmith" title="Patricia Highsmith">
       Patricia Highsmith
      </a>
      . The film stars
      <a href="/wiki/Alain_Delon" title="Alain Delon">
       Alain Delon
      </a>
      in his first major film, along with then famous
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ronet" title="Maurice Ronet">
       Maurice Ronet
      </a>
      (as young Greenleaf) and
      <a href="/wiki/Marie_Lafor%C3%AAt" title="Marie Lafort">
       Marie Lafort
      </a>
      (as Marge);
      <a href="/wiki/Romy_Schneider" title="Romy Schneider">
       Romy Schneider
      </a>
      appears briefly in an uncredited role as Freddie Miles' companion, and Billy Kearns (an expatriate American actor well liked in France
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) plays Greenleaf's friend Freddy Miles. The film, principally in French, contains brief sequences in Italian.
     </p>
     <p>
      The film's source novel was
      <a href="/wiki/The_Talented_Mr._Ripley_(film)" title="The Talented Mr. Ripley (film)">
       adapted again in 1999
      </a>
      , under the original title, directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Minghella" title="Anthony Minghella">
       Anthony Minghella
      </a>
      , starring
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Damon" title="Matt Damon">
       Matt Damon
      </a>
      (as Ripley),
      <a href="/wiki/Jude_Law" title="Jude Law">
       Jude Law
      </a>
      (as Greenleaf), and
      <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Paltrow" title="Gwyneth Paltrow">
       Gwyneth Paltrow
      </a>
      (as Marge).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Noon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The American
      <a href="/wiki/Tom_Ripley" title="Tom Ripley">
       Tom Ripley
      </a>
      (Delon) has been sent to Italy to persuade his wealthy friend, Philippe Greenleaf (
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ronet" title="Maurice Ronet">
       Maurice Ronet
      </a>
      ), to return to the United States and take over his father's business. Philippe intends to do no such thing, however, and the impoverished Tom enjoys living a life of luxury, so the two men essentially spend money all day and carouse all night. Tom is fixated on Philippe and his girlfriend Marge (
      <a href="/wiki/Marie_Lafor%C3%AAt" title="Marie Lafort">
       Marie Lafort
      </a>
      ), and covets the other man's life. Philippe eventually grows bored with his friend's fawning and becomes cruel and abusive to him. The final straw is when, during a
      <a href="/wiki/Yachting" title="Yachting">
       yachting
      </a>
      trip, Philippe strands Tom in the
      <a href="/wiki/Dinghy" title="Dinghy">
       dinghy
      </a>
      and leaves him to lie in the sun for hours.
     </p>
     <p>
      Back on board, Tom hatches a plan to kill Philippe and
      <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">
       steal his identity
      </a>
      . First, he leaves evidence of Philippe's philandering for an outraged Marge to find. After Marge goes ashore, Philippe confronts Tom, who admits his plan quite casually. Philippe, believing it to be a joke, plays along and asks Tom for the plan's details. Suddenly frightened, Philippe offers Tom a substantial sum to leave him and Marge alone, but Tom states that he can obtain this sum anyway, and far more. At last pretending to accept his offer, Tom stabs Philippe to death as the latter screams Marge's name. He casts the body overboard and returns to port.
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon returning to shore, Tom informs Marge that Philippe has decided to stay behind. He then goes traveling around Italy using Philippe's name and bank account, even flawlessly mimicking his voice and mannerisms; in effect, Tom has
      <i>
       become
      </i>
      Philippe, even affixing his own photo, with notary seal, in Philippe's passport. He rents a large suite in a Rome hotel.
     </p>
     <p>
      When Philippe's suspicious friend, Freddie Miles (
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Kearns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Billy Kearns (page does not exist)">
       Billy Kearns
      </a>
      ), begins to suspect the truth while staying in the same hotel, Tom murders him as well. Freddie's body is soon found and the Italian police inevitably get involved. However, Tom continues playing his charade, switching between his own identity and Philippe's, depending on what the situation demands. After carrying out an elaborate scheme to implicate Philippe in Freddie's murder, Tom
      <a href="/wiki/Forgery" title="Forgery">
       forges
      </a>
      a
      <a href="/wiki/Suicide_note" title="Suicide note">
       suicide note
      </a>
      and a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Will_(law)" title="Will (law)">
       will
      </a>
      , leaving the Greenleaf fortune to Marge.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the aftermath, Tom has survived a long string of close shaves, thrown the Italian police off his trail, and seems to have outwitted everybody. He even succeeds in seducing Marge, with whom he begins openly cohabiting. However, when Philippe's yacht is being moved into dry dock, his decomposed body is found still attached to the boat because the anchor cable used to sink his corpse had become tangled around the propeller. The film ends with Tom being unknowingly called toward the police.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>

      Delon was cast after the director saw him in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Women_are_Weak" title="Women are Weak">
        Women are Weak
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-la_4-0">

        </span>

        <span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
       Reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Noon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      was lauded by critics and made Delon a star. In 1962, Clment and Paul Ggauff won an
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Award" title="Edgar Award">
       Edgar Award
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Mystery_Writers_of_America" title="Mystery Writers of America">
       Mystery Writers of America
      </a>
      for Best Foreign Film Screenplay. It enjoys a loyal
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">
       cult following
      </a>
      even today, with fans including film director
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">
       Martin Scorsese
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
       Roger Ebert
      </a>
      gave
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      three stars (compared to the four-star review he gave to
      <a href="/wiki/The_Talented_Mr._Ripley_(film)" title="The Talented Mr. Ripley (film)">
       the 1999 version
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Talented_Mr._Ripley" title="The Talented Mr. Ripley">
        The Talented Mr. Ripley
       </a>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), writing that "the best thing about the film is the way the plot devises a way for Ripley to create a perfect cover-up," but criticized the "less than satisfactory ending," feeling that "
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      ends as it does only because Clement doesn't have Highsmith's iron nerve."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">
       James Berardinelli
      </a>
      , however, rated
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      higher than
      <i>
       The Talented Mr. Ripley
      </i>
      , giving it a four-star review (compared to two and a half stars for
      <i>
       The Talented Mr. Ripley
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ). Berardinelli praised Delon's acting, saying that "Tom is fascinating because Delon makes him so," and also complimented the film for "expert camerawork and crisp direction."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Berardinelli placed
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      on his All-Time 100 list, and compared it to the 1999 film: "The remake went back to the source material, Patricia Highsmith's
      <i>
       The Talented Mr. Ripley
      </i>
      . The result, while arguably truer to the events of Highsmith's book, is vastly inferior. To say it suffers by comparison to
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      is an understatement. Almost every aspect of Rene Clement's 1960 motion picture is superior to that of
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Minghella" title="Anthony Minghella">
       Minghella
      </a>
      's 1999 version, from the cinematography to the acting to the screenplay.
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Damon" title="Matt Damon">
       Matt Damon
      </a>
      might make a credible Tom Ripley, but only for those who never experienced Alain Delon's portrayal."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Highsmith's opinion of the film was mixed: she felt that Alain Delon was "excellent" in the role of Tom Ripley,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peary_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-peary-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and described the film overall as "very beautiful to the eye and interesting for the intellect."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, she criticized the ending in which Ripley is implied to be caught by the police: "[I]t was a terrible concession to so-called public morality that the criminal had to be caught."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration_and_re-release">
       Restoration and re-release
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Noon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restoration and re-release">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2012
      <a href="/wiki/StudioCanal" title="StudioCanal">
       StudioCanal
      </a>
      funded a
      <a href="/wiki/Art_restoration" title="Art restoration">
       restoration
      </a>
      of the movie by the
      <i>
       Immagine Ritrovata
      </i>
      laboratory, a restoration being all the more warranted as part of the film's atmosphere is due to the sun-drenched scenes mentioned in the film's original title. The restored version was to be shown at the
      <a href="/wiki/2013_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2013 Cannes Film Festival">
       2013 Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      as part of an
      <a href="/wiki/Homage_(arts)" title="Homage (arts)">
       homage
      </a>
      to Delon's career, prior to re-release in France at least.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On December 4, 2012, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criterion_Collection" title="Criterion Collection">
       Criterion Collection
      </a>
      released the high-definition digital restoration of
      <i>
       Purple Noon
      </i>
      on Blu-ray and DVD. Special features include an interview with Ren Clment scholar and author Denitza Bantcheva, archival interviews with Alain Delon and Patricia Highsmith, the film's original English-language trailer, and a booklet featuring an essay by film critic
      <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_O%27Brien" title="Geoffrey O'Brien">
       Geoffrey O'Brien
      </a>
      and excerpts from a 1981 interview with Clment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
