        <div class="ad-gpt-main">

        </div>
        <p>
         There's an infinite amount of research out there screaming sad statistics when it comes to young girls and working women in the world of computer science. According to
         <a href="http://girlswhocode.com/">
          girlswhocode.com
         </a>
         , women represent a mere 12% of all CS graduates. The site also states that while 74% of middle school girls are interested in STEM (science, tech, engineering, math), 0.3% of them actually study it in college.
        </p>
        <p>
         The folks at Google stared at these statistics and did some serious research of their own. And today, in an effort to get young girls interested in coding and realize the potential uses it has in their lives (i.e. you don't just have to be a developer), they've launched
         <a href="https://www.madewithcode.com/">
          MadeWithCode.com
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-breaker-container">
         <div class="ad-gpt-breaker ad-article-breaker-mobile">
          <div class="ad-article-breaker-text">
           Advertisement - Continue Reading Below
          </div>
          <div class="gpt-tag" id="gpt-ad-vertical-top-mobile">
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         <em>
          Related:
          <a href="http://www.elle.com/life-love/society-career/most-influential-women-in-technology">
           <span style="color:#009ad3;">
            Meet ELLE's Women in Tech
           </span>
          </a>
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Instead of simply focusing on the dropping numbers of girls studying CS, Google sought to figure out why they aren't drawn to the subject in the first place. They came up with three main reasons.
        </p>
        <p>
         (1) Girls don't perceive CS as interesting. (2) They believe CS is nerdy and only for socially challenged people. (3) They assume they won't be good at it, or that it would be too difficult for them to understand.
        </p>

        <p>
         <i>
          Related:
          <a href="http://www.elle.com/news/culture/tech-lady-mafia-aminatou-sow">
           <span style="color:#009ad3;">
            Meet the Woman Leading the Tech LadyMafia
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         So armed with the knowledge of what was keeping young women from studying CS, Google looked into how to make the subject more appealing. They discovered that offering encouragement and building women's confidence about their ability to master CS skills, as well as highlighting the different careers that could come from having those skills, were all key in solving the gender gap.
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         <div class="gpt-tag" id="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         MadeWithCode.com offers all of this in a seamless, easy-to-use, and fun (yes, it's really fun) way. The site houses various projects that teach users how to use Google's very own coding language, Blockly, to accomplish various tasks, like building a 3D printed bracelet, a 2D character with a "3D personality," and my personal favorite, creating your own animated GIF.
        </p>

        <p>
         One of the most unique parts of the site is the Mentors and Makers sections, which seeks to connect young girls with women who use CS in their creative careers (Mentors) or to pursue their dreams (Makers).
        </p>
        <p>
         Miral Kotb, founder of iLuminate, studied CS at Columbia and dance at Barnard. Kotb, one of MadeWithCode's Mentors, used her software development skills to form a truly unique dance show that combines music, art, and technology. You may have seen her group on
         <em>
          America's Got Talent&lt;?em&gt;.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         "Writing software is what drew me in the most and I still love to get in front of the computer," Kotb told me of her CS experience. "The reason I feel I was able to get into such a creative mindset while I was writing software was because [I saw] writing code like writing a poem. I was able to pick something that was more stagnant and combine it with something such as dance."
        </p>
        <p>
         This idea of combining CS with the unexpectedthe software engineer with a dance troop, the techy with a video game company (Robin Hunicke), the Harvard engineering grad that works at Pixar (Danielle Feinberg), is what's at the heart of MadeWithCode. It gives girls a real reason to get interested, and tangible, fun ways to learn.
        </p>
        <p>
         You won't leave the site knowing HTML or Java Script. You'll need to go to a more advanced CS class to learn to write actual coding language. But MadeWithCode plants the seed to get girls wanting to know more. And to make sure they have somewhere to go, the campaign has made a $50 million investment over the next three years in CS grants to continue to develop the Girls &amp; CS education.
        </p>

        <p>
         "I think that coding is part of a 21st century skill. Everyone should know a little bit," Megan Smith, VP at GoogleX said. "Just like everyone learns to write, everyone should learn a little bit of coding, a little bit of lab, a little bit of music, a little bit of art. These are languages of expression. That's why I think it's important for everyone to do it. My hope is that MadeWithCode opens up opportunities for young people to really be empowered to impact the world."
        </p>
        <p>
         When I was in middle school, I had to select a language. "Take French," my father begged. "It's trs chic," he said in a funny-sounding accent. So I did. I took it for six years. And while I can get around Paris (more or less), I'd say my skills are "pas bon," and have never particularly helped me career-wise.
        </p>
        <p>
         In a perfect world, coding would have been offered as an alternative to French, Spanish, or Latin. I'm pretty sure I'll be referencing the HTML I learned as a CS major at NYU more frequently than I'll be referencing my French grammar book in the next few years. Where is that thing anyway?
        </p>
