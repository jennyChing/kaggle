       <h1 class="entry-title">
        How to Refinish a Table
       </h1>
       <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/2013/10/how-to-refinish-a-table.html" rel="bookmark">
        <img align="left" alt="How to Refinish a Table" class="post-image" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Two-Toned-Table-How-to-refinish-a-table-150x150.jpg"/>
       </a>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2013-10-30T15:46:42+00:00">
         Wednesday, October 30, 2013
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
        <p>
         This poor table had been begging for a makeover for ages. It was a wedding present from my mom 21 years ago. Theres the saying, If walls could talk, well, better yet, would be, If THIS table could talk.It has survived 4 kids with all their glue sticks, glitter, and magic markers, graffiti messages written in crayon, countless spills and watermarks, a few accidental nail gun staples during DIY projects (ahem ), and a lifetime of family dinners, gatherings and memories made around it. You all know me by now, and if I can try to bring beauty back to something, I will! Im happy to show you today, my total labor of love,
         <strong>
          How to Refinish a Table
         </strong>
         !
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/How-to-Refinish-a-Table.jpg">
          <img alt="How to Refinish a Table" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5643" height="975" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/How-to-Refinish-a-Table.jpg" width="650"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Here is the sad table before. It is a sturdy table that seats 8 with a leaf. It is made of rosewood, a tropical wood. The stain on it though was very golden or orangey, much like my oak floors and kitchen cabinets. I have be drowning in a sea of orange. Yuck. I desired a more natural, brighter table, yet wanted to show the beauty of the rosewood grain. So I decided to stain the table top and chair seats and paint the table base, chair backs and legs in white.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Kitchen-Table-Before.jpg">
          <img alt="Kitchen Table Before" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5653" height="400" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Kitchen-Table-Before.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
         These are the items needed to refinish and paint a table:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <ul>
          <ul>
           <ul>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <a href="http://www.3mdiy.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               3M Safety Products
              </a>
              (Protective eye wear, protective ear wear, sanding respirator)
             </li>
             <li>
              Rotary Sander and sanding discs (60, 100, 150 grit)
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://www.3mdiy.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               3M Advanced Abrasives
              </a>
              (120 grit and an extra fine grit sanding sponge)
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://www.scotchblue.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               ScotchBlue Painters Tape
              </a>
             </li>
             <li>
              Stain (I used Minwaxs Weathered Oak)
             </li>
             <li>
              Spray Polycrylic
             </li>
             <li>
              Primer (I used Rustoleums 2x Extra Cover Primer in white)
             </li>
             <li>
              Paint (I used Sherwin Williams Emerald latex, semigloss, in extra white)
             </li>
             <li>
              Sponge brushes (multiple sizes)
             </li>
             <li>
              Flat tipped, 1/2 inch artist paint brush
             </li>
            </ul>
           </ul>
          </ul>
         </ul>
        </ul>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/3M-Tekk-Products.jpg">
          <img alt="3M Tekk Products" class="aligncenter" height="328" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/3M-Tekk-Products.jpg" width="628"/>
         </a>
         Get ready to start sanding! To be honest, this is the fun part! Take the table outside or into a garage. This process is very messy. Also make sure you have the proper, protective eye wear and ear wear.I really enjoyed using my new rotary sander to strip away all the old stain. It was far easier than I thought it would be. When sanding, make sure you sand in the linear direction of the wood grain.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Refinishing-a-Table.jpg">
          <img alt="Refinishing a Table" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5655" height="600" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Refinishing-a-Table.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Hubs wasnt convinced that my rotary sander would do the job, so he went and bought a big ol belt sander for himself. Men boys and their toys hmph I sanded my side with the rotary sander and he tackled the other side with the belt sander. Well, his side came out rough and scary! My side came out smooth and consistent. Guess what? He had to use MY SANDER to clean up his side. Hee, hee, hee. Big and powerful is not always better.
         <img alt="Rotary Sanders vs. Belt sanders" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5657" height="465" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Rotary-Sanders-vs.-Belt-sanders.jpg" width="600"/>
         Im giving him a hard time, but really, I couldnt complain. He was awesome at helping me sand all EIGHT of the chairs. I didnt think sanding down to the bare wood was necessary at all but he is rather Type-A when it comes to projects that I involve him in. Beggars cant be choosers so if he wants to sand let the man sand. 
         <br/>
         <img alt="Putting my man to work" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5649" height="900" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Putting-my-man-to-work.jpg" width="600"/>
         After everything was sanded well, (thanks to hubs), we made sure to wipe off every bit of dust. First, go over it all with a dry cloth, then over it again with a damp cloth and allow to dry. Now we were ready to stain. My favorite stain color is Weathered Oak by Minwax. Ive used it on my
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/2012/07/pallet-art-shark.html">
          Pallet Shark Art
         </a>
         and my
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/2012/05/wine-crate-storage.html">
          Wine Crate Storage Box
         </a>
         .It has a beautiful driftwood quality look about it.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/weathered-oak-stain.jpg">
          <img alt="weathered oak stain" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5661" height="400" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/weathered-oak-stain.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
         I like to use inexpensive and disposable foam brushes for this project. I applied the stain, in the direction of the grain, and allowed it to sit for about 20 minutes. Then I wiped it off with a paper towel. The depth of color was not what I wanted so I applied a second, thin coat of stain and allowed that to sit for a few hours until dry. I wiped off any residue and reveled in the gorgeous color and grain! I was exactly what I had envisioned.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Weathered-Oak-Stains.jpg">
          <img alt="Weathered Oak Stains" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5663" height="400" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Weathered-Oak-Stains.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
         After staining the table top and all the chair bases, I gave the stained areas, several coats of spray polycrylic and used my extra fine grit sanding sponge between each coat. Always follow the directions on the back of the manufacturers can.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/applying-polycrylic.jpg">
          <img alt="applying polycrylic" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5673" height="400" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/applying-polycrylic.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Sanding.jpg">
          <img alt="Sanding" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5675" height="400" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Sanding.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
         Here is where the this project starts to become a time sucker and labor intensive. I wont lie to you, it took longer than I thought it would. You need to PRIME! DO NOT IGNORE THIS STEP! I did and I paid for it in time and labor. I am such an impatient person and tried to cheat by not priming my wood, but the existing stain bled through the paint. after I had already painted several chairs grrrrr. I had to essentially start over.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          LEARN FROM MY MISTAKES! PRIME PEOPLE PRIME!
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         You can paint on the primer or use a spray primer. If you use a spray primer then you MUST make sure you cover and protect the areas that you have stained. Then its time to paint. Here is another lesson I learned. If you dont like brush strokes then use a sponge brush for your painting. Buy several sponge brushes and in different widths. They dont seem to last very long. Use a high quality paint. I love Sherwin Williams and I already had a gallon of their Emerald line in my garage. So I chose to use that. For those small areas around the spokes of the chair back, where they meet the stained seat, use a nice, flat tipped artists brush to apply the paint.
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Driftwood-stained-table.jpg">
          <img alt="Driftwood stained table" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5665" height="900" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Driftwood-stained-table.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Two-Toned-Furniture.jpg">
          <img alt="Two Toned Furniture" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5651" height="825" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Two-Toned-Furniture.jpg" title="Two Toned Painted Furniture" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Overall, Ive so happy with the results. The table is brighter and lighter, full of rich texture and interesting grain. It blends in beautifully with my coastal decor. Refinishing a table is certainly not my area of expertise, but it is a worthwhile investment of your time and effort if your table is of good quality.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Two-Toned-Table-How-to-refinish-a-table.jpg">
          <img alt="Two Toned Table - How to refinish a table" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5671" height="923" src="http://sandandsisal.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Two-Toned-Table-How-to-refinish-a-table.jpg" width="600"/>
         </a>
         In this era of such materialism and waste, Im hoping that some of my DIY projects are helping to teach my kids how to preserve, reuse, yet renew the items they already have, so that they can be enjoyed by generations to come.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now to tackle those orangey kitchen cabinets! (Hubs just rolled his eyes haha!)
        </p>
        <p>
         Happy DIY-ing my friends!

           <div class="comment-content">
            <p>
             Hi Sarah, sorry for the delay in responding. Somehow I missed this comment. I should have clairified about the priming. I started the project with a different type of white paint (a chalk paint) but the existing stain and wood tannins kept bleeding through the white chalk paint. I later found out that with some types of wood, that can sometimes happen with chalk paint. I primed over the chalk paint and then painted with 2 coats of the SW white latex. (I could have used the chalk paint over the primer, but I was getting frustrated and was running out of time.)  I only put poly on the table top and chair legs/backs.
            </p>
            <p>
             Now with your chairs. if the existing paint is already able to scratch off with your nail, then that is not a good sign. It means all of the paint needs to be removed. I recommend using a paint stripper like Citristrip first.
            </p>
            <p>
             Hope that helps! Thanks! ~KIM

            <p>
             Hi Nina!
             <br/>
             I started off using chalk paint in Pure White but the tannins from the wood bled through within a day. No matter how many coats of chalk paint I used, I couldnt mask it. I then read that you need to really research the type of wood you have and if they have a tendencies to bleed tannins. If I had primed 1st, then that would have prevented any bleed through. I actually ended up switching to latex and did prime beforehand. And to answer your question about sanding the seats first or painting first. I recommend doing all your sanding first, then stain the seats, then prime and paint last (carefully masking off the stained areas with painters tape). Hope that helps! Best of luck to you! 
            </p>

           Hi Stephanie! Im sorry you are having issues. Stain might look uneven depending on a few things:
           <br/>
           1) how you apply it
           <br/>
           2) not applying it quickly enough or in an even application
           <br/>
           3) not enough of the original stain or sealer was removed during sanding
           <br/>
           4) different types of wood will naturally absorb stain in different ways.
          </p>
          <p>
           Im not sure sanding will help even out your coats. You would need to completely sand it down to the bare wood again and start over. Do you know what type of wood you have? And do you suspect it is the wood type that is causing the different absorption issues or DIY error?  Feel free to send me a pic so I can see exactly what the issue is.
