     <p>
      <b>
       Huddie William Ledbetter
      </b>
      (January 20, 1888 December 6, 1949) was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">
       folk
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">
       blues
      </a>
      musician notable for his strong vocals, virtuosity on the
      <a href="/wiki/Twelve-string_guitar" title="Twelve-string guitar">
       twelve-string guitar
      </a>
      , and the songbook of folk standards he introduced.
     </p>
     <p>
      He is best known as
      <b>
       Lead Belly
      </b>
      . Though many releases list him as "
      <b>
       Leadbelly
      </b>
      ", he himself wrote it as "Lead Belly". This is also the spelling on his tombstone,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grave_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-grave-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as of the Lead Belly Foundation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although Lead Belly usually played the twelve-string guitar, he could also play the
      <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">
       piano
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">
       mandolin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">
       harmonica
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">
       violin
      </a>
      , and "windjammer" (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatonic_accordion" title="Diatonic accordion">
       diatonic accordion
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some of his recordings he sings while clapping his hands or stomping his foot.
     </p>
     <p>
      The topics of Lead Belly's music covered a wide range, including
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">
       gospel
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">
       blues
      </a>
      about women, liquor, prison life, and racism; and folk songs about cowboys, prison, work, sailors, cattle herding, and dancing. He also wrote songs about people in the news, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">
       Franklin D. Roosevelt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">
       Adolf Hitler
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Harlow" title="Jean Harlow">
       Jean Harlow
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Scottsboro_Boys" title="Scottsboro Boys">
       Scottsboro Boys
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes" title="Howard Hughes">
       Howard Hughes
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Lead Belly was inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">
       Rock and Roll Hall of Fame
      </a>
      in 1988 and the
      <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Music_Hall_of_Fame" title="Louisiana Music Hall of Fame">
       Louisiana Music Hall of Fame
      </a>
      in 2008.
     </p>

     <p>
      Lead Belly was born Huddie William Ledbetter on the Jeter Plantation near
      <a href="/wiki/Mooringsport,_Louisiana" title="Mooringsport, Louisiana">
       Mooringsport, Louisiana
      </a>
      in either January 1888 or 1889. The
      <a href="/wiki/1900_United_States_Census" title="1900 United States Census">
       1900 United States Census
      </a>
      lists "Hudy William Ledbetter" as 12 years old, born January 1888; and the
      <a href="/wiki/1910_United_States_Census" title="1910 United States Census">
       1910
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1930_United_States_Census" title="1930 United States Census">
       1930
      </a>
      censuses also give his birth year as 1888. However, in April 1942, Ledbetter filled out his
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      draft registration with a birth date of January 23, 1889 and a birthplace of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport,_Louisiana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Freeport, Louisiana (page does not exist)">
       Freeport, Louisiana
      </a>
      . His grave marker has the date on his draft registration.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ledbetter was the younger of two children born to Wesley Ledbetter and Sallie Brown, preceded by a sister named Australia. The pronunciation of his name is purported to be "HYEW-dee" or "HUGH-dee."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ledbetter can be heard pronouncing his name as "HYEW-dee" on the track "Boll Weevil," from the
      <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Folkways" title="Smithsonian Folkways">
       Smithsonian Folkways
      </a>
      album
      <i>
       Lead Belly Sings for Children.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His parents had
      <a href="/wiki/Cohabitation" title="Cohabitation">
       cohabited
      </a>
      for several years, but they legally married on February 26, 1888. When Huddie was five years old, the family settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Bowie_County,_Texas" title="Bowie County, Texas">
       Bowie County, Texas
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      By 1903, Huddie was already a "musicianer,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a singer and guitarist of some note. He performed for nearby
      <a href="/wiki/Shreveport,_Louisiana" title="Shreveport, Louisiana">
       Shreveport
      </a>
      audiences in St. Paul's Bottoms, a notorious
      <a href="/wiki/Red-light_district" title="Red-light district">
       red-light district
      </a>
      there. He began to develop his own style of music after exposure to a variety of musical influences on Shreveport's Fannin Street, a row of saloons, brothels, and dance halls in the Bottoms, now referred to as Ledbetter Heights.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 1910 census of
      <a href="/wiki/Harrison_County,_Texas" title="Harrison County, Texas">
       Harrison County, Texas
      </a>
      , shows "Hudy" Ledbetter living next door to his parents with his first wife, Aletha "Lethe" Henderson. Aletha, then seventeen, had been 15 when they married two years earlier. It was in Texas that Ledbetter received his first instrument, an
      <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">
       accordion
      </a>
      , from his uncle Terrell. By his early 20s, having fathered at least two children, Ledbetter left home to make his living as a guitarist and occasional laborer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Influenced by the sinking of the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic" title="Titanic">
        Titanic
       </a>
      </i>
      in April 1912, Huddie wrote the song "The Titanic",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the first composed on the 12-string guitar later to become his signature instrument. Initially played when performing with
      <a href="/wiki/Blind_Lemon_Jefferson" title="Blind Lemon Jefferson">
       Blind Lemon Jefferson
      </a>
      (18971929) in and around
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas">
       Dallas, Texas
      </a>
      , the song is about champion African-American boxer
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Johnson_(boxer)" title="Jack Johnson (boxer)">
       Jack Johnson
      </a>
      's being denied passage on the
      <i>
       Titanic
      </i>
      . While Johnson had in fact
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Johnson_(boxer)#Popular_culture" title="Jack Johnson (boxer)">
       been denied passage on a ship
      </a>
      for being
      <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">
       Black
      </a>
      , it had not been the
      <i>
       Titanic
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Still, the verse sang: "Jack Johnson tried to get on board. The Captain, he says, 'I ain't haulin' no coal!' Fare thee,
      <i>
       Titanic
      </i>
      ! Fare thee well!" Ledbetter later noted he had to leave out this passage when playing in front of
      <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">
       white
      </a>
      audiences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prison_years">
       Prison years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_Belly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prison years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AngolaLAPrison.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/AngolaLAPrison.jpg/220px-AngolaLAPrison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/AngolaLAPrison.jpg/330px-AngolaLAPrison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/AngolaLAPrison.jpg/440px-AngolaLAPrison.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AngolaLAPrison.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_Penitentiary" title="Louisiana State Penitentiary">
         Louisiana State Penitentiary
        </a>
        , where Lead Belly served time
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Ledbetter's volatile temper sometimes led him into trouble with the law. In 1915, he was convicted of carrying a pistol and sentenced to time on the Harrison County
      <a href="/wiki/Chain_gang" title="Chain gang">
       chain gang
      </a>
      . He escaped, finding work in nearby
      <a href="/wiki/Bowie_County,_Texas" title="Bowie County, Texas">
       Bowie County
      </a>
      under the assumed name of Walter Boyd. In January 1918 he was imprisoned at the Imperial Farm (now
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Unit" title="Central Unit">
       Central Unit
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perkinson184_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perkinson184-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Land,_Texas" title="Sugar Land, Texas">
       Sugar Land, Texas
      </a>
      , after killing one of his own relatives, Will Stafford, in a fight over a woman. While there he may have first heard the traditional prison song
      <a href="/wiki/Midnight_Special_(song)" title="Midnight Special (song)">
       "Midnight Special"
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2010)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In 1925 he was pardoned and released after writing a song to Governor
      <a href="/wiki/Pat_Morris_Neff" title="Pat Morris Neff">
       Pat Morris Neff
      </a>
      seeking his freedom, having served the minimum seven years of a 7-to-35-year sentence. Combined with his good behavior (which included entertaining the guards and fellow prisoners), his appeal to Neff's strong religious beliefs proved sufficient. It was a testament to his persuasive powers, as Neff had run for governor on a pledge not to issue pardons (the only recourse for prisoners, since in most Southern prisons there was no provision for
      <a href="/wiki/Parole" title="Parole">
       parole
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      According to Charles K. Wolfe and Kip Lornell's book,
      <i>
       The Life and Legend of Leadbelly
      </i>
      (1999), Neff had regularly brought guests to the prison on Sunday picnics to hear Ledbetter perform.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1930 Ledbetter was sentenced to
      <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">
       Louisiana
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_Penitentiary" title="Louisiana State Penitentiary">
       Angola Prison Farm
      </a>
      , after a summary trial for attempted homicide for stabbing a white man in a fight. He was "discovered" there three years later during a visit by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folklorist" title="Folklorist">
       folklorists
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/John_Lomax" title="John Lomax">
       John Lomax
      </a>
      and his son
      <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lomax" title="Alan Lomax">
       Alan Lomax
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc18_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pc18-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Deeply impressed by Ledbetter's vibrant tenor and extensive repertoire, the Lomaxes recorded him in 1933 on portable
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminum_disc" title="Aluminum disc">
       aluminum disc
      </a>
      recording equipment for the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      . They returned with new and better equipment in July 1934, recording hundreds of his songs. On August 1, Ledbetter was released after having again served nearly all of his minimum sentence, following a petition the Lomaxes had taken to Louisiana Governor
      <a href="/wiki/Oscar_K._Allen" title="Oscar K. Allen">
       Oscar K. Allen
      </a>
      at his urgent request. It was on the other side of a recording of his signature song, "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goodnight_Irene" title="Goodnight Irene">
       Goodnight Irene
      </a>
      ."
     </p>
     <p>
      A prison official later wrote John Lomax denying that Ledbetter's singing had anything to do with his release from Angola (state prison records confirm he was eligible for early release due to good behavior). However, both Ledbetter and the Lomaxes believed that the record they had taken to the governor had hastened his release from prison.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Moniker">
       Moniker
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_Belly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Moniker">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      There are several conflicting stories about how Ledbetter acquired the nickname "Lead Belly", though it was probably while in prison. Some claim his fellow inmates called him "Lead Belly" as a play on his family name and his physical toughness. It is recounted that during his second prison term, another inmate stabbed him in the neck (leaving him with a fearsome scar he subsequently covered with a bandana); Ledbetter nearly killed his attacker with his own knife.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mudcat_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mudcat-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Others say he earned the name after being wounded in the stomach with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buckshot" title="Buckshot">
       buckshot
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mudcat_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mudcat-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another theory is that the name refers to his ability to drink
      <a href="/wiki/Moonshine" title="Moonshine">
       moonshine
      </a>
      , the home-made liquor which Southern farmers, black and white, made to supplement their incomes. Blues singer
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Bill_Broonzy" title="Big Bill Broonzy">
       Big Bill Broonzy
      </a>
      thought it came from a supposed tendency to lay about as if "with a stomach weighted down by lead" in the shade when the chain gang was supposed to be working.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Or it may be simply a corruption of his last name pronounced with a southern accent. Whatever its origin, he adopted the nickname as a pseudonym while performing.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Huddie_Ledbetter_(Leadbelly)_and_Martha_Promise_Ledbetter,_Wilton,_Conn..jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Huddie_Ledbetter_(Leadbelly)_and_Martha_Promise_Ledbetter,_Wilton,_Conn..jpg" title="Enlarge">

        Huddie Ledbetter (Lead Belly) and Martha Promise Ledbetter,
        <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Connecticut" title="Wilton, Connecticut">

        </a>
        , February 1935.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_prison">
       Life after prison
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_Belly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Life after prison">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      By the time Lead Belly was released from prison the United States was deep in the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
      and jobs were very scarce. In September 1934, in need of regular work in order to avoid having his release canceled, Lead Belly met with John A. Lomax and asked him to take him on as a driver. For three months he assisted the 67-year-old in his folk song collecting abroad the South. (Son Alan was ill and did not accompany his father on this trip.)
     </p>
     <p>
      In December Lead Belly participated in a "smoker" (group sing) at a
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_Language_Association" title="Modern Language Association">
       Modern Language Association
      </a>
      meeting at
      <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Mawr_College" title="Bryn Mawr College">
       Bryn Mawr College
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">
       Pennsylvania
      </a>
      , where the senior Lomax had a prior lecturing engagement. He was written up in the press as a convict who had sung his way out of prison. On New Year's Day, 1935, the pair arrived in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      , where Lomax was scheduled to meet with his publisher,
      <a href="/wiki/Macmillan_Publishers_(United_States)" title="Macmillan Publishers (United States)">
       Macmillan
      </a>
      , about a new collection of folk songs. The newspapers were eager to write about the "singing convict" and
      <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
       <i>
        Time
       </i>
      </a>
      magazine made one of its first filmed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/March_of_Time" title="March of Time">
       March of Time
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Newsreel" title="Newsreel">
       newsreels
      </a>
      about him. Lead Belly attained fame (although not fortune).
     </p>
     <p>
      The following week, he began recording for the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Record_Corporation" title="American Record Corporation">
       American Record Corporation
      </a>
      , but these recordings achieved little commercial success. Of the over 40 sides he recorded for ARC (intended to be released on their Banner, Melotone, Oriole, Perfect, and Romeo labels, and their very short-lived Paramount series), only five sides were actually issued. Part of the reason for the poor sales may have been because ARC insisted on releasing only his
      <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">
       blues
      </a>
      songs rather than the folk songs for which he would later become better known. In any case, Lead Belly continued to struggle financially. Like many performers, what income he made during his lifetime would come from touring, not from record sales.
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 1935, he married his girlfriend, Martha Promise, who came north from Louisiana to join him.
     </p>
     <p>
      The month of February was spent recording his and other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">
       African-American
      </a>
      repertoire and interviews about his life with Alan Lomax for their forthcoming book,
      <i>
       Negro Folk Songs as Sung by Lead Belly
      </i>
      (1936). Concert appearances were however slow to materialize. In March 1935 Lead Belly accompanied John Lomax on a previously scheduled two-week lecture tour of colleges and universities in the Northeast, culminating at
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
       Harvard
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1415" data-file-width="1093" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg/220px-Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg/330px-Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg/440px-Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leadbelly_with_Accordeon.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Lead Belly playing an
        <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">
         accordion
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      At the end of the month John Lomax decided he could no longer work with Lead Belly and gave him and Martha money to go back to Louisiana by bus. He gave Martha the money her husband had earned during three months of performing, but in installments, on the pretext Lead Belly would drink it all if given a lump sum. From Louisiana Lead Belly successfully sued Lomax both for the full amount and release from his management contract. The quarrel was very bitter with hard feelings on both sides. Curiously, in the midst of the legal wrangling, Lead Belly wrote to Lomax proposing they team up again. It was not to be. Further, the book about Lead Belly published by the Lomaxes in the fall of the following year proved a commercial failure.
     </p>
     <p>
      In January 1936, Lead Belly returned to New York on his own without John Lomax in an attempted comeback. He performed twice a day at Harlem's
      <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Theater" title="Apollo Theater">
       Apollo Theater
      </a>
      during the Easter season in a live dramatic recreation of the
      <i>
       Time Life
      </i>
      newsreel (itself a recreation) about his prison encounter with John A. Lomax, where he had worn stripes, though by this time he was no longer associated with Lomax.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)">
       <i>
        Life
       </i>
      </a>
      magazine ran a three-page article titled, "Lead Belly - Bad Nigger Makes Good Minstrel," in its April 19, 1937 issue. It included a full-page, color (rare in those days) picture of him sitting on grain sacks playing his guitar and singing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, included was a striking picture of Martha Promise (identified in the article as his manager); photos showing Lead Belly's hands playing the guitar (with the caption "these hands once killed a man"); Texas Governor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pat_M._Neff" title="Pat M. Neff">
       Pat M. Neff
      </a>
      ; and the "ramshackle" Texas State Penitentiary. The article attributes both of his pardons to his singing of his petitions to the governors, who were so moved that they pardoned him. The article's text ends with "he... may well be on the brink of a new and prosperous period."
     </p>
     <p>
      Lead Belly failed to stir the enthusiasm of
      <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">
       Harlem
      </a>
      audiences. Instead, he attained success playing at concerts and benefits for an audience of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_left" title="Political left">
       leftist
      </a>
      folk music
      <i>
       aficionados.
      </i>
      He developed his own style of singing and explaining his repertoire in the context of Southern black culture, taking the hint from his previous participation in John A. Lomax's college lectures. He was especially successful with his repertoire of children's game songs (as a younger man in Louisiana he had sung regularly at children's birthday parties in the black community). He was written up as a heroic figure by the black novelist,
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wright_(author)" title="Richard Wright (author)">
       Richard Wright
      </a>
      , then a member of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party" title="Communist Party">
       Communist Party
      </a>
      , in the columns of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Daily_Worker" title="Daily Worker">
        Daily Worker
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      of which Wright was the Harlem editor. The two men became personal friends, though some say Lead Belly himself was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apolitical" title="Apolitical">
       apolitical
      </a>
      and if anything he was a supporter of
      <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Willkie" title="Wendell Willkie">
       Wendell Willkie
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrist" title="Centrist">
       centrist
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Republican_Party" title="U.S. Republican Party">
       Republican
      </a>
      candidate, for whom he wrote a campaign song. However, he also wrote the song Bourgeois Blues, which has radical or left wing lyrics.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1939, Lead Belly was back in jail for
      <a href="/wiki/Assault" title="Assault">
       assault
      </a>
      , after stabbing a man in a fight in
      <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">
       Manhattan
      </a>
      . Alan Lomax, then 24, took him under his wing and helped raise money for his legal expenses, dropping out of graduate school to do so. After his release (in 1940-41), Lead Belly appeared as a regular on Alan Lomax and
      <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Ray" title="Nicholas Ray">
       Nicholas Ray
      </a>
      's groundbreaking
      <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">
       CBS
      </a>
      radio show,
      <i>
       Back Where I Come From,
      </i>
      broadcast nationwide. He also appeared in night clubs with
      <a href="/wiki/Josh_White" title="Josh White">
       Josh White
      </a>
      , becoming a fixture in New York City's surging folk music scene and befriending the likes of
      <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Terry" title="Sonny Terry">
       Sonny Terry
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brownie_McGhee" title="Brownie McGhee">
       Brownie McGhee
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Woody_Guthrie" title="Woody Guthrie">
       Woody Guthrie
      </a>
      , and a young
      <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">
       Pete Seeger
      </a>
      , all fellow performers on
      <i>
       Back Where I Come From.
      </i>
      During the first half of the decade he recorded for
      <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">
       RCA
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      , and for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moe_Asch" title="Moe Asch">
       Moe Asch
      </a>
      (future founder of
      <a href="/wiki/Folkways_Records" title="Folkways Records">
       Folkways Records
      </a>
      ), and in 1944 headed to
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      , where he recorded strong sessions for
      <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">
       Capitol Records
      </a>
      .He lodged with a studio guitar player on Merrywood Drive in Laurel Canyon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Lead Belly was the first American country blues musician to see success in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mudcat_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-mudcat-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1949, Lead Belly had a regular radio broadcast on station WNYC in New York on Sunday nights on
      <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Yurchenco" title="Henrietta Yurchenco">
       Henrietta Yurchenco
      </a>
      's show. Later in the year he began his first
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       European
      </a>
      tour with a trip to
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      , but fell ill before its completion, and was diagnosed with
      <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">
       amyotrophic lateral sclerosis
      </a>
      (ALS), or
      <a href="/wiki/Lou_Gehrig" title="Lou Gehrig">
       Lou Gehrig
      </a>
      's disease.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc18_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pc18-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His final concert was at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">
       University of Texas at Austin
      </a>
      in a tribute to his former mentor, John A. Lomax, who had died the previous year. Martha also performed at that concert, singing spirituals with her husband.
     </p>
     <p>
      Lead Belly died later that year in New York City, and was buried in the Shiloh Baptist Church cemetery in Mooringsport, 8 miles (13km) west of
      <a href="/wiki/Blanchard,_Louisiana" title="Blanchard, Louisiana">
       Blanchard
      </a>
      , in Caddo Parish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grave_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-grave-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is honored with a statue across from the Caddo Parish Courthouse in Shreveport.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technique">
       Technique
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_Belly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technique">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Lead Belly styled himself "King of the 12-string guitar," and despite his use of other instruments like the accordion, the most enduring image of Lead Belly as a performer is wielding his unusually large Stella twelve-string.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This guitar had a slightly longer scale length than a standard guitar, slotted tuners, ladder bracing, and a trapeze-style tailpiece to resist bridge lifting.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Lead Belly played with finger picks much of the time, using a thumb pick to provide a walking bass line and occasionally to strum.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This technique, combined with low tunings and heavy strings, gives many of his recordings a piano-like sound. Lead Belly's tuning is debated,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2012)">
          by whom?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      but appears to be a downtuned variant of standard tuning; more than likely he tuned his guitar strings relative to one another, so that the actual notes shifted as the strings wore. Lead Belly's playing style was popularized by
      <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">
       Pete Seeger
      </a>
      , who adopted the twelve-string guitar in the 1950s and released an instructional LP and book using Lead Belly as an exemplar of technique.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some of the recordings where Lead Belly accompanied himself, he would make an unusual type of grunt between his verses, best described as "Haah!" in many of his songs such as "Looky Looky Yonder," "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Take_this_Hammer" title="Take this Hammer">
       Take this Hammer
      </a>
      ,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc18_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-pc18-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Linin' Track" and "Julie Ann Johnson" feature this unusual vocalization. In the song, "Take this Hammer," Lead Belly explained, "Every time the men say 'haah,' the hammer falls. The hammer rings, and we swing, and we sing."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The "haah" sound can be heard in the work chants sung by Southern railroad section workers, "
      <a href="/wiki/Gandy_dancer" title="Gandy dancer">
       gandy dancers
      </a>
      ," where it was used to coordinate the crews as they laid and maintained the tracks before modern machinery was available.
     </p>
