           <p>
            A rare event occurred this past weekend when two
            <a href="http://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/IOTD/view.php?id=84145&amp;eocn=image&amp;eoci=moreiotd" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/IOTD/view.php?id=84145&amp;eocn=image&amp;eoci=moreiotd', 'tropical systems');" target="_blank" title="NASA Earth Observatory">
             tropical systems
            </a>
            approached the state of Hawaii. Hurricanes happen only occasionally in this part of the world because a fairly constant high pressure system deflects most of the storms. Also, the waters around Hawaii are typically cooler than tropical systems need in order to maintain their strength.
           </p>
           <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_344293" class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_344293" style="max-width: 730px">
            <span class="image-container size-full wp-image-344293" style="max-width:720px;max-height:480px;">
             <img alt="Hurricanes Iselle and Julio as seen by the Suomi NPP satellite. Photo: NASA" class="size-full wp-image-344293" height="480" src="//ecowatch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/h1.jpg" width="720"/>
            </span>
            <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_344293">
             Hurricanes Iselle and Julio as seen by the Suomi NPP satellite. Photo: NASA
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            Hurricane Iselle made landfall Thursday night over the Big Island as a strong tropical storm (sustained winds of 60 mph), while Hurricane Julio passed safely to the north of the islands. It has been 22 years since the last direct hit. Last time, it was
            <a href="http://www.wunderground.com/blog/Civicane49/remembering-hurricane-iniki" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.wunderground.com/blog/Civicane49/remembering-hurricane-iniki', 'Hurricane Iniki');" target="_blank" title="Remembering Hurricane Iniki">
             Hurricane Iniki
            </a>
            , which hit the island of Kauai as a Category 4 storm.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             The role of natural variability
            </strong>
           </p>
           <div class="unit" id="div-gpt-ad-1433512244421-0" style="height:90px; width:728px;margin-bottom:14px;">
            <script type="text/javascript">
             googletag.cmd.push(function() { googletag.display("div-gpt-ad-1433512244421-0"); });

           <p>
            In August 1992, Hurricane Andrew struck south Florida as a Category 5 hurricane. I was only a child then, but I convinced my family to drive away from our New Orleans home when Andrew turned towards Louisiana. A few weeks later I heard of another storm: Hurricane Iniki. It was a Category 4 storm and headed for the coast. I thought we might need to leave again until I saw where the storm was. It wasnt in the Atlantic. It was in the Central Pacific and would hit Hawaii.
           </p>
           <p>
            Iniki occurred during a fairly strong El Nio, which warmed the waters of the central Pacific and contributed to its formation. The same is the case this year. An El Nio had been forming (although it is currently shifting to
            <a href="http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/enso_advisory/ensodisc.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/enso_advisory/ensodisc.html', 'neutral conditions');" target="_blank" title="CPC ENSO Diagnostic Discussions">
             neutral conditions
            </a>
            ) and the waters near Hawaii are warmer as a result. The high pressure center that usually protects Hawaii is also further north.
           </p>
           <p>
            The role of El Nio on Hawaii hurricanes requires some
            <a href="http://www.cgd.ucar.edu/staff/trenbert/trenberth.papers/defnBAMS.pdf" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'download', 'http://www.cgd.ucar.edu/staff/trenbert/trenberth.papers/defnBAMS.pdf');" target="_blank" title="The Definition of El Nino">
             basic knowledge
            </a>
            about the location of sea surface temperature anomalies related to the phenomenon. The Nio 3 Region is bounded by 90W-150W and 5S- 5N. The Nio 3.4 Region is bounded by 120W-170W and 5S- 5N.
           </p>
           <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_344294" class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_344294" style="max-width: 610px">
            <span class="image-container size-full wp-image-344294" style="max-width:600px;max-height:385px;">
             <img alt="El Nino regions definitions. Image: NOAA" class="size-full wp-image-344294" height="385" src="//ecowatch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/h2.jpg" width="600"/>
            </span>
            <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_344294">
             El Nino regions definitions. Image: NOAA
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            <strong>
             The role of climate change
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            We know El Nio is one of the
            <a href="http://journals.ametsoc.org/doi/abs/10.1175/1520-0442%281997%29010%3C2683:TCOITV%3E2.0.CO%3B2" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://journals.ametsoc.org/doi/abs/10.1175/1520-0442%281997%29010%3C2683:TCOITV%3E2.0.CO%3B2', 'main drivers');" target="_blank" title="Tropical Cyclone Occurrences in the Vicinity of Hawaii">
             main drivers
            </a>
            for hurricane formation in the central Pacific (Nio Region 3.4), but the proximity of the two storms near Hawaii is highly anomalous. Did
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/climate-change-news/" target="_blank">
             climate change
            </a>
            have a role in the Hawaii hurricanes? According to experts
            <a href="http://thinkprogress.org/climate/2014/08/06/3468196/hawaii-hurricanes-iselle-julio-climate-change/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://thinkprogress.org/climate/2014/08/06/3468196/hawaii-hurricanes-iselle-julio-climate-change/', 'the answer is no');" target="_blank" title="Two Hurricanes Are Coming For Hawaii At Once">
             the answer is no
            </a>
            . This was a natural, albeit rare phenomenon. However, new research does suggest that more hurricanes will occur in the
            <a href="http://www.wunderground.com/blog/JeffMasters/comment.html?entrynum=2749" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.wunderground.com/blog/JeffMasters/comment.html?entrynum=2749', 'vicinity of Hawaii');" target="_blank" title="Climate Change May Increase the Number of Hawaiian Hurricanes">
             vicinity of Hawaii
            </a>
            , including a recent study that shows a
            <a href="http://www.nature.com/nclimate/journal/v3/n8/full/nclimate1890.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.nature.com/nclimate/journal/v3/n8/full/nclimate1890.html', '3-4 fold increase in storms');" target="_blank" title="Projected increase in tropical cyclones near Hawaii">
             3-4 fold increase in storms
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            There is another way that climate change might be influencing weather systems in the Pacific. Usually, during an El Nio, the west Pacific (Nio 4) cools as warm waters move to the east Pacific (Nio 3, Nio 1+2). This year this has not been the case. The rains have not let up over the islands of the west Pacific and waters have
            <a href="http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/lanina/enso_evolution-status-fcsts-web.pdf" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'download', 'http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/analysis_monitoring/lanina/enso_evolution-status-fcsts-web.pdf');" target="_blank" title="ENSO: Recent Evolution, Current Status and Predictions">
             continued to be warm
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            In fact, the ocean heat content of the west Pacific has
            <a href="http://www.google.com/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=&amp;esrc=s&amp;source=web&amp;cd=4&amp;ved=0CDsQFjAD&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov%2Fproducts%2FGODAS%2Focean_briefing_gif%2Fglobal_ocean_monitoring_current.ppt&amp;ei=bfzoU9WaEcfMsQSqmILYCg&amp;usg=AFQjCNF5oa7aT_UCZP25Ll_RvN65crWxKw&amp;sig2=Tb9hG6uor2_andEXE_ZunA&amp;bvm=bv.72676100,d.cWc&amp;cad=rja" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.google.com/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=&amp;esrc=s&amp;source=web&amp;cd=4&amp;ved=0CDsQFjAD&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov%2Fproducts%2FGODAS%2Focean_briefing_gif%2Fglobal_ocean_monitoring_current.ppt&amp;ei=bfzoU9WaEcfMsQSqmILYCg&amp;usg=AFQjCNF5oa7aT_UCZP25Ll_RvN65crWxKw&amp;sig2=Tb9hG6uor2_andEXE_ZunA&amp;bvm=bv.72676100,d.cWc&amp;cad=rja', 'remained high');" target="_blank" title="Global Ocean Monitoring: Recent Evolution, Current Status, and Predictions">
             remained high
            </a>
            , hinting at a deeper pool of warm waters that has been continuously building due to strong easterly winds. These easterly winds are the same winds that have kept the tropical east Pacific
            <a href="http://www.nature.com/nclimate/journal/v4/n3/full/nclimate2106.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.nature.com/nclimate/journal/v4/n3/full/nclimate2106.html', 'cool ');" target="_blank" title="Recent intensification of wind-driven circulation in the Pacific and the ongoing warming hiatus">
             cool
            </a>
            and have contributed to a temporary speed bump in the rise of global surface temperatures.
           </p>
           <p>
            New research suggests that the anomalous easterly winds, likely the reason for a cool east Pacific, might be due to the warming of the
            <a href="http://www.nature.com/nclimate/journal/vaop/ncurrent/full/nclimate2330.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.nature.com/nclimate/journal/vaop/ncurrent/full/nclimate2330.html', 'Atlantic Ocean');" target="_blank" title="Recent Walker circulation strengthening and Pacific cooling amplified by Atlantic warming">
             Atlantic Ocean
            </a>
            . The faster warming of the Atlantic causes a difference in pressure with the Pacific, increasing the easterly winds. This is just one theory among many that describes the reason why the tropical east Pacific has been
            <a href="http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v501/n7467/full/nature12534.html" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v501/n7467/full/nature12534.html', 'continuously cool');" target="_blank" title="Recent global-warming hiatus tied to equatorial Pacific surface cooling">
             continuously cool
            </a>
            even though the rest of the planet has warmed.
           </p>
           <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_344295" class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_344295" style="max-width: 610px">
            <span class="image-container size-full wp-image-344295" style="max-width:600px;max-height:403px;">
             <img alt="Image: NOAA/NESDIS/NODC" class="size-full wp-image-344295" height="403" src="//ecowatch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/h3.jpg" width="600"/>
            </span>
            <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_344295">
             Image: NOAA/NESDIS/NODC
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            The research behind the cooling of the Pacific and the associated slowdown of the rise in global temperatures is an active area of
            <a href="http://blog.ucsusa.org/ocean-cooling-effect-global-warming-459" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://blog.ucsusa.org/ocean-cooling-effect-global-warming-459', 'climate science');" target="_blank" title="The Oceans Cooling Effect: An Air Conditioner for the Planet">
             climate science
            </a>
            . However, the unabated rise in
            <a href="http://www.climate.gov/news-features/understanding-climate/2013-state-climate-ocean-heat-content" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.climate.gov/news-features/understanding-climate/2013-state-climate-ocean-heat-content', 'ocean heat content');" target="_blank" title="2013 State of the Climate: Ocean heat content">
             ocean heat content
            </a>
            has shown us where global warming has gone: the oceans. Recent observations, such as the continuously warm west Pacific, are further proof how much heat can be trapped in the waters of our planet. And as the east Pacific warms again in the near future, global surface temperatures will likely see a sharp rise as well.
           </p>
