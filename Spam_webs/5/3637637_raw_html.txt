     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Eucalyptus regnans
       </b>
      </i>
      , known variously as
      <b>
       mountain ash
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       swamp gum
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       stringy gum
      </b>
      , is a species of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">
        Eucalyptus
       </a>
      </i>
      native to
      <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">
       Tasmania
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">
       Victoria
      </a>
      in southeastern
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      and the tallest
      <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">
       flowering plant
      </a>
      and one of the tallest trees in the world, second to the coast redwood (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">
        Sequoia sempervirens
       </a>
      </i>
      ). A straight-trunked tree with smooth grey bark and a stocking of rough brown bark to 520 metres (1666ft) above the ground, it regularly grows to 85 metres (279ft), with the tallest living specimen, the
      <a href="/wiki/Centurion_(tree)" title="Centurion (tree)">
       Centurion
      </a>
      , standing 99.6 metres (327ft.) tall in
      <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">
       Tasmania
      </a>
      . The white flowers appear in autumn. Victorian botanist
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Mueller" title="Ferdinand von Mueller">
       Ferdinand von Mueller
      </a>
      described the species in 1871.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      grows in pure stands in tall wet forest, sometimes with rainforest
      <a href="/wiki/Understory" title="Understory">
       understory
      </a>
      , in temperate areas receiving over 1,200 millimetres (47in) rainfall a year on deep loam soils. many of these have been logged, including trees of a greater height than trees of any species now livingone specimen recorded at over 132 metres (433ft) in Victoria. Killed by bushfire,
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      regenerates from seed and has a lifespan of several hundred years. Mature
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      -dominated forests have been found to store more carbon than any other forest known. Known in the timber industry as
      <b>
       Tasmanian oak
      </b>
      ,
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      is logged for its wood and grown in plantations in New Zealand and Chile as well as Australia.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">
       Taxonomy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Victorian Botanist
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Mueller" title="Ferdinand von Mueller">
       Ferdinand von Mueller
      </a>
      described
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      in 1871,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apni_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-apni-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      using the Latin
      <i>
       regnans
      </i>
      "ruling" as its species epithet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He noted, "This species or variety, which might be called Eucalyptus regnans, represents the loftiest tree in British Territory." However, until 1882 he considered the tree to be a variety of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_amygdalina" title="Eucalyptus amygdalina">
        Eucalyptus amygdalina
       </a>
      </i>
      and called it thus,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis67_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Willis67-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      not using the binomial name
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      until the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Systematic_Census_of_Australian_Plants" title="Systematic Census of Australian Plants">
        Systematic Census of Australian Plants
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1882,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and giving it a formal diagnosis in 1888 in Volume 1 of the
      <i>
       Key to the System of Victorian Plants
      </i>
      , where he describes it as "stupendously tall".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonMueller1888_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vonMueller1888-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Von Mueller did not designate a type specimen, nor did he use the name
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      on his many collections of "White Mountain Ash" at the Melbourne Herbarium. Victorian botanist
      <a href="/wiki/James_Hamlyn_Willis" title="James Hamlyn Willis">
       Jim Willis
      </a>
      selected a lectotype in 1967, one of the more complete collections of a specimen from the
      <a href="/wiki/Dandenong_Ranges" title="Dandenong Ranges">
       Dandenong Ranges
      </a>
      , that von Mueller had noted was one "of the tall trees measured by Mr D. Boyle in March 1867."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis67_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Willis67-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Genetic testing across its range of
      <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast_DNA" title="Chloroplast DNA">
       chloroplast DNA
      </a>
      by Paul Nevill and colleagues yielded 41
      <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">
       haplotypes
      </a>
      , divided broadly into Victorian and Tasmanian groups, but also showing distinct profiles for some areas such as
      <a href="/wiki/East_Gippsland" title="East Gippsland">
       East Gippsland
      </a>
      , northeastern and southeastern Tasmania, suggesting the species had persisted in these areas during the
      <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">
       Last Glacial Maximum
      </a>
      and recolonised others. There was some sharing of haplotype between populations of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otway_Ranges" title="Otway Ranges">
       Otway Ranges
      </a>
      and northwestern Tasmania, suggesting this was the most likely area for gene flow between the mainland and Tasmania in the past.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      is widely known as the mountain ash, due to the resemblance of its wood to that of the northern hemisphere ash (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">
        Fraxinus
       </a>
      </i>
      ). Swamp gum is a name given to it in Tasmania, and stringy gum in northern Tasmania.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other common names include white mountain ash, giant ash, stringy gum, swamp gum and Tasmanian oak.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apni_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-apni-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Von Mueller called it the "Giant gum-tree" and "Spurious blackbutt" in his 1888
      <i>
       Key to the System of Victorian Plants
      </i>
      `.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonMueller1888_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-vonMueller1888-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The timber has been known as "Tasmanian oak", since early settlers likened the strength of its wood that of English oak (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Quercus_robur" title="Quercus robur">
        Quercus robur
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tastimber_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tastimber-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The brown barrel (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_fastigata" title="Eucalyptus fastigata">
        Eucalyptus fastigata
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is a close relative, the two sharing the rare trait of paired inflorescences arising from axillary buds. Botanist
      <a href="/wiki/Ian_Brooker" title="Ian Brooker">
       Ian Brooker
      </a>
      classified the two in the
      <a href="/wiki/Series_(botany)" title="Series (botany)">
       series
      </a>
      <i>
       Regnantes
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The latter species differs in having brown fibrous bark all the way up its trunk, and was long classified as a subspecies of
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashton_1953_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ashton_1953-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The series lies in the
      <a href="/wiki/Section_(botany)" title="Section (botany)">
       section
      </a>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus
      </i>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Subgenus" title="Subgenus">
       subgenus
      </a>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus
      </i>
      within the genus
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">
        Eucalyptus
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euclid_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Euclid-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Hybridisation with messmate (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_obliqua" title="Eucalyptus obliqua">
        Eucalyptus obliqua
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is not uncommon and has been recorded from several sites in Victoria and Tasmania.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashton_1953_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ashton_1953-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hybrids with red stringybark (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_macrorhyncha" title="Eucalyptus macrorhyncha">
        Eucalyptus macrorhyncha
       </a>
      </i>
      ) occur in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_Range_(Victoria)" title="Cathedral Range (Victoria)">
       Cathedral Range
      </a>
      in Victoria. These trees resemble
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      in appearance though lack the paired inflorescences. They have the oil composition of
      <i>
       E. macrorhyncha
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AshtonSandiford1988_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AshtonSandiford1988-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An
      <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">
       evergreen
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">
       tree
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      is the tallest of the eucalypts, growing to 70114.4 m (230375feet), with a straight, grey trunk, smooth-barked except for the rough basal 520 metres (1666ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costermans1981_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Costermans1981-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mature trees have long strips of bark hanging from the trunk.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museumvictoria_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-museumvictoria-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The trunk can reach a diameter of 2.5 metres (8ft) at breast height (dbh),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and eventually develops a large buttress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museumvictoria_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-museumvictoria-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The crown is open and small in relation to the size of the rest of the tree.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Phyllotaxis" title="Phyllotaxis">
       Arranged alternately
      </a>
      along the stems,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euclid_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Euclid-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the adult
      <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">
       leaves
      </a>
      are
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/falcate" title="wikt:falcate">
       falcate
      </a>
      (sickle-shaped) to
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lanceolate" title="wikt:lanceolate">
       lanceolate
      </a>
      , 914 centimetres (3.55.5in) long and 1.52.5 centimetres (0.61.0in) broad, with a long
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acuminate" title="wikt:acuminate">
       acuminate
      </a>
      apex and smooth margin, green to grey-green with a reddish
      <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">
       petiole
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costermans1981_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Costermans1981-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The upper and lower surfaces of the leaves are the same colour, and are dotted with numerous circular or irregularly-shaped tiny oil glands. Secondary leaf veins arise at an acute angle from the midvein and tertiary venation is sparse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">
       flowers
      </a>
      are produced in clusters of 915 together, each flower about 1 centimetre (0.4in) diameter with a ring of numerous white
      <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">
       stamens
      </a>
      . These appear between January and May.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costermans1981_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Costermans1981-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euclid_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Euclid-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 47 millimetres (0.160.28in) long pedicels,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euclid_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Euclid-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">
       fruit
      </a>
      is a capsule 59 millimetres (0.200.35in) long and 47 millimetres (0.160.28in) broad.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicolle2006_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nicolle2006-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Roughly cone-shaped, it has a disc at the base. The disc usually has three valves, which open to release the 1.53 millimetres (0.0590.118in) brown seed. The hilum is at one end of the pyramid-shaped seed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euclid_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Euclid-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Seedlings have kidney shaped
      <a href="/wiki/Cotyledon" title="Cotyledon">
       cotyledons
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Euclid_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Euclid-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the first 2-3 pairs of leaves are oppositely arranged along the stem, before switching to an alternate arrangement. These juvenile leaves are 513 centimetres (2.05.1in) long and 2.54.5 centimetres (1.01.8in) broad and ovate in shape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tallest_specimens">
       Tallest specimens
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tallest specimens">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Largest_organism" title="Largest organism">
       tallest
      </a>
      of all
      <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">
       flowering plants
      </a>
      , and possibly the tallest of all
      <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">
       plants
      </a>
      , although no living specimens can make that claim. The tallest measured living specimen, named
      <a href="/wiki/Centurion_(tree)" title="Centurion (tree)">
       Centurion
      </a>
      , stands 99.6 metres (327ft.) tall in
      <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">
       Tasmania
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-register_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-register-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Before the discovery of Centurion, the tallest known specimen was Icarus Dream, which was rediscovered in
      <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">
       Tasmania
      </a>
      in January, 2005 and is 97 metres (318ft) high. It was first measured by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surveyor_(surveying)" title="Surveyor (surveying)">
       surveyors
      </a>
      at 98.8 metres (324ft) in 1962 but the documentation had been lost.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newtallesttree_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newtallesttree-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A total of 16 living trees in Tasmania have been reliably measured in excess of 90 metres (300ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GiantTreesRegister_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GiantTreesRegister-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Cumberland Scenic Reserve near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambarville" title="Cambarville">
       Cambarville
      </a>
      , became the site of Victoria's tallest trees, in 1939, including one measured at 92 metres high, following the extensive
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(1939)" title="Black Friday (1939)">
       Black Friday bushfires
      </a>
      . A severe storm in 1959 blew down 13 of trees and the tallest tree was reduced to a height of 84 metres after it lost part of its crown. The height of this tree was cited as 81.5 metres in 2002 following further storm damage in 1973.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mifsud2002_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mifsud2002-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2000, a tree at Wallaby Catchment in
      <a href="/wiki/Kinglake_National_Park" title="Kinglake National Park">
       Kinglake National Park
      </a>
      was discovered to be 91.6 metres (301ft) tall in 2000,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mifsud2002_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mifsud2002-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      however it perished in the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Saturday_bushfires" title="Black Saturday bushfires">
       Black Saturday bushfires
      </a>
      of 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Historically, the tallest individual is claimed to be the Ferguson Tree, at 132.6 metres (435ft), found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Watts_River" title="Watts River">
       Watts River
      </a>
      region of Victoria in 1871 or 1872. This record is often disputed as unreliable, despite first-hand documentary evidence of it being measured on the ground with surveyor's tape by a senior forestry official (see below). Widespread agreement exists, however, that an exceptionally tall individual was reliably measured at 112.8 metres (370ft) by
      <a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">
       theodolite
      </a>
      in 1880 by a surveyor, George Cornthwaite, at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorpdale" title="Thorpdale">
       Thorpdale
      </a>
      , Victoria (the tree is known both as the Cornthwaite or Thorpdale Tree). When it was felled in 1881, Cornthwaite remeasured it on the ground by chain at 114.3 metres (375ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carder_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carder-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The stump is commemorated with a plaque. That tree was about 1 metre shorter than
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(tree)" title="Hyperion (tree)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
      </i>
      , the world's current tallest living tree, a
      <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">
       coast redwood
      </a>
      measuring 379.1 feet (115.5m).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin2006_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Martin2006-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Al Carder, notes that in 1888 a cash reward of 100 pounds was offered there for the discovery of any tree measuring more than 122 metres (400ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carder_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carder-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fact that such a considerable reward was never claimed is taken as evidence that such large trees did not exist. Carder's historical research, however, revealed that the reward was offered under conditions that made it highly unlikely to be collected. First, it was made in the depths of winter and applied only for a very short time. Next, the tree had to be measured by an accredited surveyor. Since loggers had already taken the largest trees from the most accessible Victorian forests, finding very tall trees then would have demanded an arduous trek into remote wilderness and at considerable altitude. In turn, that meant that searchers also needed the services of experienced bushmen to be able to guide them and conduct an effective search. Only one expedition actually penetrated one of the strongholds of
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Baw_Baw" title="Mount Baw Baw">
       Mount Baw Baw
      </a>
      but its search was rendered ineffectual by cold and snow and managed to measure only a single living tree  the New Turkey Tree: 99.4 metres (326ft)  before appalling conditions forced a retreat, Carder notes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ferdinand von Mueller, claimed to have personally measured one tree near the headwaters of the Yarra River at 122 metres (400ft). Nurseryman David Boyle, claimed in 1862 to have measured a fallen tree in a deep gully in the Dandenongs at 119.5 metres (392ft), and with a diameter at its broken tip that indicated it might have lost another eight metres of trunk when it broke, for 128 metres (420ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANH_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ANH-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carder_21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carder-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Von Mueller's early records also mention two trees on the nearby
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Spur" title="Black Spur">
       Black Spur
      </a>
      Range, one alive and measuring 128 metres (420ft) and another fallen tree said to measure 146 metres (479ft), but these were either based on hearsay or uncertain reliability. David Boyle also reported that a tree at Cape Otway measured 170 metres (560ft), but this too was based on hearsay.
     </p>
     <p>
      None, however, had been verified by direct documentation until 1982 when Ken Simpendorfer, a Special Projects Officer for the Forests Commission, Victoria, directed a search of official Victorian archives. It unearthed a forgotten report from more than a century earlier, one that had not been referred to in other accounts of the species up to that time. It was written on 21 February 1872, by the Inspector of State Forests, William Ferguson, and was addressed to the Assistant Commissioner of Lands and Surveys,
      <a href="/wiki/Clement_Hodgkinson" title="Clement Hodgkinson">
       Clement Hodgkinson
      </a>
      . Ferguson had been instructed to explore and inspect the watershed of the Watts River and reported trees in great number and exceptional size in areas where loggers had not yet reached. He wrote: "In one instance I measured with a tape line one huge specimen that lay prostrate across a tributary of the Watts, and found it to be 435ft [133 metres] from its root to the top of its trunk. At 5 feet from the ground it measures 18 feet in diameter, and at the extreme end where it has broken in its fall, it is 3 feet in diameter. This tree has been much burnt by fire, and I fully believe that before it fell it must have been more than 500ft [150 metres] high. As it now lies, it forms a complete bridge across a deep ravine."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carder_21-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carder-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is also possible that individual trees will again attain such heights. Author Bob Beale has recorded that the tallest trees in the Black Spur Range now measure about 85 metres (279ft) but  due to major bushfires in the 1920s and 30s  are less than 80 years old and have been growing consistently at the rate of about one metre a year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beale_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beale-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_New_Zealand">
       In New Zealand
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In New Zealand">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      stand in the
      <a href="/wiki/Orokonui_Ecosanctuary" title="Orokonui Ecosanctuary">
       Orokonui Ecosanctuary
      </a>
      near
      <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">
       Dunedin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      (where
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">
       introduced species
      </a>
      ) contains that country's tallest measured tree, standing 80.5 metres high in 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odt_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-odt-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">
       Distribution and habitat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      occurs in cool, mostly mountainous areas to 1,000 metres (3,300ft) altitude with high
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">
       rainfall
      </a>
      of over 1,200 millimetres (47in) per year. In Victoria, stands of tall trees are found in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otway_Ranges" title="Otway Ranges">
       Otway
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dandenong_Ranges" title="Dandenong Ranges">
       Dandenong
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarra_Ranges" title="Yarra Ranges">
       Yarra
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Strzelecki_Ranges" title="Strzelecki Ranges">
       Strzelecki
      </a>
      ranges as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Disappointment_(Australia)" title="Mount Disappointment (Australia)">
       Mount Disappointment
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/East_Gippsland" title="East Gippsland">
       East Gippsland
      </a>
      .,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mifsud2002_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mifsud2002-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the distribution is much reduced. Most of the
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      forest across Gippsland was cleared for farmland between 1860 and 1880, and in the Otway Ranges between 1880 and 1900, while severe bushfires hit in 1851, 1898 and 1939.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashton_1953_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ashton_1953-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Tasmania,
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      is found in the Huon and Derwent River valleys in the southeast of the state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the Otways, the species is found in wet forest in pure stands or growing in association with mountain grey gum (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_cypellocarpa" title="Eucalyptus cypellocarpa">
        Eucalyptus cypellocarpa
       </a>
      </i>
      ), messmate (
      <i>
       E. obliqua
      </i>
      ) and Victorian blue gum (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">
        E. globulus
       </a>
      </i>
      subsp.
      <i>
       bicostata
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BATR1997_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BATR1997-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other trees it grows with include manna gum (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_viminalis" title="Eucalyptus viminalis">
        Eucalyptus viminalis
       </a>
      </i>
      ), shining gum (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_nitens" title="Eucalyptus nitens">
        E. nitens
       </a>
      </i>
      ), myrtle beech (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nothofagus_cunninghamii" title="Nothofagus cunninghamii">
        Nothofagus cunninghamii
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and silver wattle (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Acacia_dealbata" title="Acacia dealbata">
        Acacia dealbata
       </a>
      </i>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The mountain ash-dominated forest can be interspersed with rainforest understory, with such species as southern sassafras (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Atherosperma" title="Atherosperma">
        Atherosperma moschatum
       </a>
      </i>
      ), celery-top pine (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Phyllocladus_aspleniifolius" title="Phyllocladus aspleniifolius">
        Phyllocladus aspleniifolius
       </a>
      </i>
      ), leatherwood (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eucryphia_lucida" title="Eucryphia lucida">
        Eucryphia lucida
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and horizontal (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Anodopetalum_biglandulosum" title="Anodopetalum biglandulosum">
        Anodopetalum biglandulosum
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoodHua2010_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WoodHua2010-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The mountain ash is most suited to deep friable clay loam soils, often of volcanic origin; in areas of poorer soils, it can be confined to watercourses and valleys.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boland_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The species grows very quickly, at more than a metre a year, and can reach 65 metres (213ft) in 50 years, with an average life-span of 400 years. The fallen logs continue supporting a rich variety of life for centuries more on the forest floor.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">
       Ecology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3456" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG/220px-Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG/330px-Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG/440px-Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leadbeater%27s_Possum_called_George_-_taxidermied_02.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A taxidermied male Leadbeater's possum
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The majority of the endangered
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leadbeater%E2%80%99s_possum" title="Leadbeaters possum">
       Leadbeaters possum
      </a>
      population lives in montane ash forests (
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       E. delegatensis
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       E. nitens
      </i>
      ) in the Central Highlands of Victoria. The possums use hollows in old trees for nesting and shelter and forage for arboreal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthropods" title="Arthropods">
       arthropods
      </a>
      under bark.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BATR1997_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BATR1997-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The vegetation structure of these forests enables the possums to travel through them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BATR1997_26-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BATR1997-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both Leadbeaters possums and
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow-bellied_glider" title="Yellow-bellied glider">
       yellow-bellied gliders
      </a>
      feed on the sap from the trunks and branches.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrovesRide1982_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GrovesRide1982-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Koala" title="Koala">
       Koalas
      </a>
      feed on the foliage, though it is not one of their preferred forage species.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths2001_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Griffiths2001-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow-tailed_black-cockatoo" title="Yellow-tailed black-cockatoo">
       Yellow-tailed black-cockatoos
      </a>
      nest in the hollows of old trees,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NelsonMorris1994_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NelsonMorris1994-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in contrast to the Tasmanian
      <a href="/wiki/Wedge-tailed_eagle" title="Wedge-tailed eagle">
       wedge-tailed eagle
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Aquila audax fleayi
      </i>
      ) that builds its nest of large sticks at the top of the trees.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPRAT_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SPRAT-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a small area of rainforest in
      <a href="/wiki/Yarra_Ranges_National_Park" title="Yarra Ranges National Park">
       Yarra Ranges National Park
      </a>
      in Victoria, nine
      <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">
       epiphyte
      </a>
      species were observed growing on
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      , the most prevalent of these being the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liverwort" title="Liverwort">
       liverwort
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bazzania_adnexa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bazzania adnexa (page does not exist)">
        Bazzania adnexa
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kellar2006_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kellar2006-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The spur-legged phasmid (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Didymuria_violescens" title="Didymuria violescens">
        Didymuria violescens
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is a leaf-eating insect that can defoliate trees during major infestations such as one experienced at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powelltown" title="Powelltown">
       Powelltown
      </a>
      in the early 1960s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mifsud2002_19-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mifsud2002-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Leaves and buds are eaten by the larvae and adults of the chrysomelid leaf beetle
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chrysophtharta_bimaculata" title="Chrysophtharta bimaculata">
        Chrysophtharta bimaculata
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forestry_Tasmania_1999_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Forestry_Tasmania_1999-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Stressed trees can be damaged by the eucalyptus longhorned borer (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Phoracantha_semipunctata" title="Phoracantha semipunctata">
        Phoracantha semipunctata
       </a>
      </i>
      ), which burrows into the trunk, which exudes a red stain. Eucalypt weevils of the genus
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gonipterus" title="Gonipterus">
        Gonipterus
       </a>
      </i>
      commonly damage
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      , while the tortoise beetle (
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paropsis_atomaria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paropsis atomaria (page does not exist)">
        Paropsis atomaria
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is a common pest of plantations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CABI_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CABI-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_storage">
       Carbon storage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carbon storage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A study carried out by environmental scientist Professor Brendan Mackey of the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">
       Australian National University
      </a>
      in 2009 identified that mountain ash forests in Victorias
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Highlands_(Victoria)" title="Central Highlands (Victoria)">
       Central Highlands
      </a>
      are the best in the world at locking up carbon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCScience2009_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ABCScience2009-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mackey and colleagues found the highest amount of carbon was contained in a forest located in the
      <a href="/wiki/O%27Shannassy_River" title="O'Shannassy River">
       O'Shannassy River
      </a>
      catchment, which held 1,867 tonnes of carbon per hectare. This area was a stand of unlogged mountain ash over 100 years old, which had had minimal human disturbance. They further calculated that a
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      -dominated forest with trees up to 250 years old and a well-established mid-story and upper story could store up to 2,844 tonnes of carbon per hectare.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keith_2009_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Keith_2009-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaption_to_fire">
       Adaption to fire
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adaption to fire">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      lacks a
      <a href="/wiki/Lignotuber" title="Lignotuber">
       lignotuber
      </a>
      and does not to recover by reshooting after intense fire, instead regenerating from seed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WatersBurrows2010_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WatersBurrows2010-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The seeds are released from their woody capsules (gumnuts) by heat and for successful germination the seedlings require a high level of light, much more than reaches the forest floor when there is a mature tree canopy. Severe fires can kill all the trees in a
      <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">
       forest
      </a>
      , prompting a massive release of seed to take advantage of the nutrients in the ash bed. Seedling densities of up to 2.5 million per hectare have been recorded after a major fire. Competition and natural thinning eventually reduces the mature tree density to about 30 to 40 individuals per hectare. Because it takes roughly 20 years for seedlings to reach
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturity" title="Sexual maturity">
       sexual maturity
      </a>
      , repeated fires in the same area can cause local
      <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">
       extinctions
      </a>
      . Trees that escape severe fire may have a lifespan exceeding 500 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoodHua2010_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WoodHua2010-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cool temperate rainforest species that live in association with
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      may gradually replace it in gullies or other areas where the trees succumb to age rather than fire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museumvictoria_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-museumvictoria-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      is valued for its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">
       timber
      </a>
      , and has been harvested in very large quantities. Aside from being logged in its natural range, it is grown in plantations in New Zealand and Chile, and to a limited extent, in South Africa and Zimbabwe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CABI_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CABI-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Primary uses are sawlogging and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodchipping" title="Woodchipping">
       woodchipping
      </a>
      . It was a major source of
      <a href="/wiki/Newsprint" title="Newsprint">
       newsprint
      </a>
      in the 20th century. Much of the present woodchip harvest is exported to Japan. While the area of natural stands with large old trees is rapidly decreasing, substantial areas of regrowth exist and it is increasingly grown in
      <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">
       plantations
      </a>
      , the long, straight, fast growing trunks being much more commercially valuable than the old growth timber.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is a medium weight timber (about 680kg/m) and rather coarse (stringy) in texture. Gum veins are common. The wood is easy to work and the grain is straight with long, clear sections without knots. The wood works reasonably well for steam-bending. Primary uses for sawn wood are furniture, flooring (where its very pale blonde colour is highly prized), panelling, veneer, plywood, window frames, general construction. The wood has sometimes been used for
      <a href="/wiki/Wood_wool" title="Wood wool">
       wood wool
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cooper_(profession)" title="Cooper (profession)">
       cooperage
      </a>
      . However, the wood needs steam reconditioning for high value applications, due to a tendency to collapse on drying. This wood is highly regarded by builders, furniture makers and architects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tastimber_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tastimber-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">
       Conservation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conservation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although its status as a species is secure,
      <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">
       old-growth forests
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      are particularly susceptible to destruction by forestry. For this reason stands of very old and very tall trees exist only in pockets. Very few such stands of trees fall within those areas that have been listed as National Park or World Heritage environments. Most lie within areas controlled by state forestry management authorities and their heritage value is balanced against the commercial value of harvesting and then planting fast-growing and more productive monoculture timber crops on these comparatively well-watered and fertile areas.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">
       Tasmania
      </a>
      , of the 10,000 hectares of tall-eucalypt forest logged between 1996 and 2006,
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      made up more than 1,100 hectares. The trees continue to be logged.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oldgrowth_for_Export_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Oldgrowth_for_Export-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flanagan_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-flanagan-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Political opposition to the logging of forests by the process known as
      <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">
       clearfelling
      </a>
      has grown very strong in recent years (particularly in the case of woodchipping), and the extent of future harvesting remains uncertain.
     </p>
     <p>
      It has long been believed that while many species of eucalyptus successfully survived severe bushfires, forests of
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      are highly susceptible to destruction by fire. While the process of recovery of most eucalyptus forests is rapid, so that trees that are devoid of leaves may be fully foliaged within two years, in the case of
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      , the recovery of a forest after a severe fire might require the total regrowth from seed of the devastated area, taking perhaps 200 years or more.
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been suggested that fire is necessary for the germination of
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      , and that young
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      trees flourish best where there is open space, allowing sunlight to penetrate. Prior to European intervention, indigenous land management practices involved controlled burning in order to maintain grassland. This resulted in cleared areas in forests, around the peripheries of which young trees could germinate and grow. It is probable that these indigenous practices were used within forests of
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      . Cleared spaces also occur naturally in tall forests when an old tree falls, or dies and loses its foliage. These very tall trees do not survive independently of each other, as single trees are more subject to lightning strikes and wind damage.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tmulti tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:363px;max-width:363px">

     <p>
      Research has indicated that a stand of mountain ash in Victoria is actually a multi-age stand due to fire, having experienced seven fires since the 15th century, whereas, since European settlement, many of Australia's eucalyptus forests have suffered severe fires as often as every 20 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccarthyandlindenmayer_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mccarthyandlindenmayer-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mccarthyetal_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mccarthyetal-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindenmayeretal_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lindenmayeretal-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Studies conducted in the 20th century by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_Cunningham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Murray Cunningham (page does not exist)">
       Murray Cunningham
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/David_Ashton_(botanist)" title="David Ashton (botanist)">
       David Ashton
      </a>
      suggest that the re-growth habit of
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      requires open space, and an ash layer. For this reason clearfelling (as opposed to selective logging methods) are justified by the timber industry by claiming it is required for the successful germination and growth of seedlings,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-austehc_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-austehc-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fao_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fao-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      despite such intense burns not occurring naturally and the cleared land requiring reseeding from seed collected elsewhere. The clearfell process can lead to spectacular and uniform regrowth of commercially viable timber, if managed properly. Those who support clearfelling see it as an ideal method of land management but critics point to the impact of such activities on stream health, water yield of catchments, impacts on threatened forest fauna and flora, long term soil healthy and viability, and the loss of all biodiversity to the harvested area.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to this, opponents of clearfelling point out that the forests survived for centuries without clearfelling and that it takes perhaps 300 years to replace a giant tree, commercially valuable only as woodchip, and therefore designated as "waste" by the harvesters. Opponents of clearfelling point out that the clearfell process was unavailable until the arrival of European settlers (indigenous people practised a mosaic burn system that kept the forest open but didn't remove large amounts of timber).
     </p>
     <p>
      Half of Victoria's forested
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_catchment" title="Water catchment">
       water catchment
      </a>
      areas, which provides water requiring little treatment, are composed of
      <i>
       E. regnans
      </i>
      forest. Yields from these catchments fall significantly for 20 to 40 years after disturbance. These areas have an increased risk of bushfire due to smaller trees being more flammable, the risk increasing further with climate change.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">
       Cultivation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eucalyptus_regnans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultivation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      is too large for the majority of gardens, but may be suitable for parks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wrigley_1979_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wrigley_1979-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Propagation is from seed, with the best germination rates being obtained by refrigerating for three weeks before sowing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANPSA_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ANPSA-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Seed may be stored for several years if refrigerated and kept dry. Seedlings are grown in containers but are more prone to damping off than other eucalypts; they are highly susceptible to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_cinnamomi" title="Phytophthora cinnamomi">
        Phytophthora cinnamomi
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_nicotianae" title="Phytophthora nicotianae">
        P. nicotianae
       </a>
      </i>
      Young plants are generally planted out once they are 8 or 9 months old. These are at risk of being eaten by grazing rabbits, wallabies and possums, which can destroy young plantations in severe cases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CABI_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CABI-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      American horticulturist and entrepreneur Ellwood Cooper noted its rapid growth but demanding soil requirements in his 1876 work
      <i>
       Forest Culture and Eucalyptus Trees
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Eucalyptus regnans
      </i>
      requires fertile soil with good drainage and annual rainfall of 1,000 millimetres (39in) spread over the year, and has poor tolerance to temperatures below 7C (19F) or drought.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CABI_34-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-CABI-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Outside Australia, plantations have been successfully established in New Zealand, South Africa, Kenya and Tanzania.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borota2012_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Borota2012-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
