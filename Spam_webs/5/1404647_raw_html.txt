           <h1 class="post-title">
            David Cage: The Problem With Video Games is That They Are Too Much Like Video Games
           </h1>
           <p class="post-byline">
            by
            <a href="http://www.p4rgaming.com/author/jack/" rel="author" title="Posts by Jack">
             Jack
            </a>
             May 22, 2014
           </p>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <div class="entry share">
            <div class="entry-inner">
             <p>
              In a recent interview with
              <em>
               Play Magazine
              </em>
              , David Cage claims that video games are the most fascinating medium ever made; however, the potential to become meaningful like films and books are wasted to all but a few. The problem lies in treating video games like video games he says.
              <span id="more-5617">
              </span>
              <a href="http://www.p4rgaming.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/amitheonlyonewhothinksthis.jpg">
               <img alt="amitheonlyonewhothinksthis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-5618" height="295" src="http://www.p4rgaming.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/amitheonlyonewhothinksthis.jpg" width="494"/>
              </a>
              The moment a person holds a video game controller in their hands, all potential for that game to become meaningful is instantly lost, wrote Cage in a follow-up article for
              <em>
               Le Nouvel Observateur
              </em>
              . He claims that video games need to become less like video games in order to become more like films and novels. Levels, bosses, and gameplay are all meaningless to people who require intelligent stimulation that truly feeds the mind.
             </p>
             <p>
              Cage says the impetus for the original interview came from the realization that his video games are bad. He recently read a novelization of
              <em>
               Beyond: Two Souls
              </em>
              that was written by a dedicated fan who wanted the story to make more sense. Afterwards, Cage went on to play the game and realized how horrible it actually was. Since the problem clearly could not have come from his writing, the logical conclusion is that the video game medium is still in its infancy.
             </p>
             <p>
              As I stated before, games focus on simple themes and target teenagers who crave violence, said Cage, That is essentially the definition of a video game. I can not name a single game that does not do that. The closest I can think of is
              <em>
               Sonic the Hedgehog
              </em>
              and its pro-environmental theme, but thats not deep enough for most people. Outside of that, all I see on the shelves are violence simulators that have no redeeming qualities like
              <em>
               Spec Ops: The Line
              </em>
              and
              <em>
               The Last of Us
              </em>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              He even commented on the state of the video game industry and how traditional game makers need to wake up and realize that the world they are living in demands a more mature video game.
             </p>
             <p>
              Nintendo is slowly fading out of existence because of their refusal to adapt to what people actually want from their video games, explained Cage, Has anyone ever cried when Mario rescued the princess? Can you feel empathy towards a Goomba? The fact that Nintendo can not make a game on par with
              <em>
               Schindlers List
              </em>
              is proof that they are incompetent.
             </p>
             <p>
              And beyond Nintendo, most other developers still have a long way to go, continued Cage, They need to stop making video games that are essentially video games. I pine for the day where sophisticates can sit around a fireplace and discuss morality in the latest
              <em>
               Call of Duty
              </em>
              game.
             </p>
             <p>
              Cage knows that there should be different types of video games for different audiences, but then has a realization that maybe the audience for this mythical, meaningful video game does not exist.
             </p>
             <p>
              I dont want to blame people for not wanting to play my video games, but they definitely are stupid for not demanding that developers make more games that are like mine, remarked Cage, I dont want to dumb down my video games just to get more sales, I want to make them smarter. Smart people should not be avoiding my games, but for some reason, they do. I just dont understand. Is there something about the video game medium that lowers our intelligence?
             </p>
             <p>
              While he sees nothing but despair for the future of video games, Cage says that there is hope yet from people who are inspired by him. He knows that there is at least one person out there, making a game that no one will probably ever play.
             </p>
            </div>

               </a>
              </div>
              <p>
               David Cage has great points, and it is a real shame he is often scoffed at. Despite what many have said, most people who do not play video games still think of them as toys. Cage knows this, and he has been a vocal proponent of creating experiences that touch on empathetic attachment to character over strategic combat. He creates real RPGs in which you inhabit a character who isnt defined by a skill set or experience points. I think what he is trying to do is great, and hes reached varying degrees of success.
              </p>
              <p>
               Heavy Rain was excellent despite its shallow twist, and Beyond Two Souls was a series of great high points and very clunky low points. Cage makes mundane experiences feel important, its why getting ready for a date and cleaning your apartment in BTS was way more tense than fighting soldiers in Somalia. Tucking Ethans son in bed, finding his teddy bear, and giving him a goodnight kiss was brilliant, as was helping him with his homework prior to that scene. Yes, Cage is not the best writer, but hes a great idea man.
              </p>
              <p>
               Cage is right about games being unable to tell stories as sophisticated as those found in other mediums. Im sure everyone can name a handful of games with strong writing, but they dont really compare to writing and story in the best films. And if they do, the game can offset the characters with actions without consequences. Think of Bioshock Infinite, great writing, but the characters never seem to mind the dismembered bodies on the floor. Even The Last of Us had people who are about to die from torture tell you everything despite that they are about to die moments that undercut the drama.
              </p>
              <p>
               To those who feel games should only be about power fantasies, that nobody wants to play games that follow real people, Im afraid that perspective will keep games from growing. I dont want to get rid of games with fantastical elements at all, but I want games that are about people in the real world. There is no Citizen Cane type of game, you can not put any games from 2013 (a great year for games) up to films from 2013 like Her or 12 Years a Slave. Games (a word we would a do well to get rid of, along with gamers) and films are apples and oranges, but it is clear which reaches more people and emotional engages while highlighting the human spirit.
              </p>
              <p>
               Not every game should be an interactive drama, but we need more of them. My mother and sister, who cant even play Pac-man, began Heavy Rain and played it for 4 hours straight. They dont care about games, and I had to help a handful of times, but they couldnt put the controller down. The interactive drama genre needs more creative minds at the forefront, and I hope that one day people really will say that video games are the most fascinating medium ever created.  Feel free to discuss, and thanks for reading this extremely long post.
              </p>
