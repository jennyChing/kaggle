              <div class="shift-to-hero">
               <div class="content-brightcove" data-video="2522731766001" id="2522731766001">
               </div>
              </div>
              <p>
               An Italian
               <a href="http://mashable.com/category/surgery/">
                surgeon
               </a>
               is reportedly convinced that the technology now exists to carry out a full-on human head transplant.
              </p>
              <p>
               The science  according to Dr. Sergio Canavero, a member of the Turin Advanced Neuromodulation Group  is based on the successful head transplants of animals. In 1970,
               <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xb3Zi0DenaE" target="_blank">
                Dr. Robert White
               </a>
               , a neurosurgeon, successfully completed the procedure between two rhesus monkeys.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                <div class="see-also">
                 SEE ALSO:
                 <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2013/06/27/google-glass-in-operating-room/">
                  Google Glass Finds Its Way to the Operating Room
                 </a>
                </div>
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               Canavero notes White's efforts in a
               <a href="http://www.surgicalneurologyint.com/article.asp?issn=2152-7806;year=2013;volume=4;issue=2;spage=335;epage=342;aulast=Canavero" target="_blank">
                paper
               </a>
               on the topic, and adds that "the greatest technical hurdle to such endeavor is of course the reconnection of the donor (D)'s and recipient (R)'s spinal cords." (His report goes on to sketch out a possible human scenario and additionally outlines the technology needed to reconnect a severed cord.)
              </p>
              <p>
               Canavero's method, unlike Whites, advocates for a clean cut, the so-called key to spinal cord fusion. Canavero told
               <em>
                <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/science/10154240/First-human-head-transplant-now-possible-neurosurgeon-claims.html" target="_blank">
                 The Telegraph
                </a>
               </em>
               that the operation could provide people with a "substantial amount of extra life." The biggest hurdle? Funding.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                <div class="see-also">
                 SEE ALSO:
                 <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2013/05/24/instagram-vine-live-brain-surgery/">
                  UCLA Surgeons Live Vine Brain Surgery
                 </a>
                </div>
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               Estimates currently put the surgery around the $13 million mark.
              </p>
              <p>
               What do you think about this science fiction procedure turning into reality? Watch the videos above to learn more, and sound off in the comments.
              </p>
