      <div class="story title">
       <a class="category" href="/tags/adjectives" itemprop="articleSection">
        Adjectives
       </a>
       <h5 class="date" itemprop="datePublished">
        <span content="2013-10-08T14:05:25-04:00" datatype="xsd:dateTime" property="dc:date dc:created">
         October 8, 2013
        </span>
       </h5>
       <h1 class="title">
        <span class="headline" itemprop="headline">
         "Rapey" This, "Rapey" That. Enough.
        </span>
       </h1>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="content">
      <div class="story">
       <div class="tracking">
        <div class="body text">
         <h5 class="ui author dividing header">
          By
          <a href="/authors/nora-caplan-bricker" itemprop="author" rel="author">
           Nora Caplan-Bricker
          </a>
          <span class="twitter">
           <i class="twitter icon">
           </i>
           <a href="https://twitter.com/ncaplanbricker" target="_blank">
            @ncaplanbricker
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="attribution">
           Photo: Ethan Miller/Getty Images Entertainment/ Getty Images
          </span>
         </h5>
         <div class="body" itemprop="articleBody">
          <p>
           All of a sudden, everything is rapey. I blame Robin Thickes Blurred Lines, which conjured the adjective so many times that even the
           <em>
            <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/speakeasy/2013/08/08/is-blurred-lines-a-rapey-song/">
             Wall Street Journal
            </a>
           </em>
           deployed it (surely a sign of the apocalypse).
           <em>
            <a href="http://gawker.com/internet-pranks-are-getting-rapey-er-every-day-1284500664">
             Gawker
            </a>
           </em>
           <a href="http://gawker.com/internet-pranks-are-getting-rapey-er-every-day-1284500664">
            thinks
           </a>
           YouTube pranksters who give unsolicited hugs are rapey. Monday,
           <em>
            <a href="http://nymag.com/thecut/2013/10/this-rape-y-e-mail-teaches-frat-bros-to-dance.html">
             New York
            </a>
           </em>
           attached the term to a frat bros how-to guide for bedding drunk girls, and last month,
           <em>
            <a href="http://jezebel.com/college-sexual-assault-survivors-have-to-deal-with-rape-1301041289">
             Jezebel
            </a>
           </em>
           used it to describe graffiti that denigrated victims of sexual assault on college campuses. The list goes on.
          </p>
          <p>
           Im all for having a word for behavior that is vaguely troubling rather than full-on threatening, a warning flag to raise at the first whiff of chauvinism. But if rapey is the right adjective for a pop song prodding, I know you want it, how can it also be the term of choice for a vandal writing whore on a photo of a college rape survivor? Thats not rapey, its a terrifying and sadistic attitude toward rape. We adorn our sentences with adjectives because we want to get specific, as my colleague Noreen Malone underscored in her
           <a href="http://nymag.com/news/intelligencer/internet-suffixes-2012-4/">
            defense of the -y suffix
           </a>
           last year: Caddish may be a more eloquent choice than harass-y, but sometimes the slapdash alternative cuts closer to what you want to say. But bandying rapey around in such a broad range of situations abuses the privilege. Someone needs to buy these bloggers a dictionary.
          </p>
          <p>
           Except that eschewing specificity seems to be the whole point of calling something rapey. The word is flippant, and crude, and thats the source of its humor. Most things that get dubbed rapey on the Internet are intended to get laughs, like the aforementioned
           <a href="http://nymag.com/thecut/2013/10/this-rape-y-e-mail-teaches-frat-bros-to-dance.html">
            fraternity brother
           </a>
           and his short guide to the 7 Es of HOOKING UP! (Including, IF ANYTHING EVER FAILS, GO GET MORE ALCOHOL. All-caps emphasis obviously his.) I'll be honest: I've said rapey, probably about the neighborhood of Georgetown, where the sea of guys in popped collars threatens to wash away all the things I like about the world. But I always feel a twinge of guilt when I drop that clunky word. I think, if comedy is the goal, that we need to attach that -y suffix to a different base. Real rape, not "rapey-ness," still gets laughed off in Americahe said, she said, too much boozeand these trivializing Internet associations dont seem like a ticket to the gravity the subject deserves.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its good to have a moniker for sexism at its most flabbergasting and gross, and its even good for that word to be funny, to buy the observations some mainstream cred. But if the word needs to be funny, it shouldn't be rapey, and it should be kept clear from thingsincluding the college vandalism and that frat bros emailthat actively encourage or relate to rape. Rape, the real crime, already suffers from enough blurred lines.
          </p>
