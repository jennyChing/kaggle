           <p>
           </p>
           <center>
            <a href="http://www.psych2go.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/november.png">
             <img alt="november" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-12327" height="300" src="http://www.psych2go.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/november-300x300.png" width="300"/>
            </a>
           </center>
           <p>
            My first reaction as a psychology student is This got to be false! How is it possible that the month of birth can be correlated to a persons psychopathic tendency? Are there specific characteristics or happenings of specific months that could possibly contribute to a persons gene that causes them to be serial killers?!
           </p>
           <p>
            I did some research on the web and I found something, It turns out to be a study by a PhD Student. It had some support
            <a href="http://www.nvwoa.nl/pdfart/ruis/ruisen.pdf" target="_blank">
             Statistical analysis of the birth charts of serial killers by Jan Ruis, PhD
            </a>
            
           </p>
           <p>
            My reaction again:
            <br/>

            1) How reliable do you think that paper is?
            <br/>
            2) Whats your initial thought on this claim?
            <br/>
            3) What are potential problems associated with the research technique used by the study?
            <br/>
            4) Where are other supporting research on this?
            <br/>
            5) What implications does this claim have if it turns out to be true?
            <br/>

                     <div class="comment-body">
                      <p>
                       My two cents:
                       <br/>
                       1) One month does not necessarily have to be the one when most serial killers are born. It might well be that two or more months have the same number. The distribution of births will be seriously confounded by the size of the sample. So larger the  size of sample more the distribution of births across the months of the year would become closer to equal.
                       <br/>
                       2) Another confounding is what you accept as definition of serial killer. As in any definition of human behaviour or mental related issues what you define as being included in the sample will alter your statistical analysis.
                       <br/>
                       3) I am not sure what type of test you apply to your analysis (parametric or non-parametric). If the sample is small, non-parametric tests will provide more accurate outcome. So if you have (lets say) 20   serial killers, having 4 born in November may reach statistical significance if you use a parametric test, but most certainly will not if you use  (as you should) a non-parametric test.
                      </p>
