             In 1963, Aerojet General was given a
             <a href="http://www.keysnet.com/2010/06/10/227720/hidden-in-the-glades-a-giant-relic.html">
              $3 million contract
             </a>
             from the U.S. Air Force to build a manufacturing and testing site for rockets that would send astronauts to the moon.
            </p>
            <p class="p2">
             The plant was constructed in the center of Florida'sEverglades in the town of Homestead.
            </p>
            <p class="p2">
             Beneath a large metal shed, a 150-foot deep silo housed the largest solid-fuel rocket motor ever built.The rocket was tested three times between 1965 and 1967.
            </p>
            <p class="p2">
             Then
             <a class="hidden_link" href="http://www.businessinsider.com/blackboard/nasa">
              NASA
             </a>
             dropped the project. The agency decided to go with liquid-fuel rocket engines instead. The plant was closed in 1969, leaving the rocket behind.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Photographer Naaman Fletcher, who blogs at
             <a href="http://leftbirmingham.blogspot.com/">
              What's Left of Birmingham
             </a>
             , visited the abandoned facility in April 2010.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Here's what remains.
            </p>
