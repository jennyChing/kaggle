               <h2>
                <a href="http://gadgetzz.com/2014/12/27/was-the-sony-hack-a-publicity-stunt-for-the-interview/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Was The Sony Hack A Publicity Stunt For The Interview?">
                 Was The Sony Hack A Publicity Stunt For The Interview?
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="post-date">
               <em>
                On December 27, 2014,
in
                <a href="http://gadgetzz.com/category/hacks-mods/" rel="category tag">
                 Hacking/Modding news
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://gadgetzz.com/category/news-2/" rel="category tag">
                 News
                </a>
                ,
by Johny Krahbichler
               </em>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="copy">
              <div class="post-content">
               <div class="linksalpha_widget" style="padding-top:0px;padding-right:0px;padding-bottom:0px;padding-left:1px;;">
                <iframe allowtransparency="true" frameborder="0" scrolling="no" src="http://www.linksalpha.com/social?blog=Gadgetzz+%7C+Technology+News&amp;link=http%3A%2F%2Fgadgetzz.com%2F2014%2F12%2F27%2Fwas-the-sony-hack-a-publicity-stunt-for-the-interview%2F&amp;title=Was+The+Sony+Hack+A+Publicity+Stunt+For+The+Interview%3F&amp;desc=Obviously+I+can%27t+actually+tell+you+yes+or+no%2C+but+after+looking+at+the+facts+the+answer+seems+to+be+both+yes+and+no.+First+of+all%2C+the+hack+itself+could+have+not+been+a+PR+trick%2C+at+all.+It+would+be+a+pretty+stupid+PR+trick+to+release+sensitive+information.+Like+emails+that+would+suggest+that+Sony&amp;fc=3790e8&amp;fs=verdana&amp;fblname=like&amp;fblref=facebook&amp;fbllang=en_US&amp;fblshow=1&amp;fbsbutton=1&amp;fbsctr=1&amp;fbslang=en&amp;fbsendbutton=0&amp;twbutton=1&amp;twlang=en&amp;twmention=gadgetzz&amp;twrelated1=gadgetzz&amp;twrelated2=&amp;twctr=0&amp;lnkdshow=show&amp;lnkdctr=0&amp;buzzbutton=0&amp;buzzlang=en&amp;buzzctr=0&amp;diggbutton=1&amp;diggctr=0&amp;stblbutton=1&amp;stblctr=1&amp;g1button=1&amp;g1ctr=1&amp;g1lang=en-US" style="height:25px !important; border:0px solid gray !important; overflow:hidden !important; width:550px !important;">
                </iframe>
               </div>
               <p>
                <a data-lightboxplus="lightbox[49627]" href="http://beta.gadgetzz.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/the-interview-publicity-stunt-pr-trick.jpg">
                 <img alt="the interview publicity stunt pr trick" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-49628" height="321" src="http://beta.gadgetzz.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/the-interview-publicity-stunt-pr-trick.jpg" width="598"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                Obviously I cant actually tell you yes or no, but after looking at the facts the answer seems to be both yes and no.
                <br/>
                <span id="more-49627">
                </span>
               </p>
               <p>
                First of all, the hack itself could have not been a PR trick, at all. It would be a pretty stupid PR trick to release sensitive information. Like
                <a href="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-2880108/Shamed-Sony-head-Amy-Pascal-called-culturally-blind-Al-Sharpton-following-racist-emails-two-meet-address-racial-diversity-Hollywood.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                 emails
                </a>
                that would suggest that Sony Co chairman Amy Pascal is a blatant racists.
               </p>
               <div class="insert-post-ads" style="clear:both;float:left;width:100%;margin:0 0 20px 0;">
                <center>
                 <div class="cfmonitor">
                  <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
                  </script>
                  <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-5772459352349153" data-ad-slot="8252329822" style="display:inline-block;width:336px;height:280px">
                  </ins>
                  <script>
                   (adsbygoogle=window.adsbygoogle||[]).push({});
                  </script>
                 </div>
                </center>
               </div>
               <p>
                Still not convinced? How about the fact that Sony threatened journalists for reporting on the hack:
               </p>
               <blockquote>
                <p>
                 Just a few days after the media reported that that the MPAA and forces in Hollywood were planning the next SOPA, Sony Pictures sent threatening letters to media outlets demanding that they stop reporting on the corporate hack and delete any stolen information in their possession. These letters are legally baseless. Theyre abusive, and clearly intended to suppress suppress journalism and censor the story. Lets tell Sony it wont work.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <a href="https://cms.fightforthefuture.org/sony/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                  Source
                 </a>
                </p>
               </blockquote>
               <p>
                <a data-lightboxplus="lightbox[49627]" href="http://beta.gadgetzz.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/north-korea-hollywood-sign.jpg">
                 <img alt="north korea hollywood sign" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-49629" height="165" src="http://beta.gadgetzz.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/north-korea-hollywood-sign-300x165.jpg" width="300"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                But was it North Korea that did the hacking? Of that Im not sure, the FBI tells us yes, but the FBI is hardly a reliable source. Kim Jong Un obviously says no, he even
                <a href="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-2881698/North-Korea-offers-joint-investigation-Sony-hack-threatens-consequences-U-S-refuses.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                 offered to help
                </a>
                find out who really did it. Not that Pyongyang is a reliable source either. I guess we cant actually know if it was North Korea or not.
                <br/>
                <iframe frameborder="0" height="403" id="molvideoplayer" scrolling="no" src="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/embed/video/1145655.html" title="MailOnline Embed Player" width="600">
                </iframe>
               </p>
               <p>
                So the hack itself was obviously not an inside job. But maybe Sony saying they werent gonna release the movie was a publicity stunt?
               </p>
               <p>
                Now on this we cant be so sure, its certainly possible. If it is a PR, well, Im impressed. Because essentially they made a bad situation work for them instead of against them. And people did forget all about the other information that was leaked.
               </p>
               <p>
                Speculations on war between North Korea and the US arose, conspiracy theories and what not, but people had indeed forgotten all about the rest. Obama even had to calm everyone saying he didnt see the hack as an act of war.
               </p>
               <p>
                <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://embed.theguardian.com/embed/video/us-news/video/2014/dec/21/obama-north-korea-sony-hack-not-act-of-war-video" width="560">
                </iframe>
               </p>
               <p>
                But did they actually make any money of this supposed publicity stunt? Well, yes, but not as much as you might think. Clearly more people wanted to see the movie after all this drama surrounding it. This
                <a href="http://comicbook.com/2014/12/26/the-interview-makes-one-million-dollars-on-opening-day-at-box-of/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                 article
                </a>
                says that The interview made over a million dollars on its first day. Which is amazing, but not amazing enough to suggest anything about the plausibility of a PR stunt. Especially considering that the movie costed over $100 million to make in the first place.
               </p>
               <blockquote>
                <p>
                 Despite an extremely limited release and threats from North Korean hackers, Sonys The Interview still scored $1 million at the box office on Christmas Day.
                 <br/>
                 After saying that it wouldnt release the film at all, Sony made a last-minute decision to distribute the film to less than 10% of planned movie theaters yesterday, and made the film available for digital viewing on Christmas Eve.
                </p>
               </blockquote>
               <p>
                Oh, did you notice that? An extremely limited release. That does suggest something about the plausibility of a PR stunt, it suggests its highly implausible. If they intended to release the movie all along why not release it everywhere?
               </p>
               <p>
                We still cant say for sure, but Im guessing it was never intended to be a PR trick, but they clearly used the situation. If not to make money, at least to minimize their loss.
               </p>
