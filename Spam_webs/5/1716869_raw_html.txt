          <h1 class="entry-title">
           Chinas Middle Class Doesnt Look Like it Used to
          </h1>
         </header>
         <div class="row row-meta">
          <span class="col-md-6 author vcard">
           <i class="el-icon-user icon">

          <p>
           Widely reported this week is news the
           <strong>
            Chinese economy
           </strong>
           will outpace  the American economy in less than a decade.
          </p>
          <p>
           Chinas year-on-year average growth of 7% for the past  decade has been nothing short of remarkable. Each year, market analysts  globally have questioned if
           <strong>
            China
           </strong>
           can continue this historic economic  expansion.
          </p>
          <p>
           Kris Sayce, reasoned it was like America taking over from  the United Kingdom in the late 19th century:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            
            <em>
             theres no denying that Chinas growth story over the past 10 years  has been nothing short of astounding. But can the growth continue?
            </em>
            <br/>
            
            <em>
             Well, were sure there were plenty of folks who doubted the  sustainability of American growth in the 1870s. And yet that proved to be the  beginning of 142 years of dominance.
            </em>
            
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           However,
           <a href="http://www.moneymorning.com.au/category/economy/china-economy" title="more on China">
            Chinas economic growth
           </a>
           has long been because of  central planning.
          </p>
          <p>
           And in the long term, the central planners want their  citizens to take their recent urbanisation, and apply some Western style  principals of consumption-at-all-costs.
          </p>
          <p>
           In other words, get the Chinese people to create a  consumption driven economy with the Middle Kingdoms government still in  control.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, that may not be feasible. The problem is the  dynamics of
           <strong>
            Chinas middle class
           </strong>
           is changing
          </p>
          <p>
           Chinas middle class doesnt look like it used to.
          </p>
          <p>
           The demographics in China are changing.
          </p>
          <p>
           For a long time, the middle class in China were a happy lot.  And the upper middle class in China were the happiest.
          </p>
          <p>
           This is because the upper middle class of China was mostly  filled with officials, former officials, or those with links to government  officials that benefited them.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, thanks to Chinas urbanisation, the composition of  the middle class is changing.
          </p>
          <div align="center">
           <a href="http://portphillippublishing.com.au/images/MMW20140503a.jpg">
            <img border="0" height="183" src="http://portphillippublishing.com.au/images/MMW20140503a.jpg" width="305"/>
           </a>
           <br/>
           <strong>
            Source: the Economist / McKinsey
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <em>
            <a href="http://portphillippublishing.com.au/images/MMW20140503a.jpg" target="_blank">
             Click to enlarge
            </a>
           </em>
          </div>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           Right now, the upper middle class (those with an annual  income of 106,000229,000 yuan) account for about 14% of the urban population.  In contrast, 54% make up the mass middle class.
          </p>
          <p>
           Like I said before, for a long time this part of the  population had links to the government that benefited their lives. So up until  now, theres been no real demand for change.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, by 2022, the mass middle class will account for 22%  of the urban population. This will result in more than 50% of the urban population  being in the upper middle class bracket.
          </p>
          <p>
           And this is going to create problems for the government.
          </p>
          <p>
           To begin with, few of these new found upper middle class  folk will have links or access to government officials.
          </p>
          <p>
           And secondly, this growing segment of the population will  want more control over their lives than the Chinese government currently gives  them.
          </p>
          <p>
           Take this for example.
          </p>
          <p>
           Paraxylene, a chemical used in the manufacture of polyester  is causing citizens to protest. They worry that the fumes are a hazard to their  health.
          </p>
          <p>
           Since 2007 there have been protests in five different cities  that have proposed to build factories that use Paraxylene.
          </p>
          <p>
           In spite of the Great Fire Wall of China  the nickname for  the governments internet censorship filter for the Golden Shield Project  people  used social media to organise these protests.
          </p>
          <p>
           Now, the comparable health risk of Paraxylene and the  pollution levels in major cities is debatable.
          </p>
          <p>
           But dont ignore these protests. Because it demonstrates a growing  distrust of the government.
          </p>
          <p>
           Simply put, the growing middle class now have access to more  information than at any other time in history.
          </p>
          <p>
           Urbanisation, education and increasing wealth have led residents  to fight for a standard of living that they want.
          </p>
          <p>
           On the surface the protests appear to be a demonstration  against developers directing their communities. But its not. Because many  commercial developers are under official control, each rally is a stand against  the government.
          </p>
          <p>
           Furthermore, many in this growing middle class
           <a href="http://pro1.fitzroypress.com.au/213418?email={emailaddress}" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            want to leave the country
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Over two years, Shanghai University undertook a survey of  the middle class and their views on living in China.
          </p>
          <p>
           It found that of the middle class in Shanghai, one third would  leave the country immediately if they could. Over in Guangzhou, another major  city, 40% of the middle income group would do so too.
          </p>
          <p>
           Even those considered rich were actually keen to leave. 64%  of the population with wealth worth more than 10 million yuan (AU$1.6 million)  were emigrating or planning to do so.
          </p>
          <p>
           Why am I telling you this? Because this is another variable  that challenges the Chinese growth story.
          </p>
          <p>
           All of Chinese growth comes from central planners. However  central planners are relying on the people it moved into the middle class to  remain there.
          </p>
          <p>
           And in order for the central planning to be successful, the  middle class need to remain satisfied.
          </p>
          <p>
           Now like Kris, I have no doubt in the long term growth from  China. Its going to be big  an
           <a href="http://www.moneymorning.com.au/best-investment-opportunities" title="more on investment opportunities ">
            investing opportunity
           </a>
           not to be lost.
          </p>
          <p>
           But if the Chinese government wants a Western style economy,  it will have to let go of the political repression it has over its people. If  not then the very demographic the regimes is relying on to shift into a consumption  economy could very well interfere with their plans.
          </p>
