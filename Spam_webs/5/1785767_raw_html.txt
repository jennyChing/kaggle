     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Phantom Carriage
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
       Swedish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="sv">
       <i>
        Krkarlen
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a 1921 Swedish film generally considered to be one of the central works in the history of
      <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Sweden" title="Cinema of Sweden">
       Swedish cinema
      </a>
      . Released on
      <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">
       New Year's Day
      </a>
      1921, it was directed by and starred
      <a href="/wiki/Victor_Sj%C3%B6str%C3%B6m" title="Victor Sjstrm">
       Victor Sjstrm
      </a>
      , alongside
      <a href="/wiki/Hilda_Borgstr%C3%B6m" title="Hilda Borgstrm">
       Hilda Borgstrm
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tore_Svennberg" title="Tore Svennberg">
       Tore Svennberg
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Astrid_Holm" title="Astrid Holm">
       Astrid Holm
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-silentera_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-silentera-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is based on the novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Thy_Soul_Shall_Bear_Witness!" title="Thy Soul Shall Bear Witness!">
        Thy Soul Shall Bear Witness!
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Krkarlen
      </i>
      ; 1912), by
      <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">
       Nobel prize
      </a>
      -winning Swedish author
      <a href="/wiki/Selma_Lagerl%C3%B6f" title="Selma Lagerlf">
       Selma Lagerlf
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The film is notable for its special effects, its advanced (for the time) narrative structure with
      <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">
       flashbacks
      </a>
      within flashbacks, and for having been a major influence on
      <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">
       Ingmar Bergman
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is also known as
      <i>
       The Phantom Chariot
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Thy Soul Shall Bear Witness!
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       The Stroke of Midnight
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tartan_Films" title="Tartan Films">
       Tartan Films
      </a>
      released a DVD version of the film, with a new and contemporary score from
      <a href="/wiki/KTL" title="KTL">
       KTL
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2011, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criterion_Collection" title="Criterion Collection">
       Criterion Collection
      </a>
      released a restored version of the film on Blu-ray and DVD.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On
      <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">
       New Year's Eve
      </a>
      , the dying
      <a href="/wiki/The_Salvation_Army" title="The Salvation Army">
       Salvation Army
      </a>
      girl Edit has one last wish: to speak with David Holm. David, an alcoholic, is sitting in a graveyard with two drinking buddies, talking about his old friend Georges who told him about Death's carriagethe legend that the last person to die each year has to work under the "strict master" Death and collect the souls of everybody who dies the following year. Georges himself died on New Year's Eve last year.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gustafsson, a friend of Edit who is looking for David, finds him and tries to convince him to go and see her, but David refuses. When his friends too try to convince him, a fight breaks out where David is accidentally killed just before the clock strikes twelve. The carriage appears, and the driver is revealed as Georges.
     </p>
     <p>
      As David's soul steps out of his body, Georges reminds him of what he once had, how he once lived a happy family life with his wife Anna before ending up in bad company with Georges and the other drinkers. It is revealed how Anna left him after he was jailed for intoxication. He also reminds him how David exactly one year ago was taken care of by Edit, and while treating her badly, he gave her his promise to find her the following year so she would find out whether her prayers for him had worked or not.
     </p>
     <p>
      Georges informs David that the promise has to be fulfilled and brings him in the carriage to Edit's house. In another flashback it is shown how Edit once found David in a bar with his friend Gustafsson. Edit persuaded them to go to a Salvation Army meeting. At the meeting, Gustafsson submitted himself to God, but David stayed cynical. Present at the meeting was also David's wife. Edit tried to bring the couple together again. At first they were optimistic, but soon David's drinking drove them into despair. One night David became aggressive when Anna tried to protect their children from being infected by David's
      <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">
       tuberculosis
      </a>
      . He was locked in the kitchen, but broke through the door with an axe.
     </p>
     <p>
      When the driver arrives in Edit's room, she begs him to let her live until she sees David again. She thinks she is the one to blame for his guilt, as she brought the couple together again. When David hears this he is moved. He kisses her hands, and when Edit sees his regret she can die in peace. Georges then takes David to Anna, who is planning to kill herself and their children. David begs Georges and God to let him interfere. Georges allows him to return to life. David and Anna embrace each other and cry.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Since 1917 there was a deal between Selma Lagerlf and
      <a href="/wiki/AB_Svensk_Filmindustri" title="AB Svensk Filmindustri">
       A-B Svenska Biografteatern
      </a>
      to adapt at least one Lagerlf novel for film every year. Prior to
      <i>
       The Phantom Carriage
      </i>
      , Sjstrm had made three of these adaptions which had all been well received by critics, the audience and Lagerlf herself. Since all of them had taken place in a rural setting, Sjstrm felt that he wanted a change for the fourth and suggested the urban, gritty
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6rkarlen" title="Krkarlen">
       Krkarlen
      </a>
      . Lagerlf was initially sceptical about the possibility to adapt the novel's elements of
      <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">
       occultism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">
       mysticism
      </a>
      , and Sjstrm was well aware of the difficulties. The script took eight days to finish and in April 1920 Sjstrm travelled to Lagerlf's mansion Mrbacka in
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4rmland" title="Vrmland">
       Vrmland
      </a>
      to present it. After two hours of Sjstrm reading loud and performing the whole script by himself, Lagerlf responded by offering him dinner, which Sjstrm took as an approval.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lagerlof_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lagerlof-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Korkarlen-screen.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="250" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/92/Korkarlen-screen.png/220px-Korkarlen-screen.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/92/Korkarlen-screen.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/92/Korkarlen-screen.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Korkarlen-screen.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Double exposures on a gritty
        <a href="/wiki/Landskrona" title="Landskrona">
         Landskrona
        </a>
        -inspired street.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">
       Filming
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Shooting took place from May to July 1920 in the newly started
      <a href="/wiki/Filmstaden" title="Filmstaden">
       Filmstaden
      </a>
      studios in
      <a href="/wiki/Solna_Municipality" title="Solna Municipality">
       Solna
      </a>
      . The set design was inspired by the southern Swedish town
      <a href="/wiki/Landskrona" title="Landskrona">
       Landskrona
      </a>
      , which corresponded to what Lagerlf had in mind when writing the novel. Lagerlf's original wish was to film it on location in Landskrona, but Sjstrm chose to do it in studio for the technical benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfi_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sfi-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Post-production">
       Post-production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Post-production was famously long and intense due to the extensive use of special effects, developed by cinematographer
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_Jaenzon" title="Julius Jaenzon">
       Julius Jaenzon
      </a>
      and lab executive Eugn Hellman. Double exposures made in the camera (optical printing wasn't available until the early 1930s), had been used before by Jaenzon, already in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sir_Arne%27s_Treasure" title="Sir Arne's Treasure">
        Sir Arne's Treasure
       </a>
      </i>
      from 1919, but were here developed to be far more advanced with several layers. This allowed the ghost characters to walk around in three dimensions, being able to first be covered by an object in the foreground, but when in the same take walking up in front of the object, it would be seen through the ghost's semi-transparent body.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfi_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sfi-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One difficulty was that the cameras were hand-cranked, meaning that the camera had to be cranked at exactly the same speed in the different exposures for the end result to appear natural.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lagerlof_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-lagerlof-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Soundtracks">
       Soundtracks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Soundtracks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The original screenings didn't have an original soundtrack, instead various pieces by
      <a href="/wiki/Ture_Rangstr%C3%B6m" title="Ture Rangstrm">
       Ture Rangstrm
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">
       Mendelsohn
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Camille_Saint-Sa%C3%ABns" title="Camille Saint-Sans">
       Saint-Sans
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Reger" title="Max Reger">
       Max Reger
      </a>
      were performed by the orchestras.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfi_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sfi-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For a long time several different soundtracks, generally of low quality, were used for television screenings and video releases. However in 1998, on demand from the
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Film_Institute" title="Swedish Film Institute">
       Swedish Film Institute
      </a>
      , a new soundtrack was composed by renowned Swedish silent film composer and live pianist
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Matti_Bye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Matti Bye (page does not exist)">
       Matti Bye
      </a>
      , which was highly praised and has been featured on all following VHS and DVD releases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the 2007
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_International_Film_Festival" title="San Francisco International Film Festival">
       San Francisco International Film Festival
      </a>
      a new soundtrack was composed and performed live by pop icon
      <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Richman" title="Jonathan Richman">
       Jonathan Richman
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      No Region-1 DVD release with this version has been announced.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, the now defunct Tartan Films (now
      <a href="/wiki/Palisades_Tartan" title="Palisades Tartan">
       Palisades Tartan
      </a>
      ), under license from
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Film_Institute" title="Swedish Film Institute">
       Swedish Film Institute
      </a>
      , released a region 2 DVD version with a newly commissioned soundtrack by the electronic music group
      <a href="/wiki/KTL" title="KTL">
       KTL
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
       Influence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Phantom_Carriage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The film was a powerful influence on the later Swedish film director
      <a href="/wiki/Ingmar_Bergman" title="Ingmar Bergman">
       Ingmar Bergman
      </a>
      who also utilised the figure of Death in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Seventh_Seal" title="The Seventh Seal">
        The Seventh Seal
       </a>
      </i>
      , where the referring to him as a "strict master" is a reference to
      <i>
       The Phantom Carriage
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Bergman also cast Sjstrm in the leading role for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wild_Strawberries_(film)" title="Wild Strawberries (film)">
        Wild Strawberries
       </a>
      </i>
      , which also features references to the film. Bergman has said that he first saw it at 15 and watched it at least once every year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The television play
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Image_Makers" title="The Image Makers">
        The Image Makers
       </a>
      </i>
      (2000), directed by Bergman, is a historical drama depicting the making of
      <i>
       The Phantom Carriage
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">
       Stanley Kubrick
      </a>
      's 1980 horror film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Shining_(film)" title="The Shining (film)">
        The Shining
       </a>
      </i>
      features several thematic similarities, as well as the famous sequence where
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicholson" title="Jack Nicholson">
       Jack Nicholson
      </a>
      uses an axe to break through a wooden door.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (There is also a similar scene in
      <a href="/wiki/D._W._Griffith" title="D. W. Griffith">
       D. W. Griffith
      </a>
      's 1919 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Broken_Blossoms" title="Broken Blossoms">
        Broken Blossoms
       </a>
      </i>
      .)
     </p>
