          <h1 class="entry-title">
           Drone-mountable camera takes professional UAV filming to the masses
          </h1>
          <div class="entry-meta">
           <span class="entry-date">
            <a href="http://factor-tech.com/drones/17449-drone-mountable-camera-takes-professional-uav-filming-to-the-masses/" rel="bookmark">
             <time class="entry-date" datetime="2015-04-14T11:48:09+00:00">
              April 14, 2015
             </time>
            </a>
           </span>
           <span class="byline">
            <span class="author vcard">
             <a class="url fn n" href="http://factor-tech.com/author/lucy-ingham/" rel="author">
              Lucy Ingham
             </a>
            </span>
           </span>
          </div>
          <!-- .entry-meta -->
         </header>
         <!-- .entry-header -->
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           A camera that rivals the GoPro on size but offers a host of professional features including interchangeable lenses and RAW capturing has been announced by broadcast equipment makers Blackmagic Design.
          </p>
          <p>
           While the camera is more expensive than the ubiquitous GoPro, with a price point of $995 it remains in reach of consumers and independent filmmakers, making it the first drone-mountable camera to be so.
          </p>
          <p>
           The camera, a miniaturised Super 16mm digital camera named the Blackmagic Micro Cinema Camera, shoots 1080 HD with 13 stops of dynamic range.
          </p>
          <p>
           It is also designed to suit other professional shooting situations where a tiny camera is a must, such as hidden camera shows.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Blackmagic Micro Cinema Camera is the smallest and most expandable digital film camera in the world, explained Grant Petty, CEO, Blackmagic Design.
          </p>
          <p>
           For the first time, customers will be able to get incredible action and point of view shots that look better than anything theyve ever seen before.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <strong>
             <img alt="micro-cinema-2" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-17451" height="468" src="http://factor-tech.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/micro-cinema-2-600x468.jpg" width="600"/>
            </strong>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The camera is being presented to the television and film industry as first and foremost a professional piece of equipment that also offers drone connectivity.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Micro Cinema features ports for PWM and S.Bus connections, which allow specific adjustments including audio levels, iris and focus, as well as the more standard start and stop, to be mapped to a remote control. Those connecting with S.Bus can even design their own custom embedded controllers.
          </p>
          <p>
           The new expansion port allowing radio remote control is exciting because it opens up entirely new shooting possibilities that will let customers shoot things theyve never been able to shoot before, said Petty.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <strong>
             <img alt="micro-cinema-1" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-17450" height="483" src="http://factor-tech.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/micro-cinema-1-600x483.jpg" width="600"/>
            </strong>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The Micro Cinema also offers a host of other features targeted at filmmaking professionals.
          </p>
          <p>
           It can shoot with either a 60fps rolling shutter or a 30fps global shutter, and saves footage either as 12-bit log CinemaDNG RAW or Apple ProRes files.
          </p>
          <p>
           These ensure that capturing is lossless, allowing for a host of post-production options to improve the look of the final footage.
          </p>
          <p>
           It also has a Micro Four Thirds lens mount, which allows filmmakers to switch out lenses to suit the filming environment and can also be adapted to mount other mainstream lenses.
          </p>
          <p>
           Other professional connectors such as an external stereo microphone input further cement the camera as a professional-standard piece of equipment.
          </p>
          <p>
           While the camera will undoubtedly be embraced by many professionals, it is likely to see huge popularity among indie filmmakers, bringing a host of high-quality aerial shots to independent films.
          </p>
          <p>
           With indie filmmakers typically opting for cameras in the $500-$1000 range, the majority of which offer dramatically lower quality, the Blackmagic Micro Cinema Camera is surely set to be a hit.
          </p>

          <h1 class="entry-title">
           <a href="http://factor-tech.com/food-supply/18708-fish-feed-shortage-could-provide-missing-link-in-the-bid-to-make-insects-the-food-of-the-future/" rel="bookmark">
            Fish feed shortage could provide missing link in the bid to make insects the food of the future
           </a>
          </h1>
          <div class="entry-meta">
           <span class="entry-date">
            <a href="http://factor-tech.com/food-supply/18708-fish-feed-shortage-could-provide-missing-link-in-the-bid-to-make-insects-the-food-of-the-future/" rel="bookmark">
             <time class="entry-date" datetime="2015-07-14T14:28:51+00:00">
              July 14, 2015
             </time>
            </a>
           </span>
           <span class="byline">
            <span class="author vcard">
             <a class="url fn n" href="http://factor-tech.com/author/lucy-ingham/" rel="author">
              Lucy Ingham
             </a>
            </span>
           </span>
          </div>
          <!-- .entry-meta -->
         </header>
         <!-- .entry-header -->
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           Analysts have called for the development of insect farming to meet an expected shortfall in fish food, providing an unintentional solution to the challenge of making bugs a viable for mass human consumption.
          </p>
          <p>
           Fish farming, or aquaculture as it is otherwise known, is booming, but theres a problem: the industry is exploding at such a rate that fish feed manufacturers cannot keep up.
          </p>
          <p>
           With aquaculture now worth a whopping $17bn a year, demand for fish meal and oil is set to fall short from next year, with the gap widening every year following until by 2025 the industry is short by 16 million metric tons.
          </p>
          <p>
           The solution, according to analysts, is the development of alternative food sources for the fish, and the answer looks set to be insects, algae and recycled waste.
          </p>
          <p>
           However none of these solutions are ready yet: all need to be developed so they can be produced in higher quantities for lower costs, something that requires money and time.
          </p>
          <p>
           The future of fish feed is a blend of alternatives  no single source will dominate as fish meal has, said Sara Olson, Lux Research analyst and lead author of a report on the issue.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, most alternatives to fish meal have unmet needs of cost, nutrition and scale. To take advantage of the coming shifts, companies should find opportunities to address these challenges for these alternative sources.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_18709" style="width: 610px">
           <img alt="Image courtesy of Sid Mosdell." class="size-medium wp-image-18709" height="450" src="http://factor-tech.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/salmon-farm-600x450.png" width="600"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Image courtesy of
            <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/sidm/">
             Sid Mosdell
            </a>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Insect farming, along with algae, has been touted as a future food for humans as well as fish in the past, but production is currently only possible in small batches at high cost.
          </p>
          <p>
           This could be resolved relatively simply with adequate financing, but given the mixed views about such foods future for humans, few have been willing to put their money where their mouth is.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, if the shortfall in fish food occurs as predicted, the industry will need to invest in insect and algae farming in order to survive.
          </p>
          <p>
           Given necessity is the mother of invention, farming solutions for insects are likely to be developed much more quickly by the fish industry than the food industry.
          </p>
          <p>
           Yet once developed, these insect farming processes could prompt an unintended growth in the number of bug-based food products available for humans.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_18710" style="width: 610px">
           <img alt="Image and featured image courtesy of Richard Allaway." class="size-medium wp-image-18710" height="400" src="http://factor-tech.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/insect-food-600x400.png" width="600"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Image and f
            <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/geographyalltheway_photos/">
             eatured image courtesy of Richard Allaway
            </a>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Insects have long been
           <a href="http://factor-tech.com/food-supply/4241-insect-cuisine-the-quest-for-acceptance-of-bugs-as-the-future-of-food/">
            touted as the future of food
           </a>
           , and although to many the idea is deeply unpleasant,
           <a href="http://factor-tech.com/food-supply/7559-one-in-five-people-ready-to-adopt-bugs-into-their-diet-men-twice-as-likely/">
            one in five meat eaters in the West
           </a>
           have said they are up for trying them.
          </p>
          <p>
           But the reason we have yet to see a flood of insect-based products in our supermarkets isnt peoples squeamishness. Its the cost of their production.
          </p>
          <p>
           Some higher-end companies have started limited ranges, or in the case of British restaurant Wahaca, added them to menus as specials, but at present bugs simply cannot be produced affordably to the standards required.
          </p>
          <p>
           If fish farmers do invest in insect farming, however, this could provide the missing link to the process. Of course food retailers may need to add additional procedures to get the bugs approved for human consumption, but the bulk of the R&amp;D would have already been done, making the companies far more likely to take a punt on an insect-based snack.
          </p>
          <p>
           Whether than will be in the form of complete fried bugs, or something blending into a protein bar to widen its appeal, however, remains to be seen.
          </p>

           <a href="http://factor-tech.com/connected-world/18698-researchers-create-system-to-provide-city-wide-super-wi-fi-from-active-tv-channels/" rel="bookmark">
            Researchers create system to provide city-wide super Wi-Fi from active TV channels
           </a>
          </h1>
          <div class="entry-meta">
           <span class="entry-date">
            <a href="http://factor-tech.com/connected-world/18698-researchers-create-system-to-provide-city-wide-super-wi-fi-from-active-tv-channels/" rel="bookmark">
             <time class="entry-date" datetime="2015-07-14T10:25:01+00:00">
              July 14, 2015
             </time>
            </a>
           </span>
           <span class="byline">
            <span class="author vcard">
             <a class="url fn n" href="http://factor-tech.com/author/matt-burgess/" rel="author">
              Matt Burgess
             </a>
            </span>
           </span>
          </div>
          <!-- .entry-meta -->
         </header>
         <!-- .entry-header -->
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           Scientists have created a system that allows data to be sent wirelessly using live TV broadcasts, which they say could expand the reach of super Wi-Fi networks in our cities.
          </p>
          <p>
           Researchers from Rice University, US, have created a device that is able utilise the underused Ultra High Frequency (UHF) spectrum to transfer data.
          </p>
          <p>
           This could lead to the development of long distance Wi-Fi networks that are able to be used across large urban areas.
          </p>
          <p>
           As the UHF spectrum is able to carry signal over long distances, without easily being blocked by walls or trees, it is ideal to provide internet access, the researchers say. The
           <a href="http://networks.rice.edu/files/2015/04/CP-6.pdf">
            newly published paper the says
           </a>
           they can re-purpose the spectrum even when it is within the TV service area.
          </p>
          <p>
           The system, WiFi in Active TV Channels, WATCH, allows a secondary Wi-Fi transmission and exploits the fact that few households are receiving UHF-band TV programming in a given channel, time, and location.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="shutterstock_130544573" class="alignnone wp-image-18701 size-full" height="403" src="http://factor-tech.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/shutterstock_130544573.jpg" width="600"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           Due to the popularity of cable, satellite and Internet TV, the UHF spectrum is one of the most underutilized portions of the wireless spectrum in the United States, said the Universitys Edward Knightly.
          </p>
          <p>
           Thats a bitter irony because the demand for mobile data services is expected to grow tenfold in the next five years, and the UHF band is perfectly suited for wireless data.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its not the first proposed incorporation of Wi-Fi and television technology. Last year
           <a href="http://factor-tech.com/connected-world/9769-scientists-urge-governments-to-turn-old-tv-frequencies-into-free-super-wifi/">
            scientists from Karlsruhe Institute of Technology, in Germany, urged governments
           </a>
           to turn old TV frequencies into free super Wi-Fi.
          </p>
          <p>
           The suggested system by the German researchers would have utilised old TV frequencies around the world which are being made available thanks to a switch from analogue to completely digital transmissions.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="televisionsignal" class="alignnone wp-image-18700 size-full" height="400" src="http://factor-tech.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/televisionsignal.jpg" width="600"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           However, the researchers from Rice University say that the white space  that allows channels that are not being used to be re-purposed  varies not only in different cities, but also across city suburbs.
          </p>
          <p>
           Unfortunately, in the most densely populated areas of the country, where the need for additional wireless data services is the greatest, the amount of available white space is extremely limited, Knightly said.
          </p>
          <p>
           In our most recent tests in Houston, one channel is open in parts of the city and none are available in others. This is fairly typical of a large US urban area.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            The scientists paper
            <a href="http://networks.rice.edu/files/2015/04/CP-6.pdf">
             WATCH: WiFi in Active TV Channels can be found here.
            </a>
           </em>
          </p>
          <hr/>
          <p>

            </a>
           </em>
          </p>
