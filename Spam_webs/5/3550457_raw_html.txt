     <p>
      <b>
       Amazon Mechanical Turk
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       MTurk
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">
       crowdsourcing
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
       Internet
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marketplace" title="Marketplace">
       marketplace
      </a>
      that enables individuals and businesses (known as Requesters) to coordinate the use of human intelligence to perform tasks that computers are currently unable to do. It is one of the sites of
      <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Web_Services" title="Amazon Web Services">
       Amazon Web Services
      </a>
      . Employers are able to post jobs known as HITs (Human Intelligence Tasks), such as choosing the best among several photographs of a storefront, writing product descriptions, or identifying performers on music CDs. Workers (called Providers in Mechanical Turk's Terms of Service, or, more colloquially, Turkers) can then browse among existing jobs and complete them for a monetary payment set by the employer. To place jobs, the requesting programs use an open
      <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">
       application programming interface
      </a>
      (API), or the more limited MTurk Requester site.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Employers are restricted to US-based entities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The name
      <i>
       Mechanical Turk
      </i>
      comes from "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Turk" title="The Turk">
       The Turk
      </a>
      ", a chess-playing
      <a href="/wiki/Automaton" title="Automaton">
       automaton
      </a>
      of the 18th century, which was made by
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_von_Kempelen" title="Wolfgang von Kempelen">
       Wolfgang von Kempelen
      </a>
      . It toured Europe, beating the likes of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">
       Napoleon Bonaparte
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">
       Benjamin Franklin
      </a>
      . It was later revealed that this "machine" was not an automaton at all, but was in fact a chess master hidden in a special compartment controlling its operations. Likewise, the Mechanical Turk web service allows humans to help the machines of today perform tasks for which they are not suited.
     </p>
     <p>
      Workers set their own hours and are not under any obligation to accept any work they do not wish to do. Because workers are paid as
      <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">
       contractors
      </a>
      rather than employees, requesters do not have to file forms for, nor pay payroll taxes, and they avoid laws regarding
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">
       minimum wage
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">
       overtime
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workers_compensation" title="Workers compensation">
       workers compensation
      </a>
      . Workers, though, must report their income as self-employment income. The average wage for the multiple microtasks assigned, if they are done quickly, is about one dollar an hour, with each task averaging a few cents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Requesters can ask that Workers fulfill Qualifications before engaging a task, and they can set up a test in order to verify the Qualification. They can also accept or reject the result sent by the Worker, which reflects on the Worker's reputation. Workers can have a postal address anywhere in the world. Payments for completing tasks can be redeemed on Amazon.com via
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gift_certificate" title="Gift certificate">
       gift certificate
      </a>
      (gift certificates are the only payment option available to international workers, apart from India) or be later transferred to a Worker's U.S. bank account. Requesters pay Amazon a 10% commission on the price of successfully completed jobs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to a survey conducted in 2008 through one MTurk HIT, Turkers
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (June 2015)">
          buzzword
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      are primarily located in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with demographics generally similar to the overall Internet population in the US.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The same author carried out a second survey in 2010 (after the introduction of cash payments for Indian workers), giving new and updated results on the demographics of workers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">

         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A more recent study reports Worker demographics on over 30,000 Workers across 75 studies that have been conducted since 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blog.turkprime.com_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-blog.turkprime.com-9">
        <span>

        </span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The service was initially invented by Peter Cohen for Amazon's internal use, to find duplicates among its web pages describing products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      MTurk was launched publicly on November 2, 2005. Following its launch, the Mechanical Turk user base grew quickly. In early- to mid-November 2005, there were tens of thousands of jobs, all of them uploaded to the system by Amazon itself for some of its internal tasks that required human intelligence. Most of these were related to music CD items.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      HIT types
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (June 2015)">
          buzzword
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      have expanded to include transcribing, rating, image tagging, surveys, and writing.
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2007, there were reportedly more than 100,000 workers in over 100 countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This increased to over 500,000 workers from over 190 countries in January 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awsdevforum_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-awsdevforum-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the same year, Techlist published an interactive map pinpointing the locations of 50,000 of their MTurk workers around the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turkermap_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-turkermap-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Users">
       Users
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Users">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A user of Mechanical Turk can be either a "Worker" (employee) or a "Requester" (employer).
     </p>
     <p>
      Employees have access to a control panel that displays three tabs: total earnings, the state of the HIT and the total HIT.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (June 2015)">
          buzzword
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Total earnings: displays the total earnings that a worker has received from the realization of human intelligence tasks, the gains made by bonus and the sum of these two.
      </li>
      <li>
       State of the HIT: displays a list of daily activities and the daily income, along with the number of visits that were submitted, approved, rejected or waiting for that day.
      </li>
      <li>
       Total HIT: displays information about HIT which have been accepted or are in process (including the percentage of successes that occurred, returned or abandoned and the percentage of jobs that were approved, rejected or pending those presented).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Employers (companies or independent developers that need jobs performed) can use the Amazon Mechanical Turk API to programmatically integrate the results of that work directly into their business processes and systems. When employers set up their job, they must specify
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       how much are they paying for each HIT accomplished,
      </li>
      <li>
       how many workers they want to work on each HIT,
      </li>
      <li>
       maximum time a worker has to work on a single task,
      </li>
      <li>
       how much time the workers have to complete the work,
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      as well as the specific details about the job they want to be completed.
     </p>

     <dl>
      <dt>
       Personnel on demand
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Amazon Mechanical Turk provides access to a crowd-sourced market of workers that can help to complete work on an as-needed basis. For work that does not require significant task-specific training, this can contrast with the traditional costs of hiring and management of temporary staff. For users, it also allows them to find ways to contribute to a variety of different possible tasks.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Quality management
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Amazon Mechanical Turk allows more than one user to send a response to the same HIT. When a specific number of users give the same answer, the HIT is automatically approved. The payments are made only to those that are considered successful. If the result is not adequate, the job is rejected and the Requester is not required to pay.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Price determination
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Users are free to work on tasks that they find most interesting, those they like to complete or the best paid. Requesters are allowed to define the payments based on the desired balance of performance and cost-efficiency. Payments are made in cooperation with Amazon Payments.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       User qualification
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Amazon Mechanical Turk allows for qualifying users before they work in their tasks using rapid tests. The qualifications can be a series of questions, performing tasks or request users to have historically responded to a minimum percentage of their HIT sent correctly.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Missing_persons_searches">
       Missing persons searches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Missing persons searches">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Since 2007, the service has been used to search for prominent missing individuals. It was first suggested during the search for
      <a href="/wiki/James_Kim" title="James Kim">
       James Kim
      </a>
      , but his body was found before any technical progress was made. That summer, computer scientist
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Gray_(computer_scientist)" title="Jim Gray (computer scientist)">
       Jim Gray
      </a>
      disappeared on his yacht and Amazon's
      <a href="/wiki/Werner_Vogels" title="Werner Vogels">
       Werner Vogels
      </a>
      , a personal friend, made arrangements for
      <a href="/wiki/DigitalGlobe" title="DigitalGlobe">
       DigitalGlobe
      </a>
      , which provides satellite data for
      <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">
       Google Maps
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">
       Google Earth
      </a>
      , to put recent photography of the
      <a href="/wiki/Farallon_Islands" title="Farallon Islands">
       Farallon Islands
      </a>
      on Mechanical Turk. A front-page story on
      <a href="/wiki/Digg" title="Digg">
       Digg
      </a>
      attracted 12,000 searchers who worked with imaging professionals on the same data. The search was unsuccessful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In September 2007, a similar arrangement was repeated in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Fossett_search" title="Steve Fossett search">
       search for aviator Steve Fossett
      </a>
      . Satellite data was divided into 85 squared meter sections, and Mechanical Turk users were asked to flag images with "foreign objects" that might be a crash site or other evidence that should be examined more closely.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This search was also unsuccessful, partly due to the limited search area. The satellite imagery was mostly within a 50-mile radius.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The crash site was eventually found by hikers about a year later, 65 miles away.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_science_experiments">
       Social science experiments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Social science experiments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Beginning in 2010, numerous researchers have explored the viability of Mechanical Turk to recruit subjects of social science experiments. In general, researchers found that while the sample of respondents obtained through Mechanical Turk does not perfectly match characteristics of the U.S. population, it doesn't present a wildly inaccurate view either. They determined that the service works best for recruiting a diverse sample; it is less successful with studies that require more precisely defined populations or that require a representative sample of the population as a whole.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt-jr_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mt-jr-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt-pa_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mt-pa-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt-pb_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mt-pb-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt-pc_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mt-pc-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Overall, the US MTurk population is mostly female and white, and is somewhat younger and more educated than the US population overall. Data collected on jobs conducted since 2013 show that the US population is no longer predominantly female, and that Workers are currently slightly more likely to be male.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blog.turkprime.com_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-blog.turkprime.com-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cost of MTurk was considerably lower than other means of conducting surveys, with workers willing to complete tasks for less than half the US minimum wage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt-jh_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mt-jh-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Artistic_and_educational_research">
       Artistic and educational research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Artistic and educational research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In addition to growing interest from the social sciences, MTurk has also been used as both a tool for artistic and educational exploration. Artist
      <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Koblin" title="Aaron Koblin">
       Aaron Koblin
      </a>
      has made use of MTurk's
      <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">
       crowdsourcing
      </a>
      ability to create a number of collaborative artistic works such as The Sheep Market and Ten Thousand Cents
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which combined thousands of individual drawings of a US$100 bill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The work functions as a sort of reverse
      <a href="/wiki/Exquisite_corpse" title="Exquisite corpse">
       exquisite corpse
      </a>
      drawing.
     </p>
     <p>
      Inspired by Koblin's collaborative artworks a
      <a href="/wiki/Concordia_University" title="Concordia University">
       Concordia University
      </a>
      graduate research student turned to MTurk to see if the crowdsourcing technology could also be used for educational research. Scott McMaster conducted two pilot projects which used HITs to request drawings; but unlike Koblin's work, the Turkers
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (June 2015)">
          buzzword
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      knew exactly what the drawings were being used for. The jobs required participants to visually represent sets of words in drawings and fill out a short demographic survey. Although the research would be considered in its infancy, McMaster made several findings which suggest that a
      <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
       globalizing
      </a>
      effect is taking place within visual cultural representations. It is a published instance of this type of online research into
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_culture" title="Visual culture">
       visual culture
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Third-party_programming">
       Third-party programming
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Third-party programming">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Programmers have developed various browser extensions and
      <a href="/wiki/Scripting_language" title="Scripting language">
       scripts
      </a>
      designed to simplify the process of completing jobs. According to the Amazon Web Services Blog, however, Amazon appears to disapprove of the ones that completely automate the process and preclude the human element.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Accounts using so-called automated bots have been banned.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="API">
       API
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: API">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Amazon makes available an
      <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">
       application programming interface
      </a>
      (API) to give users another access point into the MTurk system. The MTurk API lets a programmer submit jobs to MTurk, retrieve completed work, and approve or reject that work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Web sites and web services can use the API to integrate MTurk work into other web applications, providing users with alternatives to the interface Amazon has built for these functions.

       Processing photos / videos
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Processing photos / videos">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Amazon Mechanical Turk is ideal for processing images. Although it is difficult for computers, it is easy for people to do tasks such as:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Label the objects found in an image to make the search easier
      </li>
      <li>
       Select the best picture of a group of pictures (best represents the product)
      </li>
      <li>
       Audit images uploaded by users for inappropriate content
      </li>
      <li>
       Classify objects found in images by satellite
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Data_cleaning_.2F_verification">
       Data cleaning / verification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Data cleaning / verification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Companies with large online catalogs use Mechanical Turk to identify duplicates and verify details of items entries. Some examples are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Deduplication listings yellow pages directories
      </li>
      <li>
       Identify duplicate online product catalogs
      </li>
      <li>
       Check restaurant details (phone number, hours, etc.)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Information_collection">
       Information collection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Information collection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Diversification and scale of personal of Mechanical Turk allow collecting more information that would be almost impossible, for example:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Allows users to ask questions, either from a computer or mobile device, on any subject, so that applicants can give results on these questions
      </li>
      <li>
       Fill surveys data on various themes
      </li>
      <li>
       Write comments, descriptions and blog entries to websites
      </li>
      <li>
       Search data elements or specific fields in large government and legal documents
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Data_processing">
       Data processing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Data processing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Companies use the potential of the template Mechanical Turk to understand and respond intelligently to different types of data:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Editing and transcription of podcasts
      </li>
      <li>
       Human translation service
      </li>
      <li>
       Classification accuracy of the results of a search engine
      </li>
     </ul>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Monetary_compensation">
       Monetary compensation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Monetary compensation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Because tasks are typically simple and repetitive and users are paid often only a few cents to complete them, some have criticized Mechanical Turk for exploiting and not compensating workers for the true value of the task they complete.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieeexplore_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ieeexplore-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Computer scientist
      <a href="/wiki/Jaron_Lanier" title="Jaron Lanier">
       Jaron Lanier
      </a>
      notes how the
      <a href="/wiki/Web_design" title="Web design">
       design
      </a>
      of Mechanical Turk "allows you to think of the people as software components" that conjures "a sense of magic, as if you can just pluck results out of the cloud at an incredibly low cost."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lanier2014_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lanier2014-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the other hand, in one psychological study done by the University of Texas, evidence showed that many of the workers did not complete the task for monetary compensation, and instead did the work for enjoyment and self-fulfillment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt-pb_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mt-pb-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      '
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fraud">
       Fraud
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Fraud">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Nation
      </i>
      magazine said in 2014 that some requesters had taken advantage of workers by having them do the tasks, then rejecting their submissions in order to avoid paying them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_relations">
       Labor relations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Labor relations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Others have criticized that the marketplace does not have the ability for the workers to negotiate with the employers. In response to the growing criticisms of payment evasion and lack of representation, a group has developed a third party platform called Turkopticon which allows workers to give feedback on their employers allowing other users to avoid potentially shady jobs and to recommend superior employers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newscientist-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_systems">
       Related systems
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amazon_Mechanical_Turk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Related systems">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">
       Crowdsourcing
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Amazon coined the term
      <i>
       artificial artificial intelligence
      </i>
      for processes outsourcing some parts of a computer program to humans, for those tasks carried out much faster by humans than computers.
      <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bezos" title="Jeff Bezos">
       Jeff Bezos
      </a>
      was responsible for the concept that led to Amazon's Mechanical Turk being developed to realize this process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      MTurk is comparable in some respects to the now discontinued
      <a href="/wiki/Google_Answers" title="Google Answers">
       Google Answers
      </a>
      service. However, the Mechanical Turk is a more general
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marketplace#Internet_Markets" title="Marketplace">
       marketplace
      </a>
      that can potentially help distribute any kind of work tasks all over the world. The
      <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_human_interpreter" title="Collaborative human interpreter">
       Collaborative Human Interpreter
      </a>
      (CHI) by Philipp Lenssen also suggested using distributed human intelligence to help computer programs perform tasks that computers cannot do well. MTurk could be used as the execution engine for the CHI.
     </p>
