           Column: Universities should defend freedom of speech
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="author vcard">
          <span class="fn">
           By William E Richardson, accounting junior and guest columnist
          </span>

        <div class="entry-content" id="blox-story-text">
         <div class="container" id="paging_container">
          <div class="content">
           <span class="first-paragraph">
            <span class="paragraph-0">
             <p>
              I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it, Evelyn Beatrice Hall said.
             </p>
            </span>
           </span>
           <span class="paragraph-1">
            <p>
             This simple sentence forms the foundation for the right to freedom of speech, the second of five rights listed in the First Amendment to the United States Constitution. Nowhere is this right more embodied in the American culture than the university. At least, that was the case in years past.
            </p>
           </span>
           <div class="p402_hide no-images no-quotes no-pagination view-standard">
            <div id="in-story">
             <div class="tncms-region-ads" id="tncms-region-ads-in-story">
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            A new threat to this right is being brought to the forefront of American culture. Tolerance and its redefinition have far-reaching implications on previously secure rights. The modern university is its prime target.
           </p>
           <p>
            As a conservative and firm supporter of Israel, I find the concept of an Israeli Apartheid Week, with events held at UTA last February and March, very disconcerting. However, just like the Berkeley students in the 1960s had a right to express their opinions on campus, so do those in favor of such a week.
           </p>
           <p>
            While I disapprove of what they say, they do have a right to proselytize their beliefs. This is what tolerance means. You may not agree with another individuals viewpoint, but you do not try to diminish the individual.
           </p>
           <p>
            The modern-day university is supposedly a place where individuals of different worldviews and ideologies can come together and freely discuss differing opinions in a safe and nonthreatening manner. Freedom of speech should be held above all other rights as the most sacred, and as Elliot Hirshman, president of San Diego State University, said in a recent article on Huffington Post, one that we must protect and nurture so our academic communities can continue their important work.
           </p>
           <p>
            So why am I bringing up the obvious importance of the freedom of expression? It is not as if the modern American university is actively suppressing the constitutional rights of its students and faculty? Well, unfortunately, that very thing has happened and is currently taking place on university campuses across the United States.
           </p>
           <p>
            According to the Foundation for Individual Rights in Education,
            <a href="http://www.thefire.org/new-report-u-s-colleges-violate-students-free-speech-rights/">
             hundreds of colleges and universities
            </a>
            actively suppress students and faculty members right to free speech. They do so by imposing speech codes, redefining words, such as sexual harassment, and imposing free speech zones.
           </p>
           <p>
            These may be unconstitutional measures taken against individuals on these campuses, but how does that relate to the issue of tolerance?
           </p>
           <p>
            Tolerance has slowly been redefined in the years since the rise of postmodernism in the years after World War II. Oxford Dictionaries defines tolerance as, The ability or willingness to tolerate something, in particular the existence of opinions or behavior that one does not necessarily agree with.
           </p>
           <p>
            However, a new definition of tolerance seems to be gaining traction in the pluralistic havens of Western liberalism. Merriam-Webster defines tolerance as, Sympathy or indulgence for beliefs or practices differing from or conflicting with one's own.
           </p>
           <p>
            Comparing these two definitions, the contrast between respect for the holder of the view has been replaced by respect for the view itself. Unfortunately, this change in semantics has brought about extensive changes on the university campus.
           </p>
           <p>
            Free speech zones are a common occurrence across U.S. college campuses. These are places where individuals are permitted to freely express opinions and ideas otherwise prohibited by the schools conduct policies. This repression of First Amendment rights is a sharp contrast to the perceived desire for open discussion that universities allegedly promote.
           </p>
           <p>
            Students should be aware of this suppressive idea and freedom of speech should be the primary right a university seeks to defend. In my opinion, this is not a political issue. Both liberals and conservatives should be in agreement that free speech is a right of preeminent importance.
           </p>
           <p>
            Hopefully, both left and right can come together and unite on this issue of preserving freedom of speech in the face of political correctness. If not, what issue can we come to agreement on?
           </p>
