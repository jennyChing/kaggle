&lt;span class="label"&gt;Print&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div class="entry-content" itemprop="text"&gt;&lt;p&gt;In a few weeks, Seattles new, highest in the country, $15 per hour minimum wage will go into effect. Like many liberal policies, it was passed by City Hall with the best of intentions.The only problem is, in the end, it may do more harm than good for many.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Private businesses, unlike government entities (which, in theory, can always raise taxes or borrow), must make more than they spend in order to pay the rent, make payroll, keep the lights on, pay their business taxes, and, heaven forbid, have some left over for the owners and investors who are taking the risk and putting in the long hours.&lt;/p&gt;

     <p>
      Earlier this month, Seattle Magazine
      <a href="http://www.seattlemag.com/article/why-are-so-many-seattle-restaurants-closing-lately" target="_blank">
       asked
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Why Are So Many Seattle Restaurants Closing Lately?
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Last monthand particularly last week Seattle foodies were downcast as the blows kept coming: Queen Annes Grub closed February 15. Pioneer Squares Little Uncle shut down February 25. Shaniks Meeru Dhalwala announced that it will close March 21. Rene Ericksons Boat Street Caf will shutter May 30 after 17 years with her at the helmWhat the #*%&amp;$* is going on? A variety of things, probablyand a good chance there is more change to come.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The magazine wenton to report that one major factor affecting restaurant futures in our city is the impending minimum wage hike. Anthony Anton, president and CEO of Washington Restaurant Association, toldthe magazine, Its not a political problem; its a math problem. He estimates that restaurants usually have a budget breakdown of about 36 percent for labor, 30 percent for food costs, and 30 percent to cover other operational costs. That leaves 4 percent for a profit margin. When labor costs shoot up to say 42 percent, something has to give.
     </p>

     <p>
      Restaurants can take actions to adjust, such as raise their prices, acquire cheaper ingredients, and cut their operating hours and labor force. However, all those actions generate reactions from the public which can still lead to lower revenues for the restaurant and, for some, the decision to close their doors.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Washington Policy Center
      <a href="http://www.washingtonpolicy.org/blog/post/seattles-15-wage-law-factor-restaurant-closings" target="_blank">
       explains
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       When prices rise consumers seek alternatives, a behavior economists call the substitution effect, which results in lower demand for the higher-priced product. In the case of restaurants, consumers have access to the ultimate substitution  they can stay home.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      A spokesman for the Washington Restaurant Association told the Washington Policy Center, Every [restaurant] operator Im talking to is in panic mode, trying to figure out what the new world will look like.
     </p>
     <p>
      Seattle had a foretaste of the effect of the $15 minimum wage earlier this year when Prop 1, which made a $15 minimum wage for those working in parking garages and hotels near Seattle-Tacoma International Airport, took effect. A reporter
      <a href="http://www.nwasianweekly.com/2014/05/blog-seatac-tells-us-15-minimum-wage/" target="_blank">
       asked
      </a>
      a cleaning woman and a part-time banquet server, who work in a hotel near SEATAC, what they thought of the new law:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The cleaning woman responded, It sounds good, but its not good,
      </p>
      <p>
       Why? I asked.
      </p>
      <p>
       I lost my 401k, health insurance, paid holiday, and vacation, she responded. No more free food, she added.
      </p>
      <p>
       The hotel used to feed her. Now, she has to bring her own food. Also, no overtime, she said. She used to work extra hours and received overtime pay.
      </p>
      <p>
       What else? I asked.
      </p>
      <p>
       I have to pay for parking, she said.
      </p>
      <p>
       I then asked the part-time waitress, who was part of the catering staff.
      </p>
      <p>
       Yes, Ive got $15 an hour, but all my tips are now much less, she said. Before the new wage law was implemented, her hourly wage was $7. But her tips added to more than $15 an hour. Yes, she used to receive free food and parking. Now, she has to bring her own food and pay for parking.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
     </p>

      <a href="http://www.seattletimes.com/seattle-news/15-wage-floor-slowly-takes-hold-in-seatac/" target="_blank">
       reported
      </a>
      that a Clarion Hotel recently made the decision to close its full service restaurant (laying off 15 people) and let go of a night desk clerk and a maintenance worker. It also plans to raise its rates by 10 percent to offset increased labor costs.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the April 1 deadline approaches, the residents of Seattle will have a front row seat to the effects of the $15 per hour minimum wage, but early indicators suggestit will not be as positive asCity Hall intended.
