       The startlingly strange strategy of Seamus ORegan
      </a>
     </h1>
     <div id="indy_sublines_singlepost">
      By:
      <a href="http://theindependent.ca/author/hansrollmann/" rel="author" title="Posts by Hans Rollmann">
       Hans Rollmann
      </a>

      <p>
       Newfoundland and Labrador has entered a new era. Some would like to drag us back to the old.
      </p>
     </div>
     <div id="opinion_promote">
      <div id="author_bio_photo_opinion">
       <img alt="Hans Rollmann" class="photo" height="100" src="http://theindependent.ca/wp-content/uploads/userphoto/hansrollmann.jpg" width="70"/>

      <div id="opinion_text">
       <b>
        To Each Their Own
       </b>
       examines political issues impacting Newfoundland and Labrador.
       <br/>
       <br/>

     <a href="http://theindependent.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/TrudeauORegan.jpg" rel="lightbox">
      <img src="http://theindependent.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/TrudeauORegan.jpg"/>
     </a>
     <h5>
      Liberal leader Justin Trudeau and Liberal candidate for St. John's South-Mount Pearl Seamus O'Regan. Photo by Alex Guibord via
      <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/58246681@N03/15173053107/in/photolist-p7MS4V-nvq2GV-8nRsGj-8o2zy7">
       Flickr Commons
      </a>
      .
     </h5>
    </div>
    <div id="indy_single_main">
     <div id="fb-root">
     </div>
     <p>
      Whats the best way for a Liberal candidate to try to unseat a New Democratic MP?
     </p>
     <p>
      Chastise people for not voting Conservative, apparently.
     </p>
     <p>
      At least thats the strange logic of Seamus ORegan. The former journalist and now federal Liberal candidate for St. Johns South-Mount Pearl was
      <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.ca/2014/12/10/seamus-oregan-anybody-but-conservative_n_6301706.html" target="_blank">
       recently quoted
      </a>
      in the
      <em>
       Huffington Post
      </em>
      saying this province was hurt by the Anybody But Conservative (ABC) campaign against Stephen Harper in the 2008 federal election. He continues his criticism by suggesting the province has been hurt by not currently having an elected federal Conservative representative (hey, we actually had one remember what
      <a href="http://theindependent.ca/2013/05/13/more-questions-loom-as-labrador-votes-in-controversial-by-election/" target="_blank">
       happened to him
      </a>
      ?).
     </p>
     <p>
      I actually had to read ORegans comments a few times before I fully understood what he was saying, and why hea Liberalwas saying it. Eventually I realized: Oh! Its a threat. Sort of.
     </p>
     <p>
      The implication of this argument  intended or not  appears to be that voters should vote primarily not according to conscience or belief, nor according to their political convictions, but according to their best guess of which party is going to win a majority (presumably because somewhere there are other peoplebetter people, wiser people, Ontario people?who are the ones actually entitled to vote according to conscience, I suppose).
     </p>
     <p>
      Not only is an argument like this undemocratic and politically immature, it marks a throwback to the era of clientelism voting for whoever you think is gonna be best positioned to get you nice stuff.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its the era of yesterdays Newfoundland and Labrador, the era where you vote for the buddy who slaps you on the back and winks and most compellingly hints that hell pave your communitys roads or upgrade your fishing wharf and if you dont vote for him then hell do his best to sabotage you and ruin your life.
     </p>
     <p>
      Yesterdays politics.
     </p>
     <p>
      Of course, it lingers. There are still politicians who act like that, and there are still people who vote like that. But by and large Newfoundland and Labrador is moving out of that era, as indeed we must if we are to mature and take our place on the national and global political stage. Become masters of our own destiny, as Danny Williams
      <a href="http://news.nationalpost.com/2010/11/25/transcription-danny-williams-retirement-speech/" target="_blank">
       famously put it
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      We can now make choices in our own, vested self-interest without depending upon the handouts of others, who deemed us unworthy of great success and chose to restrict us, Williams said.
     </p>
     <p>
      But if indeed the Williams era was intended to mark our renewed pride as a people, then the message ORegans comments send is, Lets go back to business as usual.
     </p>
     <p>
      ORegan says the ABC campaign hurt Newfoundland and Labrador. Did it? Well, its unlikely that groveling at Stephen Harpers feet and begging for some extra scraps in exchange for voteswhich is basically the alternativewould have done us much better. At least by showing our solidarity and sending a unified message to Ottawa, we upheld our integrity, our dignity and our pride. We showed we were willing and able to unite together in the face of a common foe who was working against our collective interests.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ontario groveled and voted for Harper, electing a Conservative majority to represent the province. Yet right now relations between Queens Park and Parliament Hill could
      <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.ca/2014/12/12/stephen-harper-kathleen-wynne_n_6316146.html" target="_blank">
       hardly be worse
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Moreover, ORegans comments reek of the blame the victim mentality. Why should we blame ourselves for the actions of a government that has caused as much anger and outrage in British Columbia and Nova Scotia as it has in Newfoundland and Labrador? Why are we the ones ORegan holds guilty for Harpers power-seeking austerity measures? Its an odd way to seek Newfoundland and Labrador votes: by criticizing those voters self-respect and democratic choices.
     </p>
     <h2>
      ABC lives on in 2011 election
     </h2>
     <p>
      We ought to be proud of the message that we sent to Ottawa during the past two federal elections. Every province in this country has suffered under the Harper government, and lost jobs, public services, and regional funding. But at least this province retained its pride, its dignity, and its sense of self-reliance in sending a well-publicized and unified rejection notice to the Harper regime.
     </p>
     <p>
      Besides, if this province had not elected four Liberal MPs in 2011, and six in 2008, the Liberal PartyORegans ownwould be in a far worse position today. In fact, ORegan himself might not even have wound up running. I wonder what his potential colleaguesthose four elected Liberal MPsthink of the implications of his suggestion that at least a couple of them should have been defeated by the Conservatives?
     </p>
     <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_6636" style="width: 310px">
      <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-group="40415" data-slb-internal="6636" href="http://theindependent.ca/2011/04/26/photo-abcs-not-forgotten/abc3/" rel="attachment wp-att-6636">
       <img alt="A house in St. John's displaying an &quot;ABC&quot; (&quot;Anything But Conservative&quot;) sign in its window during the 2008 federal election. Independent file photo." class="size-medium wp-image-6636" height="233" src="http://theindependent.ca/wp-content/uploads/2011/04/abc3-300x233.jpg" width="300"/>
      </a>
      <p class="wp-caption-text">
       A house in St. Johns displaying an ABC (Anything But Conservative) sign in its window during the 2008 federal election.
       <em>
        Independent file photo.
       </em>
      </p>
     </div>
     <p>
      The point of writing this is not to embarrass poor old Seamus. Hes new to politics and perhaps hadnt fully thought through his comments and realized (or intended) the message that they send. Its not to criticize the Liberals either Justin Trudeau has turned out to be a better leader than anyone probably expected. But it is to say that the narrative ORegan has proposedthat we should simply bow down to the majority wave in the hopes of getting some scraps from the tableis one that we must resist and reject.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ABC campaign was not just a singular phenomenon. It was a reflection of our provinces growing political maturity and confidence. ORegan said, just as shockingly, that in Newfoundland and Labrador we dont have the luxury of an ideology.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sorry Seamus, todays generation
      <em>
       does
      </em>
      have ideology, and integrity, and pride, and we are willing to stand by all three. This is no longer your grandparents politics: no longer a politics of servility and tit-for-tat and rubbing each others backs and votes-for-roads.
     </p>
     <p>
      Todays Newfoundland and Labrador is intelligent, proud, and not afraid to demand the good things we deserve.
     </p>
     <p>
      If the ABC campaign is to have a lasting meaning in this broader context, then its a gesture we need to remain proud of the fact that when we are treated poorly and exploited by the federal government, or by anyone, we wont cower and beg. We will stand strong and defiant and turn our backs on those who do not treat us in good faith.
     </p>
     <p>
      If they will not work with us, then were better off without them.
     </p>
