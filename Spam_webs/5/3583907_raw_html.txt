             Nausea is a common side effect of cancer treatments, and specifically chemotherapy. While it is important to stay strong during treatment, nausea can make eating difficult. Here are a few helpful tips:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Try to eat small meals multiple times per day
             </li>
             <li>
              Cold or room temperature foods may be better tolerated than hot foods
             </li>
             <li>
              If you are sensitive to smells, try to not be in the room where your meal is being prepared.
             </li>
             <li>
              Separate your solid and liquids in each meal. Eating solid foods first and then drinking liquids may help relieve nausea in some people.
             </li>
             <li>
              If you are nauseous first thing in the morning, eating a little dry toast or crackers may help settle the stomach
             </li>
             <li>
              Drink warm ginger ale or ginger tea
             </li>
             <li>
              Drink plenty of water to avoid dehydration
             </li>
             <li>
              Short answer: Eat small meals, cold foods rather than hot foods, and crackers or toast first thing in the morning. Drink ginger tea.
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>

       <div class="module-content clearfix">
        <h4 class="title">
         <a href="/health/chemotherapy-for-cancer">
          Chemotherapy For Cancer
         </a>
        </h4>
        <div class="qa-answer-text">
         One of three common treatments for cancer, chemotherapy uses drugs to kill or slow down the progression of cancer cells. Different chemotherapy drugs are used for different types of cancers and can be combined with other treatments, like radiation therapy or surgery.
Chemotherapy often causes side effects, such as fatigue, nausea and mouth sores. Most side effects subside after treatment ends; however, some side effects can develop late in treatment and cause long-lasting issues, such as heart and kidney problems, or damage to nerve and lung tissue. Make sure to talk to your oncologist early about these potential side effects.
To prepare for chemotherapy, ask your doctor for tests to check your heart and liver functions to make sure you are healthy enough to undergo treatment. Once youre ready for treatment, you may receive the drugs intravenously through a port in your chest, orally by taking a pill or through an injection into a muscle.
