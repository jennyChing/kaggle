That's the rather bizarre question which has been doing the rounds here and in Britain for the last week following the unusual juxtaposition of Jade Goody and Gail Trimble, the woman who virtually single-handedly captured the coveted University Challenge title for her college, Corpus Christi Oxford.

         <p>
          On the one hand, we have Goody, a former hate figure for many who has now become beloved by many more because of the way she is dealing with her cancer -- one journalist actually likened her to the "new Princess Diana" the other day.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          On the other hand we have brainbox Trimble, who has been attacked as "a horse-faced bitch", "insufferable snob" and "annoying know-it-all" -- simply because she happens to be bloody brilliant at quizzes.
         </p>
         <p>
          Goody has been through enough in recent times, but the two women represent something far more important than just themselves -- they are the front line of a culture war which is waging throughout the Western World.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          On the one hand, we have the celebration of people like Paris Hilton, Big Brother contestants Nikki Graham and Chantelle who proclaim their self-confessed stupidity from the rooftops -- encouraging a generation of young girls to believe that it is more important to be "ditzy" and "charmingly nave" than it is to be academically diligent and well-informed about the world.
         </p>
         <p>
          It's not just women, of course.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          Brian Dowling seems like a thoroughly pleasant chap, but when he was once asked who the first man to land on the moon was, he replied that he didn't know "because I didn't do history in school".
         </p>
         <p>
          Most of us would be mortified to admit that on national television, but then again most of us would rather take a sand blaster to our own eyelids than appear on something like Big Brother.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          Or is that even true any more?
         </p>
         <p>
          While the arrival of internet technology has thoroughly changed how we see the world, it has also thoroughly changed how the world sees us.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          And when you combine that with the inexorable, Borg-like rise of reality television and talent(less) shows, Warhol's famous prediction about everyone getting their 15 minutes of fame has become scarily prescient -- which must please the late, lamented arch-chancer no end.
         </p>
         <p>
          This means that nothing is real unless it has been recorded, spliced, edited and disseminated on Myspace or Facebook, where everyone can pretend that they enjoy a thoroughly glamorous life and are, in their own microverse, celebrities.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          Forget achievement -- who needs to do anything as old- fashioned as study for something when you can flash your breasts at the audition for some hideous programme, tell the world that you have "attitude" (shorthand for being a violent idiot who flies off the handle any time someone has the temerity to disagree with you) and sit back, safe in the knowledge that when you are evicted from the house, lads' mags will be queuing up to pay you 50,000 to pose in a bikini. Which is roughly what two nurses in the health service would earn in a year.
         </p>
         <p>
          And maybe that is why so many people have developed a truly visceral dislike of a young woman they have never met -- Trimble is obviously smarter than them and it brings out the school-yard bully in them -- fuelled with jealousy and resentment and the knowledge that they are simply inferior beings in comparison.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          We all remember from school how hated the class swot was. They were pilloried, bullied and despised.
         </p>
         <p>
          And it wasn't just for any academic achievement -- any deviation from the herd was seen as a betrayal, whether it meant simply listening to different music or wearing different clothes.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          Yet somehow, just when we thought that we had all left those hateful, stupid, pig-ignorant sheep behind, they have now got themselves a modem and are free to revert back to their schoolday selves, spewing venom and spite at those they feel are too clever by half.
         </p>
         <p>
          But let's finally stand up and be counted on this one -- there is nothing cute or ditzy about being a moron.
         </p>
         <p>
          The fact that Jade Goody thought East Anglia (or East Angular as she called it) was actually a different country is not "endearing", it was rather a searing indictment of an education system which has been hijacked by left-wing social engineers who prefer to concentrate on developing the pupil's "self-esteem" than teaching them something as unfashionable as the Three Rs.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          One teacher friend of mine is sick of constantly marking homework assignments and finding them written in 'textese', that hideous form of pidgin English, where letters and numbers combine to form some sort of incomprehensible word soup, simply because the students, even the bright ones, are just too lazy to absorb the correct rules of grammar and spelling.
         </p>
         <p>
          But we mustn't judge, must we? Well, that's what we are constantly being told -- which is why school sports days have tuned into a collective of mediocrity; a medal for every child and a pat on the back for all, no matter how inadequate.
         </p>
         <p>
          It's one of the reasons why the Leinster Schools Cup has come under such uncomfortable scrutiny in recent years.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          After all, should we be encouraging our kids to focus on doing something as hideously Neandertal and macho as actually going out to win something, rather than merely deriving pleasure from simply taking part?
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course we should.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you don't teach people to be winners, then they will inevitably end up as losers -- confused in later life as to why they don't get what they want, even though they have done their best.
         </p>
         <script src="//assets.kampyle.com/clients/1755186/d/k_config.js" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <p>
          Well, sometimes your best simply isn't good enough, and it is that failure to inculcate the values of winning and losing in people at an early age that has led to the Rise Of The Moron, the person whose first response to disappointment is the loser's mantra: But that's not fair. As if life is ever fair.
         </p>
         <p>
          If we're not careful, we'll end up in a society where everyone feels equal to everyone else, and to show superior knowledge is to end up like Gail Trimble -- derided, despised, scorned and mocked by the mob who feel comfortable with someone they can smugly relate to rather than someone who is patently of more value than they are.
         </p>
