  <p>
   <b>
    Explanation:
   </b>
   Sometimes both heaven
   <i>
    and
   </i>
   Earth erupt.  

In
   <a href="http://www.cia.gov/cia/publications/factbook/geos/ic.html">
    Iceland
   </a>
   in 1991, the
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hekla">
    volcano Hekla
   </a>
   erupted at the same time that
   <a href="http://www.exploratorium.edu/learning_studio/auroras/">
    auroras
   </a>
   were
   <a href="http://www.iww.is/art/shs/">
    visible
   </a>
   overhead.
   <a href="
http://volcano.und.nodak.edu/vwdocs/volc_images/europe_west_asia/hekla.html">
    Hekla
   </a>
   , one of the most
   <a href="http://www.crystalinks.com/volcanoesactive.html">
    famous volcanoes
   </a>
   in the world, has
   <a href="http://www.volcanolive.com/faq.html">
    erupted
   </a>
   at least 20 times over the past millennium, 
sometimes causing
   <a href="http://www.decadevolcano.net/santorini/santorini_introduction.htm">
    great destruction
   </a>
   .  

The
   <a href="http://hraun.vedur.is/ja/englishweb/heklanews.html">
    last eruption
   </a>
   occurred only six years ago but caused only minor damage.  

The
   <a href="ap010402.html">
    green auroral band
   </a>
   occurred
   <a href="
http://www.nationalgeographic.com/ngm/100best/wallpaper09.html">
    fortuitously
   </a>
   about 100 kilometers above the erupting
   <a href="http://volcano.und.nodak.edu/vwdocs/frequent_questions/group1_new.html">
    lava
   </a>
   .  

Is
   <a href="http://volcano.und.nodak.edu/vw.html">
    Earth
   </a>
   the Solar System's only planet with
both
   <a href="http://www.physicsweb.org/article/news/5/1/10">
    auroras
   </a>
   and
   <a href="http://solarsystem.nasa.gov/news/display.cfm?News_ID=12955">
    volcanos
   </a>
   ?
  </p>
