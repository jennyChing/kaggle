         <h1 class="post_title">
          <a href="http://www.delphiglass.com/blog/everything-else/ribbon-machine-replaces-glassblowers">
           Ribbon Machine Replaces Glassblowers
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div>
          <table class="maintop">
           <tr>
            <td>
             by
             <a href="http://www.delphiglass.com/blog/author/julie-bedford">
              Julie Bedford
             </a>
             on May 5, 2011 
							in
             <a href="http://www.delphiglass.com/blog/everything-else" style="overflow:hidden;">
              Everything Else
             </a>
            </td>
            <td>
             <span class="maintop2">
              <a href="http://www.delphiglass.com/blog/everything-else/ribbon-machine-replaces-glassblowers#postComment">
               Be the first to comment!
              </a>
             </span>
            </td>

       <tr>
        <td>
         <div class="post_txt">
          <div style="float:left; padding:0 15px 0 0;">
           <img align="left" alt="Ribbon Machine Replaces Glassblowers" src="http://images.delphiglass.com/blogs/P275_250.jpg" style="border:10px solid #FFF"/>
          </div>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <span style="font-size: small;">
             <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
              Last weekend, while killing time in Detroit before a flight, I wandered into the Henry Ford Museum.
             </span>
            </span>
           </strong>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             If you haven't had a chance to visit this monstrous attraction, make plans now. Its incredible.
             <br/>
             I was there for three hours and saw only a small portion of what the museum has to offer. There are huge machinery collections, transportation exhibits and much, much more  planes, trains and automobiles, oh my!
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             I was especially intrigued with one particular machine. The Corning Glass Ribbon Machine. Before the invention of this machine, every light bulb was hand blown. As you can imagine, this process was painstakingly slow and expensive. But then a man named Will Woods came along and changed the world.
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             This is his story.
            </span>
           </span>
           <img align="right" alt="" hspace="15" src="http://images.delphiglass.com/blogs/P275_yellow_sign.jpg" style="width: 222px; height: 222px;" vspace="15"/>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             In 1898, Will Woods was a 19-year-old kid looking to pursue his dream of becoming a glassblower. He traveled to the Corning Glass Manufactory in Corning, NY to learn the art.
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             Thomas Edison had commissioned Corning Glass to manufacture the envelopes for his first electric light bulbs in the early 1880s. At that time, skilled glassblowers called gaffers could produce just two bulbs per minute.
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             Eight years later, Corning Glass began working on the E-machine, a structure designed to automate the glass blowing process. By 1913, the E-machine could produce a whopping seven bulbs per minute, as opposed to two. Next came the F-machine which could produce up to 48 bulbs.
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             By this time, Will Woods was a master glass artist. And by 1921, he was convinced he could take the automation process a step further. He conceived the idea of running
            </span>
           </span>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             a flat ribbon of molten glass
            </span>
           </span>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             over a belt with holes in it. Air would blow the glass down through the holes creating a bulb shape. He purposed his idea to
             <img align="left" alt="" height="168" hspace="15" src="http://images.delphiglass.com/blogs/P275_whole_machine.jpg" vspace="15" width="281"/>
             Mechanical Engineer David Gray. Seeing Woods' vision and realizing the potential impact of such a machine, Gray began construction right away.
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             Before Woods knew it, the two men were testing a machine that could produce 400,000 blanks in 24 hours. This was five times the output of Machines E and F.
            </span>
           </span>
           <br/>
           Will Woods'
           <span style="font-size: small;">
            <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
             machine changed everything. Electricity was soon more widely available and much less expensive. A single machine had literally taken the place of entire factories. The downside? It put a generation of glass blowers, skilled in an ancient trade, out of work. Ironically, in developing his brilliant machine, Woods
             <img align="right" alt="" height="177" hspace="15" src="http://images.delphiglass.com/blogs/P275_blue_sign.jpg" vspace="15" width="237"/>
             eliminated the need for the profession he himself had grown to love and master.
             <br/>
             Want to try your hand at the art of
             <a href="http://www.delphiglass.com/flameworking-supplies/glass-blowing-kits/glaskolben-blown-glass-ornament-kit" target="_blank">
              glass blowing?
             </a>
             <br/>
            </span>
           </span>
          </p>

           <em>

             <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">

             </span>
            </span>
            <br/>
