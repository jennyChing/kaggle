            <h1 class="title dark" itemprop="title headline">
             Web's Inventor Finally Gets Twitter, but Can't Beat Al Gore's Three-Screen Mega-PC
            </h1>
            <div class="byline">
             By
             <a href="http://www.fastcolabs.com/user/chris-dannen" itemprop="byline" rel="author">
              Chris Dannen
             </a>
            </div>
           </header>
           <div class="row collapse">
            <div class="body prose article-prose twelve columns" itemprop="body">
             <p>
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee">
               Tim Berners-Lee
              </a>
              , the British MIT engineer who first proposed and later implemented the World Wide Web as we know it, has joined Twitter. And he is not impressed. "Oops confusing user interfxce [sic]" he wrote in his first tweet.
             </p>
             <p>
              <img alt="&lt;a href=" border="0" class="float-left" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2525/4037653366_a128b7305b.jpg"/>
              Tim Berners-Lee" width="259" height="261" /&gt;Berners-Lee was the first engineer to create a successful connection between an HTTP client and a server over the Internet. Until his work at
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN">
               CERN
              </a>
              , the "Internet" was limited to command-line interaction between computers at networks like that of
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency">
               ARPA
              </a>
              (now DARPA), which first made its ARPANET network
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET">
               live
              </a>
              in October of 1969.
             </p>
             <p>
              Al Gore, who (jokes aside) is also famously
              <a href="http://amsterdam.nettime.org/Lists-Archives/nettime-l-0009/msg00311.html">
               credited
              </a>
              with midwifing the Web, has been on Twitter for some time as @
              <a href="http://twitter.com/algore">
               algore
              </a>
              . Better yet, check out his turbo-nerdy three-screen home computing setup, image courtesy of his
              <a href="http://blog.algore.com/2009/10/dishonest_lobbying.html">
               blog
              </a>
              :
             </p>
             <p>
              <img alt="Al Gore" class="float-right" height="157" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2508/4037670710_cdab15e56f_m.jpg" width="215"/>
              Few other seminal figures in the birth of the Internet are on Twitter.
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Kleinrock">
               Leonard Kleinrick
              </a>
              , a CS professor at UCLA who played an integral role in ARPANET, has an
              <a href="http://twitter.com/kleinrock">
               account
              </a>
              but no tweets. Vint Cerf, Google's chief Internet evangelist and another titular "
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vint_Cerf">
               father of the Internet
              </a>
              ," is a
              <a href="http://blog.twitter.com/2007/12/vint-cerf-twitter-is-future.html">
               big believer
              </a>
              in Twitter but doesn't publicly use the service, deferring instead to use of the official
              <a href="http://googleblog.blogspot.com/2008/09/next-Internet.html">
               Google blog
              </a>
              . Neither do
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_E._Kahn">
               Robert Kahn
              </a>
