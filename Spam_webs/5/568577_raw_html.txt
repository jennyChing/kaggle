         <p>
          <strong>
           <em>
            Note:  This article is in no way intended to diagnose medical conditions.  If you feel you have symptoms of a disease or cancer related issue, please seek medical attention.
           </em>

         </p>
         <p>
          One of the biggest concerns in mens health is the worry of prostate cancer. Prostate cancer symptoms can be difficult for the man to determine if it warrants a visit to the doctor, due to the lack of some men showing signs. A regular examination by a physician is a good practice, to determine if there are indications of the disease. Catching prostate cancer immediately is the key to success.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Symptoms of Prostate Cancer
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          While there are frequently symptoms of prostate cancer, not everyone experiences symptoms.  Signs are often undetectable until a medical doctor examines the patient in a routine check-up.
         </p>
         <p>
          Some symptoms may not show up for the layman to detect, but some men may experience changes in either urinary or sexual function, which may indicate a problem and the need for a check up.  Symptoms that may indicate prostate cancer are:
         </p>
         <p>
          1.	A need to urinate often.  This occurs especially at night
         </p>
         <p>
          2.	Difficulty in starting urination or holding back on urine
         </p>
         <div style="float:left;margin:0;width:346px;">
          <script type="text/javascript">

          3.	A weak or even an interrupted flow of urine
         </p>
         <p>
          4.	Pain or burning while urinating
         </p>
         <p>
          5.	Difficulties in getting an erection
         </p>
         <p>
          6.	Painful ejaculation
         </p>
         <p>
          7.	If blood appears in urine or semen
         </p>
         <p>
          8.	Some frequent pain or stiffness in lower back area, hips or the upper thigh region
         </p>
         <p>
          If you are experiencing any of the above symptoms, it is not necessarily an indication of prostate cancer, however, a consultation with a doctor is advised.
         </p>
         <p>
          During your medical visit, you may experience a work-up, which will involve a few tests.  This is due to symptoms such as these above may be possible indications of another problem instead of prostate cancer.  Disorders or diseases such as prostatitis or BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia) may be the underlying cause.
         </p>
         <p>
          Benign prostatic hyperplasia is the fancy medical term for englargement of the prostate. Every man will increase his chances of having an enlargement of the prostate, as the chances incline every year after a man reaches the age of 40.
         </p>
         <p>
          Prostatitis is caused by the bacterial infection or other factors. This swelling of the prostate can be very painful due to the inflammation of the area.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The Prostate Location and Functions in the Body
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          When dealing with the prostate and diseases or malfunctions, one must first understand how the normal prostate functions, where its located, its development and what it is attached to.
         </p>
         <p>
          Looking at a normal prostate, you will see a relatively small gland, which is only about the size of a walnut.  The prostate is located under the bladder and lies directly in front of the rectum.  The urethra is a long narrow tube that is the length of the penis and allows the flow of both semen and urine.  The urethra runs directly through the prostate and the rectum, or lower end of the bowel, lies just behind the prostate and bladder.
         </p>
         <p>
          Above the prostate gland are two smaller glands that secrete most of the substances that create semen.  These are the seminal vesicles.  They create about 60% of the semen compound.
         </p>
         <p>
          Attached to and running alongside of the prostate are nerves, which maintain control over erectile functions.
         </p>
         <p>
          While the prostate is a key factor in reproduction, it is not essential for life.  It supplies the substances that begin the fertilization process and sperm travel.  The prostate also helps with survival of the sperm itself.  Sperm is not created in the prostate, but in the testes.  The prostate also assists the enzymes that are essential to loosen up semen to help the sperm reach the egg during intercourse.
         </p>
         <p>
          The various substances that are produced by the seminal vesicles and prostate give sperm the energy to make the traveling distance.  These substances are zinc, citrate, and fructose.  Antibodies help protect the urinary tract and sperm from enemies, such as bacteria and pathogens.
         </p>
         <p>
          During adolescence, the prostate grows under the influences of testosterone, its byproduct DHT, or dihydrotestosterone.
         </p>
         <p>
          With the prostate being close in proximity to several vital structures, cancer of the prostate and the treatments designed to rid the body of the cancer, may disrupt normal bodily functions such as urination, bowel movements and sexual functions.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Treatments and Definitions
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Since every man is different, treatments can run the gamut of the medical practices.  Each option should be weighed carefully and understood fully, including the options available, the side effects and how each treatment could affect you and your life.  Men diagnosed with localized prostate cancer today can live for many years.
         </p>
         <p>
          If diagnosed with prostate cancer, you will be asked to consider a few factors in clinical and psychological terms, which may include therapy, your personal level of risk, your circumstances and your wishes for a certain therapy based on risks, benefits, and your intuition, thoughts and feelings.
         </p>
         <p>
          A consultation with three categories of specialists in the area of prostate cancer could include a visit with an urologist, a radiation oncologist and a medical oncologist.  A medical oncologist will be able to give the most comprehensive assessment of treatments and what you can expect as far as side effects and the outcomes of each treatment.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Active Surveillance
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Coming about in more recent years, the concept of watching and waiting has emerged for those that have decided not to undergo surgery or any type of radiation series.  Prostate cancer is watched carefully during this type of treatment, for any type of progression.
         </p>
         <p>
          A PSA blood test and a digital rectal exam (DRE) are normally conducted periodically along with another biopsy of the prostate.  The biopsies are administered at a one-year mark, then during specific intervals along the way.  If the symptoms increase or if testing of the patient indicates the growth of the cancer cells, then treatment will be discussed further.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Surgical Procedures for Prostate Cancer
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          In a more direct approach to the prostate cancer deletion, surgery can be conducted.  This surgical procedure is called a prostatectomy.  Typically, it is conducted on men with the earlier stages of the disease or if the cancer is confined to the prostate alone.
         </p>
         <p>
          Some can rely on the removal of all or part of the prostate, while others may undergo the more radical option  the removal of all of the prostate plus some surrounding tissue, depending on the expansion or containment of the cancerous cells.  Other surgical procedures may be a choice on men with either advanced or recurring disease problems.
         </p>
         <p>
          The most common type of prostatectomies is the radical retropubic prostatectomy.  An incision is made in the abdomen.  The prostate is located and removed from behind the pubic bone.  The surgeon can then reconnect the urethra directly to the bladder so urine has the ability to flow properly.
         </p>
         <p>
          The surgeon will need to insert a catheter into the bladder for the body to adjust to the new set up.  Urine will automatically flow from the bladder, down the urethra and into a bag.  This set up will normally remain in place for about a week to 10 days for the adjustment period.
         </p>
         <p>
          Another surgical procedure is the radical perineal prostatectomy, which is, performed less in recent years.  The procedure requires the surgeon to make an incision in the perineum, which is the space between the anus and scrotum, and the prostate is then removed from behind.
         </p>
         <p>
          In laparoscopic surgery, a smaller incision is made in the abdomen.  The surgeon that is fitted with cameras and surgical tools then inserts narrow instruments.  The surgeon can see the problem areas without having to make a larger incision through the entire abdomen.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Radiation Treatment
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Radiation treatments involve killing the cancer cells through radioactive exposure.  Radiation therapy is an initial option for prostate cancer.  Various forms of this therapy can also be used in more advanced or serious prostate cancer patients, or in those that have recurring prostate cancer cells.
         </p>
         <p>
          An option called the external beam radiation therapy is the most common type of radiation therapy.  CT scans and MRIs are administered to detect the exact location of the tumors to create the ability to place the highest dose of radiation into the cancer cells within the gland.
         </p>
         <p>
          The IMRT or Intensity-modulated radiation therapy gives oncologists the chance to change the doses.  Radiation beams can better administer the dosage directly into the prostate, while decreasing the doses to the neighboring tissue such as the bladder and rectal tissue.
         </p>
         <p>
          Studies have shown that surgical procedures do result in a more immediate loss of erectile function that was followed up by a period of recovery, while radiation therapy results in a slower loss of erectile function over years in men who have good erectile function before the treatment.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Hormone Therapy
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          The hormone, testosterone, and gives the prostate cancer cells food to grow and survive.  Because of this, the lack of testosterone can take away the food the cancerous cells need to survive.
         </p>
         <p>
          Hormone therapy which is also known as androgen-deprivation therapy or ADT, is designed to stop testosterone in men from being released or will prevent it from aiding the prostate cancer cells.  Hormone therapy is a key part in acting on the removal of advancing prostate cancer but is also used before, during and after the local treatments as well.
         </p>
         <p>
          The vast majority of cells in prostate cancer tumors tend to respond immediately to the removal of testosterone in the male body.  Some cells do grow independent of testosterone, however, and can remain unaffected by the removal of the hormone.  The cells that do grow independent of the hormone can continue to grow unchecked.  Hormone therapies also have less and less of an effect on the continued growth of the tumors as time goes on.
         </p>
         <p>
          Hormone therapy is not a perfected design because of this.  It does not cure the disease either.  Some unwanted toxicities might be left over in the therapy as well.  It is an important step in the process of managing the disease and will most likely remain a large part of the therapeutic regimen during at least one point of the fight against recurrent or advanced stages of prostate cancer.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Chemotherapy
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Chemicals are also used to combat the diseased areas of the prostate and surrounding tissue.  The term chemotherapy defines this process.  The drugs can work in a variety of ways, but they are all based on the same method; to halt the cells from dividing and you can stop the growth and spread of the disease.
         </p>
         <p>
          Recently, chemotherapy is being realized as a more active approach to stopping the growth of this disease.  A study in 2004 shows the use of docetaxel prolonged the lives of men with prostate cancer that were no longer responding to the hormonal therapies.
         </p>
         <p>
          Because of these past studies, dozens of new clinical trials are being conducted in various combinations of chemotherapy drugs.  Some are using newer mixes of older drugs and others are trying out combinations of newer drugs.  Trials in this area are also searching to find a more suitable chemotherapy regimen that is more tolerable and effective than docetaxel in men with metastatic disease.  Others are searching for a chemotherapy use that can delay the onset of metastases and other are looking to improve upon the results with docetaxel by combining it with other agents and testing combinations.
         </p>
         <p>
          Several other agents are approved or widely available for use in the prostate cancer battle, including estramustine and mitoxantrone.  Estramustine is a medication that is taken orally, with hormonal and chemotherapeutic components that has an anti-cancer activity and is easily and safely used in combination with other chemotherapies.  Mitoxantrone is an agent that is given by IV every three weeks.  It is known to be a pain reducer from prostate cancer metastasis and is an effective weapon against the battle of cancer.  However, this agent can also cause congestive heart failure, which does limit its use, and regular heart monitoring is necessary during use.
         </p>
