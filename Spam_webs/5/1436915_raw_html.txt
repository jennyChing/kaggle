Just below that channel is a tiny button that you can press with your finger tip. That releases the watch's grip on the band. Press one of them and the link releases its hold on the adjacent link; you can then pull the band apart. If you then press the button on one of those disconnected links, you can pull it right off the band; each link is roughly a quarter of an inch wide. \nThen you just snap the band back together \u2014 you do not have to press the button on the back of the link.\nTo me this seems a smart design, surely one worthy of patents.               <div id="op-ed-banner">
               <a href="/category/analysis">
                <h2>
                 Analysis
                </h2>
               </a>
              </div>
             </header>
             <section class="article-content">
              <p>
               The Apple Watch is jam-packed with technology and innovation. It is the essence of a tiny, wearable computer. But what kind of wearable would it be without those crazy, new-fangled watch bands?
              </p>
              <div class="see-also">
               <p>
                See also:
                <a data-crackerjax="#post-slider" href="http://mashable.com/2015/04/08/apple-watch-review/">
                 Apple Watch is the best smartwatch on the market: Our review
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               From the moment I saw how Apple's custom-made watch bands slide in and out of a special channel in the Apple Watch, and noted how you could resize the pricier link band by pressing a button and pulling the little links apart and off, I was mesmerized.
              </p>
              <p>
               Granted, I'm a watch nerd who has been collecting mechanical and digital watchers for over a decade. Yet in all those years, and in all the decades of watchmaking that my collection represents, I had never seen a band design quite like it.
              </p>
              <figure class="image">
               <p>
                <img alt="AppleBandPatents" class="" data-caption="Illustrations from Apple's Band Patents. From left: Sport Band, Classic Buckle and band Attachment. No Milanese loop just yet." data-credit-label="" data-credit-link="" data-credit-name="Composite, Apple, USPTO" data-credit-provider="Custom type" data-fragment="m!0332" data-image="http://rack.2.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzI4LzY0L0FwcGxlQmFuZFBhLmIxZjY1LmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/de51f83b/106/AppleBandPatents.jpg" data-micro="1" data-width="1404" src="http://rack.2.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzI4LzY0L0FwcGxlQmFuZFBhLmIxZjY1LmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/de51f83b/106/AppleBandPatents.jpg" title="AppleBandPatents"/>
               </p>
               <figcaption class="image-caption">
                <p>
                 Illustrations from Apple's Band Patents. From left: Sport Band, Classic Buckle and band Attachment. No Milanese loop just yet.
                </p>
               </figcaption>
               <div class="image-credit">
                <p>
                 Image:  Composite,  Apple,  USPTO
                </p>
               </div>
              </figure>
              <p>
               Traditional watches employ what is known as spring bars that you slide through a loop at the end of the metal or leather band. Then you attach to the watch by compressing the spring and sliding the bars two end points into tiny holes in the watch body. You have to use a tiny screwdriver to make it work. Similarly, changing the size on a metal watch band usually involves using that tiny screwdriver to loosen and remove links.
              </p>
              <p>
               Normally, all this work is best left to jewelers. But with the Apple Watch, it's child's play.
              </p>
              <p>
               Apple certainly thinks it has something special. The company collected at least four patents just for the band design, the most recent of which was granted on Tuesday.
              </p>
              <p>
               One is devoted solely to the band attachment. It was filed in August of last year, shortly before Apple revealed its first smart watch to the world. Naturally, Apple's design head Jony Ive, who is considered by most to be the father of the Apple Watch, is listed as one of the inventors.
              </p>
              <p>
               The company also has patents on the fluoroelastomer-based Sport Band, the Classic Leather Buckle and the Link Bracelet with the butterfly enclosure.
              </p>
              <p>
               What seems to missing from all these patents, however, is any detail on the invention being patented. Most just note the "ornamental design." For anyone who wants to know how the band attachment works, the patents make disappointing reading.
              </p>
              <p>
               However, we can intuit a fair amount by looking at the Apple Watch's channel and the band attachment that slides into it. Just below that channel is a tiny button that you can press with your finger tip. That releases the watch's grip on the band. You do not have to press the button to slide the band back into place; it just slides in, and there's a gentle click when it's properly seated.
              </p>
              <blockquote class="instagram-media" data-instgrm-captioned="" data-instgrm-version="4" style=" background:#FFF; border:0; border-radius:3px; box-shadow:0 0 1px 0 rgba(0,0,0,0.5),0 1px 10px 0 rgba(0,0,0,0.15); margin: 1px; max-width:658px; padding:0; width:99.375%; width:-webkit-calc(100% - 2px); width:calc(100% - 2px);">
               <div style="padding:8px;">
                <div style=" background:#F8F8F8; line-height:0; margin-top:40px; padding:50% 0; text-align:center; width:100%;">
                 <div style=" background:url(data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAACwAAAAsCAMAAAApWqozAAAAGFBMVEUiIiI9PT0eHh4gIB4hIBkcHBwcHBwcHBydr+JQAAAACHRSTlMABA4YHyQsM5jtaMwAAADfSURBVDjL7ZVBEgMhCAQBAf//42xcNbpAqakcM0ftUmFAAIBE81IqBJdS3lS6zs3bIpB9WED3YYXFPmHRfT8sgyrCP1x8uEUxLMzNWElFOYCV6mHWWwMzdPEKHlhLw7NWJqkHc4uIZphavDzA2JPzUDsBZziNae2S6owH8xPmX8G7zzgKEOPUoYHvGz1TBCxMkd3kwNVbU0gKHkx+iZILf77IofhrY1nYFnB/lQPb79drWOyJVa/DAvg9B/rLB4cC+Nqgdz/TvBbBnr6GBReqn/nRmDgaQEej7WhonozjF+Y2I/fZou/qAAAAAElFTkSuQmCC); display:block; height:44px; margin:0 auto -44px; position:relative; top:-22px; width:44px;">
                 </div>
                </div>
                <p style=" margin:8px 0 0 0; padding:0 4px;">
                 <a href="https://instagram.com/p/2B06RPmxIR/" style=" color:#000; font-family:Arial,sans-serif; font-size:14px; font-style:normal; font-weight:normal; line-height:17px; text-decoration:none; word-wrap:break-word;" target="_blank">
                  This is how you change and resize an Apple Watch band.
                 </a>
                </p>
                <p style=" color:#c9c8cd; font-family:Arial,sans-serif; font-size:14px; line-height:17px; margin-bottom:0; margin-top:8px; overflow:hidden; padding:8px 0 7px; text-align:center; text-overflow:ellipsis; white-space:nowrap;">
                 A video posted by Lance Ulanoff (@lanceulanoff) on
                 <time datetime="2015-04-28T18:23:53+00:00" style=" font-family:Arial,sans-serif; font-size:14px; line-height:17px;">
                  Apr 28, 2015 at 11:23am PDT
                 </time>
                </p>
               </div>
              </blockquote>
              <script async="" defer="" src="//platform.instagram.com/en_US/embeds.js">
              </script>
              <p>
               Apple's link bracelet employs a similar technique for adjustment. 12 links come with a tiny button on the back. Press one of them and the link releases its hold on the adjacent link; you can then pull the band apart. If you then press the button on one of those disconnected links, you can pull it right off the band; each link is roughly a quarter of an inch wide.
              </p>
              <p>
               Then you just snap the band back together  you do not have to press the button on the back of the link.
              </p>
              <p>
               To me this seems a smart design, surely one worthy of patents. But watch expert and author of the new book
               <a href="http://marieantoinetteswatch.com/" target="_blank">
                <em>
                 Marie Antoinettes Watch: Adultery, Larceny &amp; Perpetual Motion
                </em>
               </a>
               John Biggs disagrees. He told me that luxury watch companies like Cartier and Jaeger LeCoultre have had similar band designs for years.
              </p>
              <p>
               "It's cool, but it's nothing new," Biggs told
               <em>
                Mashable
               </em>
               .
              </p>
              <figure class="image">
               <p>
                <img alt="Jaeger-LeCoultreCompressorExtreme" class="" data-caption="Jaeger-LeCoultre Compressor Extreme watch has buttons on the back -- just like the Apple Watch." data-credit-link="" data-credit-name="" data-credit-provider="" data-fragment="m!2f86" data-image="http://rack.2.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzI4LzUxL0phZWdlckxlQ291LmE2M2YzLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/af56a644/91a/Jaeger-LeCoultreCompressorExtreme.jpg" data-micro="1" data-width="843" src="http://rack.2.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDE1LzA0LzI4LzUxL0phZWdlckxlQ291LmE2M2YzLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/af56a644/91a/Jaeger-LeCoultreCompressorExtreme.jpg" title="Jaeger-LeCoultreCompressorExtreme"/>
               </p>
               <figcaption class="image-caption">
                <p>
                 Jaeger-LeCoultre Compressor Extreme watch has buttons on the back  just like the Apple Watch.
                </p>
               </figcaption>
              </figure>
              <p>
               Biggs pointed to the $10,000 Jaeger LeCoultre
               <a href="http://www.jomashop.com/jaeger-lecoultre-watch-q1778470.html" target="_blank">
                Master Compressor Extreme
               </a>
               W-Alarm Black Alligator Leather Men's Watch. It has a leather band and uses a spring bar, but like the Apple Watch it also has two tiny buttons on the back that ease the release of the bad from the watch.
              </p>
              <p>
               It's not exactly the same as Apple Watch's band attachment  but since we know Jony Ive studied horology as he was designing the Apple Watch, it's not too much of a stretch to assume he borrowed the best watch ideas from history in order to make something new.
              </p>
              <p>

                Have something to add to this story? Share it in the comments.
               </em>
              </p>
