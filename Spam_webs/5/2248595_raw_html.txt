          <h1 class="entry-title">
           <a href="http://www.ecology.com/2012/09/20/offset-plantations-risk-bio-perversities/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Offset Plantations and the Risk of Bio-Perversities">
            Offset Plantations and the Risk of Bio-Perversities
           </a>
          </h1>
         </div>
         <div class="clear">
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="left-40">
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="meta-sep">
          By
         </span>
         <span class="author vcard">
          <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.ecology.com/author/guest/" title="View all posts by Guest">
           Guest
          </a>
         </span>
         ,
         <span class="entry-date">
          September 20, 2012
         </span>
        </div>

        <div class="entry-content">
         <h2>
          The New Gold Rush?
         </h2>
         <p>
          <em>
           By David Salt
          </em>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_25823" style="width: 524px">
          <a href="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/Plantations.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-25823" height="340" src="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/Plantations.jpg" title="Plantations" width="524"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Pinus radiata and Eucalyptus nitens plantations in Victoria: leading scientists say better planning is needed for carbon plantations to protect biodiversity, ecosystem services and long-term carbon sequestration potential. Credit: Michael Ryan
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Carbon offset plantations are viewed by many as a new gold rush. But the narrow focus on planting trees to offset carbon emissions has many ecologists worried. How do carbon plantations affect local biodiversity? What about the increased pressure on water resources? Or the potential for introduced species to become weeds in the new area?
         </p>
         <p>
          When it comes to locking up carbon, one popular strategy frequently put forward is the establishment of tree plantations  large, commercial-scale plantations that offer the promise of locking up carbon while at the same time growing timber, a valued commodity.
         </p>
         <p>
          For land owners, its a financial incentive to increase the carbon storage value of their land by increasing vegetation cover. Many say this is a good thing, as it could help restore degraded land and better protect biodiversity.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Scientists are Advising Caution
         </h3>
         <p>
          But a growing number of scientists are advising caution. They say a narrow focus on carbon storage has the potential to create significant negative environmental outcomes if other values such as biodiversity are not explicitly considered.
         </p>
         <p>
          Australian researchers have even come up with a name for it  bio-perversity. They define bio-perversity as the negative biodiversity and environmental outcomes arising from a narrow focus on single issues, without consideration of the broader ecological context.
         </p>
         <p>
          In a
          <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1755-263X.2011.00213.x/abstract" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1755-263X.2011.00213.x/abstract', 'recent article ');" target="_blank">
           recent article
          </a>
          published in the journal Conservation Letters, a group of eminent scientists  led by Professor David Lindenmayer from the Australian National University and the
          <a href="http://www.ceed.edu.au/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ceed.edu.au/', 'ARC Centre of Excellence for Environmental Decisions (CEED)');" target="_blank">
           ARC Centre of Excellence for Environmental Decisions (CEED)
          </a>
           expressed concern that a poorly planned plantation boom could result in needless land clearing, increase the risk of invasive species and damage natural ecological processes.
         </p>
         <p>
          Professor Lindenmayer  whose co-authors include the University of Western Australias Professor Richard J. Hobbs, CEEDs Professor Hugh Possingham and Climate Commissioner Professor Will Steffen  points out that incentives to sequester carbon through establishing plantations are likely to increase as the impacts of climate change become more pronounced and intense.
         </p>
         <p>
          We argue that harmful outcomes for biodiversity  what we term bio-perversities  can arise as unintended consequences from a range of efforts to enhance forest-based carbon sequestration.Perhaps the greatest of the associated potential bio-perversities are those which may arise from ill-conceived or inappropriate large-scale plantation projects.
         </p>
         <p>
          Our purpose in writing this paper was twofold. First, we wanted to put up a red flag on the risks of rushing into plantation development with a narrow focus on carbon. Second, were suggesting ways these risks can be avoided.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_25822" style="width: 300px">
          <a href="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/excavation.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="size-medium wp-image-25822" height="224" src="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/excavation-300x224.jpg" title="excavation" width="300"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Carbon offset projects flagged by the researchers; growing trees can also affect catchment water use and hydrology. Credit: hermetic hermit/flickr. Creative Commons Attribution 2.0 Generic licence.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          The researchers identified three types of bio-perverse outcome arising from carbon sequestration plantations:
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           the clearing of land to establish tree plantations,
          </li>
          <li>
           the risks of plantation trees becoming invasive plants, and
          </li>
          <li>
           the potential for plantations to affect the local ecology, for example, through increasing the demand for water or increasing the risk of fire.
          </li>
         </ol>
         <h3>
          Four Strategies to Mitigate Risk
         </h3>
         <p>
          Climate change, they add, will further complicate any negative outcomes. The news, however, is not all bad. Professor Lindenmayer says the team has identified
          <a href="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/Four-strategies-to-mitigate-plantation-bioperversity.pdf" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'download', 'http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/Four-strategies-to-mitigate-plantation-bioperversity.pdf');">
           four strategies
          </a>
          to mitigate the risk of bio-perversity.
         </p>
         <p>
          These involve ecological risk assessments, full
          <a href="http://www.ecology.com/2012/06/25/deforestation-carbon-emissions/" title="Study Slashes Estimates of Carbon Emissions Due to Deforestation">
           carbon accounting
          </a>
          of ecosystems and proposed management activities, an examination of incentives used to stimulate the establishment of tree plantations, and the establishment of compliance and ecological monitoring programs to detect bio-perverse outcomes, he says.
         </p>
         <p>
          Each of these strategies would deal with the three areas of bio-perversity to differing degrees.For example, in considering the risk of land clearing to establish tree plantations, full carbon accounting assessment of areas being considered for conversion to plantations will be of particular importance.Such an accounting may demonstrate the carbon sequestration value of maintaining native forest or grassland compared with the establishment of a plantation.
         </p>
         <p>
          He continued, Several studies have shown that monocultures of plantation trees may take longer to produce a net carbon gain and ultimately store less carbon in above ground biomass and soil organic carbon than native primary forests, secondary (regenerating) native forests, and multistrata agroforestry plantings.The risk of plantation trees becoming invasive plants is another real possibility of bio-perversity arising from carbon plantations. The most frequently used species in plantations are quick growing trees  pines, gum trees and wattles for example  that are tolerant to a wide range of environmental conditions. When established outside their original range, some of these plantation tree species can become invasive.
         </p>
         <p>
          Bio-perverse outcomes include impacts such as biotic homogenisation, genetic swamping and altered ecosystem processes. These sometimes irreversible invasions can often become costly to manage and lead to significant biodiversity loss.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Reducing Invasives Risk
         </h3>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_25821" style="width: 300px">
          <a href="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/burn.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="size-medium wp-image-25821" height="199" src="http://www.ecology.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/burn-300x199.jpg" title="burn" width="300"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Burning off a harvested tree plantation: plantations could also bring about changes in fire regimes and vegetation flammability. Credit: Andy Graham/flickr. Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 2.0 Generic licence.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          But the threat of invasives would be reduced with appropriate ecological risk assessment, a careful scrutiny of the types of incentives being employed to encourage plantation establishment and appropriate ecological monitoring, both within and outside plantation estates to provide early warnings of invasives were they to occur, suggests Prof. Lindenmayer.
         </p>
         <p>
          The modification of key ecosystem processes is another well-recognised impact leading to biodiversity loss in many parts of the world, and plantation establishment could play a key role in causing it.In particular, plantation establishment is suggested to have large impacts on hydrologic, geomorphologic and
          <a href="http://www.ecology.com/2010/03/18/prescribed-burns-may-help-reduce-carbon-footprint/" title="Prescribed Burns Reduce Carbon Footprint Compared with Wildfires">
           fire regimes
          </a>
          both within plantations as well as in the surrounding landscapes.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thorough, knowledge-based risk assessments of plantation projects and ecological monitoring will be essential for early detection and minimisation of potential bio-perverse outcomes such as altered hydrologic and geomorphic cycles and altered fire regimes.
         </p>
         <p>
          Some locations will be deemed unsuitable for plantation establishment as a result of these considerations. It is imperative that incentives schemes and reward systems have clear regulations to prevent the establishment of plantations in regions that are unsuitable.
         </p>
         <p>
          If the rush to plant trees and establish plantations for carbon sequestration results in a range of other environmental values being ignored, we expect big problems to follow.
         </p>
         <p>
          A narrow focus on carbon may well exacerbate a range of existing environmental problems, contribute to further biodiversity loss, introduce additional obstacles to recovering or maintaining the ecological integrity of environments, and ultimately fail to mitigate the anthropogenic causes of climate change.
         </p>
         <p>
          And that would be a perverse outcome indeed of a venture that was intended to ameliorate the root cause of climate change.
         </p>
