          </li>
         </ul>
         <div class="body-content">
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad" id="tablet-ad-50218">
           </div>
           <div class="action-points">
            <div class="ap-heading">
             <h4>
              Action Points
             </h4>
            </div>
            <div class="ap-content">
             <ul>
              <li>
               A text-based electronic alert system didn't improve outcomes for those with acute kidney injury.
              </li>
              <li>
               Note that acute kidney injury can affect up to 10% of patients admitted to the hospital.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           A text-based electronic alert system didn't improve outcomes for those with acute kidney injury, found a randomized trial.
          </p>
          <p>
           Alerts sent to the phones of providers and pharmacists didn't change outcomes after 7 days between an alert group and a usual care group (
           <em>
            P=
           </em>
           0.88) found the study, which was led by
           <a href="http://medicine.yale.edu/intmed/patr/people/francis_p_wilson.profile" target="_blank">
            Perry Wilson, MD
           </a>
           , at Yale University School of Medicine, and published on Feb. 25 in
           <a href="http://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(15)60266-5/abstract" target="_blank">
            <em>
             The Lancet
            </em>
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "The findings of this study were surprising to me," said
           <a href="http://nephrology.medicine.duke.edu/faculty/details/0437659" target="_blank">
            Matthew Sparks, MD
           </a>
           , an instructor at Duke Nephrology, who was not involved with the study. "As nephrologists, we routinely see patients fairly late in the course of acute kidney injury, and reviewing the medical record often reveals toxic medication exposures or other factors that if caught earlier and intervened upon, the thought is that they would improve the clinical outcome," Sparks wrote in an email to
           <em>
            MedPage Today.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           In this study, the covering provider  --  usually an intern, resident, or nurse practitioner  --  received a text on their cell phone if the patient had an acute kidney injury.
          </p>
          <p>
           The alert text said that the patient had acute kidney injury based on the latest creatinine value, and urged the provider to "take appropriate diagnostic and therapeutic measures." The patient's pharmacist  --  identifiable because of the University of Pennsylvania's unit-based pharmacy system  --  also received notifications.
          </p>
          <p>
           About 2,400 patients, all of them older than 18, were included in the study; 1,201 were assigned to an acute kidney injury alert group, and 1,192 were assigned usual care with no text alerts. All patients were in the University of Pennsylvania's hospital system from September 2013 to April 2014.
          </p>
          <p>
           Outcomes included a ranking of clinical outcomes, maximum relative change in creatinine, dialysis, and death. Participants were further put into four groups: medical ward stratum (44%), intensive care unit (12%), surgical ward (26%), and surgical intensive care unit (18.6%).
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           In both groups, there was no increase in creatinine levels at the time of randomization. After 7 days, 87 (7.2%) patients in the alert group and 70 (59%) in the usual care group received dialysis (odds ratio 1.25, 95% CI 0.90-1.74;
           <em>
            P=
           </em>
           0.18). In the alert group, 71 (5.9%) patients, and 61 (5.1%) in the control group died (OR 1.16, 95% CI 0.81-1.68;
           <em>
            P=
           </em>
           0.40).
          </p>
          <p>
           The combined rate of death and dialysis was 11.7% in the alert group versus 9.9% in the control group (OR 1.22, 95% CI 0.93-1.60;
           <em>
            P=
           </em>
           0.15).
          </p>
          <p>
           Acute kidney injury can affect up to 10% of patients admitted to the hospital, and a previous study has found that it may be
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/Nephrology/GeneralNephrology/43273" target="_blank">
            more deadly
           </a>
           than myocardial infarction during hospital stays. Alert systems have been shown to improve some outcomes, like reducing drug interactions.
          </p>
          <p>
           The researchers noted that there was a possible increased rate of renal consult and dialysis in the surgical ward subgroup during a part of the trial. But the alert didn't increase the rate of fluid boluses or the time to fluid bolus, which should dampen enthusiasm for adoption of alerts for acute kidney injuries, wrote the authors.
          </p>
          <p>
           "It could be that we need a better biomarker that does a better (and earlier) job at discriminating actual kidney injury from fluctuations in serum creatinine from other causes," wrote Sparks. "It is still important to recognize acute kidney injury early and make appropriate adjustments to the patient's medication, fluids, and order appropriate diagnostic studies."
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The authors gave several possible explanations for the lack of benefit. It could just be that alerts aren't a useful intervention because the providers were already aware of the kidney injury or earlier identification had no effect. Additionally, the researchers chose a "very sensitive definition" of acute kidney injury that could have increased the rate of false positives in the cohort.
          </p>
          <p>
           It's also possible that alert effectiveness goes down as the quality of care goes up, or that the enrollment of all patients with acute kidney injury led to a heterogeneous population.
          </p>
          <p>
           Limitations of the study include that it was done at a single site. Responsiveness to alerts might be different at other locations. In addition, researchers didn't use urine output as a measure for acute kidney injury, though several studies found that it might not be as robustly associated with health outcomes as creatinine levels, according to the study.
          </p>
