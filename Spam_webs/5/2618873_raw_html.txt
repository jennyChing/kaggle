     <p>
      A
      <b>
       vertical jump
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       vertical leap
      </b>
      is the act of raising one's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_of_gravity" title="Center of gravity">
       center of gravity
      </a>
      higher in the vertical plane solely with the use of one's own
      <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">
       muscles
      </a>
      ; it is a measure of how high an individual or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsperson" title="Sportsperson">
       athlete
      </a>
      can elevate off the ground (jump) from a standstill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFL_Combine_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NFL_Combine-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]

       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_vertical_jump">
       Types of vertical jump
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of vertical jump">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The vertical jump is divided into two different types:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Young-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Running Vertical Jump
       </b>
       : This refers to a vertical jump after an approach or run to help add energy to the jump in an effort to improve on the standing vertical jump.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Young-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In general, the standing vertical jump is the one that is used as an official measurement for athletes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFL_Combine_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NFL_Combine-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Where_vertical_jump_measurements_are_used">
       Where vertical jump measurements are used
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Where vertical jump measurements are used">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Vertical jump measurements are used primarily in athletic circles to measure performance. The most common
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sports" title="Sports">
       sports
      </a>
      in which one's vertical jump is measured are
      <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">
       track and field
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Netball" title="Netball">
       netball
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">
       basketball
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Football" title="Football">
       football
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">
       volleyball
      </a>
      , but many sports measure their players' vertical jumping ability during physical examinations. In addition, single and multiple vertical jumps are occasionally used to assess muscular strength and anaerobic power in athletes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="How_to_measure_vertical_jumps">
       How to measure vertical jumps
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: How to measure vertical jumps">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The simplest method to measure an athlete's vertical jump is to get the athlete to reach up against a flat wall, with a flat surface under his/her feet (such as a gym floor or concrete) and mark off the highest point he/she can reach flat-footed (this is referred to as "standing reach"). Then, instruct the athlete to take several jumps from a standstill, marking off the highest point he/she can reach. Next, measure the distance between the two. This is the athlete's standing vertical jump and this can be monitored to track any increases in vertical jump.
     </p>
     <p>
      The method described above is the most common and simplest way to measure one's vertical jump, but other more scientifically accurate methods have been devised. A pressure pad can be used to measure the time it takes for an athlete to complete a jump, and then using a kinematics equation (h = g*t
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      /8),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the computer can calculate his/her vertical jump based on the time in the air.
     </p>
     <p>
      A second, more efficient and correct method is to use an
      <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
       infrared
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
       laser
      </a>
      placed at ground level. When an athlete jumps and breaks the plane of the laser with his/her hand, the height at which this occurs is measured.
     </p>
     <p>
      Devices based on United States Patent 5031903, "A vertical jump testing device comprising a plurality of vertically arranged measuring elements each pivotally mounted..." are also common. These devices are used at the highest levels of collegiate and professional performance testing. They are composed of several (roughly 70) 14-inch prongs placed 0.5inches apart vertically. An athlete will then leap vertically (no running start or step) and make contact with the retractable prongs to mark their leaping ability. This device is used each year at the NFL scouting combine.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ways_to_maximize_a_vertical_jump">
       Ways to maximize a vertical jump
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ways to maximize a vertical jump">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An important component of maximizing height in a vertical jump is attributed to the use of counter-movements of the legs and arm swings prior to take off, as both of these actions have been shown to significantly increase the bodys
      <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
       center of mass
      </a>
      rise. The counter-movement of the legs, a quick bend of the knees which lowers the center of mass prior to springing upwards, has been shown to improve jump height by 12% compared to jumping without the counter-movement. This is attributed to the
      <a href="/wiki/Stretch_shortening_cycle" title="Stretch shortening cycle">
       stretch shortening cycle
      </a>
      of the leg muscles enabling the muscles to create more contractile energy. Furthermore, jump height can be increased another 10% by executing arm swings during the take off phase of the jump compared to if no arm swings are utilized. This involves lowering the arms
      <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">
       distally
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posterior_(anatomy)" title="Posterior (anatomy)">
       posteriorly
      </a>
      during the leg counter-movements, and powerfully thrusting the arms up and over the head as the leg extension phase begins. As the arms complete the swinging movement they pull up on the lower body causing the lower musculature to contract more rapidly, hence aiding in greater jump height.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_jump_as_an_assessment">
       Vertical jump as an assessment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vertical jump as an assessment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A vertical jump of 40in (1.0m) or more is considered outstanding. Some athletes have even recorded vertical jumps over 50in (1.3m) which are extremely rare.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vertical_jump_and_power_output">
       Vertical jump and power output
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vertical jump and power output">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Vertical jumps are used to both train and test for power output in athletes.
      <a href="/wiki/Plyometrics" title="Plyometrics">
       Plyometrics
      </a>
      are particularly effective in training for power output, and include vertical jumps of different types in their protocol. In one recent study, training with plyometrics (which included continuous vertical jumps) was shown to increase vertical jump performance to similar degrees in combination with very different resistance training protocols, indicating that the plyometric jumping contributed to the increased jump height more than resistance training. Research into plyometric jumps found vertical jumps to be among the highest in terms of muscle recruitment (as measured by electromyography), power output, and ground reaction force produced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Fatigue has been researched in athletes for its effect on vertical jump performance, and found to decrease it in basketball players, tennis players, cyclists, rugby players, and healthy adults of both genders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">

        <span>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Common_misconceptions_about_vertical_jump">
       Common misconceptions about vertical jump
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_jump&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Common misconceptions about vertical jump">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The most common misconception about vertical jump is that the measurement displays the athlete's ability to elevate off the ground from a run-up, contrary to from a standstill. The effect of this misconception is that many athletes will grossly inflate their vertical jumps. Also, athletes have learned to "cheat" the existing systems. The vertec can be cheated by not reaching as high on the initial measurement commonly referred to as "shrugging one's shoulders".
     </p>
     <p>
      On the other hand, in practical terms, a running approach is often appropriately noted. E.g., in volleyball, defenders at the net waiting to put up a block against a spiker will have standing jumps, but the spiker will have a running approach.
     </p>
