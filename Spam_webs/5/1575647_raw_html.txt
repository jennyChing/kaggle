         <p>
          A few photos of the Breaktime Lounge on Drouillard. These photos were sent my way yesterday from my Dad who caught this while cruising past.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img src="http://www.internationalmetropolis.com/images/2013/0426a.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Some of the metal cladding either came off or was removed. Not sure which, but underneath the old brick and tile became exposed.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img src="http://www.internationalmetropolis.com/images/2013/0426b.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img src="http://www.internationalmetropolis.com/images/2013/0426c.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img src="http://www.internationalmetropolis.com/images/2013/0426d.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I bet there are many beauties trapped under ugly metal cladding. Metal cladding was the stucco of the 1970s.
          <img alt=":)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://www.internationalmetropolis.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/simple-smile.png" style="height: 1em; max-height: 1em;"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Have a good weekend everyone, see you back here Monday. Big thanks to my Dad for stopping to grab a few shots of this place.
         </p>

             In the 80s, this joint was known as Trishas. Today, having sat through hundreds of city council, planning commission and school board meetings, I wonder how local government officials allowed a strip bar to built 25 yards from an elementary school. Then again, I never felt that giving a **** about Drouillard Road was high on anyones priority list.
             <br/>
             Of course, several times a month, on the way home from school, my friends and I would poke sneak up to the door, open it, take two steps into the darkness and then go running. When we stopped, wed talked about all the beautiful naked women we saw  except I never did. Going from daylight to darkness, my eyes never adjusted fast enough to see anything.
             <br/>
             I grew up right across from the Queensmen Motorcycle Clubhouse, which is now a printshop. Not counting the motorcycle club, there were 7 taverns within a two block radius of my home  a truly incredible number  including the great Mongo Murphs, which was a giant, cavernous bar. In our grade 8 local history class at the now shuttered Holy Rosary, we learned that Mongo Murphs once had a different name (cant remember it now) and that it was once the busiest bar in southwestern Ontario back in the days of Ford City.
