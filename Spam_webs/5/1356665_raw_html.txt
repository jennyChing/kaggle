     <p>
      <b>
       John Dalton
      </b>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">
       FRS
      </a>
      (6 September 1766  27 July 1844) was an English
      <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">
       chemist
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">
       physicist
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">
       meteorologist
      </a>
      . He is best known for his pioneering work in the development of modern
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_theory" title="Atomic theory">
       atomic theory
      </a>
      ; and his research into
      <a href="/wiki/Color_blindness" title="Color blindness">
       colour blindness
      </a>
      , sometimes referred to as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daltonism" title="Daltonism">
       Daltonism
      </a>
      , in his honour.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      John Dalton was born into a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">
       Quaker
      </a>
      family at the settlement of
      <a href="/wiki/Eaglesfield,_Cumbria" title="Eaglesfield, Cumbria">
       Eaglesfield
      </a>
      , near the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Cockermouth" title="Cockermouth">
       Cockermouth
      </a>
      , in the county of
      <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">
       Cumberland
      </a>
      , England in 1766.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His father was a weaver. He received his early education from his father and from Quaker John Fletcher, who ran a private school at
      <a href="/wiki/Pardshaw_Young_Friends%27_Centre" title="Pardshaw Young Friends' Centre">
       Pardshaw Hall
      </a>
      . With his family too poor to support him for long, he began to earn his living at the age of ten in the service of a wealthy local Quaker, Elihu Robinson.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quaker_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-quaker-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is said he began teaching at a local school at age 12, and was prolific in Latin at age 14.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_careers">
       Early careers
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      He joined his older brother Jonathan at age 15 in running a Quaker school at Stramongate in
      <a href="/wiki/Kendal" title="Kendal">
       Kendal
      </a>
      , about forty five miles from his home.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quaker_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-quaker-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Around age 23 Dalton may have considered studying law or medicine, but his relatives did not encourage him, perhaps because being a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Dissenter" title="English Dissenter">
       Dissenter
      </a>
      (a Christian opposed to a state religion and mandatory membership in the Church of England), he was barred from attending English universities. He acquired much scientific knowledge from informal instruction by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Gough_(natural_philosopher)" title="John Gough (natural philosopher)">
       John Gough
      </a>
      , a blind philosopher who was gifted in the sciences and arts. At age 27 he was appointed teacher of mathematics and
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">
       natural philosophy
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Harris_Manchester_College,_Oxford" title="Harris Manchester College, Oxford">
       "New College"
      </a>
      in Manchester, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissenting_academy" title="Dissenting academy">
       dissenting academy
      </a>
      . He remained there until age 34, when the college's worsening financial situation led him to resign his post and begin a new career as a private tutor for mathematics and natural philosophy.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_contributions">
       Scientific contributions
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorology">
       Meteorology
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dalton's early life was highly influenced by a prominent Eaglesfield Quaker named Elihu Robinson,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a competent
      <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">
       meteorologist
      </a>
      and instrument maker, who got him interested in problems of mathematics and meteorology. During his years in Kendal, Dalton contributed solutions of problems and questions on various subjects to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Ladies%27_Diary" title="The Ladies' Diary">
        The Ladies' Diary
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gentleman%27s_Diary" title="Gentleman's Diary">
        Gentleman's Diary
       </a>
      </i>
      . In 1787 at age 21 he began to keep a meteorological diary in which, during the succeeding 57 years, he entered more than 200,000 observations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also rediscovered
      <a href="/wiki/George_Hadley" title="George Hadley">
       George Hadley
      </a>
      's theory of atmospheric circulation (now known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">
       Hadley cell
      </a>
      ) around this time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dalton's first publication was
      <i>
       Meteorological Observations and Essays
      </i>
      at age 27 in 1793, which contained the seeds of several of his later discoveries. However, in spite of the originality of his treatment, little attention was paid to them by other scholars. A second work by Dalton,
      <i>
       Elements of English Grammar
      </i>
      , was published at age 35 in 1801.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Colour_blindness">
       Colour blindness
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1794 at age 28, shortly after his arrival in Manchester, Dalton was elected a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Literary_and_Philosophical_Society" title="Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society">
       Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society
      </a>
      , the "Lit &amp; Phil", and a few weeks later he communicated his first paper on "Extraordinary facts relating to the vision of colours", in which he postulated that shortage in colour perception was caused by discoloration of the liquid medium of the eyeball. In fact, a shortage of colour perception in some people had not even been formally described or officially noticed until Dalton wrote about his own.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Since both he and his brother were colour blind, he recognized that this condition must be hereditary.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daltoncb_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-daltoncb-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although Dalton's theory lost credence in his own lifetime, the thorough and methodical nature of his research into his own visual problem was so broadly recognized that Daltonism became a common term for colour blindness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Examination of his preserved eyeball in 1995 demonstrated that Dalton actually had a less common kind of colour blindness,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteranopia" title="Deuteranopia">
       deuteroanopia
      </a>
      , in which medium wavelength sensitive cones are missing (rather than functioning with a mutated form of their pigment, as in the most common type of colour blindness,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteranomaly" title="Deuteranomaly">
       deuteroanomaly
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daltoncb_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-daltoncb-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Besides the blue and purple of the optical spectrum he was able to recognize only one colour, yellow, or, as he says in his paper,
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       <i>
        that part of the image which others call red appears to me little more than a shade or defect of light. After that the orange, yellow and green seem one colour which descends pretty uniformly from an intense to a rare yellow, making what I should call different shades of yellow
       </i>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_mountains_in_the_Lake_District">
       Measuring mountains in the Lake District
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dalton regularly holidayed in the
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">
       Lake District
      </a>
      where his study of meteorology involved a lot of mountain climbing: until the advent of aeroplanes and weather balloons, the only way to make measurements of temperature and humidity at altitude was to climb a mountain. The altitude achieved was estimated using a barometer. This meant that, until the Ordnance Survey started publishing their maps for the Lake District in the 1860s, Dalton was one of the few sources of such information.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dalton was often accompanied by Jonathan Otley, who was one of the few other authorities on the heights of the Lake District mountains. He became both an assistant and a friend.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_laws">
       Gas laws
      </span>
     </h3>
     <table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 210px; float: right; clear: right; margin:0 0 1.0em 1.0em">
      <caption>
       External video
      </caption>

        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">

     <p>
      In 1800, at age 34 Dalton became a secretary of the Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society, and in the following year he orally presented an important series of papers, entitled "Experimental Essays" on the constitution of mixed gases; on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_(physics)" title="Pressure (physics)">
       pressure
      </a>
      of steam and other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapour" title="Vapour">
       vapours
      </a>
      at different temperatures, both in a
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
       vacuum
      </a>
      and in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
       air
      </a>
      ; on
      <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">
       evaporation
      </a>
      ; and on the
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">
       thermal expansion
      </a>
      of gases. These four essays were published in the
      <i>
       Memoirs
      </i>
      of the Lit &amp; Phil in 1802.
     </p>
     <p>
      The second of these essays opens with the striking remark,
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       <i>
        There can scarcely be a doubt entertained respecting the reducibility of all elastic fluids of whatever kind, into liquids; and we ought not to despair of effecting it in
        <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">
         low temperatures
        </a>
        and by strong pressures exerted upon the unmixed gases further
       </i>
       .
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      After describing experiments to ascertain the pressure of steam at various points between 0 and 100C (32 and 212F), Dalton concluded from observations on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapour_pressure" title="Vapour pressure">
       vapour pressure
      </a>
      of six different liquids, that the variation of vapour pressure for all liquids is equivalent, for the same variation of temperature, reckoning from vapour of any given pressure.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the fourth essay he remarks,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       <i>
        I see no sufficient reason why we may not conclude that all elastic fluids under the same pressure expand equally by heat and that for any given expansion of
        <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
         mercury
        </a>
        , the corresponding expansion of air is proportionally something less, the higher the temperature. It seems, therefore, that general laws respecting the absolute quantity and the nature of heat are more likely to be derived from elastic fluids than from other substances.
       </i>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      He thus enunciated
      <a href="/wiki/Gay-Lussac%27s_law" title="Gay-Lussac's law">
       Gay-Lussac's law
      </a>
      or J.A.C.
      <a href="/wiki/Charles%27s_law" title="Charles's law">
       Charles's law
      </a>
      , published in 1802 at age 36 by
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Gay-Lussac" title="Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac">
       Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac
      </a>
      . In the two or three years following the reading of these essays, Dalton published several papers on similar topics, that on the absorption of gases by water and other liquids (1803), containing his law of partial pressures now known as
      <a href="/wiki/Dalton%27s_law" title="Dalton's law">
       Dalton's law
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Atomic_theory">
       Atomic theory
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The most important of all Dalton's investigations are those concerned with the
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_theory" title="Atomic theory">
       atomic theory
      </a>
      in chemistry, with which his name is inseparably associated. It has been proposed that this theory was suggested to him either by researches on
      <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">
       ethylene
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       olefiant gas
      </i>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       carburetted hydrogen
      </i>
      ) or by analysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">
       nitrous oxide
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       protoxide of azote
      </i>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">
       nitrogen dioxide
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       deutoxide of azote
      </i>
      ), both views resting on the authority of
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Thomson_(chemist)" title="Thomas Thomson (chemist)">
       Thomas Thomson
      </a>
      . However, a study of Dalton's own laboratory notebooks, discovered in the rooms of the Lit &amp; Phil,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      concluded that so far from Dalton being led by his search for an explanation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Law_of_multiple_proportions" title="Law of multiple proportions">
       law of multiple proportions
      </a>
      to the idea that chemical combination consists in the interaction of atoms of definite and characteristic weight, the idea of atoms arose in his mind as a purely physical concept, forced upon him by study of the physical properties of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      and other gases. The first published indications of this idea are to be found at the end of his paper on the absorption of gases already mentioned, which was read on 21 October 1803, though not published until 1805. Here he says:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       <i>
        Why does not water admit its bulk of every kind of gas alike? This question I have duly considered, and though I am not able to satisfy myself completely I am nearly persuaded that the circumstance depends on the weight and number of the ultimate particles of the several gases
       </i>
       .
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The main points of Dalton's atomic theory were:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Elements are made of extremely small particles called
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atoms
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Atoms of a given element are identical in size, mass, and other properties; atoms of different elements differ in size, mass, and other properties.
      </li>
      <li>
       Atoms cannot be subdivided, created, or destroyed.
      </li>
      <li>
       Atoms of different elements combine in simple whole-number ratios to form
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">
        chemical compounds
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">
        chemical reactions
       </a>
       , atoms are combined, separated, or rearranged.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Dalton proposed an additional "rule of greatest simplicity" that created controversy, since it could not be independently confirmed.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       When atoms combine in only one ratio, "..it must be presumed to be a binary one, unless some cause appear to the contrary".
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This was merely an assumption, derived from faith in the simplicity of nature. No evidence was then available to scientists to deduce how many atoms of each element combine to form compound molecules. But this or some other such rule was absolutely necessary to any incipient theory, since one needed an assumed molecular formula in order to calculate relative atomic weights. In any case, Dalton's "rule of greatest simplicity" caused him to assume that the formula for water was OH and
      <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
       ammonia
      </a>
      was NH, quite different from our modern understanding (H
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      O, NH
      <sub>
       3
      </sub>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite the uncertainty at the heart of Dalton's atomic theory, the principles of the theory survived. To be sure, the conviction that atoms cannot be subdivided, created, or destroyed into smaller particles when they are combined, separated, or rearranged in chemical reactions is inconsistent with the existence of
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
       nuclear fusion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">
       nuclear fission
      </a>
      , but such processes are nuclear reactions and not chemical reactions. In addition, the idea that all atoms of a given element are identical in their physical and chemical properties is not precisely true, as we now know that different
      <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
       isotopes
      </a>
      of an element have slightly varying weights. However, Dalton had created a theory of immense power and importance. Indeed, Dalton's innovation was fully as important for the future of the science as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antoine_Laurent_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Laurent Lavoisier">
       Antoine Laurent Lavoisier
      </a>
      's oxygen-based chemistry had been.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Atomic_weights">
       Atomic weights
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dalton proceeded to print his first published table of relative
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">
       atomic weights
      </a>
      . Six elements appear in this table, namely hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, sulfur, and phosphorus, with the atom of hydrogen conventionally assumed to weigh 1. Dalton provided no indication in this first paper how he had arrived at these numbers.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      However, in his laboratory notebook under the date 6 September 1803
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      there appears a list in which he sets out the relative weights of the atoms of a number of elements, derived from analysis of water,
      <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
       ammonia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      , etc. by chemists of the time.
     </p>
     <p>
      It appears, then, that confronted with the problem of calculating the relative diameter of the atoms of which, he was convinced, all gases were made, he used the results of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_analysis" title="Chemical analysis">
       chemical analysis
      </a>
      . Assisted by the assumption that combination always takes place in the simplest possible way, he thus arrived at the idea that chemical combination takes place between particles of different weights, and it was this which differentiated his theory from the historic speculations of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       Greeks
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">
       Democritus
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">
       Lucretius
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The extension of this idea to substances in general necessarily led him to the
      <a href="/wiki/Law_of_multiple_proportions" title="Law of multiple proportions">
       law of multiple proportions
      </a>
      , and the comparison with experiment brilliantly confirmed his deduction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It may be noted that in a paper on the proportion of the gases or elastic fluids constituting the atmosphere, read by him in November 1802, the law of multiple proportions appears to be anticipated in the words: "The elements of oxygen may combine with a certain portion of nitrous gas or with twice that portion, but with no intermediate quantity", but there is reason to suspect that this sentence may have been added some time after the reading of the paper, which was not published until 1805.
     </p>
     <p>
      Compounds were listed as binary, ternary, quaternary, etc. (molecules composed of two, three, four, etc. atoms) in the
      <i>
       New System of Chemical Philosophy
      </i>
      depending on the number of atoms a compound had in its simplest, empirical form.
     </p>
     <p>
      He hypothesized the structure of compounds can be represented in whole number ratios. So, one atom of element X combining with one atom of element Y is a binary compound. Furthermore, one atom of element X combining with two elements of Y or vice versa, is a ternary compound. Many of the first compounds listed in the
      <i>
       New System of Chemical Philosophy
      </i>
      correspond to modern views, although many others do not.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_New_System_of_Chemical_Philosophy_fp.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="536" data-file-width="300" height="393" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/A_New_System_of_Chemical_Philosophy_fp.jpg/220px-A_New_System_of_Chemical_Philosophy_fp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/97/A_New_System_of_Chemical_Philosophy_fp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/97/A_New_System_of_Chemical_Philosophy_fp.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_New_System_of_Chemical_Philosophy_fp.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Various atoms and
        <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
         molecules
        </a>
        as depicted in John Dalton's
        <i>
         A New System of Chemical Philosophy
        </i>
        (1808).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Dalton used his own symbols to visually represent the atomic structure of compounds. These were depicted in the
      <i>
       New System of Chemical Philosophy
      </i>
      , where Dalton listed twenty elements and seventeen simple molecules.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_investigations">
       Other investigations
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dalton published papers on such diverse topics as rain and dew and the origin of springs (hydrosphere); on heat, the color of the sky, steam, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Specular_reflection" title="Specular reflection">
       reflection
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
       refraction
      </a>
      of light; and on the grammatical subjects of the
      <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
       auxiliary verbs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
       participles
      </a>
      of the English language.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_approach">
       Experimental approach
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As an investigator, Dalton was often content with rough and
      <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">
       inaccurate
      </a>
      instruments, though better ones were obtainable. Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">
       Humphry Davy
      </a>
      described him as "a very coarse experimenter", who almost always found the results he required, trusting to his head rather than his hands. On the other hand, historians who have replicated some of his crucial experiments have confirmed Dalton's skill and precision.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the preface to the second part of Volume I of his
      <i>
       New System
      </i>
      , he says he had so often been misled by taking for granted the results of others that he determined to write "as little as possible but what I can attest by my own experience", but this independence he carried so far that it sometimes resembled lack of receptivity. Thus he distrusted, and probably never fully accepted, Gay-Lussac's conclusions as to the combining volumes of gases. He held unconventional views on
      <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
       chlorine
      </a>
      . Even after its elementary character had been settled by Davy, he persisted in using the atomic weights he himself had adopted, even when they had been superseded by the more accurate determinations of other chemists. He always objected to the chemical notation devised by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jns Jakob Berzelius">
       Jns Jakob Berzelius
      </a>
      , although most thought that it was much simpler and more convenient than his own cumbersome system of circular symbols.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_publications">
       Other publications
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rees%27s_Cyclop%C3%A6dia" title="Rees's Cyclopdia">
        Rees's Cyclopdia
       </a>
      </i>
      Dalton contributed articles on Chemistry and Meteorology, but the topics are not known.
     </p>
     <p>
      He contributed 117
      <i>
       Memoirs of the Literary and Philosophical Society of Manchester
      </i>
      , from 1817 until his death in 1840, while president of that organization. Of these the earlier are the most important. In one of them, read in 1814, he explains the principles of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volumetric_analysis" title="Volumetric analysis">
       volumetric analysis
      </a>
      , in which he was one of the earliest workers. In 1840 a paper on the
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">
       phosphates
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">
       arsenates
      </a>
      , often regarded as a weaker work, was refused by the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">
       Royal Society
      </a>
      , and he was so incensed that he published it himself. He took the same course soon afterwards with four other papers, two of which (
      <i>
       On the quantity of
       <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">
        acids
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">
        bases
       </a>
       and salts in different varieties of salts
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       On a new and easy method of analysing sugar
      </i>
      ) contain his discovery, regarded by him as second in importance only to the atomic theory, that certain
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhydrate" title="Anhydrate">
       anhydrates
      </a>
      , when dissolved in water, cause no increase in its volume, his inference being that the salt enters into the pores of the water.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_life">
       Public life
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Before he had propounded the atomic theory, he had already attained a considerable scientific reputation. In 1803, he was chosen to give a course of lectures on natural philosophy at the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution" title="Royal Institution">
       Royal Institution
      </a>
      in London, and he delivered another course of lectures there in 18091810. However, some witnesses reported that he was deficient in the qualities that make an attractive lecturer, being harsh and indistinct in voice, ineffective in the treatment of his subject, and singularly wanting in the language and power of illustration.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1810, Sir Humphry Davy asked him to offer himself as a candidate for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellowship_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellowship of the Royal Society">
       fellowship of the Royal Society
      </a>
      , but Dalton declined, possibly for financial reasons. However, in 1822 he was proposed without his knowledge, and on election paid the usual fee. Six years previously he had been made a corresponding member of the French
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_Sciences" title="Acadmie des Sciences">
       Acadmie des Sciences
      </a>
      , and in 1830 he was elected as one of its eight foreign associates in place of Davy. In 1833, at age 67
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grey,_2nd_Earl_Grey" title="Charles Grey, 2nd Earl Grey">
       Earl Grey
      </a>
      's government conferred on him a pension of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBP" title="GBP">
       
      </a>
      150, raised in 1836 to 300. He was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">
       American Academy of Arts and Sciences
      </a>
      in 1834 at age 68.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AAAS-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A young
      <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">
       James Prescott Joule
      </a>
      , who later studied and published (1843) on the nature of heat and its relationship to mechanical work, was a famous pupil of Dalton in his last years.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips,_1835.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="623" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips%2C_1835.jpg/190px-John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips%2C_1835.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips%2C_1835.jpg/285px-John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips%2C_1835.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips%2C_1835.jpg/380px-John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips%2C_1835.jpg 2x" width="190"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Dalton_by_Thomas_Phillips,_1835.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Dalton in later life by
        <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Phillips" title="Thomas Phillips">
         Thomas Phillips
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery,_London" title="National Portrait Gallery, London">
         National Portrait Gallery, London
        </a>
        (1835).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Dalton never married and had only a few close friends. All in all as a Quaker he lived a modest and unassuming life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For twenty-six years until Dalton's death, he lived in a room in the home of the Rev. W. Johns, a published botanist, and his wife in George Street, Manchester. Dalton and Johns died in the same year - 1844. Dalton's daily round of laboratory work and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tutoring" title="Tutoring">
       tutoring
      </a>
      in Manchester was broken only by annual excursions to the Lake District and occasional visits to London. In 1822 he paid a short visit to Paris, where he met many distinguished resident men of science. He attended several of the earlier meetings of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">
       British Association
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">
       York
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">
       Oxford
      </a>
      , Dublin and
      <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">
       Bristol
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Disability_and_death">
       Disability and death
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1076" data-file-width="702" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg/190px-Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg/285px-Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg/380px-Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg 2x" width="190"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dalton_John_Chantrey_bust.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Bust of Dalton by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_Legatt_Chantrey" title="Francis Legatt Chantrey">
         Francis Legatt Chantrey
        </a>
        , 1854
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Dalton suffered a minor stroke in 1837, and a second one in 1838 left him with a speech impairment, though he remained able to perform experiments. In May 1844 he had yet another stroke; on 26 July 1844 he recorded with trembling hand his last meteorological observation. On 27 July 1844, in Manchester, Dalton fell from his bed and was found lifeless by his attendant.
     </p>
     <p>
      Dalton was accorded a civic funeral with full honours. His body was laid in state in
      <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Town_Hall" title="Manchester Town Hall">
       Manchester Town Hall
      </a>
      for four days and more than 40,000 people filed past his coffin. The funeral procession included representatives of the citys major civic, commercial, and scientific bodies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcbio_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bbcbio-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was buried in Manchester in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ardwick_cemetery" title="Ardwick cemetery">
       Ardwick cemetery
      </a>
      . The cemetery is now a playing field, but pictures of the original grave may be found in published materials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A bust of Dalton, by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francis_Legatt_Chantrey" title="Francis Legatt Chantrey">
        Chantrey
       </a>
       , was publicly subscribed for
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         21
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       and placed in the entrance hall of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Manchester_Institution" title="Royal Manchester Institution">
        Royal Manchester Institution
       </a>
       . Chantrey also crafted a large statue of Dalton: it was erected while Dalton was still alive and it has been said: "
       <i>
        He is probably the only scientist who got a statue in his lifetime
       </i>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcbio_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbcbio-18">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         18
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was placed in
       <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Town_Hall" title="Manchester Town Hall">
        Manchester Town Hall
       </a>
       after its construction in 1877 and remains there today.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In honour of Dalton's work, many chemists and biochemists use the (as yet unofficial) designation
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">
        dalton
       </a>
       (abbreviated Da) to denote one
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">
        atomic mass unit
       </a>
       (1/12 the weight of a neutral atom of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">
        carbon-12
       </a>
       ).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       There is a John Dalton Street connecting
       <a href="/wiki/Deansgate" title="Deansgate">
        Deansgate
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Square,_Manchester" title="Albert Square, Manchester">
        Albert Square
       </a>
       in the centre of Manchester.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Metropolitan_University" title="Manchester Metropolitan University">
        Manchester Metropolitan University
       </a>
       named a building after John Dalton; it is occupied by the Faculty of Science and Engineering. A statue of Dalton, the work of William Theed, was erected in Piaccadily in 1855, and moved in 1966 to outside this building.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Dalton_statue_Manchester_City_Hall_20051020.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Dalton_statue_Manchester_City_Hall_20051020.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Statue of Dalton, by
        <a href="/wiki/Francis_Leggatt_Chantrey" title="Francis Leggatt Chantrey">
         Francis Leggatt Chantrey
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Town_Hall" title="Manchester Town Hall">
         Manchester Town Hall
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">
        University of Manchester
       </a>
       has a hall of residence called
       <a href="/wiki/Dalton-Ellis_Hall#Dalton_Hall" title="Dalton-Ellis Hall">
        Dalton Hall
       </a>
       ; it also established two Dalton Chemical Scholarships, two Dalton Mathematical Scholarships, and a Dalton Prize for Natural History. There is a Dalton Medal, awarded thus far only twelve times by the Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalton_Township,_Ontario" title="Dalton Township, Ontario">
        Dalton Township
       </a>
       in southern Ontario was named for Dalton. It 2001 the name was lost when the township was absorbed into the City of
       <a href="/wiki/Kawartha_Lakes" title="Kawartha Lakes">
        Kawartha Lakes
       </a>
       ; however in 2002 the Dalton name was affixed a massive new park there:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalton_Digby_Wildlands_Provincial_Park" title="Dalton Digby Wildlands Provincial Park">
        Dalton Digby Wildlands Provincial Park
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A lunar crater was named after Dalton.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       "Daltonism" became a common term for colour blindness and "Daltonien" is the actual French word for "colour blind".
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The inorganic section of the UK's
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">
        Royal Society of Chemistry
       </a>
       is named after Dalton (Dalton Division), and the Society's academic journal for inorganic chemistry also bears his name (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dalton_Transactions" title="Dalton Transactions">
         Dalton Transactions
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Many Quaker schools name buildings after Dalton: for example, one of the school houses in Coram House, the primary sector of
       <a href="/wiki/Ackworth_School" title="Ackworth School">
        Ackworth School
       </a>
       , is called Dalton.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Much of his written works were collected at the Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society, but were damaged during a bombing on 24 December 1940. This event prompted
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
        Isaac Asimov
       </a>
       to say, "John Dalton's records, carefully preserved for a century, were destroyed during the World War II
       <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Blitz" title="Manchester Blitz">
        bombing of Manchester
       </a>
       . It is not only the living who are killed in war". The damaged papers are now in the
       <a href="/wiki/John_Rylands_Library" title="John Rylands Library">
        John Rylands Library
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
