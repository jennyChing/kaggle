   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Nanoimprint lithography
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">

      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_Lithography&amp;redirect=no" title="Nanoimprint Lithography">

     </span>
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Nanoimprint lithography
      </b>
      is a method of fabricating nanometer scale patterns. It is a simple
      <a href="/wiki/Nanolithography" title="Nanolithography">
       nanolithography
      </a>
      process with low cost, high throughput and high resolution. It creates patterns by mechanical deformation of imprint resist and subsequent processes. The imprint resist is typically a
      <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">
       monomer
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">
       polymer
      </a>
      formulation that is cured by heat or UV light during the imprinting. Adhesion between the resist and the template is controlled to allow proper release.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term "Nanoimprint Lithography" (
      <b>
       NIL
      </b>
      ) was coined in the scientific literature in 1996, when Prof.
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.princeton.edu/~chouweb/choubio.html" rel="nofollow">
       Stephen Chou
      </a>
      and his students published a report in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">
        Science
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chou1996_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chou1996-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although
      <b>
       hot embossing
      </b>
      (now taken as a synonym of NIL) of thermoplastics had been appearing in the patent literature for a few years already. Soon after the
      <i>
       science
      </i>
      paper, many researchers developed different variations and implementations. At this point, nanoimprint lithography has been added to the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Technology_Roadmap_for_Semiconductors" title="International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors">
       International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors
      </a>
      (ITRS) for the
      <a href="/wiki/32_nanometer" title="32 nanometer">
       32
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/22_nanometer" title="22 nanometer">
       22
      </a>
      nm nodes.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Processes">
       Processes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Processes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are many different types of nanoimprint lithography, but three of them are most important: thermoplastic nanoimprint lithography, photo nanoimprint lithography and resist-free direct thermal nanoimprint lithography.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoplastic_nanoimprint_lithography">
       Thermoplastic nanoimprint lithography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermoplastic nanoimprint lithography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Thermoplastic nanoimprint lithography (T-NIL) is the earliest nanoimprint lithography developed by Prof. Stephen Chou's group. In a standard T-NIL process, a thin layer of imprint resist (thermoplastic polymer) is
      <a href="/wiki/Spin_coating" title="Spin coating">
       spin coated
      </a>
      onto the sample substrate. Then the mold, which has predefined topological patterns, is brought into contact with the sample and they are pressed together under certain pressure. When heated up above the glass transition temperature of the polymer, the pattern on the mold is pressed into the softened polymer film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chou1996_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chou1996-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After being cooled down, the mold is separated from the sample and the pattern resist is left on the substrate. A pattern transfer process (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactive_ion_etching" title="Reactive ion etching">
       reactive ion etching
      </a>
      , normally) can be used to transfer the pattern in the resist to the underneath substrate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chou1996_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chou1996-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Alternatively,
      <a href="/wiki/Cold_welding" title="Cold welding">
       cold welding
      </a>
      between two metal surfaces could also transfer low-dimensional nanostructured metal without heating (especially for critical sizes less than ~10nm),.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Three-dimensional structures can be fabricated by repeating this procedure. The cold welding approach has the advantage of reducing surface contact contamination or defect due to no heating process, which is a main problem in the latest development and fabrication of organic electronic devices as well as novel solar cells.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Photo_nanoimprint_lithography">
       Photo nanoimprint lithography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Photo nanoimprint lithography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In photo nanoimprint lithography (P-NIL), a photo(UV)
      <a href="/wiki/Curing_(chemistry)" title="Curing (chemistry)">
       curable
      </a>
      liquid resist is applied to the sample substrate and the mold is normally made of transparent material like fused silica. After the mold and the substrate are pressed together, the resist is cured in UV light and becomes solid. After mold separation, a similar pattern transfer process can be used to transfer the pattern in resist onto the underneath material. The use of a UV-transparent mold is difficult in a vacuum, because a vacuum chuck to hold the mold would not be possible.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Resist-free_direct_thermal_nanoimprint_lithography">
       Resist-free direct thermal nanoimprint lithography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Resist-free direct thermal nanoimprint lithography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Different from the aforementioned nanoimprint methods, resist-free direct thermal nanoimprint does not require an extra etching step to transfer patterns from imprint resists to the device layer.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a typical process, photoresist patterns are first defined using photolithography. A polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) elastomer stamp is subsequently replica molded from the resist patterns. Further, a single-step nanoimprint directly molds thin film materials into desired device geometries under pressure at elevated temperatures. The imprinted materials should have suitable softening characteristics in order to fill up the pattern. Amorphous semiconductors (for example
      <a href="/wiki/Chalcogenide_glass" title="Chalcogenide glass">
       Chalcogenide glass
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) demonstrating high refractive index and wide transparent window are ideal materials for the imprint of optical/photonic device.
     </p>
     <p>
      This direct imprint patterning approach offers a monolithic integration alternative with potentially improved throughput and yield, and may also enables roll-to-roll processing of devices over large substrate areas inaccessible using conventional lithographic patterning methods.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Schemes">
       Schemes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Schemes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Full_wafer_nanoimprint">
       Full wafer nanoimprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Full wafer nanoimprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In a full wafer nanoimprint scheme, all the patterns are contained in a single nanoimprint field and will be transferred in a single imprint step. This allows a high throughput and uniformity. An at least 8-inch (203mm) diameter full-wafer nanoimprint with high fidelity is possible.
     </p>
     <p>
      To ensure the pressure and pattern uniformities of full wafer nanoimprint processes and prolong the mold lifetime, a pressing method utilizing isotropic fluid pressure, named Air Cushion Press (ACP)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      by its inventors, is developed and being used by commercial nanoimprint systems.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Step_and_repeat_nanoimprint">
       Step and repeat nanoimprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Step and repeat nanoimprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Nanoimprint can be performed in a way similar to the step and repeat optical lithography. The imprint field (die) is typically much smaller than the full wafer nanoimprint field. The die is repeatedly imprinted to the substrate with certain step size. This scheme is good for nanoimprint mold creation.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nanoimprint lithography has been used to fabricate devices for electrical, optical, photonic and biological applications. For electronics devices, NIL has been used to fabricate
      <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">
       MOSFET
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OTFT" title="OTFT">
       O-TFT
      </a>
      , single electron memory. For optics and photonics, intensive study has been conducted in fabrication of subwavelength resonant grating filter,
      <a href="/wiki/Polarizer" title="Polarizer">
       polarizers
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Waveplate" title="Waveplate">
       waveplate
      </a>
      , anti-reflective structures, integrated
      <a href="/wiki/Photonics" title="Photonics">
       photonics
      </a>
      circuit and plasmonic devices by NIL. sub-10nm nanofluidic channels had been fabricated using NIL and used in DNA stretching experiment. Currently, NIL is used to shrink the size of biomolecular sorting device an order of magnitude smaller and more efficient.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">
       Benefits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Benefits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A key benefit of nanoimprint lithography is its sheer simplicity. The single greatest cost associated with chip fabrication is the optical lithography tool used to print the circuit patterns. Optical lithography requires high powered
      <a href="/wiki/Excimer_laser" title="Excimer laser">
       excimer lasers
      </a>
      and immense stacks of precision ground lens elements to achieve nanometer scale resolution. There is no need for complex optics or high-energy radiation sources with a nanoimprint tool. There is no need for finely tailored
      <a href="/wiki/Photoresist" title="Photoresist">
       photoresists
      </a>
      designed for both resolution and sensitivity at a given wavelength. The simplified requirements of the technology lead to its low cost.
     </p>
     <p>
      Imprint lithography is inherently a three-dimensional patterning process. Imprint molds can be fabricated with multiple layers of topography stacked vertically. Resulting imprints replicate both layers with a single imprint step, which allows chip manufactures to reduce chip fabrication costs and improve product throughput. As mentioned above, the imprint material does not need to be finely tuned for high resolution and sensitivity. A broader range of materials with varying properties are available for use with imprint lithography. The increased material variability gives chemists the freedom to design new functional materials rather than sacrificial etch resistant polymers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A functional material may be imprinted directly to form a layer in a chip with no need for pattern transfer into underlying materials. The successful implementation of a functional imprint material would result in significant cost reductions and increased throughput by eliminating many difficult chip fabrication processing steps.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Concerns">
       Concerns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Concerns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The key concerns for nanoimprint lithography are overlay, defects, template patterning and template wear. However, recently Kumar et al. have shown that amorphous metals (metallic glasses) can be patterned on sub-100nm scale, which can significantly reduce the template cost.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overlay">
       Overlay
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Overlay">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The current overlay 3
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">
       sigma
      </a>
      capability is 10
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">
       nm
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Overlay has a better chance with step-and-scan approaches as opposed to full-wafer imprint.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Defects">
       Defects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Defects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As with
      <a href="/wiki/Immersion_lithography" title="Immersion lithography">
       immersion lithography
      </a>
      , defect control is expected to improve as the technology matures. Defects from the template with size below the post-imprint process bias can be eliminated. Other defects would require effective template cleaning and/or the use of intermediate polymer stamps. When vacuum is not used during the imprint process, air can get trapped, resulting in bubble defects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is because the imprint resist layer and the template or stamp features are not perfectly flat. There is an elevated risk when the intermediate or master stamp contains depressions (which are especially easy air traps), or when the imprint resist is dispensed as droplets just before imprinting, rather than pre-spun onto the substrate. Sufficient time must be allowed for the air to escape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another issue is adhesion between stamp and resist. High adhesion (sticking) may delaminate resist, which than stays on stamp. This effect degrades pattern, reduces yield and damages stamp. It can be mitigated by employing an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FDTS" title="FDTS">
       FDTS
      </a>
      antistiction layer on a stamp.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Template_patterning">
       Template patterning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Template patterning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      High resolution template patterning can currently be performed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam_lithography" title="Electron beam lithography">
       electron beam lithography
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Focused_ion_beam" title="Focused ion beam">
       focused ion beam
      </a>
      patterning; however at the smallest resolution, the throughput is very slow. As a result, optical patterning tools will be more helpful if they have sufficient resolution. Such an approach has been successfully demonstrated by Greener et al. whereby robust templates were rapidly fabricated by optical patterning of a photoresist-coated metal substrate through a
      <a href="/wiki/Photomask" title="Photomask">
       photomask
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other patterning techniques (including even
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_patterning" title="Double patterning">
       double patterning
      </a>
      ) may also be used.
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.eng.yale.edu/schroerslab/people.html" rel="nofollow">
       Kumar and Schroers at Yale
      </a>
      developed the nanopatterning of amorphous metals which can be used as inexpensive templates for nanoimprinting.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Template_wear">
       Template wear
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Template wear">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The use of substantial pressure to not only contact but also penetrate a layer during imprinting accelerates the wear of imprint templates compared to other types of lithographic masks. Template wear is reduced with proper use of an anti-adhesion
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FDTS" title="FDTS">
       FDTS
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Monolayer" title="Monolayer">
       monolayer
      </a>
      coating on a stamp.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other">
       Other
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Future applications of nanoimprint lithography may involve the use of porous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-k" title="Low-k">
       low-k
      </a>
      materials. These materials are not stiff and, as part of the substrate, are readily damaged mechanically by the pressure of the imprint process.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Removal_of_residual_layers">
       Removal of residual layers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Removal of residual layers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A key characteristic of nanoimprint lithography (except for electrochemical nanoimprinting) is the residual layer following the imprint process. It is preferable to have thick enough residual layers to support alignment and throughput and low defects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, this renders the nanoimprint lithography step less critical for critical dimension (CD) control than the etch step used to remove the residual layer. Hence, it is important to consider the residual layer removal an integrated part of the overall nanoimprint patterning process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a sense, the residual layer etch is similar to the develop process in conventional lithography. It has been proposed to combine photolithography and nanoimprint lithography techniques in one step in order to eliminate the residual layer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheng2004_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cheng2004-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Proximity_effects">
       Proximity effects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Proximity effects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="475" data-file-width="620" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg/350px-Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg/525px-Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg/700px-Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nanoimprint_proximity_effect.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <b>
         Nanoimprint proximity effect.
        </b>
        <i>
         Top:
        </i>
        Array of depressions is more quickly filled at the edge than the center, resulting in less imprinting at the center of the array.
        <i>
         Bottom:
        </i>
        The wide space between two groups of protrusions tends to be filled slower than the narrow spaces between the protrusions, resulting in the formation of holes in the unpatterned area.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Nanoimprint lithography relies on displacing polymer. This could lead to systematic effects over long distances. For example, a large, dense array of protrusions will displace significantly more polymer than an isolated protrusion. Depending on the distance of this isolated protrusion from the array, the isolated feature may not imprint correctly due to polymer displacement and thickening. Resist holes can form in between groups of protrusions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Likewise, wider depressions in the template do not fill up with as much polymer as narrower depressions, resulting in misshapen wide lines. In addition, a depression at the edge of a large array fills up much earlier than one located in the center of the array, resulting in within-array uniformity issues.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="3D-patterning">
       3D-patterning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 3D-patterning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A unique benefit of nanoimprint lithography is the ability to pattern 3D structures, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper-based_chips#Patterning" title="Copper-based chips">
       damascene interconnects
      </a>
      and T-gates, in fewer steps than required for conventional lithography. This is achieved by building the T-shape into the protrusion on the template.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li2001_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Li2001-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_approaches">
       Alternative approaches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Alternative approaches">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemical_nanoimprinting">
       Electrochemical nanoimprinting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Electrochemical nanoimprinting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Electrochemical nanoimprinting can be achieved using a stamp made from a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superionic_conductor" title="Superionic conductor">
       superionic conductor
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Silver_sulfide" title="Silver sulfide">
       silver sulfide
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsu2007_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hsu2007-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the stamp is contacted with metal, electrochemical etching can be carried out with an applied voltage. The electrochemical reaction generates metal ions which move from the original film into the stamp. Eventually all the metal is removed and the complementary stamp pattern is transferred to the remaining metal.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Laser_assisted_direct_imprint">
       Laser assisted direct imprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Laser assisted direct imprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Laser assisted direct imprint (LADI)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chou2002_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chou2002-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a rapid technique for patterning nanostructures in solid substrates and it does not require etching. A single or multiple excimer laser pulses melt a thin surface layer of substrate material, and a mold is embossed into the resulting liquid layer. A variety of structures with resolution better than 10nm have been imprinted into silicon using LADI, and the embossing time is less than 250 ns. The high resolution and speed of LADI, attributed to molten silicons low viscosity (one-third that of water), could open up a variety of applications and be extended to other materials and processing techniques.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_future_of_nanoimprint">
       The future of nanoimprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoimprint_lithography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: The future of nanoimprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nanoimprint lithography is a simple pattern transfer process that is neither limited by diffraction nor scattering effects nor secondary electrons, and does not require any sophisticated radiation chemistry. It is also a potentially simple and inexpensive technique. However, a lingering barrier to nanometer-scale patterning is the current reliance on other lithography techniques to generate the template. It is possible that
      <a href="/wiki/Self-assembly" title="Self-assembly">
       self-assembled structures
      </a>
      will provide the ultimate solution for templates of periodic patterns at scales of 10nm and less.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shevchenko2006_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Shevchenko2006-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also possible to resolve the template generation issue by using a programmable template
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in a scheme based on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_patterning" title="Double patterning">
       double patterning
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As of October 2007,
      <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">
       Toshiba
      </a>
      is the only company to have validated nanoimprint lithography for 22nm and beyond.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      What is more significant is that nanoimprint lithography is the first sub-30nm lithography to be validated by an industrial user.
     </p>
