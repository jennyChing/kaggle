            <h3 data-textannotation-id="dbd1f2d83fc29418ffe770d3786bab50">
             Myth 1: Body Hair Grows Back Thicker When You Shave It
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="b02768b4f83eab4092a111bc418841ec">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--16oKgTZv--/17n1rhrz1e8yajpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhrz1e8yajpg" data-format="jpg" height="309" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--16oKgTZv--/17n1rhrz1e8yajpg.jpg" width="300"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="fa9d0aecb5c77f7d39500fd7f6c871d7">
             You might remember that episode of
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seinfeld" target="_blank">
              Seinfeld
             </a>
             where Jerry decides to shave his chest despite warnings that it will grow back twice as thick. Although the myth proves to be true on television, it's very much false in the real world. If shaving caused hair to grow in much thicker, balding men would be shaving their heads for hair loss prevention. Children's health researcher Rachel C. Vreeman and assistant professor of pediatrics Aaron E. Carrol
             <a href="http://www.bmj.com/content/335/7633/1288.full" target="_blank">
              put this myth to rest
             </a>
             :
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="ebd9780429e4fbff09aa12aed6f6261b">
             <p data-textannotation-id="54de4987f48f6d4c84623d6374a06996">
              Strong scientific evidence disproves these claims. As early as 1928, a clinical trial showed that shaving had no effect on hair growth. More recent studies confirm that shaving does not affect the thickness or rate of hair regrowth. In addition, shaving removes the dead portion of hair, not the living section lying below the skin's surface, so it is unlikely to affect the rate or type of growth. Shaved hair lacks the finer taper seen at the ends of unshaven hair, giving an impression of coarseness. Similarly, the new hair has not yet been lightened by the sun or other chemical exposures, resulting in an appearance that seems darker than existing hair.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="590f71e4ae26b207d207e6a9b6afe39b">
             Basically, shaved hair feels coarse and that leads you to believe it's thicker. In reality you're just fooling yourself and your hair remains the same.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p data-textannotation-id="db48c8f890df9cab5ba3d4e9e63a9a4a">
             <i style="font-size:80%">
              Photo by
              <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/philandpam/2053101673/" target="_blank">
               Phil and Pam Gradwell
              </a>
             </i>
             .
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="1a1ce553669f662bcc20797bb7a1e721">
             Myth 2: Calories Counting Is All That Matters for Weight Management and Health
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="243aafa277c0f144dfe540b0f2c0031a">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--nwlu0MH3--/17n1rhrz736qljpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhrz736qljpg" data-format="jpg" height="159" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--nwlu0MH3--/17n1rhrz736qljpg.jpg" width="300"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="5d0db444b10be6847c3509cc35f3a0a9">
             We might like to believe that calories-in-equals-calories-out is a sufficient weight loss theory, but that means we have to accept our bodies are pretty simple. While consuming fewer calories can certainly have an impact, not all foods have the same impact once we stuff them down our throats. If you want to think about it in a very simple way, consider the difference between a candy bar and a cucumber (in equal caloric amounts). They taste different, they consist of different nutritional elements, and are
             <em>
              not the same thing
             </em>
             . It doesn't make sense that they'd be used by your body in the same way.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="b485a8daf28ff741365174c0c4ce1a24">
             The problem with the idea of calories being the only necessary metric is that we think of a calorie as a physical thing. Calories are just a means of measuring heat, and they weren't initially a term used in reference to food. A calorie,
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calorie" target="_blank">
              according to Wikipedia
             </a>
             , "approximates the energy needed to increase the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1 degree Celsius." Basically, calories are a measurement and not something your body uses for fuel. What your body
             <em>
              does
             </em>
             use is what it finds in the foods and liquids it digests. If you put crap in your body, you're not going to be better off just because of a low-calorie rating.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="c655e3fcbeb92df04f981ceecc03868e">
             Many people contest this idea because of
             <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2010/HEALTH/11/08/twinkie.diet.professor/index.html" target="_blank">
              nutrition Professor Mark Haub's twinkie diet
             </a>
             , in which he ate a low calorie diet that consisted of about two-thirds junk food, but there are a few things to note here. This is the sole study of one person and is not indicative
             <em>
              everyone
             </em>
             . Even Haub questions his own findings:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="988fb12ac8b69e6feae9db91724ec25a">
             <p data-textannotation-id="84065937982228a6793e04a779793e79">
              What does that mean? Does that mean I'm healthier? Or does it mean how we define health from a biology standpoint, that we're missing something?
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="5e1cfaccbb7da9734ee45db09415c0ed">
             Haub also reduced his intake by about 800 calories per day, which is a very significant amount. There is no question that our caloric intake plays a role in the way we store and lose fat (as
             <a href="http://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa0804748" target="_blank">
              this study in the New England Journal of Medicine shows
             </a>
             ), but simply counting calories does not provide the full pictures.
             <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5809331/what-sugar-actually-does-to-your-brain-and-body" x-inset="1">
              The way your body processes sugar
             </a>
             is an excellent example of how different foods yield different results. Dr. Michael Eades
             <a href="http://www.fourhourworkweek.com/blog/2008/02/25/the-science-of-fat-loss-why-a-calorie-isnt-always-a-calorie/" target="_blank">
              points to two studies
             </a>
             the
             <a data-amazonasin="0816651612" data-amazonsubtag="[p|5873922[a|0816651612[au|5760613250696714512" data-amazontag="lifehackeramzn-20" href="http://www.amazon.com/o/ASIN/0816651612/104-0776638-0750307?SubscriptionId=1N9AHEAQ2F6SVD97BE02&amp;tag=lifehackeramzn-20&amp;ascsubtag=[t|link[p|5873922[a|0816651612[au|5760613250696714512" onclick="window.ga('send', 'event', 'Commerce', 'lifehacker - 10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won\'t Die, Debunked by Science', '0816651612');" target="_blank" x-inset="1">
              Ancel Keys starvation experiment
             </a>
             and the
             <a href="http://www.ajcn.org/content/23/7/948.full.pdf+html" target="_blank">
              John Yudkin study
             </a>
             that each tested two low-calorie diets with different nutrient compositions. The Keys study had higher amounts of carbohydrates and lower amounts of fat. The Yudkin study had the opposite. The results of the studies, however, yielded very different results. The Yudkin study ended with healthy men whereas the Keys study did not.
            </p>

            <h3 data-textannotation-id="64fff25b8d06f2c7dc88e6011735a27f">
             Myth 4: Reading in Dim Light Ruins Your Eyes
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="2e72ab39c1e28fd8758df89355487195">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--_cPexk7---/17n1rhty02pvtjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhty02pvtjpg" data-format="jpg" height="246" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--_cPexk7---/17n1rhty02pvtjpg.jpg" width="300"/>

              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>

            <p data-textannotation-id="dc202eb0a5e1cfdd1d38d4390994fa11">
             Reading in dim light is supposed to be bad for you, which is somewhat evidenced by a surviving bedside lamp and book light industry. If not, you've certainly been told to turn on a light when reading in the near-dark. While reading without sufficient lighting can cause eye strain, according to
             <a href="http://www.bmj.com/content/335/7633/1288.full" target="_blank">
              children's health researcher Rachel C. Vreeman and assistant professor of pediatrics Aaron E. Carrol
             </a>
             it won't cause any serious and permanent damage:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="33ff6cff0c8c3ffc21489638c7dbc75b">
             <p data-textannotation-id="94185a601f3754792e8ae7e80fd3be0b">
              The majority consensus in ophthalmology, as outlined in a collection of educational material for patients, is that reading in dim light does not damage your eyes. Although it can cause eye strain with multiple temporary negative effects, it is unlikely to cause a permanent change on the function or structure of the eyes. Even in patients with Sjgren's syndrome (an autoimmune disease that features inflammation in certain glands of the body), decreased functional visual acuity associated with strained reading improves when they stop reading. One review article on myopia concludes that increased use of one's eyes, such as reading in dim light or holding books too close to the face, could result in impaired ocular growth and refractive error. The primary evidence cited was epidemiological evidence of the increased prevalence of myopia and the high incidence of myopia in people with more academic experience. The author notes that this hypothesis is just beginning to "gain scientific credence." In the past reading conditions involved even less light, relying on candles or lanterns, so increased rates of myopia over the past several centuries does not necessarily support that dim reading conditions are to blame. In contrast to that review, hundreds of online expert opinions conclude that reading in low light does not hurt your eyes.
             </p>
            </blockquote>

             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--ejWVB2rK--/17n1rhty3dml8jpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhty3dml8jpg" data-format="jpg" height="236" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--ejWVB2rK--/17n1rhty3dml8jpg.jpg" width="300"/>

            <p data-textannotation-id="180a6a2dea39168ac917c06bf7d51595">
             When you have a fast metabolism, your body is burning more calories. That means that fit and healthy people have faster metabolisms, right? Not necessarily.
             <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/2020/story?id=2109291&amp;page=2#.Twc5syNWrWo" target="_blank">
              ABC News interviewed
             </a>
             Dr. Jim Levine, an obesity researcher at the Mayo Clinic, who studied the human metabolism in both thin and heavy people. What he found was the opposite of the myth we believe. Referring to lean patient Kathy Strickland and heavier patient Dawn Campion, he said:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="a11eb94e777757225953ae46b1b64f96">
             <p data-textannotation-id="1f339ca03c2baa1398f6cee3918e9463">
              Dawn's numbers are actually higher because we find continuously is that people with weight problems who have obesity have a higher basal metabolism compared to people who are lean. Your basal metabolism is the calories you burn to keep your body going, so if your body is bigger of course your basal metabolism is greater. If your body is smaller your basal metabolism is less.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="701ac962dfdbe703cbed779bef68d75b">
             Dr. Levin inferred that the weight problems in his patients was due less to the speed of their metabolism and more due to their sedentary lifestyles. That is, of course, only one part of the equation. Gaining unwanted weight can stem from an unhealthy diet, lack of exercise, and a number of other problems as well. It's a complicated problem, and your metabolism isn't necessarily to blame.
            </p>

            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="c759cd9968c7d9b222c3858c22e07a3a">
             <p data-textannotation-id="c4ea58d1ddfafdafc9ddf8fa8ca80e96">
              Cold or wet weather does not cause a cold, but nobody seems to want to accept this. The is common cold is caused by a virus. These viruses are everywhere and it is difficult to avoid them. When you are exposed to someone who has a cold, you are more likely to get ill yourself, so be careful about close contact and definitely wash your hands. Not getting enough sleep or eating poorly can also reduce your resistance to infection. Remember that antibiotics won't fight your everyday cold. Antibiotics work only against bacteria. To take care of a cold, rest, eat well, and a little chicken soup couldn't hurt.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="ccd6340b9c23f965ed9d1db994adb8f7">
             But if that's true, why do people contract a cold more often in the Winter? Doctors don't have a certain answer, but
             <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2005/11/22/health/22real.html" target="_blank">
              according to the New York Times
             </a>
             there are a few working theories. Because colds are spread by transferring the virus from one person to another, you need to be in contact with other people. People spend more time indoors during the Winter, and so you often find yourselves 1) around them, and 2) in an enclosed space. If one person gets sick in a household, office, or wherever, there's a good chance that virus will spread. As you should any time of year, keep your distance from the contagious.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p data-textannotation-id="a617e060c10388c261f5aca239f02827">
             <i style="font-size:80%">
              Photo by
              <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/grafixer/5347918465/" target="_blank">
               Faith Goble
              </a>
             </i>
             .
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="97fa9d5c2eaa781576b2a483a220aa3e">
             Myth 8: More Heat Escapes Through Your Head
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="11173b64fef63b78df69678bd6c60a09">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--8MkWRM8E--/17n1rhty4rj04jpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhty4rj04jpg" data-format="jpg" height="326" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--8MkWRM8E--/17n1rhty4rj04jpg.jpg" width="300"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="55d4d740059872851626c9ff8bc1916c">
             Heat rises, and your head is generally warm, so it would stand to reason that walking around outside with your head uncovered isn't the best plan if you want to stay nice and toasty. While that idea seems to make sense, you're not going to lose more heat through your head than you will from pretty much any other part of your body. According to
             <a href="http://www.bmj.com/content/337/bmj.a2769.full" target="_blank">
              Children's health researcher Rachel C. Vreeman and assistant professor of pediatrics Aaron E. Carrol
             </a>
             , wearing a hat will just keep your head warmer:
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="b4e6fdf32ea67f6a98dde5bae6155995">
             <p data-textannotation-id="fea1fccd6731f130f260ff6530242bf4">
              This myth probably originated with an old military study in which scientists put subjects in arctic survival suits (but no hats) and measured their heat loss in extremely cold temperatures. Because it was the only part of the subjects' bodies that was exposed to the cold, they lost the most heat through their heads. Experts say, however, that had this experiment been performed with subjects wearing only swimsuits, they would not have lost more than 10% of their body heat through their heads. A more recent study confirms that there is nothing special about the head and heat loss. Any uncovered part of the body loses heat and will reduce the core body temperature proportionally. So, if it is cold outside, you should protect your body. But whether you want to keep your head covered or not is up to you.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="c5a33360e1aa5182d5c313645560a321">
             For more information on the studies mentioned,
             <a href="http://www.bmj.com/content/337/bmj.a2769.full" target="_blank">
              read this
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="978f921b5e686501ed1c6dcffa370fe7">
             <i style="font-size:80%">
              Photo by
              <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/jenny-pics/5261083666/" target="_blank">
               Jenny Downing
              </a>
             </i>
             .
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="397b2f1eb44470f5be50e9bf35d16a7a">
             Myth 9: You Can Cure a Snake Bite by Sucking Out the Poison
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="7bd2408241ead086809ac4da96456e47">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--uaxRz4Uy--/17n1rhvx45iybjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhvx45iybjpg" data-format="jpg" height="274" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--uaxRz4Uy--/17n1rhvx45iybjpg.jpg" width="300"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="82109765b112124316f415bad86b1067">
             Although it would be pretty bad ass if it were true, sucking on a snake bite to remove the poison is not only gross but a bad idea. According to Mark Leyner and Dr. Billy Goldberg, authors of
             <i>
              Why Do Men Have Nipples?
             </i>
             , doing so can even lead to an infection at the wound site. Instead, do what the American Red Cross suggests:
            </p>
            <ol>
             <li data-textannotation-id="1e4cbaf0010704fb6fa7a08a618c817e">
              Wash the bite with soap and water.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="944e2129ea5ee3dc58779365713da6c1">
              Immobilize the bitten area and keep it lower than the heart.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="eb6dfa0c2d535032cf6459b79211e967">
              Get medical help.
             </li>
            </ol>
            <p data-textannotation-id="2a15ed0edf1dd66e2a02d19e52dde5fd">
             In some cases it may also be recommended to loosely apply a tourniquet to slow the spread of the venom. So do those things. Don't try to suck it out.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p data-textannotation-id="8e49d2b92e07cff50532451819e13be4">
             <i style="font-size:80%">
              Photo by
              <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/goingslo/2976871615/" target="_blank">
               Linda Tanner
              </a>
             </i>
             .
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="15174e506541490b304d18d2de060e03">
             <i>
              Note: Myth 9 previously discussed the relationship between high cholesterol and heart disease. We were working with some bad information and now believe it was incorrect, so we've added this snake bite myth instead. We apologize for any confusion or misinformation.
             </i>
            </p>
            <h3 data-textannotation-id="a892798d2b018d9738bfa818ac56e66c">
             Myth 10: It's Dangerous to Wake a Sleepwalker
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="168cb6f6c7128f2a26ad950bbf29ac56">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="10 Stubborn Body Myths That Just Won't Die, Debunked by Science" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--1L7o5IDz--/17n1rhvx8671wjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="17n1rhvx8671wjpg" data-format="jpg" height="210" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--1L7o5IDz--/17n1rhvx8671wjpg.jpg" width="300"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="6c19cc5c2276fc8faf4311ed368fde66">
             It's actually dangerous to
             <em>
              not
             </em>
             wake a sleepwalker, but many have believed this myth for ages because, perhaps, a few of them have gotten smacked when they woke up their startled somnambulatory friends. Sleepwalkers are certainly prone to feeling that surprise when they don't wake up in their beds but, rather, at the outer limits of their camping grounds. (Oh wait, that was me when I was 10.) Because this is so disorienting, many woken sleepwalkers won't know who you are and become frightened. That said, letting them just walk wherever they want is far less safe than a little fear.
             <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2005/09/13/health/13real.html" target="_blank">
              The New York Times interviewed
             </a>
             Dr. Ana C. Krieger, director of the Sleep Disorders Center at New York University, who suggested that the best thing to do is guide a sleepwalker back to bed. Wake them if you have to, but better to just help them get to where they should be in the first place.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p data-textannotation-id="bd98c008a764dc96a787c3d3c5b9c4dd">
             <i style="font-size:80%">
              Photo by
              <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/peterhess/5247464100/" target="_blank">
               Peter Hess
              </a>
             </i>
             .
            </p>
