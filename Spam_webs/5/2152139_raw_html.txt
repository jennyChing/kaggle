  <meta content="Gustave Courbet, The Origin of the World, 1866, oil on canvas. Muse d'Orsay, Paris

 Yesterday, I stumbled upon &ldquo;The Top 9 Most Amazing Vaginas&rdquo; via The Frisky. Missing from the list was Gustave Courbet&rsquo;s The Origin of the World,  one of the most difficult paintings to teach in an intro level art  history course (someone important always walk in or past the class while  the image is on the screen). Courbet&rsquo;s painting is certainly  controversial. Here&rsquo;s what the Muse d'Orsay has to say about it: 

 Courbet regularly painted female nudes, sometimes in a frankly libertine vein. But in The Origin of the World he   went to lengths of daring and frankness which gave his painting its   peculiar fascination. The almost anatomical description of female sex   organs is not attenuated by any historical or literary device. Yet   thanks to Courbet&rsquo;s great virtuosity and the refinement of his amber   colour scheme, the painting escapes pornographic status. This audacious,   forthright new language had nonetheless not severed all links with   tradition: the ample, sensual brushstrokes and the use of colour recall   Venetian painting and Courbet himself claimed descent from Titian and   Veronese, Correggio and the tradition of carnal, lyrical painting. The Origin of the World,   now openly displayed, has taken its proper place in the history of   modern painting. But it still raises the troubling question of   voyeurism.

 Although Courbet&rsquo;s painting was missing  from the list of most amazing vaginas, art history was recognized by  Carolee Schneeman&rsquo;s Interior Scroll. From The Frisky&lsquo;s website:

 In 1975, performance artist Carolee Schneeman stood on a table, covered  herself in mud, and extracted a scroll from her vagina. Then, she read  it. The paper evidence from Interior Scroll is now displayed as a work  of art. And what has your vagina done lately?

 Enjoy a less graphic look at her performance piece below.

      <h3>
       Courbets Origin of the World
      </h3>
      <div class="text">
       <p>
        <small>
         <img alt="Gustave Courbet, The Origin of the World, 1866, oil on canvas.  Muse d'Orsay, Paris" height="425" src="http://33.media.tumblr.com/tumblr_lxy631nrUx1qggdq1.jpg" width="569"/>
         Gustave Courbet,
         <em>
          The Origin of the World,
         </em>
         1866, oil on canvas. Muse d'Orsay, Paris
        </small>
       </p>
       <!-- more -->
       <p>
        Yesterday, I stumbled upon The Top 9 Most Amazing Vaginas via
        <a href="http://www.thefrisky.com/photos/the-top-10-most-amazing-vaginas/amazing-vaginas-g3-jpg/" target="_blank" title="The Top 9 Most Amazing Vaginas, The Frisky">
         <em>
          The Frisky
         </em>
        </a>
        . Missing from the list was Gustave Courbets
        <em>
         The Origin of the World
        </em>
        ,  one of the most difficult paintings to teach in an intro level art  history course (someone important always walk in or past the class while  the image is on the screen). Courbets painting is certainly  controversial. Heres what the
        <span class="st">
         Muse d'Orsay has to say about it:
         <br/>
        </span>
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Courbet regularly painted female nudes, sometimes in a frankly libertine vein. But in
         <em>
          <span class="italiquenoir">
           The Origin of the World
          </span>
         </em>
         he   went to lengths of daring and frankness which gave his painting its   peculiar fascination. The almost anatomical description of female sex   organs is not attenuated by any historical or literary device. Yet   thanks to Courbets great virtuosity and the refinement of his amber   colour scheme, the painting escapes pornographic status. This audacious,   forthright new language had nonetheless not severed all links with   tradition: the ample, sensual brushstrokes and the use of colour recall   Venetian painting and Courbet himself claimed descent from Titian and   Veronese, Correggio and the tradition of carnal, lyrical painting.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <em>
          <span class="italiquenoir">
           The Origin of the World
          </span>
         </em>
         ,   now openly displayed, has taken its proper place in the history of   modern painting. But it still raises the troubling question of   voyeurism.
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Although Courbets painting was missing  from the list of most amazing vaginas, art history was recognized by  Carolee Schneemans
        <em>
         Interior Scroll
        </em>
        . From
        <em>
         The Frisky
        </em>
        s website:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         In 1975, performance artist Carolee Schneeman stood on a table, covered  herself in mud, and extracted a scroll from her vagina. Then, she read  it. The paper evidence from Interior Scroll is now displayed as a work  of art. And what has your vagina done lately?
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Enjoy a less graphic look at her performance piece below.
       </p>
       <p>
        <small>
         <img alt="Carolee Schneeman, Interior Scroll , 1974, paper scroll in Plexiglass box.  Collection of Peter Norton and Eileen Harris Norton, Santa Monica, California; Scroll 1: Women Here and Now, East Hampton, New York, 29 August, 1975, black and white photographs, courtesy of the artist; Scroll 2: Telluride Film Festival, Telluride, Colorado, 4 September, 1977, black and white photographs, courtesy of the artist; installation photo by Brian Forrest" height="366" src="http://31.media.tumblr.com/tumblr_lxy6hkp0091qggdq1.jpg" width="577"/>
         Carolee Schneeman,
         <em>
          Interior Scroll
         </em>
         , 1974, paper  scroll in  Plexiglass box. Collection of Peter  Norton and Eileen  Harris Norton, Santa Monica, California; Scroll 1:
         <em>
          Women Here and Now
         </em>
         ,
         <em>
          East Hampton, New York,
         </em>
         29 August, 1975, black and white photographs,  courtesy of the artist; Scroll 2:
         <em>
          Telluride Film Festival, Telluride
         </em>
         ,
         <em>
          Colorado,
         </em>
         4 September, 1977, black and white photographs, courtesy of the artist; installation photo by Brian Forrest
        </small>
       </p>
