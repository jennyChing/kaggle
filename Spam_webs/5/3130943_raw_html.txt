     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-56611 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="56611" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="image-article_inline_full">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <strong>
                <img alt="" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/image-article_inline_full/public/blogs/52275/2011/03/56611-48333.jpg?itok=USSXB6Vv" title="The villian of the story"/>
               </strong>
              </div>
              <div class="caption">
               The villian of the story
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            <strong>
             T
            </strong>
            his really happened.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Just after New Years Day last year, a 29 year-old British man leaped before he looked.
           </p>
           <p>
            Had he looked at the ground six feet below where he was perched on a building foundation, he would have spotted a six-inch construction nail pointing at him.
           </p>
           <p>
            But he jumped, and landed so hard the nail pierced through his leather boot. He collapsed to the ground, shrieking in pain.
           </p>
           <p>
            Three coworkers ran to aid him, then carried him to a car which rushed him to a hospital.
           </p>
           <p>
            An ER doctor barely touched the nail, and the man screamed. So to induce drowsiness and reduce his anxiety, he injected him with midazolam. It failed to stop the pain. So he decided to a drug 100 times more potent than morphine, fentanyl. (The drug became famous months later when
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/teamwork" title="Psychology Today looks at teams">
             teams
            </a>
            investigating singer Michael Jackson's death discovered bottles of the drug in his bathroom.)
           </p>
           <p>
            The potent drug took effect. Two doctors started to remove the patient's boot. They finally freed the man's foot, removed his sock, and stared. And then they turned to look at each other in stunned silence.
           </p>
           <p>
            The nail had passed cleanly between their patient's big and second toe. His foot was untouched; his pain was all in his head.
           </p>
           <p>
            What had happened? A phenomenon similar to ther
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/placebo" title="Psychology Today looks at placebo">
             placebo
            </a>
            effect, called a nocebo effect. Nocebo effects occur when a patient develops ide effects from a drug that cannot cause those effects. (Nocebo effects are significant in
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/health" title="Psychology Today looks at health">
             health
            </a>
            care today, because they waste time and money each time their sufferers demand second medications to treat their merely-perceived pain,)
           </p>
           <p>
            This tale illustrates something fundamental: Our expectations so influence our experiences
            <em>
             that our expectations often become our experiences.
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
            Decade ago, some diners visited New York's famous Tavern on the Green, sipped the coffee, and claimed "it tastes fabulous." It could not have; it was Folger's freeze-dried coffee, planted there for what became a famous hidden camera commercial.
           </p>
           <p>
            But the diners expected fabulous coffee. And so it was, even though it wasn't.
           </p>
           <p>
            Just years ago. some New York diners visited anther restaurant, Provence. They savored its Pasta in Marina Sauce and proclaimed it delicious, just as they expected of a great Greenwich Village restaurant. But the cooks at Provence had not prepared those dishes. The nearby Pizza Hut had.
           </p>
           <p>
            Again, people experienced what they expected to experience.
           </p>
           <p>
            In tests for Rogaine, participants rave that it grows hair--including 40% of the participants who receive the control substance that grows nothing.
           </p>
           <p>
            A host introduces a keynoter speaker by reciting a resume so impressive that you feel certain you will hear a great presentation. And then you do--
            <em>
             you think.
            </em>
            The introducer has primed you to think that.
           </p>
           <p>
            We cling to the belief that our brains perform like computers, but they continue to behave like 19 years-olds after five beers. Women who carry Victoria Secret's bags feel more attractive than women who carry plain white bags.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            People exposed to the Apple logo perform better on creative tests than people exposed to the IBM logo.
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            And a six-inch nail passes cleanly between a man's toes, and the man suffers excruciating pain.
           </p>
           <p>
            These incidents raise three questions.
           </p>
           <p>
            First, what
            <em>
             is
            </em>
            reality? "Reality often is inaccurate," Douglas Adams wrote in
            <em>
             The Hitchhikers's Guide to the Galaxy
            </em>
            . When an interviewer asked Tom Clancy the difference between reality and fiction, the popular author responded as if he'd just read this Tale. "Fiction," Clancy said, "has to make sense."
           </p>
           <p>
            These incidents also raise two final questions for you, the reader:
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             How might you prime people so that they expect that your idea, product or service will work wonderfully?
            </em>
