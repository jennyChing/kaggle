        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Thanksgiving: Fishing through Awkward Conversations
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <a class="author" href="http://troutvibe.com/author/admin/" rel="author">
          Alec Griswold
         </a>
         /
         <a class="entry-date" href="http://troutvibe.com/thanksgiving-fishing-through-awkward-conversations/">
          November 28, 2013
         </a>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         Thanksgiving is terrific: food, family, and football; a time to be grateful for what you have as well as question the morality of the Pilgrims. An added bonus of the holiday is dealing with awkward familial conversations, especially with extended family members. Many ease the anxiety by numbing the relentless questions with large quantities of beverages. Others isolate or set up home base in front of the television. The rest of us put on a fake smile and listen to Aunt Mindy rant for an hour about cats and Dancing with the Stars.
        </p>
        <p>
         What if we applied our knowledge of fly-fishing to these tricky and often embarrassing situations? Here are three possible scenarios:
        </p>
        <p>
         1. A distant relative (you blank on the name) approaches you and slurs, So what are you doing with your life these days?
         <b>
          Keep it short
         </b>
         .
         <i>
          Like fishing a small stream with a 7-foot leader
         </i>
         , it is important not to spook the relative
         <i>
          or the fish
         </i>
         with the fact that you are unemployed. It may be helpful to reply swiftly, Well, I am in job search mode at the moment (
         <i>
          similar to laying down several casts in front of a downed log at the end of a deep hole
         </i>
         ) and I have several promising leads (
         <i>
          the occasional flash of a fish near your fly
         </i>
         ). Follow up this short answer with a question to the well lubricated relative, How about yourself (
         <i>
          also known as changing flies
         </i>
         )? In general people like to talk about themselves,
         <i>
          like a salmon tends to take a fly when it swings in front of their mouth
         </i>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         2. Aunt Shirley, the one who sends peculiar Christmas gifts, pinches your cheek and asks, When are you going to get married?
         <b>
          Be ambiguous
         </b>
         .
         <i>
          A fisherman tends not to reveal secret spots on the river to other fishermen
         </i>
         . Therefore it helps to be vague, Well, Auntie Shirley, I like this one person but they are difficult to get close too, but I am always searching for new prospects. In a way, you are telling nosy Aunt Shirley that you are interested in finding a lover (
         <i>
          or catching big fish
         </i>
         ) yet you have not divulged the exact details (
         <i>
          or location where the big fish congregate
         </i>
         ).
         <i>
          Fishermen are not liars; we simply have a habit of distorting the truth
         </i>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         3. Kids are the best, yet they have the propensity to ask impolite questions. For example, a 6-year-old cousin tugs on your pant leg and asks, Why are you fat? Rather than scolding the little scoundrel,
         <b>
          play mind games
         </b>
         .
         <i>
          Imagine you spend the whole day on the river with a friend and he catches several fish on a nymph rig while you manage to hook only branches and leaves on dry flies
         </i>
         . You realize it is time to resort to unorthodox and cunning tactics in order to even the match.
        </p>
        <p>
         In a patronizing tone, say, Well, little Jimmie, people with big bellys have larger brains and as a result win tons of trophies.
         <i>
          In fishing terms, put a little weight on the fly and drift it through a promising hole. After landing a trophy size fish, you notify your fellow fisherman that a size 8, dry fly is perfect. Your fishing buddy searches through his fly box for an unproductive fly
         </i>
         , and little Jimmie runs away, confused, in search of a gallon of ice cream.
        </p>
        <p>
         In essence, you can relate almost anything in life to fishing. When awkward moments arrive during the holiday season, it may be helpful to engage your fish brain to diffuse awkward situations. Fly-fishing is a cerebral sport in which knowledge and experience dictate success, therefore be creative and use that wisdom to your advantage in everyday life, especially holidays.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the spirit of Thanksgiving be grateful for the eccentricity of your family as well as thankful for the knowledge gained from spending time with a fly rod in hand. When Uncle Frank starts talking about his extensive coin collection, you can always say, That is so interesting. I collect flies.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
