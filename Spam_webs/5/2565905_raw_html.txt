     <p>
      <b>
       David Warren "Dave" Brubeck
      </b>
      (December 6, 1920 December 5, 2012) was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz_piano" title="Jazz piano">
       jazz pianist
      </a>
      and composer, considered to be one of the foremost exponents of
      <a href="/wiki/Cool_jazz" title="Cool jazz">
       cool jazz
      </a>
      . He wrote a number of
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz_standard" title="Jazz standard">
       jazz standards
      </a>
      , including "
      <a href="/wiki/In_Your_Own_Sweet_Way" title="In Your Own Sweet Way">
       In Your Own Sweet Way
      </a>
      " and "The Duke". Brubeck's style ranged from refined to bombastic, reflecting his mother's attempts at
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">
       classical
      </a>
      training and his
      <a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">
       improvisational
      </a>
      skills. His music is known for employing unusual
      <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">
       time signatures
      </a>
      , and superimposing contrasting rhythms,
      <a href="/wiki/Meter_(music)" title="Meter (music)">
       meters
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Tonality" title="Tonality">
       tonalities
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      His long-time musical partner,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alto_saxophonist" title="Alto saxophonist">
       alto saxophonist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Desmond" title="Paul Desmond">
       Paul Desmond
      </a>
      , wrote the saxophone melody for the
      <a href="#Dave_Brubeck_Quartet">
       Dave Brubeck Quartet
      </a>
      's best remembered piece, "
      <a href="/wiki/Take_Five" title="Take Five">
       Take Five
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-City_of_Concord_news_release_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-City_of_Concord_news_release-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which is in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5/4_time" title="5/4 time">
       5/4 time
      </a>
      and has endured as a jazz classic on one of the top-selling jazz albums,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(album)" title="Time Out (album)">
        Time Out
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brubeck experimented with
      <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">
       time signatures
      </a>
      throughout his career, recording "Pick Up Sticks" in 6/4, "
      <a href="/wiki/Unsquare_Dance" title="Unsquare Dance">
       Unsquare Dance
      </a>
      " in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7/4" title="7/4">
       7/4
      </a>
      , "World's Fair" in 13/4, and "
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Rondo_%C3%A0_la_Turk" title="Blue Rondo  la Turk">
       Blue Rondo  la Turk
      </a>
      " in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9/8" title="9/8">
       9/8
      </a>
      . He was also a respected composer of orchestral and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_music" title="Sacred music">
       sacred music
      </a>
      , and wrote soundtracks for television such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mr._Broadway" title="Mr. Broadway">
        Mr. Broadway
       </a>
      </i>
      and the animated
      <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">
       miniseries
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/This_Is_America,_Charlie_Brown" title="This Is America, Charlie Brown">
        This Is America, Charlie Brown
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">
       Early life and career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Brubeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Brubeck was born in the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">
       San Francisco Bay Area
      </a>
      city of
      <a href="/wiki/Concord,_California" title="Concord, California">
       Concord
      </a>
      , California,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-City_of_Concord_news_release_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-City_of_Concord_news_release-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and grew up in
      <a href="/wiki/Ione,_California" title="Ione, California">
       Ione
      </a>
      . His father, Peter Howard "Pete" Brubeck, was a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_rancher" title="Cattle rancher">
       cattle rancher
      </a>
      , and his mother, Elizabeth (ne Ivey), who had studied piano in England under
      <a href="/wiki/Myra_Hess" title="Myra Hess">
       Myra Hess
      </a>
      and intended to become a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_pianist" title="Concert pianist">
       concert pianist
      </a>
      , taught piano for extra money.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bookref1_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bookref1-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His father had Swiss ancestry (the family surname was originally "Brodbeck") and possibly Native American
      <a href="/wiki/Modoc_people" title="Modoc people">
       Modoc
      </a>
      lineage,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while his maternal grandparents were English and German.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brubeck originally did not intend to become a musician (his two older brothers, Henry and
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Brubeck" title="Howard Brubeck">
       Howard
      </a>
      , were already on that track), but took lessons from his mother. He could not
      <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">
       read music
      </a>
      during these early lessons, attributing this difficulty to poor eyesight, but "faked" his way through, well enough that this deficiency went mostly unnoticed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Intending to work with his father on their ranch, Brubeck entered the College of the Pacific in
      <a href="/wiki/Stockton,_California" title="Stockton, California">
       Stockton, California
      </a>
      (now the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Pacific_(United_States)" title="University of the Pacific (United States)">
       University of the Pacific
      </a>
      ), studying
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary_science" title="Veterinary science">
       veterinary science
      </a>
      . He changed to music on the urging of the head of
      <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">
       zoology
      </a>
      , Dr. Arnold, who told him "Brubeck, your mind's not here. It's across the lawn in the
      <a href="/wiki/Music_school" title="Music school">
       conservatory
      </a>
      . Please go there. Stop wasting my time and yours."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, Brubeck was nearly expelled when one of his professors discovered that he could not read music. Several of his professors came forward, arguing that his ability with
      <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">
       counterpoint
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">
       harmony
      </a>
      more than compensated. The college was still afraid that it would cause a scandal, and agreed to let Brubeck graduate only after he had promised never to teach piano.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowboy_to_Jazzman_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cowboy_to_Jazzman-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After graduating in 1942, Brubeck was drafted into the U.S. Army. He served in Europe in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_United_States_Army" title="Third United States Army">
       Third Army
      </a>
      . He volunteered to play piano at a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">
       Red Cross
      </a>
      show and was such a hit that he was spared from combat service and ordered to form a band. He created one of the U.S. armed forces' first
      <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">
       racially integrated
      </a>
      bands, "The Wolfpack".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowboy_to_Jazzman_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cowboy_to_Jazzman-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While serving in the military, Brubeck met
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Desmond" title="Paul Desmond">
       Paul Desmond
      </a>
      in early 1944.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He returned to college after serving nearly four years in the army, this time attending
      <a href="/wiki/Mills_College" title="Mills College">
       Mills College
      </a>
      in Oakland. He studied under
      <a href="/wiki/Darius_Milhaud" title="Darius Milhaud">
       Darius Milhaud
      </a>
      , who encouraged him to study
      <a href="/wiki/Fugue" title="Fugue">
       fugue
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Orchestration" title="Orchestration">
       orchestration
      </a>
      , but not classical piano. While on active duty, he received two lessons from
      <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">
       Arnold Schoenberg
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">
       UCLA
      </a>
      in an attempt to connect with
      <a href="/wiki/High_modernism" title="High modernism">
       High Modernism
      </a>
      theory and practice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the encounter did not end on good terms since Schoenberg believed that every note should be accounted for, an approach which Brubeck could not accept, although according to his son Chris Brubeck, there is a twelve-tone row in
      <i>
       The Light in the Wilderness
      </i>
      , Dave Brubeck's first oratorio. In it, Jesus's twelve
      <a href="/wiki/Apostle_(Christian)" title="Apostle (Christian)">
       disciples
      </a>
      are introduced each singing their own individual notes; it is described as quite dramatic, especially when Judas starts singing 'Repent' on a high and straining dissonant note.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After completing his studies under Milhaud, Brubeck worked with an
      <a href="/wiki/Octet_(music)" title="Octet (music)">
       octet
      </a>
      (the recording bears his name only because Brubeck was the best-known member at the time), and a
      <a href="/wiki/Trio_(music)" title="Trio (music)">
       trio
      </a>
      including
      <a href="/wiki/Cal_Tjader" title="Cal Tjader">
       Cal Tjader
      </a>
      and Ron Crotty. Highly experimental, the group made few recordings and got even fewer paying jobs. The trio was often joined by Paul Desmond on the bandstand, at Desmond's own insistence.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Jack Sheedy owned San Francisco-based Coronet Records, which had previously recorded area
      <a href="/wiki/Dixieland" title="Dixieland">
       Dixieland
      </a>
      bands. (This Coronet Records should not be confused with either the late 1950s New York-based budget label, nor with Australia-based
      <a href="/wiki/Coronet_Records" title="Coronet Records">
       Coronet Records
      </a>
      .) In 1949, Sheedy was talked into making the first recording of Brubeck's octet and later his trio. But Sheedy was unable to pay his bills and in 1949 turned his masters over to his record stamping company, the Circle Record Company, owned by Max and Sol Weiss. The Weiss brothers soon changed the name of their business to
      <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_Records" title="Fantasy Records">
       Fantasy Records
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      These initial Brubeck records sold well, and he recorded and issued new records for Fantasy. Soon the company was shipping 40,000 to 50,000 copies of Brubeck records each quarter, making enormous profits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        <div class="magnify">

         </a>

        <i>
         Time Out
        </i>
        sessions. From left to right: Joe Morello, Paul Desmond, Dave Brubeck, Eugene Wright.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1951, Brubeck damaged several neck vertebrae and his spinal cord while diving into the surf in
      <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
       Hawaii
      </a>
      . He would later remark that the paramedics who attended had described him as a "DOA" (dead on arrival). Brubeck recovered after a few months, but suffered with residual nerve pain in his hands for years after.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpost_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-washpost-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The injury also influenced his playing style towards complex, blocky chords rather than speedy, high-dexterity, single-note runs.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brubeck organized the Dave Brubeck Quartet in 1951, with
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Desmond" title="Paul Desmond">
       Paul Desmond
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Alto_saxophone" title="Alto saxophone">
       alto saxophone
      </a>
      . They took up a long residency at San Francisco's
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_(nightclub)" title="Black Hawk (nightclub)">
       Black Hawk nightclub
      </a>
      and gained great popularity touring
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/College_campus" title="College campus">
       college campuses
      </a>
      , recording a series of albums with such titles as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jazz_at_Oberlin" title="Jazz at Oberlin">
        Jazz at Oberlin
       </a>
      </i>
      (1953),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jazz_at_the_College_of_the_Pacific" title="Jazz at the College of the Pacific">
        Jazz at the College of the Pacific
       </a>
      </i>
      (1953), and Brubeck's debut on
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">
       Columbia Records
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jazz_Goes_to_College" title="Jazz Goes to College">
        Jazz Goes to College
       </a>
      </i>
      (1954).
     </p>
     <p>
      When Brubeck signed with
      <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_Records" title="Fantasy Records">
       Fantasy Records
      </a>
      , he thought he had a half interest in the company and he worked as a sort of
      <a href="/wiki/Artists_and_repertoire" title="Artists and repertoire">
       A &amp; R
      </a>
      man for the label, encouraging the Weiss brothers to sign other contemporary jazz performers, including
      <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">
       Gerry Mulligan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker" title="Chet Baker">
       Chet Baker
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Norvo" title="Red Norvo">
       Red Norvo
      </a>
      . When he discovered that all he owned was a half interest in his own recording, he was more than willing to sign with another label,
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">
       Columbia Records
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1954, he was featured on the cover of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      , the second jazz musician to be so honored (the first was
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Armstrong" title="Louis Armstrong">
       Louis Armstrong
      </a>
      on February 21, 1949).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brubeck personally found this accolade embarrassing, since he considered
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">
       Duke Ellington
      </a>
      more deserving of it and was convinced that he had been favored for being Caucasian.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ellington himself knocked on the door of Brubeck's hotel room to show him the cover and the only reaction Brubeck could give was, It should have been you.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Early bassists for the group included Ron Crotty,
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Bates_(musician)" title="Bob Bates (musician)">
       Bob Bates
      </a>
      , and Bob's brother
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Bates_(musician)" title="Norman Bates (musician)">
       Norman Bates
      </a>
      ; Lloyd Davis and Joe Dodge held the drum chair. In 1956 Brubeck hired drummer
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Morello" title="Joe Morello">
       Joe Morello
      </a>
      , who had been working with
      <a href="/wiki/Marian_McPartland" title="Marian McPartland">
       Marian McPartland
      </a>
      ; Morello's presence made possible the rhythmic experiments that were to come. In 1958 African-American bassist
      <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Wright" title="Eugene Wright">
       Eugene Wright
      </a>
      joined for the group's
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">
       U.S. Department of State
      </a>
      tour of Europe and Asia. Wright became a permanent member in 1959, making the "classic" Quartet's personnel complete. During the late 1950s and early 1960s Brubeck canceled several concerts because the club owners or hall managers continued to resist the idea of an integrated band on their stages. He also canceled a television appearance when he found out that the producers intended to keep Wright off-camera.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atlantic_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-atlantic-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1959, the Dave Brubeck Quartet recorded
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(album)" title="Time Out (album)">
        Time Out
       </a>
      </i>
      , an album about which the record label was enthusiastic but which they were nonetheless hesitant to release. Featuring the album art of
      <a href="/wiki/S._Neil_Fujita" title="S. Neil Fujita">
       S. Neil Fujita
      </a>
      , the album contained all original compositions, almost none of which were in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_time" title="Common time">
       common time
      </a>
      : 9/8, 5/4, 3/4, and 6/4 were used inspired by Eurasian folk music they experienced during that Department of State sponsored tour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nonetheless, on the strength of these unusual time signatures (the album included "
      <a href="/wiki/Take_Five" title="Take Five">
       Take Five
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Rondo_%C3%A0_la_Turk" title="Blue Rondo  la Turk">
       Blue Rondo  la Turk
      </a>
      ", and "Three To Get Ready"), it quickly went
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_album" title="Platinum album">
       platinum
      </a>
      . It was the first jazz album to sell more than a million copies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Time Out
      </i>
      was followed by several albums with a similar approach, including
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Further_Out" title="Time Further Out">
        Time Further Out: Miro Reflections
       </a>
      </i>
      (1961), using more 5/4, 6/4, and 9/8, plus the first attempt at 7/4;
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Countdown%E2%80%94Time_in_Outer_Space" title="CountdownTime in Outer Space">
        CountdownTime in Outer Space
       </a>
      </i>
      (dedicated to
      <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">
       John Glenn
      </a>
      ) (1962), featuring 11/4 and more 7/4;
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Changes" title="Time Changes">
        Time Changes
       </a>
      </i>
      (1963), with much 3/4, 10/4 (which was really 5+5), and 13/4; and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_In_(album)" title="Time In (album)">
        Time In
       </a>
      </i>
      (1966).
     </p>
     <p>
      These albums (except the last) were also known for using contemporary paintings as cover art, featuring the work of
      <a href="/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Joan Mir">
       Joan Mir
      </a>
      on
      <i>
       Time Further Out
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Franz_Kline" title="Franz Kline">
       Franz Kline
      </a>
      on
      <i>
       Time in Outer Space
      </i>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Francis" title="Sam Francis">
       Sam Francis
      </a>
      on
      <i>
       Time Changes
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A high point for the group was their 1963 live album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/At_Carnegie_Hall" title="At Carnegie Hall">
        At Carnegie Hall
       </a>
      </i>
      , described by critic
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Palmer_(entrepreneur)" title="Richard Palmer (entrepreneur)">
       Richard Palmer
      </a>
      as "arguably Dave Brubeck's greatest concert".
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 1960s, Brubeck and his wife Iola developed a jazz musical,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Real_Ambassadors" title="The Real Ambassadors">
        The Real Ambassadors
       </a>
      </i>
      , based in part on experiences they and their colleagues had during foreign tours on behalf of the Department of State. The soundtrack album, which featured
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Armstrong" title="Louis Armstrong">
       Louis Armstrong
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lambert,_Hendricks_%26_Ross" title="Lambert, Hendricks &amp; Ross">
       Lambert, Hendricks &amp; Ross
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Carmen_McRae" title="Carmen McRae">
       Carmen McRae
      </a>
      was recorded in 1961; the musical was performed at the 1962
      <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Jazz_Festival" title="Monterey Jazz Festival">
       Monterey Jazz Festival
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      At its peak in the early 1960s, the Brubeck Quartet was releasing as many as four albums a year. Apart from the "College" and the "Time" series, Brubeck recorded four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album">
       LPs
      </a>
      featuring his compositions based on the group's travels, and the local music they encountered.
      <i>
       Jazz Impressions of the USA
      </i>
      (1956, Morello's debut with the group),
      <i>
       Jazz Impressions of Eurasia
      </i>
      (1958),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jazz_Impressions_of_Japan" title="Jazz Impressions of Japan">
        Jazz Impressions of Japan
       </a>
      </i>
      (1964), and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Jazz_Impressions_of_New_York" title="Jazz Impressions of New York">
        Jazz Impressions of New York
       </a>
      </i>
      (1964) are less well-known albums, but all are brilliant examples of the quartet's studio work, and they produced Brubeck standards such as "Summer Song," "Brandenburg Gate," "Koto Song," and "Theme From
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mr._Broadway" title="Mr. Broadway">
        Mr. Broadway
       </a>
      </i>
      ." (Brubeck wrote, and the Quartet performed, the theme song for the
      <a href="/wiki/Craig_Stevens_(actor)" title="Craig Stevens (actor)">
       Craig Stevens
      </a>
      CBS drama series; the music from the series became material for the "New York" album.) In 1961, Brubeck appeared in a few scenes of the British jazz/beat film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/All_Night_Long_(1962_film)" title="All Night Long (1962 film)">
        All Night Long
       </a>
      </i>
      , which starred
      <a href="/wiki/Patrick_McGoohan" title="Patrick McGoohan">
       Patrick McGoohan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Attenborough" title="Richard Attenborough">
       Richard Attenborough
      </a>
      . Brubeck merely plays himself, with the film featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Close-up" title="Close-up">
       close-ups
      </a>
      of his piano fingerings. Brubeck performs "It's a Raggy Waltz" from the
      <i>
       Time Further Out
      </i>
      album and duets briefly with bassist
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mingus" title="Charles Mingus">
       Charles Mingus
      </a>
      in "Non-Sectarian Blues".
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 1960s Dave Brubeck was the program director of WJZZ-FM radio (now
      <a href="/wiki/WEZN-FM" title="WEZN-FM">
       WEZN-FM
      </a>
      ). He achieved his vision of an all-jazz format radio station along with his friend and neighbor John E. Metts, one of the first African Americans in senior radio management. The final studio album for Columbia by the Desmond/Wright/Morello quartet was
      <i>
       Anything Goes
      </i>
      (1966) featuring the songs of
      <a href="/wiki/Cole_Porter" title="Cole Porter">
       Cole Porter
      </a>
      . A few concert recordings followed, and
      <i>
       The Last Time We Saw Paris
      </i>
      (1967) was the "Classic" Quartet's swan-song.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">
       Later career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dave_Brubeck_1972_(Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="681" data-file-width="1024" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Dave_Brubeck_1972_%28Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80%29.jpg/220px-Dave_Brubeck_1972_%28Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Dave_Brubeck_1972_%28Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80%29.jpg/330px-Dave_Brubeck_1972_%28Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Dave_Brubeck_1972_%28Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80%29.jpg/440px-Dave_Brubeck_1972_%28Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dave_Brubeck_1972_(Heinrich_Klaffs_Collection_80).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>

        Brubeck in a 1972 performance in Hamburg
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Brubeck's disbanding of the Quartet at the end of 1967 allowed him more time to compose the longer, extended orchestral and choral works that were occupying his attention (to say nothing of his desire to spend more time with his family).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      February 1968 saw the premiere of
      <i>
       The Light in the Wilderness
      </i>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Baritone" title="Baritone">
       baritone
      </a>
      solo, the Miami University A Capella Singers, organ, the
      <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra">
       Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra
      </a>
      conducted by
      <a href="/wiki/Erich_Kunzel" title="Erich Kunzel">
       Erich Kunzel
      </a>
      , and Brubeck improvising on certain themes therein. The piece is an
      <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">
       oratorio
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      's teachings in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Matthew" title="Book of Matthew">
       Book of Matthew
      </a>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , and was recorded in March 1968 by Brubeck, the orchestra and choir, and performed by them throughout Europe the following summer.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next year, Brubeck produced
      <i>
       The Gates of Justice
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">
       cantata
      </a>
      mixing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical_scripture" title="Biblical scripture">
       Biblical scripture
      </a>
      with the words of Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">
       Martin Luther King, Jr.
      </a>
      The new senior management at Columbia Records decided not to renew Brubeck's contract in 1971, as they wished for focus on rock music. He moved to Atlantic Records.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His "Take Five" was used on the soundtrack of the 1973
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bakshi" title="Ralph Bakshi">
       Ralph Bakshi
      </a>
      adult cartoon movie
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Heavy_Traffic" title="Heavy Traffic">
        Heavy Traffic
       </a>
      </i>
      , and his music was also used in the 1985 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ordeal_by_Innocence_(film)" title="Ordeal by Innocence (film)">
        Ordeal by Innocence
       </a>
      </i>
      . He also composed for  and performed with his ensemble on  "The NASA Space Station," a 1988 episode of the CBS TV series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/This_Is_America,_Charlie_Brown" title="This Is America, Charlie Brown">
        This Is America, Charlie Brown
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCDB_December_2012_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BCDB_December_2012-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

       </span>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

     <p>
      Dave Brubeck married jazz lyricist Iola Brubeck in September 1942. She survived him, later dying on March 12, 2014 from cancer in
      <a href="/wiki/Wilton,_Connecticut" title="Wilton, Connecticut">
       Wilton, Connecticut
      </a>
      , at the age of 90.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Starr2009_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Starr2009-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Five of Brubeck's six children have been professional musicians.
      <a href="/wiki/Darius_Brubeck" title="Darius Brubeck">
       Darius
      </a>
      , the eldest, is a pianist, producer, educator and performer. (He was named after Dave Brubeck's mentor
      <a href="/wiki/Darius_Milhaud" title="Darius Milhaud">
       Darius Milhaud
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) Dan is a percussionist, Chris is a multi-instrumentalist and composer.
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Brubeck" title="Matt Brubeck">
       Matthew
      </a>
      , the youngest, is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellist" title="Cellist">
       cellist
      </a>
      with an extensive list of composing and performance credits. Another son, Michael, who died in 2009, was a saxophonist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpost_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-washpost-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Brubeck's children often joined him in concerts and in the recording studio.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brubeck believed that what he saw during his time as a soldier in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      contradicted the
      <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">
       Ten Commandments
      </a>
      , and the war evoked a spiritual awakening.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      He became a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">
       Catholic
      </a>
      in 1980, shortly after completing the Mass
      <i>
       To Hope
      </i>
      which had been commissioned by Ed Murray, editor of the national Catholic weekly
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Our_Sunday_Visitor" title="Our Sunday Visitor">
        Our Sunday Visitor
       </a>
      </i>
      . Although he had spiritual interests before that time, he said, "I didn't convert to Catholicism, because I wasn't anything to convert from. I just joined the Catholic Church."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1996, he received the
      <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Lifetime_Achievement_Award" title="Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award">
       Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award
      </a>
      . In 2006, Brubeck was awarded the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Notre_Dame" title="University of Notre Dame">
       University of Notre Dame
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Laetare_Medal" title="Laetare Medal">
       Laetare Medal
      </a>
      , the oldest and most prestigious
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      honor given to American Catholics, during the University's commencement. He performed "Travellin' Blues" for the graduating class of 2006.
     </p>
     <p>
      Brubeck founded the Brubeck Institute with his wife, Iola, at their alma mater, the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Pacific_(United_States)" title="University of the Pacific (United States)">
       University of the Pacific
      </a>
      in 2000. What began as a special archive, consisting of the personal document collection of the Brubecks, has since expanded to provide fellowships and educational opportunities in jazz for students, also leading to having one of the main streets on which the school resides named in his honor, Dave Brubeck Way.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Brubeck recorded five of the seven tracks of his album
      <i>
       Jazz Goes to College
      </i>
      in Ann Arbor. He returned to Michigan many times, including a performance at Hill Auditorium where he received a Distinguished Artist Award from the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">
       University of Michigan
      </a>
      's Musical Society in 2006. On April 8, 2008,
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">
       United States Secretary of State
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Condoleezza_Rice" title="Condoleezza Rice">
       Condoleezza Rice
      </a>
      presented Brubeck with a "Benjamin Franklin Award for Public Diplomacy" for offering an American "vision of hope, opportunity and freedom" through his music.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLPublicdiplomacy_33-0">

        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Department" title="State Department">
       State Department
      </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLPublicdiplomacy_33-1">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_tributes">
       Death and tributes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Brubeck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death and tributes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Brubeck died of heart failure on December 5, 2012, in
      <a href="/wiki/Norwalk,_Connecticut" title="Norwalk, Connecticut">
       Norwalk, Connecticut
      </a>
      , one day before his 92nd birthday. He was on his way to a cardiology appointment, accompanied by his son Darius.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tribune_2012_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tribune_2012-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A birthday party concert had been planned for him with family and famous guests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatoday-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was recast as a memorial tribute.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">
        Los Angeles Times
       </a>
      </i>
      noted that he "was one of Jazz's first pop stars," even though he was not always happy with his fame, uncomfortable, for example, that
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      had featured him on the cover
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      before it did so for
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">
       Duke Ellington
      </a>
      , saying, "It just bothered me".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-latimes-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
        The New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      noted he had continued to play well into his old age, performing in 2011 and in 2010 only a month after getting a
      <a href="/wiki/Artificial_cardiac_pacemaker" title="Artificial cardiac pacemaker">
       pacemaker
      </a>
      , with
      <i>
       Times
      </i>
      music writer Nate Chinen commenting that Brubeck had replaced "the old hammer-and-anvil attack with something almost airy" and that his playing at the
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Note_Jazz_Club" title="Blue Note Jazz Club">
       Blue Note Jazz Club
      </a>
      in New York City was "the picture of judicious clarity".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       The Daily Telegraph
      </i>
      , music journalist Ivan Hewett wrote: "Brubeck didnt have the rclame of some jazz musicians who lead tragic lives. He didnt do drugs or drink. What he had was endless curiosity combined with stubbornness", adding "His work list is astonishing, including oratorios, musicals and concertos, as well as hundreds of jazz compositions. This quiet man of jazz was truly a marvel."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hewett_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hewett-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       The Guardian
      </i>
      , John Fordham said "Brubeck's real achievement was to blend European compositional ideas, very demanding rhythmic structures, jazz song-forms and improvisation in expressive and accessible ways. His son Chris told the
      <i>
       Guardian
      </i>
      "when I hear Chorale, it reminds me of the very best
      <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">
       Aaron Copland
      </a>
      , something like Appalachian Spring. There's a sort of American honesty to it."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fordham_48-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fordham-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">
       Robert Christgau
      </a>
      dubbed Brubeck the "jazz hero of the rock and roll generation".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">
       The Economist
      </a>
      wrote: "Above all they found it hard to believe that the most successful jazz in America was being played by a family man, a laid-back Californian, modest, gentle and open, who would happily have been a rancher all his daysexcept that he couldnt live without performing, because the rhythm of jazz, under all his extrapolation and exploration, was, he had discovered, the rhythm of his heart."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On the night of Brubeck's death, right before the intermission of a performance for
      <a href="/wiki/Chick_Corea" title="Chick Corea">
       Chick Corea
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gary_Burton" title="Gary Burton">
       Gary Burton
      </a>
      's "
      <a href="/wiki/Hot_House_(Gary_Burton_and_Chick_Corea_album)" title="Hot House (Gary Burton and Chick Corea album)">
       Hot House
      </a>
      ", a tribute was performed solely by Corea at
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koerner_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koerner Hall (page does not exist)">
       Koerner Hall
      </a>
      at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Conservatory_of_Music" title="Royal Conservatory of Music">
       Royal Conservatory of Music
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toronto,_Ontario,_Canada" title="Toronto, Ontario, Canada">
       Toronto, Ontario, Canada
      </a>
      . The tune played was "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strange_Meadow_Lark" title="Strange Meadow Lark">
       Strange Meadow Lark
      </a>
      ", from Brubeck's perhaps most famous album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(album)" title="Time Out (album)">
        Time Out
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
