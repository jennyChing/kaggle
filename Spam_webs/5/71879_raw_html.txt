     <p>
      A
      <b>
       tin foil hat
      </b>
      is a hat made from one or more sheets of
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_foil" title="Aluminium foil">
       aluminium foil
      </a>
      , or a piece of conventional headgear lined with foil, worn in the belief it shields the
      <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">
       brain
      </a>
      from threats such as
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">
       electromagnetic fields
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mind_control" title="Mind control">
       mind control
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Telepathy" title="Telepathy">
       mind reading
      </a>
      . "Tin foil" is a common
      <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">
       misnomer
      </a>
      for aluminum foil; metal foil was formerly made out of tin before it was replaced by aluminum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The notion of wearing homemade headgear for such protection has become a popular
      <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">
       stereotype
      </a>
      and byword for
      <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">
       paranoia
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persecutory_delusions" title="Persecutory delusions">
       persecutory delusions
      </a>
      , and belief in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo_science" title="Pseudo science">
       pseudo science
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theories" title="Conspiracy theories">
       conspiracy theories
      </a>
      . Foil hats have appeared in the films
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Signs_(film)" title="Signs (film)">
        Signs
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Futurama:_Into_the_Wild_Green_Yonder" title="Futurama: Into the Wild Green Yonder">
        Futurama: Into the Wild Green Yonder
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
       Origin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin_foil_hat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The concept of a foil hat for protection against interference of the mind was mentioned in a science fiction short story by
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_Huxley" title="Julian Huxley">
       Julian Huxley
      </a>
      , "The Tissue-Culture King", first published in 1927, in which the protagonist discovers that "caps of metal foil" can block the effects of telepathy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huxley1927_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Huxley1927-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The use of foil headgear is often based on a belief that such hats prevent mind control by governments, spies, or paranormal beings that employ
      <a href="/wiki/Extrasensory_perception" title="Extrasensory perception">
       ESP
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect" title="Microwave auditory effect">
       microwave auditory effect
      </a>
      . People in many countries who believe they are "Targeted individuals" (TIs), subject to government spying or harassment, have developed websites, conference calls, and support meetings to discuss their concerns; a favorite topic is headgear and shields made of foil or other metal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over time the term "tin foil hat" has become associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">
       paranoia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">
       conspiracy theories
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crazy_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Crazy-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_basis">
       Scientific basis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin_foil_hat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific basis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electromagnetic_radiation">
       Electromagnetic radiation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin_foil_hat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electromagnetic radiation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The notion that a metal foil hat can significantly reduce the intensity of incident radio frequency radiation on the wearer's brain has some scientific validity, as the effect of strong radio waves has been documented for quite some time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref_1979_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ref_1979-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A well-constructed aluminum foil enclosure would approximate a
      <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cage" title="Faraday cage">
       Faraday cage
      </a>
      , reducing the amount of (typically harmless)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">
       radiofrequency electromagnetic radiation
      </a>
      passing through to the interior of the structure. A common high school physics demonstration involves placing an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_radio" title="AM radio">
       AM radio
      </a>
      on aluminum foil, and then covering the radio with a metal bucket. This leads to a noticeable reduction in signal strength. The efficiency of such an enclosure in blocking such radiation depends on the thickness of the foil, as dictated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Skin_effect" title="Skin effect">
       skin depth
      </a>
      , the distance the radiation can propagate in a particular non-ideal
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">
       conductor
      </a>
      . For half-millimetre-thick aluminum foil, radiation above about 20kHz (i.e., including both
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_band" title="AM band">
       AM
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FM_band" title="FM band">
       FM bands
      </a>
      ) would be partially blocked, although aluminum foil is not sold in this thickness, so numerous layers of foil would be required to achieve this effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson1998_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jackson1998-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The effectiveness of the aluminum foil hat as
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_shielding" title="Electromagnetic shielding">
       electromagnetic shielding
      </a>
      for stopping radio waves is greatly reduced by it not being a complete enclosure. Placing an AM radio under a metal bucket without a conductive layer underneath demonstrates the relative ineffectiveness of such a setup. Indeed, because the effect of an ungrounded Faraday cage is to partially reflect the incident radiation, a radio wave that is incident on the inner surface of the hat (i.e., coming from underneath the hat-wearer) would be reflected and partially 'focused' towards the user's brain. However the hat may be partially grounded by the conductive properties of the skin with which it contacts.
     </p>
     <p>
      A study by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graduate_student" title="Graduate student">
       graduate students
      </a>
      at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">
       MIT
      </a>
      determined that an aluminum foil hat could attenuate incoming radiation depending on frequency. At WIFI frequencies - 2.4GHz is attenuated by up to 90dB; the effect was observed to be roughly independent of the relative placement of the wearer and radiation source.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rahimi2005_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rahimi2005-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At some microwave wavelengths, the
      <a href="/wiki/Skin_effect" title="Skin effect">
       skin depth
      </a>
      is less than the thickness of even the thinnest foil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A belief also exists that aluminum foil is a protective measure against the effects of
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
       electromagnetic radiation
      </a>
      (EMR). Despite some allegations that EMR exposure has negative health consequences,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lean2006_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lean2006-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      at this time no link has been established between the radio-frequency EMR that foil hats are meant to protect against, and subsequent ill health.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref__10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ref_-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1962,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allan_H._Frey" title="Allan H. Frey">
       Allan H. Frey
      </a>
      discovered that reception of the induced sounds by radio-frequency electromagnetic signals heard as clicks and buzzes can be blocked by a patch of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wire_mesh" title="Wire mesh">
       wire mesh
      </a>
      (rather than foil) placed above the
      <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">
       temporal lobe
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elder_.26_Chou_2003_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Elder_.26_Chou_2003-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
