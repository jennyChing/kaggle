     <p>
      <b>
       Actornetwork theory
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       ANT
      </b>
      ) is an approach to
      <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">
       social theory
      </a>
      and research, originating in the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Science_studies" title="Science studies">
       science studies
      </a>
      , which treats objects as part of social networks. Although it is best known for its controversial insistence on the capacity of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non_human" title="Non human">
       nonhumans
      </a>
      to act or participate in systems or networks or both, ANT is also associated with forceful critiques of conventional and critical sociology. Developed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">
       science and technology studies
      </a>
      (STS) scholars
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Callon" title="Michel Callon">
       Michel Callon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Latour" title="Bruno Latour">
       Bruno Latour
      </a>
      , the sociologist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Law_(sociologist)" title="John Law (sociologist)">
       John Law
      </a>
      , and others, it can more technically be described as a "material-semiotic" method. This means that it maps relations that are simultaneously material (between things) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiotic" title="Semiotic">
       semiotic
      </a>
      (between concepts). It assumes that many relations are both material and semiotic.
     </p>
     <p>
      Broadly speaking, ANT is a
      <a href="/wiki/Social_constructivism" title="Social constructivism">
       constructivist
      </a>
      approach in that it avoids
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essentialist" title="Essentialist">
       essentialist
      </a>
      explanations of events or innovations (e.g. explaining a successful theory by understanding the combinations and interactions of elements that make it successful, rather than saying it is true and the others are false). However, it is distinguished from many other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">
       STS
      </a>
      and sociological
      <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">
       network theories
      </a>
      for its distinct material-semiotic approach.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_context">
       Background and context
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and context">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ANT was first developed at the
      <a href="/wiki/Centre_de_Sociologie_de_l%27Innovation" title="Centre de Sociologie de l'Innovation">
       Centre de Sociologie de l'Innovation
      </a>
      (CSI) of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_nationale_sup%C3%A9rieure_des_mines_de_Paris" title="cole nationale suprieure des mines de Paris">
       cole nationale suprieure des mines de Paris
      </a>
      in the early 1980s by staff (
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Callon" title="Michel Callon">
       Michel Callon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Latour" title="Bruno Latour">
       Bruno Latour
      </a>
      ) and visitors (including
      <a href="/wiki/John_Law_(sociologist)" title="John Law (sociologist)">
       John Law
      </a>
      ). The 1984 book co-authored by John Law and fellow-sociologist Peter Lodge (Science for Social Scientists; London: Macmillan Press LTD) is a good example of early explorations of how the growth and structure of knowledge could be analyzed and interpreted through the interactions of actors and networks. Initially created in an attempt to understand processes of innovation and knowledge-creation in science and technology, the approach drew on existing work in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">
       STS
      </a>
      , on studies of large technological systems (see
      <a href="/wiki/Large_technical_system" title="Large technical system">
       large technical system
      </a>
      ), and on a range of French intellectual resources including the semiotics of
      <a href="/wiki/Algirdas_Julien_Greimas" title="Algirdas Julien Greimas">
       Algirdas Julien Greimas
      </a>
      , the writing of philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Serres" title="Michel Serres">
       Michel Serres
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Annales_School" title="Annales School">
       Annales School
      </a>
      of history.
     </p>
     <p>
      ANT appears to reflect many of the preoccupations of French
      <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">
       post-structuralism
      </a>
      , and in particular a concern with non-foundational and multiple material-semiotic relations. At the same time, it was much more firmly embedded in English-language academic traditions than most post-structuralist-influenced approaches. Its grounding in (predominantly English)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">
       science and technology studies
      </a>
      was reflected in an intense commitment to the development of theory through qualitative empirical case-studies. Its links with largely US-originated work on large technical systems were reflected in its willingness to analyse large scale technological developments in an even-handed manner to include political, organizational, legal, technical and scientific factors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many of the characteristic ANT tools (including the notions of translation, generalized symmetry and the heterogeneous network), together with a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientometric" title="Scientometric">
       scientometric
      </a>
      tool for mapping innovations in science and technology (co-word analysis) were initially developed during the 1980s, predominantly in and around the CSI. The state of the art of ANT in the late 1980s is well-described in Latours 1987 text,
      <i>
       Science in Action
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-science-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From about 1990 onwards, ANT started to become popular as a tool for analysis in a range of fields beyond STS. It was picked up and developed by authors in parts of organizational analysis, informatics, health studies, geography, sociology, anthropology, feminist studies and economics.
     </p>
     <p>
      As of 2008
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , ANT is a widespread, if controversial range of material-semiotic approaches for the analysis of heterogeneous relations. In part because of its popularity, it is interpreted and used in a wide range of alternative and sometimes incompatible ways. There is no orthodoxy in current ANT, and different authors use the approach in substantially different ways. Some authors talk of after-ANT to refer to successor projects blending together different problem-focuses with those of ANT.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="A_material-semiotic_method">
       A material-semiotic method
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A material-semiotic method">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although it is called a 
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_theory" title="Philosophical theory">
       theory
      </a>
      , ANT does not usually explain why or "how" a network takes the form that it does.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RtS_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-RtS-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rather, ANT is a way of thoroughly exploring the relational ties within a network (which can be a multitude of different things). As Latour notes,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technology_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-technology-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "explanation does not follow from description; it is description taken that much further." It is not, in other words, a theory "of" anything, but rather a method, or a "how-to book" as Latour
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RtS_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-RtS-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      puts it.
     </p>
     <p>
      The approach is related to other versions of material-semiotics (notably the work of philosophers
      <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Deleuze" title="Gilles Deleuze">
       Gilles Deleuze
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">
       Michel Foucault
      </a>
      and feminist scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Donna_Haraway" title="Donna Haraway">
       Donna Haraway
      </a>
      ). It can also be seen as a way of being faithful to the insights of
      <a href="/wiki/Ethnomethodology" title="Ethnomethodology">
       ethnomethodology
      </a>
      and its detailed descriptions of how common activities, habits and procedures sustain themselves. Similarities between ANT and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolic_interactionist" title="Symbolic interactionist">
       symbolic interactionist
      </a>
      approaches such as the newer forms of
      <a href="/wiki/Grounded_theory" title="Grounded theory">
       grounded theory
      </a>
      like
      <a href="/wiki/Situational_analysis" title="Situational analysis">
       situational analysis
      </a>
      , exist,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although Latour
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      objects to such a comparison.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although ANT is mostly associated with studies of science and technology and with the sociology of science, it has been making steady progress in other fields of sociology as well. ANT is adamantly empirical, and as such yields useful insights and tools for sociological inquiry in general. ANT has been deployed in studies of identity and subjectivity, urban transportation systems, and passion and addiction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It also makes steady progress in political and historical sociology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_actor-network">
       The actor-network
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The actor-network">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As the term implies, the actor-network is the central concept in ANT. The term "network" is somewhat problematic in that it, as Latour
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RtS_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-RtS-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technology_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-technology-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-on-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      notes, has a number of unwanted connotations. Firstly, it implies that what is described takes the shape of a network, which is not necessarily the case. Secondly, it implies "transportation without deformation," which, in ANT, is not possible since any actor-network involves a vast number of
      <a href="#Translation">
       translations
      </a>
      . Latour,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-on-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      however, still contends that network is a fitting term to use, because "it has no a priori order relation; it is not tied to the axiological myth of a top and of a bottom of society; it makes absolutely no assumption whether a specific locus is macro- or micro- and does not modify the tools to study the element 'a' or the element 'b'." This use of the term "network" is very similar to Deleuze and Guattari's
      <a href="/wiki/Rhizome_(philosophy)" title="Rhizome (philosophy)">
       rhizomes
      </a>
      ; Latour
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technology_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-technology-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      even remarks tongue in cheek that he would have no objection to renaming ANT "actant-rhizome ontology" if it only had sounded better, which hints at Latour's uneasiness with the word "theory".
     </p>
     <p>
      Actornetwork theory tries to explain how materialsemiotic networks come together to act as a whole; the clusters of actors involved in creating meaning are both material and semiotic. As a part of this it may look at explicit strategies for relating different elements together into a network so that they form an apparently coherent whole. These networks are potentially transient, existing in a constant making and re-making.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RtS_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-RtS-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This means that relations need to be repeatedly performed or the network will dissolve. They also assume that networks of relations are not intrinsically coherent, and may indeed contain conflicts. Social relations, in other words, are only ever in process, and must be
      <a href="/wiki/Performativity" title="Performativity">
       performed
      </a>
      continuously.
     </p>
     <p>
      Actants denote human and non-human actors, and in a network take the shape that they do by virtue of their relations with one another. It assumes that nothing lies outside the network of relations, and as noted above, suggests that there is no difference in the ability of technology, humans, animals, or other non-humans to act (and that there are only enacted alliances.) As soon as an actor engages with an actor-network it too is caught up in the web of relations, and becomes part of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entelechy" title="Entelechy">
       entelechy
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      If taken to its logical conclusion, then, nearly any actor can be considered merely a sum of other, smaller actors. A car is an example of a complex system. It contains many electronic and
      <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">
       mechanical
      </a>
      components, all of which are essentially hidden from view to the driver, who simply deals with the car as a single object. This effect is known as
      <i>
       punctualisation
      </i>
      , and is similar to the idea of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encapsulation_(object-oriented_programming)" title="Encapsulation (object-oriented programming)">
       encapsulation
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">
       object-oriented programming
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      When an actor network breaks down, the punctualisation effect tends to cease as well. In the automobile example above, a non-working engine would cause the driver to become aware of the car as a collection of parts rather than just a vehicle capable of transporting him or her from place to place. This can also occur when elements of a network act contrarily to the network as a whole. In his book
      <i>
       Pandoras Hope
      </i>
      , Latour likens depunctualization to the opening of a black box. When closed, the box is perceived simply as a box, although when it is opened all elements inside it becomes visible.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_and_non-human_actors">
       Human and non-human actors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Human and non-human actors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      ANT is often associated with the equal treatment of human and non-human actors. ANT assumes that all entities in a network can and should be described in the same terms. This is called the principle of
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Generalized_symmetry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Generalized symmetry (page does not exist)">
        generalized symmetry
       </a>
      </i>
      . The rationale for this is that differences between them are generated in the network of relations, and should not be presupposed.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intermediaries_and_mediators">
       Intermediaries and mediators
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Intermediaries and mediators">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The distinction between intermediaries and mediators is key to ANT sociology. Intermediaries are entities which make no difference (to some interesting state of affairs which we are studying) and so can be ignored. They transport the force of some other entity more or less without transformation and so are fairly uninteresting. Mediators are entities which multiply difference and so should be the object of study. Their outputs cannot be predicted by their inputs. From an ANT point of view sociology has tended to treat too much of the world as intermediaries.
     </p>
     <p>
      For instance, a sociologist might take silk and nylon as intermediaries, holding that the former means, reflects, or symbolises the upper classes and the latter the lower classes. In such a view the real world silknylon difference is irrelevant  presumably many other material differences could also, and do also, transport this class distinction. But taken as mediators these fabrics would have to be engaged with by the analyst in their specificity: the internal real-world complexities of silk and nylon suddenly appear relevant, and are seen as actively constructing the ideological class distinction which they once merely reflected.
     </p>
     <p>
      For the committed ANT analyst, social thingslike class distinctions in taste in the silk and nylon example, but also groups and powermust constantly be constructed or performed anew through complex engagements with complex mediators. There is no stand-alone social repertoire lying in the background to be reflected off, expressed through, or substantiated in, interactions (as in an intermediary conception).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RtS_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-RtS-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_central_concepts">
       Other central concepts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other central concepts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Translation">
       Translation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Translation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Translation_(sociology)" title="Translation (sociology)">
       Translation (sociology)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Central to ANT is the concept of translation which is sometimes referred to as
      <b>
       sociology of translation
      </b>
      , in which innovators attempt to create a
      <i>
       forum,
      </i>
      a central network in which all the actors agree that the network is worth building and defending. In his widely debated 1986 study of how marine biologists try to restock the St Brieuc Bay in order to produce more scallops,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Callon" title="Michel Callon">
       Michel Callon
      </a>
      has defined 4 moments of translation:
      <a href="/wiki/Problematization" title="Problematization">
       problematization
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Interessement" title="Interessement">
       interessement
      </a>
      , enrollment and mobilisation of allies.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tokens.2C_or_quasi-objects">
       Tokens, or quasi-objects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tokens, or quasi-objects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the above examples, social order and functioning car come into being through the successful interactions of their respective actor-networks, and actor-network theory refers to these creations as
      <i>
       tokens
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       quasi-objects
      </i>
      which are passed between actors within the network.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the token is increasingly transmitted or passed through the network, it becomes increasingly punctualized and also increasingly
      <a href="/wiki/Reification_(Marxism)" title="Reification (Marxism)">
       reified
      </a>
      . When the token is decreasingly transmitted, or when an actor fails to transmit the token (e.g., the oil pump breaks), punctualization and reification are decreased as well.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Actor%E2%80%93network_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Actornetwork theory insists on the capacity of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non_human" title="Non human">
       nonhumans
      </a>
      to be actors or participants in networks and systems. Critics including figures such as
      <a href="/wiki/Langdon_Winner" title="Langdon Winner">
       Langdon Winner
      </a>
      maintain that such properties as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Intentionality" title="Intentionality">
        intentionality
       </a>
      </i>
      fundamentally distinguish humans from animals or from things (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activity_Theory" title="Activity Theory">
       Activity Theory
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ANT scholars
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2012)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      respond with the following arguments:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       They do not attribute intentionality and similar properties to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non_human" title="Non human">
        nonhumans
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Their conception of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_agency" title="Human agency">
        agency
       </a>
       does not presuppose intentionality.
      </li>
      <li>
       They locate agency neither in human subjects nor in non-human objects, but in heterogeneous associations of humans and nonhumans.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      ANT has been criticized as amoral.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2014)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Wiebe_Bijker" title="Wiebe Bijker">
       Wiebe Bijker
      </a>
      has responded to this criticism by stating that the amorality of ANT is not a necessity. Moral and political positions are possible, but one must first describe the network before taking up such positions. This position has been further explored by Stuart Shapiro who contrasts ANT with the history of ecology, and argues that research decisions are moral rather than methodological, but this moral dimension has been sidelined.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Whittle and Spicer note that "ANT has also sought to move beyond deterministic models that trace organizational phenomena back to powerful individuals, social structures, hegemonic discourses or technological effects. Rather, ANT prefers to seek out complex patterns of causality rooted in connections between actors." They argue that ANT's ontological realism makes it, "less well equipped for pursuing a critical account of organizations that is, one which recognises the unfolding nature of reality, considers the limits of knowledge and seeks to challenge structures of domination."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This implies that ANT does not account for pre-existing structures, such as power, but rather sees these structures as emerging from the actions of actors within the network and their ability to align in pursuit of their interests. Accordingly, ANT can be seen as an attempt to re-introduce
      <a href="/wiki/Whig_history" title="Whig history">
       Whig history
      </a>
      into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">
       science and technology studies
      </a>
      ; like the myth of the
      <a href="/wiki/Heroic_theory_of_invention_and_scientific_development" title="Heroic theory of invention and scientific development">
       heroic inventor
      </a>
      , ANT can be seen as an attempt to explain successful innovators by saying only that they were successful. Likewise, for organization studies, Whittle and Spicer assert that ANT is, "ill suited to the task of developing political alternatives to the imaginaries of market managerialism."
     </p>
     <p>
      Key early criticism came from other members of the STS community, in particular the "Epistemological Chicken" debate between Collins and Yearley with responses from Latour and Callon as well as Woolgar.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Collins and Yearley accused ANTs approach of collapsing into an endless relativist regress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some critics
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      have argued that research based on ANT perspectives remains entirely descriptive and fails to provide explanations for social processes. ANTlike comparable social scientific methodsrequires judgement calls from the researcher as to which actors are important within a network and which are not. Critics
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      argue that the importance of particular actors cannot be determined in the absence of out-of-network criteria. Similarly, others
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2014)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      argue that actor-networks risk degenerating into endless chains of association (
      <a href="/wiki/Six_degrees_of_separation" title="Six degrees of separation">
       six degrees of separation
      </a>
      we are all networked to one another). Other research perspectives such as
      <a href="/wiki/Social_constructionism" title="Social constructionism">
       social constructionism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Social_shaping_of_technology" title="Social shaping of technology">
       social shaping of technology
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_network_theory" title="Social network theory">
       social network theory
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normalization_Process_Theory" title="Normalization Process Theory">
       Normalization Process Theory
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffusion_of_Innovations" title="Diffusion of Innovations">
       Diffusion of Innovations
      </a>
      theory are held to be important alternatives to ANT approaches.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a workshop called On Recalling ANT,
      <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Latour" title="Bruno Latour">
       Bruno Latour
      </a>
      stated that there are four things wrong with actor-network theory: actor, network, theory and the hyphen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a later book however (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Latour#Reassembling_the_Social" title="Bruno Latour">
        Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to ActorNetworkTheory
       </a>
      </i>
      ), Latour reversed himself, accepting the wide use of the term, 
      <i>
       including
      </i>
      the hyphen (Latour 2005:9).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="A more detailed citation is required. (May 2014)">
          full citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      He also remarked how he had been helpfully reminded that the ANT acronym was perfectly fit for a blind, myopic, workaholic, trail-sniffing, and collective traveler (the ant, Latour 2005:9)  qualitative hallmarks of actor-network
      <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">
       epistemology
      </a>
      .
     </p>
