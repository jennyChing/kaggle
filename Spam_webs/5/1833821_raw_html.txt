        <h1 class="blog-post-title">
         The Ultimate Guide to Pairing Wine and Cheese
        </h1>
        <div class="ccm-blog-post-envelope">
         <span class="ccm-blog-post-timestamp">
          May 8, 2015
         </span>
         <span class="ccm-blog-post-category">
          under
          <a href="http://www.cheesesticks.com/blog/category/authority guides">
           Authority Guides
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <div class="ccm-blog-post-content">
         <div class="ccm-blog-post-prefix">
          <div class="shareaholic-canvas" data-app="share_buttons" data-app-id="17883647">
          </div>
          <div style="clear: both;">
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="ccm-blog-post-body">
          <img height="600" src="/files/cache/40235a2fb197afc6b0e25814aa5d74ea_f458.png" style="float:right;padding: 10px;" width="600"/>
          <p>
           The art of pairing cheese and wine is a savory adventure that works well for mostly all gatherings. As a host, you will want to show your guests that you've done your homework and know exactly what to pair and serve for a delightful treat. There are many pairing charts available online that can be used for reference when planning your next event. However, not all provide detail of what snacks to serve as well.
          </p>
          <p>
           This chart, available on
           <a href="http://www.savory.tv/food-wine-pairing/">
            Savory.TVs website
           </a>
           , is a good example of what you might expect to find online. Although it provides a general idea of foods that would go well with particular wines, it doesnt provide a full list of wines that would pair well, nor a complete list of cheese varieties.
           <img alt="" height="385px;" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/nqvktXKUJ38XgbtVF2ntiDWQIdItbWLVsR9_IrGwlpem6mG4BjCM3u3BgPhodC87fsWQxAgl56dGcjKVYhx6bmqNKynWP0gewEiChaTdQXhAodXzZUhVzQrgpfOtqXSikBUSEn_EY-gQXhc8" width="600px;"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           Cheese and wine can form a flawless combination when suitably matched. If you dont know which combinations will work best together, our guide of cheese varieties from A-Z will help you learn which wine and cheese will couple best. Some of the selection also include suggested snacks that you can serve alongside the cheese and wine.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Abbaye De Belloc Cheese
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: France
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with sliced pears, apple, or some olives with sliced ham or salami. Serve with fresh bread from a bakery.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wines:
          </p>
          <p>
           Cabernet Sauvignon
          </p>
          <p>
           Pinot Noir
          </p>
          <p>
           Sauvignon Blanc
          </p>
          <p>
           Zinfandel
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Ardrahan cheese (Semi-Soft)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: Ireland
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with wheat crackers, grapes or apples.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wines:
          </p>
          <p>
           Chianti
          </p>
          <p>
           Pinot Noir
          </p>
          <p>
           Sangiovese
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Asadero cheese (Semi-Soft)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: Mexico
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with Rajas (scorched chile strips) and fresh corn tortillas.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wine:
          </p>
          <p>
           Chardonnay
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Asiago cheese (Hard)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin:Italy
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with water crackers, almonds, and walnuts.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wines:
          </p>
          <p>
           Malbec
          </p>
          <p>
           Riesling
          </p>
          <p>
           Rioja
          </p>
          <p>
           Sauvignon Blanc
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Azeitao (Soft)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: Portugal
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with grapes and brown bread with nuts.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wine:
          </p>
          <p>
           Tempranillo
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Baby Swiss Cheese (Semi-Soft)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: United States
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with whole grains, dried fruits, nuts, and bread.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wine:
          </p>
          <p>
           Asti Spumante
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Ballyoak cheese (Semi-Soft)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: Ireland
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with thin sliced baguette, water crackers, or apples.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wines:
          </p>
          <p>
           Pinot Noir
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Banon Cheese (Soft)
          </h2>
          <p>
           Tip: You want to choose full-bodied wines if the cheese is aged but go with lighter wines if its still young.
          </p>
          <p>
           Origin: France
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with grapes, water crackers or lightly toasted fresh bread slices.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wines:
          </p>
          <p>
           Chenin Blanc
          </p>
          <p>
           Red Bordeaux
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <h2>
           Beaufort cheese
          </h2>
          <p>
           Origin: France
          </p>
          <p>
           Goes well with sliced apples, grapes, or ripe pear with your favorite crackers.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pair with the following wines:
          </p>
          <p>
           Chateauneuf-du-Pape

            Wine and Cheese Taste Components
           </h2>
           <p>
            Cheese and wine elements can be thought of in terms of a hierarchy of taste, from textures to flavors. The primary components that touch the senses are saltiness, sourness, sweetness, and bitterness. How you combine those elements when pairing will determine the impact of combined tastes.
           </p>
           <p>
            Primary taste components are best defined as basic elements that correspond to the tongues basic sensory ability. Cheese and wine components are foundational elements that have a strong influence on the pleasant feeling of complementary or contrasting characteristics that a positive tasting experience provides.
           </p>
           <p>
            Wine has three primary sensory components: acidity, sweetness, and effervescence. These form the foundation of a match with the main sensory elements in cheese which are: sourness, bitterness, sweetness, and saltiness.
           </p>
           <p>
            The cheese sensory range is divided into three main sections which are components, texture, and flavor. With a total of nine elements that include sweetness, acidity, saltiness, bitterness, fattiness, body, spiciness, flavor intensity, and flavor consistency.
           </p>
           <p>
            The wine sensory range is also divided into three main sections, but with a slightly different set of nine elements. The elements in wine go from dryness to sweetness, acidity, effervescence, tannin, alcohol level, body, spiciness, flavor intensity, and flavor consistency.
           </p>
           <p>
            Choosing the cheese first will set the tone of the sensory components and allow you to plan which foods to serve it with and which wine will best compliment the textures and flavors. Sometimes a hard and bitter cheese is best paired with soft and sweet fruit and a choice of wine that brings all of the flavors together.
           </p>
           <p>
            There are certain principles that
            <a href="http://theglamorousgourmet.com/homage-fromage-wine-cheese-pairing-principles/">
             gourmet experts
            </a>
            recommend when pairing wine and cheese that may help you better understand the concept of coupling one with the other.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Principle #1:
            </strong>
            Couple cheese and wines that have similar profiles as a rule of thumb. The same principles that go for food and wine also work for wine and cheese. If you prefer particular flavors in cheese, choose a wine that mirrors your preferred taste. For example, a tangy and acidic goat cheese would pair well with a citrusy Sauvignon Blanc. Similar flavors create a flavor synergy. Another example of this method is matching Chardonnay with Camembert of Brie, or Sherry with sheeps milk cheese. Try Emmentaler with Merlot for a similar result.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Principle #2:
            </strong>
            You want to pair the texture and weight of your cheese and wine. The texture of your cheese selection is a significant hint at what its flavor will be and can help you in deciding a great wine to match it. For example, if you choose a type of cheese that is creamy like Brie, you want to match it with a wine that has equal qualities. Something like a creamy and buttery Chardonnay would go well with it. If you happen to be in the mood for a semi-hard cheese such as cheddar, you want to pair it with a medium-bodied Zinfandel. Some examples of this method of pairing include Rioja with Manchego, Gewrztraminer with Gruyre, and Cabernet Franc with washed-rind cheese.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Principle #3:
            </strong>
            Pair your cheese and wine selections with opposite flavors because although it sounds contradictory, the first pairing tip suggests the opposite, however, you will better understand the concept if you happen to like sweet and salty or sour flavors together. Choosing flavors that have contrary attributes in cheese and wine may make for some delicious combinations of flavor. If youve chosen a salty cheese, then it will pair well with a wine that is sweet in taste. As long as the sweetness and saltiness are in balance, you should be safe. Some classic examples of this method of opposite pairing would include Sauternes and Roquefort and Port and Stilton.
           </p>

           <h2>
            Texture
           </h2>
           <p>
            Cheese can range from very soft to hard, with a variety of textures in between the two limits. The greater the moisture and milk fat in cheese, the smoother it feels in your mouth. In addition to smooth, cheese textures can also be:
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Creamy
            </strong>
            : Monterey Jack, High-moisture Mozzarella, Queso Panela. Mascarpone, Fromage Blanc, Crescenza, and Teleme.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Crumbly
            </strong>
            : FetaandCotija.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Very hard:
            </strong>
            Dry Jack and Aged Gouda.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Stringy:
            </strong>
            low-moisture Mozzarella and Oaxaca.
           </p>
           <p>
            The texture is also a sign of readiness for several types of cheese. The riper the cheese is, the softer or creamier it will be. If you take a small piece of the cheese between your fingers and rub it, you will feel the difference between soft, semi-hard and hard texture. Notice the moisture or dryness of each piece before tasting it. After tasting it and before swallowing, move the cheese around in your mouth to uncover all its flavors to your taste buds. You will find that some tend to coat your mouth, and others leave you with a clean palate. Other ways to describe the texture of cheese are soft, hard, firm, runny, moist, crumbly, creamy, granular, buttery, chalky, waxy, oily, and rubbery.
           </p>
           <p>
            In wine, there are many components that decide the texture. first, lets remember that wine is typically made of approximately 85% water. The remaining composition consists of tannin, acid, alcohol and sugars. These structural components is what gives wine its texture along with determining how it feels to us when consuming it.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Taste
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            Cheese has both taste and flavor. You will find that cheese tastes quite different in your mouth from the way it smelled to your nose. flavor is the value thats usually a blend of smell and taste sensations. Taste refers to the sense perception we receive via the taste buds: sweet, sour, salty and bitter. Food scientists have accepted a fifth taste, umami, the flavorful taste often associated with L-glutamates naturally occurring in foods such as meat, mushrooms, milk, seafood, and cheese. Fermenting foods seems to focus their umami flavors. Pay attention to umami when comparing a young cheese to its aged matching part. Slowly work the cheese in your mouth, and notice which tastes are apparent at first. Then decipher which tastes develop later and which, if any, remain in your mouth. Words that describe taste are sweet, mild, milky, buttery, delicate, salty, sharp, acidic, tart, tangy, lemony, bitter, nutty, piquant, smoky, and yeasty.
           </p>
           <p>
            Every time you taste cheese, make notes about its look, smell, texture, and flavor. Whether sampling one or numerous types of cheese, use the following as a guideline:
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Temperature
            </strong>
            : You should sample all cheese at room temperature except for fresh types of cheese that require refrigeration.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Soft to firm, and mild to sharp
            </strong>
            : Tasting cheese sharp to mild or vice versa will prevent the palate from being overwhelmed, allowing you to appreciate the most delicate distinctions in mild cheeses. Eating softer cheeses before the harder types exposes the increase of taste through aging.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Thin to Thick:
            </strong>
            Cut a thin slice of each cheese and let it melt on your tongue. Then try a larger slice that entails chewing. Notice the different ways the same cheese can taste.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Pairing Ideas:
            </strong>
            After defining the flavor contour of each cheese, consider pairing contrasting or complementary ingredients with your selected cheese. You can contrast a salty cheese with the sweet taste of balsamic vinegar, dates, or dried and ripe fruit. Complement a mild cheese by serving it with sundried tomatoes, salty nuts, or nut bread.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Hierarchy of flavors
           </h2>
           <p>
            flavor layering adds more elements to taste components and texture. Trying to consider all of these elements simultaneously can create confusion about the best wine choice for a particular cheese. Therefore, one objective of the cheese and wine pairing process is to isolate these flavors and identify the most prominent ones. Major flavor character categories include fruity, nutty, smoky, herbal, spicy, cheesy, earthy, and meaty. Spicy flavors in food and wine can be particularly important for matching purposes.
           </p>
           <p>
            Spices have a long and colorful tradition, having been used for centuries in cooking, for medicinal purposes, and for other reasons. In ancient Greece, bay leaves were made into crowns for scholars and victorious athletes to wear. New World Indians have eaten capsicum peppers since about 7000 BC. In past centuries, fennel was traditionally hung over doorways because the plant was believed to ward off evil spirits.
           </p>
           <p>
            Technically, spices are distinguished from herbs based on the part of the plant from which they are derived. Spices come from roots (for example, ginger), bark (cinnamon), flowers (saffron), seeds (cumin), fruit (allspice), or buds (cloves), while herbs are the fresh or dried leaves. We can classify seasonings based on their taste: sweet-spicy, savory-spicy, hot-spicy, and herbal, for example.
           </p>
           <p>
            While many experts suggest pairing cheese and wines by matching flavors, evaluators need to keep in mind that spicy or herbal flavors in wine do not come from actual spices and herbs, and spiciness in cheese and wine does not by itself guarantee a good match between the two. Certain spicy wines and many Asian and Indian dishes are frequently paired together, however, the spicy wine selection is picked because of similar levels of acidity and sweetness, not because of the spiciness. To create a match using flavors and spiciness, it is important to define the type of spice in the wine and cheese to ensure a satisfying match.
           </p>
           <p>
            flavors are closely tied to our perceptions of specific characteristics inherent in cheese or wine and are derived from both aroma and taste sensations. These sensations can be described as in-mouth smells.
           </p>
           <p>
            In assessing spice in wine or food, it is important to differentiate between herbs and spices as well as among hot, savory, and sweet spices. While many foods include more than one type of spice or herb, the evaluation process is about determining the dominant spice types that will have an impact on wine choices. Once the dominant spice types are identified, the level of intensity of the specific spice flavor has significant implications for wine choices.
           </p>
           <p>
            The term intensity refers to concentration, power, or force and applies to all flavor types. The evaluation of spice intensity defines the relative level of intensity or strength of the characteristic spice(s) in the wine or food. These levels can be verbally described on a scale ranging from no spice to weak spice, moderate spice, intense spice, and potent spice.
           </p>
           <p>
            Each spice type has differing acceptable levels in regard to wine and cheese pairing. Sweet spices in cheese are more wine-friendly because they match better with a wider variety of wines. In food, some savory, and hot spices, as well as the more intense spice levels, make it increasingly difficult to create a good match. Potential wine killers are ingredients that have an unusually high level of sweetness, sourness, saltiness, bitterness, or hot spiciness.
           </p>
           <p>
            The food sweetness level should be less than or equal to wine sweetness level. Wine and food experts suggest matching the level of perceived sweetness in food with the level of perceived sweetness in the wine. This match creates a balance in taste and allows the remaining elements to interact in a positive way. When the sweetness in wine is greater than the food, the slightly higher wine sweetness creates a pleasant contrast with the savory, salty, and bitter characteristics of most foods. When the sweetness in the food item is greater than the wine, the interaction seems to accentuate and overemphasize the acidity present in the wine and any characteristics of bitterness or astringency inherent in the wine.
           </p>
           <p>
            Many times, a savory dish will be prepared with a sauce or other accompaniment that has fruit or sweet elements (e.g., Duck with Sauce Bigarade). Sweeter wines work well with this type of dish, particularly medium-sweet Riesling. If you are preparing red meat or game with a fruit/sweet sauce, choose a wine that has a good sense of lush fruit of its own. In this situation, very ripe grapes from warm flavor zones will be appropriate (Zinfandel from Californias temperate regions or Australian Shiraz).
           </p>
           <p>
            It is a good idea to avoid serving heavily oaked white wines alongside savory dishes with sweet accompaniments. The fruit in whites aged in oak will be reduced, making the resulting food-and-wine combination taste harsh. As with reds, white wines in this situation should have ripe fruit flavors.7 If the dish has sweetness and acid, you will want to use wines that both are sweet and have a higher acidity.
           </p>
           <p>
            Cool climate whites from the Loire Valley or sweet German Rieslings are useful in this case. Foods that are served at the peak of freshness need to be matched with wines that have equally vibrant fruit flavors. Therefore, it is best to serve peak-season foods with lively young wines. These can be red or white but will generally be from New World locations (Argentina, Australia, New Zealand, Chile, the U.S., and South Africa).Wines from these locations will have a fruit-forward, more lush tendency than their Old World counterparts (France, Germany, Spain, and Italy), which can have subtle, earthier profiles.
           </p>
           <h2>
            flavor Persistency
           </h2>
           <p>
            flavor persistency is the last component used in the pairing process. The continuance of the flavor afteryou'veswallowed(or spit when it comes to wine) can be a significant indicator of the wine and cheese quality, or it can produce a negative aftertaste. In wine, persistency is the length or the finish of the taste.
           </p>
           <p>
            flavor persistency reveals flaws that may not have been apparent when first eating cheese or tasting wine. For example, you may not taste the bitterness until afterward, also known as a bitter aftertaste. A wine thathasn'tbeenstored or handled properly can create unpleasant aftertastes. The unpleasant taste can also be caused by the presence of lactic acid or yeast left in wine that was not properly fermented. On the contrary, one indicator of a good tasting wine is when the length of the taste has a balanced and clean linger in the flavor. The wines with good flavor persistency are a better buy because you get more flavor on each sip and a longer finish. This evolutionary process occurs during the final phase of the taste process. A popular technique in assessing flavor is to time how long the taste lingers and time the length of the aromatic sensations afteryou'veconsumed it. You want towait at least 15 seconds after consumption to make a determination on the quality.
           </p>
           <p>
            In cheese, flavorpersistenceis a different animal although closely connected. In cheese the intensity is relative to the initialpower of flavor when first tasting it. Other determining components are the refined flavors and aroma and how they appeal to you. The flavor persistency in cheese is measured differently than in wine. In cheese, you want to test in 3 second intervals. This hierarchical method allows you to analyze its profile in a more detailed manner. You will mentally record the aroma, flavor, and then the aftertaste by using this method of perception.
           </p>
           <p>
            When combining both cheese and wine to rate their coupled persistency, you will want to assess the maximum intensity and the total duration. Remember that with cheese, the intensity will be strongly related to the spice in each selection. Cheese also requires chewing which can release aromatic flavors that will play a role in how it tastes to you. Wine also contains aromatic flavors so to test it prior to pairing, you can swirl the wine around in a glass to release some of the aromatic flavoring and then taste test it by sipping it and then rolling the liquid around in different areas of your mouth.
           </p>

            Ideal pairings: Generally, the wine/cheese pairs selected by the experts were also the most ideal selections by the consumers. With the exception of Sauvignon Blanc (consumers proposed Gruyre rather than goat cheese) and Chardonnay (consumers suggested Gruyre or Chaource instead of Brie de Meaux).
           </p>
           <p>
            Leading cheeses and leading wines: As for the cheeses, Gorgonzola dominated all of the red wines. For the wines, Gewrztraminer and the Botrytis dessert wine dominated the cheeses. Syrah also dominated the Ossau Iraty and the Parmigiano cheeses.
           </p>
           <p>
            flexible cheeses and flexible wines: The scoring demonstrated that the most flexible wine was Gewrztraminer, then the sparkling wine, among the whites. Of the red wine, the most versatile was Sangiovese, and then Syrah. Of the cheeses, the most versatile was English cheddar, and after that was Gruyre.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            Questions that arose after this study were:
           </h2>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Does high residual sugar play a role in wines being more flexible to cheese?
            </li>
            <li>
             Is High RS, intensity, flavor, richness in texture the reason dessert wine is dominating all of the cheese types except one?
            </li>
            <li>
             Did red wines pair better with the cheese better than the white wines did because of tannins?
            </li>
            <li>
             Is there a preference between fuller-bodied wines and lighter-bodied wines when pairing with cheese?
            </li>
            <li>
             Is consumer familiarity playing a role in influencing cheese ratings of an ideal match?
            </li>
            <li>
             What are the composition limitations that decide how well wine and cheese match?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <h4>
           </h4>
           <h2>
            Based on the outcomes of the test study, the summarized findings were:
           </h2>
           <p>
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Experts and consumer mostly agreed on ideal wine and cheese combinations;
            </li>
            <li>
             Blue cheese appears to dominate red wine;
            </li>
            <li>
             Sweet wines and blue cheese match well;
            </li>
            <li>
             Cheese readily matches with Off-dry white wines;
            </li>
            <li>
             The least versatile wine with cheese was Sauvignon Blanc;
            </li>
            <li>
             The easiest choice to match was Cheddar.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <h4>
           </h4>
           <p>
            <img alt="Depositphotos_18065657_s.jpg" height="796" src="/files/3314/3102/9693/Depositphotos_18065657_s.jpg" width="628"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            The information provided in this guide can be used as a tool chest of considerations when pairing. Each section has focused on evaluating specific elements of wine and cheese and their interactions when tasted together. This process provides you with a taste, texture, and flavor memory that will be very useful when making pairing decisions.
           </p>
           <p>
            As displayed towards the beginning of this guide, there are pairing decision trees available to the public online to help you visualize the concept of wine and cheese pairing. These decision trees follow the hierarchy-of-taste philosophy, with taste components as the foundation, texture as the secondary match element and flavor as the refining element. The visualization helps you in determining the best pairing choices.
           </p>
