     <p>
      A
      <b>
       breastplate
      </b>
      is a device worn over the torso to protect it from injury, as an item of religious significance, or as an item of status. A breastplate is sometimes worn by mythological beings as a distinctive item of clothing.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Armour">
       Armour
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breastplate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Armour">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In medieval weaponry, the breastplate is the front portion of
      <a href="/wiki/Plate_armour" title="Plate armour">
       plate armour
      </a>
      covering the torso. It has been a military mainstay since ancient times and was usually made of leather, bronze or iron in antiquity. By around 1000 AD, solid plates had fallen out of use in Europe and knights of the period were wearing
      <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">
       mail
      </a>
      in the form of a
      <a href="/wiki/Hauberk" title="Hauberk">
       hauberk
      </a>
      over a padded tunic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker36_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker36-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Plates protecting the torso reappeared during the 13th century in the form of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cuirass" title="Cuirass">
       cuirass
      </a>
      or alternatively as plates directly attached to a knightly garment known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Surcoat" title="Surcoat">
       surcoat
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker36_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker36-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Around 1300 this developed into the
      <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_plates" title="Coat of plates">
       coat of plates
      </a>
      which continued to be in use for about a century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker36_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker36-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      True breastplates reappear in Europe in 1340 first composed of wrought iron and later of steel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker39_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker39-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were between 1mm and 2.5mm thick.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker39_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker39-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In order to prevent the wearer from being cut by their own armour, the design featured outward turned edges that also increased stiffness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker39_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker39-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some cases, further strength was added by a ridge running down through the centre of the plate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker39_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker39-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These early breastplates only covered the upper torso with the lower torso not being protected by plate until the development of the
      <a href="/wiki/Faulds_(armour)" title="Faulds (armour)">
       fauld
      </a>
      around 1400.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker39_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker39-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Around 1450, the breastplate had expanded to cover the entire torso and could consist of one or two plates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker43_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-walker43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The French term
      <i>
       pancier
      </i>
      , which became English
      <i>
       pauncher
      </i>
      and German
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Panzer" title="Panzer">
        panzer
       </a>
      </i>
      , was also used.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistic_vest" title="Ballistic vest">
       Bullet-proof vests
      </a>
      are the modern descendant of the breastplate.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical">
       Biblical
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breastplate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biblical">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Priestly_breastplate" title="Priestly breastplate">
       Priestly breastplate
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A "breastplate" or "breastpiece" was among the clothes of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_High_Priest" title="Jewish High Priest">
       Jewish High Priest
      </a>
      . In the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      , the word 'breastplate' is used figuratively to describe protecting oneself from unrighteousness (cf.
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Isaiah&amp;verse=59:17&amp;src=HE">
       Isaiah 59:17
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Ephesians&amp;verse=6:14&amp;src=KJV">
       Ephesians 6:14
      </a>
      , etc.).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_mythology">
       Classical mythology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breastplate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classical mythology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Both
      <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
       Zeus
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">
       Athena
      </a>
      are sometimes depicted as wearing a goatskin shield or breastplate called an
      <a href="/wiki/Aegis" title="Aegis">
       Aegis
      </a>
      . At the center of Athenas shield was the head of
      <a href="/wiki/Medusa" title="Medusa">
       Medusa
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      The hair-pipe breastplates of 19th-century Plains Indians were made from the West Indian
      <a href="/wiki/Conch" title="Conch">
       conch
      </a>
      , brought to New York docks as ballast and then traded to native Americans of the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">
       Missouri River
      </a>
      . Their popularity spread rapidly after their invention by the
      <a href="/wiki/Comanche" title="Comanche">
       Comanche
      </a>
      in 1854. They were too fragile and expensive to be considered armour, and were instead a symbol of wealth during the economic depression among
      <a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">
       Plains Indians
      </a>
      after the
      <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">
       buffalo
      </a>
      were almost exterminated.
