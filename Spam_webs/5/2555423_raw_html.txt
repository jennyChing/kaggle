        <h1>
         Clues Lie in the Strangest Places....
        </h1>
        <ul class="navigation byline">
         <li>
          <a class="nolink">

          </a>
          <a href="/profile/0de3ezd9dhz7m">
           Elizabeth Campbell

          <a class="nolink">
           on March 19, 2013 at 7:30am
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a class="xg_sprite xg_sprite-view" href="http://askgen.ning.com/profiles/blog/list?user=0de3ezd9dhz7m">

        <div class="xg_user_generated">
         <p>
          My 2g-grandmother, Ellen FLACK, it turns out, was from a Nonconformist family who attended the Independent Old Meeting Dead Lane in Ware. I've managed to learn a fair bit about the family over the years, and from sharing my knowledge with another researcher, learned another lesson, when s/he posted it all on the Internet without giving me any credit for my hours and hours of hard labour!
         </p>
         <p>
          As you know, my current course at the NIGS is on English Nonconformist denominations, and this week, the topic was Congregationialists, which is what the Flacks were. So, I approached my readings with enthusiasm! And, sure enough, I found some things out that I thought might give me a hand in learning more about Ellen's sister and brother-in-law in particular, Juliana and her husband, Robert RICARDS.
         </p>
         <p>
          I had learned that Robert was a missionary in British Guiana for a while, but I didn't know when and I didn't know for how long. I found the family - they had a son Robert Flack - on the 1871 Census for England. But were they just there on furlough? I lost them after that.
         </p>
         <p>
          Today's readings gave me some clues regarding mission related periodicals among other things. I decided to start hunting for them again.
         </p>
         <p>
          I ended up finding a periodical published by a Congregational missionary society that wasn't mentioned in my text, and through it, I learned the exact date Juliana left for South America, 3 October 1859, and the name of the boat she and her husband sailed on, the Packet "Amsterdam." Through the notice, I also learned that not only was Robert a missionary, he was an ordained minister. Then I found an article all about the history of the mission they were assigned to, in New Amsterdam on the Berbice River, in what is now Guyana.
         </p>
         <p>
          But the trail still ended in 1871 in England.
         </p>
         <p>
          More searching....
         </p>
         <p>
          I found them this time. In Hobart, Tasmania, Australia!
         </p>
         <p>
          And you'll never believe how....
         </p>
         <p>
          Through a classified ad in the Hobart
          <em>
           Clipper
          </em>
          on an amazing free site called
          <a href="http://trove.nla.gov.au/" target="_blank">
           Trove
          </a>
          . It's a programme put on by the National Library of Australia, and they have a wonderful, er, trove of historical Australian newspapers! And you'll really get a smile out of this ad:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <a href="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIPzR-bSOVRaKEXIoHOdw-d2zyXYy739s5c09M7eYwhYTKR3IcgKG6YCGLHkhkGg9rySarQ*2BKlJGtBBUWYqE6j/ForSaleforblog.jpg" target="_self">
            <img class="align-center" src="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIPzR-bSOVRaKEXIoHOdw-d2zyXYy739s5c09M7eYwhYTKR3IcgKG6YCGLHkhkGg9rySarQ*2BKlJGtBBUWYqE6j/ForSaleforblog.jpg" width="746"/>
           </a>
           FOR SALE.
           <br/>
           One bulldog. Owner selling because his wife tries to keep Persian female cats, and bully objects. Apply Flack Ricards. Con Clark guarantees that the architecture of the bulldog is all right : chewed her (Tudor) style.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Robert Flack RICARDS was an architect, it turns out, who established a well-known firm in Hobart. Con CLARK was doing his articles in Ricard's firm at the time this ad was placed. I don't know who the dog belonged to, but Flack didn't marry until 1912 as far as I've been able to ascertain.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now I'm busy cruising through all the RICARDS announcements in the Mercury archive. I'm finding obituaries, births and marriages that I need to sift through... very promising!
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Some tidbits so far:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          I found this article about what may have been Flack's first project in Tasmania:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIOu87HTqdmFlKXGhZ2wDmKtsF9k61C3R*kokgIejIrHysHeXkGdQ7R7gJzegRiX*TP44ZLOUqSQnw68wGnvoNeg/BurnieNotesforblog.jpg" target="_self">
           <img class="align-center" src="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIOu87HTqdmFlKXGhZ2wDmKtsF9k61C3R*kokgIejIrHysHeXkGdQ7R7gJzegRiX*TP44ZLOUqSQnw68wGnvoNeg/BurnieNotesforblog.jpg?width=750" width="750"/>
          </a>
          This led me into going for a walk, using Google Maps, in Burnie, to Catley Street and Marine Terrace, where I found perhaps the first building Flack designed in Tasmania. It's still standing! This is a screen shot of what it looks like today:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIONZnsjqoR9rb28Vc4ZvBEUHsUZdjz9eZqveRix-MqfYoWGIZs0CIJBq9Jk8Y2RSiIBJ8BPThHs8LUwFc8Oawtm/BankofVanDiemensLand200.JPG" target="_self">

          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIOHxdtJXIi0DFInNmb6VYDocuc5KJGjHOSDZyypo*rpBFqVgr5GiWiPgsfMT9ceBuV3ntr6hWKZEAMLkISZ-NsT/RobertFlackRicardsblog.JPG" target="_self">
           <img class="align-right" src="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIOHxdtJXIi0DFInNmb6VYDocuc5KJGjHOSDZyypo*rpBFqVgr5GiWiPgsfMT9ceBuV3ntr6hWKZEAMLkISZ-NsT/RobertFlackRicardsblog.JPG?width=250" width="250"/>
          </a>
          Over at the Tasmanian Archives, I found architectural plans he had drawn. I also learned that he had been a season ticket holder for the Tasmanian Exhibition of 1894-1895. And guess what? Season ticket holders all had their photos taken and placed in a book, perhaps so that they could be identified readily at the entrance? For whatever reason, what a genealogical bonus that proved for me! I can see points of resemblance to my brother...
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          I also found this, which had to be the highlight of the day:
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIPZ0k675dR5tQYXcd1*Ha6wQImxyRh4*IBKTBmDtPOAjhetcfWQPmwGRkGGTT8FsgAZZpDxdpzpO6ambxlSSYNG/Kimbraphotoforblog.JPG" target="_self">
           <img class="align-left" src="http://api.ning.com:80/files/TpH3GRQjXIPZ0k675dR5tQYXcd1*Ha6wQImxyRh4*IBKTBmDtPOAjhetcfWQPmwGRkGGTT8FsgAZZpDxdpzpO6ambxlSSYNG/Kimbraphotoforblog.JPG?width=425" width="425"/>
          </a>
          Flack's daughter, Ellen Kimbra RICARDS is the bridesmaid on the right. The photo was taken in 1938when Kimbra was 24 years of age. By that time, she was a nurse, and sometime during the war, she moved to England, perhaps as a nursing sister? I don't know yet. What I do know is that she met her own husband there, Kenneth Duncan ANDERSON. They were married in 1942 in Oxford. I wonder if that makes him a War Husband? He was a Flight Sergeant with the RAF at the time, so maybe not...
         </p>
         <p>
          The couple had a daughter in 1946. I know her name, and would love to track her down.
         </p>
         <p>
          Kimbra died in 2001 in England, but I was able to find a gravestone
          <a href="http://thegardensfamily.com/cemeteries/Pontville/StMarksAng/index.htm" target="_blank">
           recorded in Tasmania
          </a>
          with both her name, year of birth and death and those of her father.
         </p>
         <p>
          I'm still working on these folks. There are over 120 newspaper records to sort through at the Trove site, so you may not see me for a while!
         </p>
