         <h1>
          Voters have turned against 'politics as usual'
         </h1>

           <p>
            Leaving the Conservative Party and joining Ukip was an extraordinarily difficult decision to take. But after five weeks campaigning in Essex, Iam certain I have made the right choice.
           </p>
           <p>
            A Conservative all my adult life, I know the party is full of good people, who want the best for this country. The problem is with those at its top. They are not on our side. They are not serious about the change Britain needs.
           </p>
           <p>
            Sitting in the Commons tea rooms, their talk was always about what they needed to say to win elections  literally, talk about talk. Seldom did they discuss what actually needed to be done to make Britain better.
           </p>
           <p>
            You do not need to listen to Westminster chit-chat to know that they are not serious about change. Look at a few facts. Net immigration is back up to the levels seen under Labour  something Clactons constituents consistently raise. Rather than sorting out the public finances, left in such a mess by the last government, the Government is still running an annual deficit of around 100 billion a year. The Coalition will have borrowed more in five years that Gordon Brown managed in 13.
           </p>
           <p>
            As former Labour voters in once staunchly red wards keep pointing out, the banks have been endlessly bailed out. But where is the real reform? MPs, we were told, were to be made more accountable to the voters. None of the promised changes has come to pass.
           </p>
           <p>
            As I keep hearing on doorstep after doorstep, each of the established parties seems the same. They are run by those who became MPs by working in the offices of MPs. They hire opinion pollsters to work out what they might say. Its all about positioning. And the positions are adopted on the basis of where the other side stands, not what is right for this country.
           </p>
           <p>
            Once mass membership organisations, political parties have shrunk  so much so that those at the top can treat them as their private property. Politics has been reduced to a competition between rival cliques to sit on the Downing Street sofa.
           </p>
           <p>
            Over the past month, members of these cliques have descended on our part of Essex, parading around Clacton like Harry Enfields Tory Boy. What they possess in shrill certainty, they lack in self-awareness. I have seen voters literally turn away.
           </p>
           <p>
            Only Ukip, a party for the kind of ordinary folk who throng our campaign office, can change politics for the better. Only with a bit of choice and competition in politics can we shake up that smug, self-regarding clique called Westminster.
           </p>
           <p>
            I did not have to resign from Parliament and face this by-election. Dozens of MPs have switched parties in the past, without doing the decent thing. But to me, there was only one honourable thing to do. Having always answered directly to independent-minded Essex folk, I trust them to know what is right  in the same way that Mark Reckless, my fellow former Tory MP, trusts his constituents in Rochester and Strood.
           </p>
           <p>
            By resigning, I hoped in some small way to help us rediscover a half-forgotten truth: that MPs answer not to their party whips, but to their voters. And a sense that this might actually be happening is helping to generate an enthusiasm for politics here that I have never witnessed before. An 11-year-old schoolgirl posts a supportive rap video on YouTube. Pensioners from Frinton come over on the bus to deliver leaflets. All of them want change.
           </p>
           <p>
            Until 1968, it was illegal to put the name of a political party on a ballot paper. Voters were expected to vote for a person, not a party. Until the Nineties, MPs had to write their own election addresses  a personal contract between them and the voters.
           </p>
           <p>
            Today, too many MPs end up talking in clich. Why? Because they are expected to spout the line to take. They are meant to stay on message, rather than answer the question. This has diminished our democracy. It has helped to rob Parliament of its purpose. It helps explain why the governing are no longer meaningfully accountable to the governed.
           </p>
           <p>
            I once believed that David Cameron recognised all this. Indeed, the 2010 Conservative manifesto was full of excellent ideas to change our politics for the better. Local people were to be given the power to recall wayward MPs. There would be a system of open primary candidate selection to open up the process to those beyond Westminster. We would have fewer MPs, but they would be given greater power to hold ministers to account. Government was to be made answerable to Parliament, and Parliament to you.
           </p>
           <p>
            We now know that none of it has happened. Instead, Zac Goldsmiths noble attempt to ensure that the Government honours its promise to deliver on recall has been disgracefully sabotaged. It was above all this failure to deliver on political reform that forced me to trigger this by-election.
           </p>
           <p>
            My former party once understood the importance of choice and competition in business and commerce. Britain today desperately needs the same in politics. Like those nationalised industries in the Seventies, the Westminster parties are big, bloated and complacent. They mistake having a long and glorious history for customer satisfaction. And just as corporate monoliths resent start-ups taking their market share, so the big parties rail against Ukip. Youre taking our votes! complained one rather grumpy MP, sent to campaign in Clacton last week.
           </p>
           <p>
            No, my friend. They are not your votes. They belong to the voters  and the voters want change. The party that produced Margaret Thatcher ought to see that.
           </p>
