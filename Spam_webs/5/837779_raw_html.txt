     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Equilibrium
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2002 American dystopian
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">
       science fiction film
      </a>
      written and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Wimmer" title="Kurt Wimmer">
       Kurt Wimmer
      </a>
      and starring
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">
       Christian Bale
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Emily_Watson" title="Emily Watson">
       Emily Watson
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Taye_Diggs" title="Taye Diggs">
       Taye Diggs
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The film follows John Preston (Bale), an enforcement officer in a future in which both feelings and artistic expression are outlawed and citizens take daily injections of drugs to suppress their emotions. After accidentally missing a dose, Preston begins to experience emotions, which makes him question his own morality and moderate his actions while attempting to remain undetected by the suspicious society in which he lives. Ultimately, he aids a resistance movement using advanced
      <a href="/wiki/Martial_arts" title="Martial arts">
       martial arts
      </a>
      , which he was taught by the very regime he is helping to overthrow.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equilibrium_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Equilibrium
      </i>
      is set in 2072 in Libria, a city state established by the survivors of World War III that devastated the world, where a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totalitarian_state" title="Totalitarian state">
       totalitarian
      </a>
      government requires all citizens to take daily injections of "Prozium" to suppress emotion and encourage obedience. All emotionally stimulating material has been banned, and "Sense Offenders"  those who fail to take their Prozium  are put to death, as the government claims that the cause of all wars and violence is emotion. Libria is governed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">
       Tetragrammaton
      </a>
      Council, led by "Father", who is seen only on giant video screens throughout the city. At the pinnacle of Librian law enforcement are the Grammaton Clerics, who are trained in the martial art of
      <a href="/wiki/Gun_fu" title="Gun fu">
       gun kata
      </a>
      . The Clerics frequently raid the "Nether" region outside the city to search for and destroy illegal materials  art, literature, and music  and execute the people hiding them. A resistance movement, known as the "Underground", emerges with the goal of toppling Father and the Tetragrammaton Council.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Libria_(Equilibrium).svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="480" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1c/Flag_of_Libria_%28Equilibrium%29.svg/220px-Flag_of_Libria_%28Equilibrium%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1c/Flag_of_Libria_%28Equilibrium%29.svg/330px-Flag_of_Libria_%28Equilibrium%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1c/Flag_of_Libria_%28Equilibrium%29.svg/440px-Flag_of_Libria_%28Equilibrium%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Libria_(Equilibrium).svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The flag of Libria. The four Ts on the flag represent the Tetragrammaton Council
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      John Preston (
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bale" title="Christian Bale">
       Christian Bale
      </a>
      ) is a high-ranking Cleric. His wife, Viviana (Alexa Summer and Maria Pia Calzone), was executed as a Sense Offender. Following a raid, Preston notices his partner, Errol Partridge (
      <a href="/wiki/Sean_Bean" title="Sean Bean">
       Sean Bean
      </a>
      ), saving a book of poems instead of turning it in for incineration. Preston tracks down Partridge, who is hiding in the Nether region reading the book. Partridge confesses to Preston that he believes he was wrong to serve the government. He acknowledges that the consequences of feeling emotions are a "heavy cost", but remarks, "I pay it gladly" as he slowly reaches for his gun. Preston is forced to execute him.
     </p>
     <p>
      Preston dreams occasionally about his wife and the day she was arrested. After Preston accidentally breaks his daily vial of Prozium, his son Robbie enters and reminds him that he needs to report the loss and request a replacement, but Preston is unable to before going on the next raid. As a result, he begins to experience brief episodes of emotion that evoke memories, stir feelings, and make him more aware of his surroundings. He intentionally skips more doses of Prozium and hides them behind the mirror in his bathroom.
     </p>
     <p>
      Preston's partner is replaced with career-conscious Brandt (
      <a href="/wiki/Taye_Diggs" title="Taye Diggs">
       Taye Diggs
      </a>
      ), who expresses his admiration for Preston's "uncompromising" work as a cleric. On their first raid, they arrest Mary O'Brien (
      <a href="/wiki/Emily_Watson" title="Emily Watson">
       Emily Watson
      </a>
      ), a citizen identified as a Sense Offender. To Brandt's surprise, Preston prevents him from executing O'Brien on the spot, saying that he wants to keep her alive for interrogation. Brandt grows suspicious of Preston's hesitation to execute Sense Offenders and destroy contraband. As a result of his new secret emotions, Preston feels remorse for the having killed Partridge, and he also develops an emotional relationship with Mary O'Brien. Preston eventually uncovers clues that lead to a meeting with Jurgen (
      <a href="/wiki/William_Fichtner" title="William Fichtner">
       William Fichtner
      </a>
      ), the leader of the Underground resistance. Jurgen convinces him that Father must be assassinated. They plan to disrupt Prozium production in an attempt to spark a popular uprising.
     </p>
     <p>
      Preston is brought before Vice-Counsel DuPont (
      <a href="/wiki/Angus_Macfadyen" title="Angus Macfadyen">
       Angus Macfadyen
      </a>
      ), who reveals that there is a traitor in the upper ranks of the Clerics. DuPont appears to be onto Preston, but then assigns him the task of uncovering and stopping the traitor. Relieved to learn he has not been implicated, Preston accepts and renews a promise to locate the Underground's leadership. He learns of O'Brien's scheduled execution, but Jurgen advises against interfering which could sabotage plans for the revolution. Jurgen decides to allow the leaders of the resistance to be captured by Preston, so that Preston can gain the government's trust in order to approach Father and assassinate him. Unable to bear O'Brien's execution, however, Preston unsuccessfully rushes to stop it. Brandt catches Preston having an emotional breakdown in the streets, arrests him, and brings him before the Vice-Counsel. Preston tricks DuPont into believing that Brandt is the real traitor. Following Brandt's arrest, Preston is informed that a search of his home will take place as a formality. He rushes home to destroy the hidden vials, only to find his son  who stopped taking Prozium after his mother died  already has.
     </p>
     <p>
      Preston turns in Jurgen and others from the resistance, and as a reward, is granted an exclusive audience with Father. Upon arriving, he discovers that Brandt was not actually arrested but was part of a ruse to expose Preston and the Underground. DuPont is also revealed as the real Father; he had secretly replaced the original Father after his death. It also becomes clear that DuPont does not take Prozium and can feel emotions. He taunts Preston, asking him how it felt to betray the Underground. Enraged, Preston fights his way through an army of bodyguards into DuPont's office and defeats Brandt in a
      <a href="/wiki/Katana" title="Katana">
       katana
      </a>
      battle. In a final gun kata showdown with DuPont, Preston wins. DuPont pleads for his life reminding Preston that he represents life and feeling and asks, "Is it really worth the price?" Preston responds with Partridge's last words, "I pay it gladly", shoots DuPont, and destroys the command center that broadcasts propaganda videos of Father. The Underground carries out the destruction of Prozium manufacturing and storage facilities and attacks key points throughout the city. Preston watches the successful revolution with satisfaction from above in DuPont's office.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gun_Kata">
       Gun Kata
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equilibrium_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gun Kata">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Gun_fu" title="Gun fu">
       Gun fu
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Vice-Counsel DuPont (
      <a href="/wiki/Angus_Macfadyen" title="Angus Macfadyen">
       Angus Macfadyen
      </a>
      ) describes the gun kata in the film:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       "Through analysis of thousands of recorded gunfights, the Cleric has determined that the geometric distribution of antagonists in any gun battle is a statistically-predictable element. The Gun Kata treats the gun as a total weapon, each fluid position representing a maximum kill zone, inflicting maximum damage on the maximum number of opponents, while keeping the defender clear of the statistically-traditional trajectories of return fire. By the rote mastery of this art, your firing efficiency will rise by no less than 120%. The difference of a 63% increased lethal proficiency makes the master of the Gun Katas an adversary not to be taken lightly."
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Gun Kata is a fictional gun-wielding martial art discipline. It is based on the premise that, given the positions of the participants in a gun battle, all trajectories of fire are statistically predictable. By memorizing the positions, one can fire at the most likely location of an enemy without aiming at him in the traditional sense. By the same token, the trajectories of incoming fire are also statistically predictable, so by assuming the appropriate stance, one can keep clear of the most likely path of enemy bullets.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Gun Kata shown in
      <i>
       Equilibrium
      </i>
      is a hybrid mix of the film's writer and director
      <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Wimmer" title="Kurt Wimmer">
       Kurt Wimmer
      </a>
      's own style of Gun Kata (invented in his backyard)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the martial arts style of the fight choreographer Jim Vickers, with elements of the Chinese
      <a href="/wiki/Wing_Chun" title="Wing Chun">
       Wing Chun
      </a>
      martial art style. Wimmer and Vickers disagreed on the appropriate form of Gun Kata, with Wimmer advocating a smoother, flowing style and the choreographer supporting a more rigid style. Much of the Gun Kata seen in the film is based on the choreographer's style.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Kurt Wimmer's Gun Kata style is dispersed sparsely throughout the movie, most notably in the introductory scene with the silhouetted man, played by Wimmer himself, practicing with dual pistols.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (April 2014)">
          original research?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Fascist_architecture" title="Fascist architecture">

      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_German" title="East German">

"I wanted to create more of an alternate reality than get caught up in the gadgetry of science fiction," he explained. "In fact, theres no technology in

       Equilibrium
      </i>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
       Critical reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equilibrium_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Critics generally gave
      <i>
       Equilibrium
      </i>
      mixed to negative reviews. The review aggregator
      <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">
       Rotten Tomatoes
      </a>
      reported that 38% of critics gave the film positive reviews and an average rating of 4.8/10, based on 84 reviews, with the consensus being "
      <i>
       Equilibrium
      </i>
      is a reheated mishmash of other sci-fi movies."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">
       Metacritic
      </a>
      reported that the film had an generally unfavorable score of 33 out of 100, based on 22 reviews.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       New York Times
      </i>
      dismissed
      <i>
       Equilibrium
      </i>
      for having heavily borrowed from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_451" title="Fahrenheit 451">
        Fahrenheit 451
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four">
        Nineteen Eighty-Four
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Brave_New_World" title="Brave New World">
        Brave New World
       </a>
      </i>
      , and other science-fiction classics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimesReviewOfEquilibrium2002_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTimesReviewOfEquilibrium2002-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
       Roger Ebert
      </a>
      , however, awarded the film 3 stars out of 4, noting that "
      <i>
       Equilibrium
      </i>
      would be a mindless action picture, except that it has a mind. It doesn't do a lot of deep thinking, but unlike many futuristic combos of sf and f/x, it does make a statement."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Wimmer said in a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dreamwatch" title="Dreamwatch">
        Dreamwatch
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine interview that "the paying customers seemed to get it," and said the critics "didn't seem to see that the film had a different message than"
      <i>
       Fahrenheit 451
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       1984
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Responding to the critics' views, Wimmer later said, "Why would I make a movie for someone I wouldn't want to hang out with? Have you ever met a critic who you wanted to party with? I haven't."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">
       Box office
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equilibrium_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The film had an estimated production budget of $20 million. International pre-release sales had already made a profit, so the studio reduced the film's promotion and advertising budget to avoid the risk of the film losing money; as a consequence, theatrical release was limited.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The film was shown in only 301 theaters at its widest release in the United States, earning $541,512 in its opening week, and only $1.2 million when it closed on December 26, 2002; the film earned $4.1 million internationally, for a total of $5.3 million worldwide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
