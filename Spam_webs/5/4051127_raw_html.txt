        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         A better metric for comparing products on Amazon
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-12-19T01:37:17+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">

        <p>
         A few weeks ago, my PC of a number of years began warning me to replace my hard disk drive soon. That in itself is pretty cool. (Not that my disk drive was failing, but that the operating system warned me
         <em>
          before
         </em>
         it actually did!)
        </p>
        <p>
         Rather than replace the hard drive, I opted for an updated computer. I have a daughter entering her teen years faster than Id like, and I want to postpone her passing me with the tech as long as possible.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <strong>
          Customer Reviews: More than meets the eye
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         As with most things on
         <a href="http://www.mightybargainhunter.com/go/amazon">
          Amazon
         </a>
         , there are choices.
         <em>
          Boy
         </em>
         are there choices! After doing a small bit of research based on what I had hoped to use the computer for, a gaming machine seemed to be a good compromise between cost and performance. (My main use for the machine isnt gaming, actually!)
        </p>
        <p>
         So, now I start trying to decipher amongst a bunch of fairly competitive choices. They were roughly all the same price for what you got, and the number of stars, and number of reviews, were similar too.
        </p>
        <p>
         I began reading the customer reviews. I usually start with the critical reviews. Not that glowing reviews arent helpful, but they can be padded by the vendor. Critical reviews tend to reveal more.
        </p>
        <p>
         As I was reading the reviews for some
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00D1KWW7U/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B00D1KWW7U&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=mightybargain-20&amp;linkId=6ENZEBURRIRLOQHT">
          CybertronPC
         </a>
         machines in my price range, I noticed something. It wasnt so much whether or not the computer had issues or not, or even what the customer said about the computer.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          It was CybertronPCs response to the reviews.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         It wasnt even just that they responded to the critical reviews professionally, and made every effort to fix the problem.
        </p>
        <p>
         Im quite sure that they read each and every review  even the five-star ones  and take every opportunity to go above and beyond to fix any issues that may have arisen during the purchase. One of the five-star reviews on the machine I bought (linked above) mentioned a missing wifi adapter, as well as the prompt response by the company to fix the issue. (This was certainly a
         <a href="http://www.mightybargainhunter.com/pays-reasonable-customer/" target="_blank">
          good customer
         </a>
         !)
        </p>
        <p>
         CybertronPC took the opportunity to apologize
         <em>
          again
         </em>
         for the already-resolved issue, and throw in another reminder about the technical support for their PCs (which is free lifetime support, by the way).
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <strong>
          A sign of taking business seriously
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Does it take a lot of time, money, and energy to monitor, and respond to, these reviews? Not a whole lot.
        </p>
        <p>
         Is it a good return on investment to take the opportunity to build up relationships with satisfied customers, and reduce collateral damage with unsatisfied ones, in a high-traffic, high-influence forum? Of course.
        </p>
        <p>
         Good, or even great, customer service isnt rocket science, and responding thoughtfully to Amazon reviews is low-hanging fruit.
        </p>
        <p>
         But a company engaging in this way with its customers it does leave an impression. And it cant be just lip service, either; the customers can respond to the response and call the company out if they dont do what they said!
        </p>
        <p>
         This care was the distinguishing factor for me.
         <strong>
          It was a sign that they took their business very seriously
         </strong>
         and worked to uphold their reputation, and its why I dropped nearly a grand with them, sight unseen and never having owned one of their PCs before.
        </p>
        <p>
         I was buying a computer, sure. But I was also buying into a company that, by all appearances, stands behind their products 100%.
        </p>
        <p>
         So far, it looks like I made a wise purchase. Oh, and it looks really cool, too!
        </p>
