      <h1>
       Do it Yourself: Beer Soap
      </h1>
      <div id="blog-content">
       <h2>
        Please note that soap making is fun, but it can also be dangerous. Take note of this, and keep all children and animals away from your soap making area from the time you start to make your soap, till the time the soap is cut and ready to cure.
       </h2>
       <p>
        <img alt="DIY-Header-Beer-Soap" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-2686" height="248" src="http://www.warsteiner.us/files/2014/07/DIY-Header-Beer-Soap-620x248.jpg" width="620"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Beer Soap Ingredients:
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
         Coconut Oil
        <br/>
         Palm Oil
        <br/>
         Olive Oil
        <br/>
         Liquid &amp; Sodium Hydroxide (better known as lye)*
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         *You must make sure it is pure lye, not plumbing lye that may be mixed with metal filings.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Tools you will need to make Beer Soap:
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
         Kitchen Scale (make sure it can measure in grams and can measure up to 32 oz.)
        <br/>
         2  Large Pyrex Containers
        <br/>
         Soap Mold (a simple Tupperware container that can hold at least 30 oz. will do)
        <br/>
         Tupperware Container (must be able to hold at least 16 oz of fluid and seal completely)
        <br/>
         Medium Stainless Steel Stock Pot or Large Glass Bowl
        <br/>
         Stainless Steel Whisk
        <br/>
         Protective Gloves
        <br/>
         Protective Eyewear
        <br/>
         Stick Blender
       </p>
       <p>
        The first step to making a good soap is figuring out your recipe. A soap recipe can be as simple as one single ingredient, such as Olive Oil. Or it can be elaborate, with many different oils, butters, herbs, and extracts. A soap recipe calculator is a handy tool that enables you to calculate the exact amount of liquid and sodium hydroxide (better known as lye) to mix to make your soap. We like to use a free calculator found on the site
        <a href="http://www.soapcalc.net/calc/SoapCalcWP.asp" target="_blank" title="Soap Calculator">
         Soap Calc
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        The below 1lb. recipe will yield 4 bars of soap, and contains 40% Olive Oil, 30% Coconut Oil, and 30% Palm Oil.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.warsteiner.us/files/2014/07/Warsteiner-Beer-Soap-Calculation.jpg">
         <img alt="Warsteiner-Beer-Soap-Calculation" class="alignright size-thumbnail wp-image-2312" height="270" src="http://www.warsteiner.us/files/2014/07/Warsteiner-Beer-Soap-Calculation-310x270.jpg" width="310"/>
        </a>
        You will notice that in this recipe, it calls for 6.080 oz. of water (172.365 grams of liquid), which we will substitute with Warsteiner beer. Due to evaporation in boiling, scale this up to 8 oz. of beer. The reason why you boil the beer, is to remove the alcohol. If you do not do so, you will end up with a failed batch that will look very much like a botched volcano science experiment!
       </p>
       <p>
        Pour your 8 oz. of
        <a href="http://www.warsteiner.us/our-beer/" target="_blank" title="Warsteiner Beer">
         Warsteiner beer
        </a>
        into a pot, and bring it to a slow boil for about 30 minutes.
       </p>
       <p>
        Pour the beer into a plastic container that is at least 32 oz, or a large Pyrex jug that is at least 32 oz. Allow to cool to room temperature on your kitchen counter. Once it is cool, place it into your fridge until it is completely cold.
       </p>
       <p>
        Remove the beer from the fridge and place on a steady, flat surface. Don your gloves and protective eyewear. Measure out your lye into a glass Pyrex cup. You will need 65.382 grams of lye. A critical thing to mention is that you
        <strong>
         <em>
          must always pour lye into a liquid, never the other way around. Pouring a liquid into lye is not safe.
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Place the container that has your cold beer into your kitchen sink. You will gently pour the lye into the cold beer, a little at a time, stirring slowly as you pour. Lye can often make the beer erupt out of the container, so this is why you must pour slowly and stir gently. The mixture will also get extremely hot. Once all your lye has been added to your beer, continue to stir until the mixture stops bubbling. Take your 16 oz Tupperware container and place it into your sink. Slowly pour the lye mixture into it and snap the cover in place. You will place the mixture is a safe place that cannot be accessed by children or pets (or other adults, for that matter) for 24 hours, so it can cool off completely.
        <strong>
         Do not under any circumstanced place the mixture into your fridge.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        24 hours later, it is time to melt your oils.
        <em>
         Make sure everything you use that will touch the lye/soap is either made of Pyrex glass or stainless steel. Anything else will either corrode or shatter.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Measure out your Coconut Oil and Palm Oil into the stainless steel stock pot. These oils are normally solid at room temperature. Place on low heat until the oils are melted completely. Measure your Olive Oil and add it to your Coconut/Palm Oil mixture. You can either use this stainless steel pot to make your soap, or pour the oil mixture into your large bowl and mix it there.
       </p>
       <p>
        Wear your gloves and protective eyewear. Pour your cold lye mixture into your oil, mixing with your stick blender on a low setting. Continue to mix until the soap is the consistency of a watery pea soup.
       </p>
       <p>
        Now, it is time to pour into your mold! Once you have your soap batter in your mold, you will need to cover it with a blanket and let it saponify (turn into soap) over a 24 hour period.
       </p>
       <p>
        Depending on your mold, you may have to place the soap in a fridge or freezer to release the soap from the mold. If this is the case, place into the fridge for about 30 minutes, then let it sit on a counter for 10 minutes. Turn your mold upside down, and your soap should fall out of your mold.
       </p>
       <p>
        Depending on the mold used, you can use a kitchen knife to cut your mold into bars. Once you have released your soap from the molds (and cut into bars), find a cool place to lay your soap and let them cure for 4 weeks before using. Allowing the soap to cure enables the excess liquid that is in the soap to evaporate. It also allows the soap to mellow out and become more gentle over time. You will be left with a soap that is hard, gentle, and great for your skin!
       </p>
