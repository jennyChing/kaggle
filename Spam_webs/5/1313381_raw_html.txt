     <p>
      The two
      <b>
       <i>
        Hindenburg
       </i>
       -class airships
      </b>
      were passenger carrying
      <a href="/wiki/Rigid_airship" title="Rigid airship">
       rigid airships
      </a>
      built in
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      in the 1930s and named in honor of
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">
       Paul von Hindenburg
      </a>
      . They were the last such aircraft ever built, and in terms of their length and volume, the largest aircraft ever to fly. During the 1930s, airships like the
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      class were widely considered the future of air travel, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Lead_ship" title="Lead ship">
       lead ship
      </a>
      of the class,
      <a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg">
       LZ 129
       <i>
        Hindenburg
       </i>
      </a>
      , established a regular transatlantic service. The destruction of this same ship in a
      <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">
       spectacular and highly publicized accident
      </a>
      was to prove the death knell for these expectations. The second ship,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LZ_130_Graf_Zeppelin" title="LZ 130 Graf Zeppelin">
       LZ 130
       <i>
        Graf Zeppelin
       </i>
      </a>
      was never operated on a regular passenger service, and was scrapped in 1940 at the order of
      <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Gring">
       Hermann Gring
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      class were built to an all-
      <a href="/wiki/Duralumin" title="Duralumin">
       duralumin
      </a>
      design. The leader of the design team was Doctor
      <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_D%C3%BCrr" title="Ludwig Drr">
       Ludwig Drr
      </a>
      , who had overseen the design of all Zeppelins except LZ-1 (on which he was a crew member), under the overall direction of
      <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Eckener" title="Hugo Eckener">
       Hugo Eckener
      </a>
      , the head of the company. They were 245m (804ft) long and 41m (135ft) in diameter, longer than three
      <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">
       Boeing 747s
      </a>
      placed end-to-end, longer than four
      <a href="/wiki/Goodyear_GZ-20" title="Goodyear GZ-20">
       Goodyear GZ-20
      </a>
      "blimps" end-to-end, and only 24m (79ft) shorter than the
      <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">
       RMS
       <i>
        Titanic
       </i>
      </a>
      . The previous largest civilian airship was the British
      <a href="/wiki/R101" title="R101">
       R101
      </a>
      at 777ft (236.83 m) long and 40 m wide completed in 1929. The US Navy's
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Akron_(ZRS-4)" title="USS Akron (ZRS-4)">
       <i>
        Akron
       </i>
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       Macon
      </i>
      were 239 m (785 feet) long and 144 feet wide.
     </p>
     <p>
      The design originally called for cabins for 50 passengers and a crew complement of 40.
     </p>
     <p>
      Construction of the first ship, LZ 129, later named
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      , began in 1931, but was suddenly stopped when
      <a href="/wiki/Luftschiffbau_Zeppelin" title="Luftschiffbau Zeppelin">
       Luftschiffbau Zeppelin
      </a>
      went bankrupt. This led Eckener to make a deal with the
      <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">
       Nazi Party
      </a>
      which came to power in 1933. He needed money to build the airship, but in return he was forced to display the
      <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">
       swastikas
      </a>
      on the tail fins. Construction then resumed in 1935. The keel of the second ship, LZ 130
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      was laid on June 23, 1936, and the cells were inflated with hydrogen on August 15, 1938. As the second Zeppelin to carry the name
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      (after the
      <a href="/wiki/LZ_127_Graf_Zeppelin" title="LZ 127 Graf Zeppelin">
       LZ 127
      </a>
      ), she is often referred to as
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin II
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      The duralumin frame was covered by cotton cloth varnished with
      <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">
       iron oxide
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">
       cellulose
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">
       acetate
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Butyrate" title="Butyrate">
       butyrate
      </a>
      impregnated with
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      powder.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The aluminium was added to reflect both ultraviolet, which damaged the fabric, and infrared light, which caused heating of the gas. This was an innovation that was first used on the LZ 126 which was taken as
      <a href="/wiki/War_reparations" title="War reparations">
       war reparations
      </a>
      by the US and served as the
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Los_Angeles_(ZR-3)" title="USS Los Angeles (ZR-3)">
       USS
       <i>
        Los Angeles
       </i>
       (ZR-3)
      </a>
      from 1924 until decommissioned in 1933. Following the destruction of
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      , the doping compound for the outer fabric covering of
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      was changed:
      <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">
       bronze
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">
       graphite
      </a>
      were added to prevent flammability and improve the outer covering's electrical conductivity.
     </p>
     <p>
      The rigid structure was held together by many large rings up to the size of a
      <a href="/wiki/Ferris_wheel" title="Ferris wheel">
       Ferris wheel
      </a>
      , 15 of which were gas cell boundaries which formed bulkheads. These bulkheads were braced by steel wires which connected up into the axial catwalk. The longitudinal duralumin girders connected all the rings together and formed "panels". The 16 gas cells were made of cotton and a gas-tight material. On
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      , the cells were lightened and one was made of lightweight
      <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">
       silk
      </a>
      instead of cotton.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hydrogen was vented out through valves on the top of the ship. These valves could be controlled both manually and automatically. The axial catwalk was added across the center of the ship to provide access to the gas valves. A keel catwalk provided access to the crew quarters and the engines. Alongside the keel were water ballast and fuel tanks. The tail fins of the airship were over 100ft (30m) in length, and were held together with a cross-like structure. The lower tail fin also had an auxiliary control room in case the controls in the gondola malfunctioned.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      was powered by four reversible 890kW (1,190hp)
      <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" title="Daimler-Benz">
       Daimler-Benz
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">
       diesel engines
      </a>
      which gave the airship a maximum speed of 135km/h (84mph). The engine pods were completely redesigned for
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      , using diesel engines powering tractor propellers. The engines had a water recovery system which captured the exhaust of the engines to minimize weight lost during flight.
     </p>
     <p>
      To reduce drag, the passenger rooms were contained entirely within the hull, rather than in the gondola as on the
      <a href="/wiki/LZ_127_Graf_Zeppelin" title="LZ 127 Graf Zeppelin">
       LZ 127
       <i>
        Graf Zeppelin
       </i>
      </a>
      , on two decks. The upper deck, "A", contained the passenger quarters, public areas, a dining room, a lounge, and a writing room. The lower deck, "B", contained washrooms, a mess hall for the crew, and a smoking lounge. Long slanted windows ran the length of both decks. The passenger decks were redesigned for
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      ; the restaurant was moved to the middle of the quarters and the promenade windows were half a panel lower.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lift_gas">
       Lift gas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindenburg-class_airship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lift gas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      was originally designed for
      <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
       helium
      </a>
      , heavier than
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
       hydrogen
      </a>
      but nonflammable. Most of the world's supply of helium comes from
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_field" title="Natural gas field">
       natural gas fields
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , which had banned its export under the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Helium_Control_Act_(1927)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Helium Control Act (1927) (page does not exist)">
       Helium Control Act (1927)
      </a>
      . Eckener expected this ban to be lifted, but to save helium the design was modified to have double gas cells (an inner hydrogen cell protected by an outer helium cell).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moondance_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Moondance-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The ban remained however, so the engineers used only hydrogen despite its extreme flammability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botting_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-botting-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It held 200,000 cubic metres (7,062,000cuft) of gas in 16 bags or cells with a useful lift of approximately 232t (511,000lb). This provided a margin above the 215t (474,000lb) average gross weight of the ship with fuel, equipment, 10,000kg (22,000lb) of mail and cargo, about 90 passengers and crew and their luggage.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Germans had extensive experience with hydrogen as a lifting gas. Accidental hydrogen fires had never occurred on civilian Zeppelins, so the switch from helium to hydrogen did not cause much concern. Hydrogen also increased lift by about 8%. After the
      <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">
       <i>
        Hindenburg
       </i>
       disaster
      </a>
      Eckener vowed never to use hydrogen again in a passenger airship. He planned to use helium for the second ship and went to
      <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
       Washington, D.C.
      </a>
      to personally lobby
      <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">
       President Roosevelt
      </a>
      , who promised to supply the helium only for peaceful purposes. After the
      <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">
       annexation of Austria
      </a>
      in March 1938, U.S. Secretary of the Interior
      <a href="/wiki/Harold_L._Ickes" title="Harold L. Ickes">
       Harold L. Ickes
      </a>
      refused to supply the gas, and the
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      was also filled with hydrogen.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">
       Operational history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindenburg-class_airship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operational history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="LZ_129_Hindenburg">
       LZ 129
       <i>
        Hindenburg
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindenburg-class_airship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: LZ 129 Hindenburg">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg#Operational_history" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg">
       LZ 129
       <i>
        Hindenburg
       </i>
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      made her first flight on 4 March 1936, but before commencing her intended role as a passenger liner, was put to use for propaganda purposes by the Nazi government. Together with
      <a href="/wiki/LZ_127_Graf_Zeppelin" title="LZ 127 Graf Zeppelin">
       LZ 127
       <i>
        Graf Zeppelin
       </i>
      </a>
      , she spent four days dropping leaflets, playing music, and making radio broadcasts in the lead up to the March 29 plebiscite mandating Hitler's Chancellorship and
      <a href="/wiki/Remilitarization_of_the_Rhineland" title="Remilitarization of the Rhineland">
       remilitarization of the Rhineland
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Commercial services commenced on 31 March 1936 with the first of seven round trips to
      <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">
       Rio de Janeiro
      </a>
      that
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      was to make during her first passenger season. Together with ten round trips to
      <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">
       New York
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      covered 308,323km (191,583mi) that year with 2,798 passengers and 160 tons of freight and mail.
     </p>
     <p>
      Following refurbishment during the winter,
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      set out on her first flight to North America for the 1937 season (she had already made one return trip to South America in 1937) on 3 May, bound for New York. This flight would end in tragedy with
      <i>
       Hindenburg
      </i>
      being utterly consumed by fire as she prepared to dock at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAS_Lakehurst" title="NAS Lakehurst">
       NAS Lakehurst
      </a>
      in New Jersey.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="LZ_130_Graf_Zeppelin_II">
       LZ 130
       <i>
        Graf Zeppelin II
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindenburg-class_airship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: LZ 130 Graf Zeppelin II">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/LZ_130_Graf_Zeppelin_II" title="LZ 130 Graf Zeppelin II">
       LZ 130 Graf Zeppelin II
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      By the time the
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin II
      </i>
      was completed, it was obvious that the ship would never serve its intended purpose as a passenger liner; the lack of a supply of inert helium was one cause. The ship was christened and made her first flight on September 14, 1938, making a circuit from
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrichshafen" title="Friedrichshafen">
       Friedrichshafen
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnchen" title="Mnchen">
       Mnchen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">
       Augsburg
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">
       Ulm
      </a>
      , and back. The total distance covered was 925km (575mi). The
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin II
      </i>
      made a total of thirty flights, mainly spy missions for the
      <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">
       Luftwaffe
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scrapping">
       Scrapping
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindenburg-class_airship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scrapping">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="800" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg/220px-LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg/330px-LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg/440px-LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LZ130-Bugspitze_WL.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The bow of LZ 130 in the
        <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin_Museum_Friedrichshafen" title="Zeppelin Museum Friedrichshafen">
         Zeppelin Museum Friedrichshafen
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In April 1940,
      <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Gring">
       Hermann Gring
      </a>
      issued the order to scrap both
      <i>
       Graf Zeppelin
      </i>
      and the unfinished framework of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LZ_131&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LZ 131 (page does not exist)">
       LZ 131
      </a>
      , since the metal was needed for other aircraft. By April 27, work crews had finished cutting up the airships. On May 6, the enormous
      <a href="/wiki/Airship_hangar" title="Airship hangar">
       airship hangars
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" title="Frankfurt am Main">
       Frankfurt
      </a>
      were leveled by explosives, three years to the day after the destruction of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">
       <i>
        Hindenburg
       </i>
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </div>
