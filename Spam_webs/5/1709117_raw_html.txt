   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Novikov self-consistency principle
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Novikov self-consistency principle
      </b>
      , also known as the
      <b>
       Novikov self-consistency conjecture
      </b>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Principle" title="Principle">
       principle
      </a>
      developed by Russian physicist
      <a href="/wiki/Igor_Dmitriyevich_Novikov" title="Igor Dmitriyevich Novikov">
       Igor Dmitriyevich Novikov
      </a>
      in the mid-1980s to solve the problem of
      <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">
       paradoxes
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">
       time travel
      </a>
      , which is theoretically permitted in certain solutions of
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      (solutions containing what are known as
      <a href="/wiki/Closed_timelike_curve" title="Closed timelike curve">
       closed timelike curves
      </a>
      ). The principle asserts that if an event exists that would give rise to a paradox, or to any "change" to the past whatsoever, then the
      <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">
       probability
      </a>
      of that event is zero. It would thus be impossible to create
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_paradox" title="Time paradox">
       time paradoxes
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_principle">
       History of the principle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Novikov_self-consistency_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the principle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Physicists have long been aware that there are solutions to the theory of general relativity which contain
      <a href="/wiki/Closed_timelike_curve" title="Closed timelike curve">
       closed timelike curves
      </a>
      , or CTCssee for example the
      <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6del_metric" title="Gdel metric">
       Gdel metric
      </a>
      . Novikov discussed the possibility of CTCs in books written in 1975 and 1983, offering the opinion that only self-consistent trips back in time would be permitted. In a 1990 paper by Novikov and several others,
      <i>
       Cauchy problem in spacetimes with closed timelike curves
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-friedman-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the authors state:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The only type of causality violation that the authors would find unacceptable is that embodied in the science-fiction concept of going backward in time and killing one's younger self ("changing the past"). Some years ago one of us (Novikov
       <sup>
        10
       </sup>
       ) briefly considered the possibility that CTCs might exist and argued that they cannot entail this type of causality violation: Events on a CTC are already guaranteed to be self-consistent, Novikov argued; they influence each other around a closed curve in a self-adjusted, cyclical, self-consistent way. The other authors recently have arrived at the same viewpoint.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       We shall embody this viewpoint in a
       <i>
        principle of self-consistency,
       </i>
       which states that
       <i>
        the only solutions to the laws of physics that can occur locally in the real Universe are those which are globally self-consistent.
       </i>
       This principle allows one to build a local solution to the equations of physics only if that local solution can be extended to a part of a (not necessarily unique) global solution, which is well defined throughout the nonsingular regions of the spacetime.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Among the coauthors of this 1990 paper were
      <a href="/wiki/Kip_Thorne" title="Kip Thorne">
       Kip Thorne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Morris_(physicist)" title="Mike Morris (physicist)">
       Mike Morris
      </a>
      , and Ulvi Yurtsever, who in 1988 had stirred up renewed interest in the subject of time travel in general relativity with their paper
      <i>
       Wormholes, Time Machines, and the Weak Energy Condition
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which showed that a new general relativity solution known as a
      <a href="/wiki/Wormhole#Traversable_wormholes" title="Wormhole">
       traversable wormhole
      </a>
      could lead to closed timelike curves, and unlike previous CTC-containing solutions it did not require unrealistic conditions for the universe as a whole. After discussions with another coauthor of the 1990 paper, John Friedman, they convinced themselves that time travel need not lead to unresolvable paradoxes, regardless of what type of object was sent through the wormhole.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timewarps_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-timewarps-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :509
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In response, another physicist named
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Polchinski" title="Joseph Polchinski">
       Joseph Polchinski
      </a>
      sent them a letter in which he argued that one could avoid questions of free will by considering a potentially paradoxical situation involving a
      <a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball" title="Billiard ball">
       billiard ball
      </a>
      sent through a wormhole which sends it back in time. In this scenario, the ball is fired into a
      <a href="/wiki/Wormhole" title="Wormhole">
       wormhole
      </a>
      at an angle such that, if it continues along that path, it will exit the wormhole in the past at just the right angle to collide with its earlier self, thereby knocking it off course and preventing it from entering the wormhole in the first place. Thorne deemed this problem "Polchinski's paradox".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timewarps_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-timewarps-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :510511
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After considering the problem, two students at Caltech (where Thorne taught), Fernando Echeverria and Gunnar Klinkhammer, were able to find a solution beginning with the original billiard ball trajectory proposed by Polchinski which managed to avoid any inconsistencies. In this situation, the billiard ball emerges from the future at a different angle than the one used to generate the paradox, and delivers its younger self a glancing blow instead of knocking it completely away from the wormhole, a blow which changes its trajectory in just the right way so that it will travel back in time with the angle required to deliver its younger self this glancing blow. Echeverria and Klinkhammer actually found that there was more than one self-consistent solution, with slightly different angles for the glancing blow in each case. Later analysis by Thorne and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Forward" title="Robert Forward">
       Robert Forward
      </a>
      showed that for certain initial trajectories of the billiard ball, there could actually be an infinite number of self-consistent solutions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timewarps_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-timewarps-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :511513
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Echeverria, Klinkhammer and Thorne published a paper discussing these results in 1991;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in addition, they reported that they had tried to see if they could find
      <i>
       any
      </i>
      initial conditions for the billiard ball for which there were no self-consistent extensions, but were unable to do so. Thus it is plausible that there exist self-consistent extensions for every possible initial trajectory, although this has not been proven.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earman_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-earman-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :184
      </sup>
      This only applies to initial conditions which are outside of the chronology-violating region of spacetime,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earman_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-earman-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :187
      </sup>
      which is bounded by a
      <a href="/wiki/Cauchy_horizon" title="Cauchy horizon">
       Cauchy horizon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This could mean that the Novikov self-consistency principle does not actually place any constraints on systems outside of the region of spacetime where time travel is possible, only inside it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even if self-consistent extensions can be found for arbitrary initial conditions outside the Cauchy Horizon, the finding that there can be multiple distinct self-consistent extensions for the same initial conditionindeed, Echeverria et al. found an infinite number of consistent extensions for every initial trajectory they analyzed
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earman_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-earman-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :184
      </sup>
      can be seen as problematic, since classically there seems to be no way to decide which extension the laws of physics will choose. To get around this difficulty, Thorne and Klinkhammer analyzed the billiard ball scenario using quantum mechanics,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timewarps_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-timewarps-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :514515
      </sup>
      performing a quantum-mechanical sum over histories (
      <a href="/wiki/Path_integral_formulation" title="Path integral formulation">
       path integral
      </a>
      ) using only the consistent extensions, and found that this resulted in a well-defined probability for each consistent extension. The authors of
      <i>
       Cauchy problem in spacetimes with closed timelike curves
      </i>
      write:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The simplest way to impose the principle of self-consistency in quantum mechanics (in a classical space-time) is by a sum-over-histories formulation in which one includes all those, and only those, histories that are self-consistent. It turns out that, at least formally (modulo such issues as the convergence of the sum), for every choice of the billiard ball's initial, nonrelativistic
       <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">
        wave function
       </a>
       before the
       <a href="/wiki/Cauchy_horizon" title="Cauchy horizon">
        Cauchy horizon
       </a>
       , such a sum over histories produces unique, self-consistent probabilities for the outcomes of all sets of subsequent measurements. ... We suspect, more generally, that for any quantum system in a classical wormhole spacetime with a stable Cauchy horizon, the sum over all self-consistent histories will give unique, self-consistent probabilities for the outcomes of all sets of measurements that one might choose to make.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Assumptions_of_the_Novikov_self-consistency_principle">
       Assumptions of the Novikov self-consistency principle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Novikov_self-consistency_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Assumptions of the Novikov self-consistency principle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Novikov consistency principle assumes certain conditions about what sort of time travel is possible. Specifically, it assumes either that there is only one
      <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">
       timeline
      </a>
      , or that any alternative timelines (such as those postulated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation" title="Many-worlds interpretation">
       many-worlds interpretation
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
       quantum mechanics
      </a>
      ) are not accessible.
     </p>
     <p>
      Given these assumptions, the constraint that time travel must not lead to inconsistent outcomes could be seen merely as a
      <a href="/wiki/Tautology_(logic)" title="Tautology (logic)">
       tautology
      </a>
      , a self-evident truth that cannot possibly be false, because if you make the assumption that it is false this would lead to a logical
      <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">
       paradox
      </a>
      . However, the Novikov self-consistency principle is intended to go beyond just the statement that history must be consistent, making the additional nontrivial assumption that the universe obeys the same local laws of physics in situations involving time travel that it does in regions of spacetime that lack closed timelike curves. This is made clear in the above-mentioned
      <i>
       Cauchy problem in spacetimes with closed timelike curves
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friedman_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-friedman-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where the authors write:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       That the principle of self-consistency is not totally tautological becomes clear when one considers the following alternative: The laws of physics might permit CTC's; and when CTC's occur, they might trigger new kinds of local physics which we have not previously met. ... The principle of self-consistency is intended to rule out such behavior. It insists that local physics is governed by the same types of physical laws as we deal with in the absence of CTC's: the laws that entail self-consistent single valuedness for the fields. In essence, the principle of self-consistency is a principle of no new physics. If one is inclined from the outset to ignore or discount the possibility of new physics, then one will regard self-consistency as a trivial principle.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Time_loop_logic">
       Time loop logic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Novikov_self-consistency_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Time loop logic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Time loop logic
      </i>
      , coined by the
      <a href="/wiki/Roboticist" title="Roboticist">
       roboticist
      </a>
      and futurist
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Moravec" title="Hans Moravec">
       Hans Moravec
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is the name of a hypothetical system of computation that exploits the Novikov self-consistency principle to compute answers much faster than possible with the standard model of
      <a href="/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory" title="Computational complexity theory">
       computational complexity
      </a>
      using
      <a href="/wiki/Turing_machine" title="Turing machine">
       Turing machines
      </a>
      . In this system, a computer sends a result of a computation
      <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">
       backwards through time
      </a>
      and relies upon the self-consistency principle to force the sent result to be correct, providing the machine can reliably receive information from the future and providing the algorithm and the underlying mechanism are
      <a href="/wiki/Formal_verification" title="Formal verification">
       formally correct
      </a>
      . An incorrect result or no result can still be produced if the time travel mechanism or algorithm are not guaranteed to be accurate.
     </p>
     <p>
      A simple example is an
      <a href="/wiki/Iterative_method" title="Iterative method">
       iterative method
      </a>
      algorithm. Moravec states:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Make a computing box that accepts an input, which represents an approximate solution to some problem, and produces an output that is an improved approximation. Conventionally you would apply such a computation repeatedly a finite number of times, and then settle for the better, but still approximate, result. Given an appropriate negative delay something else is possible: [...] the result of each iteration of the function is brought back in time to serve as the "first" approximation. As soon as the machine is activated, a so-called "fixed-point" of F, an input which produces an identical output, usually signaling a perfect answer, appears (by an extraordinary coincidence!) immediately and steadily. [...] If the iteration does not converge, that is, if F has no fixed point, the computer outputs and inputs will shut down or hover in an unlikely intermediate state.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Physicist
      <a href="/wiki/David_Deutsch" title="David Deutsch">
       David Deutsch
      </a>
      showed in 1991 that this model of computation could solve NP problems in
      <a href="/wiki/Time_complexity#Polynomial_time" title="Time complexity">
       polynomial time
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Scott_Aaronson" title="Scott Aaronson">
       Scott Aaronson
      </a>
      later extended this result to show that the model could also be used to solve
      <a href="/wiki/PSPACE" title="PSPACE">
       PSPACE
      </a>
      problems in polynomial time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
