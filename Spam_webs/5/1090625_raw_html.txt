            <p>
             <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-15385" height="500" src="http://www.adventure-journal.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/01/8c82a18e16cf7d5027300e8b172cd740.jpg" title="8c82a18e16cf7d5027300e8b172cd740" width="500"/>
             Iceland is a landscape of the imagination. Bjork called it an emotional terrain, though perhaps moody might be a better description. Perched as it is in the North Atlantic, small and subject to every whim of climate and weather, conditions are ever changing. Clouds, mist, fog, rain, a bit of sun, all can visit in 15 minutes and be gone again. Its no surprise, then, that photographers love Iceland, especially those who are fascinated by dramatic and brooding light.
            </p>
            <p>
             The south coast where German photographer
             <a href="http://www.behance.net/gallery/Iceland/631225">
              Michael Schlegel
             </a>
             shot these stark images is not as sparsely populated and gothic as the pictures would suggest. There are towns and farms and flesh and blood people. But Schlegels photos show an Iceland that lingers at the farthest edges of our fancies, desolate and inhospitable, a dream of northern contrasts and timeless symbolism of light and dark.
            </p>
