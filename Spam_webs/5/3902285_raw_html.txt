             How brands and publishers define success in native
            </h2>
           </div>

           <p>
            Back in1900, Michelin released itsfirst guide for French motorists. Complete with helpful maps and hotel names, the Michelin Guide was an immediate success. As the content itself had little to do with the brands product  tires  this was native advertising in its earliest form.
           </p>
           <p>
            Michelins goal was clear: More drivers, more tires. But somehow over the past 115 years, the goals of branded content have become less defined. The industry has yet to determine a definition of native success. Many brands still live and die by publisher metrics (impressions, shares and clicks), despite a lack of data connecting them with engagement or revenue.
           </p>
           <p>
            The definition of success innative advertising, it turns out, varies by publisher and brand. Here, then, is a look at how native gets measured in the wild.
           </p>
           <p class="advertisement">
            Advertisement
           </p>
           <div class="ad-wrapper">
            <div data-tid="8" data-url="http://digiday.com/publishers/brands-publishers-define-success-native/" id="article_1">
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            <strong>
             The waning of CTR
            </strong>
            <br/>
            Click-through rate (CTR) has ruled digital advertising for decades, but brands need to dig deeper with native content. A successful campaign should generate more complex reporting, said Justin Choi, CEOof Nativo. [These metrics] are fighting for relevancy in the face of data suggesting consumers who engage less share more, and vice versa.
           </p>
           <p>
            That isnt to say publisher metrics like CTR are meaningless. CTR has a time and a place in branded content, said Paul Josephsen, vp of integrated marketing at Thrillist Media Group. Its great for things like optimizing headlines and measuring engagement against a specific exposure, but its inherently flawed.
           </p>
           <p>
            The flaw is that a click may have little to no impact on a particular brand. BuzzFeed, for example, might struggle to prove how
            <a href="http://www.buzzfeed.com/thelaughingcow/cows-that-are-too-cute-to-handle#.xbnPBkBM3" target="_blank">
             this listicle of the worlds cutest cows
            </a>
            improved perception of or drove revenue for Laughing Cow  despite its nearly 20,000 shares (figure provided by Nudge Analytics).
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="Screen Shot 2015-02-20 at 12.41.10 PM" class="alignnone wp-image-106629 size-full" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/digiday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Screen-Shot-2015-02-20-at-12.41.10-PM.png?resize=688%2C434"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            We try to get our advertisers not to look exclusively at CTR, said Rich Antoniello, CEOof Complex Media. It sets a very dangerous precedent wherein branded content reads like clickbait.
           </p>
           <p>
            And while CTR itself has merit  it reflects discovery, interest and engagement, said Choi  it shouldnt make or break a campaign. To truly understand performance, one must be versatile. Ben Young,CEOof Nudge Analytics, for example, will explore CTR as well as average attention minutes.
           </p>
           <p>
            The idea isnt death to CTR, explained Josephsen. Its that theres more to life than CTR.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             The metrics that matter
            </strong>
            <br/>
            No two initiatives are alike, and so performance metrics should vary by campaign. Publishers need to fully understand the objectives of a campaign before pen is ever put to paper.
           </p>
           <p>
            The first thing we do is define our advertisers objectives, said Josephsen. We ask them whether theyre focused on awareness, engagement, brand affinity, etc., and then tell them, Thrillist will be successful at that one thing. This allows their team to be more creative, untethered from the promise of clicks.
           </p>
           <p>
            Antoniellos team at Complex also defines itsapproach by the clients objectives. Everything from strategy to copy to optimization is geared entirely toward a single goal.
           </p>
           <p>
            The first question we ask isnt what is your metric? Antoniello said. Its what is your objective? Well recommend a completely different campaign for someone looking to build a social community, versus someone looking to start a conversation. 
           </p>
           <p>
            So when Mountain Dew approached Complex looking for a consistent influx of unique users to engage with itsbrand, Complexdeveloped Green Label  an always-on, digital magazine. The content is custom fit to the soda brands specific goals and objectives.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="greenlabel" class="alignnone wp-image-106848 size-inline" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/digiday.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/greenlabel.png?resize=744%2C653"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             The metrics of the future
            </strong>
            <br/>
            By 2017, brands will measure success based on audience reach and average attention minutes, predicted Young. The industry also needs to connect the dots between devices and audience overlap.
           </p>
           <p>
            The idea of connecting dots dominates this fragmented industry. Choi, for example, is focused on the idea that content not be an island apart from conversions, and that we need to close the loop between engagement and actions.
           </p>
           <p>
            Josephsen sees the native industry as one that needs to evolve along with its performance metrics. One of the biggest changes he predicts is programmatic native, which he feels will soon be driven by cookies and audience profiles.
           </p>
           <p>
            Antoniello, however, struggles to see how programmatic native can ever really drive success. It doesnt make any sense to me, he said. The context of native is what makes it successful  not its location. And voice simply cannot be automated.
