     <p>
      <b>
       Geocaching
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/d/ 'j' in 'jam'">
           d
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ long 'e' in 'bead'">
           i
          </span>
          <span title="/o/ long 'o' in 'code'">
           o
          </span>
          <span title="// secondary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
           k
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'a' in 'bad'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'sh' in 'shy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'i' in 'bid'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'ng' in 'sing'">
           
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      is an outdoor recreational activity, in which participants use a
      <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
       Global Positioning System
      </a>
      (GPS)
      <a href="/wiki/GPS_navigation_device" title="GPS navigation device">
       receiver
      </a>
      or mobile device and other navigational techniques to hide and seek containers, called "geocaches" or "caches", anywhere in the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      A typical cache is a small waterproof container containing a
      <a href="/wiki/Logbook" title="Logbook">
       logbook
      </a>
      (with a pen or pencil). The geocacher enters the date they found it and signs it with their established code name. After signing the log, the cache must be placed back exactly where the person found it. Larger containers such as plastic storage containers (
      <a href="/wiki/Tupperware#Product_lines" title="Tupperware">
       Tupperware
      </a>
      or similar) or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammo_box" title="Ammo box">
       ammunition boxes
      </a>
      can also contain items for trading, usually toys or trinkets of little financial value, although sometimes they are sentimental. Geocaching shares many aspects with
      <a href="/wiki/Benchmarking_(geolocating)" title="Benchmarking (geolocating)">
       benchmarking
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigpointing#Trigpointing" title="Trigpointing">
       trigpointing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Orienteering" title="Orienteering">
       orienteering
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Treasure_hunt_(game)" title="Treasure hunt (game)">
       treasure-hunting
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Letterboxing_(hobby)" title="Letterboxing (hobby)">
       letterboxing
      </a>
      , and waymarking.
     </p>

     <p>
      Geocaching was originally similar to the 160-year-old game
      <a href="/wiki/Letterboxing_(hobby)" title="Letterboxing (hobby)">
       letterboxing
      </a>
      , which uses clues and references to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landmarks" title="Landmarks">
       landmarks
      </a>
      embedded in stories. Geocaching was conceived shortly after the removal of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selective_Availability" title="Selective Availability">
       Selective Availability
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
       Global Positioning System
      </a>
      on May 2, 2000, because the improved accuracy
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the system allowed for a small container to be specifically placed and located. The first documented placement of a GPS-located cache took place on May 3, 2000, by Dave Ulmer of
      <a href="/wiki/Beavercreek,_Oregon" title="Beavercreek, Oregon">
       Beavercreek
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The location was posted on the
      <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">
       Usenet
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newsgroup" title="Newsgroup">
       newsgroup
      </a>
      sci.geo.satellite-nav
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geocaches">
       Geocaches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geocaches">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Classic_Geocache.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1356" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Classic_Geocache.jpg/220px-Classic_Geocache.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Classic_Geocache.jpg/330px-Classic_Geocache.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Classic_Geocache.jpg/440px-Classic_Geocache.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Classic_Geocache.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A classic geocache -- trade items in a military ammunition box
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      For the traditional geocache, a geocacher will place a waterproof container containing a log book (with
      <a href="/wiki/Pen" title="Pen">
       pen
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Pencil" title="Pencil">
       pencil
      </a>
      ) and trade items then record the cache's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitude_and_longitude" title="Latitude and longitude">
       coordinates
      </a>
      . These coordinates, along with other details of the location, are posted on a
      <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">
       listing site
      </a>
      (see list of some sites below). Other geocachers obtain the coordinates from that listing site and seek out the cache using their GPS handheld receivers. The finding geocachers record their exploits in the logbook and online, but then must return the cache to the same coordinates so that other geocachers may find it. Geocachers are free to take objects (except the logbook, pencil, or stamp) from the cache in exchange for leaving something of similar or higher value.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1712" data-file-width="2288" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG/220px-Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG/330px-Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG/440px-Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geocoin_at_Spier%27s.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Bear" title="Smokey Bear">
         Smokey Bear
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Geocoin" title="Geocoin">
         Geocoin
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Typical cache "treasures" are not high in monetary value but may hold personal value to the finder. Aside from the logbook, common cache contents are unusual
      <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">
       coins
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">
       currency
      </a>
      , small toys, ornamental buttons, CDs, or books. Also common are objects that are moved from cache to cache called "hitchhikers", such as
      <a href="/wiki/Travel_Bug" title="Travel Bug">
       Travel Bugs
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Geocoin" title="Geocoin">
       Geocoins
      </a>
      , whose travels may be logged and followed online. Cachers who initially place a Travel Bug or Geocoins often assign specific goals for their trackable items. Examples of goals are to be placed in a certain cache a long distance from home, or to travel to a certain country, or to travel faster and farther than other hitchhikers in a race. Higher-value items are occasionally included in geocaches as a reward for the First to Find (called "FTF"), or in locations which are harder to reach. Dangerous or illegal items, weapons, food and drugs are generally not allowed and are specifically against the rules of most geocache listing sites.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/220px-Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/330px-Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/440px-Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Travel_Bug_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Travel_Bug" title="Travel Bug">
         Travel Bug
        </a>
        from
        <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
         Hong Kong
        </a>
        attached to a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Stored_Value_Ticket" title="Common Stored Value Ticket">
         Common Stored Value Ticket
        </a>
        .

      </div>

     <p>
      If a geocache has been vandalized or stolen, it is said to have been "muggled". The former term plays off the fact that those not familiar with geocaching are called
      <a href="/wiki/Muggle" title="Muggle">
       muggles
      </a>
      , a term borrowed from the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">
        Harry Potter
       </a>
      </i>
      series of books which was rising in popularity at the same time geocaching got its start.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">
       Variations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Traditional geocaching gave birth to
      <a href="/wiki/Encounter_(game)#GeoCaching" title="Encounter (game)">
       GeoCaching
      </a>
       an active urban game of the
      <a href="/wiki/Encounter_(game)" title="Encounter (game)">
       Encounter
      </a>
      project. The game is quite similar to geocaching but has time limitations and hints in it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Google
      <a href="/wiki/Ingress_(game)" title="Ingress (game)">
       Ingress
      </a>
      , in which players must be physically near objects' locations, in order to have simulated interactions with them, using an Android or iPhone app. This is somewhat like virtual geocaches, or geodashing, with addition of more advanced game characteristics.
     </p>
     <p>
      Geocaches vary in size, difficulty, and location. Simple caches are often called "drive-bys," "park 'n grabs" (PNGs), or "cache and dash". Geocaches may also be complex, involving lengthy searches or significant travel. Different geocaching websites list different variations per their own policies.
     </p>
     <p>
      Container sizes range from "nanos", which can be smaller than the tip of finger and have only enough room to store the log sheet, to 20-liter (5 gallon) buckets or even larger containers, such as entire trucks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The most common cache containers in rural areas are lunch-box-sized plastic storage containers or surplus military ammunition cans. Ammo cans are considered the gold standard of containers because they are very sturdy, waterproof, animal- and fire-resistant, relatively cheap, and have plenty of room for trade items. Smaller containers are more common in urban areas because they can be more easily hidden.
     </p>
     <p>
      Caches can be grouped in three categories - with and without a paper log, and events.
     </p>
     <p>
      The following types do not have paper logs. Similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Munzee" title="Munzee">
       Munzee
      </a>
      , a BIT Cache is a laminated card with a QR code, URL and a password needed for logging. More information is available at bitcaching.com.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They are listed exclusively on opencaching.us.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cache_Parameters_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cache_Parameters-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Virtual_Cache.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Virtual_Cache.JPG/220px-Virtual_Cache.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Virtual_Cache.JPG/330px-Virtual_Cache.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Virtual_Cache.JPG/440px-Virtual_Cache.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Virtual_Cache.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A Geocacher finding a Virtual Cache at
        <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Station" title="McMurdo Station">
         McMurdo Station
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
         Antarctica
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Virtual caches are coordinates for a location, which has some other described object. Validation for finding a virtual cache generally requires one to email the cache hider with information such as a date or a name on a plaque, or to post a picture of oneself at the site with GPS receiver in hand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      New virtual caches are no longer allowed by Groundspeak,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geocaching.com_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-geocaching.com-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but they remain fully supported at other sites such as Opencaching.us and the rest of the Opencaching Network.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cache_Parameters_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cache_Parameters-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Earthcaches are virtual caches that are organized by the
      <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_America" title="Geological Society of America">
       Geological Society of America
      </a>
      . The cacher usually has to perform a task which teaches him/her an educational lesson about the earth science of the cache area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Locationless/Reverse caches are similar to a
      <a href="/wiki/Scavenger_hunt" title="Scavenger hunt">
       scavenger hunt
      </a>
      . A description is given for something to find, such as a one-room schoolhouse, and the finder locates an example of this object. The finder records the location using their GPS receiver and often takes a picture at the location showing the named object and his or her GPS receiver. Typically others are not allowed to log that same location as a find.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Webcam caches are virtual caches whose coordinates have a public
      <a href="/wiki/Webcam" title="Webcam">
       webcam
      </a>
      . The finder is often required to capture their image from the webcam for verification of the find.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      New webcam caches are no longer allowed by Groundspeak,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geocaching.com_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-geocaching.com-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but they remain fully supported at other sites such as Opencaching.us and the rest of the Opencaching Network.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cache_Parameters_15-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cache_Parameters-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finally, a USB Cache or Dead Drop cache
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cache_Parameters_15-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cache_Parameters-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      location has a USB drive embedded (with permission) into walls or other structures. The cache is retrieved by connecting a device that has a USB port and that is able to read standard text files.
     </p>
     <p>
      The following types have logs, at least at the final location.
     </p>
     <p>
      A Multi-cache consists of multiple discoveries of one or more intermediate points containing the coordinates for the next stage; the final stage contains the log book.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A Night Cache is multi-stage and intended to be found at night by following a series of reflectors with a flashlight to the final cache location.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A Chirp Cache is a Garmin-created innovative advance on multi caches using new wireless beacon technology. The Chirp stores hints, multicache coordinates, counts visitors and confirms the cache is nearby.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These caches are fully supported at Opencaching.com, but they caused considerable discussion and some controversy at Groundspeak, where they were given a new "attribute."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Wherigo" title="Wherigo">
       Wherigo
      </a>
      cache is a multi-stage cache hunt that uses a Wherigo "cartridge" to guide the player to find a physical cache sometime during cartridge play, usually at the end. Not all Wherigo cartridges incorporate geocaches into game play. Wherigo caches are unique to the geocaching.com website.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An Offset cache is a multi-cache in which the initial coordinates are for a location containing information that encodes the final cache coordinates. An example would be to direct the finder to a plaque where the digits of a date on the plaque correspond to coordinates of the final cache.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Guest Book Caches use guest books often found in museums, tourist information centers, etc. They are listed exclusively at Opencaching.us.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cache_Parameters_15-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cache_Parameters-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Letterboxing_(hobby)" title="Letterboxing (hobby)">
       letterbox
      </a>
      hybrid cache is a combination of a geocache and a letterbox in the same container. A letterbox has a rubber stamp and a logbook instead of tradable items. Letterboxers carry their own stamp with them, to stamp the letterbox's log book and inversely stamp their personal log book with the letterbox stamp. The hybrid cache contains the important materials for this and may or may not include trade items. Whether the letterbox hybrid contains trade items is up to the owner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moving/Travelling caches are found at a listed set of coordinates. The finder hides the cache in a different location, and updates the listing, essentially becomes the hider, and the next finder continues the cycle. This cache type is fully supported at
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.opencaching.us/" rel="nofollow">
       Opencaching.us
      </a>
      and other Opencaching Network sites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cache_Parameters_15-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cache_Parameters-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mystery/puzzle caches require one to discover information or solve a puzzle to find the cache. Some mystery caches provide a false set of coordinates with a puzzle that must be solved to determine the final cache location. In other cases, the given location is accurate, but the name of the location or other features are themselves a puzzle leading to the final cache. Alternatively, additional information is necessary to complete the find, such as a padlock combination to access the cache.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_types_17-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-geo_types-17">
        <span>
         [

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Converting_and_filtering_data">
       Converting and filtering data
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Converting and filtering data">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A variety of geocaching applications are available for geocache data management, file-type translation, and personalization. Geocaching
      <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">
       software
      </a>
      can assign special icons or search (filter) for caches based on certain criteria (e.g. distance from an assigned point, difficulty, date last found).
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Paperless geocaching
      </b>
      means hunting a geocache without a physical printout of the cache description. Traditionally, this means that the seeker has an electronic means of viewing the cache information in the field, such as pre-downloading the information to a
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">
       PDA
      </a>
      or other electronic device. Various applications are able to directly upload and read
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS_eXchange_Format" title="GPS eXchange Format">
       GPX
      </a>
      files without further conversion. Newer GPS devices released by
      <a href="/wiki/Garmin" title="Garmin">
       Garmin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/DeLorme" title="DeLorme">
       DeLorme
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Magellan_Navigation" title="Magellan Navigation">
       Magellan
      </a>
      have the ability to read GPX files directly, thus eliminating the need for a
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">
       PDA
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other methods include viewing real-time information on a portable computer with internet access or with a web-enabled smart phone. The latest advancement of this practice involves installing dedicated applications on a smart phone with a built-in GPS receiver. Seekers can search for and download caches in their immediate vicinity directly to the application and use the on-board GPS receiver to find the cache.
     </p>
     <p>
      A more controversial version of paperless caching involves mass-downloading only the coordinates and cache names (or waypoint IDs) for hundreds of caches into older receivers. This is a common practice of some cachers and has been used successfully for years. In many cases, however, the cache description and hint are never read by the seeker before hunting the cache. This means they are unaware of potential restrictions such as limited hunt times, park open/close times, off-limit areas, and suggested parking locations.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics">
       Ethics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ethics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Geocache listing websites have their own guidelines for acceptable geocache publications. Government agencies and others responsible for public use of land often publish guidelines for geocaching, and a "Geocacher's Creed" posted on the Internet asks participants to "avoid causing disruptions or public alarm."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP070927_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WP070927-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Generally accepted rules are to not endanger others, to minimize the impact on nature, to respect
      <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">
       private property
      </a>
      , and to avoid public alarm.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">
       Controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cachers have been approached by
      <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">
       police
      </a>
      and questioned when they were seen as acting suspiciously.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other times, investigation of a cache location after suspicious activity was reported has resulted in police and bomb squad discovery of the geocache.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Schools have been occasionally evacuated when a cache has been seen by teachers or police, as in the case of Fairview High School in 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Vogel_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mike_Vogel-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A number of caches have been destroyed by bomb squads.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Diverse locations, from rural cemeteries to
      <a href="/wiki/Disneyland" title="Disneyland">
       Disneyland
      </a>
      , have been locked down as a result of such scares.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The placement of geocaches has occasional critics among some government personnel and the public at large who consider it littering. Some geocachers act to mitigate this perception by picking up litter while they search for geocaches, a practice referred to in the community as CITO (Cache-In-Trash-Out). Events and caches are often organized revolving around this practice, with many areas seeing significant cleanup that would otherwise not take place, or would instead require federal, state or local funds to accomplish. Geocachers are also encouraged to clean up after themselves by retrieving old containers once a cache has been removed from play.
     </p>
     <p>
      Geocaching is not illegal in the United States and is usually positively received when explained to law enforcement officials. However, certain types of placements can be problematic. Although generally disallowed, hiders could place caches on private property without adequate permission (intentionally or otherwise), which encourages cache finders to trespass. Caches might also be hidden in places where the act of searching can make a finder look suspicious (e.g. near schools, children's playgrounds, banks, courthouses, or in residential neighborhoods), or where the container placement could be mistaken for a
      <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">
       drug
      </a>
      stash or a
      <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">
       bomb
      </a>
      (especially in urban settings, under bridges, near banks, courthouses, or embassies). As well as concerns about littering and bomb threats, some geocachers hide their caches in inappropriate locations, such as electrical boxes, that may encourage risky behaviour, especially amongst children. Hides in these areas are discouraged,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_Vogel_54-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mike_Vogel-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and cache listing websites enforce guidelines that disallow certain types of placements. However, as cache reviewers typically cannot see exactly where and how every particular cache is hidden, problematic hides can slip through. Ultimately it is also up to cache finders to use discretion when attempting to search for a cache, and report any problems.
     </p>
     <p>
      Regional rules for placement of caches have become quite complex. For example in Virginia,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Virginia Department of Transportation and the Wildlife Management Agency now forbids the placement of geocaches on all land controlled by those agencies. Some cities, towns and recreation areas allow geocaches with few or no restrictions, but others require compliance with lengthy permitting procedures.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_House_of_Representatives" title="South Carolina House of Representatives">
       South Carolina House of Representatives
      </a>
      passed Bill 3777
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

     <p>
      The largest site is Geocaching.com, owned by

      </a>
      Inc., which began operating on September 2, 2000. With a worldwide membership and a
      <a href="/wiki/Freemium" title="Freemium">
       Freemium
      </a>
      business model, the website claims millions of caches and members in over 200 countries. Hides and events are reviewed by volunteer regional cache reviewers before publication. Free membership allows users access to coordinates, descriptions, and logs for some caches; for a fee, users are allowed additional search tools, the ability to download large amounts of cache information onto their gps at once, instant email notifications about new caches, and access to premium-member-only caches.

Earthcaches are one of the two exceptions to the no-container rule; they are caches in which players must answer geological questions to complete the cache. The other exception is for event caches; for an event to qualify, it must be specifically or mainly for geocachers, and must have a minimum duration dependent upon its category (CITO, regular, Mega, or Giga).

Hunters use the clues to try to find the benchmark; the benchmark can be logged as Found, Not Found, Note, or Destroyed. The "Destroyed" log should be used only if there is evidence that the mark has been permanently destroyed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Opencaching_Network">
       Opencaching Network
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Opencaching Network">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Opencaching Network provides independent, non-commercial listing sites based in the cacher's country or region. The Opencaching Network lists the most types of caches, including traditional, virtual, moving, multi, quiz, webcam, BIT, guest book, USB, event and MP3. The Opencaching Network is less restrictive than many sites, and does not charge for the use of the sites. All listings are reviewed by the network operators before being published and although cross-listing is permitted, it is discouraged. Some listings are listed on other sites, but there are many that are unique to the Opencaching Network. Features include the ability to organize one's favourite caches, build custom searches, be instantly notified of new caches in one's area, seek and create caches of all types, export GPX queries, statpics, etc. Each Opencaching Node provides the same API for free (called "OKAPI"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85">
       <a href="#cite_note-85">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        85
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) for developers who want to create third-party application with Opencaching Network's content.
     </p>
     <p>
      Countries with associated opencaching websites include the United States at www.opencaching.us; Germany at www.opencaching.de;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86">
       <a href="#cite_note-86">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        86
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sweden at www.opencaching.se; Poland at www.opencaching.pl; Czech Republic at www.opencaching.cz; Norway at www.opencaching.no; The Netherlands at www.opencaching.nl, and the United Kingdom at www.opencaching.org.uk.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpenCaching_United_States_Wiki_87-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OpenCaching_United_States_Wiki-87">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        87
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The main difference between opencaching and traditional listing sites is that all services are open to the users at no cost. Generally, most geocaching services or websites offer some basic information for free, but users may have to pay for premium information or advanced searching capabilities. This is not the case with opencaching; every geocache is listed and accessible to everyone for free.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpenCaching_United_States_Wiki_87-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OpenCaching_United_States_Wiki-87">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        87
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Additionally, Opencaching sites allow users to rate and report on existing geocaches. This allows users to see what other cachers think of the cache and it encourages participants to place higher quality caches. The rating system also greatly reduces the problem of abandoned or unsatisfactory caches still being listed after repeated negative comments or posts in the cache logs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpenCaching_United_States_Wiki_87-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-OpenCaching_United_States_Wiki-87">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        87
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Opencaching.com">

        ]

     <p>

Caches are approved by a community process and coordinates are available without an account. Traditional, puzzle, virtual, and multi caches are supported.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Source" title="Open Source">
       Open Source
      </a>
      license. The site was created by
      <a href="/wiki/Garmin" title="Garmin">
       Garmin
      </a>
      , but owning a Garmin device is not required for the full use of the site, as there are several Android and iPhone apps that let users access the site while on the trail.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89">
       <a href="#cite_note-89">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        89
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

       <a href="#cite_note-90">

        90
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sites">
       Other sites
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other sites">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>

     <p>
      In many countries there are regional geocaching sites, but these mostly only compile lists of caches in the area from the three main sites. Many of them also accept unique listings of caches for their site, but these listings tend to be less popular than the international sites, although occasionally the regional sites may have more caches than the international sites. There are some exceptions though, e.g. in the former
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      , the site Geocaching
      <a href="/wiki/.su" title=".su">
       .su
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">
       Cyrillic script
      </a>
      . Additional international sites include Geocaching.de, a German website, and Geocaching Australia, which accepts listings of cache types deprecated by geocaching.com as well as traditional geocaches.

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98">

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While many of the website's listings have been posted to other sites, it also offers unique listings. The website lists nearly any type of geocache and does not charge to access any of the caches listed in its database. All submissions are reviewed and approved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101">
       <a href="#cite_note-101">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        101
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Navicache is under transition to new owners, who said they "plan to develop a site that geocachers want, with rules that geocachers think are suitable. GC.com and OX are both backed by large enterprises, and while that means they have more funding and people, were a much smaller team  so our advantage is the ability to be dynamic and listen to the users.

        <span>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="TerraCaching">
       TerraCaching
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geocaching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: TerraCaching">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Terracaching.com seeks to provide high-quality caches made so by the difficulty of the hide or from the quality of the location. Membership is managed through a sponsorship system, and each cache is under continual peer review from other members. Terracaching.com embraces virtual caches alongside traditional/multi-stage caches and includes many locationless caches among the thousands of caches in its database. It is increasingly attracting members who like the point system. In Europe, TerraCaching is supported by Terracaching.eu. This site is translated in different European languages, has an extended FAQ and extra supporting tools for TerraCaching. Terracaching strongly discourages caches that are listed on other sites, so called double-listing.
