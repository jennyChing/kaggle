     <p>
      <b>
       John William Vessey, Jr.
      </b>
      (born June 29, 1922) is a retired
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
       United States Army
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/General_(United_States)" title="General (United States)">
       general
      </a>
      . He served as the tenth
      <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">
       Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff
      </a>
      from June 18, 1982 to September 30, 1985.
     </p>
     <p>
      When he retired in 1985 at the age of 63, General Vessey was the longest serving active duty member in the United States Army. He began his 46-year military career in the
      <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_National_Guard" title="Minnesota National Guard">
       Minnesota National Guard
      </a>
      in 1939 when he was still 16. He received a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlefield_commission" title="Battlefield commission">
       battlefield commission
      </a>
      during the Battle of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Shingle" title="Operation Shingle">
       Anzio
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . General Vessey also served in the
      <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">
       Korean
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam Wars
      </a>
      . As a Lieutenant Colonel, he graduated from college in 1963 at the age of 41. As a Colonel, he was a student at the Army helicopter school at the age of 48.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1982, President
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
       Ronald Reagan
      </a>
      appointed him as Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, a position he would hold for over three years, a comparatively short term. He was the last four star combat veteran of World War II on active service.
     </p>
     <p>
      After retiring from the Army, General Vessey became involved in efforts to account for military personnel listed as missing in action from the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      . He made several trips to Vietnam to search for remains as part of resolving the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War_POW/MIA_issue" title="Vietnam War POW/MIA issue">
       Vietnam War POW/MIA issue
      </a>
      . General Vessey was awarded the
      <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">
       Presidential Medal of Freedom
      </a>
      in 1992.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">
       Early life and education
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Vessey was born in Minneapolis, Minnesota, on June 29, 1922. In May 1939, thirteen months before he graduated from Roosevelt High School, he enlisted in the
      <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_National_Guard" title="Minnesota National Guard">
       Minnesota National Guard
      </a>
      as a motorcycle rider in Headquarters, 59th Field Artillery Brigade, 34th Infantry Division.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMM_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MMM-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His unit was activated in February 1941.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_Life">
       Personal Life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal Life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      He is married to the former Avis Funk of Minneapolis. They have two sons, John III and David, and a daughter, Sarah. They retired to a home in central Minnesota near Garrison, Minnesota.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMM_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MMM-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">
       World War II
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During World War II, Vessey served with the
      <a href="/wiki/34th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="34th Infantry Division (United States)">
       34th Infantry Division
      </a>
      . The experience of early American setbacks in
      <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
       North Africa
      </a>
      left Vessey with a lifelong appreciation of the need for realistic combat training, modern equipment, physical fitness, and air-ground cooperation. When
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General_(United_States)" title="Major General (United States)">
       Major General
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bradley" title="Omar Bradley">
       Omar Bradley
      </a>
      , Commander of
      <a href="/wiki/II_Corps_(United_States)" title="II Corps (United States)">
       II Corps
      </a>
      in North Africa, launched the U.S. drive on
      <a href="/wiki/Bizerte" title="Bizerte">
       Bizerte
      </a>
      in April 1943, he gave the 34th the most difficult objective: the well-defended Hill 609. In the first clear-cut U.S. Army victory of the campaign, the 34th Division took its objective, opening the way for the U.S. advance on Bizerte. Vessey, who had been a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Sergeant" title="First Sergeant">
       First Sergeant
      </a>
      since 1 September 1942, later described being a first sergeant in combat as the "toughest job" he had. He was with the 34th when it entered the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Shingle" title="Operation Shingle">
       Anzio beachhead
      </a>
      in Italy in May 1944; there he received a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battlefield_commission" title="Battlefield commission">
       battlefield commission
      </a>
      as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Lieutenant#United_States" title="Second Lieutenant">
       second lieutenant
      </a>
      , serving as a
      <a href="/wiki/Artillery_observer" title="Artillery observer">
       forward observer
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_War_and_afterwards">
       Korean War and afterwards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Korean War and afterwards">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After the war, most of Vessey's service continued to be in
      <a href="/wiki/Field_artillery" title="Field artillery">
       field artillery
      </a>
      assignments. In the 1950s he served with the
      <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Division (United States)">
       4th Infantry Division
      </a>
      in Germany and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Eighth_Army" title="U.S. Eighth Army">
       Eighth U.S. Army
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">
       Republic of Korea
      </a>
      . During this period he also attended the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_and_General_Staff_College" title="Command and General Staff College">
       Command and General Staff College
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Leavenworth" title="Fort Leavenworth">
       Fort Leavenworth
      </a>
      , Kansas.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the time Vessey became a
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant colonel (United States)">
       lieutenant colonel
      </a>
      , he had earned enough credits through night school and correspondence courses for a
      <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">
       bachelor of science
      </a>
      degree, which he received from the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland_University_College" title="University of Maryland University College">
       University of Maryland University College
      </a>
      in 1963. In 1965, he received a
      <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">
       master of science
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">
       George Washington University
      </a>
      . From 1963 to 1965 Vessey commanded the 2d Battalion, 73rd Field Artillery in the
      <a href="/wiki/3rd_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="3rd Armored Division (United States)">
       3rd Armored Division
      </a>
      ; then he spent a year as a student at the
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_College_of_the_Armed_Forces" title="Industrial College of the Armed Forces">
       Industrial College of the Armed Forces
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam_War">
       Vietnam War
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vietnam War">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      , Vessey served for a year as Executive Officer of the
      <a href="/wiki/25th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="25th Infantry Division (United States)">
       25th Infantry Division
      </a>
      Artillery in Vietnam. In March 1967, when acting as Commander of the 2nd Battalion, 77th Artillery, he was given the mission of establishing a
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_support_base" title="Fire support base">
       fire support base
      </a>
      at Suoi Tre during
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Junction_City" title="Operation Junction City">
       Operation Junction City
      </a>
      . Located deep in enemy-controlled territory, Vessey and his men oriented the firebase's defenses on the enemy's likely avenues of approach and rehearsed counterattack plans. During the attack by a reinforced
      <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">
       regiment
      </a>
      , the base was partially overrun. Vessey and his men fired their
      <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">
       howitzers
      </a>
      directly into the enemy ranks. Although greatly outnumbered, the defenders, aided by
      <a href="/wiki/Gunship" title="Gunship">
       gunships
      </a>
      and artillery, killed four hundred of their assailants while successfully defending the firebase. Lieutenant Colonel Vessey received the
      <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Service_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Service Cross (United States)">
       Distinguished Service Cross
      </a>
      for his actions during the battle.
     </p>
     <p>
      From Vietnam, he went to Germany, to serve first as Commander of the
      <a href="/wiki/3rd_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="3rd Armored Division (United States)">
       3d Armored Division
      </a>
      Artillery from October 1967 until March 1969 and then as Division Chief of Staff for a year. He was promoted to
      <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">
       colonel
      </a>
      in November 1967. Vessey went back to Southeast Asia in December 1970 to head the U.S. Army Support Command, Thailand. In January 1972 he went into Laos to coordinate all US military operations in support of the war in Laos. Vessey worked with the U.S. ambassador, the CIA station chief, and an assortment of military contingents. When the Laotian ceasefire came in February 1973, the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Lao_Government" title="Royal Lao Government">
       Royal Lao government
      </a>
      controlled all major cities and the vast majority of the population.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Vietnam_War">
       After the Vietnam War
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: After the Vietnam War">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Upon his return to the United States, Vessey became Director of Operations in the Office of the Deputy Chief of Staff for Operations and Plans. Promoted to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_General#United_States" title="Major General">
       major general
      </a>
      in August 1974, he assumed command of the
      <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Division (United States)">
       4th Infantry Division (Mechanized)
      </a>
      at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Carson,_Colorado" title="Fort Carson, Colorado">
       Fort Carson, Colorado
      </a>
      . Promoted to
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant general (United States)">
       lieutenant general
      </a>
      in September 1975, he became the Army's Deputy Chief of Staff for Operations and Plans.
     </p>
     <p>
      Vessey received his
      <a href="/wiki/General_(United_States)" title="General (United States)">
       fourth star
      </a>
      in November 1976. From 1976 to 1979 he served in the Republic of Korea as Commanding General of the Eighth U.S. Army; Commander of U.S. Forces, Korea; and Commander in Chief of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Command_(Korea)" title="United Nations Command (Korea)">
       United Nations Command
      </a>
      . In 1978, he became the first Commander in Chief of the Republic of Korea-United States Combined Forces Command. His tour was marked by increased tension caused by evidence of a North Korean buildup and by President
      <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">
       Jimmy Carter
      </a>
      's 1977 announcement that U.S. ground forces would be withdrawn. Vessey worked to assuage South Korean concerns and change the President's decision. After Carter's 1979 visit, withdrawal plans were suspended and then cancelled. From July 1979 until June 1982, General Vessey served as Vice Chief of Staff of the Army.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chairmanship_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff">
       Chairmanship of the Joint Chiefs of Staff
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chairmanship of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On June 18, 1982, he became the tenth
      <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">
       Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff
      </a>
      and the last World War II combat veteran to serve in the position. General Vessey was the only Chairman who had been neither a
      <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Chief of Staff of the United States Army">
       Service Chief
      </a>
      nor a commander of a unified or specified command. He served as Chairman during a period of unprecedented growth in peacetime defense spending and an expanded U.S. military presence worldwide intended to counter growing Soviet military power.
     </p>
     <p>
      Vessey and the Service Chiefs believed that their overriding task lay in convincing Soviet leaders that their quest for military superiority and geostrategic advantage was fruitless. In Europe, they pushed the controversial but successful deployment of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pershing_II" title="Pershing II">
       Pershing II
      </a>
      and ground-launched
      <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">
       cruise missiles
      </a>
      to offset the Soviet
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RT-21M_Pioneer" title="RT-21M Pioneer">
       SS-20
      </a>
      missiles. In Southwest Asia, highly visible US military activities underscored the US commitment to defend its vital interests in the region. In Central America, training and intelligence were provided to support
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_insurgency" title="Counter insurgency">
       counter-insurgency
      </a>
      efforts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Believing that it was a mistake to commit a superpower's forces to a peacekeeping mission, Vessey and the Joint Chiefs in 1982 and 1983 advised against deployment of a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">
       Marine contingent
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">
       Lebanon
      </a>
      as part of a multinational peacekeeping force intended to restore peace among warring factions there. Their advice was not taken, and on 23 October 1983 a
      <a href="/wiki/1983_Beirut_barracks_bombing" title="1983 Beirut barracks bombing">
       truck-bomb attack on the Marine headquarters building in Beirut
      </a>
      killed 241 US Marines and Army soldiers. In late February 1984 President
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
       Reagan
      </a>
      withdrew the contingent from Lebanon.
     </p>
     <p>
      Vessey stressed the need for improvement of war plans and, for the first time, JCS members along with commanders of unified and specified commands personally participated in
      <a href="/wiki/Military_simulation" title="Military simulation">
       war games
      </a>
      . Realizing the need to strengthen the joint system, Vessey and the Service Chiefs improved Joint Staff operations by adding a capability for budgetary analysis and by improving the quality of its personnel, changes that did not require legislation.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">
       Secretary of Defense
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Weinberger" title="Caspar Weinberger">
       Caspar Weinberger
      </a>
      understood the importance of decentralization; he authorized Vessey to direct military operations on the Secretary's behalf. The 1983
      <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Grenada" title="Invasion of Grenada">
       Grenada operation
      </a>
      , for example, was planned by Atlantic Command, reviewed by the JCS, and approved by Secretary Weinberger and the Presidentall in four days. Vessey oversaw execution of the operation that rescued US citizens and brought a pro-US government into power.
     </p>
     <p>
      During Vessey's tenure there was increased emphasis on space as a theater of operations. In early 1983, the Joint Chiefs mentioned to the President that defense against nuclear-armed missiles might be technically feasible in the next century. To their surprise, Reagan seized upon the concept and on 23 March 1983 announced his vision of the
      <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">
       Strategic Defense Initiative
      </a>
      (SDI). Realizing the enormous military advantages to be gained from operations in space and to support SDI, the JCS recommended the establishment of a unified command for space.
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Strategic_Command" title="United States Strategic Command">
       US Space Command
      </a>
      was activated on 23 September 1985.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnamese_emissary">
       Vietnamese emissary
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vietnamese emissary">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Vessey retired on September 30, 1985, several months before the expiration of his second term as Chairman. He was the last four-star World War II combat veteran on active duty and, with forty-six years of service, had served the longest of anyone then in the Army. In retirement, he served President Reagan and his successors, Presidents
      <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
       George Bush
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
       William J. Clinton
      </a>
      , as a special emissary to Vietnam on the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War_POW/MIA_issue" title="Vietnam War POW/MIA issue">
       question of American service personnel missing from the Vietnam War
      </a>
      . For his work in Vietnam, General Vessey was awarded the prestigious
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Academy" title="United States Military Academy">
       United States Military Academy's
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Sylvanus_Thayer_Award" title="Sylvanus Thayer Award">
       Sylvanus Thayer Award
      </a>
      in 1996. He was also awarded the nation's highest civilian award, the
      <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">
       Presidential Medal of Freedom
      </a>
      , in 1992.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Assignments">
       Assignments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_William_Vessey,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Assignments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       1939: National Guard enlisted service
      </li>
      <li>
       1941: 34th Division Artillery, Camp Claiborne, LA, Northern Ireland, North Africa, and Italy as S/Sgt, 1st Sgt, and then battlefield commission to 2Lt (Communications Officer/Forward Observer/Air Observer)
      </li>
      <li>
       1945: US Army Field Artillery School, Fort Sill, OK
      </li>
      <li>
       1949: Student, Field Artillery Officers Advanced Course, Field Artillery School, Fort Sill, OK
      </li>
      <li>
       1950: Battery Officer; then Battery Commander, 18th Field Artillery, Fort Sill, OK
      </li>
      <li>
       1951: Assistant S-3 and Liaison Officer; then Headquarters Battery Commander; then Assistant S-3 and Liaison Officer, 4th Infantry Division Artillery US Army, Europe
      </li>
      <li>
       1954: Student, Artillery Officer Advanced Course, Artillery and Guided Missile School, Fort Sill, OK
      </li>
      <li>
       1955: Battery Commander, Artillery and Guided Missile School Officer Candidate School
      </li>
      <li>
       1956: Gunnery Instructor, Artillery and Guided Missile School, Fort Sill, OK
      </li>
      <li>
       1957: Student, US Army Command and General Staff College, Fort Leavenworth, KS
      </li>
      <li>
       1958: Artillery Section, Eighth US Army with duty station CINCPAC Coordination Center, Philippines
      </li>
      <li>
       1958: Chief, Operations Branch, Artillery Section, Eighth US Army, Korea
      </li>
      <li>
       1959: Assignment Officer, then Executive Officer, Artillery Officers Division, Office of Deputy Chief of Staff for Personnel, Washington, D.C.
      </li>
      <li>
       1963: Student, Armed Forces Staff College, Norfolk, VA
      </li>
      <li>
       1963: Commander, 2d Battalion, 73d Artillery, 3d Armored Division, US Army, Europe
      </li>
      <li>
       1965: Student, Industrial College of the Armed Forces, Washington, D.C.
      </li>
      <li>
       1966: Executive Officer, 25th Infantry Division Artillery, Vietnam
      </li>
      <li>
       1967: Commander, 3d Armored Division Artillery, US Army, Europe
      </li>
      <li>
       1969: Chief of Staff, 3d Armored Division, US Army, Europe
      </li>
      <li>
       1970: Student, US Army Primary Helicopter School,
       <a href="/wiki/Fort_Wolters" title="Fort Wolters">
        Fort Wolters
       </a>
       , TX; later US Army Aviation School, Fort Rucker, AL
      </li>
      <li>
       1970: Commanding General, US Army Support Command, Thailand
      </li>
      <li>
       1972: Deputy Chief, JUSMAGTHAI (Chief MAAG, Laos)
      </li>
      <li>
       1973: Director of Operations, Office Deputy Chief of Staff for Operations and Plans, Washington, D.C.
      </li>
      <li>
       1974: Commanding General, 4th Infantry Division (Mechanized), Fort Carson, CO
      </li>
      <li>
       1975: Deputy Chief of Staff for Operations and Plans, US Army, Washington, D.C.
      </li>
      <li>
       1976: Commanding General, Eighth US Army; and Commander in Chief, US Forces, Korea; and Commander in Chief, United Nations Command
      </li>
      <li>
       1978: Commander in Chief, Republic of Korea-United States Combined Forces Command, Korea
      </li>
      <li>
       1979: Vice Chief of Staff, US Army, Washington, D.C.
      </li>
      <li>
       1982: Chairman, Joint Chiefs of Staff, Washington, D.C.

        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
