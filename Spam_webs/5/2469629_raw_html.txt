        <header>
         <h1 class="title entry-title">
          European Stamps &amp; Card Making Trends
         </h1>
        </header>
        <div class="post-meta">
         <span class="small">
          By
         </span>
         <span class="author vcard">
          <span class="fn">
           <a href="http://www.craftcritique.com/author/suzyh/" rel="author" title="Posts by Suzy Haghighi">
            Suzy Haghighi
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="small">
          on
         </span>
         <abbr class="date time published updated" title="2008-11-14T08:00:00-0400">
          November 14, 2008
         </abbr>
         <span class="small">
          in
         </span>
         <span class="categories">
          <a href="http://www.craftcritique.com/category/uncategorized/" title="View all items in Uncategorized">
           Uncategorized
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <section class="entry">
         <div>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Reported by
            <a href="http://blog.papermonkey.org/">
             Suzy Haghighi
            </a>
           </span>
          </strong>
          <img alt="Add Image" border="0" src="http://www.blogger.com/img/blank.gif"/>
         </div>
         <p>
          (Editors note: This is a LONG article, chock full of fabulous information go grab yourself a cuppa, and sit a spell)
         </p>
         <div>
         </div>
         <p>
          While there may be as many different kinds of stamps and card making styles in Europe as there are in North America, I am going to generalize for the purposes of this article and discuss the stamps and styles of five European stamping companies I am addicted to:
          <a href="http://www.dreamcottage.nu/english1/">
           Magnolia Stamps
          </a>
          from Sweden,
          <a href="http://www.whiffofjoy.com/">
           Whiff of Joy
          </a>
          from Switzerland,
          <a href="http://stampavie.com/">
           Stampavie
          </a>
          from France,
          <a href="http://www.stampgalaxy.co.uk/catalog/index.php?cPath=13_193">
           Toodles &amp; Binks
          </a>
          from the UK, and
          <a href="http://elzybells.co.uk/">
           Elzybells Stamps
          </a>
          in the UK. This article will discuss these European companies, as well as the European style of paper crafting that is often used with these kinds of stamps. I will also briefly talk about the rise in the popularity of European stamps and the European style of card making in North America as it relates to these companies.
         </p>
         <div>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Magnolia Stamps
           </span>
          </strong>
         </div>
         <div>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/408_large1.jpg">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/408_large.jpg"/>
          </a>
          Magnolia Stamps from Sweden are the oldest and most popular of the five companies. Designed by Pauline Bertilsson, their cute, homespun style has been highly influential, and served as a model to many fledgling European stamp manufacturers. They were my first foray into European stamps. I love to color, paper piece and add dimension to my cards, and had been looking for stamps that had a large surface area and the right designs for doing that.
         </div>
         <p>
          Magnolia Stamps has two different lines within the company that revolve around different characters. The most popular line in the company is the
          <em>
           Tilda and Edwin Collection
          </em>
          , accompanied by
          <em>
           Baby Alvin
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Tingla
          </em>
          . Often (but not always) with angel wings, they are little children with chubby cheeks that can be seen in different poses wearing clothing from different time periods, events, seasons and holidays. The style is a unique fusion between homespun folk and modern trendy.
         </p>
         <p>
          They were not easy to find in North America until recently, but are now experiencing a sudden rise in popularity thanks to bloggers, popular paper crafting forums, and retailers.
         </p>
         <div>
         </div>
         <p>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/tildawood.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/411_large1.jpg">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/411_large.jpg"/>
          </a>
          North American distributor
          <a href="http://www.magnoliastamps.us/">
           Magnolia-licious
          </a>
          owner Diana Crick says, Magnolia pays great attention to creating landscape stamps that co-ordinate beautifully with the images. When creating a card these accessory stamps take your card making to the next level. For example, there is a side view stamp of Tilda and chair or step stamps you can buy to seat her and create scenes without having to do a lot of masking (crafters usually cut Tilda out to fit on these prop stamps). The larger designs of these stamps allow for lots of open, solid space. This makes for fun paper piecing. It is the rubber stamp equivalent of playing paper dolls.
         </div>
         <p>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/mag.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <div>
          Magnolia stamps are lilac colored rubber that come either unmounted or on
          <a href="http://www.sundayint.com/index.asp?PageAction=VIEWCATS&amp;Category=57">
           EZMount
          </a>
          (a foam that cushions the stamp and clings to your acrylic block without the use or adhesive). The average size of a Tilda stamp is 3.5 x 1.75 inches.  The backgrounds can get quite large  an Oak Tree background stamp is 4.25 x 5.5 inches. Crafters often stamp the image then cut half the tree out to fit within a card, and mask a Tilda in front of the tree.
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Whiff of Joy
           </span>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/12_01.jpg">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/12_0.jpg"/>
          </a>
          Owner Katharina Frei loved card-making so much she left her job in the medical field to start her own stamp company,
          <a href="http://www.shop.whiffofjoy.com/">
           Whiff of Joy
          </a>
          , with her husband in July 2008.  Using established professional artists, American Elisabeth Bell, and Canadian Alison Acton as illustrators, the unmounted red rubber stamps are highly detailed and the largest of the group (for example,
          <em>
           Willow with Lantern
          </em>
          is 2 3/4 wide by 4.25 high).
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/41_01.jpg">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/41_0.jpg"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          The quality is amazing; of all the stamp companies, Whiff of Joy has the thickest and most deeply etched rubber. The stamps focus around four lines  Guardian Angels with wings
          <em>
           Melinda
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Matthew
          </em>
          , Kids
          <em>
           Charlotte
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Charlie
          </em>
          , and
          <em>
           Willow &amp; Oakley
          </em>
          , who are a bit older. New characters
          <em>
           Lizzie,
          </em>
          a teenager and
          <em>
           Herb,
          </em>
          a snowman, were introduced November 8th for the holidays.
         </p>
         <p>
          One of the companys most popular stamps,
          <em>
           Willow With Hot Chocolate
          </em>
          , can be seen in the card below. Like Magnolia stamps, Whiff of Joy has accessory and background stamps such as books for the reading angel, a pillow heart for the angel holding a heart, and grounded flower backgrounds. Whiff of Joy stamps are the most detailed of the group and perfect for coloring and shading. Alison Actons Charlotte and Charlie are kids that are cartoon like, whereas Elisabeth Bells designs are whimsical, sweet and romantic.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/apicture_076_2.jpg"/>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Toodles &amp; Binks
           </span>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/simone1.jpg">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/simone.jpg"/>
          </a>
          The newest company of the bunch,
          <a href="http://www.stampgalaxy.co.uk/catalog/index.php?cPath=13_193">
           Toodles &amp; Binks
          </a>
          , opened in October of this year, and has released three stamps so far, all unmounted red rubber. Each character will eventually have a set of accessory and friend stamps that revolve around it. Owner Kay Bolton and her husband manufacture the unmounted red rubber stamps from their farm outside London.
         </div>
         <p>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/aPicture_036.jpg"/>
         </p>
         <div>
          Asked why the European stamp style has gained such popularity in North America recently, Kay replied I think it might have something to do with the real homespun, folk art feel that emerged from Norway/Sweden over the last 18 months or so; it spread to the UK, and now also recently reached the North American crafters. There is definitely a resurgence in colouring stamped images here in Europe, and this new style has certainly fulfilled that need.
         </div>
         <p>
          Toodles &amp; Binks is the cute line of parent company Stamp Galaxy, and was created after the owner rediscovered her love of coloring thanks to North American blogs and their recent focus on Copic Markers.  Once again, the Internet had its part in the transatlantic exchange of ideas. Toodles &amp; Binks illustrator Genevieve Godbout is a French Canadian who lives in London and works for Disney. You can definitely see a cartoon influence in her stamps. While
          <em>
           Celeste the Fairy
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Joel the Deer
          </em>
          are all about fantasy, the newest stamp,
          <em>
           Simone
          </em>
          , is a stylish yet cute working woman.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Elzybells UK
           </span>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/397-11.jpg">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/397-1.jpg"/>
          </a>
          Elizabeth is both illustrator and owner of the UK based company Elzybells. Like many other rubber stamp companies, Elzybells was created from her passion for her hobby. Her line is based upon Girl and Boy, and also various animals and flowers. The Girl reminds me of a female Charlie Brown and is once again, very cute.
         </div>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div>
         </div>
         <p>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/elzy.jpg"/>
          <br/>
          Elzybells stamps do not have the fine detailing of some of the other stamps, which makes them easier to color and less intimidating than the more detailed stamps. The stamps have large, open spaces making them very easy to paper piece without a lot of intricate cutting. Elzybells stamps are available in both wood mounted and unmounted red rubber, and Elizabeth also sells a few clear stamp sets. A much smaller stamp version of Elzybells is distributed in North America by
          <a href="http://www.amuseartstamps.com/">
           A Muse Artstamps
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <div>
         </div>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div>
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Stampavie
           </span>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/SAV-RM023.gif">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/SAV-RM023.png"/>
          </a>
          <a href="http://www.stampavie.com/">
           Stampavie
          </a>
          , formerly a scrapbooking retail company, was launched in May of 2008. The company is the only one of the five to sell their images primarily on photopolymer clear acrylic stamps. The French corporation has lines by two different artists so far with a third collection by Alaskan Leer Aldrich coming soon.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/aPicture_055.jpg"/>
         </div>
         <p>
          Canadian Rachelle Anne Millers
          <em>
           Lily and Milo
          </em>
          line is taken right off the pages of her childrens book illustrations. Her sweet and happy children are very stylized and detailed with tiny dots, stars, and flourishes.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div>
          <a data-slb-active="1" data-slb-internal="0" href="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/SK-XMAS-ART-01B1.png">
           <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/SK-XMAS-ART-01B.png"/>
          </a>
          Illustrator Sarah Kays detailed scenes of girls with rag doll dresses and bonnets, or bell bottoms and halter tops hearken back to the art and style of Holly Hobbie. The retro images are tender, sweet and above all very cute.
         </div>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Commonalities
           </span>
          </strong>
          :
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Size:
           </span>
          </strong>
          As mentioned above, the main images of these European stamps tend to be larger (an average of 3.5 X 4 inches) than their North American counterparts, which leads to different designing methods. Did the size of the stamps influence card design methods or vice versa? While I cant answer that, I can say that the European style of card making typically involves creating larger sized cards.
          <a href="http://patternedpaper.typepad.com/patterned_paper/">
           Inge Groot
          </a>
          , a Netherlands based designer for Whiff of Joy, Magnolia and Elzybells Stamps says My cards are usually 6 x 6 inches, but a lot of ladies make cards 14 x 14 cm (5.55.5 inches).
         </p>
         <div>
          <br/>
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://www.craftcritique.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/woj.jpg"/>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <span>
             Design:
            </span>
           </strong>
           When asked about the difference in styles between North American card makers and their European counterparts. Whiff of Joy owner Katharina Frei says The American/Canadian stamp style is often simpler, but just as beautiful. The advantage to the simpler style is that people produce a lot of cards in less time, but with a great effectiveness. Europeans tend to use a lot of patterned paper, whereas Americans/Canadians often create their own backgrounds. They also tend to use sentiments as their center of cards and embellish around them. With Europeans sentiments only play a minor role when making cards. Complexly coloured images with a lot of embellishments, often overloaded cards form the European style. But I notice an assimilation on both sides.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          A larger canvas allows for more layers and embellishments. These layers are created with the use of patterned papers and embossed cardstock. As for the embellishments used, Primas, buttons, threads, and beads are used for a busy and detailed collage like card.
         </p>
         <div>
          <br/>
          Layers are not for patterned paper alone: these larger stamps are often cut using a paper tole process whereby an image is stamped two or more times, cut and layered to product a 3D effect. Cutting and masking against backgrounds to create scenes is very popular.
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Subject Matter:
           </span>
          </strong>
          These companies all have one thing in common: the cute factor. Stamp subject matter often consists of sweet, whimsical or romanticized images of women, children at play, and chubby cheeked angels. Happy and playful, they embody happy childhood memories.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Unmounted:
           </span>
          </strong>
          With the exception of Elzybells, the five companies reviewed today all sell unmounted stamps (Elzybells sells both wood mounted, unmounted and photopolymer). All sell rubber stamps except Stampavie, which only sells photopolymer (clear) stamps.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Detailed Images:
           </span>
          </strong>
          It is no coincidence that many European card makers love to color their images. These larger stamps are perfect for coloring. Copic markers are quickly becoming the coloring tool of choice alongside colored pencils and Peerless Watercolours.
         </p>
         <div>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Pros:
           </span>
          </strong>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           High quality overall
          </li>
          <li>
           Large, detailed stamps are fun to color and paper piece
          </li>
          <li>
           Cute and unique, many with a quirky personality
          </li>
          <li>
           Unmounted stamps dont take up a lot of room
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <span>
            Cons:
           </span>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Shipping and customs clearance can take a long time (although North American retailers stock some of these companies now too)
          </li>
          <li>
           It can be hard to find envelopes for the larger size cards made with these stamps
          </li>
          <li>
           Cost: The average European card costs more to make than than an average North American card because of the embellishments; extra postage is often required for mailing
          </li>
          <li>
           Some people do not like the cutesy card style and/or the time it takes to make a European style card. These type of cards are not easy to mass produce.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Conclusion
          </strong>
          :
         </p>
         <p>
          The stamps from these 5 companies have a wonderfully sweet style that allow me to make the most of my coloring and paper tole skills. The subject matter makes me smile and brings back happy childhood memories. The quality of the stamps is very high overall, and the fact that they are unmounted allows room for storage in my small craft room. I highly recommend them. I also like that they are unique and different, and often come with accessory stamps to create scenes.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Where to buy:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Elzybells stamps are available in the larger size I discussed in this article from
          <a href="http://elzybells.co.uk/">
           Elzybells UK
          </a>
          . A smaller version, called Elzybells NA, is available through
          <a href="http://www.amuseartstamps.com/">
           A Muse Artstamps
          </a>
          , who distributes them exclusively for North America. Elzybells UK stamps average about 3.25 each for unmounted rubber ($5 US) or 6.50 each for wood mounted ($10 US).
         </p>
         <p>
          Magnolia Stamps are available directly from the
          <a href="http://www.dreamcottage.nu/english1/index1.html" target="_blank">
           manufacturer
          </a>
          in Sweden or in North America from
          <a href="http://www.magnoliastamps.us/">
           Magnolia-licious
          </a>
          . Unmounted EZMount cushioned rubber stamps average in price from $9  $15 US for the larger background stamps.
         </p>
         <p>
          Stampavie Stamps (Rachelle Anne Miller, Sarah Kay) are available in North America from the
          <a href="http://sweetnsassystamps.biz/shop/">
           Sweet n Sassy Stamp Shop
          </a>
          and many other North American retailers. For a full listing of retailers around the world see the Stampavie site
          <a href="http://www.stampavie.com/store_locate.php" target="_blank">
           here
          </a>
          . They average about $9 US each for unmounted photopolymer (clear) stamps.
         </p>
         <p>
          Toodles &amp; Binks Stamps are currently only available from
          <a href="http://www.stampgalaxy.co.uk/catalog/index.php?cPath=13_193">
           Stamp Galaxy
          </a>
          . They are 4.50 each (about $7 US) for unmounted red rubber.
         </p>
         <p>
          Whiff of Joy Stamps are available from the
          <a href="http://www.whiffofjoy.com/">
           manufacturer
          </a>
          in Switzerland or in North America from
          <a href="http://allthatscraps.com/">
           All That Scraps
          </a>
          . For a full listing of retailers around the world please see the Whiff of Joy website. They average about $10-11 US each for unmounted red rubber.
         </p>

          I would love to hear your thoughts and comments concerning European stamps and this article. I will be happy to answer your questions as well.
