     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Legend of Zelda: Link's Awakening
       </b>
      </i>
      , known as
      <i>
       Zelda no Densetsu: Yume o Miru Shima
      </i>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       :
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">
         
       </span>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">
         <span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">
          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       , lit.
       <i>
        The Legend of Zelda: Dream Island
       </i>
       )
      </span>
      in Japan, is a 1993
      <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">
       action-adventure
      </a>
      video game developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_Analysis_%26_Development" title="Nintendo Entertainment Analysis &amp; Development">
       Nintendo Entertainment Analysis &amp; Development
      </a>
      and published by
      <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">
       Nintendo
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">
       Game Boy
      </a>
      . It is the fourth installment in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda" title="The Legend of Zelda">
        The Legend of Zelda
       </a>
      </i>
      series, and the first for a
      <a href="/wiki/Handheld_game_console" title="Handheld game console">
       handheld game console
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      began as a port of the
      <a href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Super Nintendo Entertainment System">
       Super Nintendo
      </a>
      title
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_to_the_Past" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past">
        The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past
       </a>
      </i>
      , developed after-hours by Nintendo staff. It grew into an original project under the direction of
      <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Tezuka" title="Takashi Tezuka">
       Takashi Tezuka
      </a>
      , with a story and script created by
      <a href="/wiki/Yoshiaki_Koizumi" title="Yoshiaki Koizumi">
       Yoshiaki Koizumi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kensuke_Tanabe" title="Kensuke Tanabe">
       Kensuke Tanabe
      </a>
      . It is one of the few
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      games not to take place in the land of
      <a href="/wiki/Universe_of_The_Legend_of_Zelda#Hyrule" title="Universe of The Legend of Zelda">
       Hyrule
      </a>
      , and does not feature
      <a href="/wiki/Princess_Zelda" title="Princess Zelda">
       Princess Zelda
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Triforce" title="Triforce">
       Triforce
      </a>
      relic. Instead, protagonist
      <a href="/wiki/Link_(The_Legend_of_Zelda)" title="Link (The Legend of Zelda)">
       Link
      </a>
      begins the game stranded on Koholint Island, a place guarded by a creature called the Wind Fish. Assuming the role of Link, the player fights monsters and solves puzzles while searching for eight musical instruments that will awaken the sleeping Wind Fish and allow him to escape from the island.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      was critically and commercially successful. Critics praised the game's depth and number of features; complaints focused on its control scheme and monochrome graphics. A remake titled
      <i>
       <b>
        The Legend of Zelda: Link's Awakening DX
       </b>
      </i>
      was released for the
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">
       Game Boy Color
      </a>
      in 1998 featuring color graphics, compatibility with the
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Printer" title="Game Boy Printer">
       Game Boy Printer
      </a>
      , and an exclusive color-based dungeon. Together, the two versions of the game have sold more than six million units worldwide, and have appeared on multiple game publications' lists of the best games of all time.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Setting_and_characters">
       Setting and characters
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Setting and characters">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Unlike most
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda" title="The Legend of Zelda">
        The Legend of Zelda
       </a>
      </i>
      titles,
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      is set outside the kingdom of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyrule" title="Hyrule">
       Hyrule
      </a>
      . It omits locations and characters from previous games, aside from protagonist Link and a passing mention of
      <a href="/wiki/Princess_Zelda" title="Princess Zelda">
       Princess Zelda
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Instead, the game takes place entirely on Koholint Island,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      an isolated landmass cut off from the rest of the world. The island, though small, contains a large number of secrets and interconnected pathways.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_la_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-history_of_la-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      , the player is given advice and directions by
      <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">
       non-player characters
      </a>
      such as Ulrira, a shy old man who communicates with Link exclusively by telephone. The game contains
      <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">
       cameo appearances
      </a>
      by characters from other Nintendo titles, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Wart_(character)" title="Wart (character)">
       Wart
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yoshi" title="Yoshi">
       Yoshi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kirby_(character)" title="Kirby (character)">
       Kirby
      </a>
      , Dr. Wright (renamed Mr. Write) from the Super Nintendo version of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SimCity_SNES" title="SimCity SNES">
        SimCity
       </a>
      </i>
      , and the exiled prince Richard from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kaeru_no_Tame_ni_Kane_wa_Naru" title="Kaeru no Tame ni Kane wa Naru">
        Kaeru no Tame ni Kane wa Naru
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nintendopower-guide_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nintendopower-guide-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chomp_(Mario)" title="Chomp (Mario)">
       Chomp
      </a>
      , an enemy from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mario_(series)" title="Mario (series)">
       <i>
        Mario
       </i>
       series
      </a>
      , was included after a programmer gave Link the ability to grab the creature and take it for a walk. Enemies from Super Mario Bros. such as Goombas also appear in underground side-scrolling sections; Link may land on top of them much as with Super Mario Bros., or he can attack them in the usual way: both methods yield different bonuses. Director Takashi Tezuka said that the game's "freewheeling" development made
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      seem like a parody of
      <i>
       The Legend of Zelda
      </i>
      series.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Certain characters in the game break the
      <a href="/wiki/Fourth_wall" title="Fourth wall">
       fourth wall
      </a>
      ; for example, little children inform the player of game mechanics such as saving, but state that they do not understand their own advice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_2">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After the events of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Oracle_of_Seasons_and_Oracle_of_Ages" title="The Legend of Zelda: Oracle of Seasons and Oracle of Ages">
       <i>
        Oracle of Ages
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Oracle of Seasons
       </i>
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the hero
      <a href="/wiki/Link_(The_Legend_of_Zelda)" title="Link (The Legend of Zelda)">
       Link
      </a>
      travels abroad to train for further threats. A storm destroys his boat at sea, and he washes ashore on Koholint Island,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where he is taken to the house of Tarin and his daughter Marin. She is fascinated by Link and the outside world, and tells Link wistfully that, if she were a seagull, she would leave and travel across the sea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After Link recovers his sword, a mysterious owl tells him that he must wake the Wind Fish, Koholint's guardian, in order to return home. The Wind Fish lies dreaming in a giant egg on top of Mt. Tamaranch, and can only be awakened by the eight instruments of the Sirens.
     </p>
     <p>
      During his search for the sixth instrument, Link goes to the Ancient Ruins. There he finds a mural that details the reality of island, that it is merely a dream world created by the Wind Fish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After this revelation, the owl tells Link that only the Wind Fish is aware of this fact. Throughout Koholint Island, nightmare creatures attempt to obstruct Link's quest for the instruments, as they wish to rule the Wind Fish's dreamworld.
     </p>
     <p>
      After collecting all eight instruments from the eight dungeons across Koholint, Link climbs to the top of Mt. Tamaranch and plays the Ballad of the Wind Fish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manual_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-manual-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This breaks open the egg in which the Wind Fish sleeps; Link enters and confronts the last evil being, a Nightmare that takes the form of
      <a href="/wiki/Ganon" title="Ganon">
       Ganon
      </a>
      and other enemies from Link's past.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its final transformation is "DethI", a cyclopean, dual-tentacled Shadow.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After he defeats DethI, the Wind Fish explains that Koholint is all Link's dream. When Link plays the Ballad of the Wind Fish again, he and the Wind Fish awaken; Koholint Island and all its inhabitants slowly disappear.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Link finds himself lying on driftwood in the middle of the ocean, with the Wind Fish flying overhead. If the player did not lose any lives during the game, Marin is shown flying after the ending credits finish
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faq_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-faq-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      - she is shown in the form of a winged woman when played in the original black and white format, while she takes the form of a seagull if played with color in the DX version.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">
       Gameplay
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gameplay">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZeldaLA-DX_comparison.png">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZeldaLA-DX_comparison.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The protagonist, Link, battles an enemy in a castle. The bottom portion of the screen displays
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyrule#Currency" title="Hyrule">
         rupees
        </a>
        , equipped items, and the character's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_(game_mechanic)" title="Health (game mechanic)">
         health
        </a>
        (represented by hearts).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manual_20-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-manual-20">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          20
          <span>
           ]

     <p>
      Like most games in
      <i>
       The Legend of Zelda
      </i>
      series,
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">
       action-adventure game
      </a>
      focused on exploration and combat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_dx_rev_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ign_dx_rev-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The majority of the game takes place from an overhead perspective.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The player traverses the
      <a href="/wiki/Overworld" title="Overworld">
       overworld
      </a>
      of Koholint Island while fighting monsters and exploring underground dungeons. Dungeons steadily become larger and more difficult, and feature "Nightmare"
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boss_(video_games)" title="Boss (video games)">
       boss
      </a>
      characters that the player must defeat, taking different forms in each dungeon, and getting harder to defeat each time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manual_20-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-manual-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Success earns the player heart containers, which increase the amount of damage the
      <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">
       player character
      </a>
      can survive; when all of the player's heart containers have been emptied, the game restarts at the last doorway entered by the character. Defeating a Nightmare also earns the player one of the eight instruments necessary to complete the game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manual_20-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-manual-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      was the first overhead-perspective
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      game to allow Link to jump; this enables
      <a href="/wiki/Side-scrolling_video_game" title="Side-scrolling video game">
       sidescrolling
      </a>
      sequences similar to those in the earlier
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zelda_II:_The_Adventure_of_Link" title="Zelda II: The Adventure of Link">
        Zelda II: The Adventure of Link
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Players can expand their abilities with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Item_(game)" title="Item (game)">
       items
      </a>
      , which are discovered in dungeons and through character interactions. Certain items grant access to previously inaccessible areas, and are needed to enter and complete dungeons. The player may steal items from the game's shop, but doing so changes the player character's name to "THIEF" for the rest of the game and causes the shopkeeper to knock out the character upon re-entry of the shop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the main quest,
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      contains side-missions and diversions. Collectible "secret seashells" are hidden throughout the game; when twenty of these are found, the player can receive a powerful sword that fires energy beams when the player character is at full health, similarly to the sword in the original
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda_(video_game)" title="The Legend of Zelda (video game)">
        The Legend of Zelda
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      is the first
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      game to include a trading sequence
      <a href="/wiki/Minigame" title="Minigame">
       minigame
      </a>
      : the player may give a certain item to a character, who in turn gives the player another item to trade with someone else.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manual_20-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-manual-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also the first game in the
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      series in which the
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy#Features" title="Game Boy">
       A and B buttons
      </a>
      may be assigned to different items, which enables more varied puzzles and item combinations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other series elements originating in
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      include fishing, and learning special songs on an
      <a href="/wiki/Ocarina" title="Ocarina">
       ocarina
      </a>
      ; the latter mechanic is central to the next
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      game released,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Ocarina_of_Time" title="The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time">
        Ocarina of Time
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history13_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-history13-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Link%27s_Awakening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      began as an unsanctioned side project; programmer Kazuaki Morita created a
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      -like game with one of the first
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">
       Game Boy
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Development_kit" title="Development kit">
       development kits
      </a>
      , and used it to experiment with the platform's capabilities. Other staff members of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_Analysis_and_Development" title="Nintendo Entertainment Analysis and Development">
       Nintendo Entertainment Analysis and Development
      </a>
      division joined him
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/after_hours" title="wikt:after hours">
       after-hours
      </a>
      , and worked on the game in what seemed to them like an "afterschool club". The results of these experiments with the Game Boy started to look promising, and following the 1991 release of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Nintendo" title="Super Nintendo">
       Super Nintendo
      </a>
      game
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_A_Link_to_the_Past" title="The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past">
        The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past
       </a>
      </i>
      , director
      <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Tezuka" title="Takashi Tezuka">
       Takashi Tezuka
      </a>
      asked permission to develop a handheld
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      title; he intended it to be a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_porting" title="Video game porting">
       port
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       A Link to the Past
      </i>
      , but it evolved into an original game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwata_asks-afterschool_club_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-iwata_asks-afterschool_club-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The majority of the team that had created the Super Nintendo
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      game was reassembled to advance this new project. Altogether, it took them one and a half years to develop
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laguide_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-laguide-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg">
        <img alt="A long-haired man wearing a coat and a striped shirt." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="979" data-file-width="759" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg/170px-Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg/255px-Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg/340px-Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yoshiaki_Koizumi_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Designer Yoshiaki Koizumi was in charge of the game's story, and conceived major plot points such as the dream world island setting.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Tezuka recalled that the early free-form development of
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      resulted in the game's "unrestrained" contents, such as the unauthorized cameo appearances of characters from the
      <i>
       Mario
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Kirby
      </i>
      series.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       A Link to the Past
      </i>
      script writer
      <a href="/wiki/Kensuke_Tanabe" title="Kensuke Tanabe">
       Kensuke Tanabe
      </a>
      joined the team early on, and came up with the basis of the story.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tezuka sought to make
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      a
      <a href="/wiki/Spin-off_(media)" title="Spin-off (media)">
       spin-off
      </a>
      , and gave Tanabe instructions to omit common series elements such as
      <a href="/wiki/Princess_Zelda" title="Princess Zelda">
       Princess Zelda
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Triforce" title="Triforce">
       Triforce
      </a>
      relic, and the setting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyrule" title="Hyrule">
       Hyrule
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a consequence, Tanabe proposed his game world idea of an island with an egg on top of a mountain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Later on,
      <a href="/wiki/Yoshiaki_Koizumi" title="Yoshiaki Koizumi">
       Yoshiaki Koizumi
      </a>
      , who had previously helped with the plot of
      <i>
       A Link to the Past
      </i>
      , was brought into the team.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laguide_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-laguide-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Koizumi was responsible for the main story of
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      , provided the idea of the island in a dream, and conceived the interactions with the villagers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      was described by series producer
      <a href="/wiki/Eiji_Aonuma" title="Eiji Aonuma">
       Eiji Aonuma
      </a>
      as the first
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      game with a proper plot, which he attributed to Koizumi's romanticism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suspicious_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-suspicious-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tezuka intended the game's world to have a similar feeling to the American television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Twin_Peaks" title="Twin Peaks">
        Twin Peaks
       </a>
      </i>
      , which, like
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      , features characters in a small town.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He suggested that the characters of
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      be written as "suspicious types", akin to those in
      <i>
       Twin Peaks
      </i>
      a theme which carried over into later
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      titles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suspicious_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-suspicious-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tanabe created these "odd" characters; he was placed in charge of the subevents of the story, and wrote almost all of the character dialog, with the exception of the owl's and the Wind Fish's lines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tanabe implemented the previous idea of the world ends when a massive egg breaks on top of a mountain that was originally meant for
      <i>
       A Link to the Past
      </i>
      . Tanabe really wanted to see this idea in a game and was able to implement it in
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      as the basic concept.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Masanao Arimoto and
      <a href="/wiki/Shigefumi_Hino" title="Shigefumi Hino">
       Shigefumi Hino
      </a>
      designed the game's characters, while
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoichi_Kotabe" title="Yoichi Kotabe">
       Yoichi Kotabe
      </a>
      served as illustrator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-credits_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-credits-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Save for the opening and the ending, all pictures in the game were drawn by Arimoto.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Yasahisa Yamamura designed the dungeons, which included the conception of rooms and routes, as well as the placement of enemies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">
       Shigeru Miyamoto
      </a>
      , who served as the producer of
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      , did not provide creative input to the staff members. However, he participated as
      <a href="/wiki/Game_testing" title="Game testing">
       game tester
      </a>
      , and his opinions greatly influenced the latter half of the development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laguide_28-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-laguide-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The music for
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      was composed by Minako Hamano and Kozue Ishikawa, for whom it was their first game project.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laguide_28-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-laguide-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Kazumi_Totaka" title="Kazumi Totaka">
       Kazumi Totaka
      </a>
      was responsible for the sound programming and all sound effects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laguide_28-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-laguide-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As with most
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      games,
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      includes the recurring overworld music;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-second_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-second-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Game Boy arrangement of this theme, titled "Field", was created by Ishikawa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The staff credits theme, "Yume o Miru Shima e" was later arranged for orchestra by
      <a href="/wiki/Yuka_Tsujiyoko" title="Yuka Tsujiyoko">
       Yuka Tsujiyoko
      </a>
      , and performed at the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Orchestral_Game_Music_Concerts" title="Orchestral Game Music Concerts">
        Orchestral Game Music Concert 3
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1993.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Super_Smash_Bros._Brawl" title="Super Smash Bros. Brawl">
        Super Smash Bros. Brawl
       </a>
      </i>
      includes a remix of the game's "Tal Tal Heights" theme.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In an interview about the evolution of the
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      series, Aonuma called
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      the "quintessential
      <a href="/wiki/Isometric_projection" title="Isometric projection">
       isometric
      </a>
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      game".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At another time, he stated that, had the game not come after
      <i>
       A Link to the Past
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda:_Ocarina_of_Time" title="The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time">
        Ocarina of Time
       </a>
      </i>
      would have been very different.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suspicious_32-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-suspicious-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several elements from
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      were re-used in later
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      titles; for example, programmer Morita created a fishing minigame that reappeared in
      <i>
       Ocarina of Time
      </i>
      , among others. Tanabe implemented a trading sequence; Tezuka compared it to the Japanese
      <a href="/wiki/Straw_Millionaire" title="Straw Millionaire">
       Straw Millionaire
      </a>
      folktale, in which someone trades up from a piece of straw to something of greater value. This concept also appeared in most sequels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirbychomp_13-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-kirbychomp-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Releases">
       Releases
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Game_Boy_Printer.png">

       </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Printer" title="Game Boy Printer">
         Game Boy Printer
        </a>
        accessory was compatible with the
        <i>
         DX
        </i>
        release of
        <i>
         Link's Awakening
        </i>
        . Players could print out certain screenshots obtained in the game.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      To support the North American release of
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      , Nintendo sponsored a crosscountry train competition called the Zelda Whistle Stop Tour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whistle_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-whistle-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The event, which lasted three days, allowed select players to test
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      in a timed race to complete the game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The event was meant not only to showcase the game, but also the Game Boy's superior battery life and portability, the latter of which was critical to the accessibility of a portable
      <i>
       Zelda
      </i>
      title.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-retrospective_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-retrospective-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whistle_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-whistle-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The company-owned magazine
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Power" title="Nintendo Power">
        Nintendo Power
       </a>
      </i>
      published a guide to the game's first three areas in its July 1993 issue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nintendopower-guide_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nintendopower-guide-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1998, to promote the launch of the
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">
       Game Boy Color
      </a>
      , Nintendo re-released
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      as
      <i>
       The Legend of Zelda: Link's Awakening DX
      </i>
      . It features fully colorized graphics and is
      <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">
       backward compatible
      </a>
      with the original Game Boy.
      <i>
       Link's Awakening DX
      </i>
      contains a new optional dungeon, with unique enemies and puzzles based on color (due to this - despite the backwards compatibility - the dungeon cannot be accessed on the earlier non-color Game Boy models).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_la_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-history_of_la-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After completing the dungeon, the player may choose to receive either a red or blue tunic, which increase attack and defense, respectively. The
      <i>
       DX
      </i>
      version also allows players to take
      <a href="/wiki/Screenshot" title="Screenshot">
       screenshots
      </a>
      ; after the player visits a camera shop, its owner will appear in certain locations throughout the game. A total of twelve screenshots can be taken, which may be viewed at the shop, or printed with the
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Printer" title="Game Boy Printer">
       Game Boy Printer
      </a>
      accessory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_of_la_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-history_of_la-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirror-review_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mirror-review-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For
      <i>
       Link's Awakening DX
      </i>
      , Tezuka returned as project supervisor, with Yoshinori Tsuchiyama as the new director.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nobuo Matsumiya collaborated with Tsuchiyama on applying changes to the original script; for example, hint messages were added to the boss battles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For the new dungeon, Yuichi Ozaki created a musical piece based on Kondo's dungeon theme from the
      <a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda_(video_game)" title="The Legend of Zelda (video game)">
       original
       <i>
        The Legend of Zelda
       </i>
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ladxguide_29-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-ladxguide-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2010, Nintendo announced that the
      <i>
       DX
      </i>
      version would be re-released on the
      <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Console" title="Virtual Console">
       Virtual Console
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_3DS" title="Nintendo 3DS">
       Nintendo 3DS
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scullion.2C_Chris_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Scullion.2C_Chris-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and became available June 2011. In July 2013,
      <i>
       Link's Awakening DX
      </i>
      was offered as one of several Virtual Console games which "elite status" members of the North America
      <a href="/wiki/Club_Nintendo" title="Club Nintendo">
       Club Nintendo
      </a>
      could redeem as a free gift.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clubnin_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-clubnin-45">

        45
        <span>

     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      was well received by critics, and holds an average score of 90% on aggregate site
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_Rankings" title="Game Rankings">
       Game Rankings
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameRankings_49-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GameRankings-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a retrospective article,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Gaming_Monthly" title="Electronic Gaming Monthly">
        Electronic Gaming Monthly
       </a>
      </i>
      writer Jeremy Parish called
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      the "best Game Boy game ever, an adventure so engrossing and epic that we can even forgive the whole thing for being one of those 'It's all a dream!' fakeouts".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">
        Game Informer
       </a>
      </i>
      's Ben Reeves called it the third best Game Boy game and called it influential.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">
        The Washington Post
       </a>
      </i>
      's Chip Carter declared that Nintendo had created a "legend that fits in the palm of your hand", and praised its portability and depth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpost_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-washpost-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An Jkiri of ITMedia echoed similar comments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A writer for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mainichi_Shimbun" title="Mainichi Shimbun">
        Mainichi Shimbun
       </a>
      </i>
      enjoyed the game's music and story.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Multiple sources touted it as an excellent portable adventure for those without the time for more sophisticated games.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Complaints about the game included its monochrome graphics; certain critics believed that they made it difficult to discern the screen's contents,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottowacitizen-review_62-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ottowacitizen-review-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and wished that the game was in color. Critic William Burrill dismissed the game's visuals as "Dim Boy graphics [that are] nothing to write home about".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both Carter and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ottawa_Citizen" title="The Ottawa Citizen">
        The Ottawa Citizen
       </a>
      </i>
      's Bill Provick found the two-button control scheme awkward, as they needed to switch items on almost every screen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpost_57-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-washpost-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottowacitizen-review_62-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ottowacitizen-review-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Vancouver_Sun" title="The Vancouver Sun">
        The Vancouver Sun
       </a>
      </i>
      's Katherine Monk called the dialogue "stilted", but considered the rest of the game to be "ever-surprising".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening DX
      </i>
      also received positive reviews; based on ten media outlets, it holds an average score of 92% on Game Rankings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GameRankingsDX_50-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GameRankingsDX-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">
       IGN
      </a>
      's Adam Cleveland awarded the game a perfect score, and noted that "throughout the color-enhanced version of
      <i>
       Zelda DX
      </i>
      , it can easily be inferred that Nintendo has reworked its magic to fit new standards", by adding new content while keeping the original game intact.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ign_dx_rev_26-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ign_dx_rev-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cameron Davis of
      <a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">
       GameSpot
      </a>
      applauded the game's camera support and attention to detail in coloration and style,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamespot-review_47-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gamespot-review-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while reviewers for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Courier-Mail" title="The Courier-Mail">
        Courier Mail
       </a>
      </i>
      believed that the camera added gameplay depth and allowed players to show off trophies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       The Daily Telegraph
      </i>
      's Samantha Amjadali wrote that the addition of color made the game easier by reducing deaths caused by indistinct graphics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_telegraph-review_66-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-daily_telegraph-review-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Total Games
      </i>
      noted that the new content added little to the game, but found it addictive to play nonetheless.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      sold well, and helped boost Game Boy sales 13 percent in 1993making it one of Nintendo's most profitable years in North America up to that time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The game remained on bestseller lists for more than 90 months after release,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and went on to sell 3.83million units by 2004. The
      <i>
       DX
      </i>
      version sold another 2.22million units.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeldasales_70-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zeldasales-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The game won several awards, including those in the Game Boy categories for Graphics and Sound, Challenge, Theme and Fun, Play Control, and Best Overall in the reader-chosen 1993
      <i>
       Nintendo Power
      </i>
      Awards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP-60_48-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NP-60-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was awarded Best Game Boy Game of 1993 by
      <i>
       Electronic Gaming Monthly
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egmbuyersguide1994_54-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-egmbuyersguide1994-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Nintendo Power
      </i>
      later named it the fifty-sixth best Nintendo game,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_Top_200_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NP_Top_200-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and, in August 2008, listed the
      <i>
       DX
      </i>
      version as the second best
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">
       Game Boy
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">
       Game Boy Color
      </a>
      game.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      IGN's readers ranked it as the 40th best game of all time, while the staff placed it at 78th;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGN40_52-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IGN40-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_53-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-top-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the staff believed that, "while handheld spin-offs are generally considered the low point for game franchises,
      <i>
       Link's Awakening
      </i>
      proves that they can offer just as rich a gameplay experience as their console counterparts".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_53-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-top-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The game took 42nd place on the
      <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">
       Guinness World Records
      </a>
      ' 2009 list of the top 50 most important and influential video games of all time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
