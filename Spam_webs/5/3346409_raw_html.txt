        <h4 itemprop="name">
         Pyne the Fixer!
        </h4>
        <p itemprop="description">
         Can he fix it?  He says he can. By Rocco Fazzari and Denis Carnahan with apologies to Bob The Builder.
        </p>
        <span class="gone" itemprop="duration">
         PT1M7S
        </span>
        <span class="gone" itemprop="embedUrl">

      <!-- id:video-player-content -->
      <p>
       Comedy can be tough, especially if it's not your natural game. Just ask Prime Minister Tony Abbott, who this week was slammed from all sides for a seemingly harmless - though rather puzzling -
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=T2vFa9mN3WQ" target="_blank">
        video address celebrating St Patrick's Day
       </a>
       . First Abbott was criticised for being "patronising" in his delivery, then
       <a href="http://www.theage.com.au/federal-politics/political-news/tony-abbotts-st-patricks-day-message-causes-offence-in-ireland-20150317-1m10h4.html" target="_blank">
        Irish Prime Minister Enda Kenny took umbrage
       </a>
       at the suggestion that the Irish like a Guinness or two, which he viewed as a dim trade in national stereotypes. Tough room.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you haven't seen the address yet, this line - said with Abbott's typically stilted delivery - sums it up: "It's safe to say that this is the one day of the year when it's good to be green." Geddit? If this were an episode of
       <i>
        The Simpsons
       </i>
       , you'd then hear a cricket chirping in the otherwise-silent background, or a "you suck!" from the crowd.
      </p>
      <p>
       It wasn't exactly champagne comedy, but it was another reminder of the absence of wit, or even good humour, in politics these days. Few politicians dare try it, and most of the humour emerging from Canberra is either lame (Bill Shorten's "zingers" have been very cleverly pilloried by Shaun Micallef on
       <i>
        Mad as Hell
       </i>
       ) or unintentional (see
       <a href="http://www.theage.com.au/federal-politics/political-news/i-want-it-to-be-a-surprise-for-you-christopher-pyne-smiles-through-grilling-from-david-speers-20150317-1m0p91.html" target="_blank">
        Christopher Pyne's interview with Sky News' David Speers
       </a>
       this week).
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Criticism of Prime Minister Tony Abbott for merely trying to be funny about the Irish revealed  one reason why humour is absent from contemporary politics." src="/content/dam/images/1/m/2/p/t/g/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.1m2nwd.png/1426910998365.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Criticism of Prime Minister Tony Abbott for merely trying to be funny about the Irish revealed  one reason why humour is absent from contemporary politics.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Andrew Meares
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       Humour and politics used to get on a lot better. Gough Whitlam was famous for his rapier wit, and Paul Keating was so sharp-tongued that there is a book devoted to his witticisms, mostly used to skewer the opposition.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       Joking is a risky business in modern public life because people are so quick to take offence and so thunderous with it - politicians steer clear of self-deprecation for fear of giving opponents even the slightest ammunition. Some also no doubt worry that people simply won't "get" the joke, and they'll need to devote time to an awkward explanation and clean-up.
      </p>
      <p>
       But what turns off a lot of Australians from seriously engaging in politics is the tone in which it is conducted: the constant sniping, the opposition to everything, the propensity to talk in T-shirt slogans or like automatons. The utter humourlessness of it all.
      </p>
      <p>
       I'm not for one moment suggesting that politicians start doing sketch comedy or Seinfeldesque observational gags ("what is WITH the Senate? What's that about?"), because governing the country is ostensibly a serious matter, and no one wants it to be done by clowns. But occasional levity, and especially good humour in the broadest sense, does not undermine credibility, it enhances it.
      </p>
      <p>
       US President Barack Obama uses humour very effectively, and is blessed with enough charisma to carry it off. The annual White House Correspondents' Dinner is a chance for the President to make political gags, some at his own expense, and in doing so he humanises himself, acknowledges some of his foibles and, importantly, lets people in. Humour has a warming effect.
      </p>
      <p>
       Kevin Rudd - not one of history's great wits - made the smart choice to appear on
       <i>
        Rove Live
       </i>
       several times as opposition leader and as prime minister. Even though he was as stiff as a board and about as funny, it was the tone of the show, and his willingness to play along, that worked in his favour.
      </p>
      <p>
       Of course, humour has to be coupled with good judgment, or it can go horribly awry. One of the most gasp-worthy examples of this in our recent political history was former Liberal leader Alexander Downer's unfathomably bad "joke" that his party's domestic violence policy could be called "the things that batter". There are some issues that politicians should never make light of.
      </p>
      <p>
       Wit and humour are part of having charisma, or luring people in, enticing them to listen to what you are saying. Certainly, it was a big part of Whitlam's aura and of Keating's too - such is the latter's reputation that his every word is still hung on to, waiting for the quotable to make its inevitable appearance.
      </p>
      <p>
       Good humour and credibility are not mutually exclusive. Yes, humour is risky - and not for everyone - but its potential power is great. Amid modern politics' constant sniping, accusations and "gotchas", voters may well relish the chance, every now and then, to laugh with politicians instead of just at them.
      </p>
