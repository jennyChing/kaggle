   //set primary image container to same width as primary image
	function primaryImageSizer() {
		var primaryImageContainer = $("div.primaryImage");
		var primaryImage = $("div.primaryImage img");
		var imageWidth = primaryImage.width();

		if (primaryImage.length &gt; 0) {
			imageWidth = primaryImage.width();

				primaryImage.css("width","300px");

   //set top padding of the workspace to the height of the ribbon
function setTopPadding() {
	   
	var wrkElem = document.getElementById('s4-workspace');
	var ribHeight = document.getElementById('topfixedcontentarea').offsetHeight;

	if (window.location.search.match("[?&amp;]IsDlg=1")) {
		//margin works better for dialogs b/c of scrollbars

	    wrkElem.style.paddingTop = '0px';

	   else {
	     //padding works better for the main window
	     wrkElem.style.paddingTop = ribHeight + 'px';
	   }
	}
	 
	// bind top padding reset to ribbon resize event so that the page always lays out correctly.
	ExecuteOrDelayUntilScriptLoaded(function () { SP.UI.Workspace.add_resized(setTopPadding); }, "init.js");

 });

              <h1>
               <span class="prettify headline2010test">
                Discovery of stone monument at El Per-Waka adds new chapter to ancient Maya history
                <h1>
                </h1>
               </span>
              </h1>
             </div>
             <div class="optionalsubHeader prettify">
              New World 'Cleopatra story' waits 1,000 years to be retold
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div class="articleDate">
              July 16, 2013
             </div>
             <div class="prettify">
             </div>

             <div class="PrimaryAudio">
              <div id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ctl02_label" style="display:none">

              </div>
              <div aria-labelledby="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ctl02_label" class="ms-rtestate-field" id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ctl02__ControlWrapper_RichHtmlField" style="display:inline">

             </div>
             <div class="primaryImage">
              <div class="primaryImagephotoCredit prettify">
              </div>
              <div class="photocaption prettify" style="background-color:#cdcdcd; margin-top: -5px;">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="prettify">
              <div id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ArticleBody2010Display_label" style="display:none">
               Article Body 2010
              </div>
              <div aria-labelledby="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ArticleBody2010Display_label" class="ms-rtestate-field" id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ArticleBody2010Display__ControlWrapper_RichHtmlField" style="display:inline">
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivcenter" style="width:475px;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto">
                <div style="width:475px;height:227px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/Chak%20Took%20Ichaak%201_standalone.jpg" style="width:475px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Photo by Francico Castaneda; courtesy of Proyecto Arqueolgico El Per-Wakay PACUNAM.
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Stone-carved representation of Maya King Chak Took Ich'aak (Red Spark Claw) who died in 556 AD.
                 <a href="/news/PublishingImages/Chak%20Took%20Ichaak%201_hi-res.jpg" target="_blank">
                  Download high-res &gt;&gt;
                 </a>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
                Archaeologists tunneling beneath the main temple of the ancient Maya city of El Per-Waka in northern Guatemala have discovered an intricately carved stone monument with hieroglyphic text detailing the exploits of a little-known sixth-century princess whose progeny prevailed in a bloody, back-and-forth struggle between two of the civilizations most powerful royal dynasties, Guatemalan cultural officials
                <a href="http://www.mcd.gob.gt/patrimonio/patrimonio-descubren-estela-en-el-peru-waka-noroeste-de-peten" target="_blank">
                 announced
                </a>
                July 16.
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivright" style="width:150px">
                <div style="width:150px;height:150px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/FreidelDavid_rollup-2.jpg" style="width:150px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Freidel
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Great rulers took pleasure in describing adversity as a prelude to ultimate success, said research director David Freidel, PhD, a professor of anthropology in Arts &amp; Sciences at Washington University in St. Louis. Here the Snake queen, Lady Ikoom, prevailed in the end.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Freidel, who is studying in Paris this summer, said the stone monument, known officially as El Per Stela 44, offers a wealth of new information about a dark period in Maya history, including the names of two previously unknown Maya rulers and the political realities that shaped their legacies.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                The narrative of Stela 44 is full of twists and turns of the kind that are usually found in time of war but rarely detected in Precolumbian archaeology, Freidel said.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivright" style="width:200px">
                <div style="width:200px;height:310px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/estela%2044%20foto%20jcpc_secondary.jpg" style="width:200px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Photo by Juan Carlos Prez; courtesy of Proyecto Arqueolgico El Per-Wakay PACUNAM.
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Maya Stela 44 photographed in the tunnel below a Maya temple where it was discovered March 5, 2013.
                 <br/>
                 <a href="/news/PublishingImages/estela%2044%20foto%20jcpc_hi-res.JPG">
                  Download high-res &gt;&gt;
                 </a>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
                The information in the text provides a new chapter in the history of the ancient kingdom of Waka and its political relations with the most powerful kingdoms in the Classic period lowland Maya world.
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Carved stone monuments, such as Stela 44, have been unearthed in dozens of other important Maya ruins and each has made a critical contribution to the understanding of Maya culture.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Freidel says that his epigrapher, Stanley Guenter, who deciphered the text,  believes that Stela 44 was originally dedicated about 1,450 years ago, in the calendar period ending in A.D. 564, by the Wak dynasty King Waoom Uchab Tzikin, a title that translates roughly as He Who Stands Up the Offering of the Eagle.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                After standing exposed to the elements for more than 100 years, Stela 44 was moved by order of a later king and buried as an offering inside new construction that took place at the main El Per-Waka temple about A.D. 700, probably as part of funeral rituals for a great queen entombed in the building at this time, the research team suggests.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                El Per-Waka is about 40 miles west of the famous Maya site of Tikal near the San Pedro Martir River in Laguna del Tigre National Park. In the Classic period, this royal city commanded major trade routes running north to south and east to west.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivcenter" style="width:470px;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto">
                <div style="width:470px;height:700px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/Map-of-the-Maya-World-Eppich.jpg" style="width:470px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Map courtesy of Keith Eppich
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Map of the Maya world
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Freidel has directed research at this site in collaboration with Guatemalan and foreign archaeologists since 2003.  At present,
                <a href="http://www.linkedin.com/pub/juan-carlos-perez-calderon/a/a16/92b" target="_blank">
                 Lic. Juan Carlos Prez Calderon
                </a>
                is co-director of the project and
                <a href="http://www.wooster.edu/academics/areas/socio-anthro/faculty-staff/navarro-farr" target="_blank">
                 Olivia Navarro Farr
                </a>
                , an assistant professor at the College of Wooster in Ohio, is co-principal investigator and long-term supervisor of work in the temple, known as Structure M13-1. Gautemalan archaeologist
                <a href="https://www.facebook.com/griselda.perezrobles.5" target="_blank">
                 Griselda Perez
                </a>
                discovered Stela 44 in this temple.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                The project carries out research under the auspices of the Ministry of Culture and Sports of Guatemala and its Directorate for Cultural and Natural Patrimony, the Council for Protected Areas, and it is sponsored by the Foundation for the Cultural and Natural Patrimony (PACUNAM) and the U.S. Department of the Interior.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Early in March, Prez was excavating a short tunnel along the centerline of the stairway of the temple in order to give access to other tunnels leading to
                <a href="/news/Pages/24167.aspx" target="_blank">
                 a royal tomb discovered in 2012
                </a>
                when her excavators encountered Stela 44.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivright" style="width:300px">
                <div style="width:300px;height:200px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/GriseldaPerez300x200.jpg" style="width:300px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Photo courtesy of David Freidel/WUSTL
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Guatemalan archaeologist Griselda Perez
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Once the texts along the side of the monument were cleared, archaeologist Francisco Castaneda took detailed photographs and sent these to Guenter for decipherment.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Guenters glyph analysis suggests that Stela 44 was commissioned by Wak dynasty King Waoom Uchab Tzikin to honor his father, King Chak Took Ichaak (Red Spark Claw), who had died in A.D. 556. Stela 44s description of this royal father-son duo marks the first time their names have been known to modern history.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                A new queen, Lady Ikoom, also is featured in the text and she was important to the king who recovered this worn stela and used it again.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivcenter" style="width:470px;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto">
                <div style="width:470px;height:310px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/LadyIkoomStela44web470x310.jpg" style="width:470px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Photo by Francico Castaneda; courtesy of Proyecto Arqueolgico El Per-Wakay PACUNAM.
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Maya Snake queen Lady Ikoom as depicted on Stela 44.
                 <a href="/news/PublishingImages/LadyIkoomStela44.jpg" target="_blank">
                  Download high-res &gt;&gt;
                 </a>
                 <br/>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
                Researchers believe that Lady Ikoom was one of two Snake dynasty princesses sent into arranged marriages with the rulers of El Per-Waka and another nearby Maya city as a means of cementing Snake control over this region of northern Guatemala.
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Lady Ikoom was a predecessor to one of the greatest queens of Classic Maya civilization, the seventh-century Maya Holy Snake Lord known as Lady Kabel who ruled El Per-Waka for more than 20 years with her husband, King Kinich Bahlam II. She was the military governor of the Wak kingdom for her family, the imperial house of the Snake King, and she carried the title Kaloomte, translated as Supreme Warrior, higher in authority than her husband, the king.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Around A.D. 700,  Stela 44 was brought to the main city temple by command of King Kinich Bahlam II to be buried as an offering, probably as part of the funeral rituals for his wife, queen Kaloomte Kabel.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Last year, the project discovered fragments of another stela built into the final terrace walls of the city temple, Stela 43, dedicated by this king in A.D. 702. Lady Ikoom is given pride of place on the front of that monument celebrating an event in 574. She was likely an ancestor of the king.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivright" style="width:300px">
                <div style="width:300px;height:267px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/FreidelStela43_primary.jpg" style="width:300px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Photo courtesy of David Freidel/WUSTL
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 WUSTL archaeologist David Freidel works to uncover Stela 43, which includes one of the first known references to the Maya Snake queen Lady Ikoom.
                 <a href="/news/PublishingImages/FreidelStela43_hi-res.jpg" target="_blank">
                  Download high-res &gt;&gt;
                 </a>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Freidel and colleagues
                <a href="/news/Pages/24167.aspx" target="_blank">
                 discovered Lady Kabels tomb
                </a>
                at the temple in 2012. Located near Kabels tomb, Stela 44 was set in a cut through the plaster floor of the plaza in front of the old temple and then buried underneath the treads of the stairway of the new temple.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Stela 44 originally was raised in a period when no stelae were erected at Tikal, a period of more than a century called
                <em>
                 The Hiatus
                </em>
                from A.D. 557 until 692. This was a turbulent era in Maya history during which there were many wars and conquests. Tikals hiatus started when it was defeated in battle by King Yajawte Kinich of Caracol in Belize, probably under the auspices of the Snake King Sky Witness. The kingdom of Waka also experienced a hiatus that was likely associated with changing political fortunes but one of briefer duration from A.D. 554 to 657. That period is now shortened by the discovery of Stela 44.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                The front of the stela is much eroded, no doubt from more than a century of exposure, but it features a king standing face forward cradling a sacred bundle in his arms. There are two other stelae at the site with this pose, Stela 23 dated to 524 and Stela 22 dated to 554, and they were probably raised by King Chak Took Ichaak. The name Chak Took Ichaak is that of two powerful kings of Tikal and it is likely that this king of Waka was named after them and that his dynasty was a Tikal vassal at the time he came to the throne, the research team suggests.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                The text describes the accession of the son of Chak Took Ichaak, Waoom Uchab Tzikin, in A.D. 556 as witnessed by a royal woman Lady Ikoom, who was probably his mother. She carries the titles Sak Wayis, White Spirit, and Kuhul Chatan Winik, Holy Chatan Person. These titles are strongly associated with the powerful Snake or Kan kings who commanded territories to the north of El Per-Waka, which makes it very likely that Lady Ikoom was a Snake princess, Guenter argues.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivcenter" style="width:470px;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto">
                <div style="width:470px;height:710px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/Lady-Ikoom-Stela-43web470x710.jpg" style="width:470px"/>
                </div>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Photo by Francico Castaneda; courtesy of Proyecto Arqueolgico El Per-Wakay PACUNAM.
                </p>
                <p class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Maya Snake queen Lady Ikoom as represented on Stela 43.
                 <br/>
                 <a href="/news/PublishingImages/Lady%20Ikoom%20Stela%2043.jpg" target="_blank">
                  Download high-res &gt;&gt;
                 </a>
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
                We infer that sometime in the course of his reign King Chak Took Ichaak changed sides and became a Snake dynasty vassal, Freidel said. But then, when he died and his son and heir came to power, he did so under the auspices of a foreign king, which Guenter argues from details is the reigning king of Tikal. So Tikal had reasserted command of Waka and somehow Queen Ikoom survived this imposition.
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Then in a dramatic shift in the tides of war that same Tikal King, Wak Chan Kawiil, was defeated and sacrificed by the Snake king in A.D. 562. Finally, two years after that major reversal, the new king and his mother raised Stela 44, giving the whole story as outlined above.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Stela 44s tales of political intrigue and bloodshed are just a few of the many dramatic stories of Classic Maya history that have been recovered through the decipherment of Maya glyphs, a science that has made great strides in the last 30 years, Freidel said.
               </p>
               <div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Freidel and his project staff will continue to study Stela 44 for more clues about the nuances of Maya history. While the text on Stela 44 is only partially preserved, it clearly reveals an important moment in the history of Waka, he concludes.
               </p>
               <div style="text-align:center">
                # # #
               </div>

               </p>
