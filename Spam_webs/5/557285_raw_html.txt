            <div class="articlehead">
             <header>
              <span>
              </span>
              <h1>
               How to Help Someone Having an Asthma Attack
              </h1>
             </header>
             <aside class="disclaimer">
             </aside>
             <h2>
              Would you know what to do with a person having trouble breathing? Armed with a little knowledge about asthma attacks, you may be able to help save a life.
             </h2>
             <div class="bottom">
              <div class="byline">
               By Robin Seaton Jefferson
              </div>
              <div class="reviewedby">
               Medically reviewed by
               <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/medical-reviewers.aspx" rel="nofollow" target="_new">
                Pat F. Bass III MD, MPH
               </a>
               and
               <a href="http://www.everydayhealth.com/medical-reviewers.aspx" rel="nofollow" target="_new">
                Lindsey Marcellin, MD, MPH
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="clear">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div id="video-player-top">
            </div>
            <aside class="aside">
            </aside>
            <p>
             Imagine you offered to take your nephew to the movies. When you arrive to pick him up, you find him in bed, gasping for breath. You know he has
             <a href="/health-report/asthma-management-guide.aspx">
              asthma
             </a>
             and allergies, but youve never actually seen him in the middle of an asthma attack. You feel your own throat swelling up from fear.
            </p>
            <p>
             What do you do?
            </p>
            <p>
             Rather than panic when a relative, friend, or stranger has an asthma attack in front of you, you can keep a cool head if you know the right steps to take. It all starts with a plan.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Understanding an Asthma Attack
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             First, you need to know how to
             <a href="/health-report/asthma-management/asthma-attack-anatomy.aspx">
              recognize an asthma attack
             </a>
             . Impaired breathing is the clearest sign. Its caused by inflammation in the airways. The inflammation in turn stimulates tightening of the muscles around the airways. An asthma attack can cause coughing spells, wheezing, tightening of the chest, or an inability to move air out of the chest.
            </p>
            <p>
             Many people mistakenly think asthma attacks always go hand-in-hand with wheezing. This is not always the case, says Ernesto Ruiz-Huidobro, MD, an allergist with Allergy and Asthma Healthcare in Kirkwood and Lake St. Louis, Missouri. To wheeze, you have to move air, so if they are really obstructed, they may not be moving air, and you may not hear wheezing. Asthma is extremely variable. We always look at how it affects the individual.
            </p>
            <p>
             Theres no statistical time frame for how long an asthma attack lasts. Some are milder ones you can manage at home and others require treatment at the hospital.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              What to Do During an Asthma Attack
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Advances in asthma treatment have minimized asthma attacks. The latest standard of care and goals for asthma treatment are complete control of symptoms and prevention of asthma attacks, Dr. Ruiz-Huidobro says. We aim for total control of symptoms, and our goal is to allow patients to have a normal life.
            </p>
            <p>
             But, of course, asthma attacks still happen. And they can happen to someone whose asthma plan you know nothing about. The person may not even have a plan yet.
            </p>
            <p>
             Use these tips to offer asthma attack help:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Stay calm.
              </strong>
              Be a calming presence. Ruiz-Huidobro says your first responsibility when dealing with someone having an asthma attack is to help them
              <a href="/health-report/asthma-management-guide/asthma-and-stress.aspx">
               stay calm
              </a>
               reassure them youre there to help. Symptoms of panic include difficulty breathing, so you dont want to worsen an already stressful event by seeming panicked yourself. Make sure the person is sitting up so his or her breathing remains as unobstructed as possible.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Eliminate the trigger.
              </strong>
              If you know about the persons history of asthma attacks, rely on what you know, such as their asthma triggers, Ruiz-Huidobro says. Assess the situation to get them away from the trigger or remove the trigger, if possible. For example if youre near people who are smoking, move away from them. The list of potential asthma triggers is lengthy  pollen, dust mites, mold, feathers, animal dander, certain foods, smoke, dirt, gases, illness, exercise, stress, cold or windy weather, and even acetaminophen  so if you dont know the persons triggers, see if they can tell you what they are so you can better assess the situation.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Emergency plan.
              </strong>
              If you dont already know it, try to determine if there is an
              <a href="/health-report/asthma-management/asthma-action-plan.aspx">
               emergency plan
              </a>
              , which may include rescue inhalers (albuterol, in most cases), corticosteroids, and other anti-inflammatory agents. Read the label to determine the appropriate dose. Make sure the person follows through with the instructions. Then ask about his or her action plan for worsening symptoms  whether its the use of rescue inhalers, breathing treatments, or to call an ambulance at the first signs of chest tightening.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Know the signs of a severe attack.
              </strong>
              Look for any signs that this is a severe attack, one that warrants a trip to the emergency room or at least a call to a physician. Signs include skin that looks sucked in between the ribs and on the neck, a bluish discoloration of the lips, and continue struggling for breath several minutes after using the rescue inhaler.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             Finally, recognize when your actions arent enough. If the steps in the emergency plan arent working, or if there is no plan, consider going to an urgent care center or calling an ambulance immediately. The sooner, the better, Ruiz-Huidobro says. You have no way of treating it, and asthma attacks usually dont just go away on their own.
            </p>
