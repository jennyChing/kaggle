          By
          <a href="/authors/bunny-ware">
           Bunny Ware
          </a>

         <div class="hnews hentry item">
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            Hoofs for Healing Equestrian Services held its grand opening celebration at 1514B Old Dean Forest Road in Garden City on Jan. 18.
           </p>
           <p>
            Following the history of this property I learn that is was once home to an old rice plantation in the 1700s, a dairy farm for the
            <strong>
             Constantine
            </strong>
            family, the Savannah-Chatham police departments Mounted Patrol, and then the Savannah College of Art and Designs Equestrian Team Center.
           </p>
           <p>
            The propertys story picks back up when
            <strong>
             Karrie Griffin Henry
            </strong>
            left Horsin Around after 10 years and leased the 45 acres so she could follow her childhood vision of utilizing horses to help special needs individuals.
           </p>
           <p>
            After parking and walking down the dirt road entrance, the first person I see when I arrive at Karries riding oasis is volunteer
            <strong>
             Hunter Cole
            </strong>
            and his four-legged female friend,
            <strong>
             Miss Montana
            </strong>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            Born in captivity because her mom and dad were captured in roundup in Oregon, Miss Montana is a Kiger Mustang and is one of the therapy horses here, shares a very happy Hunter.
           </p>
           <p>
            Hanging out with Hunter is fellow Savannah native
            <strong>
             Raymond Sanderson
            </strong>
            , a handyman who helped Karrie create the center and works here daily. With the two men trying to get Miss Montana into a model moment, I chat with concession volunteers
            <strong>
             Katie
            </strong>
            and
            <strong>
             Amanda Drought
            </strong>
            as they get the spaghetti, hot chocolate and hot dogs ready for the gathering crowd.
           </p>
           <p>
            Finding a warm spot in the Art Cottage, I pause Armstrong Atlantic State University Tri Sigma beauties
            <strong>
             Rebecca Hefner
            </strong>
            and
            <strong>
             Rachel Riddick
            </strong>
            at their craft tables to pose with the centers arts &amp; crafts instructor,
            <strong>
             Becky Marcussen
            </strong>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            While the ladies are busy helping tiny tots draw, color and fill colored sand bottles, I head out to meet a few of the local artist who have set up shop in the warm sunlight. Meet
            <strong>
             Joseph Ebberwein
            </strong>
            , a super-talented Lowcountry landscape artist and part owner of Saints and Shamrocks.
           </p>
           <p>
            After Joseph shakes my hand, I grab his hands back and ask him to continue to warm my hands up. Holding a strangers hand doesnt seem so odd when he tells me that we have met before and it gets even better when his good friend and fellow artist
            <strong>
             Ann Lutz
            </strong>
            , says, Bunny, you know my mother-in-law
            <strong>
             Barbara Lutz
            </strong>
            from Dr. Bob Grants office.
           </p>
           <p>
            OK. I know who I am talking to now! Anns art logo of Thanx for adding a splash of my color to your world could also describe her stylish bohemian attire and her vivacious personality.
           </p>
           <p>
            Ann introduces me to her close friend
            <strong>
             Denise Fischer
            </strong>
            , a gorgeous child provider who answers all my questions about Ann and her family before Ann does! The ladies tell me they are here because Anns daughter
            <strong>
             Rachel
            </strong>
            has high functioning autism and like a wild horse, we dont try to tame her when she explodes with happiness out here.
           </p>
           <p>
            About that time Rachel runs toward the pet station where a crowd has gathered to hug on Norwood Stables chickens, goats and miniature horses, or is it a pony when they are super tiny? Either way, beautiful
            <strong>
             Kira King
            </strong>
            has found her true love  its a big goat named Kit Kat who loves having his picture taken.
           </p>
           <p>
            Another person busy snapping pictures is former Benedictine ninth-grade science teacher
            <strong>
             Coach Michael Sevier
            </strong>
            . Tall, quick and chatty, Coach tells me that he has volunteered to photograph events here and asks me about attending ABATEs Poker Run this Sunday.
           </p>
           <p>
            As a true Southerner, I rest on the Sabbath but promise to send the word out to all the media in town before heading over to meet United Celebral Palsy of Georgias
            <strong>
             Wakfwen Danpome
            </strong>
            . With perfect diction and lilting accent, I pose Wakfwen with a very enthusiastic
            <strong>
             Charles Bostic
            </strong>
            and
            <strong>
             Donald McReady
            </strong>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            Speaking of posing, someone very familiar to the camera is here!
            <strong>
             Kevin Sheehan
            </strong>
            from AMBUCS is smiling for the multiple cameras that are flashing as I make my way over to pose for a picture with
            <strong>
             Darlene Dumont
            </strong>
            , a dynamic Southern lady representing Angela Beasleys Puppet People.
           </p>
           <p>
            Petting a few of the horses in the well-maintained barn, I glance over and see two Garden City Fire Department trucks arriving while the Magic Carpet Dance Troupe begins performing a lively dance for the still-arriving crowd.
           </p>
           <p>
            While pausing to watch the dancers, I chat with Pepsi plant manager
            <strong>
             Mark Begley
            </strong>
            and fellow Pepsi peeps
            <strong>
             Travis Pryor
            </strong>
            and
            <strong>
             Darnell Chisholm
            </strong>
            . Mark fills me in local projects like Wounded Warriors and the Hockey Classic that he and his team are working on or sponsoring.
           </p>
           <p>
            By now the center is brimming with kids,
            <strong>
             Sparky
            </strong>
            the Firefighting Mascot has arrived and I have gotten a chance to compliment Karrie on her massively successful grand opening.
           </p>
           <p>
            With H4H providing free riding services for disabled persons, veterans and active military, Ive got a feeling that the personality, power and movement of the horse will create a mental and physical bond with the individual that no other therapy can provide. Well said
            <strong>
             Karrie Henry
            </strong>
            and congratulations on fulfilling your childhood dream of helping others!
           </p>
           <p>
            PS. Karrie needs volunteers. Give her a shout at 912-398-7802 or check out her website at hoofs4healing.com.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Invite Bunny to your gathering, gala or other social event. Contact Bunny at 912-844-1122 or email her at
            <a href="mailto:bunnyware@aol.com">
             bunnyware@aol.com
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
