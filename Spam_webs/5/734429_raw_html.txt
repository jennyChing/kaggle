     <p>
      <b>
       le-de-France
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       French pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">
        [ildfs]
       </a>
      </span>
      <small class="nowrap metadata">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/File:Fr-Paris--%C3%8Ele-de-France.ogg" title="File:Fr-Paris--le-de-France.ogg">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/>
       </a>
       <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Fr-Paris--%C3%8Ele-de-France.ogg" title="Fr-Paris--le-de-France.ogg">
        listen
       </a>
       )
      </small>
      ) (literally "Island of France";
      <a href="#Etymology">
       see the Etymology section
      </a>
      ), also known as the
      <i>
       rgion parisienne
      </i>
      , "Paris Region". is one of the twenty-seven
      <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">
       regions of France
      </a>
      , and includes the city of Paris. It covers 12,012 square kilometers (4,638 square miles), and has its own regional council and president. It has a population of 12,005,077 as of January 2014, or 18.2 percent of the population of France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pop_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pop-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The region is made up of eight administrative
      <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">
       departments
      </a>
      :
      <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
       Paris
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Essonne" title="Essonne">
       Essonne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-Seine" title="Hauts-de-Seine">
       Hauts-de-Seine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Seine-Saint-Denis">
       Seine-Saint-Denis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-et-Marne" title="Seine-et-Marne">
       Seine-et-Marne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Val-de-Marne" title="Val-de-Marne">
       Val-de-Marne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Val-d%27Oise" title="Val-d'Oise">
       Val-d'Oise
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yvelines" title="Yvelines">
       Yvelines
      </a>
      . Created as the "District of the Paris Region" in 1961, it was renamed after the historic province of le-de-France in 1976 when its administrative status was aligned with the other French administrative regions created in 1972. Residents are sometimes referred to as
      <i>
       "Franciliens"
      </i>
      , an administrative word created in the 1980s.
     </p>
     <p>
      The GDP of the region in 2012 was 612.3 billion Euros, or (US$788billion at
      <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product#Cross-border_comparison" title="Gross domestic product">
       market exchange rates
      </a>
      ). It has the highest per-capita GDP among regions in France, and the third highest of regions in the European Union. In 2014 thirty companies from the
      <a href="/wiki/Fortune_Global_500" title="Fortune Global 500">
       Fortune Global 500
      </a>
      had their headquarters in the Paris Region.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8Ele-de-France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although the modern name "
      <i>
       le-de-France
      </i>
      " clearly means "Island of France", the etymology is in fact unclear. The "island" may refer to the land between the rivers
      <a href="/wiki/Oise_(river)" title="Oise (river)">
       Oise
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marne_(river)" title="Marne (river)">
       Marne
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">
       Seine
      </a>
      , or it may also have been a reference to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_de_la_Cit%C3%A9" title="le de la Cit">
        le de la Cit
       </a>
      </i>
      , in which case "Island of France" was originally a
      <a href="/wiki/Pars_pro_toto" title="Pars pro toto">
       pars pro toto
      </a>
      or perhaps a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metonym" title="Metonym">
       metonym
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Yet another possibility is that the term is a corruption of a hypothesized
      <a href="/wiki/Frankish_language" title="Frankish language">
       Frankish language
      </a>
      term "
      <i>
       Liddle Franke
      </i>
      " meaning "Little France" or "little Frankish land", so the modern reference to an "island" may be coincidental. However, this theory might be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anachronistic" title="Anachronistic">
       anachronistic
      </a>
      , since the name "
      <i>
       L'le-de-France
      </i>
      " (including the definite article) is not documented prior to 1387.
     </p>

     <p>
      The ancien rgime
      <b>
       Isle of France
      </b>
      (then referred as such in English), is one of the historical
      <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_France" title="Provinces of France">
       provinces of France
      </a>
      , and the one at the centre of power during most of
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_France" title="History of France">
       French history
      </a>
      . The province was centred on Paris, seat of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_France" title="Crown of France">
       Crown of France
      </a>
      . The area around Paris was the original personal domain of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_France" title="King of France">
       king of France
      </a>
      , as opposed to areas ruled by feudal lords of whom he was the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzerain" title="Suzerain">
       suzerain
      </a>
      . This is reflected by divisions such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Vexin" title="French Vexin">
       Vxin Franais
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Vexin" title="Norman Vexin">
       Vxin Normand
      </a>
      , the former being within the King of France's domain, the latter being within the
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Normandy" title="Duke of Normandy">
       Duke of Normandy
      </a>
      's fief.
     </p>
     <p>
      The old provinces were abolished during the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      in the late 18th century and divided between newly devised subdivisions called departments. An area not entirely corresponding to the historical le-de-France province was created in 1959 as
      <i>
       district de la rgion de Paris
      </i>
      ("District of the Paris Region"). The district was reconstituted as the le-de-France region on 6 May 1976 and increased administrative and political powers
      <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">
       devolved
      </a>
      in the process of
      <a href="/wiki/Regionalisation" title="Regionalisation">
       regionalisation
      </a>
      in the 1980s and 1990s.
     </p>

     <p>
      le-de-France has a land area of 12,011km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      (4,637sqmi). It is composed of eight
      <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">
       departments
      </a>
      centered around its innermost department and capital, Paris. Around the department of Paris, urbanization fills a first concentric ring of three departments commonly known as the
      <i>
       petite couronne
      </i>
      ("small ring"), and extends into a second outer ring of four departments known as the
      <i>
       grande couronne
      </i>
      ("large ring"). The
      <a href="/wiki/Seine_(department)" title="Seine (department)">
       former department of Seine
      </a>
      , abolished in 1968, included the city proper and parts of the
      <i>
       petite couronne
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petite_couronne" title="Petite couronne">
        petite couronne
       </a>
      </b>
      consists of the departments of
      <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-Seine" title="Hauts-de-Seine">
       Hauts-de-Seine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Seine-Saint-Denis">
       Seine-Saint-Denis
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Val-de-Marne" title="Val-de-Marne">
       Val-de-Marne
      </a>
      , and the
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grande_couronne" title="Grande couronne">
        grande couronne
       </a>
      </b>
      of those of
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-et-Marne" title="Seine-et-Marne">
       Seine-et-Marne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yvelines" title="Yvelines">
       Yvelines
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Essonne" title="Essonne">
       Essonne
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Val-d%27Oise" title="Val-d'Oise">
       Val-d'Oise
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The river Seine runs through the region. The Seine has many tributaries, including the rivers Oise and Aube. It is France's second largest river after the Loire. The region is in an area of lowland called the Paris Basin. South of this region lies the
      <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">
       Massif Central
      </a>
      , an area of highlands that are higher than the surrounding countryside but far lower than the Alps.
     </p>
     <p>
      The climate of the region is quite similar to those of England and western Germany, except that it has warmer summers and milder winters than England, and receives less rain than England does.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">
       Economy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8Ele-de-France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economy">

       </a>

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Paris region is France's premier center of economic activity, with a 2012
      <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">
       gross domestic product
      </a>
      (GDP) of
      <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">
       
      </a>
      612 billion (US$760 billion). It's GDP is estimated to reach 650 billion (US$904 billion) by 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paris_GDP_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Paris_GDP-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2011, its GDP ranked second among the regions of Europe and its per-capita GDP was the fourth-highest in Europe.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKinsey_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McKinsey-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the Paris region's population accounted for 18.8 percent of metropolitan France in 2011,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Paris region's GDP accounted for 30.1 percent of metropolitan France's GDP.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paris_GDP_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Paris_GDP-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It hosts the world headquarters of 30
      <a href="/wiki/Fortune_Global_500" title="Fortune Global 500">
       Fortune Global 500
      </a>
      companies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortune500_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fortune500-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The regional economy has been gradually shifting towards high-value-added service industries (
      <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">
       finance
      </a>
      , IT services, etc.) and high-tech manufacturing (electronics, optics, aerospace, etc.).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INSEE_201210_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-INSEE_201210-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Paris region's most intense economic activity takes place in the central
      <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-Seine" title="Hauts-de-Seine">
       Hauts-de-Seine
      </a>
      department and suburban La Dfense business district, in a triangle between the
      <a href="/wiki/Palais_Garnier" title="Palais Garnier">
       <i>
        Opra Garnier
       </i>
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       La Dfense
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Val_de_Seine" title="Val de Seine">
        Val de Seine
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INSEE_201210_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-INSEE_201210-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the Paris economy is dominated by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Service_Sector" title="Service Sector">
       services
      </a>
      , and employment in manufacturing sector has declined sharply, the region remains an important manufacturing centre, particularly for aeronautics, automobiles, and "eco" industries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INSEE_201210_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-INSEE_201210-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The per-capita income of the region is the largest of NUTS-1 Regions in the European Union and is third per capita after Luxembourg and Brussels with $71,126.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Tourism is a major component of the regional economy, since the region includes both Paris and
      <a href="/wiki/Disneyland_Paris" title="Disneyland Paris">
       Disneyland Paris
      </a>
      . The Paris region received 32.3 million visitors in 2013, putting the region just ahead of London as the world's top tourist destination region, measured by hotel occupancy. The largest numbers of foreign tourists to the Paris region came in order from the United Kingdom, the United States, Germany, Italy and China.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

     <p>
      According to the 2011 census, 59.0 percent of the regional workforce is in commerce, transportation, and market services: 26.8 percent worked in non-market services (public administration, education, human health and social work activities); 8.6 percent worked in manufacturing, mining, and utilities; 5.3 percent worked in construction; and 0.3 percent worked in agriculture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ParisAU_workforce_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ParisAU_workforce-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The majority of the region's salaried employees fill 370,000 businesses services jobs, concentrated in the north-western 8th, 16th and 17th arrondissements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlapageINSEE_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AlapageINSEE-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Paris' financial service companies are concentrated in the central-western 8th and 9th arrondissement banking and insurance district.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlapageINSEE_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AlapageINSEE-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]

       </a>
      </sup>
      Paris' department store district in the 1st, 6th, 8th and 9th arrondissements employs 10 percent of mostly female Paris workers, with 100,000 of these registered in the retail trade.

        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AlapageINSEE_14-4">

       La Dfense
      </a>
      district specializing in finance, insurance and scientific research district is the largest dedicated business district in all of Europe and employs more than 150,000 as of 2012 and estimated to reach 200,000 by 2015 and 300,000 by 2019.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INSEE_201210_10-3">

Most of this is due to companies relocating outside the Paris region. The Paris region's 800 aerospace companies employed 100,000.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">
       Politics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        edit

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Regional Council is the legislative body of the region. Its seat is in Paris, at 33 rue Barbet-de-Jouy in the 7th arrondissement. Since 1998, it is presided by the Socialist Jean-Paul Huchon. The next regional elections will take place 6 December and 13 December 2015.

     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <b>
        Petite Couronne
       </b>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (Little Crown, i.e.
      <i>
       Inner Ring
      </i>
      ) is the hub of the urban agglomeration of
      <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
       Paris
      </a>
      . It is formed by the 3
      <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">
       departments
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France_(region)" title="le-de-France (region)">
       le-de-France
      </a>
      bordering with the French capital and forming a geographical
      <i>
       crown
      </i>
      around it. The departments, until 1968 part of the disbanded
      <a href="/wiki/Seine_(department)" title="Seine (department)">
       Seine
      </a>
      department, are
      <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-Seine" title="Hauts-de-Seine">
       Hauts-de-Seine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Seine-Saint-Denis">
       Seine-Saint-Denis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Val-de-Marne" title="Val-de-Marne">
       Val-de-Marne
      </a>
      . The most populated towns of the
      <i>
       Petite Couronne
      </i>
      are
      <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-Billancourt" title="Boulogne-Billancourt">
       Boulogne-Billancourt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Montreuil,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Montreuil, Seine-Saint-Denis">
       Montreuil
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Saint-Denis" title="Saint-Denis">
       Saint-Denis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nanterre" title="Nanterre">
       Nanterre
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A9teil" title="Crteil">
       Crteil
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grande_Couronne">
       Grande Couronne
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%8Ele-de-France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grande Couronne">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <b>
        Grande Couronne
       </b>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (Greater Crown, i.e.
      <i>
       Outer Ring
      </i>
      ) includes the towns of the metropolitan area part of the other 4 departments of le-de-France not bordering with Paris. They are
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-et-Marne" title="Seine-et-Marne">
       Seine-et-Marne
      </a>
      (77),
      <a href="/wiki/Yvelines" title="Yvelines">
       Yvelines
      </a>
      (78),
      <a href="/wiki/Essonne" title="Essonne">
       Essonne
      </a>
      (91) and
      <a href="/wiki/Val-d%27Oise" title="Val-d'Oise">
       Val-d'Oise
      </a>
      (95). The latter three departments formed the
      <a href="/wiki/Seine-et-Oise" title="Seine-et-Oise">
       Seine-et-Oise
      </a>
      department until this was disbanded in 1968. The city of
      <a href="/wiki/Versailles_(city)" title="Versailles (city)">
       Versailles
      </a>
      is part of this area.
     </p>

     <p>
      Paris and the le-de-France region hosts one of the largest concentrations of immigrants in Europe. As of 2006, about 35% of people (4 million) living in the region were either immigrant (17%) or born to at least one immigrant parent (18%).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the 2010 census, 23.0% of the total population in the le-de-France region had been born outside
      <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">
       Metropolitan France
      </a>
      , up from 19.7% at the 1999 census.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
