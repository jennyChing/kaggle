     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Weiwurstquator
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       German pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">
        [vasvstkvato]
       </a>
      </span>
      <small class="nowrap metadata">
       (
       <a href="/wiki/File:De-Wei%C3%9Fwurst%C3%A4quator.ogg" title="File:De-Weiwurstquator.ogg">
        <img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/>
       </a>
       <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/De-Wei%C3%9Fwurst%C3%A4quator.ogg" title="De-Weiwurstquator.ogg">
        listen
       </a>
       )
      </small>
      ,
      <i>
       white sausage equator
      </i>
      ) is a humorous term describing the supposed cultural boundary separating
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Germany" title="Southern Germany">
       Southern Germany
      </a>
      , especially
      <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">
       Bavaria
      </a>
      , from
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Germany_(cultural_area)" title="Central Germany (cultural area)">
       Central Germany
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      It is named for the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fwurst" title="Weiwurst">
        Weiwurst
       </a>
      </i>
      sausage of Bavaria. There is no precise definition of the Weiwurstquator; it is sometimes taken to correspond with the linguistic boundary known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Speyer_line" title="Speyer line">
       Speyer line
      </a>
      separating
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_German" title="Upper German">
       Upper German
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Central_German" title="Central German">
       Central German
      </a>
      dialects, roughly following the
      <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">
       Main River
      </a>
      . It is sometimes taken to run further south, more or less along the
      <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">
       Danube
      </a>
      , or between the Main and the Danube, roughly along the
      <a href="/wiki/49th_parallel_north" title="49th parallel north">
       49th parallel north
      </a>
      circle of latitude.
     </p>
