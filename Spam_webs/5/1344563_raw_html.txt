     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">
       Life and career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yevgeny_Mravinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mravinsky was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">
       Saint Petersburg
      </a>
      . The soprano
      <a href="/wiki/Yevgeniya_Mravina" title="Yevgeniya Mravina">
       Yevgeniya Mravina
      </a>
      was his aunt. His father died in 1918, and in that same year, he began to work backstage at the
      <a href="/wiki/Mariinsky_Theatre" title="Mariinsky Theatre">
       Mariinsky Theatre
      </a>
      . He first studied
      <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">
       biology
      </a>
      at the university in Leningrad, before going to the
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_Conservatory" title="Saint Petersburg Conservatory">
       Leningrad Conservatory
      </a>
      to study music. He served as a ballet repetiteur from 1923 to 1931. His first public conducting appearance was in 1929. Through the 1930s he conducted at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirov_Ballet" title="Kirov Ballet">
       Kirov Ballet
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshoi_Opera" title="Bolshoi Opera">
       Bolshoi Opera
      </a>
      . In September 1938, he won the
      <a href="/wiki/All-Union_Conductors_Competition" title="All-Union Conductors Competition">
       All-Union Conductors Competition
      </a>
      in Moscow.
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 1938, Mravinsky took up the post that he was to hold until 1988: principal conductor of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="Leningrad Philharmonic Orchestra">
       Leningrad Philharmonic Orchestra
      </a>
      , with whom he had made his debut as a conductor in 1931.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Under Mravinsky, the Leningrad Philharmonic gained a legendary reputation, particularly in Russian music such as
      <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">
       Tchaikovsky
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">
       Shostakovich
      </a>
      . During World War II, Mravinsky and the orchestra were evacuated to Siberia. But members of the Leningrad Philharmonic's reserve orchestra and the
      <a href="/wiki/Leningrad_Radio_Orchestra" title="Leningrad Radio Orchestra">
       Leningrad Radio Orchestra
      </a>
      were left behind in the
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leningrad" title="Siege of Leningrad">
       siege of Leningrad
      </a>
      , so it fell to
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Eliasberg" title="Karl Eliasberg">
       Karl Eliasberg
      </a>
      to conduct the surviving musicians in the Leningrad premiere of the
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Shostakovich)">
       Symphony No.7 "Leningrad"
      </a>
      by Shostakovich.
     </p>
     <p>
      Legend has it that at the first performance of Shostakovich's Symphony No. 5, during the applause immediately after the performance, Mravinsky took the score in his hand and waved it above his head. Mravinsky gave world premieres of six symphonies by
      <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">
       Shostakovich
      </a>
      : numbers
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._5_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 5 (Shostakovich)">
       5
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Shostakovich)">
       6
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Shostakovich)">
       8
      </a>
      (which Shostakovich dedicated to Mravinsky),
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._9_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 9 (Shostakovich)">
       9
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._10_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 10 (Shostakovich)">
       10
      </a>
      and finally
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._12_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 12 (Shostakovich)">
       12
      </a>
      in 1961. His refusal to conduct the premiere of Shostakovich's
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._13_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 13 (Shostakovich)">
       13th Symphony
      </a>
      in 1962 caused a permanent rupture in their friendship.
     </p>
     <p>
      He premiered
      <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Prokofiev" title="Sergei Prokofiev">
       Sergei Prokofiev
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Prokofiev)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Prokofiev)">
       sixth symphony
      </a>
      in Leningrad the year of its composition (1947,
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.prokofiev.org/catalog/work.cfm?WorkID=144" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Mravinsky made commercial studio recordings from 1938 to 1961. His issued recordings from after 1961 were taken from live concerts. His final recording was from an April 1984 live performance of
      <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._12_(Shostakovich)" title="Symphony No. 12 (Shostakovich)">
       Shostakovich's Symphony No. 12
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Mravinsky first went on tour abroad in 1946, including performances in Finland and in Czechoslovakia (at the Prague Spring Festival). Later tours with orchestra included a June 1956 itinerary to West Germany, East Germany,
      <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
       Austria
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      . Their only tour to Great Britain was in September 1960 to the Edinburgh Festival and the Royal Festival Hall, London. Their first tour to Japan was in May 1973. Their last foreign tour was in 1984, to West Germany.
     </p>
     <p>
      His last concert was on 6 March 1987 (
      <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">
       Schubert
      </a>
      , Symphony No. 8, and
      <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">
       Brahms
      </a>
      , Symphony No. 4). Mravinsky died in
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">
       Leningrad
      </a>
      in 1988, aged 84.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conducting_style">
       Conducting style
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yevgeny_Mravinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conducting style">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Recordings reveal Mravinsky to have an extraordinary technical control over the orchestra, especially over
      <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(music)" title="Dynamics (music)">
       dynamics
      </a>
      . He was also a very exciting conductor, frequently changing
      <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">
       tempo
      </a>
      in order to heighten the musical effect for which he was striving, often making prominent use of brass instrumentation. Surviving videos show that Mravinsky had a sober appearance
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Podium" title="Podium">
       podium
      </a>
      , making simple but very clear gestures, often without a baton. The critic
      <a href="/wiki/David_Fanning_(musicologist)" title="David Fanning (musicologist)">
       David Fanning
      </a>
      has memorably described some of Mravinsky's Tchaikovsky performances:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The Leningrad Philharmonic play like a wild stallion, only just held in check by the willpower of its master. Every smallest movement is placed with fierce pride; at any moment it may break into such a frenzied gallop that you hardly know whether to feel exhilarated or terrified".
       <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.mdt.co.uk/MDTSite/pages/product/product.asp?prod=4775911&amp;cookie%5Ftest=1" rel="nofollow">
        [2]
       </a>
       '
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Method">
       Method
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yevgeny_Mravinsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Method">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In a 1970s interview on Leningrad Television, when asked how he chose a particular interpretation of the music he conducted, Mravinsky explained that he tried to understand what the composer's intention was by immersing himself in the "atmosphere" of the music (he used the term "atmospherization").
     </p>
