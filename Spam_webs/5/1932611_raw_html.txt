        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Group Polarization: The Trend to Extreme Decisions
        </h1>
       </div>
       <div class="format_text entry-content">
        <!-- Start WP Socializer Plugin - Sharethis Button -->
        <p>
         <span class="st_facebook" displaytext="share" st_title="Group Polarization: The Trend to Extreme Decisions" st_url="http://www.spring.org.uk/2009/09/group-polarization-the-trend-to-extreme-decisions.php" st_via="psyblog">

        <p>
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: 16px;">
          Say you put 10 people
         </span>
         in a room and asked them to design a car. Would they design something average or something wacky? Would they be more likely to come up with   the Ford Focus or The Homer, designed by Homer Simpson in
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oh_Brother,_Where_Art_Thou%3F">
          this
         </a>
         classic episode of The Simpsons?
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-7217">
         </span>
         To help you decide, its handy to know that The Homer has a number of forward-thinking design features including three horns, all of which play La Cucaracha, optional restraints and muzzles for the children and an engine noise that will make people think the world is coming to an end. And the cost? A mere $82,000.
        </p>
        <p>
         We tend to think that  group decisions average out the preferences of participants so they would come up with something closer to the Ford Focus.  But the psychological research doesnt support this conclusion. In fact  group discussions tend to polarize groups so that, rather than peoples views always being averaged, their initial preferences can become exaggerated and their final position is often more extreme than it was initially.
        </p>
        <p>
         In an early set of studies all about risk, decision-making was shown to shift either towards the cautious or the risky depending on the type of problem  (
         <a href="http://psycnet.apa.org/?fa=main.doiLanding&amp;uid=1969-03942-001">
          Stoner, 1968
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <h3>
         Shift to the extreme
        </h3>
        <p>
         Since then hundreds of studies from around the world have shown the phenomenon of group polarization in action. For example, after a group discussion, people already supportive of a war become more supportive, people with an initial tendency towards racism become more racist and a group with a slight preference for one job candidate will come out with a much stronger preference.
        </p>
        <!--Ad Injection:random-->
        <div style="padding-top:5px;padding-bottom:20px;">
         <center>

        <p>
         Naturally this shift towards the extreme has all sorts of implications for government, religion, commerce and the justice system. In fact one of the neatest pieces of real-world group polarization research examines the US legal system.
         <a href="http://www.ncjrs.gov/App/Publications/abstract.aspx?ID=12134">
          Main and Walker (1973)
         </a>
         analysed the decisions of Federal district court judges sitting either alone or in groups of three to see if group discussions were a factor.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the 1,500 cases where judges sat alone they took an extreme course of action only 30% of the time. However when sitting in a group of 3 this figure more than doubled to 65%. It seems even trained, professional decision-makers are subject to the forces of group polarization.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Explaining group polarization
        </h3>
        <p>
         Psychologists have three main theories for why this group polarization occurspersuasion, comparison and differentiationbut all of them have much the same cause. In any group trying to make a decision there is likely to be an initial preference in one particular direction. Those who dont initially agree with that decision are likely to change their mind to agree with the majority. The processes involved, though, are different:
        </p>
        <ol>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Persuasion
          </strong>
          : people change their mind as a result of the
          <em>
           rational
          </em>
          arguments presented by others.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Comparison
          </strong>
          : people change their mind to
          <a href="http://www.spring.org.uk/2007/11/i-cant-believe-my-eyes-conforming-to.php">
           conform with group norms
          </a>
          , especially when those norms are socially desirable.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Differentiation
          </strong>
          : a variation on
          <em>
           comparison
          </em>
          where people change their mind to fit in with their view of the sort of decisions their group should make.
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         As you can see these are all slightly different mechanisms, each which probably operates independently, for producing much the same effect: decisions are pushed further towards the extreme.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Combating polarization
        </h3>
        <p>
         Its worth pointing out that not all groups do polarize in this way and, indeed, a few of the studies have not observed the polarizing effect of groups on decision-making. Well-established groups probably suffer less from polarization, as do groups discussing problems that are well-known to them. That said, though, where groups are relatively newly formed and tasks are novel, group polarization can emerge as an important factor in decision-making.
        </p>
        <p>
         Psychologists have been less keen to offer solutions to group polarization than they have other phenomena in group decision-making such as
         <a href="http://www.spring.org.uk/2009/07/fighting-groupthink-with-dissent.php">
          groupthink
         </a>
         or the
         <a href="http://www.spring.org.uk/2009/08/why-groups-fail-to-share-information-effectively.php">
          failure of groups to share information
         </a>
         . However, because groupthink has similar causes to polarization, much the same approach can be used. This is all about nurturing dissent in a group by encouraging the discussion of multiple perspectives and critical viewpoints. Indeed research conducted by
         <a href="http://books.google.co.uk/books?hl=en&amp;lr=&amp;id=kzhDc3a_yLgC&amp;oi=fnd&amp;pg=PA3&amp;dq=Bringing+Deliberation+to+the+Democratic+Dialogue&amp;ots=yLzSEHFScY&amp;sig=yC_dxetseFqTAhH-vENkVJTwztU#v=onepage&amp;q=Bringing%20Deliberation%20to%20the%20Democratic%20Dialogue&amp;f=false">
          Fishkin and Luskin (1999)
         </a>
         has shown that diverse groups do not polarize.
        </p>
