     <p>
      The
      <b>
       BMW R32
      </b>
      was the first motorcycle produced by
      <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">
       BMW
      </a>
      under the BMW name. An
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">
       aircraft engine
      </a>
      manufacturer during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      , BMW was forced to diversify after the
      <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">
       Treaty of Versailles
      </a>
      banned the
      <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">
       German air force
      </a>
      and German aircraft manufacture. BMW initially turned to industrial engine design and manufacturing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsGuideBMW_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ConsGuideBMW-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=BMW_R32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1919, BMW designed and manufactured the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat-twin" title="Flat-twin">
       flat-twin
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/BMW_M2B15" title="BMW M2B15">
       M2B15
      </a>
      engine for
      <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(motorcycle)" title="Victoria (motorcycle)">
       Victoria Werke AG
      </a>
      of Nuremberg. The engine was initially intended as a portable industrial engine, but found its main use in Victoria motorcycles. The engine was also used in the Helios motorcycle built by Bayerische Flugzeugwerke, which was later merged into BMW AG. Bayerische Flugzeugwerke also manufactured a small
      <a href="/wiki/Two-stroke_engine" title="Two-stroke engine">
       two-stroke engined
      </a>
      motorcycle, called the Flink, which was not successful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsGuideBMW_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ConsGuideBMW-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the merger, General Director of BMW
      <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Popp" title="Franz Josef Popp">
       Franz Josef Popp
      </a>
      asked Design Director
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Friz" title="Max Friz">
       Max Friz
      </a>
      to assess the Helios motorcycle. Upon completing his assessment, Friz suggested to Popp that the best thing that could be done with the Helios would be to dump it in the nearest lake. More specifically, Friz condemned the
      <a href="/wiki/Douglas_(motorcycles)" title="Douglas (motorcycles)">
       Douglas
      </a>
      -style transverse-crankshaft layout, which heavily restricted the cooling of the rear cylinder.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsGuideBMW_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ConsGuideBMW-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:R32-engine.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="600" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/R32-engine.jpg/220px-R32-engine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/R32-engine.jpg/330px-R32-engine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/R32-engine.jpg/440px-R32-engine.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:R32-engine.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        R32 engine and transmission unit
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Popp and Friz then agreed to a near-term solution of redesigning the Helios to make it more saleable and a long-term solution of an all new motorcycle design. This new design was designated the BMW R32 and began production in 1923, becoming the first motorcycle to be badged as a BMW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsGuideBMW_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ConsGuideBMW-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The M2B33 engine in the R32 had a displacement of 494cc and had a cast-iron sidevalve cylinder/head unit. The engine produced 8.5hp (6.3kW), which propelled the R32 to a top speed of 95km/h (59mph).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMBikesSpecPage_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BMBikesSpecPage-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The engine and gear box formed a
      <a href="/wiki/Unit_construction" title="Unit construction">
       single unit
      </a>
      . The new engine featured a recirculating
      <a href="/wiki/Wet_sump" title="Wet sump">
       wet sump
      </a>
      oiling system at a time when most motorcycle manufacturers used a
      <a href="/wiki/Total-loss_oiling_system" title="Total-loss oiling system">
       total-loss oiling system
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncycloMoto_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncycloMoto-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      BMW used this type of recirculating oiling system until 1969.
     </p>
     <p>
      To counter the cooling problems encountered with the Helios, Friz oriented the R32's M2B33 boxer engine with the cylinder heads projecting out on each side for cooling,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsGuideBMW_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-ConsGuideBMW-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as used in the earlier British-manufactured
      <a href="/wiki/ABC_motorcycles" title="ABC motorcycles">
       ABC
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncycloMoto_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncycloMoto-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike the ABC, however, the R32 used
      <a href="/wiki/Drive_shaft" title="Drive shaft">
       shaft final drive
      </a>
      from a flexible coupling on the gearbox output shaft to a pinion driving a ring gear on the rear wheel hub.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsGuideBMW_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-ConsGuideBMW-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The R32 had a tubular steel frame with twin downtubes that continued under the engine to the rear wheel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncycloMoto_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncycloMoto-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The front fork had a trailing link design suspended by a leaf spring, similar to the forks used by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_(motorcycle)" title="Indian (motorcycle)">
       Indian
      </a>
      at the time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncycloMoto_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EncycloMoto-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The rear wheel was rigidly mounted. In the original First Series, Braking was accomplished solely with a "dummy rim" on the rear wheel with two padded brake shoes set within it. One would be operated by the, now traditional, right hand lever and the other by use of a heel actuated lever on the right side. Later years would reroute the front right lever and cable to a front drum brake.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=BMW_R32&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The R32 established the boxer-twin, shaft-drive powertrain layout that BMW would use until the present. BMW used shaft drives in all of its motorcycles until the introduction of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMW_F650_single" title="BMW F650 single">
       F650
      </a>
      in 1994 and continues to use it on their boxer-twin motorcycles.
     </p>
