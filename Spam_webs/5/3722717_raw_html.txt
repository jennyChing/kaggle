           <p>
            Are roads and parking lots covered with
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/2013/12/18/how-solar-panels-can-boost-your-homes-value-by-nearly-6000/" target="_blank">
             solar panels
            </a>
            that feed power back into the grid actually feasible or does it sound like a green-energy fairy tale?
           </p>
           <p>
            Its been about eight years since an electrical engineer and his counselor set out to show that updating our traveling infrastructure with
            <a href="http://ecowatch.com/business/renewables/" target="_blank">
             renewable energy
            </a>
            is more than a fantasy. Now, theyre on the precipice of releasing their prototype for Solar Roadways, a system that would replace asphalt on our roads, giving them an energy production purpose.
           </p>
           <p>
            Withbuilt-in LED lights, materials sturdy enough for vehicles and heat that would prevent the buildup of excess snow and ice,
            <a href="http://www.solarroadways.com/intro.shtml" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.solarroadways.com/intro.shtml', 'Solar Roadways');" target="_blank">
             Solar Roadways
            </a>
            is an ambitious project, to be sure. ButScott and Julie Brusaw have believers in the federal governmentthey have receivedtwo phases of funding from the U.S. Federal Highway Administration for research and development, including $100,000 in 2009.
           </p>
           <div class="unit" id="div-gpt-ad-1433512244421-0" style="height:90px; width:728px;margin-bottom:14px;">
            <script type="text/javascript">
             googletag.cmd.push(function() { googletag.display("div-gpt-ad-1433512244421-0"); });
            </script>
           </div>
           <p>
            <span class="embed-wrapper">
             <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/SNMFKKyFU60?start=97&amp;feature=oembed" width="500">
             </iframe>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            Half of our prototype parking lot is mono-crystalline, while the other half is poly-crystalline, Julie Brusaw told
            <a href="http://www.gizmag.com/solar-roadways-parking-lot/31780/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.gizmag.com/solar-roadways-parking-lot/31780/', 'Gizmag');" target="_blank">
             <em>
              Gizmag
             </em>
            </a>
            . The parking lot is equivalent to a 3,600-watt solar array. The power collected is dependent upon the amount of sunshine received. So as with all solar, it will produce more in some parts of the country and world than others.
           </p>
           <p>
            The
            <a href="https://www.indiegogo.com/projects/solar-roadways#home" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.indiegogo.com/projects/solar-roadways#home', 'prototype parking lot');" target="_blank">
             prototype parking lot
            </a>
            is at the coupleselectronics lab in Idaho. Textured glass covers the solar panels and circuitry, and the couple says the lot is fully operational. Next, the Brusaws will addcovers for mounting holes, mastic between panels and software for LED patterns.
           </p>
           <p>
            Solar Roadways glass exceeded all requirements during rounds of testing for traction, load and impact resistance in civil engineering laboratories around the country.
           </p>
           <p>
            In 2010 and 2011, the concept claimed first place in two General Electric competitionsthe Ecomagination Challenge for Powering the Grid and the Ecomagination Challenge for Powering the Home, respectively.
           </p>

            Heres how the Brusaws envision things once their technology is brought to the market:
           </p>
           <p>
            Everyone has power. No more power shortages, no more roaming power outages, no more need to burn coal (50 percent of greenhouse gases), they write on their site.
           </p>
           <p>
            Less need for fossil fuels and less dependency upon foreign oil. Much less pollution. How about this for a long term advantage: An electric road allows all-electric vehicles to recharge anywhere: rest stops, parking lots, etc.They would then have the same range as a gasoline-powered vehicle. Internal combustion engines would become obsolete. Our dependency on oil would come to an abrupt end.
           </p>

            Its time to upgrade our infrastructureroads and power gridto the 21st century.

                         <p>
                          Would you consider a renewable fuel source (system) thats carbon neutral, eco friendly, and cost effective. Would this interest you?
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Now what if, this renewable fuel used waste (trash that would normally go to landfills) as an energy input source in its creation. Still interested?. Well lets continue on then.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          What if, the only byproducts produced from these processes where pure air, pure distilled water, and fertilizer What would you think of that?
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          So what if, this renewable fuel source didnt displace jobs, but created new jobs, a supportive technology rather than a disruptive technology (the oil, gas, and coal industry supports ~ 16,800,000) Have I peaked your interest?
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          AND NO I am NOT talking about nuclear energy. I am talking about something natural, that has been around for many eons Namely Algae, that green pond scum that you try to get rid of in your fish tank, or swimming pool. This is where crude oil comes from, there are many companies in the US and abroad that are trying to do what I am suggesting, but they are doing it the same old way, that is, in my opinion not very effective, just providing patches to a flawed system, which is what I see time, and time again from my research.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          What I have spent the past 10 years of my life doing is researching, experimenting,  and redesigning the whole algae growth system, so that it would provide the ideal conditions to grow/produce algae in abundant enough amounts year round, thus making it economically viable, and be able to compete with the cost of regular crude oil.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Heres how (the basics):
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Due to sun light dependency existing algae systems are seasonal (about 200 days per year).
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system provides light in the proper spectrum 365 days a year. We get 165 extra growth days per year, and being the light is within the proper spectrum none of the light energy is wasted, or is harmful to the algae.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Another note on lighting; in existing horizontal algae systems require a larger surface area (acreage) to absorb the needed light to provide energy for the algae.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system due to it being vertical uses considerably less land area. Do to the internal circulation, and lighting configuration effectively increases the surface area of the lighting by at least 100 fold. Instead of penetrating the algae with light, the algae are constantly circulated around the water proof light tubes, that are submerged within the algae culture.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Lack of temperature control causes existing systems to be seasonal, too cold the algae dont grow, too hot and they die.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system provides temperature control year round.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Contamination from other invasive none productive species of algae limit production, also protozoa are an issue in open pond systems.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system is designed to prevent these problems because it is closed to the outside environment, and anything that goes into the system is sterilized prior to going into the growth tanks/PBRs. Another note here is that our PBRs are independant of each other (multiple indepentant tanks per facility), so if one tank somehow gets contaminated it doesnt shut down the entire facility only the one tank.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Existing algae systems are dependant on electricity from the grid. They require large amounts of electricity, and this is one of the main costs in their systems. How they try to lower the costs is by selling all the possible bi-products they can from the algae; off setting the costs to produce oil/fuel.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system uses waste products, and/or dry bio-mass that have a large thermal energy potential, and are vertually unlimited in supply, to create its own electrical energy, through an onsite steam power plant, thus eliminating the cost of electricity entirely.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Existing algae system are somewhat location dependant, because of the need for a good carbon dioxide source.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system provides its own carbon dioxide source by burning waste/bio-mass, and methane produced from the septic tanks the Co2 is captured and used to feed the algae, as well this allows for diversity of facility locations.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Some existing systems require commercially manufactured fertilizer to feed the algae that costs considerably.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system uses waste water from municipal waste water sources, or animal waste sources broken down in large specially designed septic tanks  as a nutrient source, this also provides methane gas that is used in the system.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Water usage/loss in existing systems is a major issue.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Our system is designed to prevent water loss from evaporation, it is designed to reuse its water, and also condenses water from the intake air, and water produced by burning methane gas, and waste. What happens is we actually end up with an excess of pure distilled water.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           Harvesting is a major challenge for existing algae companies. It is both labor intensive, energy intensive, and causes considerable water losses that have to be replenished mainly do to evaporation, this is because of the relatively low concentrations of algae to water in their systems, and vast amounts of water that has to be dealt with.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                           We have devised a unique way of harvesting and dewatering the algae in our system that is not labor intensive, very effieient, plus the water is captured and reused, as well as the energy inputs for these processes are produced onsite.
