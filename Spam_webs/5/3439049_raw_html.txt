        As readers of the Risk Factor know, blaming the computer for any and alltechnicalglitchesis afairlyregular and convenientexcuse. One of the more unusualtechnical problems blamedona computerwas
        <a href="http://www.wbaltv.com/news/26821379/detail.html" rel="nofollow">
         reported
        </a>
        about a week plus ago by the
        <a href="http://www.wbaltv.com/" rel="nofollow">
         WBAL-TV 11 News I-Team
        </a>
        in Baltimore, Maryland.
        <p>
         Apparently, some 2,000 Baltimore red light camera traffic citations which require a police officer to swear that he or she has reviewed the photos as indeed showing a traffic violation had a slight problem. It seems that allthe verificationsignatures on the citationsissuedwere from a police officer who haddied in a car accident last year.
        </p>
        <p>
         WBAL-TV said the error was discovered when a retired police officer acquaintance of adriver who hadreceived a $75 red light cameraticket on 12 January 2011 noticed the signature on the citationwas fromthe police officer who he knew was deceased.
        </p>
        <p>
         A Baltimore police spokesperson, WBAL-TV said:
        </p>
        <div class="ad floating-ad" id="adsense1" style="width:468px;height:80px;">
         <script type="text/javascript">
          //
		google_ad_client = "pub-4956733744046268";

        <p>
         <em>
          "... blamed the problem on a computer glitch, which is the fault of the company that operates the camera system, and he said the problem has been rectified."
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          <div class="image_wrapper small_image">
           <img alt="policeman reaching into car" class="small_image" data-height="361" data-url="http://images.thecarconnection.com/med/policeman-reaching-into-car_100227286_m.jpg" data-width="332" height="361" name="tccimg_100227286_m" src="http://images.thecarconnection.com/med/policeman-reaching-into-car_100227286_m.jpg" title="policeman reaching into car" width="332"/>
           <p>
            policeman reaching into car
           </p>
           <a class="enlarge" href="/image/100227286_policeman-reaching-into-car" name="tccwrp_100227286" target="_blank">
            Enlarge Photo
           </a>
          </div>
          <br/>
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Exactly how the deceased officer's name ended up on the tickets wasn't fully explained, but Baltimore police insist that internal policedocuments show thata live officer reviewed and approvedthe citations. Legal experts sayBaltimore may have difficulty enforcing the tickets if they are challenged in court, however.
        </p>
        <p>
         What Baltimorecould (or should)dois follow the lead of Edmonton Canada's Chief Crown Prosecutor Steven Bilodeauwho decided late last monththat all persons who had received
         <a href="http://calgary.ctv.ca/servlet/an/local/CTVNews/20090302/CGY_green_light_090302/20090302/?hub=CalgaryHome" rel="nofollow">
          speed on green
         </a>
         camera tickets at city intersections since November 2009 are eligible for refunds. Some 140,000 tickets worth about C$13 million are involved.
        </p>
        <p>
         According to this
         <a href="http://www.edmontonsun.com/news/edmonton/2011/01/24/17011801.html" rel="nofollow">
          story
         </a>
         publishedin the
         <a href="http://www.edmontonsun.com/" rel="nofollow">
          Edmonton Sun
         </a>
         , a speed on green ticket coincidentally issued on 12 January 2011 indicated thatthatacar had gone through a cityintersection at 143 km/h, which, the Crown prosecutor said,was "obviously wrong."
        </p>
        <p>
         This erroneous speedingticketkicked off an investigation that resultedin thedecisionto dismissallthe speed on greentickets as a "matter of fairness,"Crown Prosecutor Bilodeausaid.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Sun story says that the offending cameras would be pulled for at least a month, and that the cameras' problem couldinvolve either hardware or software issues.
        </p>
        <p>
         I can't find any update to this story, such asthe confirmed cause of the glitch or when the cameras will be returned to operation. Anyone know?
        </p>
        <p>
         I also haven't seen anything that indicates that Baltimoreplans todrop all 2,000 tickets "as a matter of fairness," either. With a $150,000 at stake, I doubt the city will.
        </p>
