     <p>
      <b>
       Mosasaurs
      </b>
      (from
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       Mosa
      </i>
      meaning the '
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meuse_river" title="Meuse river">
       Meuse river
      </a>
      ', and
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       Greek
      </a>
      
      <i>
       sauros
      </i>
      meaning 'lizard') are an extinct group of large
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_reptile" title="Marine reptile">
       marine
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">
       reptiles
      </a>
      . Their first fossil remains were discovered in a limestone quarry at
      <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">
       Maastricht
      </a>
      on the Meuse in 1764. Mosasaurs probably evolved from an extinct group of aquatic
      <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">
       lizards
      </a>
      known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aigialosaur" title="Aigialosaur">
       aigialosaurs
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Early_Cretaceous" title="Early Cretaceous">
       Early Cretaceous
      </a>
      . During the last 20 million years of the Cretaceous period (
      <a href="/wiki/Turonian" title="Turonian">
       Turonian
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">
       Maastrichtian
      </a>
      ages), with the extinction of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosaur" title="Ichthyosaur">
       ichthyosaurs
      </a>
      and decline of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plesiosaur" title="Plesiosaur">
       plesiosaurs
      </a>
      , mosasaurs became the dominant marine predators.
     </p>

        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="6179" height="83" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Platecarpus2010.jpg/250px-Platecarpus2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Platecarpus2010.jpg/375px-Platecarpus2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Platecarpus2010.jpg/500px-Platecarpus2010.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Platecarpus2010.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Life restoration of a mosasaur (
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platecarpus_tympaniticus" title="Platecarpus tympaniticus">
          Platecarpus tympaniticus
         </a>
        </i>
        ) informed by fossil skin impressions.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mosasaurs breathed air, were powerful swimmers, and were well-adapted to living in the warm, shallow
      <a href="/wiki/Inland_sea_(geology)" title="Inland sea (geology)">
       inland seas
      </a>
      prevalent during the Late Cretaceous Period. Mosasaurs were so well adapted to this environment that they
      <a href="/wiki/Vivipary" title="Vivipary">
       gave birth to live young
      </a>
      , rather than returning to the shore to lay eggs as
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">
       sea turtles
      </a>
      do.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The smallest-known mosasaur was
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallasaurus_turneri" title="Dallasaurus turneri">
        Dallasaurus turneri
       </a>
      </i>
      , which was less than 1 metre (3.3ft) long. Larger mosasaurs were more typical, with many species growing longer than 4 metres (13ft).
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosasaurus_hoffmannii" title="Mosasaurus hoffmannii">
        Mosasaurus hoffmannii
       </a>
      </i>
      , the largest known species of mosasaur, may have reached up to 18 metres (59ft) in length.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mulder1999_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mulder1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Currently, the largest publicly exhibited mosasaur skeleton in the world is on display at the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Fossil_Discovery_Centre" title="Canadian Fossil Discovery Centre">
       Canadian Fossil Discovery Centre
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Morden,_Manitoba" title="Morden, Manitoba">
       Morden
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">
       Manitoba
      </a>
      . The specimen, nicknamed "Bruce", is approximately 13 metres (43ft) long.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Mosasaurs had a body shape similar to that of modern-day
      <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">
       monitor lizards
      </a>
      (varanids), but were more elongated and streamlined for swimming. Their limb bones were reduced in length and their paddles were formed by webbing between their long finger and toe bones. Their tails were broad, and supplied their locomotive power. Until recently, mosasaurs were assumed to have swum in a method similar to the one used today by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conger_eel" title="Conger eel">
       conger eels
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_snake" title="Sea snake">
       sea snakes
      </a>
      , undulating their entire bodies from side to side. However, new evidence suggests that many advanced mosasaurs had large crescent-shaped flukes on the ends of their tails, similar to those of
      <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">
       sharks
      </a>
      and some
      <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosaur" title="Ichthyosaur">
       ichthyosaurs
      </a>
      . Rather than use snake-like undulations, their bodies probably remained stiff to reduce drag through the water, while their tails provided strong propulsion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCKC10_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LCKC10-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These animals may have lurked and pounced rapidly and powerfully on passing prey, rather than chasing after it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Early reconstructions showed mosasaurs with dorsal crests running the length of their bodies, which were based on misidentified remains of
      <a href="/wiki/Trachea" title="Trachea">
       tracheal
      </a>
      cartilage. By the time this error was discovered, depicting mosasaurs with such crests in artwork had already become a trend.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology">
       Paleobiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mosasaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Paleobiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="653" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg/220px-Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg/330px-Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg/440px-Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Placenticeratidae_-_Placenticeras_whitfieldi.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Fossil shell of ammonite
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Placenticeras_whitfieldi" title="Placenticeras whitfieldi">
          Placenticeras whitfieldi
         </a>
        </i>
        showing punctures caused by the bite of a mosasaur,
        <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Peabody Museum of Natural History">
         Peabody Museum of Natural History
        </a>
        , Yale
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mosasaurs had double-hinged jaws and flexible skulls (much like those of
      <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">
       snakes
      </a>
      ), which enabled them to gulp down their prey almost whole. A skeleton of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tylosaurus" title="Tylosaurus">
        Tylosaurus
       </a>
       proriger
      </i>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">
       South Dakota
      </a>
      included remains of the diving
      <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">
       seabird
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hesperornis" title="Hesperornis">
        Hesperornis
       </a>
      </i>
      , a marine
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">
       bony fish
      </a>
      , a possible
      <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">
       shark
      </a>
      , and another, smaller mosasaur (
      <i>
       Clidastes
      </i>
      ). Mosasaur bones have also been found with shark teeth embedded in them.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the food items of mosasaurs were ammonites, molluscs with shells similar to that of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nautilus" title="Nautilus">
        Nautilus
       </a>
      </i>
      , which were abundant in the Cretaceous seas. Round holes have been found in fossil shells of some ammonites, mainly
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pachydiscus" title="Pachydiscus">
        Pachydiscus
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Placenticeras" title="Placenticeras">
        Placenticeras
       </a>
      </i>
      . These were once interpreted as a result of limpets attaching themselves to the ammonites, but the triangular shape of the holes, their size, and their presence on both sides of the shells, corresponding to upper and lower jaws, is evidence of the bite of medium-sized mosasaurs. It is not clear whether this behaviour was common across all size classes of mosasaurs.
     </p>
     <p>
      Virtually all forms were active predators of fish and ammonites; a few, such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Globidens" title="Globidens">
        Globidens
       </a>
      </i>
      , had blunt spherical teeth, specialized for crushing mollusk shells. The smaller genera, such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Platecarpus" title="Platecarpus">
        Platecarpus
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dallasaurus" title="Dallasaurus">
        Dallasaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      , which were about 16 m (320ft) long, probably fed on fish and other small prey. The smaller mosasaurs may have spent some time in fresh water, hunting for food. The larger mosasaurs, such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tylosaurus" title="Tylosaurus">
        Tylosaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mosasaurus" title="Mosasaurus">
        Mosasaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      , reached sizes of 1015 m (3349ft) long and were the apex predators of the Late Cretaceous oceans, attacking other marine reptiles as well as preying on large fish and ammonites.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Soft_tissue">
       Soft tissue
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mosasaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soft tissue">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_skin.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="884" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tylosaurus_skin.jpg/220px-Tylosaurus_skin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tylosaurus_skin.jpg/330px-Tylosaurus_skin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tylosaurus_skin.jpg/440px-Tylosaurus_skin.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_skin.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Scales of
        <i>
         Tylosaurus proriger
        </i>
        (KUVP-1075)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Despite the many mosasaur remains collected worldwide, knowledge of the nature of their skin coverings remains in its early stages. Few mosasaurid specimens collected from around the world retain fossilized scale imprints. This lack may be due to the delicate nature of the scales, which nearly eliminates the possibility of preservation, in addition to the preservation sediment types and the marine conditions under which the preservation occurred. Until the discovery of several mosasaur specimens with remarkably well-preserved scale imprints from late
      <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">
       Maastrichtian
      </a>
      deposits of the Muwaqqar Chalk Marl Formation of
      <a href="/wiki/Harrana" title="Harrana">
       Harrana
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaddumi_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kaddumi-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">
       Jordan
      </a>
      , knowledge of the nature of mosasaur integument was mainly based on very few accounts describing early mosasaur fossils dating back to the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Santonian" title="Santonian">
       Santonian
      </a>
      lower
      <a href="/wiki/Campanian" title="Campanian">
       Campanian
      </a>
      , such as the famous
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tylosaurus" title="Tylosaurus">
        Tylosaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      specimen (KUVP-1075) from Gove County, Kansas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snow_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Snow-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Material from Jordan has shown that the bodies of mosasaurs, as well as the membranes between their fingers and toes, were covered with small, overlapping, diamond-shaped scales resembling those of snakes. Much like those of modern reptiles, mosasaur scales varied across the body in type and size. In Harrana specimens, two types of scales were observed on a single specimen: keeled scales covering the upper regions of the body and smooth scales covering the lower.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaddumi_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kaddumi-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As ambush predators, lurking and quickly capturing prey using stealth tactics,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Massare_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Massare-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      they may have benefited from the nonreflective, keeled scales.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaddumi_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kaddumi-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1593" data-file-width="2024" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png/220px-Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png/330px-Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png/440px-Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soft_tissue_structures_in_Platecarpus.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Soft tissues in the head and neck of
        <i>
         Platecarpus tympaniticus
        </i>
        specimen LACM 128319: Tracheal rings are shown in the bottom three photographs.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      More recently, a fossil of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Platecarpus" title="Platecarpus">
        Platecarpus tympaniticus
       </a>
      </i>
      has been found that preserved not only skin impressions, but also internal organs. Several reddish areas in the fossil may represent the heart, lungs, and kidneys. The trachea is also preserved, along with part of what may be the
      <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">
       retina
      </a>
      in the eye. The placement of the kidneys is farther forward in the abdomen than it is in monitor lizards, and is more similar to those of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cetacean" title="Cetacean">
       cetaceans
      </a>
      . As in cetaceans, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronchi" title="Bronchi">
       bronchi
      </a>
      leading to the lungs run parallel to each other instead of splitting apart from one another as in monitors and other terrestrial reptiles. In mosasaurs, these features may be internal adaptations to fully marine lifestyles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCKC10_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-LCKC10-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prognathodon_tissue.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1552" data-file-width="2040" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Prognathodon_tissue.jpg/220px-Prognathodon_tissue.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Prognathodon_tissue.jpg/330px-Prognathodon_tissue.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Prognathodon_tissue.jpg/440px-Prognathodon_tissue.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

        <div class="magnify">

         </a>

        <i>

        </i>
        specimen IRSNB 1624

      In 2011,
      <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">

      </a>
      protein was recovered from a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Prognathodon" title="Prognathodon">
        Prognathodon

      humerus dated to the

       Cretaceous
      </a>
      .

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Coloration">
       Coloration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mosasaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coloration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The coloration of mosasaurs was unknown until 2014, when the findings of Johan Lindgren of
      <a href="/wiki/Lund_University" title="Lund University">
       Lund University
      </a>
      and colleagues revealed the pigment
      <a href="/wiki/Melanin" title="Melanin">
       melanin
      </a>
      in the fossilized scales of a mosasaur. The studies revealed that mosasaurs were likely
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countershaded" title="Countershaded">
       countershaded
      </a>
      , with black backs and white underbellies, much like a
      <a href="/wiki/Great_white_shark" title="Great white shark">
       great white shark
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Leatherback_sea_turtle" title="Leatherback sea turtle">
       leatherback sea turtle
      </a>
      , the latter of which had fossilized ancestors for which color was also determined. The findings were described in the journal
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">
        Nature
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">
       Environment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sea levels were high during the Cretaceous period, causing marine transgressions in many parts of the world, and a
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">
       great inland seaway
      </a>
      in what is now North America. Mosasaur

       fossils
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">

      </a>
      ,

      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
       Portugal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">

      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">

      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">
       Czech Republic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
       Bulgaria
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      ,

        </span>
        13
        <span>

        </span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
       Discovery
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mosasaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discovery">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mosasaurus.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1543" data-file-width="2616" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Mosasaurus.jpg/220px-Mosasaurus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Mosasaurus.jpg/330px-Mosasaurus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Mosasaurus.jpg/440px-Mosasaurus.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mosasaurus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <i>
         Mosasaurus hoffmannii
        </i>
        skull found in Maastricht between 1770 and 1774
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first publicized discovery of a partial fossil mosasaur skull in 1764 by quarry workers in a subterranean gallery of a limestone quarry in
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Saint_Peter" title="Mount Saint Peter">
       Mount Saint Peter
      </a>
      , near the Dutch city of
      <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">
       Maastricht
      </a>
      , preceded any major dinosaur fossil discoveries, but remained little known. However, a second find of a partial skull drew the
      <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">
       Age of Enlightenment
      </a>
      's attention to the existence of fossilized animals that were different from any known living creatures. When the specimen was discovered between 1770 and 1774,
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Leonard_Hoffmann" title="Johann Leonard Hoffmann">
       Johann Leonard Hoffmann
      </a>
      , a surgeon and fossil collector, corresponded about it with the most influential scientists of his day, making the fossil famous. The original owner, though, was Godding, a canon of Maastricht cathedral.
     </p>
     <p>
      When the French
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_French_Revolution" title="Wars of the French Revolution">
       revolutionary forces
      </a>
      occupied Maastricht in 1794, the carefully hidden fossil was uncovered, after a reward, it is said, of 600 bottles of wine, and transported to Paris. After it had been earlier interpreted as a fish, a crocodile, and a sperm whale, the first to understand its lizard affinities was the Dutch scientist
      <a href="/wiki/Adriaan_Gilles_Camper" title="Adriaan Gilles Camper">
       Adriaan Gilles Camper
      </a>
      in 1799. In 1808,
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">
       Georges Cuvier
      </a>
      confirmed this conclusion, although
      <i>
       le Grand Animal fossile de Mastricht
      </i>
      was not actually named
      <i>
       Mosasaurus
      </i>
      ('
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meuse_River" title="Meuse River">
       Meuse
      </a>
      reptile') until 1822 and not given its full species name,
      <i>
       Mosasaurus hoffmannii,
      </i>
      until 1829. Several sets of mosasaur remains, that had been discovered earlier at Maastricht but were not identified as mosasaurs until the 19th century, have been on display in the
      <a href="/wiki/Teylers_Museum" title="Teylers Museum">
       Teylers Museum
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">
       Haarlem
      </a>
      , procured from 1790.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Maastricht
      <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
       limestone
      </a>
      beds were rendered so famous by the mosasaur discovery, they have given their name to the final six-million-year epoch of the Cretaceous, the
      <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">
       Maastrichtian
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_history">
       Evolutionary history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mosasaur&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evolutionary history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1945" data-file-width="3419" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg/220px-Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg/330px-Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg/440px-Aigialosaurus_bucchichi.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tylosaurus_pembinensis_1DB.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>

     <p>
      Based on features such as the double row of pterygoid ("flanged") teeth on the palate, the loosely hinged jaw, modified/reduced limbs and probable methods of locomotion, many researchers believe that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snakes" title="Snakes">
       snakes
      </a>
      share a common marine ancestry with mosasaurs, a suggestion advanced in 1869, by
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_Drinker_Cope" title="Edward Drinker Cope">
       Edward Drinker Cope
      </a>
      , who coined the term
      <a href="/wiki/Pythonomorpha" title="Pythonomorpha">
       Pythonomorpha
      </a>
      to unite them. The idea lay dormant for more than a century, to be revived in the 1990s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palaeos_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-palaeos-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Recently, the discovery of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Najash_rionegrina" title="Najash rionegrina">
        Najash rionegrina
       </a>
      </i>
      , a fossorial snake from South America, cast doubt on the marine origin hypothesis.
     </p>
     <p>
      The skeleton of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dallasaurus" title="Dallasaurus">
        Dallasaurus turneri
       </a>
      </i>
      , described by Bell and Polcyn (2005), has a mixture of features present in the skeletons of derived mosasaurs and in the skeletons of mosasaurid ancestors, such as aigialosaurids.
      <i>
       Dallasaurus
      </i>
      retains facultatively terrestrial limbs similar in their structure to the limbs of aigialosaurids and terrestrial squamates (plesiopedal limb condition), unlike derived mosasaurids, which evolved paddle-like limbs (hydropedal limb condition). However, the skeleton of
      <i>
       Dallasaurus
      </i>
      simultaneously had several characters that linked it with derived members of the subfamily Mosasaurinae; the authors of its description listed "invasion of the parietal by medial tongues from the frontal, teeth with smooth medial enamel surface, high coronoid buttress on surangular, interdigitate anterior scapulo-coracoid suture, humeral postglenoid process, elongate atlas synapophysis, sharp anterodorsal ridge on synapophyses, vertically oriented vertebral condyles, elongate posterior thoracic vertebrae, and fused haemal arches" as the characters uniting
      <i>
       Dallasaurus
      </i>
      with Mosasaurinae.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dallasaurus_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dallasaurus-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The phylogenetic analysis conducted by Bell and Polcyn indicated that hydropedal mosasaurids did not form a clade that wouldn't also include plesiopedal taxa such as
      <i>
       Dallasaurus
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Yaguarasaurus" title="Yaguarasaurus">
        Yaguarasaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Russellosaurus" title="Russellosaurus">
        Russellosaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tethysaurus" title="Tethysaurus">
        Tethysaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Haasiasaurus" title="Haasiasaurus">
        Haasiasaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Komensaurus" title="Komensaurus">
        Komensaurus
       </a>
      </i>
      (in 2005 only informally known as "Trieste aigialosaur"); the analysis indicated that hydropedal limb condition evolved independently in three different groups of mosasaurs (Halisaurinae, Mosasaurinae and the group containing the subfamilies Tylosaurinae and Plioplatecarpinae).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dallasaurus_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dallasaurus-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caldwell2012_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Caldwell2012-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The result of this phylogenetic study was subsequently mostly confirmed by the analyses conducted by Caldwell and Palci (2007) and Leblanc, Caldwell and Bardet (2012);
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eremiasaurus_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eremiasaurus-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the analysis conducted by Makdi, Caldwell and si (2012) indicated that hydropedal limb condition evolved independently in two group of mosasaurs (in Mosasaurinae and in the clade containing Halisaurinae, Tylosaurinae and Plioplatecarpinae).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pannoniasaurus_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pannoniasaurus-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Conrad
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      (2011), on the other hand, recovered hydropedal mosasaurs forming a clade that excluded their plesiopedal relatives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anguimorpha_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Anguimorpha-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If the hypothesis of Bell and Polcyn (2005) is correct, then mosasaurs in the traditional sense of the word, i.e. "lizards that evolved paddle-like limbs and radiated into aquatic environments in the late Mesozoic, going extinct at the end of that era",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caldwell2012_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Caldwell2012-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      are actually
      <a href="/wiki/Polyphyly" title="Polyphyly">
       polyphyletic
      </a>
      ; Bell and Polcyn (2005) maintained
      <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">
       monophyletic
      </a>
      Mosasauridae by including
      <i>
       Dallasaurus
      </i>
      and other aforementioned plesiopedal taxa in the family as well,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dallasaurus_30-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dallasaurus-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while Caldwell (2012) suggested (though explicitly stated that it was not "a formal proposal of new nomenclature") to restrict Mosasauridae only to the genus
      <i>
       Mosasaurus
      </i>
      and its closest hydropedal relatives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caldwell2012_31-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Caldwell2012-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The exact phylogenetic position of the clade containing mosasaurids and their closest relatives (aigialosaurids and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dolichosauridae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dolichosauridae (page does not exist)">
       dolichosaurs
      </a>
      ) within Squamata remains uncertain. Some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cladistic_analyses" title="Cladistic analyses">
       cladistic analyses
      </a>
      recovered them as the closest relatives of snakes,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeMSY_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LeeMSY-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      taking into account similarities in jaw and skull anatomies;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeMSY_36-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-LeeMSY-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      however, this has been disputed
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conrad2008_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-conrad2008-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the morphological analysis conducted by Conrad (2008) recovered them as
      <a href="/wiki/Varanoidea" title="Varanoidea">
       varanoids
      </a>
      closely related to terrestrial
      <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">
       monitor lizards
      </a>
      instead.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conrad2008_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-conrad2008-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Subsequent analysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Anguimorpha" title="Anguimorpha">
       anguimorph
      </a>
      relationships conducted by Conrad
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      (2011) based on morphology alone recovered mosasaurids, aigialosaurids and dolichosaurs as anguimorphs lying outside the least inclusive clade containing monitor lizards and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helodermatidae" title="Helodermatidae">
       helodermatids
      </a>
      ; the analysis based on combined datasets of morphological and molecular data, on the other hand, found them more closely related to monitor lizards and the
      <a href="/wiki/Earless_monitor_lizard" title="Earless monitor lizard">
       earless monitor lizard
      </a>
      than helodermatids and the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_crocodile_lizard" title="Chinese crocodile lizard">
       Chinese crocodile lizard
      </a>
      were.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anguimorpha_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Anguimorpha-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The large morphological analysis conducted by Gauthier
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      (2012) recovered mosasaurids, aigialosaurids and dolichosaurids in an unexpected position as basal members of the clade
      <a href="/wiki/Scincogekkonomorpha" title="Scincogekkonomorpha">
       Scincogekkonomorpha
      </a>
      (containing all taxa sharing a more recent common ancestor with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tokay_gecko" title="Tokay gecko">
        Gekko gecko
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scincus_scincus" title="Scincus scincus">
        Scincus scincus
       </a>
      </i>
      than with
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Iguana" title="Green Iguana">
        Iguana iguana
       </a>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conrad2008_38-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-conrad2008-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) that didn't belong to the clade
      <a href="/wiki/Scleroglossa" title="Scleroglossa">
       Scleroglossa
      </a>
      . The phylogenetic position of these taxa turned out to be highly dependent on which taxa were included in or excluded from the analysis. When mosasaurids were excluded from the analysis, dolichosaurs and aigialosaurids were recovered within Scleroglossa, forming a
      <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">
       sister group
      </a>
      to the clade containing snakes,
      <a href="/wiki/Amphisbaenia" title="Amphisbaenia">
       amphisbaenians
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dibamidae" title="Dibamidae">
       dibamids
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/American_legless_lizard" title="American legless lizard">
       American legless lizard
      </a>
      . When mosasaurids were included in the analysis, and various taxa with reduced or absent limbs other than snakes (such as dibamids or amphisbaenians) were excluded, mosasaurids, aigialosaurids and dolichosaurs were recovered inside Scleroglossa forming the sister group to snakes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Longrich, Bhullar and Gauthier (2012) conducted a morphological analysis of squamate relationships using a modified version of the matrix from the analysis of Gauthier
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      (2012); they found the phylogenetic position of the clade containing mosasaurs and their closest relatives within Squamata to be highly unstable, with the clade "variously being recovered outside Scleroglossa (as in Gauthier
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      , 2012) or alongside the limbless forms".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNASsquamates_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PNASsquamates-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
