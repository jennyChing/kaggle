                      Students at the University of Heidelberg go to college tuition-free, and spend more time worrying about grades than student loan debt.
                     </span>
                    </p>
                   </div>

             <div id="content-sidebar">
              <div class="field field-name-field-links field-type-link-field field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
               <div class="field-items">
                <div class="field-item first even">
                 <a href="http://www.marketplace.org/topics/education/learning-curve/american-students-head-germany-free-college">
                  American students head to Germany for free college
                 </a>
                </div>
                <div class="field-item odd">
                 <a href="http://www.marketplace.org/topics/education/learning-curve/blue-collar-aristocrats-thrive-german-economy">
                  'Blue-collar aristocrats' thrive in German economy
                 </a>
                </div>
                <div class="field-item last even">
                 <a href="http://www.marketplace.org/topics/education/learning-curve/stopping-german-students-their-tracks">
                  Stopping German students in their tracks?
                 </a>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div id="story-content">
              <div class="field field-name-field-description field-type-text-long field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
               <div class="field-items">
                <div class="field-item first even">
                 <p>
                  <em>
                   The next time you pull out your checkbook to pay that hefty tuition bill or pay down your student loan, consider this: there are countries where students pay nothing to attend university. Denmark, Sweden and Germany for example all have tuition-free college.
                  </em>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <em>
                   <a href="http://blogs.wgbh.org/on-campus/" target="_blank">
                    WGBH Radios "On Campus"
                   </a>
                   team wondered how these countries do it, and if there are things the U.S. can learn from their model. Their search to understand how German universities keep costs down and quality up began in the Rhineland.
                  </em>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  It was a frigid evening on the banks of the Rhine in the medieval city of Cologne. Under the vaulted ceiling of an old Gothic church, the 80-piece university orchestra was tuning up.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  In the land of Beethoven and Handel, it makes sense that a university would invest a lot in its orchestra. But that commitment extends far beyond the music program. In Germany, one of the world's wealthiest countries, taxpayers fully subsidize the cost of public higher education. W
                  <span style="line-height: 1.42857em;">
                   hile American students now graduate with an average of nearly $30,000 of debt, college in Germany has always been free.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Since tuition is free here, German students dont really worry about student loan debt. Instead, they worry about their exams or learning a trade. Seventy percent of the students at the University of Cologne work part-time jobs.
                  <span style="line-height: 1.42857em;">
                   Students, parents, administrators and business leaders of all political stripes say the same thing: higher education in Germany is seen as a public good.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   Controlling costs
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The University of Cologne is Germanys largest university with 48,000 students, a medical school and a law school.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "I have to be honest, I really like this university even though it's not the most beautiful one, as you can see," says tour guide Valerija Schwarz, a Ph.D. student in German Literature.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The universitys central square swarms with bicycles. Many students bike to school or take public transportation, Schwartz explains  thats why theres no big parking garage.
                 </p>
                 <div class="media media-element-container media-default">
                  <img alt="Students park their bikes outside one of the University of Cologne's newest buildings." class="media-element file-default lazy" data-src="http://www.marketplace.org/sites/default/files/Germany-27.jpg" height="400" src="/sites/default/themes/marketplacev2Responsive/images/pixel.gif" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-description field-type-text-long field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                     <p>
                      <em>
                       Students park their bikes outside one of the University of Cologne's newest buildings.
                      </em>
                     </p>
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-byline-text field-type-text field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                     Photo by Mallory Noe-Payne/WGBH
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 <p>
                  <span style="line-height: 1.42857em;">
                   Students in Germany also tend to stay local, so there aren't any dorms. There are no active student clubs, or big football stadium. And every lecture hall looks huge.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <span style="line-height: 1.42857em;">
                   Most of the time you don't even know who is sitting next to you or who your professor is, Schwarz says. You just listen and then reproduce your knowledge during the exams.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                 </p>
                 <div class="media media-element-container media-default">
                  <img alt="" class="media-element file-default lazy" data-src="http://www.marketplace.org/sites/default/files/Germany-17_2.jpg" height="222" src="/sites/default/themes/marketplacev2Responsive/images/pixel.gif" style="float: left;" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="300"/>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-description field-type-text-long field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-byline-text field-type-text field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 All of this translates to savings: the average cost of an undergraduate degree in Germany is $32,000, paid for by the state. In the U.S., some schools charge that much for one year, and student loan debt has surpassed $1.2 trillion.
                 <p>
                  South 160 miles from the University of Cologne, tucked in the heart of Heidelbergs quaint but vibrant city center, the University of Heidelberg offers a full program of courses from ancient history to biochemistry. It is one of Germanys oldest and most prestigious institutions.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  A majority of German voters agree that a decent start in life includes the possibility of a free higher education, says Frieder Wolf, a political science professor at the school.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  To limit spending, Wolf says, professors teach more and earn less than their American colleagues.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "This is not to complain. I love my job and I have a lot of freedom but this is how we keep costs down  larger classrooms, Wolf says. Weve got courses with 40 participants, 50 participants in the social sciences, where [American universities] might have tutorials of four or five students.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  And unlike their American counterparts, German universities have very little administrative bloat.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "Many administrative tasks for which you would have specialized personnel in the States is done by the teachers and professors here, Wolf says.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   The trade-off
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  But are German students getting the same quality learning experience?
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Germany isn't widely known for having
                  <a href="http://www.usnews.com/education/best-global-universities/rankings" target="_blank">
                   top-tier
                  </a>
                  colleges like Harvard, Yale and Princeton. But, says Wolf, what it does have is reliable quality."
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "With all due respect, [America has] the best colleges but [it also has] some of the worst, Wolf says. Theres probably a new sort of class divide between people who get there and who dont get there, where as in Germany basically most everybody who wants to go to an average college can go there and get a decent education."
                  <br/>
                 </p>
                 <div class="media media-element-container media-default">
                  <img alt="" class="media-element file-default lazy" data-src="http://www.marketplace.org/sites/default/files/Germany-16.jpg" height="100" src="/sites/default/themes/marketplacev2Responsive/images/pixel.gif" style="float: right;" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="300"/>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-description field-type-text-long field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-byline-text field-type-text field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 <p>
                  In the U.S., the closest comparison to Germanys no-frills, low-cost higher education is probably state and community colleges.
                  <a href="http://www.marketplace.org/topics/education/learningcurve/obama-proposes-free-community-college-plan" target="_blank">
                   President Obama has proposed making two years of community college tuition-free
                  </a>
                  for students who keep their grades up, but Republican leaders in Congress have plans to stall that plan.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "Its a very big commitment, says Sandy Baum, a higher education economist with the
                  <a href="http://www.urban.org/" target="_blank">
                   Urban Institute
                  </a>
                  . People want it to be free, but they dont really mean they want to pay higher taxes to make it free."
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Baum says Americans also don't want to give up the residential college experience, with all its bells and whistles. But, she says, the U.S. needs more affordable choices.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "In many European universities, you go and you listen to a lecture and that is what is involved in the university, Baum says. Its a lot cheaper to do that than the many things that people are asking for on college campuses here. And people are voting with their feet, and we need to have multiple options."
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Baum says those options should include more online learning and apprenticeships.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong>
                   Willing to pay, despite free tuition
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Still, there are German families who, despite the promise of free college, are willing to pay for the elite American experience.
                 </p>
                 <div class="media media-element-container media-default">
                  <img alt="Jane Park and two of her three children in their home in Essen, Germany. " class="media-element file-default lazy" data-src="http://www.marketplace.org/sites/default/files/Germany-42.jpg" height="400" src="/sites/default/themes/marketplacev2Responsive/images/pixel.gif" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-description field-type-text-long field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                     <p>
                      <em>
                       Jane Park and two of her three children in their home in Essen, Germany.
                      </em>
                     </p>
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="field field-name-field-byline-text field-type-text field-label-hidden multi-value-field">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item first even">
                     Photo by Mallory Noe-Payne/WGBH
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                 <p>
                  <span style="line-height: 1.42857em;">
                   Johannes Kim and Jane Park live in Essen, a neighboring city 50 miles north of Cologne. In the kitchen one recent evening, as Park prepares dinner, their three small children were listening to an opera lesson on tape.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <span style="line-height: 1.42857em;">
                   Kim graduated from the University of Heidelberg and he thinks the opportunity to build relationships with professors is something you really can't put a price tag on. He wants their kids to attend schools with strong brand recognition.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "The American college experience is something that instills some sort of emotional bond to your university. That is something that is completely missing from the German system, Kim says. Although I went to the oldest university in Germany, there is not the feeling that I'm a proud alumni or graduate of that school."
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Park, who is Korean-American, finds Germany's tuition-free model appealing, though she's conflicted about the American system.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "I like to think that I have a strong sense of social justice and great education is almost reserved for the elite despite scholarship opportunities and financial aid, Park says. That, I find disturbing and in some ways I am squaring, 'OK, do I want to feed my children into that sort of system?
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Their kids are young, so it will be a while before they go to college. And by that time, there's no guarantee that Germany will still be committed to the idea of free college education.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  German states are on a five-year deadline to balance their budgets, meaning states, and taxpayers, will be taking a close look at what they can afford.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <em>
                   <strong>
                    This is part one in a WGBH series that examines higher education in Germany, and compares it to the challenges we face here in the U.S.
                    <a href="http://www.marketplace.org/topics/education/learning-curve/american-students-head-germany-free-college" target="_blank">
                     Part two can be found here
                    </a>
                    <br/>
                   </strong>
                  </em>
                 </p>
                </div>
