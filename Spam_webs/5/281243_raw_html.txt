        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignright" height="250" src="http://i247.photobucket.com/albums/gg158/MDA2008/MDA2009/766px-Leptin.png" title="Structure of w:Leptin, PDB id 1AX8, generated with w:UCSF Chimera" width="320"/>
         I mentioned a few of the working hypotheses for
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/leptin/" target="_self" title="A Primal Primer: Leptin">
          avoiding leptin resistance
         </a>
         in my posts last week, including
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/leptin-fructose-appetite/" target="_self" title="The Many Faces of the Sugar Monster">
          fructose
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/lectins/" target="_self" title="The Lowdown on Lectin">
          lectin
         </a>
         avoidance, getting adequate nightly sleep, and eating at or above maintenance levels  or at least throwing in periodic
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/carb-refeeding-and-weight-loss/" target="_self" title="Carb Refeeding and Weight Loss">
          carb refeeds
         </a>
          but theres even more involved in establishing a good relationship with your leptin levels and leptin sensitivity. Depending on your general approach to life, this could be good news or bad news. Its either more stuff to worry about, or more ways to buttress your health. As I alluded to last week, experimenting with carb refeeding and leptin manipulation within the broader Primal Blueprint eating style is butter on the steak (because icing on the cake just wont do for us Primal folks).
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-13119">
         </span>
        </p>
        <h4>
         (Excessive, Heavy-handed, Omega-6 Influenced) Inflammation and Leptin Resistance
        </h4>
        <p>
         As if you needed another reason to avoid chronic, systemic inflammation (heart disease, weight gain, and cancer werent enough for ya?), it seems that inflammation also plays a role in leptin resistance.
        </p>
        <p>
         Excessive inflammation has always been strongly associated with
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/diabetes/" target="_self" title="The Definitive Guide to Insulin, Blood Sugar &amp; Type 2 Diabetes (and youll understand it)">
          insulin resistance
         </a>
         , obesity, and other hallmarks of the metabolic syndrome. It figures into pretty much everything we do and everything we experience.
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/category/lift-heavy-things/?submit=view" target="_self" title="Lifting Heavy Things Category">
          Lifting heavy things
         </a>
         and breaking down muscle fibers elicits an inflammatory response. Its beneficial if we do enough, but harmful if we do too much.
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/inflammation/" target="_self" title="What's All This Talk About Inflammation?">
          Inflammation
         </a>
         helps heal wounds; its how our body responds to stressors. Too much inflammation can cause disease, but inadequate amounts can also be dangerous. Leading a healthy, productive existence, then, requires finding a balance between pro-and-anti-inflammatory states. The average SAD eater dealing with
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/how-stress-can-make-you-fat/" target="_self" title="How Stress Can Make You Fat">
          stress
         </a>
         induces entirely too much stress on his or her body, so we can safely assume that pro-inflammatory states are the issue here.
        </p>
        <p>
         Interleukin-6 (IL-6) is one of the more potent agents of the bodys inflammatory response. This particular cytokine is well-known for mediating fevers and managing the acute phase response of our immune system. When tissue concentrations of fat are
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/omega-3-fatty-acid/" target="_self" title="Omega 3:6 Ratio">
          weighted toward omega-6
         </a>
         (like most people eating a modern diet high in omega-6 fats),
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12578976?dopt=Abstract" target="_blank" title="Differential effects of prostaglandin derived from omega-6 and omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids on COX-2 expression and IL-6 secretion.">
          excessive amounts of IL-6 are created
         </a>
         in the inflammatory response. IL-6 is necessary, sure, but (surprise, surprise) too much is dangerous. Remember, these arent intelligent micro-organisms making decisions based on your welfare; theyre simply doing what theyre programmed to do by the signals you help provide. If you unleash a horde of IL-6, theyre going to wreak havoc because thats what theyre supposed to do. Everything has a reaction, of course, and the bodys reaction to IL-6 (especially large amounts of it) is to release something called suppressor of cytokine signaling (SOCS), which is designed to downregulate cytokine activity once the job is done.
        </p>
        <p>
         SOCS-3, a variety of SOCS, has an interesting relationship to leptin. After leptin has been secreted and runs its course, SOCS-3 is released to bind to the leptin receptor and downregulate leptin signaling. This is normal, physiological leptin resistance; its not necessarily pathological. Mice without the genes for SOCS-3 synthesis, for example,
         <a href="http://www.nature.com/nm/journal/v10/n7/abs/nm1072.html" target="_blank" title="Enhanced leptin sensitivity and attenuation of diet-induced obesity in mice with haploinsufficiency of Socs3">
          are remarkably sensitive to leptin
         </a>
         , and obese, hyperleptinemic,
         <a href="http://www.springerlink.com/content/01125u6h41982562/" target="_blank" title=" Inhibition of SOCS-3 in adipocytes of rats with diet-induced obesity increases leptin-mediated fatty acid oxidation ">
          leptin-resistant rats were given SOCS-3 inhibitors
         </a>
         (an inhibitor of a suppressant; is that a double negative?) to increase fat burning and lose weight. But when youve got a guy or gal eating a terrible diet and leading an unhealthy lifestyle with high tissue concentrations of omega-6 fatty acids, suffering from steady levels of systemic inflammation which release tons of IL-6 and leptin receptor-binding SOCS-3, leptin resistance becomes pathological.
        </p>
        <p>
         Minimize omega-6 intake, cut out the
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/healthy-oils/" target="_self" title="Vegetable Oils">
          vegetable oils
         </a>
         , and
         <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16239836?ordinalpos=1&amp;itool=EntrezSystem2.PEntrez.Pubmed.Pubmed_ResultsPanel.Pubmed_SingleItemSupl.Pubmed_Discovery_RA&amp;linkpos=4&amp;log$=relatedreviews&amp;logdbfrom=pubmed" target="_blank" title="Leptin sensitivity in skeletal muscle is modulated by diet and exercise.">
          restore the tissue balance
         </a>
         by
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/salmon-factory-farm-vs-wild/" target="_self" title="Salmon: Factory Farm vs. Wild">
          eating fish
         </a>
         or
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/fish-oil-health-benefits/" target="_self" title="The Definitive Guide to Fish Oils">
          taking fish oil
         </a>
         (ignore the word of caution regarding obesity causing high fat diets, for yours is certainly not) so that your inflammatory response system is more reasonable  and less apt to wreak collateral damage. Avoid excessive levels of inflammation, whether diet-induced or exercise-induced. No
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/chronic-cardio-2/" target="_self" title="Chronic Cardio">
          Chronic Cardio
         </a>
         , no
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/how-to-deal-with-overtraining/" target="_self" title="How to Deal with Overtraining">
          overtraining
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Okay, the big one is out of the way. Heres another, smaller consideration to keep in mind.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Leptin Resistance and Insulin Resistance
        </h4>
        <p>
         If you remember from last time, refeeds are to be carb-based, rather than fat or protein-based, because carbohydrate stimulates greater leptin secretion. Why is that? Insulin stimulates leptin release. Yes,
         <a href="http://jcem.endojournals.org/cgi/content/abstract/94/8/2900" target="_blank" title="Insulin-Stimulated Increase in Serum Leptin Levels Precedes and Correlates with Weight Gain during Insulin Therapy in Type 2 Diabetes">
          insulin begets leptin
         </a>
         . Insulin releases in response to food intake,
         <a href="http://ajpcell.physiology.org/cgi/content/full/283/1/C244" target="_blank" title="Mechanisms of leptin secretion from white adipocytes ">
          leptin releases in response to insulin
         </a>
         , and the rest of the body knows that energy levels are adequate. In the healthy individual, this arrangement goes swimmingly, and appetite is reduced. The problem, of course, is that nature never envisioned an epidemic of insulin resistance. See, most folks trying do the right thing by CW to get healthy and lose weight are insulin resistant with high serum levels of circulating insulin. Chronic levels of insulin are going to produce chronic levels of leptin. Hello, leptin resistance.
        </p>
        <p>
         Of course,
         <a href="http://wholehealthsource.blogspot.com/2008/04/leptin-and-lectins.html" target="_blank" title="Leptin and Lectins">
          elevated serum leptin predicts the onset of obesity and insulin resistance
         </a>
         , so this is just another one of those vicious hormonal cycles in which modern eaters seem to find themselves entangled. Leptin resistance (from lectins, or elevated triglycerides, or systemic inflammation and omega-6 heavy tissue composition) leads to insulin resistance leads to more leptin resistance leads to you see where this is going.
        </p>
        <p>
         Researchers are still teasing out the full extent of leptins impact on the bodys inner workings, so I imagine therell be more posts in the future. Until then, sleep well, exercise smartly, eat Primally, and pass on the sugar, stress, seed oils, and spelt (yeah, yeah, its an obscure, archaic grain, but I felt like alliterating).
