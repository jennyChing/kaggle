              <b class="whb">
               Know what size you need.
              </b>
              Test this by holding your chin on the chin rest. The chin rest is the black piece towards the bottom of the fingerboard. Most newer violins have one, but some people take them off or an older violin may simply not have one. Getting one attached is a simple matter of going to a music store or a violin instructor. Then, grab the scroll. The scroll is the elegantly carved piece at the very end of the fingerboard.
              <ul>
               <li>
                Once you are holding it, if your arm is stretched out but you aren't straining to reach it, that's your size. The sizes are Full, Three-Quarter, Half, and Quarter. Keep in mind, however, if you're between 9 and 18, you may want to buy a Full size one. That way when you're finished growing for a while you won't need a new one right away, it's pretty easy to play one a size big. If you're a little kid and you need a Quarter, a Half will most likely accommodate your size for a little bit longer.

              <b class="whb">
               Learn the anatomy of your instrument.
              </b>
              It goes as follows. The body is the basic wooden structure. The fingerboard is the long, often black, piece containing most of the strings. The scroll is the end piece, which contains the peg box, where the strings are attached to the violin. F-holes are the black scrolling openings on either side. These allow the vibrations to escape from the sound post, the part that is responsible for turning vibrations into music. The bridge is where the strings run over on their way down. This is connected to the internal sound post, causing the vibrating strings to make the whole violin come to life and make music appreciated worldwide.

         <div class="section tips sticky ">
          <h2>
           <span class="mw-headline" id="Tips">
            Tips
           </span>
          </h2>
          <div class="section_text" id="tips">
           <ul>
            <li>
             A good violin is useless without a good bow, like having the intelligence to be a rocket scientist but being unable to go to a good college! Fancy bows can cost tens of thousands, but that's not for the bow, that's for embellishments like precious gems or colored horsehair. You'll find a good one for twenty or thirty dollars anywhere you find a violin.
            </li>
            <li>
             Care for the bow as well as you care for the violin. Keep resin handy. Resin (say it RAW-sin)is a hardened sap rubbed over the bow hair to make it pull on the strings. Good blocks of the stuff run for around ten dollars in music shops. It's not sticky, except during a summer heatwave, in which case it's still only mildly annoying. If it cracks, hold it above boiling water. Once it melts into one piece again, cool it off rapidly. You have manufactured new resin, congratulations!
            </li>
            <li>
             Buy a good violin. Good instruments DON'T come from Wal-Mart or Target, not Sears either. While you might be able to get away with their electric guitars or drum sets or keyboards for your garage band, this isn't the case for violins. As a rule of thumb, anything played in an orchestra will need to come from a music store to be good quality. That includes woodwinds (flutes, clarinets, oboes, saxophones), non-guitar string instruments (violin, viola, bass, cello), and brass (trumpets, tubas, trombones, French horns). Be wary of Internet ads. If you use one, DON'T USE AMAZON UNLESS THE SELLER IS REPUTABLE! Click on the link to the seller's personal website. If it's a violin website or it's a used violin for sale, inquire further. If not, keep looking. Amazon does have some quality violins for reasonable prices, but you need to be careful and do your homework. Use a violin/instrument Website. I suggest www.shar.com because they have a wonderful selection including all sizes and even colored violins in all colors imaginable. Bear in mind however, if you plan to become a professional, some orchestras discourage colored violins. But for school, casual performances or your own personal entertainment, go ahead and buy a purple, blue or lime green one if it makes you happy. If you have questions, contact an instructor or even an orchestra in your area to see what they recommend.
            </li>
            <li>
             Be prepared to pay upwards of one hundred dollars when you purchase a violin. It may be expensive now, but you will be thanked later by its longer life and less need for repair, provided you take care of it. Violins under $100 are not necessarily cheap, but realize that you get what you pay for.
            </li>
            <li>
             Over time, any instrument will become your friend. You know it like no one else. It will always be there for you in times of trial for you to play on. Over the years, it may make you a buck or two, or help you make people's day. If you play it with a group, or bring it with you in your travels, whether in a 5-Star hotel as a celeb or on the streets of Queens as a hobo or runaway, you can make a few cents with it and name it, even talk to it.
            </li>
            <li>
             Care for your instrument. Furniture polish can be harmful; a monthly wipe down with a water-dampened cloth only will suffice. You will need to keep it fairly humid inside the case as well. If it gets too dry in there, the wood will crack. That's a defective violin and the damage can't be repaired! Wet a sponge, smash it all up, put it in a film container with a hole in the top and leave that in your case at all times.
            </li>
           </ul>

         <div class="section warnings sticky ">
          <h2>
           <span class="mw-headline" id="Warnings">
            Warnings
           </span>
          </h2>
          <div class="section_text" id="warnings">
           <ul>
            <li>
             Do not assume your violin is protected simply by being in its case. Any impact on the case can damage it and possibly its contents. When your case suffers any impact, replace it. It will no longer do any good in keeping the instrument safe.
            </li>
            <li>
             Never buy from department stores and be very sure of what you are getting online.
            </li>
            <li>
             Never let someone make fun of you for playing a "dorky" instrument. They might not know it, but realize in your own conscience that the violin is played and cherished for its music around the world, from Italy to Ireland to Latin America.
            </li>
            <li>
             Don't automatically buy a used one on the assumption it's cheaper, much like the way a used car is cheaper versus a new car. Check to see WHY the person selling is selling. If it got too small, then get more info. If they don't say, look someplace else.
            </li>
            <li>
             Keep your violin hydrated like you would a child. Unlike kids, violins don't get 'dehydrated' easily, but when they do, the damage is permanent.
            </li>
            <li>
             Always handle your full case as if you were handling the bare violin.
            </li>
            <li>
             If you'd like to learn tricks or styles from a particular country, still learn the basics. Buying a "How to Play Irish Violin Music" book for your very first time won't do you any good if you don't know E-String from D-String.
            </li>
            <li>
             Keep in mind that most professional orchestras don't hire lefties-they could stab someone in the eye with their bow, so lefties looking to play with others, find an all-lefty group. No offense, just preparing you if you decide to go pro. Of course, if you can train your right hand to control the bow as well as your left hand, go for it. Congratulations, you've just made yourself ambidextrous (able to write with both hands)
            </li>
            <li>
             As a rule, treat it like a bike helmet.
            </li>
           </ul>
