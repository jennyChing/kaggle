    <h1>
     About Digital Collections
    </h1>
    <p>
     Digital Collections contains
     <a href="/search/index?utf8=%E2%9C%93&amp;keywords=">
      834,438 items
     </a>
     and counting. While that's a small fraction of the New York Public Library's overall holdings, the aim of Digital Collections is to provide context for the materials we have digitized and to inspire people to use and reuse the media and data on offer here to advance knowledge and create new works.
    </p>
    <p>
     Spanning a wide range of historical eras, geography, and media, NYPL Digital Collections offers drawings, illuminated manuscripts, maps, photographs, posters, prints, rare illustrated books, videos, audio, and more. Encompassing the subject strengths of the vast collections of The Library, these materials represent the applied sciences, fine and decorative arts, history, performing arts, and social sciences.
    </p>
    <p>
     You can
     <a href="http://digitalcollections.nypl.org/">
      search
     </a>
     if you have something in mind, or you can start browsing directly in a number of ways:
    </p>
    <ul>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://digitalcollections.nypl.org/search/index?utf8=%E2%9C%93&amp;keywords=">
        Items
       </a>
      </strong>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://digitalcollections.nypl.org/collections">
        Collections
       </a>
      </strong>
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://digitalcollections.nypl.org/divisions">
        Divisions
       </a>
      </strong>
     </li>
    </ul>
    <p>
     For a more extensive user guide and primer to Digital Collections, please see our blog post, "
     <a href="http://www.nypl.org/blog/2015/01/21/digital-collections-platform-intro">
      NYPL Digital Collections Platform: An Introduction
     </a>
     ."
    </p>
    <p>
     <a name="overview">
     </a>
    </p>
    <h3>
     What does "Digital Collections" mean?
    </h3>
    <p>
     Right now, what we mean by "Digital Collections" is reformatted and digital surrogates of materials held among the physical holdings of the
     <a href="http://www.nypl.org/">
      New York Public Library
     </a>
     , across all of its divisions. This includes digital images of visual materials, texts, streaming video and more.
    </p>
    <p>
     It's important to note, however, that "Digital Collections" is just the tip of the iceberg. Even in the cases where we've got great coverage, just a fraction of NYPL's collections are represented here.
    </p>
    <p>
     That's why, wherever possible, we link items in our Digital Collections platform to further resources where you may be able to find out more about the objects and, importantly, find more intersections with other relevant materials.
    </p>
    <p>
     Some of the places we link to:
    </p>
    <ul>
     <li>
      The
      <strong>
       <a href="http://catalog.nypl.org/">
        NYPL Catalog
       </a>
      </strong>
      (essentially, our inventory tracking system across all divisions)
     </li>
     <li>
      The
      <strong>
       <a href="http://archives.nypl.org/">
        NYPL Archives Portal
       </a>
      </strong>
      (describing 10,000 archival and manuscript collections comprising over 50,000 linear feet of material in nearly every format imaginable)
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://dp.la/">
        The Digital Public Library of America
       </a>
      </strong>
      (a national effort that makes NYPL collections searchable along with those of hundreds of other institutions)
     </li>
    </ul>
    <p>
     Meanwhile, we're also hard at work extracting data from historical sources and materials, and we've incorporated contextual links to those data extraction experiments wherever possible. Some of those include:
    </p>
    <ul>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://maps.nypl.org/warper/">
        The Map Warper
       </a>
      </strong>
      (See
      <a href="/items/510d47e2-086e-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99">
       an example
      </a>
      )
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://buildinginspector.nypl.org/">
        Building Inspector
       </a>
      </strong>
      (See
      <a href="/items/510d47e0-bf3e-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99">
       an example
      </a>
      )
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://stereo.nypl.org/">
        Stereogranimator
       </a>
      </strong>
      (See
      <a href="/items/5e66b3e8-f484-d471-e040-e00a180654d7">
       an example
      </a>
      )
     </li>
     <li>
      <strong>
       <a href="http://menus.nypl.org/">
        What's on the Menu
       </a>
      </strong>
      (See
      <a href="/items/c1e518e3-db8f-5ef2-e040-e00a18063c7a">
       an example
      </a>
      )
     </li>
    </ul>
    <p>
     See our
     <a href="#use">
      Data and Image Reuse
     </a>
     section below for more information about how you can work with the raw data from the projects listed above.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a name="history">
     </a>
    </p>
    <h3>
     Where Did Digital Collections Come From?
    </h3>
    <p>
     In March 2005, the New York Public Library debuted a site called Digital Gallery, which featured 275,000 images from the library's collections. Since then, the Library has added hundreds of thousands of images to the repository, along with metadata records providing context for the materials.
    </p>
    <p>
     Over time, however, we reconsidered the purpose of a site showcasing digitized materials to aid in research and spur creative reuse, and we increasingly felt that the concept of a passive "gallery" was insufficient to encompass all that we wanted to accomplish.
    </p>
    <p>
     So in the summer of 2013, the NYPL Technology Digital Repository Team paired up with NYPL Labs to create a sustainable, modern replacement to the decade-old Digital Gallery, and thus Digital Collections was born!
    </p>
    <p>
     <a name="details">
     </a>
    </p>
    <h3>
     How Does Digital Collections Work?
    </h3>
    <p>
     NYPL uses Fedora Commons software for our repository, while our bibliographic, technical, and rights metadata is managed by a custom Ruby on Rails app we call, appropriately enough, the Metadata Management System.  Our Digital Collections platform itself is also a Rails application, but uses a lot of JavaScript (including libraries like the
     <a href="https://openlibrary.org/dev/docs/bookreader">
      Internet Archive's BookReader
     </a>
     ).
    </p>
    <p>
     For more info, email
     <a href="mailto:digitalcollections@nypl.org">
      digitalcollections@nypl.org
     </a>
    </p>

    <h2>
     Using Images
    </h2>
    <p>
     The New York Public Library is actively reviewing and labeling materials in our Digital Collections with statements that indicate how you may reuse the image, and what sort of permission, if any, you need to do so.
    </p>
    <p>
     <a name="no-us-copyright-restrictions">
     </a>
    </p>
    <h3>
     What does no known US copyright restrictions" mean?
    </h3>
    <p>
     That label means exactly that: We believe that the items marked as such have no known US copyright restrictions. You do not need NYPLs permission to reuse these materials. However, the items may still be subject to rights of privacy, rights of publicity, and other restrictions depending on the format of the materials and what the items depict. It is your responsibility to make sure that you respect these rights.
    </p>
    <p>
     Though its not required, if you can, please credit us as the source by using the cite this item preformatted citation at the bottom of every item page. Doing so helps us track how our collections are used and helps justify freely releasing even more content in the future.
    </p>
    <p>
     Looking for lots of images you can reuse freely? You can
     <a href="/search/index?filters%5Brights%5D=pd&amp;keywords=">
      browse just the items that have no known US copyright restrictions
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <p>
     <a name="copyright-nypl">
     </a>
    </p>
    <h3>
     What does Copyright The New York Public Library mean?
    </h3>
    <p>
     The New York Public Library holds or manages the copyright for a small number collections on this site. If you need information about reusing these items, please contact
     <a href="http://www.nypl.org/help/get-what-you-need/photographic-services/obtaining-images">
      Permissions and Reproductions
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <h3>
     What about the rest?
    </h3>
    <p>
     We're working on itevery day, we review as many items as possible for public use and reuse. In many cases, it takes a lot of research to allow us to make a confident statement that materials have no known US copyright restrictions. At this time, we're not able to take requests about which items are at the top of our queue for review, but the materials we are reviewing will have updated item labels within a few hours of a determination being made.
    </p>
    <p>
     If you need more information about reusing item in our Digital Collections, please contact
     <a href="http://www.nypl.org/help/get-what-you-need/photographic-services/obtaining-images">
      Permissions and Reproductions
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <p>
     <a name="data">
     </a>
    </p>
    <h2>
     Using Data
    </h2>
    <p>
     All of NYPL's data published via the tools below is released under a
     <a href="https://creativecommons.org/publicdomain/zero/1.0/">
      Creative Commons CC0 1.0 Universal Public Domain Dedication
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <p>
     There are a lots of ways to access and reuse the data weve gathered about our collections:
    </p>
    <ul>
     <li>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Bulk metadata download
       </strong>
       <a name="bulk-download">
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       Digital Collections metadata records are available for bulk download via the
       <a href="http://dp.la/info/developers/download/">
        Digital Public Library of America
       </a>
       (DPLA). This includes all publicly available record descriptions for items on this site. The metadata standard we use is MODS (What is
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metadata_Object_Description_Schema">
        MODS
       </a>
       ?), and the bulk data offered through DPLA is stored in JSON. The bulk download data is refreshed roughly every other month.
      </p>
     </li>
     <li>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Metadata API
       </strong>
       <a name="api">
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       All Digital Collections metadata is also available via
       <a href="http://api.repo.nypl.org/">
        The New York Public Library Digital Collections API
       </a>
       . This data is available in XML and JSON.
      </p>
     </li>
     <li>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Geographic data
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Map Warper data, including TIF and KML files, is available via item pages on the
        <a href="http://maps.nypl.org/warper/">
         NYPL Map Warper
        </a>
         a tool for "geo-rectification," or digitally aligning ("rectifying") historical maps from the NYPL's collections to match today's precise maps.
       </li>
       <li>
        Data about historical NYC buildings is available via the
        <a href="http://buildinginspector.nypl.org/general/data">
         NYPL Building Inspector
        </a>
        , a set of tool designed by The Library to extract, correct and analyze data from historical maps.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </li>
     <li>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Menus data
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       All data generated through
       <a href="http://menus.nypl.org">
        <em>
         What's on the Menu?
        </em>
       </a>
       is available in two ways: via
       <a href="http://menus.nypl.org/data">
        CSV export
       </a>
       , and via the
       <a href="http://nypl.github.io/menus-api/">
        Menus API
       </a>
      </p>
     </li>
    </ul>
    <p>
     For more information about the projects above, see the individual project pages, or contact
     <a href="mailto:DigitalCollections@nypl.org">
      DigitalCollections@nypl.org
     </a>
     .
    </p>
