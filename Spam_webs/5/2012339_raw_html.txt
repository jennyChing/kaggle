      <article class="post-17817 post type-post status-publish format-standard category-destinations tag-argentina tag-festivals tag-girlfriend-getaways tag-south-america entry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         Celebrations in Argentina
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2011-10-31T10:23:00+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          October 31, 2011
         </time>
         By
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.travelbelles.com/author/katystewart/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Katy Stewart
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <h3 style="text-align: center;">
         With a calendar full of festivals, Argentineans are pros at making every day a celebration
        </h3>
        <p>
         <a href="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/15642702_wsMxqh#1559036981_3xRrDsv-A-LB" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug">
          <img alt="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" class="aligncenter" src="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/i-3xRrDsv/0/M/A-Street-Performance-in-Salta-M.jpg" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         In Argentina, people like to party  and I mean
         <em>
          really
         </em>
         like to party. Instead of waiting sensibly for big annual festivals to come around, they jump at any excuse to get together for some music,
         <a href="http://www.travelbelles.com/2011/10/bright-lights-and-tango-steps-in-la-bonita-buenos-aires/" title="Bright Lights and Tango Steps in la Bonita Buenos Aires">
          tango
         </a>
         , food and fun.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          I recently spent five weeks in
          <a href="http://www.travelbelles.com/2011/10/bright-lights-and-tango-steps-in-la-bonita-buenos-aires/" title="Bright Lights and Tango Steps in la Bonita Buenos Aires">
           Argentina
          </a>
          and there was at least one national festival per week, often more.
         </strong>
         Although this makes the work ethic a little relaxed, it creates a wonderfully happy atmosphere wherever you go, because there is always something to celebrate.
        </p>
        <figure class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 600px;">
         <a href="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/15642702_wsMxqh#1559036960_wCwLQbx-A-LB" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug">
          <img alt="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" height="450" src="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/i-wCwLQbx/0/M/A-festive-pasteleria-M.jpg" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
          Friends Day is celebrated with cakes! Even for breakfast!
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         Just after I arrived in
         <strong>
          Salta
         </strong>
         , a city in the Andean North region, I was informed that everyone had to bring cake into work the next day. I assumed it was someones birthday, but it turned out to be Friends Day, when everyone buys cake and cards for their pals and celebrates friendship.
         <strong>
          We had cake for breakfast and spent the rest of the day feeling super cheerful. I realized then that I was going to like Argentina.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         One evening the following week, I happened to wander down to the main square in Salta just after midnight, on my way to meet a friend. Argentineans are notorious for starting their nights out at a ridiculously late hour! It was something of a surprise to see the square full of people, lights and music, and a costume parade appeared to be underway.
        </p>
        <p>
         I had no idea what it was for, but the giant fish costumes were certainly interesting, and a little creepy. I asked the lady next to me, who said it was a celebration of Inca culture, though that didnt quite explain the fish. They were followed by dancing troupes and more animal-themed groups. The restaurants and cafes all around the square were full to bursting, and the party went on and on through the night.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/15642702_wsMxqh#1559037962_BhNd2qm-A-LB" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug">
          <img alt="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" class="aligncenter" src="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/i-BhNd2qm/0/M/Fish-costume-at-Saltas-M.jpg" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         It cant have been any more than a few days later that the
         <strong>
          National
          <a href="http://www.travelbelles.com/2012/10/traditional-foods-argentina/" title="Traditional Foods in Argentina that are not Steak">
           Empanada
          </a>
          Festival,
         </strong>
         a major
         <a href="http://www.travelbelles.com/2010/05/fried-foods-and-the-southern-festival/" title="Fried Foods and the Southern Festival">
          food festival
         </a>
         ,took place, celebrating Argentinas favorite street food snack. An empanada is a little pastry parcel stuffed with things like meat, chicken, eggs and vegetables  and Argentineans go crazy for them.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the company of some new friends, I went to the citys Empanada Competition. It was held in a large outdoor exhibition area, where hundreds of food stalls vied for customers while the results of the winning empanada makers were being announced over a loud speaker.
        </p>
        <figure class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 600px;">
         <a href="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/15642702_wsMxqh#1559036895_rBPqgkM-A-LB" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug">
          <img alt="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" height="443" src="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/i-rBPqgkM/0/M/A-chef-at-the-Empanada-M.jpg" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
          National Empanada Festival
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         We took a seat at one of the trestle tables and were quickly served a plate of empanadas, as well as a pot of quince jelly and walnuts. Although no stall varied from this theme, each announced that it had empanadas with a kind of surprised delight, as if they were the last thing you would expect to find there.
        </p>
        <p>
         The stage where a folk band had just started playing was lost behind a haze of heat coming from the deep pans where the freshly-rolled dough parcels were being fried.
         <strong>
          Everywhere, people were eating, drinking and being merry,
         </strong>
         wrapped up against the winter chill and polishing off countless numbers of empanadas and plastic cups of wine. It felt like a Christmas market without the craft stalls  and in August.
        </p>
        <p>
         It was September before
         <a href="http://www.travelbelles.com/2011/10/bright-lights-and-tango-steps-in-la-bonita-buenos-aires/" title="Bright Lights and Tango Steps in la Bonita Buenos Aires">
          I made my way to Buenos Aires
         </a>
         and although the air was still crisp and cool, the first blossoms were appearing on the trees and the sun was shining brightly. Everywhere I looked, people were holding flowers and stalls with the blooming plants took over the pavements.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/15642702_wsMxqh#1559037845_SQsh76g-A-LB" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug">
          <img alt="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug" class="aligncenter" src="http://gomarwrites.smugmug.com/Travel/Katy/i-SQsh76g/0/M/Dia-de-la-Primavera-flowers-M.jpg" title="Photo &amp; Video Sharing by SmugMug"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         At first I just thought they must really like their fresh flowers, but then a man passed me and grinned.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Feliz Dia de Primavera! he greeted me, joyfully. Happy first day of spring.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         As I turned a corner, I encountered a banner strung between lampposts declaring the same thing at the entrance to a park. Shop windows were decorated with floral stickers and the flower sellers were doing a roaring trade.
         <strong>
          Restaurants advertised special
          <em>
           primavera
          </em>
          deals and the whole city was ablaze with color and life. All of this just because it was officially the first day of spring.
         </strong>
         Where Im from, that kind of thing goes by completely unnoticed.
        </p>
        <p>
         Argentina was unforgettable for so many reasons, but perhaps the greatest thing I learned there was to
         <strong>
          celebrate the ordinary as well as the extraordinary.
         </strong>
         Celebrating things like friendship and empanadas on a national scale may very well just be a clever marketing ploy, but its lovely.
        </p>
        <p>
         Its still an ordinary day with work and errands, but you do it all with a spring in your step because you get to eat a load of empanadas or because somebody gives you a flower. With that kind of thing happening all the time, its no wonder the people are so inexhaustibly cheerful. Theres just no time for doom and gloom, because the next reason to celebrate is just around the corner.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
