      <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">
       Basque Autonomous Community
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      . It lies on the coast of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">
       Bay of Biscay
      </a>
      , 20km (12 miles) from the
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       French
      </a>
      border. Capital city of
      <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
       Gipuzkoa
      </a>
      , the municipality's population is 186,409 (2012), with its metropolitan area reaching 436,500 (2010).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Locals call themselves
      <i>
       donostiarra
      </i>
      (singular), both in
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
       Spanish
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
       Basque
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The main economic activities are commerce and tourism, and it is one of the most famous tourist destinations in Spain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite the citys small size, events such as the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_International_Film_Festival" title="San Sebastin International Film Festival">
       San Sebastin International Film Festival
      </a>
      have given it an international dimension. San Sebastin, along with
      <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocaw">
       Wrocaw
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      , will be the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Capital_of_Culture" title="European Capital of Culture">
       European Capital of Culture
      </a>
      in 2016.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-european_capital_of_culture_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-european_capital_of_culture-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In spite of appearances, both the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_(language)" title="Basque (language)">
       Basque
      </a>
      form
      <i>
       Donostia
      </i>
      and the Spanish form
      <i>
       San Sebastin
      </i>
      have the same meaning of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Sebastian" title="Saint Sebastian">
       Saint Sebastian
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       dona/dono/doni
      </i>
      element in Basque place-names signifies "saint" and is derived from
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      <i>
       domine
      </i>
      ; the second part of
      <i>
       Dono(stia)
      </i>
      contains a shortened form of the saint's name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trask-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
       Geography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; width:99%; max-width:808px;">
      <div class="thumbinner">
       <div class="overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg" title="Panoramic view of the Bay of La Concha (Baha de la Concha/Kontxako Badia) at day">
         <img alt="" data-file-height="1246" data-file-width="4500" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg/800px-Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg/1200px-Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg/1600px-Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg 2x" width="800"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Donostia_Igeldotik.jpg" title="File:Donostia Igeldotik.jpg">
         </a>
        </div>
        Panoramic view of the Bay of La Concha (Baha de la Concha/Kontxako Badia) at day
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The city is in the north of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(historical_territory)" title="Basque Country (historical territory)">
       Basque Country
      </a>
      , on the southern coast of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">
       Bay of Biscay
      </a>
      . San Sebastin's picturesque shoreline makes it a popular beach resort. The seaside environment is enhanced by hilly surroundings that are easily accessible, i.e.,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Urgull" title="Urgull">
        Urgull
       </a>
      </i>
      (at the heart of the city by the seashore), romantic
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ulia" title="Mount Ulia">
        Mount Ulia
       </a>
      </i>
      extending east to
      <a href="/wiki/Pasaia" title="Pasaia">
       Pasaia
      </a>
      , Mount
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Adarra" title="Adarra">
        Adarra
       </a>
      </i>
      rising proud far on the south and
      <i>
       Igeldo
      </i>
      , overlooking the bay from the west.
     </p>
     <p>
      The city sits at the mouth of the River
      <a href="/wiki/Urumea" title="Urumea">
       Urumea
      </a>
      , Donostia having been built to a large extent over the river's wetlands during the last two centuries. In fact, the city centre and the districts of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donostia#Amara_Berri" title="Donostia">
        Amara Berri
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Riberas de Loiola
      </i>
      lie on such terrain and the former bed of the river, diverted to its current canalized course in the first half of the 20th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="426" data-file-width="1907" height="49" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg/220px-Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg/330px-Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg/440px-Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monumentos_de_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Monuments of the city
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Parts_of_the_city">
       Parts of the city
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parts of the city">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg/220px-Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg/330px-Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg/440px-Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plaza_Constituci%C3%B3n.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Faade of the Constitution Square (Plaza) in San Sebastin
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      As a result of Donostia's sprawling in all directions, first into the flatlands shaped by the river
      <a href="/wiki/Urumea" title="Urumea">
       Urumea
      </a>
      and later up the hills, new districts arose after the walls of the city were demolished in 1863. The first expansion of the old town stretched out to the river's mouth, on the old quarter called Zurriola (a name later given by Council decision to the sand area and the avenue across the river).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :13, 322
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The orthogonal layout nowadays making up the city centre (the Cortazar development) was built up to 1914 (first phase finished) much in tune with a Parisian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baron_Haussmann#Haussmann.27s_plan_for_Paris" title="Baron Haussmann">
       Haussmannian
      </a>
      style. The arcades of the Buen Pastor square were fashioned after the ones of the
      <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_Rivoli" title="Rue de Rivoli">
       Rue de Rivoli
      </a>
      , with the Maria Cristina Bridge being inspired by the
      <a href="/wiki/Pont_Alexandre_III" title="Pont Alexandre III">
       Pont Alexandre III
      </a>
      that spans the Seine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :257
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       Estacin del Norte
      </i>
      train station standing right across the bridge was inaugurated in 1864 just after the arrival of the railway to San Sebastin, with its metallic roof being designed by
      <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Eiffel" title="Gustave Eiffel">
       Gustave Eiffel
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      </span>

       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parte Vieja">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The Parte Vieja (Old Town) is the traditional core area of the city, and was surrounded by walls up to 1863, when they were demolished so as to occupy the stretch of sand and land that connected the town to the mainland (a stretch of the walls still limits the Old Part on its exit to the port through the
      <i>
       Portaletas
      </i>
      gate). The Old Part is divided in two parishes relating to the Santa Maria and San Vicente churches, the inhabitants belonging to the former being dubbed traditionally
      <i>
       joxemaritarrak
      </i>
      , while those attached to the latter are referred to as
      <i>
       koxkeroak
      </i>
      . Historically, the koxkeroak up to the early 18th century were largely
      <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
       Gascon
      </a>
      speaking inhabitants. Especially after the end of Franco's dictatorship, scores of bars sprang up all over the Old Part which are very popular with the youth and the tourists, although not as much with the local residents. Most current buildings trace back to the 19th century, erected thanks to the concerted effort and determination of the town dwellers
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n#Ransacking_and_burning_of_San_Sebastian" title="Siege of San Sebastin">
       after the 1813 destruction
      </a>
      of the town by the allied Anglo-Portuguese troops.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :7375, 8189
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a small fishing and recreation port, with two-floor picturesque houses lined under the front-wall of the mount
      <a href="/wiki/Urgull" title="Urgull">
       Urgull
      </a>
      . Yet these houses are relatively new, resulting from the demilitarization of the hill,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :218
      </sup>
      sold to the city council by the Ministry of War in 1924.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Antiguo">
       Antiguo
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Antiguo">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This part stands at the west side of the city beyond the
      <i>
       Miramar Palace
      </i>
      . It is arguably the first population nucleus, even before the land at the foot of Urgull (Old Part) was settled. A monastery of
      <i>
       San Sebastin el Antiguo
      </i>
      ('the Old') is attested in documents at the time of the foundation (12th century).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :35
      </sup>
      At the mid 19th century, industry developed (
      <i>
       Cervezas El Len
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Suchard
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Lizarriturry
      </i>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the nucleus coming to be populated by workers. Industry has since been replaced by services and the tourist sector. The
      <i>
       Matia kalea
      </i>
      provides the main axis for the district.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amara_Zaharra">
       Amara Zaharra
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Amara Zaharra">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Or Old Amara, named after the farmhouse
      <i>
       Amara
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :30
      </sup>
      It has eventually merged with the city centre to a large extent, since former Amara lay on the marshes at the left of the
      <a href="/wiki/Urumea" title="Urumea">
       River Urumea
      </a>
      . The core of this district is the
      <i>
       Easo Plaza
      </i>
      , with the railway terminal of Euskotren closing the square at its south.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Amara_Berri">
       Amara Berri
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Amara Berri">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This city expansion to the south came about as of the 1940s, after the works to canalize the river were achieved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :3031, 92
      </sup>
      Nowadays the name Amara usually applies to this sector, the newer district having overshadowed the original nucleus both in size and population. The district harbours the main road entrance to the city, with Donostia's central bus station being located between the roundabout and the river (
      <i>
       Plaza Pio XII
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pi.C3.B1eiro_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pi.C3.B1eiro-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Facilities of many state run agencies were established here and presently Amara's buildings house many business offices. The district revolves around the axis of
      <i>
       Avenida Sancho el Sabio
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Avenida de Madrid
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gros">
       Gros
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gros">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The district is built on the sandy terrain across the river. The
      <i>
       Gros
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Zurriola
      </i>
      surf beach by the river's mouth bears witness to that type of soil. In the 19th century, shanties and workshops started to dot the area, Tomas Gros being one of its main proprietors as well as providing the name for this part of the city.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :148149
      </sup>
      The area held the former monumental bullring
      <i>
       Chofre
      </i>
      demolished in 1973, on a site currently occupied by a housing estate. The district shows a dynamic commercial activity, recently boosted by the presence of the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.kursaal.com.es/antcatalogo.asp?nombre=2785&amp;hoja=0&amp;sesion=1347" rel="nofollow">
       Kursaal Congress Centre
      </a>
      by the beach.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aiete">
       Aiete
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aiete">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      One of the newest parts in the city, it kept a rural air until not long ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :6061
      </sup>
      The postwar city council bought the quaint compound of the Aiete Palace for the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
       Francisco Franco
      </a>
      in 1940, right after the conclusion of the Civil War. The place in turn became the summer residence for the dictator up to 1975.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :62
      </sup>
      Nowadays home to the Bakearen Etxea or Peace Memorial House.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Egia">
       Egia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Egia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Egia, stemming from (H)Egia (
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
       Basque
      </a>
      for either bank/shore or hill), is a popular district of Donostia on the right side of the
      <a href="/wiki/Urumea" title="Urumea">
       Urumea
      </a>
      beyond the train station. At the beginning of the 20th century a patch of land by the railway started to be used as a football pitch, eventually turning into the official stadium of the local team
      <i>
       Real Sociedad
      </i>
      before it was transferred in the 1990s to
      <i>
       Anoeta
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :111
      </sup>
      south of Amara Berri (nowadays the site harbours houses). The cigarette factory conjures up the former industrial past of the area,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :111
      </sup>
      while the building has been made recently into a
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.tabakalera.eu/" rel="nofollow">
       Contemporary Culture Centre
      </a>
      . Right opposite to this building lies the Cristina Enea park, a public compound with a botanic vocation. Egia holds the city cemetery,
      <i>
       Polloe
      </i>
      , at the north-east fringes of the district, stretching out to South Intxaurrondo.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intxaurrondo">
       Intxaurrondo
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Intxaurrondo">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This part (meaning 'walnut tree' in Basque) is a large district to the east of the city. The original nucleus lies between the railway and the
      <i>
       Ategorrieta
      </i>
      Avenue, where still today the farmhouse
      <i>
       Intxaurrondo Zar
      </i>
      , declared "National Monument", is situated since the mid-17th century. The railway cuts across the district, the southern side being the fruit of the heavy development undergone in the area during the immigration years of the 1950s and 1960s. In addition, further housing estates have been built up more recently souther beyond the N-1 E-5 E-80 E-70 ring road (
      <i>
       South Intxaurrondo
      </i>
      ). The police force
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_Guard_(Spain)" title="Civil Guard (Spain)">
       Guardia Civil
      </a>
      runs controversial barracks there (works for new housing are underway).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Altza">
       Altza
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Altza">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Altza (
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
       Basque
      </a>
      for alder tree) is the easternmost district of San Sebastin along with
      <i>
       Bidebieta
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Trintxerpe
      </i>
      . It was but a quaint village comprising scattered farmhouses and a small nucleus a century ago (2,683 inhabitants in 1910), yet on the arrival of thousands of immigrants in the 1960s and 1970s a rapid and chaotic housing and building activity ensued, resulting in a maze of grey landscape of skyscrapers and 32,531 inhabitants crammed in them (data of 1970), the figure is 20,000 as of 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ibaeta">
       Ibaeta
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ibaeta">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Ibaeta stands on the former location for various factories (e.g.,
      <i>
       Cervezas El Leon
      </i>
      ) of San Sebastin, with the buildings of the old industrial estate being demolished in the late 20th century. The levelling of this large flat area paved the ground for a carefully planned modern and elegant housing estate, featuring a new university campus for the public
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Basque_Country" title="University of the Basque Country">
       University of the Basque Country
      </a>
      (UPV-EHU)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pi.C3.B1eiro_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pi.C3.B1eiro-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and institutions such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Donostia_International_Physics_Center" title="Donostia International Physics Center">
       Donostia International Physics Center
      </a>
      or the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.nanogune.eu/en" rel="nofollow">
       Nanotechnology Center
      </a>
      . A stream called
      <i>
       Konporta
      </i>
      flows down along the eastern side of the area, but it was canalized under the ground almost all along to its mouth on the bay pushed by urban building pressure.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Loiola">
       Loiola
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Loiola">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      It lies by the Urumea at the south-east end of the city. It comprises a small patch of detached houses (
      <i>
       Ciudad Jardn
      </i>
      ) and a core area of 6-odd floor buildings. The district has recently gone through a major makeover, with works finishing in 2008. The road axis coming from important industrial areas (Astigarraga-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hernani,_Spain" title="Hernani, Spain">
       Hernani
      </a>
      ) crosses the district heading downtown. A military base stands across the river,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaezGarcia_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-SaezGarcia-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      home to an uprising in 1936. Attempts by the city council to close it have been unsuccessful so far.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Riberas_de_Loiola">
       Riberas de Loiola
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Riberas de Loiola">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      New modern district erected in the 2000s next to the city's inner bypass and south road entrance to Donostia. A pedestrian bridge spans the Urumea river onto the Cristina Enea Park.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Martutene">
       Martutene
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Martutene">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The district bordering to the south on the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Astigarraga" title="Astigarraga">
       Astigarraga
      </a>
      comes next to Loiola in the south direction. This part of the city features an industrial area, a football pitch for lower leagues, a disused vocational training building and enclosure as well as a prison, much in decay and due to be transferred soon to a new location, probably in the municipality's exclave of
      <i>
       Zubieta
      </i>
      , while this option is coming in for much opposition.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Zubieta">
       Zubieta
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Zubieta">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The exclave Zubieta (meaning 'place of bridges') was a picturesque old village up to recent years, with a bunch of houses, a unique
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_pelota" title="Basque pelota">
       handball
      </a>
      pitch (on account of its single wall as opposed to the regular two) and a church. Yet it has undergone a great urban development, which has rendered the location a built-up area with paved streets and due equipment. Two contested projects are under way to build a solid-waste incinerator and a prison nearby. Historically, neighbours from Donostia held a meeting at a house in the former village in the wake of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_San_Sebastian#Burning_of_San_Sebastian" title="Siege of San Sebastian">
       1813 burning
      </a>
      , in order to decide the reconstruction of the town.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
       Climate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Climate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      San Sebastin features an
      <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">
       oceanic climate
      </a>
      with warm, but not hot summers, and cool but not cold winters. Like many cities with this climate, San Sebastin typically experiences cloudy or overcast conditions for the majority of the year, typically with some precipitation. The city averages roughly 1,750mm (69in) of precipitation annually, which is fairly evenly spread throughout the year. However, the city is somewhat drier and noticeably sunnier in the summer months, experiencing on average approximately 100mm (4in) of precipitation during those months. Average temperatures range from about 9C (48F) in January to about 21C (70F) in August.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">
       Prehistory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Prehistory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first evidence of human stationary presence in the current city is the settlement of Ametzagaa, between South Intxaurrondo and
      <a href="/wiki/Astigarraga" title="Astigarraga">
       Astigarraga
      </a>
      . The unearthed remains, such as carved stone used as knives to cut animal skin, date from 24,000 to 22,000 BC. The open-air findings of the
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">
       Upper Paleolithic
      </a>
      have revealed that the settlers were hunters and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">
        Homo sapiens
       </a>
      </i>
      , besides pointing to a much colder climate at the time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Age">
       Ancient Age
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ancient Age">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      San Sebastin is thought to have been in the territory of the
      <a href="/wiki/Varduli" title="Varduli">
       Varduli
      </a>
      in Roman times. 10km (6mi) east of the current city lay the Basque Roman town of
      <a href="/wiki/Oiasso" title="Oiasso">
       Oiasso
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Irun" title="Irun">
       Irun
      </a>
      ), which was for a long time wrongly identified with San Sebastin.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Age">
       Middle Age
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Middle Age">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After a long period of silence in evidence, in 1014 the monastery of St. Sebastin with its apple orchards (for cider), located in the term of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hernani,_Spain" title="Hernani, Spain">
        Hernani
       </a>
      </i>
      , is donated to the Abbey of
      <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Leyre" title="Monastery of Leyre">
       Leire
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Sancho_III_of_Navarre" title="Sancho III of Navarre">
       Sancho III of Pamplona
      </a>
      . By 1181, the city is chartered (given
      <a href="/wiki/Fuero" title="Fuero">
       fuero
      </a>
      ) by king
      <a href="/wiki/Sancho_VI_of_Navarre" title="Sancho VI of Navarre">
       Sancho VI of Pamplona
      </a>
      on the site of Izurum, having jurisdiction over all the territory between the rivers
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oria_River" title="Oria River">
       Oria
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bidasoa" title="Bidasoa">
       Bidasoa
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1200, the city was conquered by
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
       Castile
      </a>
      , whose king
      <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VIII_of_Castile" title="Alfonso VIII of Castile">
       Alfonso VIII
      </a>
      , confirmed its charter (fuero), but the
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
       Kingdom of Navarre
      </a>
      was deprived of its main direct access out to the sea. Perhaps as soon as 1204 (or earlier), the city nucleus at the foot of
      <a href="/wiki/Urgull" title="Urgull">
       Urgull
      </a>
      started to be populated with
      <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
       Gascon
      </a>
      -speaking colonizers from
      <a href="/wiki/Bayonne" title="Bayonne">
       Bayonne
      </a>
      and beyond, who left an important imprint in the city's identity in the centuries to come.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOS_GASCONES_EN_GUIP.C3.9AZCOA_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LOS_GASCONES_EN_GUIP.C3.9AZCOA-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1265, the use of the city as a seaport is granted to Navarre as part of a wedding pact. The large quantity of Gascons inhabiting the town favoured the development of trade with other European ports and
      <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">
       Gascony
      </a>
      . The city steered clear of the destructive
      <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Bands" title="War of the Bands">
       War of the Bands
      </a>
      in Gipuzkoa, the only town in doing so in that territory. In fact, the town only joined
      <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
       Gipuzkoa
      </a>
      in 1459 after the war came to an end.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOS_GASCONES_EN_GUIP.C3.9AZCOA_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-LOS_GASCONES_EN_GUIP.C3.9AZCOA-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Up to the 16th century, Donostia remained mostly out of wars, but by the beginning of the 15th century, a line of walls of simple construction is attested encircling the town. The last chapter of the town in the Middle Ages was brought about by a fire that devastated Donostia in 1489. After burning to the ground, the town began a new renaissance by building up mainly on stone instead of bare timber.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Age">
       Modern Age
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Modern Age">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The advent of the Modern Age brought a period of instability and war for the city. After the fall of Navarre, new state boundaries started to be drawn that left Donostia at the forefront of the Spanish border with France. New chunky and more sophisticated walls were erected and the town got involved in the wars engaged between Spain and France on the aftermath of the disappearance of the independent
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
       Kingdom of Navarre
      </a>
      in 1521. Actually, the town provided critical naval help to the Spanish king on the frontier disputes that took place in
      <a href="/wiki/Hondarribia" title="Hondarribia">
       Hondarribia
      </a>
      , which earned the town the titles "Muy Noble y Muy Leal", recorded on its coat of arms. Moreover, the town took sides with the new emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">
       Charles V
      </a>
      by sending a party to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Noain" title="Battle of Noain">
       Battle of Noain
      </a>
      and providing help to the emperor against the
      <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Comuneros" title="Revolt of the Comuneros">
       Revolt of the Comuneros
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      After the conquest of the Iberian Navarre and the attachment of Donostia to
      <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
       Gipuzkoa
      </a>
      , Gascons, who had played a leading role in the political and economic life of the town since its foundation, began to be excluded from influential public positions by means of a string of regional sentences upheld by royal decision (regional diets of
      <a href="/wiki/Zestoa" title="Zestoa">
       Zestoa
      </a>
      1527,
      <a href="/wiki/Hondarribia" title="Hondarribia">
       Hondarribia
      </a>
      1557,
      <a href="/wiki/Bergara" title="Bergara">
       Bergara
      </a>
      1558,
      <a href="/wiki/Tolosa,_Spain" title="Tolosa, Spain">
       Tolosa
      </a>
      1604 and
      <a href="/wiki/Deba,_Gipuzkoa" title="Deba, Gipuzkoa">
       Deba
      </a>
      1662).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LOS_GASCONES_EN_GUIP.C3.9AZCOA_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-LOS_GASCONES_EN_GUIP.C3.9AZCOA-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meanwhile, the climate of war and disease left the town in a poor condition that drove many fishermen and traders to take to the sea as corsairs as a way of getting a living, most of the times under the auspices of the king
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">
       Philip II of Spain
      </a>
      , who benefited from the disruption caused to and wealth obtained from the French and Dutch trade ships.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1656, the city was used as the royal headquarters during the marriage of
      <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Maria Theresa of Spain">
       the Infanta
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_XIV" title="Louis XIV">
       Louis XIV
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Saint-Jean-de-Luz" title="Saint-Jean-de-Luz">
       Saint-Jean-de-Luz
      </a>
      nearby. After a relatively peaceful 17th century, the town was besieged and taken over by the troops of the French Duke of Berwick up to 1721. However, San Sebastin was not spared by shelling in the French assault and many urban structures were reconstructed, e.g. a new opening in the middle of the town, the Plaza Berria (that was to become the current Konstituzio Plaza).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1728, the "
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compa%C3%B1%C3%ADa_Guipuzcoana_de_Caracas" title="Compaa Guipuzcoana de Caracas">
        Real Compaa Guipuzcoana de Caracas
       </a>
      </i>
      was founded and boosted commerce with the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
       Americas
      </a>
      . Thanks to the profit the company generated, the town underwent some urban reforms and improvements and the new Santa Maria Church was erected by subscription. This period of wealth and development was to last up to the end of 18th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1808,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">
       Napoleonic
      </a>
      forces captured San Sebastin in the
      <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">
       Peninsular War
      </a>
      . In 1813, after a siege of various weeks, on 28 August, during the night, a landing party from a British
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
       Royal Navy
      </a>
      squadron captured Santa Clara Island, in the bay. Situated on a narrow promontory that jutted out into the sea between the waters of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">
       Bay of Biscay
      </a>
      and the broad estuary of the
      <a href="/wiki/Urumea" title="Urumea">
       Urumea
      </a>
      River, the town was hard to get at and well fortified  "it was the strongest fortification I ever saw,
      <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
       Gibraltar
      </a>
      excepted", wrote
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Dent" title="William Dent">
       William Dent
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Three days later, on 31 August, British and Portuguese troops
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_San_Sebastian" title="Siege of San Sebastian">
       besieging San Sebastin
      </a>
      assaulted the town. The relieving troops ransacked and burnt the city to the ground. Only the street at the foot of the hill (now called 31 August Street) remained.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_history">
       Contemporary history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Contemporary history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After the destructive events, the reconstruction of the city was decided in the same spot with an only slightly altered layout, since a modern octagonal draft project by the architect P.M. Ugartemendia was turned down and eventually M. Gogorza's blueprint was approved, while supervised and implemented by the former. This area, the Old Part, oozes neoclassical, austere and systematic style in its architectural construction. The Constitution Square was built in 1817 and the town hall (current library) between 1828 and 1832.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :100
      </sup>
      Housing works were carried out gradually during various decades until they were achieved.
     </p>
     <p>
      The liberal and bourgeois San Sebastin became capital of Gipuzkoa (at the expense of
      <a href="/wiki/Tolosa,_Spain" title="Tolosa, Spain">
       Tolosa
      </a>
      ) until 1823, when absolutists assailed the town again (only 200 inhabitants remained in town when the assaulting troops broke in), but it was made capital city again in 1854.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berruso_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berruso-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1833, British volunteers under Sir
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_de_Lacy_Evans" title="George de Lacy Evans">
       George de Lacy Evans
      </a>
      defended the town against
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlist" title="Carlist">
       Carlist
      </a>
      attack, and their fallen were buried at the "English Cemetery" on the hill
      <a href="/wiki/Urgull" title="Urgull">
       Urgull
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      At the beginning of the 19th century, the local government was still ruled on the principle of nobility, while the inhabitants of foreign origin or descent had always been ubiquitous in the town, especially the traders. Although San Sebastin benefited greatly from the charts system established in the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Basque_Country" title="Southern Basque Country">
       Basque Country
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fuero" title="Fuero">
        foruak
       </a>
      </i>
      , with borders in the Ebro river and no duties for overseas goods), the town was at odds with the more traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
       Gipuzkoa
      </a>
      , even requesting the detachment from the province and the annexation to
      <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
       Navarre
      </a>
      in 1841.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1863, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defensive_walls" title="Defensive walls">
       defensive walls
      </a>
      of the town were demolished (their remains are visible in the underground car-park at the Boulevard) and an expansion of the town began in an attempt to escape the military function it had held before.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pi.C3.B1eiro_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pi.C3.B1eiro-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Works were appointed to Jose Goicoa and Ramon Cortazar, who modeled the new city according to an orthogonal shape much in an neoclassical Parisian style, and the former designed elegant buildings, like the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Miramar_Palace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Miramar Palace (page does not exist)">
       Miramar Palace
      </a>
      , or the Concha Promenade.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :145146
      </sup>
      The city was chosen by the Spanish monarchy to spend the summer following the French example of the near
      <a href="/wiki/Biarritz" title="Biarritz">
       Biarritz
      </a>
      . Subsequently the Spanish nobility and the diplomatic corps opened residences in the
      <a href="/wiki/Summer_capital" title="Summer capital">
       summer capital
      </a>
      . As the "wave baths" at La Concha conflicted with shipbuilding activity, shipyards relocated to
      <a href="/wiki/Pasaia" title="Pasaia">
       Pasaia
      </a>
      , a near bay formerly part of San Sebastin.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, in 1875, war battered the town again and shelling over the city by
      <a href="/wiki/Carlist_Wars" title="Carlist Wars">
       Carlists
      </a>
      caused acclaimed
      <a href="/wiki/Bertsolari" title="Bertsolari">
       bertsolari
      </a>
      and poet
      <a href="/wiki/Bilintx" title="Bilintx">
       Bilintx
      </a>
      to die in 1876.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berruso_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berruso-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 1885, King
      <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XII of Spain">
       Alfonso XII of Spain
      </a>
      's widow
      <a href="/wiki/Maria_Christina_of_Austria" title="Maria Christina of Austria">
       Maria Cristina
      </a>
      spent her summer in Donostia on a yearly basis (took accommodation at the Miramar Palace), bringing along her retinue. In 1887, the Casino was erected, which eventually turned into the current city hall, and somewhat later the Regional Government's (Diputacin's) building was completed in the Gipuzkoa Plaza following Jose Goicoa's design. Cultural life thrived on this period, giving rise to various typical events in the city, such as the Caldereros or the
      <a href="/wiki/Tamborrada" title="Tamborrada">
       Tamborrada
      </a>
      , and journalistic and literary productions both in Spanish and Basque.
     </p>
     <p>
      After much debate within the city over its vocation, either tourism or manufacturing, Donostia developed into a fully-fledged seaside resort, but some industry developed in the district of Antiguo and outskirts of the city. Following the outbreak of World War I, San Sebastin became an attracting focus for renowned international figures of culture and politics,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berruso_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berruso-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Mata_Hari" title="Mata Hari">
       Mata Hari
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">
       Leon Trotsky
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">
       Maurice Ravel
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conde_de_Romanones" title="Conde de Romanones">
       Romanones
      </a>
      , etc.
     </p>
     <p>
      Various rationalist architectural landmarks, typically white or light toned, were erected and dotted the urban landscape in the 20s and 30s (La Equitativa, Nautico, building Easo, etc.). In 1924-1926, works to canalize the Urumea river were carried out on the southern tip of the city. However, after the city's Belle Epoque in the European war time, repression under
      <a href="/wiki/Primo_de_Rivera" title="Primo de Rivera">
       Primo de Rivera
      </a>
      's dictatorship didn't favour the city. In 1924, gambling was prohibited by the authoritarian regime, causing the Grand Casino and the Kursaal (1921) to struggle to survive.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1930, Spanish republican forces signed up the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pact_of_San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="Pact of San Sebastin">
        Pact of San Sebastin
       </a>
      </i>
      leading to the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">
       Second Spanish Republic
      </a>
      . Unrest and repression did not stop with the new political regime, and large-scale industrial action was taken several times by the growing anarchist, communist and socialist unions. The
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War,_1936" title="Spanish Civil War, 1936">
       1936 military coup
      </a>
      was initially defeated by resistance led by the
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_nationalism" title="Basque nationalism">
       Basque Nationalists
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      anarchists and communists, but later that year the province fell to Spanish Nationalist forces in the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The occupation proved disastrous for the city dwellers: 380 were executed by the Spanish Nationalists, including the mayor, many children were evacuated to foreign countries and the city drained on an exodus estimated at 40,000 to 50,000 inhabitants.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the aftermath of war, the city was stricken by poverty, famine and repression, coupled with thriving smuggling. Many republican detainees were held at the Ondarreta prison in grim and humid conditions (building demolished in 1948) right at the beach with the same name. However, industry developed and paved the way for the urban expansion in the popular district Egia and eclectic styled Amara Berri, on the marshes and riverbed of the Urumea, at the end of the 1940s and beginning of the 1950s.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1943, the seeds of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ikastola" title="Ikastola">
       Basque language schools
      </a>
      were being sown by Elvira Zipitria, who started to give instruction in Basque at her own house in the Old Part. In 1947, the Grand Casino was turned into the City Hall.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SadaSanse_9-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-SadaSanse-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :95
      </sup>
      A decade later, in 1953, businessmen from the city organised the first
      <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_International_Film_Festival" title="San Sebastin International Film Festival">
       San Sebastin International Film Festival
      </a>
      to stimulate the economic life and national and international profile of the city.
     </p>
     <p>
      The massive immigration from various parts of Spain, spurred by growing industrial production, greatly increased population, in turn bringing about a quick and chaotic urban development on the outskirts of the city (Altza, Intxaurrondo, Herrera, Bidebieta, etc.), but social, cultural and political contradictions and inequities followed, so sparking dissatisfaction. A general climate of protest and street demonstrations ensued, driven by Basque nationalists (especially armed separatist organisation ETA) and underground unions, triggering in 1968 the first state of emergency in Gipuzkoa. Several more were imposed by the Francoist authorities in the run-up to the dictator's death in 1975.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the middle of the shaky economic situation and real estate speculation, the iconic buildings Kursaal and Chofre bullring in Gros were demolished in 1973.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berruso_19-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berruso-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the other hand, sculptor
      <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Chillida" title="Eduardo Chillida">
       Eduardo Chillida
      </a>
      's and architect
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pe%C3%B1a_Ganchegui&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pea Ganchegui (page does not exist)">
       Pea Ganchegui
      </a>
      's landmark The Comb of the Winds was built at the bay's western tip (19751977). The 1970s to the mid-1980s were years of general urban and social decay marked by social and political unrest and violence.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1979, the first democratic municipal elections were held, won by the
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_Nationalist_Party" title="Basque Nationalist Party">
       Basque Nationalist Party
      </a>
      , who held office along with splinter party
      <a href="/wiki/Eusko_Alkartasuna" title="Eusko Alkartasuna">
       Eusko Alkartasuna
      </a>
      (Basque Solidarity) until 1991. The
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Socialist_Workers%27_Party" title="Spanish Socialist Workers' Party">
       Spanish Socialist Workers' Party
      </a>
      's
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Odon_Elorza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Odon Elorza (page does not exist)">
       Odon Elorza
      </a>
      took over as mayor that year until 2011, when he was overthrown unexpectedly in elections by Juan Carlos Izagirre (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bildu" title="Bildu">
       Bildu
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berruso_19-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berruso-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As of the 1990s, a major makeover of the city centre was undertaken aimed at enhancing and revamping the neoclassical and modernist side of San Sebastin's architecture. Other milestone works include the reshape and enlargement of the Zurriola beach and promenade and the inauguration of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kursaal_Palace" title="Kursaal Palace">
       Kursaal Palace
      </a>
      cubes (1999),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berruso_19-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berruso-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or the new university campus and technological facilities in Ibaeta, the provision of a wide bike lane network, underground car-parks and significant public transport improvements. Districts of cutting-edge design have been erected, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donostia#Ibaeta" title="Donostia">
       Ibaeta
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donostia#Riberas_de_Loiola" title="Donostia">
       Riberas de Loiola
      </a>
      , while some important projects hang on the balance prompted by financial tensions.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_events">
       Culture and events
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Culture and events">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG/170px-San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG/255px-San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG/340px-San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:San_Sebastian_Festival_Entrada.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        San Sebastin International Film Festival, Red Carpet
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      San Sebastin shows a dynamic cultural scene, where grass-roots initiative based on different parts of the city and the concerted private and public synergy have paved the ground for a rich range of possibilities and events catering to the tastes of a wide and selected public alike. The city was selected as
      <a href="/wiki/European_Capital_of_Culture" title="European Capital of Culture">
       European Capital of Culture
      </a>
      for 2016 (shared with
      <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocaw">
       Wrocaw
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      ) with a basic motto, "Waves of people's energy", summarizing a clear message: people and movements of citizens are the real driving force behind transformations and changes in the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      Events ranging from traditional city festivals to music, theatre or cinema take place all year round, while they specially thrive in summer. In the last week of July, San Sebastin's Jazz Festival (Jazzaldia), the longest, continuously running Jazz Festival in Europe is held. In different spots of the city gigs are staged, sometimes with free admission. The Musical Fortnight comes next extending for at least fifteen days well into August and featuring classical music concerts. In September, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Sebastian_International_Film_Festival" title="San Sebastian International Film Festival">
       San Sebastin International Film Festival
      </a>
      comes to the spotlight, an event with more than 50 years revolving around the venues of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kursaal_Palace" title="Kursaal Palace">
       Kursaal
      </a>
      and the Victoria Eugenia Theatre.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sticking to the cinematic language but lacking its echo, Street Zinema is an international audiovisual festival exploring contemporary art and urban cultures. Other rising and popular events include the Horror and Fantasy Festival in October (21st edition in 2010) and the Surfilm Festibal, a cinema festival featuring surfing footage, especially shorts. During centuries, the city has been open to many influences that have left a trace, often mingling with the local customs and traditions and eventually resulting in festivals and new customs.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="San_Sebasti.C3.A1n_Day">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>

     <p>
      Every year on 20 January (the feast of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Sebastian" title="Saint Sebastian">
       Saint Sebastian
      </a>
      ), the people of San Sebastin celebrate a festival known as the "Tamborrada". At midnight, in the Konstituzio plaza in the "Alde Zaharra/Parte Vieja" (Old Part), the mayor raises the flag of San Sebastin (see in the infobox). For 24 hours, the entire city is awash with the sound of drums. The adults, dressed as cooks and soldiers, march around the city. They march all night with their cook hats and white aprons with the March of San Sebastin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaezGarcia_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-SaezGarcia-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On this day a procession was held in the early 19th century from the Santa Maria Church in the Old Part to the San Sebastin Church in the district of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donostia#Antiguo" title="Donostia">
       Antiguo
      </a>
      , while later limited on the grounds of weather conditions to the in-wall area. The event finished with a popular dancing accompanied on the military band's flutes and drums. In addition, every day a soldier parade took place to change the guards at the town's southern walls. Since the San Sebastin Day was the first festival heralding the upcoming Carnival, it's no surprise that some youths in Carnival mood followed them aping their martial manners and drumrolls, using for the purpose the buckets left at the fountains.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the period spanning the 1860s and 1880s the celebrations started to shape as we know them today with proper military style outfits and parades and the tunes fashioned by music composer Raimundo Sarriegui.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n_10-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Historia_de_San_Sebasti.C3.A1n-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Adults usually have dinner in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Txoko" title="Txoko">
        sociedades gastronmicas
       </a>
      </i>
      ("gourmet clubs"), who traditionally admitted only males, but nowadays even the strictest ones allow women on the "Noche de la Tamborrada". They eat sophisticated meals cooked by themselves, mostly composed of seafood (traditionally
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elver" title="Elver">
       elvers
      </a>
      , now no longer served due to its exorbitant price) and drink the best wines. For "Donostiarras" this is the most celebrated festival of the year.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basque_Week">
       Basque Week
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Basque Week">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This decades long festivity taking place at the beginning of September features events related to Basque culture, such as performances of traditional improvising poets (
      <a href="/wiki/Bertsolari" title="Bertsolari">
       bertsolaris
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_pelota" title="Basque pelota">
       Basque pelota
      </a>
      games,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harrijasotzaileak" title="Harrijasotzaileak">
       stone lifting
      </a>
      contests,
      <a href="/wiki/Idi_probak" title="Idi probak">
       oxen wagers
      </a>
      , dance exhibitions or the
      <a href="/wiki/Cider" title="Cider">
       cider
      </a>
      tasting festival. Yet the main highlight may be the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estropadak" title="Estropadak">
       rowing boat competition
      </a>
      , where teams from different towns of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">
       Bay of Biscay
      </a>
      contend for the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kontxako_Bandera" title="Kontxako Bandera">
        Flag of La Concha
       </a>
      </i>
      . Thousands of supporters coming from these coastal locations pour into the city's streets and promenades overlooking the bay to follow the event, especially on the Sunday of the final race. All day long the streets of the Old Part play host to droves of youths clad in their team colours who party there in a cheerful atmosphere.
     </p>

     <p>
      Saint Agatha's Eve is a traditional event taking place at the beginning of February or end of January in many spots of the
      <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">
       Basque Country
      </a>
      . It holds a small but cherished slot in the city's run-up to the Carnival. Groups dressed up in Basque traditional farmer costume march across the neighbourhood singing and wielding a characteristic stick beaten on the ground to the rhythm of the traditional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Agatha" title="Saint Agatha">
       Saint Agatha
      </a>
      's tune.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaezGarcia_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-SaezGarcia-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The singers ask for a small donation, which can be money, a drink or something to eat.
     </p>

     <p>
      This is a local festival held on the first Saturday of February linked to the upcoming
      <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">
       Carnival
      </a>
      , where different groups of people dressed in
      <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">

They were just men voices some time ago, but women participate and sing currently too. The festival is 131 years old in 2015.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Santo_Tomas">
       Santo Tomas
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Sebasti%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Santo Tomas">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This popular festival takes place on the 21 December, a date frequently shrouded in winter cold. From early in the morning, stalls are arranged across the city centre and people from all
      <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
       Gipuzkoa
      </a>
      flock to the streets of the centre and the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n#Old_Part" title="San Sebastin">
       Old Part
      </a>
      , with crowds of people often dressed in traditional Basque "farmer" outfit turning out and filling the area. Traditional and typical produce is showcased and sold on the stalls, while the main drink is cider and the most popular snacks are txistorra (a type of thin, uncured chorizo) wrapped in talos (flatbread). A large pig is on display in the
      <i>
       Konstituzio Plaza
      </i>
      , which is raffled off during the festival.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaezGarcia_14-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-SaezGarcia-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     <p>
      As in other Basque cities, towns and villages, on Christmas Eve the
      <a href="/wiki/Olentzero" title="Olentzero">
       Olentzero
      </a>
      and the accompanying carol singers usually dressed in Basque farmer costume take over the streets, especially in the city centre, asking for small donations in bars, shops and banks after singing their repertoire. Sometimes Olentzero choirs roam around the streets in later dates, on the 31st for example, and are often related to cultural, social or political associations and demands.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="University">
       University
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     <p>
      Donostia-San Sebastin has become an important University town. Hosting different Universities like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universidad_de_Navarra" title="Universidad de Navarra">
       Universidad de Navarra
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universidad_del_Pa%C3%ADs_Vasco" title="Universidad del Pas Vasco">

      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mondrag%C3%B3n" title="University of Mondragn">
       University of Mondragn
      </a>
      , the secondary studies activity is having an increasing impact on social, cultural, technological and economical levels of the city and surroundings. With its pushing innovative and research centers and its research strategies it is becoming one of Spain's main Science production locations, along with Barcelona, Madrid, Bilbao, Sevilla and Valencia, among others. Donostia-San Sebastian's Scientific production covers areas like Materials Science, Cancer Research, Alzheimer and Parkinson, Architecture, Polymer Science, Biomaterials, Nanotechnology, Robotics or Informatics.
