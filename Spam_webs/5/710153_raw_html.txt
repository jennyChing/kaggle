        <h1>
         By Justine Hofherr @Jhofherr29
        </h1>
        <p>
         Boston.com Staff
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="date">
       April 10, 2015 8:44 AM
      </div>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Nick Martins Allston studio is technically a micro apartment  a unit below 450 square feet  yet he lives roughly 6 miles away from the Seaport District, the area Boston officials have approved for micro living.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Sometimes I want more space, but its the perfect amount of space for maintenance and upkeep, Martin said of his 350-square-foot apartment. I dont actually have that many belongings, so its the perfect compromise.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Martin, a spokesperson for the Boston Redevelopment Authority, said his teeny unit is in an older building, and was probably grandfathered in to the citys housing stock.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Seaports Innovation District, located on the South Boston waterfront, is currently the only neighborhood in Boston where the BRA allows developers to build innovation units, also known as micro apartments, because the BRA wants to monitor the quality of living tiny apartments offer on a case-by-case basis. But Martin, like so many others, enjoys living alone in a small space, and cant afford the Seaports much talked about micro units.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       What we really intended when we started the innovation housing unit experience was that wed incentivize building smaller units because they could be constructed, leased, and rented at a lower price, Martin said of the BRAs initial push for micro units in 2010. But we were surprised the level of demand was so high for these, so the prices were higher than we expected.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       There are approximately 300 micro units constructed or under construction in Seaport, and many rent for upwards of $2,000 per month, Martin said. But a
       <a href="http://realestate.boston.com/listings-search/?purchase_types=rental&amp;min_sqft=250&amp;max_sqft=450&amp;sort_field=featured_weight&amp;sort_direction=desc&amp;search_num_results=10&amp;curr_page=1" target="_blank">
        quick search on any local real estate site
       </a>
       shows that Boston already has quite a few micro apartments up for grabs in Back Bay, the South End, and Fenway, for example. Like Martins studio, many have been grandfathered in.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       So will the new micro unit trend break out of the Innovation District?
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Aeron Hodges, a designer and manager at architectural design firm ADD Inc./Stantec, hopes so.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Hodges is used to small spaces. She grew up in a tiny apartment in Shanghai, and currently lives in a 375-square-foot studio in Fenway with her husband. While affordability is what first drew her to their micro apartment, Hodges said she also loves using the city as her living room.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <br/>
      <figure class="content-media content-media--wide">
       <img alt="
" class="content-media__image" src="//p.o0bc.com/rf/image_700w/Boston/2011-2020/2015/04/09/Boston.com/BCOM/Images/ADD-Inc-Boston-Micro-Unit.jpg"/>
       <figcaption class="content-media__caption">
        <div class="content-media__credit">
         Courtesy of Aeron Hodges
        </div>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Being able to live in the city and close to all the urban amenitiesI think thats the best draw, she said. Hodgess work for ADD Inc. involves neighborhood outreach and cultural studies, and she said shes found many underdeveloped areas of Boston where micro apartments could be built and rented at a relatively low cost.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Dorchester, Somerville, and East Boston are well-established with neighborhood amenities like being close to public transit, restaurants, and public laundry areas, Hodges said. Our view is that the city should work with small nonprofit and for-profit developers to fill this need for smaller, more affordable units, she added.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       While the average household size in Boston is two people, one-bedroom units make up only 25 percent of the citys housing stock,
       <a href="http://factfinder.census.gov/faces/nav/jsf/pages/index.xhtml" target="_blank">
        according to the 2013 U.S. census
       </a>
       . The discrepancy in what Bostons housing stock offers and what Bostonians need is part of what drives architect Tamara Roy, president-elect of the Boston Architectural Society. Her firm ADD Inc./Stantec has designed 200 micro units around Boston and shes currently working on a 100 percent compact and affordable housing unit in South Boston.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <br/>
      <figure class="content-media content-media--wide">
       <img alt="
" class="content-media__image" src="//p.o0bc.com/rf/image_700w/Boston/2011-2020/2015/04/09/Boston.com/BCOM/Images/399Congress_390SF.jpg"/>
       <figcaption class="content-media__caption">
        The Residences at 399 Congress Street, designed by ADD Inc., is a 22-story complex whose 414 apartments will include 60 innovation units of 325 to 450 square feet.
        <div class="content-media__credit">
         Courtesy of Tamara Roy
        </div>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Roy has been an advocate for Bostons micro unit trend ever since former Mayor Thomas Menino asked for Roys opinion in 2012.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       They asked a bunch of architects what innovative housing could be in the Innovation District, Roy said. So she crowd sourced her architectural firm of 150 employees (average age of 31) to find out what innovative housing meant to them.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <br/>
      <figure class="content-media content-media--wide">
       <img alt="Boston, MA 040814 FOR CHANGE THE WORLD MAGAZINE info:ADD Inc. (Boston) Is an architectural firm that specializes in microapartments, which began to appear in Boston last year. These are really small apartments (400 square feet or less) meant to provide affordable accommodations targeting the young professional crowd. It was a way to get more affordable units near places like the Innovation District. The person behind ADD?s microapartment design is Tamara Roy. ADD had some actual-size mockups of micro units, which we have shot previously, but might be an interesting. Tamara Roy?s cell phone is 781-859-9550.. Her email is troy@addinc.com (Essdras M Suarez/ Globe Staff)/ MAG" class="content-media__image" src="//p.o0bc.com/rf/image_700w/Boston/2011-2020/2015/04/09/Boston.com/BCOM/Images/ADDStantec_Micro.jpg"/>
       <figcaption class="content-media__caption">
        The staff at ADD Inc./Stantec who specialize in micro-apartments.
        <div class="content-media__credit">
         Essdras Suarez/Globe
        </div>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       The answer came back and was, Who cares? We cant afford to live here anyway, Roy said.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Roy thought about this for a while, creating a policy statement for city officials that requested a portion of new developments in the Innovation District be affordable, through less parking and smaller units with more communal social space. The city took Roys advice and let developers play around with micro units, but many ended up being unaffordable, she said.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       Its a free market. I cant control the social policy of who lives in them, Roy said. The push for me is to create more of them. When the supply goes up, the price will start to come down. Through further policy change and education, Roy said shed like to see the minimum unit size abolished, so developers outside Seaport can experiment with inexpensive micro-unit design.
      </p>
      <p class="generic-p-tag">
       This is not at all new, and its not going to pass, Roy said, citing the frequent usage of micro units on the West Coast, and in many Asian and European cities. If you dont want to live in one thats fine for me. Thats okay. But then to say that there has to be a metric for everyone else is where I argue with you. Its kind of suburban elitism to say to somebody elseif theyre perfectly happythat they shouldnt be allowed to live in 350 square feet.
      </p>
