     <div class="articleBody" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Christiana Figueres, executive secretary of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, in February." src="/content/dam/images/1/3/e/m/x/d/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.ggvfvz.png/1430922678396.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Christiana Figueres, executive secretary of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, in February.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Reuters
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       Australia sharesasimilar challengein movingaway from coal asSaudi Arabia does in reducing its economic dependency on oil, the United Nations' top climate negotiator says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Speaking at a conference inMelbourne onWednesday, ChristianaFigueresdrew aparallelbetween Australia and the oil kingdom as countries that would needto diversify their economiesas the world grapples with global warming.
      </p>
      <p>
       MsFigueres who heads the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change,overseeing international climate negotiations  also saidAustralia shouldtake on a leadership position on climate change.
      </p>
      <p>
       In an interview with Fairfax Mediaon Wednesday evening, Ms Figuereswas careful to stress that the comparisonbetween Australia and Saudi Arabiaonly stretched as far as that both hadpreviouslybenefited themselves and the worldfrom large fossil fuel endowments, but that point in history had come to an end. Otherwise they were completely different countries, she said.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ms Figueres said ultimately Australia would have to move away from coal for environmental reasons and changes in global economic patterns. She said nobody was saying thiswould have to happen overnight, butthe transition shouldbeorderly, gradualand progressive.
      </p>
      <p>
       '"The science is very clear, there is no space for any new coal," she said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Prime Minister Tony Abbott has in recent times championed coal  Australia's second largest export earneras "good for humanity" and the "foundation of prosperity" for the foreseeable future.
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of global agencies are increasing voicing concerns that if the world is to limit global warming to less than two degrees  an internationally agreed goal  then the majority of fossil fuel reserves will have to be left in the ground unused.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="http://www.climatecouncil.org.au/uploads/a904b54ce67740c4b4ee2753134154b0.pdf">
        recent report
       </a>
       by the Climate Council found that over 90 per cent of Australiancoal reserves would need to be leftunburntforthe two degree goal to remain viable.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="" src="http://www.smh.com.au/content/dam/images/g/g/v/f/7/m/image.imgtype.articleLeadwide.620x349.png/1430888776252.jpg" width="100%"/>
      </p>
      <div style="margin-bottom: 10px;">
       <div style="margin-bottom: 10px;">
        <em>
         CO2 emissions from burningglobal fossil fuel resources and reserves (orange) VSthe estimated global carbon budget for two degrees (green). Source: Climate Council.
        </em>
       </div>
       <p>
        MsFigueresvisited Saudi Arabia before coming to Australia. She told the conferencethat country was "making amazing investments in renewable energy" andwas completely focusedon the challenge of economic diversificationfrom its dominantoil industry.
       </p>
      </div>
      <p>
       "Which is a challenge I would say Australia shares to a certain extent. Economicdiversificationis going to be a challenge that Australia faces," MsFigueressaid.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the world lookingtowardstheParis climate summit at the end of the year, at which it is hoped a new global climate deal will be finalised, Ms Figueressaid Australia could be an important globalvoice.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Australia prides itself on leadership on many fields, not just cricket, and this cannot be an exception," she said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "You represent a huge potential to lead the world into a very healthy economic diversification path. You haven't figured out how to do that yet but that, my friends, is your homework."
      </p>
      <p>
       Speaking after MsFigueres' speech on Wednesday morning, EnvironmentMinisterGreg Hunt saidAustralia was committed to playingits part in helping the world to achieve the two degreegoal.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mr Hunt also used his speech to defend the government's direct action climate plan, saying it wasperformingbetter than the carbon price the Abbott government had repealed. He said the policy was designed forthe next half century.
      </p>
      <p>
       Direct action has again faced criticism in recent weeks, with some submissions to the government saying it was
       <a href="http://www.theage.com.au/environment/direct-actions-fitness-for-tougher-emissions-cuts-questioned-20150427-1mufvt.html">
        not up to the task of meeting tougher post-2020 emissions cuts
       </a>
       and proposed emissionslimits to beset on heavy polluting industries
       <a href="http://www.theage.com.au/federal-politics/political-news/direct-actions-emissions-safeguards-designed-to-fail-grattan-institute-20150505-ggupb5.html">
        would not work
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Asked whether he agreed thatcoal had to be left in the ground, Mr Hunt said he would not "speculate on individual resources in individual places," and what mattered was that global targets were met.
      </p>
      <p>
       MsFigueressaid each country would chose their own ways to cut emissions and there should be an open mind about how this should be done. What was important is thatcountriespushed whatever mechanism they adoptedto themaximum outcome possible.
      </p>
