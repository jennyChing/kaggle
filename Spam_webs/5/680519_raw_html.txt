     <p>
      Dr.
      <b>
       Edmond Locard
      </b>
      (13 December 1877  4 April 1966) was a pioneer in
      <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">
       forensic science
      </a>
      who became known as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">
       Sherlock Holmes
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      ". He formulated the basic principle of forensic science: "Every contact leaves a
      <a href="/wiki/Trace_evidence" title="Trace evidence">
       trace
      </a>
      ". This became known as
      <a href="/wiki/Locard%27s_exchange_principle" title="Locard's exchange principle">
       Locard's exchange principle
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Locard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Locard studied medicine and law at
      <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">
       Lyon
      </a>
      , France, eventually becoming the assistant of
      <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Lacassagne" title="Alexandre Lacassagne">
       Alexandre Lacassagne
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminologist" title="Criminologist">
       criminologist
      </a>
      and professor. He held this post until 1910, when he began the foundation of his criminal laboratory.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1910, Locard succeeded in persuading the Police Department of Lyon to give him two attic rooms and two assistants, to start what became the first
      <a href="/wiki/Crime_lab" title="Crime lab">
       police laboratory
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He produced a monumental, seven-volume work,
      <i>
       Trait de Criminalistique
      </i>
      . He continued with his research until his death in 1966.
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2012, he is nominated to the French Forensic Science Hall of Fame of the Association Qubcoise de Criminalistique
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French_forensic_science_Hall_of_fame_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-French_forensic_science_Hall_of_fame-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Locard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The young
       <a href="/wiki/Georges_Simenon" title="Georges Simenon">
        Georges Simenon
       </a>
       , later to become a well-known
       <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">
        detective
       </a>
       writer, is known to have attended some Locard lectures in 1919 or 1920.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quotation">
       Quotation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Edmond_Locard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quotation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       <i>
        "Wherever he steps, whatever he touches, whatever he leaves, even unconsciously, will serve as a silent witness against him. Not only his fingerprints or his footprints, but his hair, the fibers from his clothes, the glass he breaks, the tool marks he leaves, the paint he scratches, the blood or semen he deposits or collects. All of these and more, bear mute witness against him. This is evidence that does not forget. It is not confused by the excitement of the moment. It is not absent because human witnesses are. It is factual evidence. Physical evidence cannot perjure itself, it cannot be wholly absent. Only human failure to find it, study and understand it, can diminish its value"
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       - Paul L. Kirk, from his book: Crime Investigation: Physical Evidence and the Police Laboratory (Published in 1953)
      </p>
