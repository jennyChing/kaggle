     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
       statistics
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       power law
      </b>
      is a functional relationship between two quantities, where one quantity varies as a
      <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">
       power
      </a>
      of another. For instance, the number of cities having a certain population size is found to vary as a power of the size of the population. Empirical power-law distributions hold only approximately or over a limited range.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_examples_of_power_laws">
       Empirical examples of power laws
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Empirical examples of power laws">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The distributions of a wide variety of physical, biological, and man-made phenomena approximately follow a power law over a wide range of magnitudes: these include the sizes of
      <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
       earthquakes
      </a>
      , craters on the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       moon
      </a>
      and of
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
       solar flares
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Newman-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the foraging pattern of various species,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humphries_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Humphries-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the sizes of activity patterns of neuronal populations,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klaus_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Klaus-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the frequencies of
      <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">
       words
      </a>
      in most languages, frequencies of
      <a href="/wiki/Family_name" title="Family name">
       family names
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">
       species richness
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clades" title="Clades">
       clades
      </a>
      of organisms,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the sizes of
      <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
       power outages
      </a>
      , wars, criminal charges per convict, and many other quantities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Few empirical distributions fit a power law for all their values, but rather follow a power law in the tail.
      <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_attenuation" title="Acoustic attenuation">
       Acoustic attenuation
      </a>
      follows frequency power-laws within wide frequency bands for many complex media.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allometric_scaling" title="Allometric scaling">
       Allometric scaling laws
      </a>
      for relationships between biological variables are among the best known power-law functions in nature.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_power_laws">
       Properties of power laws
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties of power laws">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_invariance">
       Scale invariance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scale invariance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One attribute of power laws is their
      <a href="/wiki/Scale_invariance#Scale_invariance_of_functions_and_self-similarity" title="Scale invariance">
       scale invariance
      </a>
      . Given a relation
      <img alt="f(x) = ax^k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69648120a545f9ad7f3749ff2f6d0bdd.png"/>
      , scaling the argument
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      by a constant factor
      <img alt="c" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/a/8/4a8a08f09d37b73795649038408b5f33.png"/>
      causes only a proportionate scaling of the function itself. That is,
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="f(c x) = a(c x)^k = c^k f(x) \propto f(x).\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/f/5/9f5df47fb28d1fd14e87808c10f79f66.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      That is, scaling by a constant
      <img alt="c" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/a/8/4a8a08f09d37b73795649038408b5f33.png"/>
      simply multiplies the original power-law relation by the constant
      <img alt="c^k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/7/7/a774a10f898af74cdd211d7ba6764b16.png"/>
      . Thus, it follows that all power laws with a particular scaling exponent are equivalent up to constant factors, since each is simply a scaled version of the others. This behavior is what produces the linear relationship when logarithms are taken of both
      <img alt="f(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/0/b/50bbd36e1fd2333108437a2ca378be62.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      , and the straight-line on the log-log plot is often called the
      <i>
       signature
      </i>
      of a power law. With real data, such straightness is a necessary, but not sufficient, condition for the data following a power-law relation. In fact, there are many ways to generate finite amounts of data that mimic this signature behavior, but, in their asymptotic limit, are not true power laws (e.g., if the generating process of some data follows a
      <a href="/wiki/Log-normal_distribution" title="Log-normal distribution">
       Log-normal distribution
      </a>
      ). Thus, accurately fitting and validating power-law models is an active area of research in statistics.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_well-defined_average_value">
       Lack of well-defined average value
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lack of well-defined average value">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Power-laws have a well-defined
      <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">
       mean
      </a>
      only if the exponent exceeds 2 and have a finite
      <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">
       variance
      </a>
      only when the exponent exceeds 3; most identified power laws in nature have exponents such that the mean is well-defined but the variance is not, implying they are capable of
      <a href="/wiki/Black_swan_theory" title="Black swan theory">
       black swan
      </a>
      behavior.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This can be seen in the following thought experiment:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCSSCS9_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CCSSCS9-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      imagine a room with your friends and estimate the average monthly income in the room. Now imagine the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_richest_person" title="World's richest person">
       world's richest person
      </a>
      entering the room, with a monthly income of about 1
      <a href="/wiki/1,000,000,000" title="1,000,000,000">
       billion
      </a>
      US$. What happens to the average income in the room? Income is distributed according to a power-law known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_distribution" title="Pareto distribution">
       Pareto distribution
      </a>
      (for example, the net worth of Americans is distributed according to a power law with an exponent of 2).
     </p>
     <p>
      On the one hand, this makes it incorrect to apply traditional statistics that are based on
      <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">
       variance
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">
       standard deviation
      </a>
      (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">
       regression analysis
      </a>
      ). On the other hand, this also allows for cost-efficient interventions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCSSCS9_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CCSSCS9-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, given that car exhaust is distributed according to a power-law among cars (very few cars contribute to most contamination) it would be sufficient to eliminate those very few cars from the road to reduce total exhaust substantially.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The median does exist, however: for a power law x
      <sup>
       -a
      </sup>
      , with exponent a &gt; 1, it takes the value 2
      <sup>
       1/(a - 1)
      </sup>
      x
      <sub>
       min
      </sub>
      , where x
      <sub>
       min
      </sub>
      is the minimum value for which the power law holds
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Universality">
       Universality
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Universality">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The equivalence of power laws with a particular scaling exponent can have a deeper origin in the dynamical processes that generate the power-law relation. In physics, for example,
      <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">
       phase transitions
      </a>
      in thermodynamic systems are associated with the emergence of power-law distributions of certain quantities, whose exponents are referred to as the
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_exponent" title="Critical exponent">
       critical exponents
      </a>
      of the system. Diverse systems with the same critical exponentsthat is, which display identical scaling behaviour as they approach
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">
       criticality
      </a>
      can be shown, via
      <a href="/wiki/Renormalization_group" title="Renormalization group">
       renormalization group
      </a>
      theory, to share the same fundamental dynamics. For instance, the behavior of water and CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      at their boiling points fall in the same universality class because they have identical critical exponents. In fact, almost all material phase transitions are described by a small set of universality classes. Similar observations have been made, though not as comprehensively, for various
      <a href="/wiki/Self-organized_criticality" title="Self-organized criticality">
       self-organized critical
      </a>
      systems, where the critical point of the system is an
      <a href="/wiki/Attractor" title="Attractor">
       attractor
      </a>
      . Formally, this sharing of dynamics is referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Universality_(dynamical_systems)" title="Universality (dynamical systems)">
       universality
      </a>
      , and systems with precisely the same critical exponents are said to belong to the same
      <a href="/wiki/Renormalization_group#Relevant_and_irrelevant_operators.2C_universality_classes" title="Renormalization group">
       universality class
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Power-law_functions">
       Power-law functions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Power-law functions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Scientific interest in power-law relations stems partly from the ease with which certain general classes of mechanisms generate them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESornette2006_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESornette2006-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The demonstration of a power-law relation in some data can point to specific kinds of mechanisms that might underlie the natural phenomenon in question, and can indicate a deep connection with other, seemingly unrelated systems;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimon1955_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimon1955-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      see also
      <a href="#Universality">
       universality
      </a>
      above. The ubiquity of power-law relations in physics is partly due to
      <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">
       dimensional constraints
      </a>
      , while in
      <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems" title="Complex systems">
       complex systems
      </a>
      , power laws are often thought to be signatures of hierarchy or of specific
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stochastic_processes" title="Stochastic processes">
       stochastic processes
      </a>
      . A few notable examples of power laws are the
      <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg%E2%80%93Richter_law" title="GutenbergRichter law">
       GutenbergRichter law
      </a>
      for earthquake sizes,
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_principle" title="Pareto principle">
       Pareto's law
      </a>
      of income distribution, structural self-similarity of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fractals" title="Fractals">
       fractals
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allometric_law" title="Allometric law">
       scaling laws in biological systems
      </a>
      . Research on the origins of power-law relations, and efforts to observe and validate them in the real world, is an active topic of research in many fields of science, including
      <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
       physics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
       computer science
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">
       geophysics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">
       neuroscience
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economics
      </a>
      and more.
     </p>
     <p>
      However much of the recent interest in power laws comes from the study of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probability_distributions" title="Probability distributions">
       probability distributions
      </a>
      : The distributions of a wide variety of quantities seem to follow the power-law form, at least in their upper tail (large events). The behavior of these large events connects these quantities to the study of
      <a href="/wiki/Extreme_value_theory" title="Extreme value theory">
       theory of large deviations
      </a>
      (also called
      <a href="/wiki/Extreme_value_theory" title="Extreme value theory">
       extreme value theory
      </a>
      ), which considers the frequency of extremely rare events like
      <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_crash" title="Stock market crash">
       stock market crashes
      </a>
      and large
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">
       natural disasters
      </a>
      . It is primarily in the study of statistical distributions that the name "power law" is used; in other areas, such as physics and engineering, a power-law functional form with a single term and a positive integer exponent is typically regarded as a
      <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">
       polynomial
      </a>
      function.
     </p>
     <p>
      In empirical contexts, an approximation to a power-law
      <img alt="o(x^k)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/c/3cc8bd3f43725f7d3389451c2cb73146.png"/>
      often includes a deviation term
      <img alt="\varepsilon" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/6/9/c691dc52cc1ad756972d4629934d37fd.png"/>
      , which can represent uncertainty in the observed values (perhaps measurement or sampling errors) or provide a simple way for observations to deviate from the power-law function (perhaps for
      <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">
       stochastic
      </a>
      reasons):
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="y = ax^k + \varepsilon.\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/f/6/ef6f2e9f43dbfb50051de7268f99858d.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Mathematically, a strict power law cannot be a probability distribution, but a distribution that is a truncated
      <a href="/wiki/Power_function" title="Power function">
       power function
      </a>
      is possible:
      <img alt="p(x) = C x^{-\alpha}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/b/0/2b0b400f6a74969ff90caaad6edba9cb.png"/>
      for
      <img alt="x &gt; x_\text{min}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/e/3/4e3597b9e3cbed7b762b0c2ac152e62d.png"/>
      where the exponent
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      is greater than 1 (otherwise the tail has infinite area), the minimum value
      <img alt="x_\text{min}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/3/6/a3620f73523ec5c8dd83fc3b7b1374e9.png"/>
      is needed otherwise the distribution has infinite area as
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      approaches 0, and the constant
      <i>
       C
      </i>
      is a scaling factor to ensure that the total area is 1, as required by a probability distribution. More often one uses an asymptotic power law  one that is only true in the limit; see
      <a href="#Power-law_probability_distributions">
       power-law probability distributions
      </a>
      below for details. Typically the exponent falls in the range
      <img alt="2 &lt; \alpha &lt; 3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/1/b/91bd73c75e2418b2e76e86a61f975eba.png"/>
      , though not always.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Power-law_probability_distributions">
       Power-law probability distributions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Power-law probability distributions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In a looser sense, a power-law
      <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">
       probability distribution
      </a>
      is a distribution whose density function (or mass function in the discrete case) has the form
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="p(x) \propto L(x) x^{-\alpha}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/e/d/0ed58256045ecc61e271b97bcfd3d511.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
      <img alt="\alpha &gt; 1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/a/3ca07d8e69d2c1864d7cf392da6bd50f.png"/>
      , and
      <img alt="L(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/5/2054ddef5ff1df5a5e8c1d50a812fefe.png"/>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Slowly_varying_function" title="Slowly varying function">
       slowly varying function
      </a>
      , which is any function that satisfies
      <img alt="\lim_{x\rightarrow\infty} L(r\,x) / L(x) = 1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/c/7/6c760be91b63471ca864f0f985596314.png"/>
      for any positive factor
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      . This property of
      <img alt="L(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/5/2054ddef5ff1df5a5e8c1d50a812fefe.png"/>
      follows directly from the requirement that
      <img alt="p(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/3/4130c89f2d12c3ac81aba3adbff28685.png"/>
      be asymptotically scale invariant; thus, the form of
      <img alt="L(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/5/2054ddef5ff1df5a5e8c1d50a812fefe.png"/>
      only controls the shape and finite extent of the lower tail. For instance, if
      <img alt="L(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/5/2054ddef5ff1df5a5e8c1d50a812fefe.png"/>
      is the constant function, then we have a power law that holds for all values of
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      . In many cases, it is convenient to assume a lower bound
      <img alt="x_{\mathrm{min}}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/b/3/8b30948c22aad8b881dc5644953e527a.png"/>
      from which the law holds. Combining these two cases, and where
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      is a continuous variable, the power law has the form
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="p(x) = \frac{\alpha-1}{x_\min} \left(\frac{x}{x_\min}\right)^{-\alpha}," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/9/1/e91eb126cb94da740a3fc9243769424f.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where the pre-factor to
      <img alt="\frac{\alpha-1}{x_\min}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/4/8/448e0829fbd24a7434d24ce695b3ebf9.png"/>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Normalizing_constant" title="Normalizing constant">
       normalizing constant
      </a>
      . We can now consider several properties of this distribution. For instance, its
      <a href="/wiki/Moment_(mathematics)" title="Moment (mathematics)">
       moments
      </a>
      are given by
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\langle x^{m} \rangle = \int_{x_\min}^\infty x^{m} p(x) \,\mathrm{d}x = \frac{\alpha-1}{\alpha-1-m}x_\min^m" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/2/f/12f2f2b1aa13d7a803ea35d0fbe289d8.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      which is only well defined for
      <img alt="m &lt; \alpha -1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/2/822880a7fc14f1c9ec98b5209569117b.png"/>
      . That is, all moments
      <img alt="m \geq \alpha - 1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/a/7/6a75e3b8a551697dbc1d6cb1bfee7136.png"/>
      diverge: when
      <img alt="\alpha&lt;2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/6/d/86d90d89234a5f554e2ffa82095c7997.png"/>
      , the average and all higher-order moments are infinite; when
      <img alt="2&lt;\alpha&lt;3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/1/b/91bd73c75e2418b2e76e86a61f975eba.png"/>
      , the mean exists, but the variance and higher-order moments are infinite, etc. For finite-size samples drawn from such distribution, this behavior implies that the
      <a href="/wiki/Central_moment" title="Central moment">
       central moment
      </a>
      estimators (like the mean and the variance) for diverging moments will never converge  as more data is accumulated, they continue to grow. These power-law probability distributions are also called
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_distribution" title="Pareto distribution">
       Pareto-type distributions
      </a>
      , distributions with Pareto tails, or distributions with regularly varying tails.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another kind of power-law distribution, which does not satisfy the general form above, is the power law with an exponential cutoff
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="who counts this as a power law? (May 2012)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="p(x) \propto L(x) x^{-\alpha} \mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/f/def19fcf1ee599ca6eda0e1d418320bd.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In this distribution, the exponential decay term
      <img alt="\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/4/e/d4e21d8dc70c613b85addc594dd31c08.png"/>
      eventually overwhelms the power-law behavior at very large values of
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      . This distribution does not scale and is thus not asymptotically a power law; however, it does approximately scale over a finite region before the cutoff. (Note that the pure form above is a subset of this family, with
      <img alt="\lambda=0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/4/9d42faef3cf38affb94af883f23a4cc2.png"/>
      .) This distribution is a common alternative to the asymptotic power-law distribution because it naturally captures finite-size effects. For instance, although the
      <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg%E2%80%93Richter_law" title="GutenbergRichter law">
       GutenbergRichter law
      </a>
      is commonly cited as an example of a power-law distribution, the distribution of earthquake magnitudes cannot scale as a power law in the limit
      <img alt="x\rightarrow\infty" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/4/c/24ce3ddd88d39e1052cb8262ed701be2.png"/>
      because there is a finite amount of energy in the Earth's crust and thus there must be some maximum size to an earthquake. As the scaling behavior approaches this size, it must taper off.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tweedie_distributions" title="Tweedie distributions">
       Tweedie distributions
      </a>
      are a family of statistical models characterized by
      <a href="/wiki/Closure_(mathematics)" title="Closure (mathematics)">
       closure
      </a>
      under additive and reproductive convolution as well as under scale transformation. Consequently these models all express a power-law relationship between the variance and the mean. These models have a fundamental role as foci of mathematical
      <a href="/wiki/Limit_(mathematics)" title="Limit (mathematics)">
       convergence
      </a>
      similar to the role that the
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">
       normal distribution
      </a>
      has as a focus in the
      <a href="/wiki/Central_limit_theorem" title="Central limit theorem">
       central limit theorem
      </a>
      . This convergence effect explains why the variance-to-mean power law manifests so widely in natural processes, as with
      <a href="/wiki/Taylor%27s_law" title="Taylor's law">
       Taylor's law
      </a>
      in ecology and with fluctuation scaling
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendal2011a_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kendal2011a-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in physics. It can also be shown that this variance-to-mean power law, when demonstrated by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tweedie_distributions" title="Tweedie distributions">
       method of expanding bins
      </a>
      , implies the presence of 1/
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      noise and that 1/
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      noise can arise as a consequence of this
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tweedie_distributions" title="Tweedie distributions">
       Tweedie convergence effect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kendal2011b_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kendal2011b-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Graphical_methods_for_identification">
       Graphical methods for identification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Graphical methods for identification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Although more sophisticated and robust methods have been proposed, the most frequently used graphical methods of identifying power-law probability distributions using random samples are Pareto quantile-quantile plots (or Pareto
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q-Q_plot" title="Q-Q plot">
       Q-Q plots
      </a>
      ),
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      mean residual life plots
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Log-log_plot" title="Log-log plot">
       log-log plots
      </a>
      . Another, more robust graphical method uses bundles of residual quantile functions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaz-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (Please keep in mind that power-law distributions are also called Pareto-type distributions.) It is assumed here that a random sample is obtained from a probability distribution, and that we want to know if the tail of the distribution follows a power law (in other words, we want to know if the distribution has a "Pareto tail"). Here, the random sample is called "the data".
     </p>
     <p>
      Pareto Q-Q plots compare the
      <a href="/wiki/Quantile" title="Quantile">
       quantiles
      </a>
      of the log-transformed data to the corresponding quantiles of an exponential distribution with mean 1 (or to the quantiles of a standard Pareto distribution) by plotting the former versus the latter. If the resultant scatterplot suggests that the plotted points " asymptotically converge" to a straight line, then a power-law distribution should be suspected. A limitation of Pareto Q-Q plots is that they behave poorly when the tail index
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      (also called Pareto index) is close to 0, because Pareto Q-Q plots are not designed to identify distributions with slowly varying tails.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaz-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On the other hand, in its version for identifying power-law probability distributions, the mean residual life plot consists of first log-transforming the data, and then plotting the average of those log-transformed data that are higher than the
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      -th order statistic versus the
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      -th order statistic, for
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      =1,...,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      , where n is the size of the random sample. If the resultant scatterplot suggests that the plotted points tend to "stabilize" about a horizontal straight line, then a power-law distribution should be suspected. Since the mean residual life plot is very sensitive to outliers (it is not robust), it usually produces plots that are difficult to interpret; for this reason, such plots are usually called Hill horror plots
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="392" data-file-width="485" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF/300px-LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF/450px-LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LogLogPlot_of_Line.GIF" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A straight line on a log-log plot is strong evidence for power-laws, the slope of the straight line corresponds to the power law exponent.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Log-log_plot" title="Log-log plot">
       Log-log plots
      </a>
      are an alternative way of graphically examining the tail of a distribution using a random sample. This method consists of plotting the logarithm of an estimator of the probability that a particular number of the distribution occurs versus the logarithm of that particular number. Usually, this estimator is the proportion of times that the number occurs in the data set. If the points in the plot tend to "converge" to a straight line for large numbers in the x axis, then the researcher concludes that the distribution has a power-law tail. Examples of the application of these types of plot have been published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A disadvantage of these plots is that, in order for them to provide reliable results, they require huge amounts of data. In addition, they are appropriate only for discrete (or grouped) data.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another graphical method for the identification of power-law probability distributions using random samples has been proposed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_23-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaz-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This methodology consists of plotting a
      <i>
       bundle for the log-transformed sample
      </i>
      . Originally proposed as a tool to explore the existence of moments and the moment generation function using random samples, the bundle methodology is based on residual
      <a href="/wiki/Quantile_function" title="Quantile function">
       quantile functions
      </a>
      (RQFs), also called residual percentile functions,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">

      </sup>
      which provide a full characterization of the tail behavior of many well-known probability distributions, including power-law distributions, distributions with other types of heavy tails, and even non-heavy-tailed distributions. Bundle plots do not have the disadvantages of Pareto Q-Q plots, mean residual life plots and log-log plots mentioned above (they are robust to outliers, allow visually identifying power laws with small values of
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      , and do not demand the collection of much data).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In addition, other types of tail behavior can be identified using bundle plots.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plotting_power-law_distributions">
       Plotting power-law distributions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Plotting power-law distributions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In general, power-law distributions are plotted on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Log-log_graph" title="Log-log graph">
       doubly logarithmic axes
      </a>
      , which emphasizes the upper tail region. The most convenient way to do this is via the (complementary)
      <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_distribution_function#Complementary_cumulative_distribution_function_.28tail_distribution.29" title="Cumulative distribution function">
       cumulative distribution
      </a>
      (cdf),
      <img alt="P(x) = \mathrm{Pr}(X &gt; x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/f/9/ff99eaa26bd4131e242c4c9b8969baf0.png"/>
      ,
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="P(x) = \Pr(X &gt; x) =  C \int_x^\infty p(X)\,\mathrm{d}X =  \frac{\alpha-1}{x_\min^{-\alpha+1}} \int_x^\infty X^{-\alpha}\,\mathrm{d}X = \left(\frac{x}{x_\min} \right)^{-\alpha+1}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/0/e/b0ebc2b06d6f2d00600be0dcdb8ac142.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Note that the cdf is also a power-law function, but with a smaller scaling exponent. For data, an equivalent form of the cdf is the rank-frequency approach, in which we first sort the
      <img alt="n" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/8/7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.png"/>
      observed values in ascending order, and plot them against the vector
      <img alt="\left[1,\frac{n-1}{n},\frac{n-2}{n},\dots,\frac{1}{n}\right]" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/6/bc687c349e1bc9bd909b7e3a33a4aa33.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Although it can be convenient to log-bin the data, or otherwise smooth the probability density (mass) function directly, these methods introduce an implicit bias in the representation of the data, and thus should be avoided.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The cdf, on the other hand, introduces no bias in the data and preserves the linear signature on doubly logarithmic axes.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_the_exponent_from_empirical_data">
       Estimating the exponent from empirical data
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Estimating the exponent from empirical data">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are many ways of estimating the value of the scaling exponent for a power-law tail, however not all of them yield
      <a href="/wiki/Maximum_likelihood#Asymptotics" title="Maximum likelihood">
       unbiased and consistent answers
      </a>
      . Some of the most reliable techniques are often based on the method of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_likelihood_estimation" title="Maximum likelihood estimation">
       maximum likelihood
      </a>
      . Alternative methods are often based on making a linear regression on either the log-log probability, the log-log cumulative distribution function, or on log-binned data, but these approaches should be avoided as they can all lead to highly biased estimates of the scaling exponent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Maximum_likelihood">
       Maximum likelihood
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Maximum likelihood">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      For real-valued,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically_distributed" title="Independent and identically distributed">
       independent and identically distributed
      </a>
      data, we fit a power-law distribution of the form
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="p(x) = \frac{\alpha-1}{x_\min} \left(\frac{x}{x_\min}\right)^{-\alpha}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/e/c/1ec4199c12165bfe225981701a10780f.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      to the data
      <img alt="x\geq x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/8/618f85ff5b478eb05214d3b7a42b8266.png"/>
      , where the coefficient
      <img alt="\frac{\alpha-1}{x_\min}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/4/8/448e0829fbd24a7434d24ce695b3ebf9.png"/>
      is included to ensure that the distribution is
      <a href="/wiki/Normalizing_constant" title="Normalizing constant">
       normalized
      </a>
      . Given a choice for
      <img alt="x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/7/047e850e31d82f786ea973ca2d5e779d.png"/>
      , the log likelihood function becomes:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\mathcal{L}(\alpha)=\log  \prod _{i=1}^n \frac{\alpha-1}{x_\min} \left(\frac{x}{x_\min}\right)^{-\alpha}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/d/e/4de86a761f1fb3472dafe15c4ffaac32.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The maximum of this likelihood is found by differentiating with respect to parameter
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      , setting the result equal to zero. Upon rearrangement, this yields the estimator equation:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\hat{\alpha} = 1 + n \left[ \sum_{i=1}^n \ln \frac{x_i}{x_\min} \right]^{-1}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/e/0/fe09ec9f979040234cd0535462e8a9d2.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
      <img alt="\{x_i\}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/a/b/3ab3ac507e36c93ce399136f06cc7baf.png"/>
      are the
      <img alt="n" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/8/7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.png"/>
      data points
      <img alt="x_{i}\geq x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/1/1/21186528d4781696b2151320117516b3.png"/>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Newman-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hall-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This estimator exhibits a small finite sample-size bias of order
      <img alt="O(n^{-1})" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/8/c/18c1648902858ebcdfc82e7b97e2b23c.png"/>
      , which is small when
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      &gt;100. Further, the standard error of the estimate is
      <img alt="\sigma = \frac{\hat{\alpha}-1}{\sqrt{n}} + O(n^{-1})" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/9/1/a91f2e301ec9e216a4598f9f9163a02a.png"/>
      . This estimator is equivalent to the popular
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hill_estimator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hill estimator (page does not exist)">
       Hill estimator
      </a>
      from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantitative_finance" title="Quantitative finance">
       quantitative finance
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Extreme_value_theory" title="Extreme value theory">
       extreme value theory
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For a set of
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      integer-valued data points
      <img alt="\{x_i\}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/a/b/3ab3ac507e36c93ce399136f06cc7baf.png"/>
      , again where each
      <img alt="x_i\geq x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/5/d/f5d92486139ab409d3730cd949ea6c0b.png"/>
      , the maximum likelihood exponent is the solution to the transcendental equation
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\frac{\zeta'(\hat\alpha,x_\min)}{\zeta(\hat{\alpha},x_\min)} = -\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \ln \frac{x_i}{x_\min} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/4/8/e486fb486494604b2313f86224916400.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
      <img alt="\zeta(\alpha,x_{\mathrm{min}})" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/9/2/892def518b108ce2edf1f8bc4d9c10e0.png"/>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function#Generalizations" title="Riemann zeta function">
       incomplete zeta function
      </a>
      . The uncertainty in this estimate follows the same formula as for the continuous equation. However, the two equations for
      <img alt="\hat{\alpha}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/a/9/da9c842dc02b628b397e0551c00039ed.png"/>
      are not equivalent, and the continuous version should not be applied to discrete data, nor vice versa.
     </p>
     <p>
      Further, both of these estimators require the choice of
      <img alt="x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/7/047e850e31d82f786ea973ca2d5e779d.png"/>
      . For functions with a non-trivial
      <img alt="L(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/5/2054ddef5ff1df5a5e8c1d50a812fefe.png"/>
      function, choosing
      <img alt="x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/7/047e850e31d82f786ea973ca2d5e779d.png"/>
      too small produces a significant bias in
      <img alt="\hat\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/9/3/5931bfc89d29d1e7a9917294ba0d3d07.png"/>
      , while choosing it too large increases the uncertainty in
      <img alt="\hat{\alpha}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/a/9/da9c842dc02b628b397e0551c00039ed.png"/>
      , and reduces the
      <a href="/wiki/Statistical_power" title="Statistical power">
       statistical power
      </a>
      of our model. In general, the best choice of
      <img alt="x_\min" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/7/047e850e31d82f786ea973ca2d5e779d.png"/>
      depends strongly on the particular form of the lower tail, represented by
      <img alt="L(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/5/2054ddef5ff1df5a5e8c1d50a812fefe.png"/>
      above.
     </p>
     <p>
      More about these methods, and the conditions under which they can be used, can be found in .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Further, this comprehensive review article provides
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.santafe.edu/~aaronc/powerlaws/" rel="nofollow">
       usable code
      </a>
      (Matlab, R and C++) for estimation and testing routines for power-law distributions.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kolmogorov.E2.80.93Smirnov_estimation">
       KolmogorovSmirnov estimation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: KolmogorovSmirnov estimation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Another method for the estimation of the power-law exponent, which does not assume
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically_distributed" title="Independent and identically distributed">
       independent and identically distributed
      </a>
      (iid) data, uses the minimization of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kolmogorov%E2%80%93Smirnov_statistic" title="KolmogorovSmirnov statistic">
       KolmogorovSmirnov statistic
      </a>
      ,
      <img alt="D" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/6/2/f623e75af30e62bbd73d6df5b50bb7b5.png"/>
      , between the cumulative distribution functions of the data and the power law:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\hat{\alpha} = \underset{\alpha}{\operatorname{arg\,min}} \, D_\alpha " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/9/5697a9bbd0540eb83c7610f550c215db.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      with
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" D_\alpha = \max_x | P_\mathrm{emp}(x) - P_\alpha(x) | " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/1/1/a11d721f21de39781a4266201e8ccc07.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
      <img alt="P_\mathrm{emp}(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/9/5/2952afbd992cf428e995d8db4f2902af.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="P_\alpha(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/2/9/529e497914fc158fb6e4b3065b4d2ec8.png"/>
      denote the cdfs of the data and the power law with exponent
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      , respectively. As this method does not assume iid data, it provides an alternative way to determine the power-law exponent for data sets in which the temporal correlation can not be ignored.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klaus_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Klaus-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Two-point_fitting_method">
       Two-point fitting method
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Two-point fitting method">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This criterion
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="need to give sme detail of method (May 2012)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      can be applied for the estimation of power-law exponent in the case of scale free distributions and provides a more convergent estimate than the maximum likelihood method.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guerriero_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guerriero-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been applied to study probability distributions of fracture apertures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guerriero_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guerriero-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some contexts the probability distribution is described, not by the
      <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_distribution_function" title="Cumulative distribution function">
       cumulative distribution function
      </a>
      , by the
      <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_frequency_analysis" title="Cumulative frequency analysis">
       cumulative frequency
      </a>
      of a property
      <i>
       X
      </i>
      , defined as the number of elements per meter (or area unit, second etc.) for which
      <i>
       X
      </i>
      &gt;
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      applies, where
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      is a variable real number. As an example,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guerriero_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guerriero-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the cumulative distribution of the fracture aperture,
      <i>
       X
      </i>
      , for a sample of
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      elements is defined as 'the number of fractures per meter having aperture greater than
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      . Use of cumulative frequency has some advantages, e.g. it allows one to put on the same diagram data gathered from sample lines of different lengths at different scales (e.g. from outcrop and from microscope).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="R_function">
       R function
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: R function">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The following function estimates the exponent in R, plotting the log-log data and the fitted line.
     </p>
     <pre>
      pwrdist &lt;- function(u,...) {
       # u is vector of event counts, e.g. how many
       # crimes was a given perpetrator charged for by the police
       fx &lt;- table(u)
       i &lt;- as.numeric(names(fx))
       y &lt;- rep(0,max(i))
       y[i] &lt;- fx
       m0 &lt;- glm(y~log(1:max(i)),family=quasipoisson())
       print(summary(m0))
       sub &lt;- paste("s=",round(m0$coef[2],2),"lambda=",sum(u),"/",length(u))
       plot(i,fx,log="xy",xlab="x",sub=sub,ylab="counts",...)
       grid()
       lines(1:max(i),(fitted(m0)),type="l")
       return(m0)
   }
     </pre>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Validating_power_laws">
       Validating power laws
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Validating power laws">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although power-law relations are attractive for many theoretical reasons, demonstrating that data does indeed follow a power-law relation requires more than simply fitting a particular model to the data.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertPowerLaw_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HilbertPowerLaw-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example
      <a href="/wiki/Log-normal_distribution" title="Log-normal distribution">
       log-normal distributions
      </a>
      are often mistaken for power-law distributions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMitzenmacher2004_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMitzenmacher2004-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Gibrat%27s_law" title="Gibrat's law">
       Gibrat's law
      </a>
      about proportional growth processes can actually produce limiting distributions that are lognormal, although their log-log plots look linear. An explanation of this is that although the logarithm of the
      <a href="/wiki/Log-normal_distribution#Probability_density_function" title="Log-normal distribution">
       lognormal density function
      </a>
      is quadratic in
      <span class="texhtml">
       log(
       <var>
        x
       </var>
       )
      </span>
      , yielding a "bowed" shape in a log-log plot, if the quadratic term is small relative to the linear term then the result can appear almost linear. Therefore a log-log plot that is slightly "bowed" downwards can reflect a log-normal distribution  not a power law. In general, many alternative functional forms can appear to follow a power-law form for some extent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELaherr.C3.A8reSornette1998_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaherr.C3.A8reSornette1998-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, researchers usually have to face the problem of deciding whether or not a real-world probability distribution follows a power law. As a solution to this problem, Diaz
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_23-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaz-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      proposed a graphical methodology based on random samples that allow visually discerning between different types of tail behavior. This methodology uses bundles of residual quantile functions, also called percentile residual life functions, which characterize many different types of distribution tails, including both heavy and non-heavy tails.
     </p>
     <p>
      One method to validate a power-law relation tests many orthogonal predictions of a particular generative mechanism against data. Simply fitting a power-law relation to a particular kind of data is not considered a rational approach. As such, the validation of power-law claims remains a very active field of research in many areas of modern science.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClausetShaliziNewman2009-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
