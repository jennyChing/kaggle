           <p>
            Some things are meant to be mixed. Do you remember the old Reese's cup ads, where a person eating from an open jar of
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="E_x8rMxv5bRyCT62svY0DQ==" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://nutrition.about.com/od/cardiovascular/f/Are-Peanuts-And-Peanut-Butter-Good-For-Your-Health.htm">
             peanut butter
            </a>
            (um... ick) would inexplicably collide with someone
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="x1iZyq9s9M01q84Fkl9cag==" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://biology.about.com/cs/healthmedicine/a/aa021204a.htm">
             eating a chocolate bar
            </a>
            (yum)? "Hey, you got your chocolate in my peanut butter!" "Well, you got your peaut butter on my chocolate!" Reese's cups are awesome.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Other substances don't combine quite so favorably. Bleach and ammonia are an excellent example.
           </p>
           <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
           </div>
           <div class="radlinks ads-half" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">

           </div>
           <p class="cb-split">
            Another example is
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/toxicchemicals/a/Mixing-Bleach-And-Vinegar.htm">
             bleach and vinegar
            </a>
            . I know, I know... if you run a search on Google you'll read that
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="4" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.eurekalert.org/pub_releases/2006-02/asfm-vik021306.php">
             vinegar increases the killing power of bleach
            </a>
            . Yes, it makes the chemicals more likely to kill
            <em>
             you
            </em>
            or your kids or your pets. It also helps to kill anthrax spores. If you find yourself in a position to do an emergency decontamination of an item coated with anthrax, by all means, mix the bleach and vinegar. If you just want a sparkling clean bathroom, use a product designed for that purpose. Bleach combines with acids (e.g., vinegar,
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="y_3EI4FbBTyLNpKNjZ-ImQ==" data-ordinal="5" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/cs/howtos/ht/invisibleink3.htm">
             lemon juice
            </a>
            ) or certain bases (e.g., ammonia, drain cleaner, urine in the catbox) to release
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="6" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/elementfacts/a/chlorine.htm">
             chlorine
            </a>
            and
            <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="7" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://ptcl.chem.ox.ac.uk/MSDS/CH/chloramine.html">
             chloramine
            </a>
            vapors. Realistically, you're probably not going to kill yourself mixing a little bleach and vinegar in a bucket of water (as opposed to mixing them directly). You can get a lethal dose of chlorine by taking a few breaths of it
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="TqJb3tnvjXOG17zhEmZOAg==" data-ordinal="8" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/chemistryglossary/g/concentration.htm">
             at a concentration of
            </a>
            1 part per thousand in air. You can't gauge the concentration by smell, since most people can smell chlorine at a reasonably safe concentration of 35
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="S8B-D0qd4Rn1708412DmkQ==" data-ordinal="9" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/chemistryglossary/g/Parts-Per-Million-Definition.htm">
             parts per million
            </a>
            .
           </p>

           <p>
            The reason that mixing bleach and vinegar is a health hazard is not related to the toxicity levels of Chlorine (chemical symbol Cl2) but due to the toxicity of Chlorine Monoxide (also called Dichlorine Monoxide,
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="J_y2zrL_2EOWnInopCrhvQ==" data-ordinal="12" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/chemistryglossary/g/Chemical-Formula-Definition.htm">
             Chemical formula
            </a>
            Cl2O). Adding any
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="8zingA4FHZf7ecxVff6PBQ==" data-ordinal="13" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/chemistryglossary/a/weakaciddef.htm">
             weak acid
            </a>
            to bleach (or
            <a data-component="link" data-inlink="UCvyvYqBHFI5uASwK-r1TQ==" data-ordinal="14" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://chemistry.about.com/od/factsstructures/ig/Chemical-Structures---S/Sodium-Hypochlorite-Structure.htm">
             Sodium Hypochlorite
            </a>
            , chemical formula NaClO) produces Hypochlorous acid (HClO):
           </p>
           <p>
            NaClO + H2CO3 = NaHCO3 + HClO
           </p>
           <p>
            Dehydrating HClO (with, for example, Calcium Nitrate) releases Cl2O, the gaseous anhydride of HClO. Chlorine Monoxide is a stronger oxiderizer than Cl2, which means it is more reactive with lung tissue and your eyes among other things.
           </p>
