     <p>
      <b>
       -Linolenic acid
      </b>
      (ALA) is an essential
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega-3" title="Omega-3">
       omega-3
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">
       fatty acid
      </a>
      found in seeds (chia, flaxseed, see also table below), nuts (notably walnuts), and many common
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">
       vegetable oils
      </a>
      . In terms of its
      <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">
       structure
      </a>
      , it is named
      <i>
       all
      </i>
      -
      <i>
       cis
      </i>
      -9,12,15-octadecatrienoic acid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beare_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beare-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In physiological literature, it is listed by its lipid number, 18:3, and (
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      3); its isomer GLA is 18:3 (
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      6).
     </p>
     <p>
      -Linolenic acid is a
      <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">
       carboxylic acid
      </a>
      with an 18-carbon chain and three
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cis-trans_isomerism" title="Cis-trans isomerism">
        cis
       </a>
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">
       double bonds
      </a>
      . The first double bond is located at the third carbon from the methyl end of the fatty acid chain, known as the
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      end. Thus, -linolenic acid is a
      <a href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated_fatty_acid" title="Polyunsaturated fatty acid">
       polyunsaturated
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid">
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       3
      </a>
      (omega-3) fatty acid. It is an
      <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">
       isomer
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-linolenic_acid" title="Gamma-linolenic acid">
       gamma-linolenic acid
      </a>
      (GLA), a polyunsaturated
      <a href="/wiki/Omega-6_fatty_acid" title="Omega-6 fatty acid">
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       6
      </a>
      (omega-6) fatty acid.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha-Linolenic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      -Linolenic acid was first isolated by Rollett
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as cited in J. W. McCutcheon's synthesis in 1942,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and referred to in Green and Hilditch's 1930s survey.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was first artificially synthesized in 1995 from C6 homologating agents. A Wittig reaction of the phosphonium salt of [(
      <i>
       Z-Z
      </i>
      )-nona-3,6-dien-1-yl]triphenylphosphonium bromide with methyl 9-oxononanoate, followed by
      <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">
       saponification
      </a>
      , completed the synthesis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_sources">
       Dietary sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha-Linolenic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dietary sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Seed oils are the richest sources of -linolenic acid, notably those of
      <a href="/wiki/Salvia_hispanica" title="Salvia hispanica">
       chia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Perilla_oil" title="Perilla oil">
       perilla
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flaxseed" title="Flaxseed">
       flaxseed
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">
       linseed oil
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">
       rapeseed
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Canola" title="Canola">
       canola
      </a>
      ), and
      <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">
       soybeans
      </a>
      . -Linolenic acid is also obtained from the
      <a href="/wiki/Thylakoid" title="Thylakoid">
       thylakoid membranes
      </a>
      in the leaves of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pisum_sativum" title="Pisum sativum">
        Pisum sativum
       </a>
      </i>
      (pea leaves).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Plant chloroplasts consisting of more than 95 percent of photosynthetic thylakoid membranes are highly fluid due to large abundance of linolenic acid, that shows up as sharp resonances in high resolution carbon-13 NMR spectra, invariably.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some studies state that ALA remains stable during processing and cooking.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, other studies state that ALA might not be suitable for baking, as it will
      <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">
       polymerize
      </a>
      with itself, a feature exploited in
      <a href="/wiki/Drying_oil" title="Drying oil">
       paint
      </a>
      with transition metal catalysts. Some ALA may also oxidize at baking temperatures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_role_in_nutrition_and_health">
       Potential role in nutrition and health
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha-Linolenic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Potential role in nutrition and health">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter,_Linum_usitatissimum,_Nordisk_familjebok.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1082" data-file-width="440" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter%2C_Linum_usitatissimum%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/100px-Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter%2C_Linum_usitatissimum%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter%2C_Linum_usitatissimum%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/150px-Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter%2C_Linum_usitatissimum%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter%2C_Linum_usitatissimum%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png/200px-Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter%2C_Linum_usitatissimum%2C_Nordisk_familjebok.png 2x" width="100"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bekl%C3%A4dnadsv%C3%A4xter,_Linum_usitatissimum,_Nordisk_familjebok.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Flax is a rich source of -linolenic acid.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      -Linolenic acid, an
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      3 fatty acid, is a member of the group of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acids" title="Essential fatty acids">
       essential fatty acids
      </a>
      (EFAs), so called because they cannot be produced within the body and must be acquired through diet. Most seeds and seed oils are much richer in an
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      6 fatty acid,
      <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">
       linoleic acid
      </a>
      . Exceptions include flaxseed (must be ground for proper nutrient absorption) and walnuts. Linoleic acid is also an EFA, but it, and the other
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      6 fatty acids, compete with
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      3s for positions in cell membranes and have very different effects on human health.
      <i>
       (See
       <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid_interactions" title="Essential fatty acid interactions">
        Essential fatty acid interactions
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      -Linolenic acid can only be obtained by humans through their diets because the absence of the required 12- and 15-desaturase enzymes makes
      <i>
       de novo
      </i>
      synthesis from
      <a href="/wiki/Stearic_acid" title="Stearic acid">
       stearic acid
      </a>
      impossible.
      <a href="/wiki/Eicosapentaenoic_acid" title="Eicosapentaenoic acid">
       Eicosapentaenoic acid
      </a>
      (EPA; 20:5,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      3) and
      <a href="/wiki/Docosahexaenoic_acid" title="Docosahexaenoic acid">
       docosahexaenoic acid
      </a>
      (DHA; 22:6,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      3) are readily available from fish and algae oil and play a vital role in many metabolic processes. These can also be synthesized by humans from dietary -linolenic acid, but with an efficiency of only a few percent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because the efficacy of
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      3 long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acid (LC-PUFA) synthesis decreases down the cascade of -linolenic acid conversion, DHA synthesis from -linolenic acid is even more restricted than that of EPA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Conversion of ALA to DHA is higher in women than in men.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A large 2006 study found no association between total -linolenic acid intake and overall risk of prostate cancer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16783606_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid16783606-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Multiple studies
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      have shown a relationship between -linolenic acid and an increased risk of prostate cancer. This risk was found to be irrespective of source of origin (e.g., meat, vegetable oil).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A recent (2009) meta-analysis, however, found evidence of publication bias in earlier studies, and concluded that if ALA contributes to increased prostate cancer risk, the increase in risk is quite small.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stability_and_hydrogenation">
       Stability and hydrogenation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha-Linolenic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stability and hydrogenation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      -Linolenic acid is relatively more susceptible to oxidation and will become rancid more quickly than many other oils.
      <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">
       Oxidative instability
      </a>
      of -linolenic acid is one reason why producers choose to partially
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">
       hydrogenate
      </a>
      oils containing -linolenic acid, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Soybean_oil" title="Soybean oil">
       soybean oil
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">
       Soybeans
      </a>
      are the largest source of edible oils in the U.S., and 40% of soy oil production is partially hydrogenated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, when partially hydrogenated, part of the unsaturated fatty acids become unhealthy
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans_fats" title="Trans fats">
       trans fats
      </a>
      . Consumers are increasingly avoiding products that contain trans fats, and governments have begun to ban trans fats in food products. These regulations and market pressures have spurred the development of low--linolenic acid soybeans. These new soybean varieties yield a more stable oil that doesn't require hydrogenation for many applications, thus providing trans fat-free products, such as frying oil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Several consortia are bringing low--linolenic acid soy to market.
      <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">
       DuPont
      </a>
      's effort involves
      <a href="/wiki/Gene_silencing" title="Gene silencing">
       silencing
      </a>
      the FAD2 gene that codes for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desaturase" title="Desaturase">
       
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       -desaturase
      </a>
      , giving a soy oil with very low levels of both -linolenic acid and linoleic acid.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsanto_Company" title="Monsanto Company">
       Monsanto Company
      </a>
      has introduced to the market
      <a href="/wiki/Vistive" title="Vistive">
       Vistive
      </a>
      , their brand of low -linolenic acid soybeans, which is less controversial than
      <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">
       GMO
      </a>
      offerings, as it was created via conventional breeding techniques.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular">
       Cardiovascular
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alpha-Linolenic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cardiovascular">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There is some evidence ALA consumption might have a slight preventative effect against
      <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">
       cardiovascular diseases
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The effect only registers through
      <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">
       fish
      </a>
      consumption; ALA taken in capsule form has no effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
