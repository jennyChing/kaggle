          <div class="entry-content hentry" id="ec-40848">
           <article>
            <span content="http://tomahawktake.com/2014/06/16/tony-gwynn-braves-pitching-cope/" itemprop="permalink">
            </span>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             You may think that the greatest bane to Braves pitching during its heyday through the 1990s was a slugger like
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/bondsba01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Barry Bonds
              </a>
             </strong>
             . Indeed, Bonds thumped
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/m/maddugr01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Greg Maddux
              </a>
             </strong>
             for 9 homers,
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/g/glavito02.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Tom Glavine
              </a>
             </strong>
             for 5, and
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/s/smoltjo01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               John Smoltz
              </a>
             </strong>
             for another 9. Those three pitchers combined to see him for 385 plate appearances, ranking 1-2-3 for the most of any pitchers vs. Bonds.
            </p>
            <p>
             But they also held that claimant to the homer throne to a combined batting average of under .300.
            </p>
            <p>
             No, there was another thorn in their sides.
             <strong>
              His name was
             </strong>
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/player_search.cgi?results=gwynnto01,gwynnto02&amp;utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Tony Gwynn
              </a>
             </strong>
             , And it wasnt really a close contest.
            </p>
            <p>
             ___________
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Anthony Keith Gwynn, Sr.
             </strong>
             passed away today from cancer at the age of 54.
             <strong>
              He was the best hitter of his generation
             </strong>
             , amassing 3142 hits vs. a ridiculous-sounding 434 strikeouts.
             <strong>
              Thats a career K rate of 4.2%.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              The Best Hitter?
             </strong>
             But shouldnt that be
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/player_search.cgi?results=rosepe02,rosepe01&amp;utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Pete Rose
              </a>
             </strong>
             ? Or
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/c/carewro01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Rod Carew
              </a>
             </strong>
             ? Maybe even
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/brettge01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               George Brett
              </a>
             </strong>
             ?
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/boggswa01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Wade Boggs
              </a>
             </strong>
             ? Well, lets check real quick:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Pete Rose:
              </strong>
              .784 lifetime OPS, 1143 strikeouts, .303 average
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Rod Carew:
              </strong>
              .822 lifetime OPS, 1028 strikeouts, .328 average
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               George Brett:
              </strong>
              .857 lifetime OPS, 907 strikeouts, .305 average
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Wade Boggs:
              </strong>
              .858 lifetime OPS, 745 strikeouts, .328 average
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Tony Gwynn:
              </strong>
              .847 lifetime OPS, 434 strikeouts, .338 average.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             OPS within 11 points of all these players, batting average 10 points higher, and strikeouts less than half most of them.
             <strong>
              Yes. Clearly better.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             As for the strikeout numbers, Ive seen a lot today to put that number into perspective, but lets try this:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Gwynn: 434 career strikeouts in 10,232 plate appearances
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               This year, every major league team has
               <em>
                already
               </em>
               exceeded 434
              </strong>
              excepting 2: the Tigers (with 430, they will hit that shortly), and the Royals (389). That has happened with only 24-26% of the plate appearances that Gwynn had.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
            </p>
            <h2>
             The Braves Connection
            </h2>
            <p>
            </p>
            <div class="related-category">
             <strong>
              LIVE Feed
              <span>
               tony gwynn
              </span>
             </strong>
             <ul>
              <li>
               <a href="http://climbingtalshill.com/2015/06/26/mlb-all-stars-tony-gwynn-don-mattingly-and-my-favorites/?utm_source=FanSided&amp;utm_medium=Network&amp;utm_campaign=RelatedTag">
                <img src="//cdn.fansided.com/wp-content/blogs.dir/141/files/2015/06/mlb-san-diego-padres-tony-gwynn-memorial1-268x150.jpg" style="width: 100%;margin-bottom: 8px;"/>
                MLB All-Stars - Tony Gwynn, Don Mattingly, and My Favorites

              </li>
              <li>
               <a href="http://friarsonbase.com/2015/06/16/my-salute-to-tony-gwynn-one-year-later/?utm_source=FanSided&amp;utm_medium=Network&amp;utm_campaign=RelatedTag">

               </a>
               <span style="font-size:12px;color:#666;font-style:italic;margin-left:5px;">

              </li>
              <li>
               <a href="http://friarsonbase.com/2015/04/03/padres-birthday-spotlight-quilvio-veras/?utm_source=FanSided&amp;utm_medium=Network&amp;utm_campaign=RelatedTag">

            </div>
            When Gwynn retired after the 2001 season, the Braves pitchers should have pooled funds and bought him a gold watch. From 1969 until the end of the 1993 season, the Braves were members of the National Leagues Western Division  along with Gwynns Padres, where Mr. Padre played from 1982-2001. There were lots of opportunities for these teams to face one another, and plenty of time for pitchers to figure out Gwynns weaknesses.
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Apparently, he didnt actually have any.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             Of all pitchers that Gwynn faced, Greg Maddux was the one who saw him the most. Tom Glavine was second on his guest list. John Smoltz was 10th.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              So how did they fare? This will amaze you.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <h3>
             MADDUX.
            </h3>
            <p>
             107 plate appearances.
             <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
              Now mind you, Greg Maddux didnt walk hitters very often
             </span>
             . 1.8 walks per 9 innings for his career  999 total. Of those, 18% (177) were
             <em>
              intentional
             </em>
             . Thats of the 4.9% of the 20,421 batters he faced.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              He walked Tony Gwynn 11 times
             </strong>
              a 10.3% rate. Maddux knew when to cut and run (to the next hitter).
            </p>
            <p>
             Mad Dog
             <em>
              never
             </em>
             was able to strike out Gwynn. While other batters hit .245 against him,
             <strong>
              Gwynn hit .415 and OPSd .997.
             </strong>
             Heck,
             <em>
              Barry Bonds only got him for an .883 OPS
             </em>
             . And he had help!
            </p>
            <p>
             The stats include 8 doubles and a triple with 9 RBI. His 39 hits were the most for any player vs. Maddux, save for
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/biggicr01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Craig Biggio
              </a>
             </strong>
             and
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/player_search.cgi?results=gonzalu01,gonzalu02,gonzal012lui,gonzal022lui,gonzal018lui,gonzal011lui,gonzal014lui&amp;utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Luis Gonzalez
              </a>
             </strong>
             (40 apiece). Im guessing that a lot of additional possible RBI damage was mitigated by Maddux choosing to avoid Gwynn with runners in scoring position.
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <h3>
             GLAVINE.
            </h3>
            <p>
             105 plate appearances. This was at least somewhat in Glavines favor, due to the left-handedness of both pitcher and hitter.
             <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
              So yes, of these three, Glavine fared the best
             </span>
             .
             <strong>
              Kinda
             </strong>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Gwynn got Tom Glavine for 30 hits, a .303 average and .741 OPS.
             </strong>
             <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
              Gwynn also managed 2 homers
             </span>
             . At least Glavine managed to strike him out on 2 occasions, and even induced 4 double plays. Still: not exactly domination over 105 encounters: those 30 hits rank as 3rd most vs. Glavine all-time.
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/bellja01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Jay Bell
              </a>
             </strong>
             and
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/l/larkiba01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Barry Larkin
              </a>
             </strong>
             knocked him around slightly more (33, 31 hits respectively; Bonds matched the 30 hits), though all of these players had more plate appearances than Gwynn.
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <h3>
             SMOLTZ.
            </h3>
            <p>
             Just 75 matchups here.
             <strong>
              Darn good thing. Tony Gwynn destroyed John Smoltz.
             </strong>
             Check this slash line:
             <strong>
              .444/.467/.694/.1.161
             </strong>
             . Thats ridiculous.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              It gets better:
             </strong>
             <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
              recognize that John Smoltz was a strikeout pitcher  his 3084 Ks rank 16th
             </span>
             <em>
              <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
               all-time
              </span>
              .
             </em>
             So how many times did he get Gwynn?
             <strong>
              Once.
             </strong>
             Thats it.
            </p>
            <p>
             Hardly anyone smacked around John Smoltz but Gwynn did so
             <strong>
              regularly
             </strong>
             :
             <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
              Gwynns 32 hits was the most Smoltzy ever gave up to anyone
             </span>
             .
             <strong>
              Biggio
             </strong>
             and
             <strong>
              Bagwell
             </strong>
             had 28 with a
             <em>
              lot
             </em>
             more attempts. Others also got to Smoltz (the Pirates
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/player_search.cgi?results=martial03,martial01&amp;utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Al Martin
              </a>
             </strong>
             ,
             <strong>
              Bonds
             </strong>
             ,
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/g/gracema01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
               Mark Grace
              </a>
             </strong>
             , but none with the consistency of Tony Gwynn.
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              It didnt seem to matter who was thrown out there to face Gwynn:
             </strong>
             heres more numbers from other occasional Braves hurlers:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <strong>
               <a href="http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/s/smithza01.shtml?utm_campaign=Linker&amp;utm_source=direct&amp;utm_medium=linker-tomahawktake.com" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                Zane Smith
