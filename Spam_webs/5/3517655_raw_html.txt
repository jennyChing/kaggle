      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt='"And there is absolutely no truth to the rumour that I am currently growing horns and/or antlers. There will be no further questions."' src="/content/dam/images/1/3/l/e/7/q/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.13lecq.png/1424596344840.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        "And there is absolutely no truth to the rumour that I am currently growing horns and/or antlers. There will be no further questions."
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Glenn Campbell
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       <b>
        What if they gave a troop surge in Iraq and nobody came?
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a decent chance that at some point in the last few months you've been sitting in a pub chatting with friends about the Prime Minister and made a snide joke of the flavour "what Tony Abbott needs to do to ramp up his approval rating is get Australia involved in a proper war, like he's always wanted." And then everyone laughed, and you got your round in because you're a good person and not some sort of monster.
      </p>
      <p>
       So it immediately makes intuitive sense to hear that the PM reportedly gave it a red-hot go last year - not once, but twice.
      </p>
      <p>
       That's the tenor of the jaw-dropping piece in
       <i>
        The Australian
       </i>
       yesterday claiming that
       <a href="http://www.theaustralian.com.au/news/features/tony-abbott-in-command-but-is-peta-credlin-in-control/story-e6frg6z6-1227233035933" target="_blank">
        on November 25th Abbott announced that he wanted to send 3500 Australian ground troops to Northern Iraq to take out ISIL
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       A little later he told his nifty plan to members of the military, who thought - correctly - that it was an incredibly stupid idea, and that maybe it was better to decide strategy by means other than just playing
       <i>
        Clash of Clans
       </i>
       on the toilet.
      </p>
      <p>
       It also reported that four months earlier he'd decided that Australia should send 1000 troops to Ukraine, since his sabre-rattling at Vladimir Putin had seen a nice bump in the opinion polls. Again, the military managed to convince him that maybe starting a war with Russia wasn't the single best idea in the world.
      </p>
      <p>
       Extraordinary, huh?
      </p>
      <p>
       <iframe frameborder="0" height="349" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/0J2QdDbelmY" width="100%">
        &amp;amp;amp;amp;amp;amp;amp;amp;nbsp;
       </iframe>
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Hmmm actually
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Well yes, but maybe not in the way the way it appears.
      </p>
      <p>
       Naturally
       <a href="/federal-politics/political-news/new-wave-of-leaks-forces-tony-abbott-on-to-back-foot-20150221-13l52i.html" target="_blank">
        the PM angrily insisted that there's not truth to the report
       </a>
       : "We have lots of discussions about lots of things all the time but the idea that I would propose a unilateral dispatch of a large Australian force to another country is just fanciful. Simply fanciful."
      </p>
      <p>
       Defence also released a statement declared that there had been no such suggestion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sure, perhaps it was Tony trying to show off what a killer military strategist he would have been after reading all those histories of Gallipoli, but it seems rather more likely that this was a bit of a made-up story leaked to the media from someone close enough to Abbott to know what the effect would be.
      </p>
      <p>
       And not just any media but the one most closely aligned with the Liberal Party generally and Tony Abbott specifically: the Rupert Murdoch-owned paper at which the PM's BFF Greg Sheridan is the foreign editor.
      </p>
      <p>
       The piece also all but declares who was behind it by outing disgruntled former assistant finance minister Arthur Sinodinos as the architect of the spill motion the other week.
      </p>
      <p>
       In other words, this isn't just an annoyed backbench any more. Abbott's fracturing leadership is causing deeper schisms in the party - and there's only two ways back from here.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Jinkies!
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       One is for Abbott to appear grubby and breathless with rope burns on his wrists, declaring that he's the real Tony Abbott and that he was replaced by his evil twin brother on the eve of the election.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ideally the announcement should culminate with Joe Hockey being unmasked as Old Man Withers, who had disguised himself as the Treasurer and made up the Legend of the Budget Crisis in order to scare Australia away from the abandoned amusement park where he was searching for a legendary hidden stash of diamonds ("And I would have gotten away with it too if it wasn't you meddling independents on the senate crossbench!").
      </p>
      <p>
       The less Scooby Doo-themed option would be for Abbott to genuinely reboot his leadership - no more "barnacle scraping", no more "good government starts today" - by blaming his internal woes on Chief of Staff Peta Credlin and his policy missteps on the Treasurer. And he'd have to not just remove them from their roles, but really throw them under the bus.
      </p>
      <p>
       He'd need to argue that his only real mistake was trusting the people closest to him, who were not just incompetent at their jobs but were actively working to undermine him, and that with their perfidy now revealed he can learn from the experience and carry on while justice is served on his treacherous underlings.
      </p>
      <p>
       The problem with that option is that it's impossible, not least because neither Hocket nor Credlin is going to quietly accept their characters and careers being publicy demolished.
      </p>
      <p>
       In other words: um, anyone know some plucky crime-solving teens with a van and a Great Dane?
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        What's in the week ahead?
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       The PM will have plenty to keep him busy over the next week though: there's the big national security speech in which he'll argue, incorrectly, that mandatory data retention is necessary to fight terrorism and child pornography (despite neither being true)
      </p>
      <p>
       And he's got some colourful re-wrangling of
       <a href="/federal-politics/political-news/coin-for-abbott-indonesians-angry-backlash-at-pms-bali-nine-diplomacy-20150221-13l5n2.html" target="_blank">
        the diplomatic fall out with Indonesia
       </a>
       over the whole ill-phrased "we gave you tsunami aid so you owe us" thing regarding asking President Joko Widono to call off the executions of Andrew Chan and Myuran Sukumaran.
      </p>
      <p>
       Oh, and there's
       <i>
        The Saturday Paper
       </i>
       's report that SA senator and long-time Abbott pal
       <a href="http://www.thesaturdaypaper.com.au/news/politics/2015/02/21/inside-sa-senator-cory-bernardis-business-web/14244372001523" target="_blank">
        Cory Bernardi was using his parliamentary office to run his personal business
       </a>
       and failing to report his business dealings on his pecuniary interest statement, while also being the chair of the Parliamentary committee ensuring that parliamentarians declared their pecuniary interests. Which looks a little let's say "problematic".
      </p>
      <p>
       Man, being PM sounds like a nightmare of a job. It'd be a little easier for someone who knew what they were doing, sure, but even so
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        The cocktail hour: a flashback to more innocent times
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the spirit of organisational discord, enjoy this immortal 1978 video to 'I Wanna Love You Tender', aka 'Tahdon olla sulle hell' by Finland's Armi ja Danny: a song that basically screams "we worked out a dance routine for this, and then the song changed at the last minute". That, or the choreographer had fallen asleep watching
       <i>
        Grease
       </i>
       after a hell of a bender.
      </p>
      <p>
       It's an internet classic for a reason, and seems like a good palate cleanser as we begin our descent into what promises to be one of the most baffling weeks in Australian politics.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pour a strong one, and
       <i>
        kippis!
       </i>
      </p>
