       <h1 id="post-344272">
        <a href="http://blog.hemmings.com/index.php/2014/11/21/amc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange/" rel="bookmark">
         AMC designer Vince Geraci on the Marlin: Theres a very fine line between unique and strange
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="meta-line">
        <span class="gray-meta">
         <a href="http://blog.hemmings.com/index.php/author/dan/" rel="author" title="Posts by Daniel Strohl">
          Daniel Strohl
         </a>
         <span class="edit">
         </span>
         Nov 21st, 2014 at 8am
                         |
         <a href="http://blog.hemmings.com/index.php/2014/11/21/amc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange/#comments">
          119 comments
         </a>
         <i class="icon-comment">
         </i>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="main ">
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1965RamblerMarlin_01_700.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1965RamblerMarlin_01_700.jpg">
           <img alt="1965RamblerMarlin_01_700" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-344282" height="485" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1965RamblerMarlin_01_700.jpg" width="700"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <br/>
         <em>
          Image courtesy
          <a href="http://oldcarbrochures.org/NA/AMC/1965_AMC" title="OldCarBrochures">
           OldCarBrochures
          </a>
          .
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         I have a saying that Ive stuck with as a designer, said retired AMC and Chrysler designer Vince Geraci. Theres a fine line between unique and strange. In the styling world, you want to be unique, but you dont want to cross over to strange. I think the Marlin, for its time, was unique.
        </p>
        <p>
         Geraci would know. During his 28 years at AMC, he had a hand in designing both the Gremlin and Pacer  two vehicles that straddled, if not crossed, that fine line  as well as the fastback Marlin, a car that, despite some shortsighted decisions and meddling from AMC management, still caught the eye of a small legion of fans. Those fans are now planning a 50th birthday celebration for the unique Marlin, a celebration that will honor Geraci for his involvement in the cars development.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Marlins story begins with the Bob Nixon-designed Tarpon show car that AMC unveiled at the Society of Automotive Engineers convention in January 1964 in Detroit. A sporty fastback design atop the Rambler American concocted to appeal to the youth market of the time, it generated a good amount of enthusiasm for the ho-hum conservative automaker that primarily concerned itself with building staid economy-oriented family sedans. Yet when AMC management  led by new president Roy Abernethy, who favored building larger cars to compete directly with other Detroit manufacturers  decided to put the fastback design into production, they wanted the V-8 and full complement of seating that a larger car offered.
        </p>
        <p>
         Abernethy was a salesman, he came up through the sales ranks, and so being a salesman, he wanted AMC to have a full line of vehicles, Geraci said. He definitely wanted the Marlin as a regular part of the lineup, not as some sort of halo car.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1965RamblerMarlin_02_700-700x467.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1965RamblerMarlin_02_700.jpg">
           <img alt="1965RamblerMarlin_02_700" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-344292" height="467" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1965RamblerMarlin_02_700-700x467.jpg" width="700"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <br/>
         <em>
          Image courtesy OldCarBrochures.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Thus the project left the small-car studio that Nixon helmed and became a priority for the large-car studio that Geraci led. Geraci  who joined AMC in 1959 after four years with Chrysler in the De Soto, Plymouth, and Imperial studios  set about adapting the fastback roofline to the intermediate-sized Rambler Classic. At about the same time, Plymouth announced its Barracuda fastback, followed a couple weeks later by Fords announcement of the Mustang. The sporty youth market was suddenly getting hot, and American Motors needed to act swiftly to take advantage of it.
        </p>
        <p>
         There was an urgency there, definitely, Geraci said. We were hustling. The Tarpon had whetted their appetite for something exciting.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1967AMCMarlin_03_2000-700x466.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1967AMCMarlin_03_2000.jpg">
           <img alt="1967AMCMarlin_03_2000" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-344322" height="466" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1967AMCMarlin_03_2000-700x466.jpg" width="700"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <br/>
         <em>
          Photo by the author.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Geraci said he and his team  John Star, Dick Jones, Jack Kenitz, and clay modeler Keith Goodnough  considered a number of proposals, mostly to work out the shape of the side window, but they never benchmarked any other vehicle in their design process. As he
         <a href="http://www.hemmings.com/store/detail?product_id=778&amp;source=magazine1" title="Marlin history in HCC #2">
          told Patrick Foster in 2004
         </a>
         , his team largely focused on the shape of the quarter windows, which could make or break the overall roof shape. Whereas Nixons Tarpon used quarter windows that kicked up to meet the roofline, Geracis team went with an elliptical shape repeated in trim just aft of the window opening. Geraci even stepped in to design the Marlins jumping-fish and script logos.
        </p>
        <p>
         AMC management wasnt finished imposing its will on the Marlin, though. While Geraci and his team had a low  almost flat  roof ready to go, he said Abernethy stepped in and asked Geraci to raise the slope of the rear roof by an inch and a half to create more headroom in the backseat.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/VinceGeraci_1500-700x558.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/VinceGeraci_1500.jpg">
           <img alt="VinceGeraci_1500" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-344332" height="558" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/VinceGeraci_1500-700x558.jpg" width="700"/>
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         It bothered the hell out of me, Geraci said. It really took the sweeping line out, so we had to disguise that inch and a half.
        </p>
        <p>
         He credits Goodnough with making the redesigned roofline  now a bit more bulbuous and not as slick  look nowhere near as bad as it couldve.
        </p>
        <p>
         AMC announced the Marlin in February 1965 as a mid-year standalone model, and while its 10,327 sales in that first year hardly posed a threat to the Barracuda, they wouldnt have seemed out of place were the Marlin considered simply another Classic bodystyle. Marlin sales, however, slid to less than half that amount  4,547  for 1966. The decision to shuffle the fastback bodystyle and Marlin nameplate to the
         <a href="http://www.hemmings.com/hcc/stories/2007/08/01/hmn_feature11.html" title="1967 Marlin driveReport">
          longer Ambassador platform for 1967
         </a>
          a change that some observers say led to better overall proportions  didnt help matters; AMC sold just 2,545 Marlins that year before spiking the nameplate partway through the year.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1967AMCMarlin_01_2000-700x459.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1967AMCMarlin_01_2000.jpg">
           <img alt="1967AMCMarlin_01_2000" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-344302" height="459" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/1967AMCMarlin_01_2000-700x459.jpg" width="700"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <br/>
         <em>
          Photo by the author.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         I do consider the 1967 Marlin as the best-looking of the three years, Geraci said. I did the drawing for that one at Dick Teagues behest, and it ended up a very handsome vehicle.
        </p>
        <p>
         Plenty of AMC historians and enthusiasts have since played what-if with the Marlin, speculating whether it would have sold in larger numbers had AMC just put the Tarpon into production or kept Geracis original sleeker roofline. Geraci seems not to obsess too much over what could have been, though.
        </p>
        <p>
         In retrospect, we probably could have found a way to put the V-8 into the American chassis, though I still wouldve changed to the loop rear window, Geraci said. (AMC did, in fact, make its newly redesigned 290-cu.in. V-8s optional in the American in 1966.) Maybe we could have done both the Tarpon and the Marlin  there was a lot of opportunity in that time for unique vehicles.
        </p>
        <p>
         Geraci remained with AMC until Chrysler bought it in 1987, then transferred to Highland Park to continue as the chief stylist in Chryslers exterior studio until he retired in 1990. In the years since, he said hes seen the Marlins styling elements occasionally pop up in new cars, most prominently in the Chrysler Crossfire coupes fastback roofline. That rearend has a taste of the Marlin, much more than the Barracuda, he said.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/modernMarlinclay_700.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/modernMarlinclay_700.jpg">
           <img alt="modernMarlinclay_700" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-345092" height="248" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/modernMarlinclay_700.jpg" width="700"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <br/>
         <em>
          Front and rear views of the modern Marlin clay. Photos courtesy Marlin Auto Club.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_01_700-150x88.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_01_700.jpg">
           <img alt="Marlinofthefuture_01_700" class="alignnone size-thumbnail wp-image-349382" height="88" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_01_700-150x88.jpg" width="150"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_02_700-150x100.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_02_700.jpg">
           <img alt="Marlinofthefuture_02_700" class="alignnone size-thumbnail wp-image-349392" height="100" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_02_700-150x100.jpg" width="150"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="pinterest-button">
          <a class="pin-it" count-layout="horizontal" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.hemmings.com%2Findex.php%2F2014%2F11%2F21%2Famc-designer-vince-geraci-on-the-marlin-theres-a-very-fine-line-between-unique-and-strange%2F&amp;media=http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_03_700-150x112.jpg&amp;description=AMC+designer+Vince+Geraci+on+the+Marlin%3A+%26%238220%3BThere%26%238217%3Bs+a+very+fine+line+between+unique+and+strange%26%238221%3B">
          </a>
          <a href="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_03_700.jpg">
           <img alt="Marlinofthefuture_03_700" class="alignnone size-thumbnail wp-image-349402" height="112" src="http://assets.blog.hemmings.com/wp-content/uploads//2014/10/Marlinofthefuture_03_700-150x112.jpg" width="150"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <br/>
         <em>
          Three more views of the modern Marlin clay. Photos courtesy Frank Pascoe.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         He also believes the Marlin could make it as a modern car with some tweaking to the bumpers and fascias, which is what led him and a small group of other retired Detroit stylists to work up a clay model of a modern Marlin a few years ago. The 1/5-scale model, which he completed with Goodnoughs assistance and introduced at the 2010 American Motors Owners club meet, has yet to progress beyond the clay stage, but he said he hopes to soon produce some more permanent models from it.
        </p>
        <p>
         The model will accompany Geraci to next years 50th anniversary celebration of the Marlin, which the Marlin Auto Club has planned to take place during the 2015 AMO National meet. Mark Zeno, board chairman of the club, said he hopes to make the celebration the largest-ever gathering of Marlins.
        </p>
        <p>
         The celebration and the AMO National meet will take place July 23-24 in Independence, Ohio. For more information, visit
         <a href="http://www.marlinautoclub.com/" title="Marlin Auto Club">
          MarlinAutoClub.com
         </a>
         or
         <a href="http://www.amonational.com/" title="AMO National">
          AMONational.com
         </a>
         .
        </p>

           <p>
            The 1965 Marlin was great (as long as you didnt look at its profile directly from the side) and the 66 was slightly better due to the cleaner grill (it was slightly different). The 67Marlin was beautiful and the front was an Ambassador front end (the car was, of course, based on the Ambassador that year) with the grill somewhat blacked out to make the already clean design look even cleaner. The back-ends of all Marlins were very well-done and the 67 was the best of all in that regard. Also, with the longer Ambassador wheelbase the roof hump was gone but there was apparently no market for such a large luxury (not performance based) fastback and, in any regards, AMC did not really promote it well.
           </p>
           <p>
            AMC had some of the cleanest designs starting with the 1964 Rambler American which got me interested in their cars.
           </p>
           <p>
            I always wished that they would have used the Marlin back-ends on the Matadors or Ambassadors just as I wished they would have grafted the Tarpon back-end onto the 1965 Rambler American.
           </p>
           <p>
            The best year for AMC model lineup wise was 1967 for their almost totally redesigned lineup and, although they did spend some bucks on promotion (the American was a carryover) there was no way they could compete with the big three model for model.
           </p>
           <p>
            They continued with great designs like the 1968 Javelin, the AMX, the 1970 Hornet and yes, even the 1975-1977 Pacer until they screwed up the grill to accommodate a V-8 for 1978 and beyond. The Pacer Wagons were also very attractive but only for 1977 due to the same grille problem.  As time went on they really mucked-up the Hornet up by adding vinyl roof to the aging Hornet and calling it the Concord and by and screwing up what began as the Rebel (in 1967) by altering their rear ends and rooflines.
           </p>
           <p>
            I could have cried when I saw what they did as early as 1968 to the back end of the Ambassador. It was an abomination as they welded a piece of metal over what were the taillights and moved them beneath the trunk lid. Even with such little money for redesigns they could have done better. Heck. I could have done better.
           </p>
           <p>
            Near the end of their years I thought that perhaps they should have abandoned them all
            <br/>
            to build and promote  just one model fresh from the ground up designed to appeal to the people who would buy a Volvo, Saab etc.
           </p>
           <p>
            BTW I owned a 1966 Rambler American, a 1972 Hornet (my first new car) and a 1976 Pacer and they were all great cars.

                     <p>
                      I never claimed to be an AMC historian but, beginning in the early 1960s, I did become an avid AMC fan and followed that corporation very, very closely. I read all related periodicals at that time and, if there was any fallout from the Consumer Reports alleged criticism of the 1967 Ambassador, said fallout would have to have so been small to virtually non-existent with an equally negligible impact on sales. I have yet to hear you explain why Consumer Reports did not criticize the 1967 Rebel as it was virtually the exact same car in terms of the problem you alluded to.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      I never said that advertising was the only barrier to entry into the US auto market. The barrier is the problem that independents faced which was higher costs of per unit production resulting from having the same fixed costs (including advertising) spread over a smaller number of units. The big three had economies of scale. Nash and Hudson combined (as did Studebaker and Packard) to try to be able to deal with that issue but, in the end, it was to no avail.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      The foreign auto companies like VW, Toyota, and Datsun were already producing cars in their home countries and, given the shoddy quality of US cars and gas prices (especially by the 1970s) it was not all that difficult for them to establish a foothold in the US.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      I was amazed to read your statement that all it takes to succeed in the auto market was a superior product with good advertising as there have been many cases of car companies failing that had both.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      AMC made many good decisions and very few missteps and even one major misstep for such a small company could have been fatal. That misstep was, in my opinion, hitching their wagon to the falling star of Renault which has always been a poor (to say the least) product.  The Alliance deal sounded good (I even considered buying one) but the car was a piece of junk as was the Encore and Fuego.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      I think AMC would have had a fighting chance had they focused their resources on one car with a different limited options approach. One problem would have been the development costs of engineering a front wheel drive vehicle (fixed costs a problem once again) but perhaps they could have stuck with rear-wheel drive as Mercedes and Volvo had done.
                     </p>
