     <p>
      <b>
       Rosewood
      </b>
      refers to any of a number of richly hued
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">
       timbers
      </a>
      , often brownish with darker veining but found in many different hues.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="True_rosewoods">
       True rosewoods
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosewood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: True rosewoods">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      All genuine rosewoods belong to the
      <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">
       genus
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia" title="Dalbergia">
        Dalbergia
       </a>
      </i>
      . The pre-eminent rosewood appreciated in the Western world is the wood of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia_nigra" title="Dalbergia nigra">
        Dalbergia nigra
       </a>
      </i>
      which is now
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rosewood&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species_of_Wild_Flora_and_Fauna" title="Convention on the International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Flora and Fauna">
       CITES
      </a>
      -listed endangered species. It is best known as Brazilian Rosewood, but also as
      <i>
       Bahia Rosewood
      </i>
      . This wood has a strong sweet smell, which persists over many years, explaining the name rosewood.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another classic rosewood comes from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia_latifolia" title="Dalbergia latifolia">
        Dalbergia latifolia
       </a>
      </i>
      known as (East) Indian Rosewood or sonokeling. It is native to
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      and is also grown in plantations elsewhere in
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Madagascar rosewood (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia_maritima" title="Dalbergia maritima">
        Dalbergia maritima
       </a>
      </i>
      ), known as
      <i>
       bois de rose
      </i>
      , is highly prized for its red color but is overexploited in the wild. Despite a 2010 moratorium on trade, illegal logging continues on a large scale.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Throughout S.E. Asia
      <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia_oliveri" title="Dalbergia oliveri">
       Dalbergia oliveri
      </a>
      is harvested for use in woodworking. It has a very fragrant and dense grain near the core, however the outer sapwood is soft and porous.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia_sissoo" title="Dalbergia sissoo">
        Dalbergia sissoo
       </a>
      </i>
      is also a rosewood species from India and Bangladesh. It is extremely dense, strong, and durable. It is stronger than teak and is comparable in price. The valuable timber is used for quality furnishings.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of the world's rosewood comes from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dalbergia_retusa" title="Dalbergia retusa">
        Dalbergia retusa
       </a>
      </i>
      , also known as the Nicaraguan rosewood or as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palisander" title="Palisander">
       palisander
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Several species are known as Guatemalan
      <i>
       Rosewood
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Panama Rosewood
      </i>
      : D. tucerencis, D. tucarensis, and D. cubiquitzensis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Not all species in the large genus
      <i>
       Dalbergia
      </i>
      yield rosewoods; only about a dozen species do. The woods of some other species in the genus
      <i>
       Dalbergia
      </i>
      are notableeven famouswoods in their own right:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_Blackwood" title="African Blackwood">
       African Blackwood
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cocobolo" title="Cocobolo">
       Cocobolo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kingwood_(wood)" title="Kingwood (wood)">
       Kingwood
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Tulipwood" title="Tulipwood">
       Tulipwood
      </a>
      . The woods of some other species are usable for tool handles at best.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_rosewoods">
       Other
       <i>
        rosewoods
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosewood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other rosewoods">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The timber trade will sell many timbers under the name rosewood (usually with an adjective) due to some (outward) similarities. A fair number of these timbers come from other
      <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">
       legume
      </a>
      genera; one such species that is often mentioned is Bolivian
      <i>
       Machaerium scleroxylon
      </i>
      sold as Bolivian Rosewood. Another that may be found in advertisements from Asia is
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pterocarpus_indicus" title="Pterocarpus indicus">
        Pterocarpus indicus
       </a>
      </i>
      sold as New Guinea Rosewood (and related species).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although its wood bears no resemblance whatsoever to the true rosewoods, the Australian Rose Mahogany (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dysoxylum_fraserianum" title="Dysoxylum fraserianum">
        Dysoxylum fraserianum
       </a>
      </i>
      , family
      <a href="/wiki/Meliaceae" title="Meliaceae">
       Meliaceae
      </a>
      ) is sometimes also called "rosewood" due to the strong smell of roses from freshly cut bark.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">
       Uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosewood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      All rosewoods are strong and heavy, taking an excellent polish, being suitable for
      <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">
       guitars
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marimba" title="Marimba">
       marimbas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">
       recorders
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turnery" title="Turnery">
       turnery
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">
       billiard cues
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fountain_pens" title="Fountain pens">
       fountain pens
      </a>
      , black pieces in
      <a href="/wiki/Chess_piece" title="Chess piece">
       chess sets
      </a>
      , etc.), handles,
      <a href="/wiki/Furniture" title="Furniture">
       furniture
      </a>
      , luxury
      <a href="/wiki/Wood_flooring" title="Wood flooring">
       flooring
      </a>
      , etc.
      <a href="/wiki/Rosewood_oil" title="Rosewood oil">
       Rosewood oil
      </a>
      , used in
      <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">
       perfume
      </a>
      , is extracted from the wood of
      <a href="/wiki/Aniba_rosaeodora" title="Aniba rosaeodora">
       Aniba rosaeodora
      </a>
      , which is not related to the rosewoods used for lumber.
     </p>
     <p>
      In general, world stocks are poor through
      <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">
       overexploitation
      </a>
      . Some species become
      <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">
       canopy
      </a>
      trees (up to 30 m high), and large pieces can occasionally be found in the trade.
     </p>
     <p>
      The dust created from sanding rosewood is considered a
      <a href="/wiki/Allergic_response" title="Allergic response">
       sensitizing irritant
      </a>
      and can trigger asthma and other respiratory ailments. Often, the more a person is exposed to rosewood dust, the more sensitive they can become to exposure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Madagascar_rosewood_logging_controversy">
       Madagascar rosewood logging controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosewood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Madagascar rosewood logging controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging_in_Madagascar" title="Illegal logging in Madagascar">
       Illegal logging in Madagascar
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 2009 controversy arose surrounding the intensification of rosewood logging in
      <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">
       Madagascar
      </a>
      's national parks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Logging was linked to criminal syndicates that laundered rosewood logs through
      <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Runion">
       Reunion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">
       Mauritius
      </a>
      before transporting timber to
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      for processing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finished wood and furniture was then shipped to
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      . In November 2009
      <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Guitar_Corporation" title="Gibson Guitar Corporation">
       Gibson Guitar Corporation
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">
       Nashville
      </a>
      was raided by federal authorities for its alleged use of illegally sourced rosewood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The case was settled on August 6, 2012, with Gibson admitting to violating the
      <a href="/wiki/Lacey_Act_of_1900" title="Lacey Act of 1900">
       Lacey Act
      </a>
      and agreeing to pay a fine of $300,000 in addition to a $50,000 community payment. Gibson also forfeited the wood seized in the raids, which was valued at roughly the same amount as the settlement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_6Aug2012_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC_6Aug2012-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In March 2010 the Malagasy government finally announced a ban of the rosewood export for a period between 2 and 5 years.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
