       <h1>
        $15 Minimum Wage To Appear on Chicago Ballots
       </h1>
       <span class="author">
        BY
        <a href="/community/profile/321918">

        <p class="photo-caption">
         Activists with Fight for 15 in Chicago have been agitating for over a year for a $15 minimum wage. Now, the Raise Chicago Coalition is putting the question to voters. (
         <a href="http://instagram.com/p/corKO9Pgq4/">
          fightfor15 on Instagram
         </a>
         )
         <span>
         </span>
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        <em>
         Reprinted with permission from
         <a href="http://www.chicagoreporter.com/chicago-labor-activists-seek-voters-support-raising-minimum-wage#.UrSqt67gvmY">
          The Chicago Reporter
         </a>
         .
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        For years now, lawmakersin both Chicago and the Illinois legislaturehave been going back and forth over whether they should raise the floor on wages.
       </p>
       <p>
        Few disagree that to keep a roof over your head and food in your mouth, a job that pays minimum wage, $8.25 an hour, just isnt going to cut it. But each time a proposal in introduced, the prevailing question ends up being less about people living hand-to-mouth and more about whether businesses can afford to pay higher wages.
       </p>
       <p>
        Not even cajoling
        <a href="http://www.suntimes.com/news/22302220-418/quinn-keeps-pushing-to-raise-illinois-minimum-wage.html">
         from the governor
        </a>
        or
        <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/11/08/business/10-minimum-wage-proposal-has-obamas-backing.html">
         the president
        </a>
        has brought change,at least not yet.
       </p>
       <p>
        Now some Chicago labor activists are appealing to a new audience for support: voters.
       </p>
       <p>
        On December 11, they turned in a pile of signed petitions calling for a March 18, 2014 ballot initiative that will ask voters whether they support hiking the minimum wage to $15 an hour for employees who work in companies that clear at least $50 million in annual profits. The referendum question is only advisory. Itll be on ballots in 102 precincts that span 20 of the citys aldermanic wards.
       </p>
       <p>
        Were hoping to show that a majority of people who live, breathe and work in Chicago recognize that minimum-wage workers need a raise, said Katelyn Johnson, the executive director of Action Now, a community organization backed by union money that spearheaded the petition drive. Under home rule authority, Chicago has the ability to set its own wage standard, she points out.
       </p>
       <p>
        The last time the Chicago City Council took up a proposal to do so was in 2006. After a long, drawn-out campaign, aldermen agreed to raise the minimum wage to $10 an hour by 2010but only for employees at big box stores. Under the ordinance, those companies would also have been required to pay $3-an-hour more toward benefits.
       </p>
       <p>
        Former Mayor Richard M. Daley used the sole veto vote of his career to block that measure. And aldermen
        <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2006/09/14/us/14bigbox.html">
         failed to override
        </a>
        the veto.
       </p>
       <p>
        These days, public opinion polling for raising the minimum wage appears to be strong.
       </p>
       <p>
        Earlier this year, Crains Chicago Business
        <a href="http://www.chicagobusiness.com/article/20130220/BLOGS02/130219739/">
         surveyed
        </a>
        Illinois residents and found that a majority support bringing the minimum wage up to $10 an hour. Support was strongest in Chicago, where 78 percent of people polled expressed support, Crains reported.
       </p>
       <p>
        National support appears high as well. In July, the public opinion polling firm Rasmussen Reports
        <a href="http://www.rasmussenreports.com/public_content/business/jobs_employment/july_2013/61_favor_raising_minimum_wage_to_10_10_an_hour">
         reported
        </a>
        that 90 percent of people surveyed said they couldnt make it on the national minimum wage (which at $7.25, is a whole dollar below Illinois).
       </p>
       <p>
        Neither poll tested opinions on raising the wage to $15 an hour, which Johnson calls the bare minimum for pulling more working families out of poverty.
       </p>
