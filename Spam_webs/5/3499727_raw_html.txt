     <p>
      <b>
       Air Canada Flight 797
      </b>
      was a scheduled trans-border flight that flew from
      <a href="/wiki/Dallas/Fort_Worth_International_Airport" title="Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport">
       Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al%E2%80%93Pierre_Elliott_Trudeau_International_Airport" title="MontralPierre Elliott Trudeau International Airport">
       MontralPierre Elliott Trudeau International Airport
      </a>
      , with an intermediate stop at
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson_International_Airport" title="Toronto Pearson International Airport">
       Toronto Pearson International Airport
      </a>
      . On 2 June 1983, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-9-32" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-9-32">
       McDonnell Douglas DC-9-32
      </a>
      operating the service developed an in-flight fire behind the
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_lavatory" title="Aircraft lavatory">
       washroom
      </a>
      that spread between the outer skin and the inner decor panels, filling the plane with toxic smoke. The spreading fire also burned through crucial electrical cables that knocked out most of the instrumentation in the cockpit, forcing the plane to divert to an alternative landing field. Ninety seconds after the plane landed and the doors were opened, the heat of the fire and fresh oxygen from the open exit doors created
      <a href="/wiki/Flashover" title="Flashover">
       flashover
      </a>
      conditions, and the plane's interior immediately became engulfed in flames, killing 23 passengers who had yet to evacuate the aircraft.
     </p>
     <p>
      As a result of this accident, aviation regulations around the world were changed to make aircraft safer, with new requirements to install
      <a href="/wiki/Smoke_detector" title="Smoke detector">
       smoke detectors
      </a>
      in lavatories and emergency lighting leading to exit doors, and increased fire fighting training and equipment for crew members.
     </p>
     <p>
      At the time of the accident, Air Canada had 41 DC-9s in its fleet (excluding the accident aircraft).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Details">
       Details
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Canada_Flight_797&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      At 16:20
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Time" title="Australian Time">
       CDT
      </a>
      (21:20 UTC) on 2 June 1983,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Air Canada aircraft,
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_registration" title="Aircraft registration">
       registered
      </a>
      C-FTLU,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      took off from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas-Fort_Worth_International_Airport" title="Dallas-Fort Worth International Airport">
       Dallas-Fort Worth International Airport
      </a>
      ; it was to make a stop at
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson_International_Airport" title="Toronto Pearson International Airport">
       Toronto International Airport
      </a>
      , ultimately bound for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al-Pierre_Elliott_Trudeau_International_Airport" title="Montral-Pierre Elliott Trudeau International Airport">
       Dorval Airport
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorval,_Quebec" title="Dorval, Quebec">
       Dorval, Quebec
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Donald Cameron, 51, was the captain and Claude Ouimet, 34, served as first officer. Both were experienced pilots  Cameron had accrued approximately 13,000 flight hours and Ouimet had 5,650 hours. While flying over
      <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">
       Louisville, Kentucky
      </a>
      , an in-flight fire started in or around the rear lavatory of the aircraft. The pilots heard a popping sound around 18:51, during dinner service, and discovered that the lavatory's
      <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker" title="Circuit breaker">
       circuit breakers
      </a>
      had tripped. It was not uncommon, however, for a plane's lavatory circuits to pop occasionally, precipitated by passengers using the toilet after eating,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      so Cameron waited around eight minutes to give the tripped circuits time to cool down before attempting to reactivate them at 18:59.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A strong, noxious odour coming from the rear of the plane was first reported to the cabin crew around 19:00.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      33-year-old flight attendant Judi Davidson traced the odour to the lavatory. She attempted to look into the lavatory but was forced back by thick grey smoke rapidly filling the small room; she then ordered the third ranking crew member to find Sergio Benetti, 37, the chief cabin crew officer, and ask him to investigate. As Benetti sprayed the interior of the affected lavatory with a CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      extinguisher, Davidson reported the fire to the captain while 28-year-old flight attendant Laura Kayama began urging the passengers on the sparsely-populated flight to come away from the front and rear of the plane and sit close together near the over-wing exits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At no time did any of the cabin crew mention to either Cameron or Ouimet that they had not yet seen flames, and Benetti, the one person who had managed to get a good look at the scene, had not reported seeing anything resembling the traditional causes of airline fires in pre-smoking-ban days: flames from a
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_container" title="Waste container">
       trash bin
      </a>
      fire or the paper towel dispenser,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      both of which were commonly set ablaze by passengers smoking in the lavatories on long flights.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      First officer Ouimet went to investigate himself at 19:03, but was driven back by the thick smoke. At 19:04, Benetti reported that he thought the fire was probably out because he had completely doused the lavatory with fire retardant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Just three minutes later, however, at 19:07, passengers reported smelling smoke in the cabin again. Two minutes after that the "master breaker" alarm went off in the cockpit, and electrical systems throughout the plane began to fail, including power for the elevator trim system. This made controlling the plane's descent extremely difficult and required great physical exertion from the pilot and first officer. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PA_system" title="PA system">
       PA system
      </a>
      also failed, leaving the flight attendants unable to communicate efficiently with the passengers; nevertheless, attendants were able to instruct passengers sitting in the exit rows on how to open the doors, a practice that was not standard on commercial airline flights at the time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At 19:20, Cameron and Ouimet made an extremely difficult
      <a href="/wiki/Emergency_landing" title="Emergency landing">
       emergency landing
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati/Northern_Kentucky_International_Airport" title="Cincinnati/Northern Kentucky International Airport">
       Cincinnati/Northern Kentucky International Airport
      </a>
      , located in
      <a href="/wiki/Boone_County,_Kentucky" title="Boone County, Kentucky">
       Boone County, Kentucky
      </a>
      across the
      <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">
       Ohio River
      </a>
      from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">
       Cincinnati, Ohio
      </a>
      . During the evacuation, the overwing aircraft doors were opened, causing an influx of air that fuelled the fire. Ouimet escaped through the co-pilot's emergency window shortly after the plane landed, but Cameron, who had expended a great deal of physical energy while trying to keep the plane under control, was unable to move due to exhaustion. Firefighters doused Cameron in firefighting foam through Ouimet's window, shocking him back to consciousness; Cameron was then able to open the pilot's emergency escape window and drop to the ground, where he was dragged to safety by Ouimet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cameron was the last person to make it out of the plane alive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Less than 90 seconds after touchdown, the interior of the plane flashed over and ignited, killing 23 of the 41 passengers. The passengers trapped inside the plane died from
      <a href="/wiki/Smoke_inhalation" title="Smoke inhalation">
       smoke inhalation
      </a>
      and burns from the
      <a href="/wiki/Flash_fire" title="Flash fire">
       flash fire
      </a>
      . Dianne Fadley, a survivor, said that "it was almost like anybody who got out had nothing wrong";
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the eighteen surviving passengers, three received serious injuries, thirteen received minor injuries, and two were uninjured, while none of the five crew members sustained any injuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "You made it and you were completely fine," Fadley concluded, "or you didn't make it."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Twenty-one Canadians and two Americans died. Many of the bodies were burned beyond recognition. Almost all of the victims were in the forward half of the aircraft between the wings and the cockpit. Some bodies were in the aisles, and some bodies were still in the seats. Two victims were in the back of the aircraft, even though the passengers were moved forward after the fire had been detected; the disoriented passengers moved beyond the overwing exits and succumbed. The blood samples from the bodies revealed high levels of
      <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">
       cyanide
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">
       fluoride
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      , chemicals produced by the burning plane.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="NTSB_investigation">
       NTSB investigation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Canada_Flight_797&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NTSB investigation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Because the accident occurred in the United States, it was investigated by the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Board" title="National Transportation Safety Board">
       National Transportation Safety Board
      </a>
      (NTSB). Though the fuselage was nearly destroyed by the intensity of the fire, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockpit_voice_recorder" title="Cockpit voice recorder">
       cockpit voice recorder
      </a>
      (CVR) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_data_recorder" title="Flight data recorder">
       flight data recorder
      </a>
      (FDR) for flight 797 were still in good condition and produced vital data for the NTSB investigation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the CVR, NTSB investigators heard eight sounds of electrical arcinglikely inaudible to the crewbeginning at 18:48 CDT. Three minutes later, at 18:51, the popping sounds that Cameron and Ouimet would later identify as the left toilet circuit breaker tripping are audible on the CVR; Cameron attempts to reset the circuits twice over the next 60 seconds, but the CVR records the breakers immediately popping again after each reset attempt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVR_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CVR-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cameron would attempt once more to reset the breakers at 18:59, but the CVR records arcing sounds followed by the popping sound of the breakers continuing to trip again after each reset over the next 60 seconds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVR_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CVR-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At 19:02, the CVR records flight attendant Judi Davidson entering the cockpit to deliver the first report of a possible fire in the lavatory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVR_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CVR-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Though a number of wires in the lavatory section were later found with insulation stripped away, NTSB investigators were unable to determine whether this insulation damage was the cause of the fire or was caused by the fire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This particular DC-9 had experienced a number of problems over the months leading up to the incident; 76 maintenance reports had been filed in the plane's logs in the previous year,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the CVR records Cameron telling Ouimet to "put [the tripping breakers] in the book there" when the breakers fail to respond to the first reset attempt at 18:52.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CVR_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-CVR-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nearly four years earlier, on 17 September 1979, the plane, then serving as Air Canada Flight 680 (Boston, Massachusetts to Yarmouth, Nova Scotia), had suffered an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_decompression" title="Explosive decompression">
       explosive decompression
      </a>
      in the rear bulkhead that required rebuilding the tail section and replacing or splicing most of the wiring and hydraulic lines in the back of the plane; Cameron later noted that the Air Canada maintenance crew "did a heck of a job getting everything put back together" after the decompression incident.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Investigators were unable to find signs of arcing in any of the wire splices from the repairs done four years earlier, though much of the wiring in the rear of the plane was severely damaged or destroyed by the fire itself.
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite finding neither the specific wires that caused the short circuit (the usual cause of arcing sounds and the likely cause of the breaker trips) nor the origin point of the fire that later consumed the plane, investigators determined that a short circuit likely sparked and ignited surrounding materials (such as insulation blankets) that sustained and propagated a fire behind the wall of the lavatory, with the plane's outer skin serving as a conduit for smoke to seep in through the seams in the interior panels and collect near the apex of the cabin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">
       Aftermath
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Canada_Flight_797&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Air Canada has not suffered another fatal accident since 1983.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_recommendations">
       Safety recommendations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Canada_Flight_797&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety recommendations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As a result of this accident
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and other incidents of in-flight fires on passenger aircraft, the NTSB issued several recommendations to the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">
       Federal Aviation Administration
      </a>
      (FAA), including:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Safety Recommendation A-83-70, which asked the FAA to expedite actions to require
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smoke_detectors" title="Smoke detectors">
        smoke detectors
       </a>
       in lavatories;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Safety Recommendation A-83-71, which asked the FAA to require the installation of automatic fire extinguishers adjacent to and in lavatory waste receptacles;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Strong recommendation that all US-based air carriers review their fire training and evaluation procedures; procedures were to be shortened and focused on taking "aggressive actions" to determine the source and severity of suspected cabin fires while finding the shortest and safest possible emergency descents, including landing or ditching;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Strong suggestion that passenger instruction in how to open emergency exits become standard practice within the airline industry.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Strong recommendation for expedited FAA rule changes mandating that all US-based air carriers install (or improve existing) in-cabin fire safety enhancements, including (but not limited to):
       <ul>
        <li>
         Fire-blocking seat materials to limit both the spread of fire and the generation of toxic chemicals through ignition;
        </li>
        <li>
         Emergency track lighting at or near the floor, strong enough to cut through heavy fuel fire smoke;
        </li>
        <li>
         Raised markings on overhead bins indicating the location of exit rows to aid passengers in locating these rows in case of passenger visual impairment (either pre-existing or caused by emergency conditions);
        </li>
        <li>
         Hand-held fire extinguishers using advanced technology extinguishing agents such as
         <a href="/wiki/Halomethane#Fire_extinguishing" title="Halomethane">
          Halon
         </a>
         .
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTSB_1-10">
          <a href="#cite_note-NTSB-1">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           1
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversy">
       Criticism and controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Canada_Flight_797&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism and controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Newspapers and other media criticized the actions taken by the crew and said that the pilots took too long to initiate an emergency descent; the initial NTSB report was especially critical of Cameron for not asking about the exact nature of the fire and not immediately initiating emergency descent when the fire was first reported. Cameron admitted in a press conference following the issuance of the NTSB report that he assumed the problem was a
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_container" title="Waste container">
       garbage bin
      </a>
      fire, a common cause of lavatory fires when smoking was still allowed on flights.
     </p>
     <p>
      Pilots and airline personnel throughout the industry petitioned the NTSB to revise its report.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      First Officer Claude Ouimet sent the NTSB a detailed defence of the crew's actions, including the decision to land in Cincinnati instead of
      <a href="/wiki/Louisville_International_Airport" title="Louisville International Airport">
       Standiford Field Airport
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">
       Louisville, Kentucky
      </a>
      , the airport closest to where the crew first notified Air Traffic Control in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indianapolis,_Indiana" title="Indianapolis, Indiana">
       Indianapolis, Indiana
      </a>
      that they needed to make an emergency landing. Ouimet stated that Louisville was too close to be able to descend from cruising altitude to an emergency landing safely, and even landing in Cincinnati was a questionable proposition given Cameron's difficulties in controlling the plane. After reviewing Ouimet's missive and re-evaluating the available data, the NTSB issued a revised version of the report which included Ouimet's explanation of the landing decision, though the report was still critical of Cameron's decision not to inquire about the fire itself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Of his actions that day, Cameron later said "all I know was that I did the best I could."
     </p>
     <p>
      The crew of Flight 797 later received a number of citations from Canadian aviation organizations for their heroic actions in landing the plane safely.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_Flight_3-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fire_Flight-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="C-FTLU_and_N994Z">
       C-FTLU and N994Z
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Canada_Flight_797&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: C-FTLU and N994Z">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After this incident, Air Canada sold the right wing of this DC-9 aircraft to
      <a href="/wiki/Ozark_Air_Lines" title="Ozark Air Lines">
       Ozark Air Lines
      </a>
      . On December 20, 1983 an aircraft (tail number N994Z
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) operating as
      <a href="/wiki/Ozark_Air_Lines_Flight_650" title="Ozark Air Lines Flight 650">
       Ozark Air Lines Flight 650
      </a>
      , hit a snow plow in
      <a href="/wiki/Sioux_Falls_Regional_Airport" title="Sioux Falls Regional Airport">
       Sioux Falls
      </a>
      , killing the snow plow operator and separating the right wing from the aircraft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OzarkAirLinesFlight650_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OzarkAirLinesFlight650-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A wing from C-FTLU was used to replace the one separated on N994Z after the incident. The aircraft was later sold to
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airlines" title="Republic Airlines">
       Republic Airlines
      </a>
      , and acquired by
      <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">
       Northwest Airlines
      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dramatization">
       Dramatization
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     </h2>
     <p>
      The episode "Fire Flight" of Canadian television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mayday_(TV_series)" title="Mayday (TV series)">
        Mayday
       </a>
      </i>
      (known as "Fiery Landing" of
      <i>
       Air Emergency
      </i>
      in the USA,
      <i>
       Air Crash Investigation
      </i>
      in the UK and Australia) portrays the disaster.
