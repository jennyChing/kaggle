         <meta content="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/Wimbledon-tennis-technology-484242.jpg" itemprop="image"/>

       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         Top players including reigning champ Andy Murray, will be battling it out as they try and win the Wimbledon title.
        </p>
        <p>
         And if the British weather doesn't ruin the party, its sure to serve up treats for tennis fans.
        </p>
        <p>
         The rules of this popular game have remained unchanged for years, but the technology and science behind it have undergone radical advancements.
        </p>
        <p>
         From modern raquets with ultra lightweight design to ball tracking technology that captures every moment of balls flight path, tennis is being transformed.
        </p>
        <p>
         A recent study from
         <a href="http://www.milletsports.co.uk/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Millet Sports
         </a>
         takes an in-depth look at how the game has changed.
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173032.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          1874: THE FIRST
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The first lawn tennis rackets were strung with the outer skin of sheep intestine.
        </p>
        <p>
         The design was registered by Walter C. Wingfield and within a year the equipment was being sold for use in India, Russia, China and Canada.
        </p>
        <p>
         The racket was heavy, wooden, and slightly lopsided.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1911: WOOD
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The first laminated tennis racket frame was patented by Albert Altman. Less splinters meant for a more pleasant game!
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1967: METAL
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Wilson introduced the first commercially successful steel racket, named the T2000.
        </p>
        <p>
         The racket was used by Jimmy Connors when he famously won the Wimbledon singles title in 1974 and 1981.
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173033.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          1976: OVERSIZED
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The Prince brand patented the first oversized tennis racket. Competitor, Weed USA, was quick to point out that they had introduced the first oversized racket a year earlier  but it was not commercially successful.
        </p>
        <p>
         The increased size, light weight and huge sweet spot made tennis much easier for amateur players.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1980: COMPOSITE
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         These rackets were designed with advanced players in mind.
        </p>
        <p>
         They were constructed from a stiffer frame material that mixed carbon fibres with a plastic resin.
        </p>
        <p>
         The new material was named graphite and the most famous early racket to use the technology was the Dunlop Max 200G.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1987: WIDEBODY
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The first "widebody" racket was introduced by Wilson.
        </p>
        <p>
         The increased beam width allowed for more powerful shots by improving the resistance of the frame against the impact of the ball.
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173034.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          2005: AERODYNAMIC
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Prince unveiled O3 technology in 2005 that promised to improve racket speed.
        </p>
        <p>
         The old pin-sized string holes were made much larger on the Prince O3 racket.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          CURRENT TRENDS
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Graphite rackets can last for years without a significant drop in performance.
        </p>
        <p>
         Manufacturers continue to innovate with incremental changes to a variety of different rackets: oversized heads, ultra lightweight frames, and heavy power rackets are all evident in todays designs.
        </p>
        <p>
         A racket by Babolat includes gyroscopes and accelerometers in the handle to collected data on your performance.
        </p>
        <p>
         Power, spin, and the area the ball came into contact with the racket are all recorded and synced to a smartphone.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          THE FUTURE
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         There are a lot of predictions about how tennis racket technology will progress in the future.
        </p>
        <p>
         HSBC conducted research and predicted that by 2036 rackets could have hi-tech strings that bruise before breaking.
        </p>
        <p>
         There could also be wireless sensors in the base of the racket that record the players swing speed.
        </p>
        <p>
         ...........................................................................................
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173036.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          CLOVER-LEAF BALL
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In the 1870s, Indian rubber was used to manufacture lawn tennis balls.
        </p>
        <p>
         They were made solely of rubber and covered with flannel stitched around the core.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          YELLOW BALL
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In 1972, yellow tennis balls replaced the white design to make it easier for TV cameras to pick them up.
        </p>
        <p>
         Wimbledon stuck with the white tennis balls until 1986, when they finally made the switch.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Australian Open is supplied with more than 48,000 tennis balls during the tournament.
        </p>
        <p>
         ...........................................................................................
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173039.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          WIMBLEDON'S FAMOUS COURTS
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         They are sown with 100 per cent Perennial Ryegrass to improve strength and durability during the game.
        </p>
        <p>
         The soil is kept dry and hard to improve the amount of bounce the ball gets.
        </p>
        <p>
         The grass has been cut to a height of 8mm (since 1995) for optimum present day play and survival.
        </p>
        <p>
         ...........................................................................................
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173041.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          RADAR GUNS
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         IBM service speed technology was first implemented at Wimbledon in 1991.
        </p>
        <p>
         The information it recorded was supplied and used by commentators during the games. Two specially designed radar sensors were positioned behind the baseline at both ends of the court.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          HOW IT WORKS
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         When a player strikes the ball, the radar guns detect the speed and display the information on the courtside screens.
        </p>
        <p>
         The serve data is automatically recorded on IBMs central tournament database.
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173044.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          BALL TRACKING TECHNOLOGY
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Hawk-Eye is an electric line-calling technology and it was first used at Wimbledon in 2006.
        </p>
        <p>
         It uses multiple cameras to capture the ball as it travels and creates a model of the field of play. The cameras cant capture every movement of the balls flight path due to frame rate limitations.
        </p>
        <p>
         It therefore predicts the trajectory of the ball between the missed frames.
        </p>
        <p>
         Players are entitled to challenge the Hawk-Eye verdict three times per set, plus an additional call in the event of a tie-break.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          HOW IT WORKS
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Each camera follows the balls centre and records its 2D position as it moves across the frame. The system then triangulates the 2D information from each calibrated camera to provide a 3D position of the ball in the court.
        </p>
        <p>
         This process is repeated for each frame to construct the single trajectory that the ball took. Finally, the trajectory path is used to calculate the exact area where the ball bounces.
        </p>
        <p>
         On average, 30 per cent of the calls made against Hawk-Eye by tennis players are correct and the call is overturned.
        </p>
        <p>
         ...........................................................................................
        </p>
       </section>

        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173047.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          NET-CORD SENSOR
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         It may not be the most complicated piece of technology but the net-cord sensor is an essential part of the courts decision-making technology.
        </p>
        <p>
         The sensor, known as a piezoelectric device, translates vibrations into electrical power. It is attached to the net and helps the referee decide whether to call a serve or not.
        </p>
        <p>
         ...........................................................................................
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173050.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          KEYS TO THE MATCH
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In 2012, IBM expanded on the SlamTrackerTM by incorporating their "Keys to the Match" technology.
        </p>
        <p>
         By analysing over eight years worth of historical Grand Slam data (over 41 million data points), the technology is able to predict how the match will go and identify patterns in players and their styles.
        </p>
        <p>
         Before each match, the SlamTrackerTM analyses data on each player and their previous clashes; by looking at key performance indicators such as aces, serve speeds and wins, the technology gives fans a deeper level of insight as the match unfolds.
        </p>
        <p>
         The tech is less about predicting who will win, and more about what a player needs to do to pull forward during the game.
        </p>
        <p>
         More than 140 countries and 1,000 journalists at the Wimbledon Championships rely on the statistical output from the various pieces of technology.
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="photo changeSpace">
        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173051.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">
          MILLET SPORTS
         </span>
        </p>
       </section>
       <section class="text-description">
        <p>
         <strong>
          SECONDSIGHT
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In 2012, IBM showcased SecondSight.
        </p>
        <p>
         This technology goes beyond analysing the tennis ball by tracking direction, speed and distance of the actual players as they move about the court.
        </p>
        <p>
         ...........................................................................................
        </p>

        <p class="withoutCaption">
         <img alt=" " src="http://cdn.images.express.co.uk/img/dynamic/143/590x/secondary/173048.jpg" title=" "/>
         <span class="photo-caption defaultLeft">

        <p>
         <strong>
          THE FUTURE
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Fans at the Australian Open in 2014 were able to trial an exciting piece of technology.
        </p>
        <p>
         Visitors stepped into a virtual reality tennis court using the power of the Oculus Rift headset.
        </p>
        <p>
         They were handed a specifically designed motion-sensitive tennis racket and were challenged to return a virtual serve from the best tennis players on earth.
        </p>
