      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Eight Ways 'Dragon Age' Can Still Improve After 'Inquisition'
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">
        Comment Now
       </a>
       <div class="comments_follow followable_block">
        <div class="followable_block initializing" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Eight Ways 'Dragon Age' Can Still Improve After 'Inquisition'&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/1174-13787" data-target-name="" data-target-type="article" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow" data-type-advoice="false">
         <div class="follow_unireg">
          <a class="follow_unireg_link" href="javascript://follow">

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         Dragon Age: Inquisition is a hit, at least critically, as its share of the holiday gaming pie is yet to be determined given that we have to wait and see how well it actually sells. But if you read the reviews and play the game yourself these first few days, youll realize pretty quickly that the game has dramatically improved over its predecessor, and in fact has drawn the best elements from both of its past iterations to produce a game of a higher caliber than either, jammed with so much content you can probably extract Skyrim-level playing time out of it.
        </p>
        <p>
         While Ive enjoyed my time with Dragon Age: Inquisition so far, Im not sure I needed to extol its virtues in a way that cant be better said by Erik Kains
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/erikkain/">
          ongoing review series
         </a>
         for Forbes. Rather, I want to think about how the series can continue to improve from here.
        </p>
        <p>
         Given that the game is pretty damn great for the most part, most of my points are admittedly nitpicky. Some have to do with Biowares aging engine, and some are more related to the genre as a whole. But playing the first 15 or so hours of the game, little things just kept irking me from time to time. So while the game gets most major things right, going forward this is what I would love to see improved upon for the next go-round.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="dragon age inquisition2" class=" size-full wp-image-13788" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/insertcoin/files/2014/11/dragon-age-inquisition2.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <div style="padding: 20px 0pt;margin: 20px 0pt;border-bottom: 1px solid #DDDDDD;border-top: 1px solid #DDDDDD">
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/ebooks/fanboy-wars-the-fight-for-the-future-of-video-games/?utm_source=articlelink&amp;utm_medium=direct&amp;utm_campaign=fanboyebook" style="text-decoration: none;color: #900;font: bold 20px/26px Georgia">
          Fanboy Wars: The Newest eBook From Forbes
         </a>
         <br/>
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/ebooks/fanboy-wars-the-fight-for-the-future-of-video-games/?utm_source=articlelink&amp;utm_medium=direct&amp;utm_campaign=fanboyebook" style="text-decoration: none;font: 15px/18px  Georgia;color: #000">
          <em>
           The Fight For The Future Of Video Games
          </em>
          is a warts-and-all look at the clashes between the video game business and its passionate fans.
         </a>
        </div>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          1. Bad Hair Day
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         While coming to next gen has done wonders for many aspects of Dragon Ages visuals, from its armor textures to its environments, theres one thing that still feels decidedly 2009. That would be character hair, which again, I told you this list would get nitpicky, but it really is noticeably terrible. Its like stuck-together pasta, and the engine mandates almost every hairstyle be close-cropped to avoid compounding this problem. This results in especially terrible hairstyles for female Inquisitors, but guys without a shaved head look pretty silly as well. Grand Theft Auto struggled with crab-claw hands for years before they figured it out, and it seems hair is Biowares cross to bear at present. Hopefully this will be the last time we see it in such a state, as it has barely evolved at all since the first game five years ago.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          2. Lazy Horses
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         While horses end up being a pretty key part of many open world games like Skyrim or Red Dead Redemption, I rather despite my horse in Dragon Age so far. I am hoping I can cross this item off the list later if I unlock a better stallion to ride, but now? I feel like Im riding a tranquilized manatee. The sprinting mechanic feels painfully slow and
         <em>
          is
         </em>
         actually painful trying to eternally depress the L3 stick on a PS4 controller. And DA horses cant jump over anything higher than a twig, so for the time youll save riding, youll spend more swerving around obstacles to zig zag your way to objectives. And for as much as people loved to make fun of Skyrims horses being able to cling to cliff faces like the lovechild of a mountain goat and Spider-Man, that served to make them actually effective for traversing terrain, and more often than not if theres a hill to climb (much less a mountain), youre better off doing it on foot.
         <br/>
         <!--donotpaginate-->
         <br/>
         <strong>
          3. Difficulty Curve
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         One of my complaints with the original Dragon Age: Origins was the weirdly off-putting difficulty curve that would sometimes lead me 90% of the way through a mission, only to hit a brick wall in the form of an overbuffed miniboss, and I would have to go farm quests for three hours before I could come back and face him. DA2 solved this for the most part with extreme linearity, but now in the more free-roaming Inquisition, the difficulty curve issue manifests itself in strange ways. For example, the starting zone where youll probably spend the first 10 hours of the game is a weird smattering of different difficulty levels with no way to tell how strong youre supposed to be while trying to take them on. Some areas are pretty obviously off limits, a northeast corner filled with dragons and a southwest corner filled with ice beam shooting demons, but randomly the game will throw in pockets of insanity, like a random demon breach near a waterfall that you simply cant beat until yourewhat level exactly?
        </p>
        <p>
         Though you have levels in the game, your enemies apparently do not. The only indicator of their strength Ive seen is a little skull icon plastered on them when youre trying to take on something that
         <em>
          clearly
         </em>
         outmatches you. And yet, it creates weird situations where you die in an area, and come back five levels later only to continue to be killed just as quickly, and you have no idea when or how youre supposed to take on these tougher areas. Now once more, Ive hit the problem of getting nearly all the way through a quest, only to be rebuffed by a final wave of enemies that isnt
         <em>
          remotely
         </em>
         possible to defeat untilagain, I have no idea. I could come back in two levels or ten, for all I know.
        </p>
        <p>
         It messes with your mind, as you really dont know if youre A) really bad at the game B) undergeared and not spending enough time crafting and enchanting or C) simply supposed to come back later. This isnt a
         <em>
          huge
         </em>
         pervasive issue, but its starting to come up enough to be annoying.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="dragon age inquisition3" class=" size-full wp-image-13789" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/insertcoin/files/2014/11/dragon-age-inquisition3.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          4. Party Down
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         One of my favorite Bioware moments across any of their games so far was in the Mass Effect 3 Citadel DLC where you invite everyone over to your house for a giant kick-ass party before you embark on your final suicide mission. It got everyone together in a non-traditional setting that wasnt a three-man ground team or visiting their assigned quarters in the Normandy.
        </p>
        <p>
         But with Dragon Age: Inquisition the party system still feels limited given that you have so many party members for so few slots. Yes, you can visit them in town, but ten hours into the game I have so many party members Im losing track of them all. What always happens is that you pick your three favorites (because you cant afford to gear up everyone properly) and will probably use them until the game ends. I just wish there were more instances to get everyone together either in battle or out of it. I dont have a terribly good solution for this, but I am actively searching for a fix for this 27 party members for four roles problem that Ive found in Bioware games and elsewhere. I think loyalty missions do a good job of making you bond with everyone to at least some extent, but perhaps I just havent reached those yet.
         <br/>
         <!--donotpaginate-->
         <br/>
         <strong>
          5. Puppet Faces
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Back to the aging Bioware engine, in an era when so many games are using facial capture to create cutscenes or even in-game expressions, Dragon Age is starting to feel behind the curve. In a game that relies
         <em>
          so much
         </em>
         on up-close dialogue, its weird to see characters talking like theres a someone above them with strings attached to their lips and eyebrows, pulling them to form words or change their mood. It can get downright goofy sometimes as a switch is flipped between happy, mad or concerned in a way thats jarring. And the lip syncing is starting to feel noticeably not-great, like youre watching a movie where the audio track is
         <em>
          just
         </em>
         out of sync enough to be bothersome. The problem with next-gen is that its more than just better lighting and textures and particle effects. Without underlying engine changes, animation can easily look very dated if youre not careful, and thats how facial expression and dialogue come across in Inquisition. (
         <strong>
          Update
         </strong>
         : Im told that Inquisition
         <em>
          is
         </em>
         using a new engine in the form of Frostbite 3, which looks great for everything except these face and hair problems Im addressing here. Theyre going to need to figure out a way to improve that aspect of the animation).
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          6. Loading Screens
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         This is probably the most nitpicky item on the list, but why have a loading screen with giant paragraphs of text to read, only to them cut away to a different, black loading screen after five seconds when youve only gotten to read the first two sentences?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          7. Role Redundancy
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         This is a more overarching problem with gameplay, and maybe it will change down the road, but right now, roles feel very redundant, especially given that youre constantly drowning in party members. For example, Im a mage, I start with another mage in my party (Solas), I recruited another mage later (Vivienne), and Im pretty sure I have my eye on a fourth one (Freddy Mercury, aka Dorian) to join my team later.
        </p>
        <p>
         And yet, there are just four schools of magic, where each mage can pretty much specialize in at least two by the time they get a decent amount of skill points. But do I really need a healer mage, a lightning mage, a fire mage and a freeze mage? The same goes for the two-handed vs. one-handed warrior. The only class with two really distinct styles appears to be the rogue with double blades vs. bows. Otherwise it feels like youre just piling on characters for the sake of it, despite them doing very little differently from one another.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="dragon age inquisition4" class=" size-full wp-image-13790" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/insertcoin/files/2014/11/dragon-age-inquisition4.jpg"/>
         <!--donotpaginate-->
         <strong>
          8. Character Previewing
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Im singling out Bioware for this because I get more invested in my created characters in those games than any other. I spend a solid
         <em>
          hour
         </em>
         designing my characters in Dragon Age and Mass Effect games, yet on more than a few occasions, Ive had to re-do the entire process because when it came time to load the game, they did not look as good as I thought they did once I saw them in action. I think its a lighting/perspective problem that cant just be solved by allowing you to rotate your characters head around in creation. But in this instance, my human female mage ended up with a completely different hair color than what I thought, disappearing make-up and cheeks so red in-game she looked like shed been slapped. Not at all what I thought I was getting (yet as a cross between Jessica Chastain and Natalie Dormer, not bad enough to re-do in this case).
        </p>
        <p>
         I get that the character probably needs to have their animation locked in after the initial selection, as not every game can be Saints Row with plastic surgery stations to change your appearance on the fly. But if thats the case, there needs to be a different kind of preview mode that shows your character in action in the game itself, and also in cutscenes, so you can get a full picture of what theyre really going to look like once youre no longer just looking at a zoomed in mugshot version of their face.
        </p>
        <p>
         So yeah, as you can see, outside of one or two of these, most of these are just quality of life issues that I think Bioware would be wise to consider for the future. Again, the game is rather great and Im having fun with it, but I also have fun critiquing things I like, so here we are.
        </p>
        <p>
         Alright, this was an hour Ive spent writing and not killing Templars, so if youll excuse me
        </p>

           RE: #5, aka image capture The expense of image capture can be greatly reduced by using inverse kinematics/probabilistic motion models. Aka, a small set of actors can be used to generate a probabilistic model of many different emotional states/facial expressions, and the transitions between them. Then, these animations can be auto-mapped to unique meshes aka faces, such that it becomes relatively easy for new animations to be authored with only minimal need for more labor intensive manual key frame alterations.

Its been done many many times.
          </p>
          <p>
           If youre looking for a game without healing magic that will be a substantial challenge for you, perhaps you should try playing the Dark Souls series.
          </p>
          <p>
           In Dragon Age, healing magic was an established phenomena, and has been for years now. Suddenly, with the release of DA:I, it disappeared, without any real explanation. Are you saying Bioware did that to make the game more challenging?
          </p>
          <p>
           So they ripped a giant plot whole in their franchise cannon to increase the difficulty level of their newest game?
          </p>
          <p>
           Excuse me, but I believe there are much better ways to change a games difficulty and make it more challenging, without creating a paradox.
          </p>
          <p>
           At the very least, I wish they would have made it an option like it always has been. So then people who enjoy the game and are comfortable with the challenge it presents with healing magic can have their cake, and those, like yourself, that find it too easy that way and would rather use potions instead, can have their cake as well. Simply dont use, or dont spec your characters with healing abilities.
          </p>
          <p>
           Personally, I find the potion system the easier of the two. I found it more challenging to manage mana and lyrium resources to keep my front line alive and happy, than to have this infinite supply of healing pots that fix my toons up with the touch of a button.
          </p>
          <p>
           Does your tank have low health? click. Too much red, not enough green? click. Low on pots? teleport to camp and resupply for free.
          </p>

          </p>
          <p>
           There are several advantages to having so many followers.
          </p>
          <p>
           First, from a strictly mechanical standpoint, having nine followers, three from each class, each with a different specialization that unlocks mid-game, provides for more flexibility in party selection, making it possible to craft your party to your playstyle, class, and specialization. A two-handed Qunari damage dealer specialized as a Champion is going to need different supporting team from a dual-wielding dwarf specialized in Tempest.
          </p>
          <p>
           Second, theres personality. I switched back and forth between Solas and Dorian because I couldnt stand Vivienne, and thirty seconds after I met Sera I knew I wasnt going to be seeing much more of Varric in the archer role. Varric is great, but Sera is awesome.
          </p>
          <p>
           Third, theres story. Talking to people is one of the core game mechanics of this or any other Bioware game, and theyre all interesting to talk to, even the ones I dont like very much, like Vivienne and Cullen. I would much prefer have exposition delivered through interactive dialog with an interesting character rather than reading codex entries.
          </p>
          <p>
           Finally, romance. Eight of the twelve main supporting characters can be romanced  more characters means more chances for romances that differ based on personality type, race, and orientation. Having a variety of characters with distinct individual characteristics increases the possibility of the player finding a partner that suits their personality and orientation. This is a great improvement over Dragon Age 2s approach of having every romance prospect be available to every player character regardless of sex or orientation  it makes each romance feel more like a relationship than a gameplay mechanic.
          </p>
          <p>
           I suspect the hair and facial animation problems will be fixed in the next installment. This is a game that bridges the 7th and 8th generation consoles, so certain elements that had to be in place for the game to even run on the PS3 and 360 limit what could be done with the 8th gen consoles. When theyre coding for PS4 and Xbox One, the ceiling will be much higher. Yes, Im aware that its on PC too, but the least powerful platform is going to be a limiting factor for what the makers can do.
          </p>

         <div class="comment_content">
          <p>
           For number one, you have to remember whos engine they are using. Its the frostbite engine. You know, the one DICE made? For their military shooter? The one where literally everyone is wearing a helmet? Id be surprised if hair physics was even on the checklist when they made the thing.
          </p>
          <p>
           As far as 3, 4, and 7 are concerned, I feel like youve not invested enough time into the game to actually know what you are talking about. For number 3, theres literally a button you can hit which will highlight whatever enemy your target icon is on. Youll notice two things happen. One, the icon goes from white to yellow, meaning youve locked on to that target. Youll specifically focus that target, even if you look at another one, until it is dead or you change target. Two, a big health bar appears at the top of your screen, accompanied by an icon with a *number* inside of it. That number is the fabled level of the creature you face. The icon also tells you other things based on the styling. A bland one indicates its a trash mob. A bigger icon with more fancy stuff inside of it indicates its an elite mob or possibly a boss mob. That tells you that even though you may be the same level, it will clearly be a challenge regardless. On PC I can tell you that button is tab or the right mouse button, but I dont know what it is on console. I know it is there though because I watched my roommate do it on PS4. As far as your one point where you may not be skilled enough is concerned, that may be an issue. I watched my roommate try to take on the first level 12 dragon in the game. He was also level 12 and thus supposedly able to complete the battle. I watched him get annihilated, he didnt even get it to 75%. I told two things. One, where to get the recipe for rejuvenation potions which would help him greatly. Two, how he should respec his characters so they could do what they do best. After that he was able to beat the dragon and have some pots left over for everyone. The difference was like night and day.
          </p>
          <p>
           For number four, there are several times where your interactions with one character involve several others. In one conversation, literally every supporting character joins you for a game of cards at the tavern, where you interact with them actively. I feel like youve just not invested enough time to discover these things, but they exist. Its unfair to say they dont when you havent yet seen if they do.
          </p>
          <p>
           For number seven, each character has a specialization unique to them. On top of that, you can spend their skill points however you please. This means that each one not only brings their own flair to the battle, but at the same time you dont have to feel like their role is already taken. Even Varric, the one person in the party who starts out with a unique crossbow that is the only weapon which can literally get progressively better as the game progresses can be completely respecced out of archery and into melee combat, because the bastard can still equip daggers too. In fact, they created literally just enough party members to fill each role. There are three specializations for each class. Youd be thus surprised to learn that there are exactly three companions for each class. The party members you bring affects your play style uniquely. No one companion is invalidated by the presence of another. In fact Id say the most limiting factor is your own main character. You pick a role at the beginning, and of course have to go along on every mission because youre the main character. If youre a rogue, that means that your rogue companions are less likely to get the chance to stretch their legs. Same for each respective class.
          </p>
          <p>
           One thing Inquisition actually deserves credit for is the destruction of the holy trinity of RPGs. Sort of. For one, gone is the role of healer. But the second thing, and this one is important, is that *every main class can be a tank, a dps, or a utility*. Tank roles: Rogue tempest, warrior champion, mage knight-enchanter. DPS roles: Rogue assassin, warrior ravager, mage necromancer. Utility roles: Rogue artificer, warrior templar, mage rift mage.
          </p>
