     <p>
      <b>
       Hiromu Arakawa
      </b>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">
         
       </span>
       <span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">
        ,
       </span>
       <i>
        Arakawa Hiromu
       </i>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">
         <span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">
          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       , born May 8, 1973)
      </span>
      is a Japanese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manga_artist" title="Manga artist">
       manga artist
      </a>
      from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaid">
       Hokkaid
      </a>
      . She is best known for the manga
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist" title="Fullmetal Alchemist">
        Fullmetal Alchemist
       </a>
      </i>
      , which became a hit both domestically and internationally, and was later adapted into two
      <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">
       anime
      </a>
      television series.
     </p>
     <p>
      She often portrays herself as a bespectacled cow. Her given name is Hiromi
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">
         <span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">
          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </span>
      , the first character being written identically to her male pen name, Hiromu.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiromu_Arakawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Born on May 8, 1973 in Tokachi,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Hokkaid">
       Hokkaid
      </a>
      , Japan, Arakawa was born and raised on a dairy farm with three elder sisters and a younger brother. Arakawa thought about being a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manga_artist" title="Manga artist">
       manga artist
      </a>
      "since [she] was little" and during her school years, she would often draw on textbooks. After graduating high school, she took
      <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">
       oil painting
      </a>
      classes once a month for seven years while working on her family's farm. During this time, she also created
      <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Djinshi" title="Djinshi">
       djinshi
      </a>
      manga with her friends and drew
      <a href="/wiki/Yonkoma" title="Yonkoma">
       yonkoma
      </a>
      for a magazine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YahooBooks_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-YahooBooks-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquivalentExchange_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EquivalentExchange-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Arakawa moved to Tokyo in the summer of 1999,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and started her career in the manga industry as an assistant to Hiroyuki Et, author of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mah%C5%8Djin_Guru_Guru" title="Mahjin Guru Guru">
        Mahjin Guru Guru
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProfileBookInterview_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ProfileBookInterview-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Her own career began with the publication of
      <i>
       Stray Dog
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Square_Enix" title="Square Enix">
       Square Enix
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monthly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Gangan" title="Monthly Shnen Gangan">
        Monthly Shnen Gangan
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1999.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquivalentExchange_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EquivalentExchange-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Stray Dog
      </i>
      won the ninth 21st Century "Shnen Gangan" Award.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YahooBooks_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-YahooBooks-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She published one chapter of
      <i>
       Shanghai Ymakikai
      </i>
      in
      <i>
       Monthly Shnen Gangan
      </i>
      in 2000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In July 2001, Arakawa published the first chapter of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist" title="Fullmetal Alchemist">
        Fullmetal Alchemist
       </a>
      </i>
      in
      <i>
       Monthly Shnen Gangan
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The series spanned 108 chapters, with the last one published in July 2010, and the series was collected in twenty-seven volumes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JapaneseVolume27_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JapaneseVolume27-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the studio
      <a href="/wiki/Bones_(studio)" title="Bones (studio)">
       Bones
      </a>
      adapted it into an
      <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">
       anime
      </a>
      series, Arakawa assisted them in its early development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide2_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-guide2-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, she was not involved in the making of the script, so the anime had a different ending from the manga, which she developed further.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquivalentExchange_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EquivalentExchange-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The series won the 49th
      <a href="/wiki/Shogakukan_Manga_Award" title="Shogakukan Manga Award">
       Shogakukan Manga Award
      </a>
      in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dnen" title="Shnen">
        shnen
       </a>
      </i>
      category in 2004.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShogakukanAward_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ShogakukanAward-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the second anime adaptation was reaching its ending, Arakawa showed director
      <a href="/wiki/Yasuhiro_Irie" title="Yasuhiro Irie">
       Yasuhiro Irie
      </a>
      her plans for the manga's ending, making both end in near dates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She gave birth to a son in 2007 but did not take a maternity leave. In a February 12, 2014 interview, Arakawa mentioned that her third child had been born a few days earlier. Other than this she has chosen to keep her personal life private.
     </p>
     <p>
      She is currently living in
      <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">
       Tokyo
      </a>
      and has published three more works,
      <i>
       Raiden 18
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Sten no Kmori
      </i>
      (also known as
      <i>
       Bat in Blue Sky
      </i>
      ), and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hero_Tales" title="Hero Tales">
        Hero Tales
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquivalentExchange_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EquivalentExchange-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Arakawa has collaborated with the creation of
      <i>
       Hero Tales
      </i>
      with Studio Flag under the name of Huang Jin Zhou. In the anime adaptation of the series, Arakawa was responsible for the character designs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She has also drawn the cover from the Japanese edition of the novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Demon%27s_Lexicon" title="The Demon's Lexicon">
        The Demon's Lexicon
       </a>
      </i>
      authored by
      <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Rees_Brennan" title="Sarah Rees Brennan">
       Sarah Rees Brennan
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2011, Arakawa began a new series called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Silver_Spoon_(manga)" title="Silver Spoon (manga)">
        Silver Spoon
       </a>
      </i>
      in Shogakukan's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Weekly_Sh%C5%8Dnen_Sunday" title="Weekly Shnen Sunday">
        Weekly Shnen Sunday
       </a>
      </i>
      . Rather than writing another fantasy series like
      <i>
       Fullmetal Alchemist
      </i>
      , Arakawa wanted to challenge herself by trying a more realistic story with
      <i>
       Silver Spoon
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-animeland_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-animeland-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It quickly rose among Shogakukan's best-selling titles and an anime series by
      <a href="/wiki/A-1_Pictures" title="A-1 Pictures">
       A-1 Pictures
      </a>
      began airing in July 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also in July 2013 she began her manga adaptation of
      <a href="/wiki/Yoshiki_Tanaka" title="Yoshiki Tanaka">
       Yoshiki Tanaka
      </a>
      's
      <i>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">
       Influences
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hiromu_Arakawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influences">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Arakawa states that
      <a href="/wiki/Suih%C5%8D_Tagawa" title="Suih Tagawa">
       Suih Tagawa
      </a>
      , the author of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Norakuro" title="Norakuro">
        Norakuro
       </a>
      </i>
      , is the "root of [her] style as an artist". She also learned
      <a href="/wiki/Composition_(visual_arts)" title="Composition (visual arts)">
       composition
      </a>
      and drawing during her time as Hiroyuki Et's assistant. She also cites
      <a href="/wiki/Rumiko_Takahashi" title="Rumiko Takahashi">
       Rumiko Takahashi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Mizuki" title="Shigeru Mizuki">
       Shigeru Mizuki
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kinnikuman" title="Kinnikuman">
        Kinnikuman
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Yudetamago" title="Yudetamago">
       Yudetamago
      </a>
      as influences and is a fan of
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Mignola" title="Mike Mignola">
       Mike Mignola
      </a>
      's work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EquivalentExchange_2-4">

        </span>
        2
        <span>

      </sup>
