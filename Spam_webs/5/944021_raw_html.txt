     <section id="article-section-1">
      <p>
       When a 36-year-old bibliophile in Daegu, South Korea, sat down at his computer and googled the word library, he didnt expect to find anything particularly noteworthy. But as DooSun You scrolled through the results, an appealingly anti-tech concept popped up.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Internet led him to Little Free Librarieshand-built boxes where neighbors can trade novels, memoirs, comics, and cookbooks, and connect with each other in the process.
      </p>
      <p>
       The little libraries immediately appealed to DooSun. Reading books is one of the most valuable things in my life. I think a book is equal to a treasure, he says. I hoped to share that feeling with my neighborsthats the reason I wanted a Little Free Library. The website showed pictures of the diminutive structures standing in front yards, on city curbs, and alongside country roads all over the world, along with their GPS locations. The
       <a href="http://littlefreelibrary.org/ourmap/">
        Little Free Library map
       </a>
       was a treasure map, he says.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:75.0877192982%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/07/You_8/c6f605813.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Soon after his online discovery, DooSun built a Little Free Librarythe first one in South Koreain front of his apartment building. Then he built a second at a different spot. Then a third. Slowly, his take a book, return a book libraries began bringing people together, garnering book donations and handwritten notes of thanks from strangers. He now pastes a QR code on the front of each library, so passersby can use their smartphones to learn more about them, and he regularly exchanges emails with others who want to build their own. He recently started a
       <a href="https://www.facebook.com/groups/LFLasia/">
        Facebook group
       </a>
       where other Little Free Library stewards throughout Asia can swap ideas and experiencesas easily as visitors to their libraries swap physical books.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">
       <noscript>

         </a>

     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       In 2009, Tod Bol built the first Little Free Library in the Mississippi River town of Hudson, Wisconsin, as a tribute to his mothera dedicated reader and former schoolteacher. When he saw the people of his community gathering around it like a neighborhood water cooler, exchanging conversation as well as books, he knew he wanted to take his simple idea farther.
      </p>
      <p>
       We have a natural sense of wanting to be connected, but there are so many things that push us apart, Bol says. I think Little Free Libraries open the door to conversations we want to have with each other.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since then, his idea has become a full-fledged movement, spreading from state to state and country to country. There are now 18,000 of the little structures around the world, located in each of the 50 states and in 70 countriesfrom Ukraine to Uganda, Italy to Japan. Theyre multiplying so quickly, in fact, that the understaffed and underfunded nonprofit struggle to keep its world map up to date.
      </p>
      <p>
       Khalid and Yasmin Ansari, who live in Qatar, say they get a special satisfaction out of seeing their six-year-old son
       <a href="https://www.facebook.com/UmayrsLittleFreeLibrary">
        Umayrs Little Free Library
       </a>
       represented on the website. When looking at the LFL world map, says Khalid, you almost feel obliged to have one in the neighborhood to fill the gap. It's like doing your part in your part of the world.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:66.4912280702%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/07/Ansari_5/4d56a5f88.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
      </figure>
      <p>
       In some places, Little Free Libraries are filling a role usually served by brick-and-mortar libraries; the organizations
       <a href="http://littlefreelibrary.org/give/books-for-all/">
        Books Around the Block program
       </a>
       , for example, aims to bring LFLs to places where kids and adults dont have easy access to books. In North Minneapolis, an area more often in the news for shootings than community engagement, the Books Around the Block initiative set up 40 of the little libraries. Two hundred more sprung up shortly thereafter.
      </p>
      <p>
       Last year, Sarah Maxey of Winston-Salem, North Carolina, discovered Little Free Libraries when browsing the crowdfunding site Kickstarter. She was then inspired to launch her own LFL Kickstarter campaign. The response was enthusiastic: By the time the campaign ended,
       <a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1106918453/little-free-library-project-winston-salem-nc">
        Maxey had raised more than $10,000 for her cause
       </a>
       enough money to build dozens (and dozens) of little libraries.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxright-wrapper" data-pos="boxright">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxright1">

        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_education&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxright1&amp;sz=300x250%7C300x600&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=12" title="">

         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-3">
      <p>
       What happens is, you start the momentum, and then the communitythe Lions Club, the Rotary, the churches, the neighborssteps up and builds more. It just keeps going, Bol says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Individual stewards are using their Little Free Libraries in altruistic ways, too. Tina Sipula of Clare House, a food pantry in Bloomington, Illinois, does more than distribute groceries; she distributes books via an on-site Little Free Library. As she points out, homeless people dont have addresseswhich means they cant get public library cards. Linda Prout was instrumental in bringing dozens of Little Free Libraries to New Orleans after Hurricane Katrina, and Lisa Heydlauff of Bihar, India, is working to bring a thousand Little Free Libraries to girls schools in her country, filling them with books that teach business and entrepreneurial skills.
      </p>
      <p>
       Little Free Libraries create neighborhood heroes, says Bol. Thats a big part of why its succeeding.
      </p>
      <p>
       Though they owe their spread largely to the Internet, Little Free Libraries often serve as an antidote to a world of Kindle downloads and data-driven algorithms. The little wooden boxes are refreshingly physicaland human. When you open the door, serendipity (and your neighbors taste) dictates what youll find. The selection of 20 or so books could contain a Russian novel, a motorcycle repair manual, a Scandinavian cookbook, or a field guide to birds.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:95.2631578947%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/07/Aldrich_1-1/f970ee53a.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
      </figure>
      <p>
       For many peopleparticularly in more affluent areas where libraries aboundthis sense of discovery is an LFLs main appeal. A girl walking home from school might pick up a graphic novel that gets her excited about reading; a man on his way to the bus stop might find a volume of poetry that changes his outlook on life. Every book is a potential source of inspiration.
      </p>
      <p>
       Added to that is a kind of literary voyeurism, in which visitors get to contemplate the reading habits of their neighbors.
       <em>
        Who left the Brazilian travel guides, and whos reading Camus? Who added Haruki Murakamis
       </em>
       1Q84
       <em>
        , my favorite novel of the last five years? And where will my copy of
       </em>
       The Odyssey
       <em>
        end up when I leave it in the library for someone else?
       </em>
       By peeking into the reading lives of fellow Little Free Library users, you get to know your block better.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector2">
       <noscript>

         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-4">
      <p>
       I think it warms peoples' hearts that a stranger would go through the trouble to leave a gift for passersby, says Suzanne Pettypiece, steward of a Brooklyn Little Free Librarythe first one in New York. And everyone knows books are magical, so when you build a little house for them and say, Hey, take one of these, because we think you'll like itwell, that's kind of exciting.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some people use Little Free Libraries specifically to share something about themselves, says founder Todd Bol:
      </p>
      <p style="margin-left: 40px;">
       There was one woman who was excited to put up her Little Free Library quickly, and when I asked why, she said that she wanted to put books in it about Nepal, where her kids are from. She wanted the neighborhood to get to know more about their background.
      </p>
      <p>
       After all, no matter how closely a computer studies your search habits, its algorithms will never have the charm and mystique of a simple wooden box filled with a neighbors literary treasures. It wont be able to lend you a cup of sugar either.
      </p>
     </section>

        <h3 class="hed">
         The Destruction of a Black Suburb
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/alana-semuels/" title="Alana Semuels">
          Alana Semuels
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Lincoln Heights, Ohio, once had the potential to become a thriving community, but today its in crisis. This is no accident.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            LINCOLN HEIGHTS, OhioAfrican Americans started coming to Cincinnati more than a century ago, fleeing the violence and economic constraints of the South for jobs and homes.
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            But redlining and other restrictive zoning laws prohibited black families from buying homes in many of the citys neighborhoods. So when developers started selling off lots of unincorporated land north of Cincinnati to black buyers, it seemed like a good opportunity, one of the few paths to homeownership in the segregated North.
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>

          </a>
