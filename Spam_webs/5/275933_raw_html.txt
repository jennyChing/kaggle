               <h1 class="section-heading-main">
                Animal crusader takes on overcrowded animal shelters
               </h1>
               <div class="aux-info">
                <p class="publish-date">
                 Wednesday May 6th, 2009
                </p>
               </div>
               <div class="social-for-content">
                <a data-popup="true" href="https://twitter.com/share?url=http://dogtime.com/spay-neuter-bill-end-to-homeless-pets.html&amp;via=@dogtimedotcom&amp;text=Animal crusader takes on overcrowded animal shelters: " rel="nofollow">
                 <span class="fa-stack fa-lg">
                  <i class="fa fa-circle fa-stack-2x">
                  </i>
                  <i class="fa fa-twitter fa-stack-1x fa-inverse">
                  </i>
                 </span>
                </a>
                <a data-popup="true" href="https://www.facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?u=http://dogtime.com/spay-neuter-bill-end-to-homeless-pets.html" rel="nofollow">
                 <span class="fa-stack fa-lg">

                  </i>
                  <i class="fa fa-facebook fa-stack-1x fa-inverse">
                  </i>
                 </span>
                </a>
                <a data-popup="true" href="https://plus.google.com/share?url=http://dogtime.com/spay-neuter-bill-end-to-homeless-pets.html" rel="nofollow">
                 <span class="fa-stack fa-lg">
                  <i class="fa fa-circle fa-stack-2x">
                  </i>
                  <i class="fa fa-google-plus fa-stack-1x fa-inverse">
                  </i>
                 </span>
                </a>
                <a data-popup="true" href="http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://dogtime.com/spay-neuter-bill-end-to-homeless-pets.html&amp;media=http://dogtime.com/images/badges/default_dogtime_content.jpg&amp;description=The bill that would make fixing your pets the law in California was actually born far away in a New Orleans shelter. That's where Judie M..." rel="nofollow">
                 <span class="fa-stack fa-lg">
                  <i class="fa fa-circle fa-stack-2x">
                  </i>
                  <i class="fa fa-pinterest fa-stack-1x fa-inverse">
                  </i>
                 </span>
                </a>
                <span class="fa-stack fa-lg">
                 <i class="fa fa-comment fa-stack-2x">
                 </i>
                 <a class="fa-stack-1x comment-count" href="http://dogtime.com/spay-neuter-bill-end-to-homeless-pets.html#disqus_thread">
                 </a>
                </span>
               </div>
               <div class="template-article cel">
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-asset-group">
                  <div class="element-asset">
                   <div class="element-asset-left">
                    <figure>
                     <img alt="Square 140 mancuso" class="element-asset-image" src="http://assets.dogtime.com/asset/image/4cecae0f0106191885000bd7/square_140_mancuso.jpg"/>
                     <figcaption class="caption" style="">
                      Once Judie Mancuso concluded there was no way to find homes for all the pets in this country, she crafted a spay/neuter bill.
                     </figcaption>
                    </figure>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="element-content">
                   <p>
                    The bill that would make
                    <a href="http://dogtime.com/why-spay-neuter-hsus.html">
                     fixing
                    </a>
                    your pets the law in California was actually born far away in a New Orleans shelter. That's where Judie Mancuso, who'd flown down from her home in Laguna Beach to aid the post-
                    <a href="http://dogtime.com/california-wildfire-dogs-cats.html">
                     Hurricane Katrina
                    </a>
                    animal rescue effort, concluded that enough was enough.
                   </p>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "When I walked into that shelter and saw that every animal in there was unaltered--animals who'd been running loose on the streets!--I thought, we have a
                   <em>
                    huge
                   </em>
                   crisis on our hands," she says. "I knew my next goal would be to put together a statewide
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/spay-neuter.html">
                    spay/neuter
                   </a>
                   bill."
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Not being a resident of Louisiana, she settled for introducing the bill in her own state of California, and hopes other states will follow suit.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div id="ingageunit">
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <h2>
                   Chucking the "pinky in a dam" approach
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Mancuso's activism began long before then. In 1990, a TV special on pet overpopulation turned her from a carefree, meat-eating high-tech professional into a
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/vegetarian-dog-food-evolution-diet-christine-agro.html">
                    vegan
                   </a>
                   animal rescuer. "It showed all these healthy, beautiful, wonderful animals going to the
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/euthanasia.html">
                    euthanasia
                   </a>
                   table," she recalls. "I was just blown away. I couldn't believe that's how we dealt with the problem."
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   She started raising money for shelters,
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/pet-foster-tips.html">
                    fostering animals
                   </a>
                   , trapping feral cats, and staffing adoption events. Just prior to her fateful trip to New Orleans, she'd even quit her information technology job to devote herself full-time to animal rescue work. She and her husband, Rolf Wicklund, had already decided to forgo kids for the cause.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   But it felt a bit like sticking her pinky in a dam that was constantly springing more holes--an estimated 800,000 holes a year, according to one estimate of how many dogs and
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/cats.html">
                    cats
                   </a>
                   are abandoned in California each year. Roughly half those animals are euthanized.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "Every time we'd make some headway and save a couple animals, someone would dump more," says Mancuso wearily. "The litters just kept coming through the door. Oh my god, kitten season...!"
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <h2>
                   Crafting a new rescue strategy
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   While setting up yet another adoption event with fellow volunteers to place still more homeless animals, the talk kept circling back to the same question: Why aren't more people spaying and neutering their pets? Since Mancuso had observed, time and again, that many owners can't be bothered, she decided it was time for a new strategy.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   When she got back to California, Mancuso asked Ed Bok, general manager of Animal Services in Los Angeles, to work with her on crafting a mandatory spay/neuter bill. He agreed, and the two started making the rounds, visiting animal care and control officers,
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/finding-vet-hsus.html">
                    veterinarians
                   </a>
                   , police officers,
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/finding-a-good-breeder.html">
                    breeders
                   </a>
                   , and
                   <a href="http://dogtime.com/top-10-service-dogs.html">
                    service dog groups
                   </a>
                   for help in turning the idea into AB 1634, or the California Healthy Pets Act.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <h2>
                   The American Kennel Club objects
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   When Assemblyman Lloyd Levine introduced the bill to the California assembly in February of 2007, it churned up a political storm the likes of which the capitol hadn't seen since the debate on gay marriage.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "It's amazing how motivated people are, both for and against," Assemblyman Anthony Adams told the
                   <em>
                    Capitol Weekly
                   </em>
                   last summer, at the height of the frenzy. "I've never been lobbied this hard on anything."
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   On the con side were many breeders and the American Kennel Club. Not only does the AKC object to taking the spay/neuter decision out of the owners' hands, says their spokesperson, Lisa Peterson, they doubt such a law would work. She argues that backyard breeders won't comply with the law, and reputable breeders will end up getting penalized.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "The animals that show up in shelters come from a wide variety of situations, from dogs who've been relinquished by their owners to feral cats that are brought in," says Peterson. "To come up with one solution that only targets one segment of the pet owning population--the responsible owners--doesn't seem like a good plan."
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Although many animal welfare groups back the bill, at least a few will sit out this legislative round. San Mateo County has one of the oldest mandatory spay-neuter ordinances in the state--of 20 cities in the county, five have required spaying and neutering since the early '90s--but the head of the local humane society, Scott Delucchi, says he won't be lobbying for the bill.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "We've tried things on our own that we think are far more effective in getting people to alter their pets," he says, "such as educational outreach and low-cost spay/neuter services." He adds that when people are forced to fix their pets, they can become more angry than enlightened. "In some ways, the ordinance hurts us."
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <h2>
                   Santa Cruz makes a compelling case
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Proponents counter by holding up Santa Cruz as an example of what the law could be. The county has had a spay/neuter ordinance in place since 2006 and the local SPCA says its euthanasia rate has dropped 64 percent since then. "We've had a tremendous decrease in the number of animals coming in," says Tricia Geisreiter, a spokesperson for the Santa Cruz Animal Services Authority.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Lake County, Santa Barbara County, and L.A. County have all recently passed similar ordinances. Officials there say it's too soon to tell if it's making a dent in the overpopulation problem. But they do note that the program is proceeding smoothly, with none of the big brother elements that critics fear.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "We're not knocking on the doors of responsible owners saying, let's see your papers," says Paula Werner, program manager of Lake County Animal Care and Control. "But if you're a bad breeder who doesn't take care of your animals, you bet we'll be on your doorstep."
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Supporters also dismiss fears that the law would mean the demise of the beloved mutt. At best, they say, the law would cut down on 'oops' matings and on litters coming into the shelter. But it's not going to stop them completely.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   "There are still going to be plenty of dogs and cats in California," reassures Michael Markarian, executive vice president of the Humane Society of the United States, one of the bill's supporters.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <h2>
                   A second push to pass the bill
                  </h2>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   The bill squeaked through the assembly in June of 2007, passing by a single vote. But the following month, Levine pulled the bill when it seemed like members of a Senate committee would vote it down. But he didn't give up on it.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   In January of 2008, Levine reintroduced the bill to the State assembly. It faces a vote in April before the same committee; if passed, it will move on to the full Senate.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   Mancuso's confident that the second go around will be the charm. "We have tons of support, especially when the state's experiencing a $14 billion deficit and we're offering a bill that saves taxpayers' money," she says.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   If the bill passes and succeeds, Mancuso and her supporters believe other states would be close behind with their own spay/neuter laws. "As California goes, so goes the country. We think it could be pathbreaking animal welfare reform," says HSUS's Markarian.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   In the meantime, Mancuso's still meeting with legislators to craft a version that will pass. She celebrated the annual Spay Day, on February 26, at a signing ceremony that will make spay/neuter mandatory in the city of Los Angeles; the rest of L.A. county has already signed on.
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <div class="element">
                 <div class="element-content">
                  <p>
                   The same vision that ignited her rescue work, feeds her resolve now. She's just tired of seeing healthy cats and dogs killed for lack of a home. "It's going to be an honor to make this bill happen," she says.
                  </p>
                 </div>

             <h2>
              What does the Healthy Pets Act mean?
             </h2>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Pet owners must spay or neuter their dogs or cats by the age of six months, unless they buy an intact permit each year. The cost will be determined by local governments.
              </li>
              <li>
               Only certain animals would qualify for an intact permit, including those owned by a licensed breeder; show dogs and cats and dogs participating in canine sports; dogs used in law enforcement, fire departments, and search and rescue; those who are too old or sick to be safely spayed or neutered; dogs used to herd or protect livestock; and assistance dogs for the disabled.
              </li>
              <li>
               Owners could get an extension of up to one year of age, with a letter from a vet explaining that there medical reasons to wait.
              </li>
              <li>
               Until January 1, 2012, the bill would allow owners to buy an intact permit for one male and one female dog per household for a single litter.
              </li>
              <li>
               Money from the intact permits would fund the program.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              To read the latest version of the bill, click
              <a href="http://www.cahealthypets.com/ca-healthy-pets-ab-1634-read-the-bill.php">
               here
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <h3>
              Will it improve the puppy mill problem?
             </h3>
             <p>
              Not really. There are only about 20 licensed
              <a href="/puppy-mill-rescue.html">
               puppy mills
              </a>
              in the state, says Curt Ransom, regional program manager for the west coast regional office of the Humane Society. Most puppies in California pet store windows come from out of state breeders. Although the puppies would fall under the law once they arrived in California, their breeders wouldn't be affected.
             </p>
             <h3>
              What can I do to help pass this bill?
             </h3>
             <p>
              If you live in California, there's a lot you can do:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               The Humane Society is inviting California residents to send a letter to their local representatives in support of the bill. Click
               <a href="http://community.hsus.org/campaign/CA_2007_spay6">
                here
               </a>
               to send a message.
              </li>
              <li>
               The Healthy Pets Act campaign is looking for volunteers. You can sign up
               <a href="http://www.cahealthypets.com/ca-healthy-pets-ab-1634-join-us.php">
                here
               </a>
               to be contacted about volunteer opportunities.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              If you live outside California, you can fax a support letter to the Healthy Pets Act campaign at (310) 274-2003. And of course, anyone can
              <a href="http://www.socialcompassion.org/scil.htm">
               donate
              </a>
              to the campaign.
             </p>
