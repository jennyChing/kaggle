           <h1>
            Amazing Oil Paintings Of Lee Chen Dao
           </h1>
          </div>
          <div class="entry_copy">
           <input data-frizzlyhovercontainer="" data-frizzlypostcontainer="" data-frizzlyposttitle="Amazing Oil Paintings Of Lee Chen Dao" data-frizzlyposturl="http://www.numbermag.com/amazing-oil-paintings-lee-chen-dao/" type="hidden" value=""/>
           <p>
            Young Taiwanese Realist artist 
            <a class="external" href="http://bit.ly/1Bjl1db" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Chen Dao LEE
            </a>
            was born in 1982 in Taiwan. He obtained his MFA from the Department of Fine Arts (Masters degree) ,National Taiwan Normal University. Between the years of 2004  2009, he spent time developing his craft in LA, London, Paris, Beijing, Singapore, New York and San Francisco.
            <a class="external" href="http://bit.ly/1Bjl1db" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Chen Dao
            </a>
            has employed a strange way to rebel against new medias intervention in the traditional art of painting, he allows his art to become extremely Classic. Audiences with a brief understanding of art history can easily spot Lees manipulation of the subjects poses in his paintings to recreate Caravaggios Supper at Emmaus (1598), or Georges de La Tours The Cheat with the Ace of Clubs, c. late 1620s. Therefore, people have used the words parody or even describe
            <a class="external" href="http://bit.ly/1Bjl1db" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Chen Dao
            </a>
            as a Modern Story Teller with an Old Soul  to describe
            <a class="external" href="http://bit.ly/1Bjl1db" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Chen Dao
            </a>
            s ways of being able to tell modern stories with classical methods. In his recent series, Lee has shifted from depicting a logical scenario in his paintings to focusing on the figures entanglement, to describe emotions or relationships which are ambiguous, embarrassing or even helpless. Beautiful young women and fallen heroes frequently appear in Lees works as a symbol of the projection of modern peoples inner contradictions.
           </p>
