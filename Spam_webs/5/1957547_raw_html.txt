   <b>
    Explanation:
   </b>
   How old are Saturn's rings?  

No one is quite sure. 

One possibility is that the rings formed relatively recently in our
Solar System's history, perhaps only about 100 million years ago when
a moon-sized object
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_limit">
    broke up
   </a>
   near Saturn.  

Evidence for a young ring age includes a basic
   <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2007AJ....133..656V">
    stability
analysis for rings
   </a>
   , 
and the fact that the rings are so bright and
relatively unaffected by numerous small dark
   <a href="ap011118.html">
    meteor
   </a>
   impacts.
   <a href="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/news/news.cfm?release=2007-149">
    More recent 
evidence
   </a>
   , however, raises the possibility that some of
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn%27s_rings">
    Saturn's rings
   </a>
   may be billions of years old and so almost as
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeanne_Calment">
    old
   </a>
   as Saturn itself.   

Inspection of images by the Saturn-orbiting Cassini spacecraft
indicates that some of
   <a href="ap040723.html">
    Saturn's ring
   </a>
   particles temporarily
bunch and collide, effectively recycling
   <a href="ap071024.html">
    ring
   </a>
   particles by bringing
fresh bright ices to the surface.
   <a href="http://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/catalog/PIA09788">
    Seen here
   </a>
   , 
Saturn's rings were imaged in their true colors by the
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens">
    robotic
Cassini
   </a>
   in late October.  

Icy bright
   <a href="ap070909.html">
    Tethys
   </a>
   , 
a moon of Saturn likely brightened by a sandblasting
   <a href="ap071013.html">
    rain of ice
   </a>
   from sister moon
   <a href="ap060310.html">
    Enceladus
   </a>
   , is visible in front of
the darker rings.
