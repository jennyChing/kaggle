   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Avant-garde
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       avant-garde
      </b>
      (from French, "advance guard" or "vanguard", literally "fore-guard"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictref_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dictref-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) are people or works that are
      <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">
       experimental
      </a>
      or innovative, particularly with respect to
      <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">
       art
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">
       culture
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">
       politics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The avant-garde pushes the boundaries of what is accepted as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" title="Norm (sociology)">
       norm
      </a>
      or the
      <a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">
       status quo
      </a>
      , primarily in the cultural realm. The avant-garde is considered by some to be a hallmark of
      <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">
       modernism
      </a>
      , as distinct from
      <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">
       postmodernism
      </a>
      . Many artists have aligned themselves with the avant-garde movement and still continue to do so, tracing a history from
      <a href="/wiki/Dada" title="Dada">
       Dada
      </a>
      through
      <a href="/wiki/Situationist_International" title="Situationist International">
       the Situationists
      </a>
      to postmodern artists such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Language_poets" title="Language poets">
       Language poets
      </a>
      around 1981.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The avant-garde also promotes radical social reforms. It was this meaning that was evoked by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Simonian" title="Saint Simonian">
       Saint Simonian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Olinde_Rodrigues" title="Olinde Rodrigues">
       Olinde Rodrigues
      </a>
      in his essay "L'artiste, le savant et l'industriel" ("The artist, the scientist and the industrialist", 1825), which contains the first recorded use of "avant-garde" in its now customary sense: there, Rodrigues calls on artists to "serve as [the people's] avant-garde", insisting that "the power of the arts is indeed the most immediate and fastest way" to social, political and economic reform.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">
       Theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Avant-garde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="968" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg/200px-Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg/300px-Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg/400px-Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duchamp_Fountaine.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp" title="Marcel Duchamp">
         Marcel Duchamp
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Fountain_(Duchamp)" title="Fountain (Duchamp)">
          Fountain
         </a>
         ,
        </i>
        1917. Photograph by
        <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz" title="Alfred Stieglitz">
         Alfred Stieglitz
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Several writers have attempted, with limited success, to map the parameters of avant-garde activity. The Italian essayist
      <a href="/wiki/Renato_Poggioli" title="Renato Poggioli">
       Renato Poggioli
      </a>
      provides one of the best-known analyses of vanguardism as a cultural phenomenon in his 1962 book
      <i>
       Teoria dell'arte d'avanguardia
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       The Theory of the Avant-Garde
      </i>
      ). Surveying the historical, social, psychological and philosophical aspects of
      <a href="/wiki/Vanguardism" title="Vanguardism">
       vanguardism
      </a>
      , Poggioli reaches beyond individual instances of art, poetry, and music to show that vanguardists may share certain ideals or values which manifest themselves in the non-conformist lifestyles they adopt; he sees vanguard culture as a variety or subcategory of
      <a href="/wiki/Bohemianism" title="Bohemianism">
       Bohemianism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other authors have attempted both to clarify and to extend Poggioli's study. The
      <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">
       German
      </a>
      literary critic Peter Brger's
      <i>
       Theory of the Avant-Garde
      </i>
      (1974) looks at
      <a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">
       the Establishment
      </a>
      's embrace of socially critical works of art and suggests that in complicity with capitalism, "art as an institution neutralizes the political content of the individual work".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Brger's essay also greatly influenced the work of contemporary American art-historians such as the German
      <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_H._D._Buchloh" title="Benjamin H. D. Buchloh">
       Benjamin H. D. Buchloh
      </a>
      (born 1941), while older critics like Brger continue to view the postwar neo-avant-garde as the empty recycling of forms and strategies from the first two decades of the twentieth century, others like
      <a href="/wiki/Clement_Greenberg" title="Clement Greenberg">
       Clement Greenberg
      </a>
      (19091994) view it, more positively, as a new articulation of the specific conditions of cultural production in the postwar period.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Buchloh, in the collection of essays
      <i>
       Neo-avantgarde and Culture Industry
      </i>
      (2000) critically argues for a dialectical approach to these positions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Subsequent criticism theorized the limitations of these approaches, noting their circumscribed areas of analysis, including Eurocentric, chauvinist, and genre-specific definitions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_mainstream_society">
       Relation to mainstream society
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Avant-garde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to mainstream society">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Media_culture" title="Media culture">
       Media culture
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Spectacle_(critical_theory)" title="Spectacle (critical theory)">
       Spectacle (critical theory)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The concept of avant-garde refers primarily to artists, writers, composers and thinkers whose work is opposed to mainstream cultural values and often has a trenchant social or political edge. Many writers, critics and theorists made assertions about vanguard culture during the formative years of modernism, although the initial definitive statement on the avant-garde was the essay
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Avant-Garde_and_Kitsch" title="Avant-Garde and Kitsch">
        Avant-Garde and Kitsch
       </a>
      </i>
      by New York art critic
      <a href="/wiki/Clement_Greenberg" title="Clement Greenberg">
       Clement Greenberg
      </a>
      , published in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Partisan_Review" title="Partisan Review">
        Partisan Review
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1939.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As the essays title suggests, Greenberg argued that vanguard culture has historically been opposed to "high" or "mainstream" culture, and that it has also rejected the artificially synthesized
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_culture" title="Mass culture">
       mass culture
      </a>
      that has been produced by industrialization. Each of these media is a direct product of
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       Capitalism
      </a>
       they are all now substantial industries  and as such they are driven by the same profit-fixated motives of other sectors of manufacturing, not the ideals of true art. For Greenberg, these forms were therefore
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kitsch" title="Kitsch">
        kitsch
       </a>
      </i>
      : phony, faked or mechanical culture, which often pretended to be more than they were by using formal devices stolen from vanguard culture. For instance, during the 1930s the advertising industry was quick to take visual mannerisms from
      <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">
       surrealism
      </a>
      , but this does not mean that 1930s advertising photographs are truly surreal. On the contrary, they express a style without underlying substance. In this sense Greenberg carefully distinguished true avant-garde creativity from the market-driven fashion change and superficial stylistic innovation that are sometimes used
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2014)">
          by whom?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      to claim privileged status for these manufactured forms of the new
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_culture" title="Consumer culture">
       consumer culture
      </a>
      .
     </p>

        </a>
        (front left),
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodor_Adorno" title="Theodor Adorno">

        </a>
        (front right), and
        <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jrgen Habermas">
         Jrgen Habermas
        </a>
        in the background, right, in 1965 at
        <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">
         Heidelberg
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">
         West Germany
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Various members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_School" title="Frankfurt School">
       Frankfurt School
      </a>
      argued similar views: thus
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodor_Adorno" title="Theodor Adorno">
       Theodor Adorno
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Horkheimer" title="Max Horkheimer">
       Max Horkheimer
      </a>
      in their essay
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dialectic_of_Enlightenment" title="Dialectic of Enlightenment">
        The Culture Industry: Enlightenment as Mass-Deception
       </a>
      </i>
      (1944), and also
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Benjamin" title="Walter Benjamin">
       Walter Benjamin
      </a>
      in his highly influential "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Work_of_Art_in_the_Age_of_Mechanical_Reproduction" title="The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction">
       The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction
      </a>
      " (1936).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Where Greenberg used the
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kitsch" title="Kitsch">
        kitsch
       </a>
      </i>
      to describe the antithesis of avant-garde culture, members of the Frankfurt School coined the term "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_culture" title="Mass culture">
       mass culture
      </a>
      " to indicate that this bogus culture is constantly being manufactured by a newly emerged
      <a href="/wiki/Culture_industry" title="Culture industry">
       culture industry
      </a>
      (comprising commercial publishing houses, the movie industry, the record industry, and the electronic media).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also pointed out that the rise of this industry meant that artistic excellence was displaced by sales figures as a measure of worth: a novel, for example, was judged meritorious solely on whether it became a best-seller, music succumbed to ratings charts and to the blunt commercial logic of the Gold disc. In this way the autonomous artistic merit so dear to the vanguardist was abandoned and sales increasingly became the measure, and justification, of everything. Consumer culture now ruled.
     </p>
     <p>
      The avant-garde's
      <a href="/wiki/Recuperation_(politics)" title="Recuperation (politics)">
       co-option
      </a>
      by the global capitalist market, by
      <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">
       neoliberal
      </a>
      economies, and by what
      <a href="/wiki/Guy_Debord" title="Guy Debord">
       Guy Debord
      </a>
      called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Society_of_the_Spectacle" title="The Society of the Spectacle">
        The Society of the Spectacle
       </a>
      </i>
      , have made contemporary critics speculate on the possibility of a meaningful avant-garde today. Paul Mann's
      <i>
       Theory-Death of the Avant-Garde
      </i>
      demonstrates how completely the avant-garde is embedded within institutional structures today, a thought also pursued by Richard Schechner in his analyses of avant-garde performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite the central arguments of Greenberg, Adorno and others, various sectors of the mainstream culture industry have co-opted and misapplied the term "avant-garde" since the 1960s, chiefly as a marketing tool to publicise popular music and commercial cinema. It has become common to describe successful rock musicians and celebrated film-makers as "avant-garde", the very word having been stripped of its proper meaning. Noting this important conceptual shift, major contemporary theorists such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matei_Calinescu" title="Matei Calinescu">
       Matei Calinescu
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       Five Faces of Modernity: Modernism, Avant-garde, Decadence, Kitsch, Postmodernism
      </i>
      (1987),
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2012)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and Hans Bertens in
      <i>
       The Idea of the Postmodern: A History
      </i>
      (1995),
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2012)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      have suggested that this is a sign our culture has entered a new
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-modern" title="Post-modern">
       post-modern
      </a>
      age, when the former
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist" title="Modernist">
       modernist
      </a>
      ways of thinking and behaving have been rendered redundant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nevertheless the most incisive
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
         <span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (September 2012)">
          need quotation to verify
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      critique of vanguardism as against the views of mainstream society was offered by the New York critic
      <a href="/wiki/Harold_Rosenberg" title="Harold Rosenberg">
       Harold Rosenberg
      </a>
      in the late 1960s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (May 2008)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Trying to strike a balance between the insights of Renato Poggioli and the claims of Clement Greenberg, Rosenberg suggested that from the mid-1960s onward progressive culture ceased to fulfill its former adversarial role. Since then it has been flanked by what he called "avant-garde ghosts" to the one side, and a changing mass culture on the other, both of which it interacts with to varying degrees. This has seen culture become, in his words, "a profession one of whose aspects is the pretense of overthrowing it".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
       Music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Avant-garde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde_music" title="Avant-garde music">
       Avant-garde music
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Avant-garde in music can refer to any form of music working within traditional structures while seeking to breach boundaries in some manner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iha_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-iha-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term is used loosely to describe the work of any musicians who radically depart from tradition altogether.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By this definition, some avant-garde composers of the 20th century include
      <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">
       Arnold Schoenberg
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_xiv_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_xiv-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Ives" title="Charles Ives">
       Charles Ives
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">
       Igor Stravinsky
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_xiv_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_xiv-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Anton_Webern" title="Anton Webern">
       Anton Webern
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_50_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_50-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/George_Antheil" title="George Antheil">
       George Antheil
      </a>
      (in his earliest works only),
      <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">
       Alban Berg
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_50_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_50-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cowell" title="Henry Cowell">
       Henry Cowell
      </a>
      (in his earliest works),
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">
       Philip Glass
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Harry_Partch" title="Harry Partch">
       Harry Partch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">
       John Cage
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Morton_Feldman" title="Morton Feldman">
       Morton Feldman
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">
       Richard Strauss
      </a>
      (in his earliest work),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_xiii_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_xiii-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Stockhausen" title="Karlheinz Stockhausen">
       Karlheinz Stockhausen
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Edgard_Var%C3%A8se" title="Edgard Varse">
       Edgard Varse
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Iannis_Xenakis" title="Iannis Xenakis">
       Iannis Xenakis
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_xiv_17-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_xiv-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although most avant-garde composers have been men, this is not exclusively the case. Women avant-gardists include
      <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Oliveros" title="Pauline Oliveros">
       Pauline Oliveros
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Diamanda_Gal%C3%A1s" title="Diamanda Gals">
       Diamanda Gals
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Meredith_Monk" title="Meredith Monk">
       Meredith Monk
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Laurie_Anderson" title="Laurie Anderson">
       Laurie Anderson
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_xvii_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_xvii-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is another definition of "Avant-gardism" that distinguishes it from "modernism": Peter Brger, for example, says avant-gardism rejects the "institution of art" and challenges social and artistic values, and so necessarily involves political, social, and cultural factors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to the composer and musicologist
      <a href="/wiki/Larry_Sitsky" title="Larry Sitsky">
       Larry Sitsky
      </a>
      , modernist composers from the early 20th century who do not qualify as avant-gardists include Arnold Schoenberg, Anton Webern, and Igor Stravinsky; later modernist composers who do not fall into the category of avant-gardists include
      <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Carter" title="Elliott Carter">
       Elliott Carter
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Milton_Babbitt" title="Milton Babbitt">
       Milton Babbitt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Ligeti" title="Gyrgy Ligeti">
       Gyrgy Ligeti
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Witold_Lutos%C5%82awski" title="Witold Lutosawski">
       Witold Lutosawski
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Luciano_Berio" title="Luciano Berio">
       Luciano Berio
      </a>
      , since "their modernism was not conceived for the purpose of goading an audience."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitsky_xv_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sitsky_xv-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">
       Theatre
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Experimental_theatre" title="Experimental theatre">
       Experimental theatre
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Whereas the avant-garde has a significant history in 20th-century music, it is more pronounced in theatre and performance art, and often in conjunction with music and sound design innovations, as well as developments in visual media design. There are movements in theatre history that are characterized by their contributions to the avant-garde traditions in both the United States and Europe. Among these are
      <a href="/wiki/Fluxus" title="Fluxus">
       Fluxus
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Happenings" title="Happenings">
       Happenings
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Neo-Dada" title="Neo-Dada">
       Neo-Dada
      </a>
      .
     </p>
