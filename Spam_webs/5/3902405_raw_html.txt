     <p>
      <b>
       Dinah Shore
      </b>
      (born
      <b>
       Frances Rose Shore
      </b>
      ; February 29, 1916  February 24, 1994) was an
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      singer, actress, television personality, and the top-charting female vocalist of the 1940s. She reached the height of her popularity as a recording artist during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Band" title="Big Band">
       Big Band
      </a>
      era of the 1940s and 1950s, but achieved even greater success a decade later, in television, mainly as hostess of a series of variety programs for Chevrolet.
     </p>
     <p>
      After failing singing auditions for the bands of
      <a href="/wiki/Benny_Goodman" title="Benny Goodman">
       Benny Goodman
      </a>
      and both
      <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dorsey" title="Jimmy Dorsey">
       Jimmy Dorsey
      </a>
      and his brother
      <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Dorsey" title="Tommy Dorsey">
       Tommy Dorsey
      </a>
      , Shore struck out on her own to become the first singer of her era to achieve huge solo success. She had a string of 80 charted popular hits, lasting from 1940 into the late 1950s, and after appearing in a handful of films went on to a four-decade career in American television, starring in her own music and variety shows in the 1950s and 1960s and hosting two talk shows in the 1970s.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">
        TV Guide
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine ranked her at #16 on their list of the top fifty television stars of all time. Stylistically, Shore was compared to two singers who followed her in the mid-to-late 1940s and early 1950s,
      <a href="/wiki/Doris_Day" title="Doris Day">
       Doris Day
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Patti_Page" title="Patti Page">
       Patti Page
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Frances Rose Shore was born to Anna (ne Stein) and Solomon Shore in
      <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Tennessee" title="Winchester, Tennessee">
       Winchester, Tennessee
      </a>
      , joining an older sister, Bessie. When she was two years old, she was stricken with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">
       polio
      </a>
      (infantile paralysis), a disease that was not preventable at the time, and for which the only real treatment was bed rest. Her parents provided intensive care for her and she recovered, but she sustained a deformed foot and limp; luckily it wasn't a physical impediment. She loved to sing as a small child; her mother, a
      <a href="/wiki/Contralto" title="Contralto">
       contralto
      </a>
      with operatic aspirations, encouraged her. Her father would often take her to his store where she would perform impromptu songs for the customers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1924, the Shore family moved to
      <a href="/wiki/McMinnville,_Tennessee" title="McMinnville, Tennessee">
       McMinnville, Tennessee
      </a>
      , where her father had opened a department store. By her fifth-grade year the family had moved to Nashville, Tennessee,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where she completed elementary school. Although shy because of her limp, she became actively involved in sports, was a cheerleader at Nashville's
      <a href="/wiki/Hume-Fogg_High_School" title="Hume-Fogg High School">
       Hume-Fogg High School
      </a>
      , and was involved in other activities. At 14 she debuted as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torch_singer" title="Torch singer">
       torch singer
      </a>
      at a Nashville nightclub only to find her parents sitting ringside, having been tipped off to their daughter's performance ahead of time. They allowed her to finish, but put her professional career on hold. She was paid $10, equivalent to $141 in 2015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-US_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When Shore was 16, her mother died unexpectedly of a
      <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">
       heart attack
      </a>
      and she decided to pursue her education. She went to
      <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_University" title="Vanderbilt University">
       Vanderbilt University
      </a>
      , where she participated in many events and activities, including the Chi chapter of the
      <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Epsilon_Phi" title="Alpha Epsilon Phi">
       Alpha Epsilon Phi
      </a>
      Sorority. She graduated from the university in 1938 with a degree in
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociology
      </a>
      . She also visited the
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">
       Grand Ole Opry
      </a>
      and made her radio debut on Nashville's
      <a href="/wiki/WSM_(AM)" title="WSM (AM)">
       WSM (AM)
      </a>
      radio station in these years. Shore decided to return to pursuing her career in singing, so she went to
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      to audition for orchestras and radio stations, first on a
      <a href="/wiki/Summer_vacation" title="Summer vacation">
       summer break
      </a>
      from Vanderbilt, and after graduation, for good. In many of her auditions, she sang the popular song "
      <a href="/wiki/Dinah_(song)" title="Dinah (song)">
       Dinah
      </a>
      ." When disc jockey
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Block" title="Martin Block">
       Martin Block
      </a>
      could not remember her name, he called her the "Dinah girl," and soon after the name stuck, becoming her
      <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">
       stage name
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shore eventually was hired as a vocalist at radio station
      <a href="/wiki/WBBR" title="WBBR">
       WNEW
      </a>
      , where she sang with
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">
       Frank Sinatra
      </a>
      . She recorded and performed with the
      <a href="/wiki/Xavier_Cugat" title="Xavier Cugat">
       Xavier Cugat
      </a>
      orchestra, and signed a
      <a href="/wiki/Recording_contract" title="Recording contract">
       recording contract
      </a>
      with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" title="RCA Victor">
       RCA Victor Records
      </a>
      in 1940.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">
       Music career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_1940s">
       The 1940s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The 1940s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In March 1939, Shore debuted on national radio on the Sunday afternoon
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBS_radio" title="CBS radio">
       CBS radio
      </a>
      program,
      <i>
       Ben Bernie's Orchestra
      </i>
      . In February 1940, she became a featured vocalist on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBC_Radio" title="NBC Radio">
       NBC Radio
      </a>
      program
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Chamber_Music_Society_of_Lower_Basin_Street" title="The Chamber Music Society of Lower Basin Street">
        The Chamber Music Society of Lower Basin Street
       </a>
      </i>
      , a showcase for traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Dixieland" title="Dixieland">
       Dixieland
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">
       Blues
      </a>
      songs. With Shore, the program became so popular that it was moved from 4:30 Sunday afternoon to a 9:00 Monday night time slot in September. In her
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime-time" title="Prime-time">
       prime-time
      </a>
      debut for "the music of the Three Bs, Barrelhouse,
      <a href="/wiki/Boogie-woogie" title="Boogie-woogie">
       Boogie-woogie
      </a>
      and the Blues", she was introduced as "Mademoiselle Dinah 'Diva' Shore, who starts a fire by rubbing two notes together!"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She recorded with the two
      <i>
       Basin Street
      </i>
      bands for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" title="RCA Victor">
       RCA Victor
      </a>
      ; one of her records was the eponymous "Dinah's Blues."
     </p>
     <p>
      Shore's singing came to the attention of
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cantor" title="Eddie Cantor">
       Eddie Cantor
      </a>
      . He signed her as a regular on his radio show,
      <i>
       Time to Smile
      </i>
      , in 1940. Shore credits him for teaching her self-confidence, comedic timing, and the ways of connecting with an audience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cantor bought the rights to an adapted Ukrainian
      <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">
       folk song
      </a>
      with new lyrics by
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lawrence" title="Jack Lawrence">
       Jack Lawrence
      </a>
      for Shore to record for RCA Victor's
      <a href="/wiki/Bluebird_Records" title="Bluebird Records">
       Bluebird
      </a>
      label. This song, "
      <a href="/wiki/Yes,_My_Darling_Daughter" title="Yes, My Darling Daughter">
       Yes, My Darling Daughter
      </a>
      ", became her first major hit, selling 500,000 copies in weeks, which was unusual for that time.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shore soon became a successful singing star with her own radio show in 1943,
      <i>
       Call to Music
      </i>
      . Also in 1943, she appeared in her first movie,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thank_Your_Lucky_Stars_(1943_film)" title="Thank Your Lucky Stars (1943 film)">
        Thank Your Lucky Stars
       </a>
      </i>
      , starring Cantor. She soon went to another radio show,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Whiteman" title="Paul Whiteman">
        Paul Whiteman
       </a>
       Presents
      </i>
      . During this time, the United States was involved in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      and Shore became a favorite with the troops. She had hits, including "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blues_In_the_Night" title="Blues In the Night">
       Blues In the Night
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc1a_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pc1a-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Jim", "You'd Be So Nice To Come Home To", and "I'll Walk Alone", the first of her number-one hits. To support the troops overseas, she participated in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USO" title="USO">
       USO
      </a>
      tours to
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      . She met
      <a href="/wiki/George_Montgomery_(actor)" title="George Montgomery (actor)">
       George Montgomery
      </a>
      , a young actor ready to go into military service. They married on December 3, 1943, shortly before he went into service. When he returned, they settled in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando,_California" title="San Fernando, California">
       San Fernando, California
      </a>
      . In 1948, their first child was born, a daughter named Melissa Ann, and they adopted a son in 1954 named John David before moving to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills" title="Beverly Hills">
       Beverly Hills
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shore continued appearing in radio shows throughout the 1940s, including
      <i>
       Birds Eye-Open House
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Ford Radio Show
      </i>
      . In early 1946, she moved to another label,
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">
       Columbia Records
      </a>
      . At Columbia, Shore enjoyed the greatest commercial success of her recording career, starting with her first Columbia single release, "Shoo Fly Pie And Apple Pan Dowdy", and peaking with the most popular song of 1948, "
      <a href="/wiki/Buttons_and_Bows" title="Buttons and Bows">
       Buttons and Bows
      </a>
      ", (with
      <a href="/wiki/Henri_Ren%C3%A9" title="Henri Ren">
       Henri Ren
      </a>
      &amp; Orchestra) which was number one for ten weeks. Other number one hits at Columbia included "The Gypsy" and "The Anniversary Song". One of her most popular recordings was the holiday perennial "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby,_It%27s_Cold_Outside_(song)" title="Baby, It's Cold Outside (song)">
       Baby, It's Cold Outside
      </a>
      " with
      <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Clark" title="Buddy Clark">
       Buddy Clark
      </a>
      from 1949. The song was covered by many other artists, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ella_Fitzgerald" title="Ella Fitzgerald">
       Ella Fitzgerald
      </a>
      . Other hits during her four years at Columbia included "Laughing on the Outside (Crying on the Inside)", "I Wish I Didn't Love You So", "I Love You (For Sentimental Reasons)", "Doin' What Comes Naturally", and "Dear Hearts And Gentle People". She was a regular with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%22Smilin%27%22_Jack_Smith_(music)" title="&quot;Smilin'&quot; Jack Smith (music)">
       Jack Smith
      </a>
      on his
      <a href="/wiki/The_Jack_Smith_Show" title="The Jack Smith Show">
       quarter-hour radio show
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">
       CBS
      </a>
      . Shore was a musical guest in the films
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thank_Your_Lucky_Stars_(1943_film)" title="Thank Your Lucky Stars (1943 film)">
        Thank Your Lucky Stars
       </a>
      </i>
      (1943),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Follow_the_Boys" title="Follow the Boys">
        Follow the Boys
       </a>
      </i>
      (1944) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Till_the_Clouds_Roll_By" title="Till the Clouds Roll By">
        Till the Clouds Roll By
       </a>
      </i>
      (1946) and had starring roles in Danny Kaye's debut
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Up_in_Arms" title="Up in Arms">
        Up in Arms
       </a>
      </i>
      (1944) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Belle_of_the_Yukon" title="Belle of the Yukon">
        Belle of the Yukon
       </a>
      </i>
      (1945). She lent her musical voice to two
      <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">
       Disney
      </a>
      films:
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Make_Mine_Music" title="Make Mine Music">
        Make Mine Music
       </a>
      </i>
      (1946) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fun_and_Fancy_Free" title="Fun and Fancy Free">
        Fun and Fancy Free
       </a>
      </i>
      (1947). Her last starring film role was for
      <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
       Paramount Pictures
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Slick_from_Punkin_Crick" title="Aaron Slick from Punkin Crick">
        Aaron Slick from Punkin Crick
       </a>
      </i>
      (1952), co-starring
      <a href="/wiki/Alan_Young" title="Alan Young">
       Alan Young
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Opera" title="Metropolitan Opera">
       Metropolitan Opera
      </a>
      star
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Merrill" title="Robert Merrill">
       Robert Merrill
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">
       1950s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1950s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1950, Shore went back to RCA with a deal to record 100 sides for $1,000,000, equal to some ten million dollars in 2013 money. The hits kept coming, but with less frequency, and were not charting as high as in the '40s. Dinah's biggest hits of this era were "
      <a href="/wiki/My_Heart_Cries_for_You" title="My Heart Cries for You">
       My Heart Cries for You
      </a>
      " and "Sweet Violets", both peaking at number three in 1951. Several duets with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Martin_(entertainer)" title="Tony Martin (entertainer)">
       Tony Martin
      </a>
      did well, with "A Penny A Kiss" being the most popular, reaching number eight. "Blue Canary" was a 1953 hit and her covers of "Changing Partners" and "If I Give My Heart To You" were popular top twenty hits. "Love and Marriage" and "Whatever Lola Wants" were top twenty hits from 1955. "Chantez, Chantez" was her last top twenty hit, staying on the charts for over twenty weeks in 1957. Shore stayed with RCA until 1958, and during that time released albums including
      <i>
       Bouquet of Blues
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Once in a While
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       Vivacious
      </i>
      , which were collections of singles with different orchestras and conductors such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_DeVol" title="Frank DeVol">
       Frank DeVol
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Winterhalter" title="Hugo Winterhalter">
       Hugo Winterhalter
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Holding Hands at Midnight
      </i>
      a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">
       studio album
      </a>
      from 1955 and
      <i>
       Moments Like These
      </i>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">
       studio album
      </a>
      from 1958, recorded in stereo, with orchestra under the musical direction of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Zimmerman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harry Zimmerman (page does not exist)">
       Harry Zimmerman
      </a>
      , who performed the same duties on
      <i>
       The Dinah Shore Chevy Show
      </i>
      , being the exceptions.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_career_after_the_1950s">
       Recording career after the 1950s
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recording career after the 1950s">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1958 Dinah was wooed from RCA by
      <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">
       Capitol Records
      </a>
      . Although she recorded only one "almost" hit for her new label (
      <i>
       I Ain't Down Yet
      </i>
      , which peaked at 102 on
      <a href="/wiki/Billboard_charts" title="Billboard charts">
       Billboard
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">
       pop chart
      </a>
      in 1960), the collaboration produced four "
      <a href="/wiki/Theme_music" title="Theme music">
       theme albums
      </a>
      " that paired Dinah with arranger
      <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Riddle" title="Nelson Riddle">
       Nelson Riddle
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Dinah, Yes Indeed!
      </i>
      ) conductor and accompanist
      <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Previn" title="Andr Previn">
       Andr Previn
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Somebody Loves Me
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Dinah Sings, Previn Plays
      </i>
      ) and jazz's
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Norvo" title="Red Norvo">
       Red Norvo
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Dinah Sings Some Blues With Red
      </i>
      ). Her final two Capitol albums were
      <i>
       Dinah, Down Home
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       The Fabulous Hits (Newly Recorded)
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Shore left Capitol in 1962 and recorded only a handful of albums over the next two decades, including
      <i>
       Lower Basin Street Revisited
      </i>
      for friend Frank Sinatra's
      <a href="/wiki/Reprise_Records" title="Reprise Records">
       Reprise
      </a>
      label in 1965,
      <i>
       Songs For Sometime Losers
      </i>
      (Project 3, 1967),
      <i>
       Country Feelin'
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">
       Decca
      </a>
      , 1969),
      <i>
       Once Upon A Summertime
      </i>
      (Stanyan, 1975) and
      <i>
       Dinah!
      </i>
      , a double LP for Capitol in 1976. She recorded this album at the height of her talk show fame, and it featured her take on contemporary hits such as
      <i>
       50 Ways To Leave Your Lover
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       The Hungry Years
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       Do You Know Where You're Going To (Theme from "Mahogany")
      </i>
      . Her final studio album was released in 1979,
      <i>
       Dinah! Visits Sesame Street,
      </i>
      for the Children's Television Workshop.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">
       Radio
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Radio">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Shore starred in seven radio series of her own between 1941 and 1954.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She made hundreds of guest appearances in shows including an episode of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Suspense_(radio_drama)" title="Suspense (radio drama)">
        Suspense
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Frankie and Johnny", May 5, 1952).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_television_career">
       Early television career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinah_Shore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early television career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dinah_in_Chevy.jpeg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="110" data-file-width="150" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/98/Dinah_in_Chevy.jpeg" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dinah_in_Chevy.jpeg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Dinah Shore singing "
        <a href="/wiki/See_the_USA_in_Your_Chevrolet" title="See the USA in Your Chevrolet">
         See the USA in Your Chevrolet
        </a>
        " in a television advertisement.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Soon after she arrived in New York in 1937, Shore made her first television appearances on experimental broadcasts for
      <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">
       NBC
      </a>
      over station W2XBS in New York (now
      <a href="/wiki/WNBC" title="WNBC">
       WNBC
      </a>
      ). Twelve years later, in 1949, she made her commercial television debut on the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ed_Wynn" title="Ed Wynn">
        Ed Wynn
       </a>
       Show
      </i>
      from Los Angeles over
      <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">
       CBS
      </a>
      and on Easter Sunday 1950, made a guest appearance on
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">
       Bob Hope
      </a>
      's first network television show on NBC. After being on other television shows, she got her own program,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Dinah_Shore_Show" title="The Dinah Shore Show">
        The Dinah Shore Show
       </a>
      </i>
      on NBC in 1951.
      <a href="/wiki/Vic_Schoen" title="Vic Schoen">
       Vic Schoen
      </a>
      was her musical director from 195154, and also arranged music for her on the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colgate_Comedy_Hour" title="Colgate Comedy Hour">
        Colgate Comedy Hour
       </a>
      </i>
      (1954). She did two 15-minute shows a week for NBC. She guest starred on another 15-minute series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tony_Martin_Show" title="The Tony Martin Show">
        The Tony Martin Show
       </a>
      </i>
      starring entertainer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tony_Martin_(entertainer)" title="Tony Martin (entertainer)">
       Tony Martin
      </a>
      , but Martin's short program never acquired the popular success as did Shore's. In 1956, Shore won the first of her many
      <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">
       Emmy Awards
      </a>
      for the program, which was sponsored by
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">
       Chevrolet
      </a>
      . The sponsor's theme song, "
      <a href="/wiki/See_the_USA_in_Your_Chevrolet" title="See the USA in Your Chevrolet">
       See the USA in Your Chevrolet
      </a>
      ", soon became the singer's signature piece.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1956, Shore began hosting a monthly series of one-hour full-color spectaculars as part of NBC's
      <i>
       The Chevy Show
      </i>
      series. These proved so popular that the show was renamed
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Dinah_Shore_Chevy_Show" title="The Dinah Shore Chevy Show">
        The Dinah Shore Chevy Show
       </a>
      </i>
      the following season, with Shore becoming the full-time host, helming three out of four weeks in the month. Broadcast live and in NBC's famous "Living Color," this variety show was one of the most popular of the 1950s and early 1960s and featured the television debuts of stars of the era, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Yves_Montand" title="Yves Montand">
       Yves Montand
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Maureen_O%27Hara" title="Maureen O'Hara">
       Maureen O'Hara
      </a>
      , and featured Dinah in performances alongside
      <a href="/wiki/Ella_Fitzgerald" title="Ella Fitzgerald">
       Ella Fitzgerald
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mahalia_Jackson" title="Mahalia Jackson">
       Mahalia Jackson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Lee" title="Peggy Lee">
       Peggy Lee
      </a>
      , Frank Sinatra and
      <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Bailey" title="Pearl Bailey">
       Pearl Bailey
      </a>
      . She also appeared as a guest on another Chevrolet-sponsored variety show,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pat_Boone_Chevy_Showroom" title="The Pat Boone Chevy Showroom">

       </a>
      </i>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
       ABC
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       The Dinah Shore Chevy Show
      </i>
      ran through the 19611962 season, after which Chevrolet dropped sponsorship, and Shore hosted a series of monthly broadcasts sponsored by The American Dairy Association and
      <a href="/wiki/S%26H_Green_Stamps" title="S&amp;H Green Stamps">
       Green Stamps
      </a>
      . Simply called "The Dinah Shore Show", Dinah's guests included
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nat_%22King%22_Cole" title='Nat "King" Cole'>
       Nat "King" Cole
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">
       Bing Crosby
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lemmon" title="Jack Lemmon">
       Jack Lemmon
      </a>
      , and a young
      <a href="/wiki/Barbra_Streisand" title="Barbra Streisand">
       Barbra Streisand
      </a>
      . Over twelve seasons, from 1951 to 1963, Shore made 125 hour-long programs and 444 fifteen-minute shows. She always ended her televised programs by throwing an enthusiastic kiss directly to the cameras (and viewers) and exclaiming "MWAH!" to the audience.
     </p>

     <p>
      From 1970 through 1980, Shore hosted two daytime programs,
      <i>
       Dinah's Place
      </i>
      (19701974) on NBC and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dinah!" title="Dinah!">
        Dinah!
       </a>
      </i>
      (later
      <i>
       Dinah and Friends
      </i>
      ) in syndication from 1974 through 1980 and a third cable program from 19891992.
      <i>
       Dinah's Place
      </i>
      , primarily sponsored by
      <a href="/wiki/Colgate-Palmolive" title="Colgate-Palmolive">
       Colgate-Palmolive
      </a>
      (which later sponsored her women's golf tournament), was a 30-minute Monday-through-Friday program broadcast at 10:00am(et) over NBC, her network home since 1939. Shore described this show as a "Do-Show" as opposed to a chat show because she would have her guests demonstrate an unexpected skill, for example Frank Sinatra sharing his
      <a href="/wiki/Tomato_sauce" title="Tomato sauce">
       spaghetti sauce
      </a>
      recipe,
      <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">
       Spiro Agnew
      </a>
      playing keyboard accompanying Dinah on "Sophisticated Lady", or
      <a href="/wiki/Ginger_Rogers" title="Ginger Rogers">
       Ginger Rogers
      </a>
      showing Shore how to throw a clay pot on a
      <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel" title="Potter's wheel">
       potter's wheel
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Although
      <i>
       Dinah's Place
      </i>
      featured famous guest stars, often Shore grilled lesser-known lifestyle experts on nutrition, exercise or homemaking. Despite being one of the more popular programs in NBC's morning lineup, this show left the air in 1974 after NBC sent a telegram to Dinah congratulating her on her
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmy" title="Emmy">
       Emmy
      </a>
      winat the same time informing her the show was being cancelled, because it broke up a "game show programming block". Thus ended the network's 35-year association with Shore. She returned that fall with
      <i>
       Dinah!
      </i>
      , a syndicated 90 minute daily talk show (also seen in a 60-minute version on some stations) that put the focus on top guest stars and entertainment. This show was seen as competition for
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Douglas" title="Mike Douglas">
       Mike Douglas
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Merv_Griffin" title="Merv Griffin">
       Merv Griffin
      </a>
      , whose shows had been on the air for 10 years when
      <i>
       Dinah!
      </i>
      debuted. Frequent guests included entertainment figures (
      <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Ball" title="Lucille Ball">
       Lucille Ball
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">
       Bob Hope
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">
       James Stewart
      </a>
      ) as well as regular contributors including lifestyle guru Dr.
      <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Dyer" title="Wayne Dyer">
       Wayne Dyer
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">

      <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">

      </a>

This program was taped live in front of a studio audience and the "Egg" segment was never aired; it is believed that the offending footage was destroyed. Shore's producers superimposed titles such as "This is a put on" over the footage that was eventually aired, including an uncomfortable duet of "Anything You Can Do", and his solo of "On The Street Where You Live". Shooting was stopped and Kaufman was escorted out of the studio.

      <a href="/wiki/Gene_Krupa" title="Gene Krupa">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Golf">
       Golf
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     <p>
      Shore, who played golf, was a longtime supporter of women's
      <a href="/wiki/Professional_golf" title="Professional golf">
       professional golf
      </a>
      . In 1972, she helped found the Colgate Dinah Shore golf tournament, which in its current identity as the
      <a href="/wiki/ANA_Inspiration" title="ANA Inspiration">
       ANA Inspiration
      </a>
      remains one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_major_golf_championships" title="Women's major golf championships">
       major golf tournaments
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/LPGA" title="LPGA">
       LPGA Tour
      </a>
      . The tournament is held each spring near Shore's former home in
      <a href="/wiki/Rancho_Mirage,_California" title="Rancho Mirage, California">
       Rancho Mirage, California
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Shore was the first female member of the famed
      <a href="/wiki/Hillcrest_Country_Club_(Los_Angeles)" title="Hillcrest Country Club (Los Angeles)">
       Hillcrest Country Club
      </a>
      in Los Angeles.
     </p>
     <p>
      In acknowledgment of her contributions to golf, Shore was elected an honorary member of the LPGA Hall of Fame in 1994.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

      <a href="/wiki/World_Golf_Hall_of_Fame" title="World Golf Hall of Fame">
       World Golf Hall of Fame
      </a>
      when it absorbed the LPGA Hall in 1998. She also received the 1993
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Tom_Morris_Award" title="Old Tom Morris Award">
       Old Tom Morris Award
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Golf_Course_Superintendents_Association_of_America" title="Golf Course Superintendents Association of America">
       Golf Course Superintendents Association of America
      </a>
      , GCSAA's highest honor.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Donald_Wexler" title="Donald Wexler">

     <p>
      In both
      <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_City,_California" title="Cathedral City, California">
       Cathedral City, California
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rancho_Mirage,_California" title="Rancho Mirage, California">
       Rancho Mirage, California
      </a>
      , there are streets named after her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In her birthplace of
      <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Tennessee" title="Winchester, Tennessee">
       Winchester, Tennessee
      </a>
      , there is Dinah Shore Boulevard.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1991, Dinah Shore was inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/Television_Hall_of_Fame" title="Television Hall of Fame">
       Television Hall of Fame
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006, a Golden Palm Star on the
      <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs_Walk_of_Stars" title="Palm Springs Walk of Stars">
       Palm Springs Walk of Stars
      </a>
      was dedicated to her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She is survived by her two children, and three grandchildren; Jennifer Hime, Adam Hime, and Alex Hime.
     </p>
