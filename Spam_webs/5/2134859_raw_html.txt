         <p>
          <strong>
           Oxford University has decided to add consensual sex as a new subject in its syllabus.
          </strong>
          This is no joke, for the simple reason that many students dont understand the clear distinction between pleasure and sexual violence, especially when under the influence of alcohol.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Rather than just offering sex education courses, the universitys workshops teach students the concept of sexual consen
          </strong>
          t: what is it; how do you get it; how do you give it? Statistics indicate that these courses are more necessary than ever because 61% of students who took part in the courses knew friends or acquaintances affected by the issue. One in four female students in Oxford have been a victim of sexual abuse, a total of 1,400 graduates, according to the organisers of the new classes. One of them, Anna Bradshaw, said: I want to break the culture of silence.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           In most cases, the abuser is not an armed psychopath in a dark alley but a person met at a party, who mistakes the victims inebriation and sexy clothing for consent
          </strong>
          . This is what happened to Maria Marcello, a second-year student, who wrote a post under a false name, titled: I was raped at Oxford University. She says she was raped by a friend of a friend while she slept in her bed, drunk after a night of poker and alcohol. She has an indelible image of him in her mind although the vision lasted only a few moments before Maria lost consciousness. The next morning the girl filed a complaint, but the police told her that her testimony was not credible because she had been drinking.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           To make students think about the spread of the phenomenon, the Oxford workshops almost never used the word rape, but emphasise the absence of consent.
          </strong>
          The class leaders describe invented but plausible situations and students are asked to decide whether or not consent was given. One of the examples used is as follows: a student has been seeing her boyfriend for two years and says that they have had consensual sex, including oral sex and masturbation. One night he suggests trying anal sex. She feels uncomfortable with the idea. He justifies himself saying: We had a lot of sex before so I just carried on. It was great, it felt great. The students were asked whether or not there was consent. The right answer here is no, the consent must be continuous, not occasional. Consenting to one act doesnt mean saying yes to all successive acts.
         </p>
