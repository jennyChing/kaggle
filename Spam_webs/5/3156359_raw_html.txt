          MIT Project Mimics Iconic Portrait Photogs, Takes Your Selfies to the Next Level
         </a>
        </h2>
        <div class="postmetadata addthis_native_toolbox visible-xs">
         <ul>
          <li class="author">
           Jun 01, 2014 
           <a href="http://petapixel.com/author/gannonburgett/">
            Gannon Burgett

          <li class="fblikebtn">

          <li class="tweetbtn">
           <a class="twitter-share-button" data-related="petapixel" data-text="MIT Project Mimics Iconic Portrait Photogs, Takes Your Selfies to the Next Level" data-url="http://petapixel.com/2014/06/01/researchers-turn-average-smartphone-portraits-stylized-pieces-art/" href="https://twitter.com/share">

          <li class="hidden-xs">

        <div class="entry">
         <p>
          <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" height="349" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/ixCxK75jl9M?rel=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;iv_load_policy=3&amp;wmode=transparent frameborder=" width="620">
          </iframe>
         </p>
         <p>
          Are you not impressed with your average Instagram selfie? Is the lighting too bland and out of place for your liking? If so, a team made up of a researcher from MIT and a few individuals from Adobe and the University of Virginia might just have a solution to your problem.
         </p>
         <p>
          Theyve created an algorithm capable of accurately stylizing an average, otherwise insignificant selfie to look like the works of some of the best-known and well-respected portrait photographers of all time.
          <span id="more-137108">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="MITPhotoReplication" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-137109" height="288" src="http://petapixel.com/assets/uploads/2014/06/MITPhotoReplication-620x288.png" width="620"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          As explained by MIT grad
          <a href="http://newsoffice.mit.edu/2014/spruce-your-selfie" target="_blank" title="">
           YiChang Shih
          </a>
          , the program uses the works of Richard Avedon, Diane Arbus and Martin Schoeller (just to name a few) to inspire alterations that are then applied to the image being analyzed.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once the standard snapshot is put through the software, the algorithm goes to work making sure eyes, skin, beards and lighting are as perfectly matched to an image in the archive as possible, stylizing the image accordingly. And although we dont know exactly how the algorithm calculates and replicates the look of the images in their archive, its safe to say that it does an impressive (albeit a bit strange in some cases) job.
         </p>
         <p>
          As to what these algorithms might turn into, Adobes
          <a href="https://twitter.com/robert_bailey" target="_blank" title="">
           Robert Bailey
          </a>
          told
          <a href="http://techcrunch.com/2014/05/30/mit-researchers-create-an-app-that-turns-selfies-into-works-of-art/" target="_blank" title="">
           TechCrunch
          </a>
          that theyre, looking at creating a consumer application utilizing the technology. So watch out for this sort of capability in future iPhone and Android apps.
         </p>
         <p>
          (via
          <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2014/05/31/mit-selfie-portrait-project/?ncid=rss_truncated" target="_blank" title="">
           Engadget
          </a>
          )
         </p>
