           <h1>
            Could a New Discovered Manuscript Rewrite Australian History?
           </h1>
           <!-- Post content-->
           <!--Ad Injection:top-->
           <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
           </script>
           <!-- 2015 Leaderboard -->
           <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-3483836950374598" data-ad-slot="4009382067" style="display:inline-block;width:728px;height:90px">
           </ins>
           <script>
            (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
           </script>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            For a long time there has been a theory that the Portuguese were the first to set foot upon Australian soil, long before the Dutch or the English. Found within the letters of a 16
            <sup>
             th
            </sup>
            century Portuguese manuscript is a tiny drawing of a kangaroo curled up.
            <span id="more-7445">
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.littlegatepublishing.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/kangaroo.jpg">
             <img alt="kangaroo" class=" wp-image-7447 size-full aligncenter" height="349" src="http://www.littlegatepublishing.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/kangaroo.jpg" width="620"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            The document, acquired by Les Enluminures Gallery in New York, depicts a sketch of an apparent kangaroo (canguru in Portuguese) nestled in its text and is dated between 1580 and 1620. This has led researchers to believe that images of the marsupial were already being circulated by the time the Dutch ship Duyfken landed upon the Australian coast in 1606.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            It is known that Portugal was extremely secretive about her trade routes during this period, which explains why their presence there wasnt widely known.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            The processional (pocket-sized manuscript), contains text and music for a liturgical procession and is inscribed with the name Caterina de Carvalho, believed to be a nun from Caldas da Rainha in western Portugal.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            If proved correct the manuscript could precede what is believed to be the first European docking in Australia. Which could cause waves as the European discovery of Australia has officially been credited to the Dutch voyage headed by Willen Janszoon in 1606, but historians have suggested the country may already have been explored by other western Europeans.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            <b>
             A kangaroo or a wallaby in a manuscript dated this early is proof that the artist of this manuscript had either been in Australia, or even more interestingly, that travellers reports and drawings of the interesting animals found in this new world were already available in Portugal,
            </b>
             Les Enluminures researcher Laura Light said.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            <b>
             Portugal was extremely secretive about her trade routes during this period, explaining why their presence there wasnt widely known.
            </b>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            Peter Trickett, an award-winning historian and author of Beyond Capricorn, has been a long standing advocate for the theory that a Portuguese maritime expedition mapped the coast of Australia in 1521-22, nearly a century before the Dutch landing.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            <b>
             It is not surprising at all that an image of a kangaroo would have turned up in Portugal at some point in the latter part of the 16th century. It could be that someone in the Portuguese exhibition had this manuscript in their possession,
            </b>
            he said.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            The National Library of Australia curator of maps Martin Woods said that while the image looked like a kangaroo or a wallaby, it alone was not proof enough to alter Australias history books.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            The likeness of the animal to a kangaroo or wallaby is clear enough, but then it could be another animal in south-east Asia, like any number of deer species, some of which stand on their hind legs to feed off high branches, Dr Woods said.
           </p>
           <p>
            Additionally what has not been taken into account is that ancient texts have been defaced by scholars in the past with the sole intention of causing trouble.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             For now, unfortunately the appearance of a long-eared big-footed animal in a manuscript doesnt really add much.
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            The value of the manuscript has been set at $US15,000 ($16,000) by Les Enluminures Gallery, who acquired the processional from a rare book dealer in Portugal and will exhibit the piece as part of an exhibition.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            <a href="http://www.littlegatepublishing.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/kangaroo1.jpg">
             <img alt="kangaroo1" class="aligncenter wp-image-7449 size-full" height="349" src="http://www.littlegatepublishing.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/kangaroo1.jpg" width="620"/>
            </a>
            The kangaroo is not alone, in other letters of text two male figures in tribal dress with bare naked torsos and crowns of leaves can be seen and Ms. Light said they could very well be Aborigines.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            John Gascoigne, Fellow of the Australian Academy of the Humanities, said that proving this theory that the Portuguese were the first Europeans to arrive in Australia would be forever difficult to document because of their secrecy and because so many of the records were destroyed in the Lisbon earthquake of 1755.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            The possible date span for the manuscript goes up to 1620, which would accommodate the arrival of Willen Janszoon in the Duyfken in northern Australia in 1606, Professor Gascoigne said.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            He speculated the images could come from a 1526 trip to Papua.
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: justify;">
            Looking at it from a European perspective, it is surely evocative to wonder what these exotic images must have meant to the Portuguese nun gazing at them from within the confines of her convents walls, Ms Light said.
           </p>
