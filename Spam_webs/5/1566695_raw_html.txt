     <p>
      <b>
       Phobos
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       AncientGreek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      ,
      <small>
       pronounced
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Greek" title="Help:IPA for Greek">
        [pbos]
       </a>
      </span>
      , meaning "fear") is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">
       personification
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Fear" title="Fear">
       fear
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
       Greek mythology
      </a>
      . He is the offspring of
      <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">
       Aphrodite
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">
       Ares
      </a>
      . He was known for accompanying Ares into battle along with the ancient war goddess
      <a href="/wiki/Enyo" title="Enyo">
       Enyo
      </a>
      , the goddess of discord
      <a href="/wiki/Eris_(mythology)" title="Eris (mythology)">
       Eris
      </a>
      (both sisters of Ares), and Phobos' twin brother
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deimos_(mythology)" title="Deimos (mythology)">
       Deimos
      </a>
      (terror).
     </p>
     <p>
      In Classical Greek mythology, Phobos is more of a personification of the fear brought by war and does not appear as a character in any myths.
      <b>
       Timor
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Timorus
      </b>
      is his
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">
       Roman
      </a>
      equivalent. In Roman mythology, he has also been referred to as
      <b>
       Pavor
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Phobos is associated with Greek deity
      <a href="/wiki/Pan_(god)" title="Pan (god)">
       Pan
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Worship">
       Worship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Worship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Those who worshipped Phobos often made bloody sacrifices in his name. In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Seven_Against_Thebes" title="Seven Against Thebes">
        Seven Against Thebes
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">
       Aeschylus
      </a>
      , the seven warriors slaughter a bull over a black shield and then "touching the bulls gore with their hands they swore an oath by Phobos who delights in blood"(Atsma). Aress son, Kyknos, "beheaded strangers who came along in order to build a temple to Phobos (fear) from the skulls." (Atsma).
     </p>
     <p>
      Warriors and heroes who worshipped Phobos, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">
       Heracles
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Agamemnon" title="Agamemnon">
       Agamemnon
      </a>
      , carried shields with depictions of Phobos on them.
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of Aphrodite being their mother, Phobos and Deimos were gods of the fear of loss.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Depictions">
       Depictions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Depictions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Hesiod depicts Phobos on the shield of Heracles as "staring backwards with eyes that glowed with fire. His mouth was full of teeth in a white row, fearful and daunting" (Atsma) and again later during a war scene as being "eager to plunge amidst the fighting men," (Atsma).
     </p>
     <p>
      Deimos and Phobos were represented as young boys. Phobos often is depicted as having a lions or lion-like head. This may be seen in
      <i>
       Description of Greece
      </i>
      by Pausanias, "On the shield of Agamemnon is Phobos (Fear), who[se] head is a lions" (Atsma).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_reference">
       Historical reference
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical reference">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
       Plutarch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
       Alexander the Great
      </a>
      offered sacrifices to Phobos on the eve of the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gaugamela" title="Battle of Gaugamela">
       Battle of Gaugamela
      </a>
      (in all probability asking for Darius to be filled with fear). This was believed by
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Renault" title="Mary Renault">
       Mary Renault
      </a>
      to be part of Alexanders psychological warfare campaign against
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_III_of_Persia" title="Darius III of Persia">
       Darius III
      </a>
      . Darius fled from the field of Gaugamela, making Alexanders praying to Phobos seem successful as a tactic.
     </p>
     <p>
      Phobos was depicted on the chest of Cypselus on the shield of Agamemnon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      American
      <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
       astronomer
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Asaph_Hall" title="Asaph Hall">
       Asaph Hall
      </a>
      named a satellite of the planet
      <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
       Mars
      </a>
      , "
      <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
       Phobos
      </a>
      ", which he discovered along with the second Mars satellite, "
      <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
       Deimos
      </a>
      ", in 1877. Phobos is the larger of the two satellites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology">
       Psychology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Psychology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word "
      <a href="/wiki/Phobia" title="Phobia">
       phobia
      </a>
      " derives from
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phobos" title="wikt:phobos">
        phobos
       </a>
      </i>
      , (
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%A6%CF%8C%CE%B2%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      ), meaning
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fear" title="wikt:fear">
       fear
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Deimos_and_Phobos_in_Sparta">
       Deimos and Phobos in Sparta
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Deimos and Phobos in Sparta">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The two brothers Deimos and Phobos were particularly worshiped in the city state of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">
        Sparta
       </a>
      </i>
      as they were the sons of Ares, the god of war. Phobos in Greek literally means
      <i>
       fear
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       AncientGreek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      ). Spartan soldiers would idolize Phobos, because he symbolized discipline, and consistency of the armed forces. Because Phobos and Deimos were key mythological characters of war, individuals would conduct sacrifices to their name on battlefields, and other places were war had taken place.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
       Literature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Literature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are many places within the Iliad, where Homer mentions the presence of Phobos and Deimos. Some are:
     </p>
     <p>
      Homer, Iliad 11. 36 ff: "[The shield of Agamemnon:] And he took up the man-enclosing elaborate stark shield, a thing of splendour. There were ten circles of bronze upon it, and set about it were twenty knobs of tin, pale-shining, and in the very centre another knob of dark cobalt. And circled in the midst of all was the blank-eyed face of the Gorgo (Gorgon) with her stare of horror, and Deimos (Fear) was inscribed upon it, and Phobos (Terror).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Homer Iliad 15. 119 ff: "So he [Ares] spoke, and ordered Deimos (Terror) and Phobos (Fear) to harness his horses, and himself got into his shining armour."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      "Phobos and his brother
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deimos_(mythology)" title="Deimos (mythology)">
       Deimos
      </a>
      are the main antagonists in
      <a href="/wiki/Rick_Riordan" title="Rick Riordan">
       Rick Riordan's
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">
       short story
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percy_Jackson:_The_Demigod_Files" title="Percy Jackson: The Demigod Files">
        Percy Jackson and the Stolen Chariot
       </a>
      </i>
      . Phobos is shown as individual fear, while his brother focuses on crowds.
     </p>
     <p>
      Phobos and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Deimos(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Deimos(mythology) (page does not exist)">
       Deimos
      </a>
      also appear in
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Rising" title="Red Rising">
       Red Rising
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Pierce_Brown" title="Pierce Brown">
       Pierce Brown
      </a>
      as the names of two towers in the north of the Institute, between House Mars and House Ceres
     </p>
