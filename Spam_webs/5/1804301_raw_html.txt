    <h1 class="article_title">
     Sex and drugs: unusual boost for GDP
    </h1>
    <div class="article_content" id="article_content">
     <div class="drop_shadow" id="article_single_attachment">
      <img alt="" id="attachment_349018" src="https://3a09223b3cd53870eeaa-7f75e5eb51943043279413a54aaa858a.ssl.cf3.rackcdn.com/cb49dda4246c7e26264f67e214cac1743256886586-1404379545-53b52199-620x348.jpg"/>
     </div>
     <div class="default_top_margin" id="article_body">
      <p>
       The National Statistics Office is to include the estimated revenue from narcotics and prostitution in its GDP reports as from September  but with its eyes wide open with regard to the problems with collecting data about illegal activity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Getting an estimate of undeclared revenue is never easy but I can assure you that we have to gather the data in the same professional way that we gather all data, director general Michael Pace Ross told The Business Observer.
      </p>
      <p>
       UN international standards require that estimates of the non-reported economy are included in the calculation of GDP, he explained. Mr Pace Ross said that the NSO tapped into the experience of a number of entities to compile the data, ranging from police and government agencies like Sedqa and Appo, to hotels, nightclubs and councils.
      </p>
