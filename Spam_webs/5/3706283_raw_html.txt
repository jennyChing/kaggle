         <h2 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/10/09/tenure-track-assistant-professor-digital-history-george-mason-university/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Tenure Track Assistant Professor, Digital History @ George Mason University">
           Tenure Track Assistant Professor, Digital History @ George Mason University
          </a>
         </h2>
         <p class="meta-line">
          <a class="meta-label meta-date" href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/10/09/" rel="bookmark" title="8:17 am">
           <i class="icon-left icon-calendar">
           </i>
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-10-09T08:17:31+00:00">
            October 9, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="meta-label meta-publisher vcard">
           Published by:
           <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/author/sharonmleon/" rel="author" title="Posts by Sharon Leon">
            Sharon Leon
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="row">
         <div class="columns sixteen">
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            The George Mason University, Department of History and Art History, home of the
            <a href="http://chnm.gmu.edu/" title="Roy Rosenzweig Center for History and New Media">
             Roy Rosenzweig Center for History and New Media
            </a>
            , invites applications for a tenure-track position in Digital History at the rank of Assistant Professor. The teaching load is 2-2.
           </p>
           <h3>
            Qualifications:
           </h3>
           <p>
            While the historical field is open, candidates must have the ability to teach digital theory and methods at the undergraduate and graduate levels including a graduate course in programming (e.g., PHP, Python, Perl, JavaScript, XML). Ph.D. must be in-hand by August 2014.
           </p>
           <p>
            George Mason University is a public research university located approximately 14 miles from Washington, D.C., with over 30,000 students. The Department of History and Art History has a strong record of scholarly research and is home to the award-winning Roy Rosenzweig Center for History and New Media. The department also has the largest M.A. program in the country and a nationally ranked Ph.D. program.
            <br/>
            Special Instructions to Applicants
           </p>
           <p>
            For full consideration, applicants must apply for position number
            <strong>
             F5343z
            </strong>
            at
            <a href="http://jobs.gmu.edu/" title="GMU Jobs">
             http://jobs.gmu.edu/
            </a>
            ; complete and submit the online application; and upload a letter of interest, CV, and a writing sample and/or a link to a digital project. Letters of reference should be sent separately to Professor Paula Petrik, Chair, Digital History Search, Department of History and Art History, George Mason University, MSN 3G1, 4400 University Drive, Fairfax, VA 22030. Review of applications will begin on November 15, 2013, and continue until the position is filled.
           </p>

         <h2 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/08/20/tenure-track-assistant-professor-of-digital-humanities-iupui/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Tenure Track Assistant Professor of History (Digital Humanities) (IUPUI)">
           Tenure Track Assistant Professor of History (Digital Humanities) (IUPUI)
          </a>
         </h2>
         <p class="meta-line">
          <a class="meta-label meta-date" href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/08/20/" rel="bookmark" title="7:43 am">
           <i class="icon-left icon-calendar">
           </i>
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-08-20T07:43:55+00:00">
            August 20, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="meta-label meta-publisher vcard">
           Published by:
           <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/author/sharonmleon/" rel="author" title="Posts by Sharon Leon">
            Sharon Leon
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="row">
         <div class="columns sixteen">
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            IN-LART13013
            <br/>
            Indiana University-Purdue University
            <br/>
            Indianapolis (IUPUI)
            <br/>
            IU School of Liberal Arts
            <br/>
            Department of History
            <br/>
            Start date: August 1, 2014
            <br/>
            Title: Assistant Professor of History (Digital Humanities)
           </p>
           <p>
            The Department of History at Indiana University-Purdue University Indianapolis (IUPUI) seeks a tenure-track assistant or associate professor in Digital Humanities, to begin August 1, 2014. We are looking for a scholar with a strong academic background in historical research. Period and area of specialization are open. Ph.D. required by August 1, 2014.
           </p>
           <p>
            The successful candidates research should integrate the theoretical perspectives and digital tools of the evolving field of digital humanities and demonstrate the potential to produce significant scholarship. While technical skills, such as programming or expertise in database design would be beneficial, we are looking for candidates who have a firm understanding of the critical application of digital tools, the desire to continuously develop their technical expertise, and the ability to collaborate with specialists across the disciplines in order to build digital humanities capacity on campus. The IUPUI campus as well as Indiana University offer significant research support opportunities for digital scholarship within and across disciplines.
           </p>
           <p>
            Teaching responsibilities include history survey courses and upper-level undergraduate and graduate courses in digital humanities and the candidates fields of expertise. We seek a candidate interested in working with graduate students, the majority of which are in our nationally recognized MA Program in Public History.
           </p>
           <p>
            IUPUI is one of the Midwests premier urban universities, with more than 30,000 students enrolled on a campus adjacent to downtown Indianapolis. The entire metropolitan area counts nearly 1.8 million residents and hosts museums, historical organizations, libraries, archives, and government agencies (many within walking distance of campus). The department and the public history program enjoy long-standing partnerships with many of these organizations. Our nationally-ranked programs reflect our institutions traditions in interdisciplinary cooperation, innovative thinking, and community partnerships. The successful candidate will join a department with a strong record of community engagement and public scholarship.
           </p>
           <p>
            Please send a letter of application, which includes evidence of digital scholarship, curriculum vitae, and three current letters of recommendation. Review of applications will begin on October 1, 2013, and will continue until the position is filled. Applications and letters of recommendation must be submitted electronically to the IUPUI Department of History, history@iupui.edu.
           </p>
           <p>
            IUPUI is an EEO/AA employer, M/F/D, and encourages applications from women and minority candidates. Individuals who require a reasonable accommodation in order to participate in the application process must provide sufficient advanced notice to Ms. Amy Schramm, 425 University Blvd., CA 504L, Indianapolis, IN 46202, (317) 274-5840.
           </p>
          </div>

         <h2 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/07/24/aha-statement-on-embargoing-dissertations/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to AHA Statement on Embargoing Dissertations">
           AHA Statement on Embargoing Dissertations
          </a>
         </h2>
         <p class="meta-line">
          <a class="meta-label meta-date" href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/07/24/" rel="bookmark" title="8:44 am">
           <i class="icon-left icon-calendar">
           </i>
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-07-24T08:44:29+00:00">
            July 24, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="meta-label meta-publisher vcard">
           Published by:
           <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/author/sharonmleon/" rel="author" title="Posts by Sharon Leon">
            Sharon Leon
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="row">
         <div class="columns sixteen">
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            Digital Historians, as many of you know, the American Historical Associations Council adopted a
            <a href="http://blog.historians.org/2013/07/american-historical-association-statement-on-policies-regarding-the-embargoing-of-completed-history-phd-dissertations/" title="AHA Statement on Embargoing Completed Dissertations">
             statement
            </a>
            at their July meeting encouraging the embargoing of dissertations for six years. This statement has generated a great deal of conversation on twitter and in the world of digital history about the changing conditions of scholarly communication and the kinds of leadership and direction the AHA should be taking on these matters:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.trevorowens.org/2013/07/notes-toward-a-bizarro-world-aha-dissertation-open-access-statement/" title="Trevor Owen's post">
              Trevor Owens post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://adamcrymble.blogspot.com/2013/07/students-should-be-empowered-not.html" title="Adam Crymble's post">
              Adam Crymbles post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.jasonmkelly.com/2013/07/23/american-historical-association-statement-on-phd-dissertations-an-initial-point-by-point-response/" title="Jason Kelly's post">
              Jason Kellys post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://stephenramsay.us/2013/07/23/the-american-shrugshouldercal-association/" title="Stephen Ramsey's Post">
              Stephen Ramsays post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://blogs.library.duke.edu/scholcomm/2013/07/24/etds-publishing-policy-based-on-fear/?utm_source=twitterfeed&amp;utm_medium=twitter" title="Kevin Smith's post">
              Kevin Smiths post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://quintessenceofham.org/2013/07/24/a-poorly-reasoned-suicide-note/" title="Sean Takats's post">
              Sean Takatss post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.6floors.org/bracket/2013/07/22/always-looking-backward-in-a-bad-way/" title="Sharon Leon's post">
              Sharon Leons post
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.insidehighered.com/news/2013/07/24/historians-association-faces-criticism-proposal-embargo-dissertations" title="Inside Higher Ed Article">
              Inside Higher Education article
             </a>
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            We can and should certainly continue this conversation here:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What are the implications for those of us working in digital history?
            </li>
            <li>
             What about our students who are working on digital dissertations?
            </li>
            <li>
             Shouldnt our focus be on the quality of the scholarship, not necessarily on the form of transmission?
            </li>
            <li>
             Has anyone had a bad experience with a publisher because too much material was already available?
            </li>
            <li>
             What about article publications?
            </li>
            <li>
             What should we be saying to tenure and promotion committees about their duty to evaluate a range of forms of scholarship?
            </li>
           </ul>
          </div>

          <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/06/12/help-build-the-wiki/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Help Build the Wiki">
           Help Build the Wiki
          </a>
         </h2>
         <p class="meta-line">
          <a class="meta-label meta-date" href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/06/12/" rel="bookmark" title="6:59 pm">
           <i class="icon-left icon-calendar">
           </i>
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-06-12T18:59:12+00:00">
            June 12, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="meta-label meta-publisher vcard">
           Published by:
           <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/author/footnotesrising/" rel="author" title="Posts by Susan Garfinkel">
            Susan Garfinkel
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="row">
         <div class="columns sixteen">
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            Hi all, I started a few pages in the Wiki section of this site, devoted to sharing links to Resources, broadly defined. I know thats an overly obvious thing for us to do, but it does seem like a good starting point, and a way for some of us to start getting involved with the site (and getting used to this
            <a href="http://commonsinabox.org/" target="_blank">
             Commons In a Box
            </a>
            software). Theres no need to reinvent existing wheels, however, so if there are other good portals or aggregators of useful digital-historical tools or content, please point to those too.
           </p>
           <p>
            For now, if you go to Wiki from the lefthand menu, youll see a listing of pages with their metadata. That will change to something more elegant at some point soon. In the meantime, click into the individual pages, or go to
            <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/wiki/digital-history-resources/">
             http://www.digitalhistorians.org/wiki/digital-history-resources/
            </a>
            which has links to the other Resource pages.
           </p>
           <p>
            Nothing is carved in stone here, so please improve the pages as you see fit. And by all means please start other pages! They can be children of the Resources page (if so, please add a link to the main Resources page), or you can dive in on some entirely different topic. Just keep in mind that each of the groups can also make its own wiki, so try to stick to site-wide topics of interest in the general one.
           </p>
           <p>
            Susan
           </p>
           <p>
            ps. As I learned the hard way, avoid using colons in your wiki page titles.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>

       <article class="post-19 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-meta" id="post-19">
        <header class="entry-header">

         <h2 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/06/08/four-flavors-of-digital-history/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Four Flavors of Digital History">
           Four Flavors of Digital History
          </a>
         </h2>
         <p class="meta-line">
          <a class="meta-label meta-date" href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/2013/06/08/" rel="bookmark" title="6:55 am">
           <i class="icon-left icon-calendar">
           </i>
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-06-08T06:55:26+00:00">
            June 8, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="meta-label meta-publisher vcard">
           Published by:
           <a href="http://www.digitalhistorians.org/author/tjowens/" rel="author" title="Posts by Trevor Owens">
            Trevor Owens
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="row">
         <div class="columns sixteen">
          <div class="entry-content">
           <p>
            Given out discussion at THATcamp I thought it might be useful to start this off with a run down of genres of digital history. When someone asks me what digital history is my answergenerallywill involve reference to these four distinct kinds of things.
           </p>
           <ol>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Computational analysis and interpretation of historical sources:
             </strong>
             Everything from Word Clouds to Topic Modeling. This includes both cliometrics and those who swear by the screwmeneutical imperative.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Collecting and/or preserving born-digital primary sources:
             </strong>
             From disk images of hardrives, to archived webpages. This includes but is not limited to photos on flickr, presidential emails, lolcats, the stuxnet virus, sensor-net data, the source code of ninja gaiden 2, the interface of instagram, yelp reviews of the Statue of Liberty &amp; an assortment of punch cards from the 1890s &amp; the plug board of an enigma machine.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Digital modes for presenting, organizing, and distributing historical interpretation:
             </strong>
             From building software to support historical research, These might include presenting historical games and simulations as publications or creating software for exhibiting cultural heritage collections on the web, creating digital exhibitions to communicate historical analysis.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Code:
             </strong>
             Digital History often requires code to scape web pages, clean texts, transform CSV files and perform various other sundry tasks, on both large and small scales. Have you written something youd like others to use? Or found some hidden python scripts at GitHub? Post them here!
            </li>
           </ol>
          </div>
