   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Pareto principle
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      For the optimal allocation of resources, see
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_efficiency" title="Pareto efficiency">
       Pareto efficiency
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Pareto principle
      </b>
      (also known as the
      <b>
       8020 rule
      </b>
      , the
      <b>
       law of the vital few,
      </b>
      and the
      <b>
       principle of factor sparsity
      </b>
      ) states that, for many events, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Management_consultant" title="Management consultant">
       Management consultant
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_M._Juran" title="Joseph M. Juran">
       Joseph M. Juran
      </a>
      suggested the principle and named it after Italian
      <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">
       economist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Vilfredo_Pareto" title="Vilfredo Pareto">
       Vilfredo Pareto
      </a>
      , who, while at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lausanne" title="University of Lausanne">
       University of Lausanne
      </a>
      in 1896, published his first paper "Cours d'conomie politique." Essentially, Pareto showed that approximately 80% of the land in Italy was owned by 20% of the population; Pareto developed the principle by observing that 20% of the pea pods in his garden contained 80% of the peas
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="Previous link was dead (November 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      It is a common
      <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_thumb" title="Rule of thumb">
       rule of thumb
      </a>
      in business; e.g., "80% of your sales come from 20% of your clients." Mathematically, the 8020 rule is roughly followed by a
      <a href="/wiki/Power_law" title="Power law">
       power law
      </a>
      distribution (also known as a
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_distribution" title="Pareto distribution">
       Pareto distribution
      </a>
      ) for a particular set of parameters, and many natural phenomena have been shown empirically to exhibit such a distribution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pareto principle is only tangentially related to
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_efficiency" title="Pareto efficiency">
       Pareto efficiency
      </a>
      . Pareto developed both concepts in the context of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distribution_of_income" title="Distribution of income">
       distribution of income
      </a>
      and wealth among the population.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_economics">
       In economics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareto_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In economics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The original observation was in connection with population and wealth. Pareto noticed that 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He then carried out surveys on a variety of other countries and found to his surprise that a similar distribution applied.
     </p>
     <p>
      A chart that gave the inequality a very visible and comprehensible form, the so-called 'champagne glass' effect,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was contained in the 1992 United Nations Development Program Report, which showed the distribution of global income to be very uneven, with the richest 20% of the world's population controlling 82.7% of the world's income.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The distribution is claimed to appear in several different aspects relevant to entrepreneurs and business managers. For example:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       80% of a company's profits come from 20% of its customers
      </li>
      <li>
       80% of a company's complaints come from 20% of its customers
      </li>
      <li>
       80% of a company's profits come from 20% of the time its staff spend
      </li>
      <li>
       80% of a company's sales come from 20% of its products
      </li>
      <li>
       80% of a company's sales are made by 20% of its sales staff
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Therefore, many businesses have an easy access to dramatic improvements in profitability by focusing on the most effective areas and eliminating, ignoring, automating, delegating or retraining the rest, as appropriate.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [

        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_applicability_to_science">

     <p>
      The more unified a theory is, the more predictions it makes, and the greater the chances is of some of them being cheaply testable. Modifications of existing theories makes much fewer new and unique predictions, increasing the risk of the few there is all being very expensive to test.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If the Pareto principle or any other kind of increased costs were the cause of stagnation in the unification of especially physics, the modification of existing theories would have been even more severely slowed down than ever the unification by breakthroughs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>

        <span>

        </span>

       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_software">
       In software
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareto_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In software">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
       computer science
      </a>
      and engineering control theory, such as for electromechanical energy converters, the Pareto principle can be applied to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimization_(computer_science)" title="Optimization (computer science)">
       optimization
      </a>
      efforts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">
       Microsoft
      </a>
      noted that by fixing the top 20% of the most-reported bugs, 80% of the related errors and crashes in a given system would be eliminated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Load_testing" title="Load testing">
       load testing
      </a>
      , it is common practice to estimate that 80% of the traffic occurs during 20% of the time.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">
       software engineering
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lowell_Arthur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lowell Arthur (page does not exist)">
       Lowell Arthur
      </a>
      expressed a corollary principle: "20 percent of the code has 80 percent of the errors. Find them, fix them!"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_health_and_safety">
       Occupational health and safety
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareto_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occupational health and safety">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Pareto principle is used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupational_health_and_safety" title="Occupational health and safety">
       occupational health and safety
      </a>
      to underline the importance of hazard prioritization. Assuming 20% of the hazards will account for 80% of the injuries and by categorizing hazards, safety professionals can target those 20% of the hazards that cause 80% of the injuries or accidents. Alternatively, if hazards are addressed in random order, then a safety professional is more likely to fix one of the 80% of hazards which account for some fraction of the remaining 20% of injuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Aside from ensuring efficient accident prevention practices, the Pareto principle also ensures hazards are addressed in an economical order as the technique ensures the resources used are best used to prevent the most accidents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCG001_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-USCG001-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">
       Other applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareto_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the systems science discipline,
      <a href="/wiki/Joshua_M._Epstein" title="Joshua M. Epstein">
       Epstein
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Axtell" title="Robert Axtell">
       Axtell
      </a>
      created an
      <a href="/wiki/Agent-based_social_simulation" title="Agent-based social simulation">
       agent-based simulation
      </a>
      model called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SugarScape" title="SugarScape">
       SugarScape
      </a>
      , from a
      <a href="/wiki/Decentralised_system" title="Decentralised system">
       decentralized modeling
      </a>
      approach, based on individual behavior rules defined for each agent in the economy. Wealth distribution and Pareto's 80/20 principle became emergent in their results, which suggests the principle is a natural phenomenon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pareto principle has many applications in quality control.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      It is the basis for the
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_chart" title="Pareto chart">
       Pareto chart
      </a>
      , one of the key tools used in
      <a href="/wiki/Total_quality_management" title="Total quality management">
       total quality control
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_sigma" title="Six sigma">
       six sigma
      </a>
      . The Pareto principle serves as a baseline for
      <a href="/wiki/Time_management#abc_analysis" title="Time management">
       ABC-analysis
      </a>
      and XYZ-analysis, widely used in
      <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">
       logistics
      </a>
      and procurement for the purpose of optimizing stock of goods, as well as costs of keeping and replenishing that stock.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pareto principle was also mentioned in the book
      <i>
       24/8 - The Secret for being Mega-Effective by Achieving More in Less Time
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Amit_Offir" title="Amit Offir">
       Amit Offir
      </a>
      . Offir claims that if you want to function as a one-stop shop, simply focus on the 20% of what is important in a project and that way you will save a lot of time and energy.
     </p>
     <p>
      In health care in the United States, 20% of patients have been found to use 80% of health care resources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Several criminology studies have found 80% of crimes are committed by 20% of criminals.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This statistic is used to support both
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stop-and-frisk" title="Stop-and-frisk">
       stop-and-frisk
      </a>
      policies and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_windows" title="Broken windows">
       broken windows
      </a>
      policing, as catching those criminals committing minor crimes will likely net many criminals wanted for (or who would normally commit) larger ones.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the financial services industry, this concept is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Profit_risk" title="Profit risk">
       profit risk
      </a>
      , where 20% or fewer of a company's customers are generating positive income, while 80% or more are costing the company money.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_notes">
       Mathematical notes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pareto_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mathematical notes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The idea has rule of thumb application in many places, but it is commonly misused. For example, it is a misuse to state a solution to a problem "fits the 8020 rule" just because it fits 80% of the cases; it must also be that the solution requires only 20% of the resources that would be needed to solve all cases. Additionally, it is a misuse of the 8020 rule to interpret data with a small number of categories or observations.
     </p>
     <p>
      This is a special case of the wider phenomenon of
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_distribution" title="Pareto distribution">
       Pareto distributions
      </a>
      . If the
      <a href="/wiki/Pareto_index" title="Pareto index">
       Pareto index
      </a>
      <b>
       
      </b>
      , which is one of the parameters characterizing a Pareto distribution, is chosen as
      <b>
       
      </b>
      =log
      <sub>
       4
      </sub>
      51.16, then one has 80% of effects coming from 20% of causes.
     </p>
     <p>
      It follows that one also has 80% of that top 80% of effects coming from 20% of that top 20% of causes, and so on. Eighty percent of 80% is 64%; 20% of 20% is 4%, so this implies a "64-4" law; and similarly implies a "51.2-0.8" law. Similarly for the bottom 80% of causes and bottom 20% of effects, the bottom 80% of the bottom 80% only cause 20% of the remaining 20%. This is broadly in line with the world population/wealth table above, where the bottom 60% of the people own 5.5% of the wealth.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 64-4 correlation also implies a 32% 'fair' area between the 4% and 64%, where the lower 80% of the top 20% (16%) and upper 20% of the bottom 80% (also 16%) relates to the corresponding lower top and upper bottom of effects (32%). This is also broadly in line with the world population table above, where the second 20% control 12% of the wealth, and the bottom of the top 20% (presumably) control 16% of the wealth.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term 8020 is only a shorthand for the general principle at work. In individual cases, the distribution could just as well be, say, 8010 or 8030. There is no need for the two numbers to add up to the number 100, as they are measures of different things, e.g., 'number of customers' vs 'amount spent'). However, each case in which they do not add up to 100%, is equivalent to one in which they do; for example, as noted above, the "64-4 law" (in which the two numbers do not add up to 100%) is equivalent to the "8020 law" (in which they do add up to 100%). Thus, specifying two percentages independently does not lead to a broader class of distributions than what one gets by specifying the larger one and letting the smaller one be its complement relative to 100%. Thus, there is only one degree of freedom in the choice of that parameter.
     </p>
     <p>
      Adding up to 100 leads to a nice symmetry. For example, if 80% of effects come from the top 20% of sources, then the remaining 20% of effects come from the lower 80% of sources. This is called the "joint ratio", and can be used to measure the degree of imbalance: a joint ratio of 96:4 is very imbalanced, 80:20 is significantly imbalanced (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gini_index" title="Gini index">
       Gini index
      </a>
      : 60%), 70:30 is moderately imbalanced (Gini index: 40%), and 55:45 is just slightly imbalanced.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pareto principle is an illustration of a "
      <a href="/wiki/Power_law" title="Power law">
       power law
      </a>
      " relationship, which also occurs in phenomena such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brush_fire" title="Brush fire">
       brush fires
      </a>
      and earthquakes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because it is self-similar over a wide range of magnitudes, it produces outcomes completely different from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaussian_distribution" title="Gaussian distribution">
       Gaussian distribution
      </a>
      phenomena. This fact explains the frequent breakdowns of sophisticated financial instruments, which are modeled on the assumption that a Gaussian relationship is appropriate to, for example, stock price movements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
