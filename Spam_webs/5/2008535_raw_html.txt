        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/science-software-testing.jpg">
          <img alt="science-software-testing" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-13601" height="391" src="http://blog.smartbear.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/science-software-testing.jpg" title="Software testing, science or philosophy?" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive dabbled in many roles in software  tester, product manager, project manager, program manager, customer service director, and just plain management overhead. But testing has always been a draw for me and is something I often still dabble in after-hours on personal projects.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ill be completely honest  I stumbled into software testing. I started testing software back in the 1980s while working
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/database/the-air-forces-1-billion-software-flop-part-1/" target="_blank" title="The Air Forces $1 Billion Software Flop [Part 1]">
          as a civilian for the Air Force
         </a>
         . In that project, we didnt have a bug tracking system or, for that matter, any kind of testing methodologies. As the sole tester in an office that was completely computer illiterate and with the development team housed in a separate building, I went by gut and jotted down any issues I found on scraps of printer paper. I would then either call the developers or walk over to see them, and we would talk through my notes. Sometimes they would ghost my terminal and watch me recreate the problem. Now, this sounds both primitive and modern at the same time, doesnt it?
        </p>
        <p>
         The reality is that self-taught testers are the norm. While there are certainly
         <a href="http://blog.smartbear.com/testing/why-not-train-testers-early-with-software-testing-degrees/" target="_blank" title="Why Not Train Testers Early with Software Testing Degrees?">
          students of the craft
         </a>
         (and where there are students, of course there are teachers), many testers today learned by sitting down at a desk and going by instinct. Sure, there is usually a mentor or a manager who can walk you through the job expectations  creating test plans, logging a defect, regression testing  but the industry itself is not crawling with devotees of the science of testing. Not to say that those people dont exist  they do and they are a force to be reckoned with.
        </p>
        <p>
         So how and when was it that software testing began to be organized and standardized? Its hard to say, though some people, like Matt Heusser, have
         <a href="http://www.softwaretestpro.com/Item/4537/History-of-Ideas-in-Software-Testing/Agile-Performance-Automation-Metrics-Development-Acceptance-Exploratory-Functional-Integration-Process-Software-Strategy-Testing-Teams-Unit-Six-Sigma-selenium-Web" target="_blank" title="History of Ideas in Software Testing">
          tried to document the history of testing
         </a>
         in some fashion. Perhaps Joseph
         <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/13881008" target="_blank" title="Joseph Juran">
          Jurans Trilogy of the 1950s
         </a>
          quality planning, quality control and quality improvement  are the concepts that have survived the longest, although the approach to them is ever-evolving.
        </p>
        <p>
         In reality, not much has changed about the overall goals of testing since I started out in the discipline nearly three decades ago. The concepts are generally the same:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Understand what is being built
         </li>
         <li>
          Plan all the flavors and paths of tests that need to be done
         </li>
         <li>
          Document the test, your expectations and your observations
         </li>
         <li>
          Communicate to the developers
         </li>
         <li>
          Re-test bug fixes
         </li>
         <li>
          Perform regression tests against functionality that might also have been affected
         </li>
         <li>
          Lather, rinse, repeat
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Whether you accomplish those tasks using automation, manual testing, or exploratory testing well, conceptually, its all the same. The method by which you accomplish those tasks is the mechanics and those mechanics change over time, depending on the systems/team/project you have available.
        </p>
        <p>
         Im more interested these days in the science/philosophy behind those activities. How do you plumb the depths of the product you are testing? How do you define your plans and communicate them effectively, both to yourself and to the team you are supporting? What are the best techniques for accomplishing any of these and how do I know which to employ?
        </p>
        <p>
         Luckily for todays testers, there are scientists/philosophers who are devoting their minds and time to exploring all of these concepts and how we can accomplish them better. In writing this, I tried to decide between the terms science and philosophy, but I found that I couldnt  much of the current testing conversation falls somewhere in between. If I had to draw a line, I would draw it at test design vs. test strategy  there is science in the design, and theres philosophy in the strategy. To me, as a self-taught veteran of the craft, I am most intrigued by the strategic discussions. For those of you not following along with todays testing pundits, here are some nuggets to get you thinking:
        </p>
        <h4>
         Context-Driven Testing
        </h4>
        <p>
         This
         <a href="http://context-driven-testing.com/" target="_blank" title="Context Driven Testing">
          school of thought
         </a>
         eschews the idea of best practices because there is no way to identify a practice that will work across all projects at all times. To identify the right success criteria and the right testing methodology, you need to understand the context of the testing in light of the project and its end goals.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Bias in Testing
        </h4>
        <p>
         There are
         <a href="http://www.workroom-productions.com/papers/The%20Irrational%20Tester%20v1-06.pdf" target="_blank" title="The Irrational Tester">
          several different types of bias
         </a>
         that can alter the effectiveness of software testing and its important to understand them in order to develop strategies to circumvent them. There is good biological sense in being wired for bias, but having that mindset color our test behavior (and hence the results) can make for inaccuracies that should be avoided. Recognizing that we all approach our day-to-day activities with some form of bias means we can proactively counteract them.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Oracles and Heuristics
        </h4>
        <p>
         How do you know if your test passed or failed? What is it about the test result that signaled one or the other to you, and what happened between the start of the test and the result that can influence that decision? The
         <a href="http://kaner.com/?p=190" target="_blank" title="The Oracle Problem and the Teaching of Software Testing">
          concept of oracles and heuristics
         </a>
         is decidedly complex and nuanced, and probably not something anyone can explain in a short paragraph. At least I cant. Because it is a subtle but important concept, it is still being debated in software testing circles.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Galumphing
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         This is my personal favorite and is a
         <a href="http://vimeo.com/64762883" target="_blank" title="TestBash 2.0 - A-Galumphing We Go James Bach">
          new testing term coined by James Marcus Bach
         </a>
         to describe a technique of testing in which the tester inserts variations into the test that rationally shouldnt affect the outcome of the test. I include it in the list because it speaks to an approach to software testing that many people have historically discounted because it cant be described easily in test plans or in a defect (why were you randomly clicking fields before hitting OK? uh, I dont know  I was galumphing).
        </p>
        <p>
         Of course, all of these are broader topics than Ive represented here and are worth diving into if you are a software tester or work with testers. Its refreshing to see how deeply people think about the software testing discipline, although it also seems a natural outcome of something that has become so complex in recent years. Software testers often have to juggle multiple versions in multiple environments on multiple browsers and across many devicesin some ways, being asked to make sense of it is both difficult and impossible. After all, searching for bugs is elusive  you dont know where they are, how many there are, or even if they exist at all (well, maybe that part is a given). I guess thats why so much of the conversation sounds more like philosophy than science.
        </p>
