      <div class="spl_headline">
       <h1 class="headline" itemprop="name">
        How Millennials Can Get Out of Debt &amp; Save on a Skimpy Salary
       </h1>
       <a class="featured_image_link" href="http://www.personalincome.org/get-out-of-debt-while-saving/">
        <img alt="helping millennials get out of debt" class="aligncenter wp-post-image" height="375" src="http://personalincomeorg.c.presscdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/millennials-get-out-of-debt-692x375.png" width="692"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="byline">
       <meta content="2014-12-03" itemprop="datePublished"/>
       <meta content="2014-12-03" itemprop="dateModified"/>
       <span class="post_date" title="2014-12-03">
        December 3, 2014
       </span>
       <span class="post_author" itemprop="author">
        Stephanie Christensen
       </span>
       <span class="twitter_profile">
        Follow me on Twitter
        <a href="https://twitter.com/WellnessOnLess">
         here
        </a>
        .
       </span>
      </div>
      <div class="post_content" itemprop="articleBody">
       <a id="dd_start">
       </a>
       <p>
        If youre a millennial (the demographic term used to categorize people born between 1980 and late 1990s), its likely that youre no stranger to student loan debt, high credit card balances, and potentially, less than stellar credit.
       </p>
       <p>
        The odds are also fairly high that you struggle to stretch your paycheck to the degree needed to build savings, let alone,
        <a href="http://www.personalincome.org/how-to-save-for-retirement-in-your-20s/">
         contribute to your retirement
        </a>
        account.
       </p>
       <p>
        Despite the degree of financial strain you feel as a result,
        <a href="http://www.personalincome.org/how-to-pay-off-debt/">
         resolving your debt
        </a>
        and building the savings you need to keep from falling back into it
        <em>
         is
        </em>
        possible, and necessary to moving forward in your life. Best of all, it will cost you nothingother than your effort and determination.
       </p>
       <div class="code-block code-block-2" style="text-align:center;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto;margin-top:8px;margin-bottom:8px;">
        <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">

       <p>
        I know firsthand that living with a debt is not a good place to be, and even less easy to exit. But I did. And so can you. Here are the most impactful changes that helped me take control of my financial life and why they can help you get out of debt and saveregardless of how much you currently owe, or make.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Face the numbers
       </h2>
       <p>
        You dont want to know how much debt youre in, and you certainly dont want to know what it costs. But the hard numbers are the catalyst to taking control of your money.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step #1.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Compile a list of your creditors, minimum monthly payment due, total balance, and the interest rate for each loan. Prioritize the list from high to low by interest rate. The loan at the top of the list costs you the most; focus your energy on it. (Youll continue paying the minimums on all the other loans).
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step #2.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        The next time you pay your bills, dedicate at least twice the minimum amount that is due on the first loan on your list (or twice what youve been paying if youve been more aggressive). Once its paid off, move on to the next loan and do the same.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step #3.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        When you get to the third loan, continue to double the minimum payment due (or what youve been paying), AND put half of the amount you pay to your creditor into savings. Deposit it in a no fee, no minimum required, interest bearing online savings account (where you can still find deposit rates of about 1%). If the minimum payment is $50, for example, youll pay $100 to the creditor and contribute $50 to your savings.
       </p>
       <div class="code-block code-block-4" style="text-align:center;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto;margin-top:8px;margin-bottom:8px;">
        <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
        </script>

        </ins>

        </script>
       </div>
       <h2>
        Borrow when it makes sense
       </h2>
       <p>
        Just as making retirement contributions early in your career will grow far more significantly than money you invest once youre about 35 years old, interest bearing debt you carry for months costs far less than balances you carry for years.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         If you have good credit, consider balance transfer offers that allow you to pay 0% on debt (noting when that temporary rate will expire). Though I paid a nominal fee for my transfer transactions and had to open new credit cards for the offer, it was preferable to paying down a high interest rate card slowly.
        </li>
        <li>
         Explore non-traditional lending options (not consolidation programs) that connect private lenders and borrowers, assuming the rate, terms and any applicable fees are lower than what you currently pay. There are many reputable peer to peer lending platforms that offer such opportunities, particularly if you have good credit.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h2>
        Realize the value of your time
       </h2>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Consider what you pay someone else to do that you could do yourself (like manicures, personal training, and house cleaning)
        </li>
        <li>
         Calculate the cost of how spend your time (including paying for cable, music, transportation, entertainment, and activities), and where making concessions can maximize your time.
        </li>
        <li>
         Monetize your free time by focusing on the return on investment, not the job itself. I walked dogs after work to make an additional $250 a month, which I put directly towards my credit card debt. I didnt always love the job, but my focus was the end game.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h2>
        Change your perspective about savings
       </h2>
       <p>
        <em>
         <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/11/why-arent-millennials-saving-money/382634/" target="_blank">
          The Atlantic
         </a>
        </em>
        recently published a fairly a comical explanation of why millennials dont save: They dont like banks. When you dont have money to spare, saving is a luxury you cant afford. But that logic is inaccurate, and risky.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Saving is not about preparing for what
         <em>
          might
         </em>
         happen. Its a hedge against the potential financial damage youll incur when the unexpected
         <em>
          does
         </em>
         happen and you cant pay for it.
        </li>
        <li>
         Put the effort into saving something, anything, every weekmeaningless as the amount may seem. Consider that a person who saves $3 a day for 90 days will have saved $270 for an emergency. That may not seem like much, but when the flat tire/cavity/high winter heating bill/rent increase happens, you wont be thrown into a panic. Thats priceless.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <h2>
        Conclusion
       </h2>
       <p>
        In order to eliminate debt you need to create a plan and have a process down an order execute that plan. Yes, it will take some work, dedication, and persistence, but you can do it! Just follow my tips above and you will be well on your way to conquering debt and taking back your freedom.
       </p>
