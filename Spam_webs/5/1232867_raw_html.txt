           <h2>
            Want to get the biggest camera bang for your buck? Check out these 8 incredible cameras that wont break the bank.
           </h2>
           <p>
            There are a lot of low-cost hobbies out there  and filmmaking aint one of them. Between camera gear, audio equipment, crew members, editing stations, and distribution there is a constant battle to
            <strong>
             stretch your dollar in any way possible.
            </strong>
            Unfortunately, a common misconception is that buying a fancy filmmaking camera will make your footage look great. This is simply
            <strong>
             not true.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            Artistic concepts like composition, leadingvlines, and color theory play a much bigger role in the look and feel of your film than simply
            <strong>
             camera choice
            </strong>
            . This is great news for filmmakers because it means we can allocate more of our budget to other areas of the production. So if youre looking to capture amazing footage without spending a ton of money, check out the following eight cameras.
           </p>
           <h2>
            1. Blackmagic Pocket Camera
           </h2>
           <p>
            <img alt="Filmmaking Cameras: Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera" class="alignnone wp-image-54537 size-full" height="350" src="http://pbblogassets.s3.amazonaws.com/uploads/2015/04/Blackmagic-Pocket-Camera.png" title="Filmmaking Cameras: Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera" width="1000"/>
           </p>
           <p>
            When the
            <a href="https://www.blackmagicdesign.com/products/blackmagicpocketcinemacamera" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.blackmagicdesign.com/products/blackmagicpocketcinemacamera', 'Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera']);" target="_blank" title="Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera">
             Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera
            </a>
            was released just a few years ago, it was a
            <strong>
             game changer for the indie filmmaking community.
            </strong>
            To this day, the Pocket Camera has an image quality thats unmatched at its price point. Most notably, the BMPCC has 13 stops of dynamic range with the ability to record RAW in-camera.
           </p>
           <p>
            The cameras small design also allows it to be easily stored. However, the biggest issue with shooting on a Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera is the crop factor. Without a focal reducer,
            <strong>
             the BMPCC has a crop factor of 2.88.
            </strong>
            This means a 24mm lens will actually act like a 69mm lens on a full-frame camera. So, getting wide angle shots on a BMPCC is a bit of a challenge.
           </p>
           <h3>
            <strong>
             Price:
            </strong>
            <a href="http://www.bhphotovideo.com/c/product/964117-REG/blackmagic_design_blackmagic_pocket_cinema_camera.html" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.bhphotovideo.com/c/product/964117-REG/blackmagic_design_blackmagic_pocket_cinema_camera.html', '$995 on B&amp;H']);" target="_blank" title="Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera">
             $995 on B&amp;H
            </a>
           </h3>
           <p>
            Pros:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             RAW Recording + ProRes 422 HQ
            </li>
            <li>
             13 Stops of Dynamic Range
            </li>
            <li>
             HDMI Output
            </li>
            <li>
             Uses SD Cards
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Cons:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Crop Factor
            </li>
            <li>
             Battery Life
            </li>
           </ul>

            <strong>
             Blackmagic Pocket Cinema Camera Test Footage

                <p>
                 Sony NEX-7 has worked out great for me. Compact because it is mirrorless, APS-C sensor so similar crop factor to the Canons. Not as big a lens selection without adapters, but Ive found it a very usable shooter for action and fight scenes. 1080P up to 60fps, H.264 recording. Technically a little long in the tooth technology-wise. Lacks manual audio control but the automatic adjustment is intelligent enough to be quite serviceable though you should be getting external audio fed in and/or captured to a dedicated device for best quality. Great stills and solid video, though. The newer Sony Alpha A7S is a full frame sensor with incredible low light sensitivity designed from the ground up for videographers/filmmakers, but is $2,500 and still has some limitations. Both are worth looking at. Another thing to consider is how common the Canons seem to be in use among indie and other productions. If you get familiar using them it will probably help you when you work with others with similar cameras. Best advice? Find a camera you like and will use a lot, regardless of brand and price.
