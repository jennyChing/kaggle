   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    White noise
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Signal_processing" title="Signal processing">
       signal processing
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       white noise
      </b>
      is a random
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_(information_theory)" title="Signal (information theory)">
       signal
      </a>
      with a constant
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_spectral_density" title="Power spectral density">
       power spectral density
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term is used, with this or similar meanings, in many scientific and technical disciplines, including
      <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
       physics
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustic_engineering" title="Acoustic engineering">
       acoustic engineering
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">
       telecommunications
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_forecasting" title="Statistical forecasting">
       statistical forecasting
      </a>
      , and many more. White noise refers to a statistical model for signals and signal sources, rather than to any specific signal.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:White-noise-mv255-240x180.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="240" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/White-noise-mv255-240x180.png/220px-White-noise-mv255-240x180.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/White-noise-mv255-240x180.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/White-noise-mv255-240x180.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:White-noise-mv255-240x180.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A "white noise" image.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discrete_time" title="Discrete time">
       discrete time
      </a>
      , white noise is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discrete_signal" title="Discrete signal">
       discrete signal
      </a>
      whose
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_(signal)" title="Sample (signal)">
       samples
      </a>
      are regarded as a sequence of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_correlation" title="Serial correlation">
       serially uncorrelated
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">
       random variables
      </a>
      with zero
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_(statistics)" title="Mean (statistics)">
       mean
      </a>
      and finite
      <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">
       variance
      </a>
      ; a single realization of white noise is a
      <b>
       random shock
      </b>
      . Depending on the context, one may also require that the samples be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_independence" title="Statistical independence">
       independent
      </a>
      and have the same
      <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">
       probability distribution
      </a>
      (in other words
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I.i.d" title="I.i.d">
       i.i.d
      </a>
      is a simplest representative of the white noise). In particular, if each sample has a
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">
       normal distribution
      </a>
      with zero mean, the signal is said to be Gaussian white noise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The samples of a white noise signal may be sequential in time, or arranged along one or more spatial dimensions. In
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_image_processing" title="Digital image processing">
       digital image processing
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">
       pixels
      </a>
      of a
      <i>
       white noise image
      </i>
      are typically arranged in a rectangular grid, and are assumed to be independent random variables with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_uniform_distribution" title="Continuous uniform distribution">
       uniform probability distribution
      </a>
      over some interval. The concept can be defined also for signals spread over more complicated domains, such as a sphere or a
      <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">
       torus
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      An infinite-bandwidth white noise signal is a purely theoretical construction. The bandwidth of white noise is limited in practice by the mechanism of noise generation, by the transmission medium and by finite observation capabilities. Thus, a random signal is considered "white noise" if it is observed to have a flat spectrum over the range of frequencies that is relevant to the context. For an
      <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal" title="Audio signal">
       audio signal
      </a>
      , for example, the relevant range is the band of audible sound frequencies, between 20 to 20,000
      <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">
       Hz
      </a>
      . Such a signal is heard as a hissing sound, resembling the /sh/ sound in "ash". In
      <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">
       music
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">
       acoustics
      </a>
      , the term "white noise" may be used for any signal that has a similar hissing sound.
     </p>
     <p>
      White noise draws its name from
      <a href="/wiki/White#Optics" title="White">
       white light
      </a>
      , although light that appears white generally does not have a flat spectral power density over the visible band.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term white noise is sometimes used in the context of
      <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_comparative_methods" title="Phylogenetic comparative methods">
       phylogenetically based statistical methods
      </a>
      to refer to a lack of phylogenetic pattern in comparative data.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is sometimes used in non technical contexts, in the metaphoric sense of "random talk without meaningful contents".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shipman_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-shipman-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_properties">
       Statistical properties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statistical properties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noise.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="735" data-file-width="1059" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Noise.jpg/240px-Noise.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Noise.jpg/360px-Noise.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Noise.jpg/480px-Noise.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noise.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrogram" title="Spectrogram">
         Spectrogram
        </a>
        of
        <a href="/wiki/Pink_noise" title="Pink noise">
         pink noise
        </a>
        (left) and white noise (right), shown with linear frequency axis (vertical).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Being uncorrelated in time does not restrict the values a signal can take. Any distribution of values is possible (although it must have zero
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DC_component" title="DC component">
       DC component
      </a>
      ). Even a binary signal which can only take on the values 1 or -1 will be white if the sequence is statistically uncorrelated. Noise having a continuous distribution, such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">
       normal distribution
      </a>
      , can of course be white.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is often incorrectly assumed that
      <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_noise" title="Gaussian noise">
       Gaussian noise
      </a>
      (i.e., noise with a Gaussian amplitude distribution  see
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">
       normal distribution
      </a>
      ) necessarily refers to white noise, yet neither property implies the other. Gaussianity refers to the probability distribution with respect to the value, in this context the probability of the signal falling within any particular range of amplitudes, while the term 'white' refers to the way the signal power is distributed (i.e., independently) over time or among frequencies.
     </p>
     <p>
      We can therefore find Gaussian white noise, but also
      <a href="/wiki/Poisson_distribution" title="Poisson distribution">
       Poisson
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cauchy_distribution" title="Cauchy distribution">
       Cauchy
      </a>
      , etc. white noises. Thus, the two words "Gaussian" and "white" are often both specified in mathematical models of systems. Gaussian white noise is a good approximation of many real-world situations and generates mathematically tractable models. These models are used so frequently that the term
      <a href="/wiki/Additive_white_Gaussian_noise" title="Additive white Gaussian noise">
       additive white Gaussian noise
      </a>
      has a standard abbreviation:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AWGN" title="AWGN">
       AWGN
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      White noise is the generalized mean-square derivative of the
      <a href="/wiki/Wiener_process" title="Wiener process">
       Wiener process
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">
       Brownian motion
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A generalization to
      <a href="/wiki/Random_element" title="Random element">
       random elements
      </a>
      on infinite dimensional spaces, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Random_field" title="Random field">
       random fields
      </a>
      , is the
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_space" title="Nuclear space">
       white noise measure
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_applications">
       Practical applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
       Music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      White noise is commonly used in the production of
      <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">
       electronic music
      </a>
      , usually either directly or as an input for a filter to create other types of noise signal. It is used extensively in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Audio_synthesis" title="Audio synthesis">
       audio synthesis
      </a>
      , typically to recreate percussive instruments such as
      <a href="/wiki/Cymbal" title="Cymbal">
       cymbals
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">
       snare drums
      </a>
      which have high noise content in their frequency domain.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics_engineering">
       Electronics engineering
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electronics engineering">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      White noise is also used to obtain the
      <a href="/wiki/Impulse_response" title="Impulse response">
       impulse response
      </a>
      of an electrical circuit, in particular of
      <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">
       amplifiers
      </a>
      and other audio equipment. It is not used for testing loudspeakers as its spectrum contains too great an amount of high frequency content.
      <a href="/wiki/Pink_noise" title="Pink noise">
       Pink noise
      </a>
      , which differs from white noise in that it has equal energy in each octave, is used for testing transducers such as loudspeakers and microphones.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics">
       Acoustics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acoustics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      To set up the
      <a href="/wiki/Equalization" title="Equalization">
       equalization
      </a>
      for a concert or other performance in a venue, a short burst of white or
      <a href="/wiki/Pink_noise" title="Pink noise">
       pink noise
      </a>
      is sent through the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PA_system" title="PA system">
       PA system
      </a>
      and monitored from various points in the venue so that the engineer can tell if the acoustics of the building naturally boost or cut any frequencies. The engineer can then adjust the overall equalization to ensure a balanced mix.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Computing">
       Computing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Computing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      White noise is used as the basis of some
      <a href="/wiki/Hardware_random_number_generator" title="Hardware random number generator">
       random number generators
      </a>
      . For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Random.org" title="Random.org">
       Random.org
      </a>
      uses a system of atmospheric antennae to generate random digit patterns from white noise.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tinnitus_treatment">
       Tinnitus treatment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tinnitus treatment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      White noise is a common synthetic noise source used for sound masking by a
      <a href="/wiki/Tinnitus_masker" title="Tinnitus masker">
       tinnitus masker
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/White_noise_machine" title="White noise machine">
       White noise machines
      </a>
      and other white noise sources are sold as privacy enhancers and sleep aids and to mask
      <a href="/wiki/Tinnitus" title="Tinnitus">
       tinnitus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Alternatively, the use of an FM radio tuned to unused frequencies ("static") is a simpler and more cost-effective source of white noise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, white noise generated from a common commercial radio receiver tuned to an unused frequency is extremely vulnerable to being contaminated with spurious signals, such as adjacent radio stations, harmonics from non-adjacent radio stations, electrical equipment in the vicinity of the receiving antenna causing interference, or even atmospheric events such as solar flares and especially lightning.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Work_environment">
       Work environment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The effects of white noise upon cognitive function are mixed. Recently, a small study found that white noise background stimulation improves cognitive functioning among secondary students with
      <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">
       attention deficit hyperactivity disorder
      </a>
      (ADHD), while decreasing performance of non-ADHD students.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other work indicates it is effective in improving the mood and performance of workers by masking background office noise,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but decreases cognitive performance in complex card sorting tasks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_definitions">
       Mathematical definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mathematical definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="White_noise_vector">
       White noise vector
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: White noise vector">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_vector" title="Random vector">
       random vector
      </a>
      (that is, a partially indeterminate process that produces vectors of real numbers) is said to be a
      <b>
       white noise vector
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       white random vector
      </b>
      if its components each have a
      <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">
       probability distribution
      </a>
      with zero mean and finite
      <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">
       variance
      </a>
      , and are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_independence" title="Statistical independence">
       statistically independent
      </a>
      : that is, their joint probability distribution must be the product of the distributions of the individual components.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fessler_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fessler-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A necessary (but,
      <a href="/wiki/Normally_distributed_and_uncorrelated_does_not_imply_independent" title="Normally distributed and uncorrelated does not imply independent">
       in general, not sufficient
      </a>
      ) condition for statistical independence of two variables is that they be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">
       statistically uncorrelated
      </a>
      ; that is, their
      <a href="/wiki/Covariance" title="Covariance">
       covariance
      </a>
      is zero. Therefore, the
      <a href="/wiki/Covariance_matrix" title="Covariance matrix">
       covariance matrix
      </a>
      <i>
       R
      </i>
      of the components of a white noise vector
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      with
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      elements must be an
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      by
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Diagonal_matrix" title="Diagonal matrix">
       diagonal matrix
      </a>
      , where each diagonal element
      <i>
       R
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        ii
       </i>
      </sub>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Variance" title="Variance">
       variance
      </a>
      of component
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        i
       </i>
      </sub>
      ; and the
      <a href="/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence#Correlation_matrices" title="Correlation and dependence">
       correlation
      </a>
      matrix must be the
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      by
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      identity matrix.
     </p>
     <p>
      In particular, if in addition to being independent every variable in
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      also has a
      <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">
       normal distribution
      </a>
      with zero mean and the same variance
      <img alt="\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/6/3c6a93fc922fbfa19aea78961760cefb.png"/>
      ,
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      is said to be a
      <b>
       Gaussian
      </b>
      white noise vector. In that case, the joint distribution of
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Multivariate_normal_distribution" title="Multivariate normal distribution">
       multivariate normal distribution
      </a>
      ; the independence between the variables then implies that the distribution has
      <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_distribution" title="Elliptical distribution">
       spherical symmetry
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      -dimensional space. Therefore, any
      <a href="/wiki/Orthogonal_transformation" title="Orthogonal transformation">
       orthogonal transformation
      </a>
      of the vector will result in a Gaussian white random vector. In particular, under most types of
      <a href="/wiki/Discrete_Fourier_transform" title="Discrete Fourier transform">
       discrete Fourier transform
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">
       FFT
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Discrete_Hartley_transform" title="Discrete Hartley transform">
       Hartley
      </a>
      , the transform
      <i>
       W
      </i>
      of
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      will be a Gaussian white noise vector, too; that is, the
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      Fourier coefficients of
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      will be independent Gaussian variables with zero mean and the same variance
      <img alt="\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/6/3c6a93fc922fbfa19aea78961760cefb.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_spectrum" title="Power spectrum">
       power spectrum
      </a>
      <i>
       P
      </i>
      of a random vector
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      can be defined as the expected value of the squared modulus of each coefficient of its Fourier transform
      <i>
       W
      </i>
      , that is,
      <i>
       P
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        i
       </i>
      </sub>
      = E(|
      <i>
       W
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        i
       </i>
      </sub>
      |
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ). Under that definition, a Gaussian white noise vector will have a perfectly flat power spectrum, with
      <i>
       P
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        i
       </i>
      </sub>
      =
      <img alt="\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/6/3c6a93fc922fbfa19aea78961760cefb.png"/>
      for all
      <i>
       i
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      If
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      is a white random vector, but not a Gaussian one, its Fourier coefficients
      <i>
       W
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        i
       </i>
      </sub>
      will not be completely independent of each other; although for large
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      and common probability distributions the dependencies are very subtle, and their pairwise correlations can be assumed to be zero.
     </p>
     <p>
      Often the weaker condition "statistically uncorrelated" is used in the definition of white noise, instead of "statistically independent". However some of the commonly expected properties of white noise (such as flat power spectrum) may not hold for this weaker version. Under this assumption, the stricter version can be referred to explicitly as
      <b>
       independent white noise vector
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ezivot_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ezivot-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :p.60
      </sup>
      Other authors use
      <b>
       strongly white
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       weakly white
      </b>
      instead.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diebold_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-diebold-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An example of a random vector that is "Gaussian white noise" in the weak but not in the strong sense is
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      =[
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      ,
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ] where
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      is a normal random variable with zero mean, and
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      is equal to +
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      or to 
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      , with equal probability. These two variables are uncorrelated and individually normally distributed, but they are not jointly normally distributed and are not independent. If
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      is rotated by 45 degrees, its two components will still be uncorrelated, but their distribution will no longer be normal.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some situations one may relax the definition by allowing each component of a white random vector
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      to have non-zero expected value
      <img alt="\mu" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/7/2/b72bb92668acc30b4474caff40274044.png"/>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Image_processing" title="Image processing">
       image processing
      </a>
      especially, where samples are typically restricted to positive values, one often takes
      <img alt="\mu" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/7/2/b72bb92668acc30b4474caff40274044.png"/>
      to be one half of the maximum sample value. In that case, the Fourier coefficient
      <i>
       W
      </i>
      <sub>
       0
      </sub>
      corresponding to the zero-frequency component (essentially, the average of the
      <i>
       w
      </i>
      _i) will also have a non-zero expected value
      <img alt="\mu\sqrt{n}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/d/bedac2d04bf060a88a90e97a0271f28d.png"/>
      ; and the power spectrum
      <i>
       P
      </i>
      will be flat only over the non-zero frequencies.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Continuous-time_white_noise">
       Continuous-time white noise
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Continuous-time white noise">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In order to define the notion of "white noise" in the theory of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous-time" title="Continuous-time">
       continuous-time
      </a>
      signals, one must replace the concept of a "random vector" by a continuous-time random signal; that is, a random process that generates a function
      <img alt="w" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/2/f1290186a5d0b1ceab27f4e77c0c5d68.png"/>
      of a real-valued parameter
      <img alt="t" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Such a process is said to be
      <b>
       white noise
      </b>
      in the strongest sense if the value
      <img alt="w(t)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/b/41ba91c8c0ed4326e708a7aeb91a700d.png"/>
      for any time
      <img alt="t" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png"/>
      is a random variable that is statistically independent of its entire history before
      <img alt="t" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png"/>
      . A weaker definition requires independence only between the values
      <img alt="w(t_1)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/5/9/c59c70b25a0177aabdbe643caa4e4350.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="w(t_2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/0/370c166845196dfca5ea0f8a496c5ea7.png"/>
      at every pair of distinct times
      <img alt="t_1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/3/6/7363212f8e5a9d7ace25c6991a33c6fe.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="t_2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/7/4/974cb705b0c744f1855953c1086337df.png"/>
      . An even weaker definition requires only that such pairs
      <img alt="w(t_1)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/5/9/c59c70b25a0177aabdbe643caa4e4350.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="w(t_2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/0/370c166845196dfca5ea0f8a496c5ea7.png"/>
      be uncorrelated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econterms_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-econterms-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As in the discrete case, some authors adopt the weaker definition for "white noise", and use the qualifier
      <b>
       independent
      </b>
      to refer to either of the stronger definitions. Others use
      <b>
       weakly white
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       strongly white
      </b>
      to distinguish between them.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, a precise definition of these concepts is not trivial, because some quantities that are finite sums in the finite discrete case must be replaced by integrals that may not converge. Indeed, the set of all possible instances of a signal
      <img alt="w" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/2/f1290186a5d0b1ceab27f4e77c0c5d68.png"/>
      is no longer a finite-dimensional space
      <img alt="\mathbb{R}^n" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/0/c/30c28f76ef7517dbd19df4d4c683dbe6.png"/>
      , but an infinite-dimensional
      <a href="/wiki/Function_space" title="Function space">
       function space
      </a>
      . Moreover, by any definition a white noise signal
      <img alt="w" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/2/f1290186a5d0b1ceab27f4e77c0c5d68.png"/>
      would have to be essentially discontinuous at every point; therefore even the simplest operations on
      <img alt="w" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/2/f1290186a5d0b1ceab27f4e77c0c5d68.png"/>
      , like integration over a finite interval, require advanced mathematical machinery.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some authors require each value
      <img alt="w(t)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/b/41ba91c8c0ed4326e708a7aeb91a700d.png"/>
      to be a real-valued random variable with some finite variance
      <img alt="\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/6/3c6a93fc922fbfa19aea78961760cefb.png"/>
      . Then the covariance
      <img alt="\mathrm{E}(w(t_1)\cdot w(t_2))" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/1/37163079746b25b73437b92fd3bfe920.png"/>
      between the values at two times
      <img alt="t_1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/3/6/7363212f8e5a9d7ace25c6991a33c6fe.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="t_2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/7/4/974cb705b0c744f1855953c1086337df.png"/>
      is well-defined: it is zero if the times are distinct, and
      <img alt="\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/c/6/3c6a93fc922fbfa19aea78961760cefb.png"/>
      if they are equal. However, by this definition, the integral
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="W_{[a,a+r]} = \int_a^{a+r} w(t)\, dt" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/f/e/3fe5957af18ab6f0f6a7767f5dd9bd23.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      over any interval with positive width
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      would be zero. This property would render the concept inadequate as a model of physical "white noise" signals.
     </p>
     <p>
      Therefore, most authors define the signal
      <img alt="w" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/2/f1290186a5d0b1ceab27f4e77c0c5d68.png"/>
      indirectly by specifying non-zero values for the integrals of
      <img alt="w(t)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/b/41ba91c8c0ed4326e708a7aeb91a700d.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="|w(t)|^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/6/8/c684c3173174ce9ff7fb78d8f8dd708a.png"/>
      over any interval
      <img alt="[a,a+r]" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/1/e/51e01dcff08b66dfc7038488a385363c.png"/>
      , as a function of its width
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      . In this approach, however, the value of
      <img alt="w(t)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/b/41ba91c8c0ed4326e708a7aeb91a700d.png"/>
      at an isolated time cannot be defined as a real-valued random variable. Also the covariance
      <img alt="\mathrm{E}(w(t_1)\cdot w(t_2))" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/1/37163079746b25b73437b92fd3bfe920.png"/>
      becomes infinite when
      <img alt="t_1=t_2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/8/2/3827a54fb3ab4f71a5da63f0d0b20d12.png"/>
      ; and the
      <a href="/wiki/Autocorrelation" title="Autocorrelation">
       autocorrelation
      </a>
      function
      <img alt="\mathrm{R}(t_1,t_2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/b/6/bb61afd81211618919e9cc241b0f1b17.png"/>
      must be defined as
      <img alt="N \delta(t_1-t_2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/9/7/b974963f8778ebefa3e117a2acbf9e15.png"/>
      , where
      <img alt="N" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/9/8d9c307cb7f3c4a32822a51922d1ceaa.png"/>
      is some real constant and
      <img alt="\delta" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/0/f10f03c9836c36537d2539196058bfa2.png"/>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Dirac_delta_function" title="Dirac delta function">
       Dirac's "function"
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In this approach, one usually specifies that the integral
      <img alt="W_I" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/1/3/b1382bd43aa1bb3d6b490cda15446f83.png"/>
      of
      <img alt="w(t)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/b/41ba91c8c0ed4326e708a7aeb91a700d.png"/>
      over an interval
      <img alt="I=[a,b]" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/a/9/aa922a30ce1173792a5bbaaeec5dd22c.png"/>
      is a real random variable with normal distribution, zero mean, and variance
      <img alt="(b-a)\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/5/7/0570fb6c7fc94342da228e719a059da1.png"/>
      ; and also that the covariance
      <img alt="\mathrm{E}(W_I\cdot W_J)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/1/1/e11630a623221d059214dc46089f4af1.png"/>
      of the integrals
      <img alt="W_I" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/1/3/b1382bd43aa1bb3d6b490cda15446f83.png"/>
      ,
      <img alt="W_J" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/f/8/af85da53ba80a8484ab6c4c6fda3925b.png"/>
      is
      <img alt="r\sigma^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/5/be52a8ff2f3a52832d000617fc16e386.png"/>
      , where
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      is the width of the intersection
      <img alt="I\cap J" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/3/9/839897b5fc0023f1a20c8ffc69e0365e.png"/>
      of the two intervals
      <img alt="I,J" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/1/e/01eea2c97b7016f8b1d32cec91e85538.png"/>
      . This model is called a
      <b>
       Gaussian white noise signal
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       process
      </b>
      ).
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Time_series_analysis_and_regression">
       Time series analysis and regression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Time series analysis and regression">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
       statistics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Econometrics" title="Econometrics">
       econometrics
      </a>
      one often assumes that an observed series of data values is the sum of a series of values generated by a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterministic" title="Deterministic">
       deterministic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Linear_model" title="Linear model">
       linear process
      </a>
      , depending on certain
      <a href="/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables" title="Dependent and independent variables">
       independent (explanatory) variables
      </a>
      , and on a series of random noise values. Then
      <a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">
       regression analysis
      </a>
      is used to infer the parameters of the model process from the observed data, e.g. by
      <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_least_squares" title="Ordinary least squares">
       ordinary least squares
      </a>
      , and to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypothesis_testing" title="Hypothesis testing">
       test the null hypothesis
      </a>
      that each of the parameters is zero against the alternative hypothesis that it is non-zero. Hypothesis testing typically assumes that the noise values are mutually uncorrelated with zero mean and the same Gaussian probability distribution  in other words, that the noise is white. If there is non-zero correlation between the noise values underlying different observations then the estimated model parameters are still
      <a href="/wiki/Bias_of_an_estimator" title="Bias of an estimator">
       unbiased
      </a>
      , but estimates of their uncertainties (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Confidence_interval" title="Confidence interval">
       confidence intervals
      </a>
      ) will be biased (not accurate on average). This is also true if the noise is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heteroskedasticity" title="Heteroskedasticity">
       heteroskedastic
      </a>
       that is, if it has different variances for different data points.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alternatively, in the subset of regression analysis known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_series_analysis" title="Time series analysis">
       time series analysis
      </a>
      there are often no explanatory variables other than the past values of the variable being modeled (the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dependent_variable" title="Dependent variable">
       dependent variable
      </a>
      ). In this case the noise process is often modeled as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moving_average_model" title="Moving average model">
       moving average
      </a>
      process, in which the current value of the dependent variable depends on current and past values of a sequential white noise process.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Random_vector_transformations">
       Random vector transformations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Random vector transformations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      These two ideas are crucial in applications such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Channel_estimation" title="Channel estimation">
       channel estimation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mixing_console#Channel_equalization" title="Mixing console">
       channel equalization
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">
       communications
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_reproduction" title="Sound reproduction">
       audio
      </a>
      . These concepts are also used in
      <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">
       data compression
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In particular, by a suitable linear transformation (a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Coloring_transformation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Coloring transformation (page does not exist)">
       coloring transformation
      </a>
      ), a white random vector can be used to produce a "non-white" random vector (that is, a list of random variables) whose elements have a prescribed
      <a href="/wiki/Covariance_matrix" title="Covariance matrix">
       covariance matrix
      </a>
      . Conversely, a random vector with known covariance matrix can be transformed into a white random vector by a suitable
      <a href="/wiki/Whitening_transformation" title="Whitening transformation">
       whitening transformation
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Generation">
       Generation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=White_noise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Generation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      White noise may be generated digitally with a
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">
       digital signal processor
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">
       microprocessor
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">
       microcontroller
      </a>
      . Generating white noise typically entails feeding an appropriate stream of random numbers to a
      <a href="/wiki/Digital-to-analog_converter" title="Digital-to-analog converter">
       digital-to-analog converter
      </a>
      . The quality of the white noise will depend on the quality of the algorithm used.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>
     </p>
