   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    <i>
     Don Juan
    </i>
    (Byron)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Don Juan
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <span class="Unicode" title="English pronunciation respelling">
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i>
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             JEW
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -n
        </i>
       </a>
      </span>
      ; see below) is a satiric poem
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hecimovich_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hecimovich-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">
       Lord Byron
      </a>
      , based on the
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Juan" title="Don Juan">
       legend of Don Juan
      </a>
      , which Byron reverses, portraying Juan not as a womaniser but as someone easily seduced by women. It is a variation on the
      <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">
       epic
      </a>
      form. Byron himself called it an "Epic Satire" (Don Juan, c. xiv, st. 99). Byron completed 16 cantos, leaving an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unfinished_work" title="Unfinished work">
       unfinished
      </a>
      17th canto before his death in 1824. Byron claimed he had no ideas in his mind as to what would happen in subsequent cantos as he wrote his work.
     </p>
     <p>
      When the first two cantos were published anonymously in 1819, the poem was criticised for its "immoral content", though it was also immensely popular.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Byron was a rapid as well as a voluminous writer. Nevertheless, the composition of his great poem,
      <i>
       Don Juan
      </i>
      , was coextensive with a major part of his poetical life. He began the first canto of
      <i>
       Don Juan
      </i>
      in late 1818, and he was still at work on a seventeenth canto in early 1823. The poem was issued in parts, with intervals of unequal duration. Interruptions in the composition and publication of
      <i>
       Don Juan
      </i>
      were due to the disapproval and discouragement of friends as well as the publisher's hesitation and procrastination. Canto I. was written in September 1818; Canto II. in DecemberJanuary, 18181819. Both were published on 15 July 1819. Cantos III. and IV. were written in the winter of 18191820; Canto V., after an interval of nine months, in OctoberNovember 1820, but the publication of Cantos III., IV., V. was delayed till 8 August 1821. In June 1822, Byron began to work at a sixth, and by the end of March 1823, he had completed a sixteenth canto. But the publication of these later cantos, which had been declined by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Murray_(1778-1843)" title="John Murray (1778-1843)">
       John Murray
      </a>
      , and was finally entrusted to
      <a href="/wiki/John_Hunt_(publisher)" title="John Hunt (publisher)">
       John Hunt
      </a>
      , was spread over a period of several months. Cantos VI., VII., VIII., with a Preface, were published on 15 July; Cantos IX., X., XI on 29 August; Cantos XII., XIII., XIV., on 17 December 1823; Cantos XV., XVI. on 26 March 1824.
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been said that the character of Donna Inez (Don Juan's mother) was a thinly veiled portrait of Byron's own wife,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annabella_Milbanke" title="Annabella Milbanke">
       Annabella Milbanke
      </a>
      (daughter of Sir Ralph Milbanke).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
       Synopsis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Argument">
       The Argument
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Argument">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The story, told in seventeen cantos, begins with the birth of Don Juan. As a young man he is precocious sexually, and has an affair with a friend of his mother. The husband finds out, and Don Juan is sent away to Cadiz. On the way, he is shipwrecked, survives, and meets the daughter of a pirate, whose men sell Don Juan as a slave. A young woman who is a member of a Sultans harem, sees that this slave is purchased. She disguises him as a girl and sneaks him into her chambers. Don Juan escapes, joins the Russian army, and rescues a Muslim girl named Leila. Don Juan meets Catherine the Great, who asks him to join her court. Don Juan becomes sick, is sent to England, where he finds someone to watch over the young girl, Leila. Next, a few adventures involving the artistocracy of Britain ensue. The poem ends with Canto XVII.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_I">
       Canto I
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canto I">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Don Juan lives in Seville with his father Jos and his mother Donna Inez. Donna Julia, 23 years old and married to Don Alfonso, begins to desire Don Juan when he is 16 years old. Despite her attempt to resist, Julia begins an affair with Juan. Julia falls in love with Juan. Don Alfonso, suspecting that his wife may be having an affair, bursts into their bedroom followed by a "posse concomitant" but they do not find anything suspicious upon first searching the room, for Juan was hiding in the bed. However, when Alfonso returns on his own, he comes across Juan's shoes and a fight ensues. Juan escapes, however. In order to avoid the rumors and bad reputation her son has brought upon himself, Inez sends him away to travel, in the hopes that he develops better morals, while Julia is sent to a nunnery.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_II">
       Canto II
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canto II">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Canto II describes how Juan goes on a voyage from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">
       Cadiz
      </a>
      with servants and his tutor Pedrillo. Juan is still in love with Julia, and after a period of seasickness, a storm sinks the ship. The crew climb into a long boat, but soon run out of food. The crew decide to draw lots in order to choose who will be eaten. Juan's tutor Pedrillo is chosen after Juan's dog has also been eaten. However, those that eat Pedrillo go mad and die. Juan is the sole survivor of the journey; he eventually makes it onto land at
      <a href="/wiki/Cyclades" title="Cyclades">
       Cyclades
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">
       Aegean
      </a>
      . Haide and her maid Zoe discover Juan and care for him in a cave by the beach. Haide and Juan fall in love despite the fact that neither can understand each other's language. Haide's father Lambro is a "fisherman" and pirate who makes money from capturing slaves.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_III">
       Canto III
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canto III">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Canto III is essentially a long digression from the main story in which Byron, in the style of an epic catalogue, describes Haide and Don Juan's celebrations. The islanders believe Haide's father, Lambro, has died, but he returns and witnesses these revels. Towards the end of the canto, Byron insults his contemporaries
      <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">
       William Wordsworth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">
       Robert Southey
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">
       Samuel Taylor Coleridge
      </a>
      . It is in this latter section that we find "The Isles of
      <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
       Greece
      </a>
      "  a section numbered differently from the rest of the canto with a different verse which explores Byron's views on Greece's status as a "slave" to the
      <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
       Ottoman Empire
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_IV">
       Canto IV
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Canto IV">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Haide and Juan wake to discover that Haide's father, Lambro, has returned. Lambro confronts Juan and attacks him with the aid of his pirate friends. Haide despairs at losing her lover and eventually dies of a broken heart with her unborn child still in her
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Womb" title="Womb">
       womb
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Juan is sent away on a ship and ends up at a slave market in
      <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
       Constantinople
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_V">
       Canto V
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Canto V">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan is in the slave market. He converses with an Englishman, telling of his lost love, whereas the more experienced John says he had to run away from his third wife. A black
      <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">
       eunuch
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Seraglio" title="Seraglio">
       seraglio
      </a>
      , Baba, buys Juan and John, and takes the infidels to the palace. He takes them to an inner chamber, where he insists that Don Juan dress as a woman and threatens him with castration if he resists. Finally, Juan is brought into an imperial hall to meet the sultana, Gulbeyaz, a 26-year-old beauty who is the sultan's fourth, last and favourite wife. Full of stubborn pride, he refuses to kiss her foot and finally compromises by kissing her hand. She had spotted Juan at the market and had asked Baba to secretly purchase him for her, despite the risk of discovery by the sultan. She wants Juan to "love" her, and throws herself on his breast. But he still has thoughts of Haide and spurns her advances, saying "The prisoned eagle will not pair, nor I/Serve a sultana's sensual phantasy." She is taken aback, enraged, and thinks of having him beheaded, but breaks out in tears instead. Before they can progress further in their relationship, Baba rushes in to announce that the Sultan is coming: "The sun himself has sent me like a ray/To hint that he is coming up this way." The sultan arrives, preceded by a parade of damsels, eunuchs, etc.. Looking around, he takes note of the attractive Christian woman (Juan), expressing regret that a mere Christian should be so pretty (Juan is a giaour, or non-Muslim). Byron comments on the necessity to secure the chastity of the women in these unhappy climes  that "wedlock and a padlock mean the same."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_VI">
       Canto VI
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Canto VI">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The sultan retires with Gulbeyaz. Juan, still dressed as a woman, is taken to the overcrowded seraglio. He is asked to share a couch with the young and lovely 17-year-old Dud. When asked what his name is, Don Juan calls himself Juanna. She is a "kind of sleepy Venus ... very fit to murder sleep... Her talents were of the more silent class... pensive..." She gives Juanna a chaste kiss and undresses. The chamber of odalisques is asleep at 3 AM. Dud suddenly screams, and awakens agitated, while Juanna still lies asleep and snoring. The women ask the cause of her scream, and she relates a suggestive dream of being in a wood like Dante, of dislodging a reluctant golden apple clinging tenaciously to its bough (which at last willingly falls), of almost biting into the forbidden fruit when a bee flies out from it and stings her to the heart. The matron of the seraglio decides to place Juanna with another odalisque, but Dud begs to keep her in her own bed, hiding her face in Juanna's breast. The poet is at a loss to explain why she screamed.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the morning, the sultana asks Baba to tell her how Don Juan passed the night. He tells of "her" stay in the seraglio, but carefully omits details about Dud and her dream. But the sultana is suspicious nevertheless, becomes enraged, and instructs Baba to have Dud and Juan killed in the usual manner (drowning). Baba pleads with her that killing Juan will not cure what ails her. The sultana summons Dud and Juan. Just before the canto ends, the narrator explains that the "Muse will take a little touch at warfare."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_VII">
       Canto VII
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Canto VII">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan and John Johnson have escaped with two women from the seraglio, and arrive during the siege of
      <a href="/wiki/Izmail" title="Izmail">
       Ismail
      </a>
      (historically 1790), a Turkish fort at the mouth of the Danube on the Black Sea.
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Suvorov" title="Alexander Suvorov">
       Field Marshal Suvaroff
      </a>
      , an officer in the Russian army, is preparing for an all-out final assault against the besieged fortress. The battle rages. He has been told to "take Ismail at whatever price" by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Potemkin" title="Prince Potemkin">
       Prince Potemkin
      </a>
      , the commander-in-chief of the Russian army. The Christian empress Catherine II is the Russian head-of-state. John Johnson appears to Suvaroff (with whom he has previously served in battle at Widdin) and introduces his friend Juanboth are ready to join the fight against the "pagan" Turks. Suvaroff is unhappy with the women the two men brought, but they state that they are the wives of other men, and that the women aided their escape. Suvaroff consents to the women staying.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_VIII">
       Canto VIII
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Canto VIII">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan and John join fearlessly and bravely in the savage assault on Ismail. They scale the walls of the town and charge into battle. The conquest of Ismail causes the slaughter of 40,000 Turks, among them women (a few of whom are raped) and children. Juan nobly rescues a ten-year-old Muslim girl from two murderous Cossacks intent on killing her, and immediately resolves to adopt her as his own child. A noble Tartar khan valiantly fights to the death beside his five sons, just as instructed by Mahomet, presumably to be rewarded with
      <a href="/wiki/Houri" title="Houri">
       houris
      </a>
      in heaven.
     </p>
     <p>
      Juan is a hero and is sent to
      <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">
       Saint Petersburg
      </a>
      , accompanied by the Muslim girl, whom he makes a vow to protect. Her name, Leila, is only revealed in Canto X.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_IX">
       Canto IX
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canto IX">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Dressed as a war hero in military uniform, Juan cuts a handsome figure in the court of
      <a href="/wiki/Catherine_the_Great" title="Catherine the Great">
       Catherine II
      </a>
      , who lusts after him. She is about 48 years old (historically, actually 61 or 62 years old) and "just now in juicy vigour". He becomes one of her favorites and is flattered by her interest as well as promoted for it. "Love is vanity,/Selfish in its beginning as its end,/Except where 'tis a mere insanity". Juan still lovingly cares for the Muslim girl he rescued.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_X">
       Canto X
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Canto X">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan falls ill because of the Russian cold and so is sent westward to more temperate England. His job ostensibly is that of a special envoy with the nebulous task of negotiating some treaty or other, but it is nothing more than a sinecure to justify the Empress Catherine in securing his health and loading him with money and expensive gifts.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XI">
       Canto XI
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canto XI">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan lands in England and eventually makes his way to London where he is found musing on the greatness of Britain as a defender of freedoms  until he is interrupted by a
      <a href="/wiki/Cockney" title="Cockney">
       Cockney
      </a>
      mugger, demanding money with menace. Juan shoots the man and, being of strong conscience, then regrets his haste and attempts to care for the dying mugger. However, his efforts fail, and after muttering some last words the mugger dies on the street.
     </p>
     <p>
      Later, Don Juan is received into the English court with the usual wonder and admiration at his looks, dress and mien although not without the jealousy of some of the older peers.
     </p>
     <p>
      In this Canto, Byron famously makes his comment on
      <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">
       John Keats
      </a>
      , "who was kill'd off by one critique".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XII">
       Canto XII
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canto XII">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Don Juan seeks out a suitable tutor and guardian for Leila, the orphan from the destroyed city of Ismail. He finds one in Lady Pinchbeck, a woman not unassailed by rumours on her chastity, but generally considered a good person and an admirable wit.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XIII">
       Canto XIII
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Canto XIII">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Lady Adeline Amundeville and her husband Lord Henry Amundeville host Juan and others. She is "the fair most fatal Juan ever met", the "queen bee, the glass of all that's fair,/Whose charms made all men speak and women dumb". Diplomatic relations often bring Juan ("the envoy of a secret Russian mission") and Lord Henry together, and he befriends Juan and makes him a frequent guest at their London mansion. The Amundevilles invite numerous distinguished guests for a party at their country estate. The landscape surrounding the estate, as well as the decor within their house is described. This is followed by a mock-catalogues of the ladies, the gentlemen and the activities that they participate in. Byron sees this whole party as English
      <i>
       ennui
      </i>
      . The canto ends with all of them retiring for the evening.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XIV">
       Canto XIV
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Canto XIV">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan acquits himself well on a fox hunt. He is attractive to the ladies, including the Duchess of Fitz-Fulke, who begins to flirt with him. Lady Adeline is jealous of the Duchess (who has had many amorous exploits), and resolves to protect the "inexperienced" Juan from her enticements. Juan and Adeline are both 21 years old. Lady Adeline has a vacant heart and has a cold but proper marriage. She is not in love with Juan, but the poet will only later divulge whether they have an affair (apparently not). The popular saying "truth is stranger than fiction" originates from this canto: "'Tis strange  but true; for truth is always strange; Stranger than fiction."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XV">
       Canto XV
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Canto XV">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Lady Adeline is at risk for losing her honour over Juan. Juan has a seductive manner because he never seems anxious to seduce. He neither brooks nor claims superiority. Adeline advises Juan to get married, but he acknowledges the women he is attracted to tend to be already married. Adeline tries to deduce a suitable match for Juan, but intentionally omits mention of the 16-year-old and enticing Aurora Raby, a Catholic. Juan is attracted to her  she is purer than the rest, and reminds him of his lost Haide. An elaborate dinner is described in detail. Juan is seated between Adeline and Aurora. Aurora has little to say initially, and thaws only a little during the dinner.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XVI">
       Canto XVI
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Canto XVI">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Juan is smitten with the beautiful Aurora, and thinks of her on retiring. At night, he walks into the hall, viewing the gallery of paintings. He hears footsteps, and sees a monk in cowl and beads. He wonders if it is a ghost, or just a dream. He does not see the monk's face, though he passes and repasses several times.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next morning in reaction to how pale Juan looks, Adeline turns pale herself, the Duchess of Fitz-Fulke looks at Juan hard, and Aurora surveys him "with a kind of calm surprise". Adeline wonders if he is ill, and Lord Henry guesses that he might have seen the "Black Friar" He relates the story of the "spirit of these walls" who used to be seen often but had not been seen of late. Lord Henry, himself, had seen the Black Friar on his honeymoon. Adeline offers to sing the story of the ghost, accompanying it on her harp. Aurora remains silent, but Lady Fitz-Fulke appears mischievous after the song. The narrator suggests that Adeline had sung this to laugh Juan out of his dismay. Juan's attempts to lift his spirits, as the house bustles in preparation for another feast. Before that, however, a pregnant country girl and other petitioners present themselves to Lord Henry in his capacity as Justice of the Peace.
     </p>
     <p>
      At the banquet, Juan is preoccupied with his thoughts again. When he glances at Aurora, he catches a smile on her cheek, but is uncertain of its meaning, since Aurora sits pale and only a little flushed. Adeline goes about her duties as hostess, while the Duchess of Fitz-Fulke is very much at ease.
     </p>
     <p>
      They retire for the evening. Juan thinks about Aurora, who has reawakened feelings in him which had lately been lost. After going back to his room, he hears the tiptoe of footsteps again, after sitting around in expectation of the ghost. His doors open, and again it is the sable Friar concealed in his solemn hood. He pursues the friar up against a wall, and then suddenly notices that the "ghost" has sweet breath, a straggling curl, red lips, pearls, and a glowing bust. As the hood falls down, the "friar" is revealed to be the voluptuous Duchess of Fitz-Fulke.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canto_XVII">
       Canto XVII
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Canto XVII">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A Canto that Byron failed to complete but added to in the run up to his death, it lacks any narrative and only barely mentions the protagonist. It is instead a response to his critics who object to his views on the grounds that "If you are right, then everybody's wrong!". In his defence, he lists many great people who have been considered outsiders and revolutionaries including Martin Luther and Galileo.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Canto ends on the brink of resuming the storyline from Canto The Sixteenth where Don Juan was left in a "tender moonlit situation".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">
       Sources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Byron's epic poem has a host of literary precedents. For example,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Hookham_Frere" title="John Hookham Frere">
       John Hookham Frere
      </a>
      's mock-heroic Arthurian tale
      <i>
       Prospectus and Specimen of an intended National Work
      </i>
      had suggested
      <a href="/wiki/Beppo_(poem)" title="Beppo (poem)">
       <i>
        Beppo
       </i>
      </a>
      , and, at the same time, had prompted and provoked a sympathetic study of Frere's Italian models,
      <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Berni" title="Francesco Berni">
       Francesco Berni
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Pulci" title="Luigi Pulci">
       Luigi Pulci
      </a>
      ; and, again, the success of
      <i>
       Beppo
      </i>
      , and, still more, a sense of inspiration and the conviction that he had found the path to excellence, suggested another essay of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ottava_rima" title="Ottava rima">
       ottava rima
      </a>
      , a humorous poem " la Beppo" on a larger and more important scale. If Byron possessed more than a superficial knowledge of the legendary "Don Juan", he was irresponsive and unimpressed. He speaks (letter to John Murray), of "the Spanish tradition"; but there is nothing to show that he had read or heard of
      <a href="/wiki/Tirso_de_Molina" title="Tirso de Molina">
       Tirso de Molina
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       El Burlador de Sevilla y el Convidado de Piedra
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       The Deceiver of Seville and the Stone Guest
      </i>
      ), 1626, which dramatised the "ower true tale" of the actual
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_Tenorio" title="Don Juan Tenorio">
       Don Juan Tenorio
      </a>
      ; or that he was acquainted with any of the Italian (e.g. the
      <i>
       Convitato di Pietra
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Giacinto_Andrea_Cicognini" title="Giacinto Andrea Cicognini">
       Giacinto Andrea Cicognini
      </a>
      or French adaptations of the legend (e.g.
      <i>
       Le Festin de Pierre, ou le fils criminel
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Tragicomedy" title="Tragicomedy">
       tragicomedy
      </a>
      of Abb De Villiers, 1659; and
      <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molire">
       Molire
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Dom Juan, ou Le Festin de Pierre
      </i>
      , 1665). He had seen
      <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Antonio_Delpini" title="Carlo Antonio Delpini">
       Carlo Antonio Delpini
      </a>
      's pantomime, which was based on
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Shadwell" title="Thomas Shadwell">
       Thomas Shadwell
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Libertine
      </i>
      , and he may have witnessed, at
      <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
       Milan
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">
       Venice
      </a>
      , a performance of
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">
       Mozart
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Giovanni" title="Don Giovanni">
       Don Giovanni
      </a>
      ; but in taking Don Juan for his "hero", he took the name only, and disregarded the "terrible figure" "of the Titan of embodied evil, the likeness of sin made flesh", "as something to his purpose nothing"! But many readers have also detected echoes of eighteenth-century comic novels in
      <i>
       Don Juan
      </i>
      , pinpointing the poem's rambling, desultory style, flamboyant and distractible narrator, and heavily ironic tonequalities that Byron may have gleaned from novels like Fielding's
      <i>
       Tom Jones
      </i>
      , as well as the writings of Tobias Smollett. Likewise,
      <i>
       Don Juan
      </i>
      belongs to the tradition of the
      <i>
       picaresque
      </i>
      , a genre of fiction (originating in Spain) that followed the adventures of roguish young men of low birth who made their way in a corrupt society via their cunning and courage. (Fielding's and Smollett's novels also belong to this genre.) Finally, Byron was probably inspired by Cervantes's
      <i>
       Don Quixote
      </i>
      and Ariosto's
      <i>
       Orlando Furioso
      </i>
      , two works which he greatly admired and borrowed from liberally.
     </p>

     <p>
      Many of Byron's remarks and reflections on the motive behind his poem are humorous paradoxes, provoked by advice and opposition. For instance, writing to
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Moore" title="Thomas Moore">
       Thomas Moore
      </a>
      , he says, "I have finished the first canto ... of a poem in the style and manner of Beppo, encouraged by the good success of the same. It is ... meant to be a little quietly facetious upon every thing. But I doubt whether it is notat least as far as it has gonetoo free for these very modest days."
     </p>
     <p>
      Critical opinion aligned itself with the opinion that the poem was "too free," however, a month after the two first cantos had been issued, Byron wrote to Murray , "You ask me for the plan of Donny Johnny; I have no planI had no plan; but I had or have materials.... You are too earnest and eager about a work never intended to be serious. Do you suppose that I could have any intention but to giggle and make giggle?a playful satire, with as little poetry as could be helped, was what I meant." After the completion but before the publication of Cantos III., IV., V., in a further letter to Murray , he writes, "The Fifth is so far from being the last of Don Juan, that it is hardly the beginning. I meant to take him the tour of Europe, with a proper mixture of siege, battle, and adventure, and to make him finish as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_du_Val-de-Gr%C3%A2ce,_baron_de_Cloots" title="Jean-Baptiste du Val-de-Grce, baron de Cloots">
       Anacharsis Cloots
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      .... I meant to have made him a
      <a href="/wiki/Cicisbeo" title="Cicisbeo">
       Cavalier Servente
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
       Italy
      </a>
      , and a cause for a divorce in
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      , and a Sentimental '
      <a href="/wiki/The_Sorrows_of_Young_Werther" title="The Sorrows of Young Werther">
       Werther
      </a>
      -faced' man in Germany, so as to show the different ridicules of the society in each of these countries, and to have displayed him gradually gt and blas, as he grew older, as is natural. But I had not quite fixed whether to make him end in Hell, or in an unhappy marriage, not knowing which would be the severest."
     </p>
     <p>
      Conversely, it has been argued that Byron did not "whistle" Don Juan "for want of thought"  but instead he had found a thing to say, and he meant to make the world listen. He had read, albeit with angry disapproval,
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">
       Coleridge
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Critique on (
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maturin" title="Charles Maturin">
        Charles Maturin
       </a>
       's) Bertram
      </i>
      , where Coleridge describes the legendary Don Juan as a figure not unlike Childe Harold, or for that matter, Byron himself: "Rank, fortune, wit, talent, acquired knowledge, and liberal accomplishments, with beauty of person, vigorous health...all these advantages, elevated by the habits and sympathies of noble birth and natural character, are...combined in Don Juan, so as to give him the means of carrying into all its practical consequences the doctrine of a godless nature... Obedience to nature is the only virtue." Again, "It is not the wickedness of Don Juan...which constitutes the character an abstraction, ...but the rapid succession of the correspondent acts and incidents, his intellectual superiority, and the splendid accumulation of his gifts and desirable qualities as coexistent with entire wickedness in one and the same person." It is therefore conceivable that Byron read these passages as either a suggestion or a challenge, or both.
     </p>
     <p>
      Byron was under no delusion as to the grossness of Don Juan, though he protested that he was sheltered by the superior grossness of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ariosto" title="Ariosto">
       Ariosto
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_La_Fontaine" title="Jean de La Fontaine">
       La Fontaine
      </a>
      , of Prior and of
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fielding" title="Henry Fielding">
       Fielding
      </a>
      . When Murray charges him with "approximations to indelicacy," he laughs at the euphemism, but when
      <a href="/wiki/John_Hobhouse,_1st_Baron_Broughton" title="John Hobhouse, 1st Baron Broughton">
       Hobhouse
      </a>
      talked to him "about morality," he flames out, "I maintain that it is the most moral of poems."
      <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hartley_Coleridge" title="Ernest Hartley Coleridge">
       Ernest Hartley Coleridge
      </a>
      concludes that Byron looked upon his great work as a whole, and he knew that the "raison d'tre of his song" was not only to celebrate, but, by the white light of truth, to represent and exhibit the great things of the worldLove and War, and Death by sea and land, and Man, half-angel, half-demonthe comedy of his fortunes, and the tragedy of his passions and his fate.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the 17th and 18th centuries,
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      experienced a quick decline from power in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      . This fall was accompanied by what many saw as relative cultural poverty when compared to
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . By Byron's time, Spanish culture was often considered both archaic and exotic. This led to a
      <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
       Romantic
      </a>
      valorisation of Spanish culture. Many scholars note this work as a prime example of Spanish
      <a href="/wiki/Exoticism" title="Exoticism">
       exoticism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Peer_opinion">
       Peer opinion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Peer opinion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">
       Walter Scott
      </a>
      maintained that its creator "has embraced every topic of human life, and sounded every string of the divine harp, from its slightest to its most powerful and heart-astounding tones."
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">
       Goethe
      </a>
      described Don Juan as "a work of boundless genius."
      <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">
       Percy Bysshe Shelley
      </a>
      , on the receipt of Cantos III, IV, V, bore testimony to his "wonder and delight:" "This poem carries with it at once the stamp of originality and defiance of imitation. Nothing has ever been written like it in English, nor, if I may venture to prophesy, will there be, unless carrying upon it the mark of a secondary and borrowed light.... You are building up a drama," he adds, "such as England has not yet seen, and the task is sufficiently noble and worthy of you." Again, of the fifth canto he writes, "Every word has the stamp of immortality.... It fulfils, in a certain degree, what I have long preached of producingsomething wholly new and relative to the age, and yet surpassingly beautiful." Finally,
      <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">
       Algernon Charles Swinburne
      </a>
      , neither a disciple nor encomiast of Byron, pays eloquent tribute to the strength and splendour of Don Juan: "Across the stanzas ... we swim forward as over the 'broad backs of the sea;' they break and glitter, hiss and laugh, murmur and move like waves that sound or that subside. There is in them a delicious resistance, an elastic motion, which salt water has and fresh water has not. There is about them a wide wholesome air, full of vivid light and constant wind, which is only felt at sea. Life undulates and Death palpitates in the splendid verse.... This gift of life and variety is the supreme quality of Byron's chief poem".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">
       Pronunciation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">

       </span>

     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Till, after cloying the gazettes with cant,
       <br/>
       The age discovers he is not the
       <b>
        true one
       </b>
       ;
       <br/>
       Of such as these I should not care to vaunt,
       <br/>
       I'll therefore take our ancient friend Don
       <b>
        Juan
       </b>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In the above passage, "Juan" is rhymed with "true one", the word being read according to the rules of English
      <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
       orthography
      </a>
      as
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/d/ 'j' in 'jam'">
           d
          </span>
          <span title="/u/ long 'oo' in 'food'">
           u
          </span>
          <span title="/n/ 'on' in 'button'">
           n
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      <span class="Unicode" title="English pronunciation respelling">
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i>
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             JEW
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -n
        </i>
       </a>
      </span>
      . A pattern of pronouncing foreign words according to the rules of English orthography recurs throughout the work.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_Southey_dedication">
       Robert Southey dedication
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Robert Southey dedication">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The poem is dedicated, with some scorn, to
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">
       Robert Southey
      </a>
      , then
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" title="Poet Laureate">
       Poet Laureate
      </a>
      
      <i>
       You, Bob! are rather insolent, you know, / At being disappointed in your wish / To supersede all warblers here below, / And be the only Blackbird in the dish;
      </i>
      . In its first publication, Byron cautions Murray: "As the Poem is to be published anonymously, omit the Dedication. I won't attack the dog in the dark. Such things are for scoundrels and renegadoes like himself". According to the editor of the 1833
      <i>
       Works of Lord Byron
      </i>
      the existence of the Dedication "became notorious" in consequence of Hobhouse's article in the Westminster Review, 1824. He adds, for Southey's consolation and encouragement, that "for several years the verses have been selling in the streets as a broadside," and that "it would serve no purpose to exclude them on the present occasion." But Southey was not appeased. He tells
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Cunningham_(author)" title="Allan Cunningham (author)">
       Allan Cunningham
      </a>
      that "the new edition of Byron's works is ... one of the very worst symptoms of these bad times" .
     </p>
     <p>
      The dedication also takes issue with the
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Poets" title="Lake Poets">
       Lake Poets
      </a>
      generally 
      <i>
       YouGentlemen! by dint of long seclusion / From better company, have kept your own ... There is a narrowness in such a notion, / Which makes me wish you'd change your lakes for Ocean
      </i>
       and specifically 
      <i>
       And
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">
        Coleridge
       </a>
       , too, has lately taken wing, / But like a hawk encumbered with his hood,- /  Explaining
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">
        Metaphysics
       </a>
       to the nation  / I wish he would explain his Explanation
      </i>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">
       Wordsworth
      </a>
      
      <i>
       T is poetry-at least by his assertion,
      </i>
      ; and Southey's predecessor as Laureate,
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_James_Pye" title="Henry James Pye">
       Henry James Pye
      </a>
      in the use of and pun on the old song
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sing_a_Song_of_Sixpence" title="Sing a Song of Sixpence">
        Sing a Song of Sixpence
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       four and twenty Blackbirds in a pye
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">
       Structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Juan_(Byron)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The poem is in eight line
      <a href="/wiki/Iambic_pentameter" title="Iambic pentameter">
       iambic pentameter
      </a>
      with the rhyme scheme ab ab ab cc  often the last rhyming couplet is used for a humor comic line or humorous
      <a href="/wiki/Bathos" title="Bathos">
       bathos
      </a>
      . This rhyme scheme is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Ottava_rima" title="Ottava rima">
       ottava rima
      </a>
      . In Italian, because of the common rhymed endings, the effect of ottava rima is often highly comedic or highly tragic. Because of its few rhymed endings, the effect of ottava rima in English is often comic, and Byron chose it for this reason.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
