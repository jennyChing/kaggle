    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Nonlinear narrative
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       disjointed narrative
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       disrupted narrative
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Narratology" title="Narratology">
       narrative technique
      </a>
      , sometimes used in
      <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
       literature
      </a>
      , film,
      <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">
       hypertext
      </a>
      websites and other narratives, where events are portrayed, for example out of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronological" title="Chronological">
       chronological
      </a>
      order, or in other ways where the narrative does not follow the direct causality pattern of the events featured, such as parallel distinctive plot lines, dream immersions or narrating another story inside the main plot-line. It is often used to mimic the structure and recall of human
      <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">
       memory
      </a>
      , but has been applied for other reasons as well.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
       Literature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Literature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Beginning a narrative
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/In_medias_res" title="In medias res">
        in medias res
       </a>
      </i>
      (Latin: "into the middle of things") began in ancient times and was established as a convention of
      <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">
       epic poetry
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">
       Homer
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">
        Iliad
       </a>
      </i>
      in the 8th century BC. The technique of narrating most of the story in
      <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">
       flashback
      </a>
      also dates back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_epic_poetry" title="Indian epic poetry">
       Indian epic
      </a>
      , the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">
        Mahabharata
       </a>
      </i>
      , around the 5th century BC. Several medieval
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/One_Thousand_and_One_Nights" title="One Thousand and One Nights">
        Arabian Nights
       </a>
      </i>
      tales such as "
      <a href="/wiki/Sinbad_the_Sailor" title="Sinbad the Sailor">
       Sinbad the Sailor
      </a>
      ", "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_City_of_Brass" title="The City of Brass">
       The City of Brass
      </a>
      " and "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Three_Apples" title="The Three Apples">
       The Three Apples
      </a>
      " also had nonlinear narratives employing the
      <i>
       in medias res
      </i>
      and flashback techniques.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From the late 19th century and early 20th century,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist_literature" title="Modernist literature">
       modernist
      </a>
      novelists
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">
       Joseph Conrad
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Woolf" title="Virginia Woolf">
       Virginia Woolf
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ford_Madox_Ford" title="Ford Madox Ford">
       Ford Madox Ford
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust" title="Marcel Proust">
       Marcel Proust
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">
       William Faulkner
      </a>
      experimented with narrative chronology and abandoning linear order.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples of nonlinear novels are:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Cam%C3%B5es" title="Lus Vaz de Cames">
       Lus Vaz de Cames
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lusiads" title="The Lusiads">
        The Lusiads
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Sterne" title="Laurence Sterne">
       Laurence Sterne
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Opinions_of_Tristram_Shandy,_Gentleman" title="The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman">
        The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman
       </a>
      </i>
      (175967),
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">
       Thomas Carlyle
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sartor_Resartus" title="Sartor Resartus">
        Sartor Resartus
       </a>
      </i>
      (ca. 1833),
      <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Bront">
       Emily Bront
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights" title="Wuthering Heights">
        Wuthering Heights
       </a>
      </i>
      (1847),
      <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">
       James Joyce's
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">
        Ulysses
       </a>
      </i>
      (1922) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Finnegans_Wake" title="Finnegans Wake">
        Finnegans Wake
       </a>
      </i>
      (1939),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sadeq_Hedayat" title="Sadeq Hedayat">
       Sadeq Hedayat
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Blind_Owl" title="The Blind Owl">
        The Blind Owl
       </a>
      </i>
      (1937),
      <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">
       William S. Burroughs'
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_Lunch" title="Naked Lunch">
        Naked Lunch
       </a>
      </i>
      (1959),
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Heller" title="Joseph Heller">
       Joseph Heller
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Catch-22" title="Catch-22">
        Catch-22
       </a>
      </i>
      (1961),
      <a href="/wiki/Muriel_Spark" title="Muriel Spark">
       Muriel Spark
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Prime_of_Miss_Jean_Brodie_(novel)" title="The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie (novel)">
        The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie
       </a>
      </i>
      (1961),
      <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Vonnegut" title="Kurt Vonnegut">
       Kurt Vonnegut's
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse-Five" title="Slaughterhouse-Five">
        Slaughterhouse-Five
       </a>
      </i>
      (1969),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milorad_Pavi%C4%87_(writer)" title="Milorad Pavi (writer)">
       Milorad Pavi's
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_the_Khazars" title="Dictionary of the Khazars">
        Dictionary of the Khazars
       </a>
      </i>
      (1988),
      <a href="/wiki/Irvine_Welsh" title="Irvine Welsh">
       Irvine Welsh
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(novel)" title="Trainspotting (novel)">
        Trainspotting
       </a>
      </i>
      (1993),
      <a href="/wiki/Carole_Maso" title="Carole Maso">
       Carole Maso
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Ava: a novel
      </i>
      (1993) and
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_O%27Brien_(author)" title="Tim O'Brien (author)">
       Tim O'Brien
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Going_After_Cacciato" title="Going After Cacciato">
        Going After Cacciato
       </a>
      </i>
      (1979).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several of Michael Moorcock's novels, particularly those in the Jerry Cornelius series, in particular
      <a href="/wiki/The_English_Assassin:_A_Romance_of_Entropy" title="The English Assassin: A Romance of Entropy">
       The English Assassin: A Romance of Entropy
      </a>
      (1972) and
      <a href="/wiki/The_Condition_of_Muzak" title="The Condition of Muzak">
       The Condition of Muzak
      </a>
      (1977) are notable for extending the nonlinear narrative form in order to explore the complex nature of identity within a multiversal universe.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Scott_McCloud" title="Scott McCloud">
       Scott McCloud
      </a>
      argues in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Understanding_Comics" title="Understanding Comics">
        Understanding Comics
       </a>
      </i>
      that the narration of comics is nonlinear because it relies on the reader's choices and interactions.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Film">
       Film
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Film">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_nonlinear_narrative_films" title="List of nonlinear narrative films">
       List of nonlinear narrative films
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Defining nonlinear structure in film is, at times, difficult. Films may use extensive
      <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">
       flashbacks
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Flashforward" title="Flashforward">
       flashforwards
      </a>
      within a linear storyline, while nonlinear films often contain linear sequences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blum_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blum-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">
       Orson Welles'
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">
        Citizen Kane
       </a>
      </i>
      (1941)  influenced structurally by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Power_and_the_Glory_(1933_film)" title="The Power and the Glory (1933 film)">
        The Power and the Glory
       </a>
      </i>
      (1933)  and
      <a href="/wiki/Akira_Kurosawa" title="Akira Kurosawa">
       Akira Kurosawa
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rashomon_(film)" title="Rashomon (film)">
        Rashomon
       </a>
      </i>
      (1950) use a non-chronological flashback narrative that is often labeled nonlinear.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Silent_and_early_era">
       Silent and early era
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Silent and early era">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Experimentation with nonlinear structure in film dates back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">
       silent film
      </a>
      era, including
      <a href="/wiki/D._W._Griffith" title="D. W. Griffith">
       D. W. Griffith
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Intolerance_(film)" title="Intolerance (film)">
        Intolerance
       </a>
      </i>
      (1916) and
      <a href="/wiki/Abel_Gance" title="Abel Gance">
       Abel Gance
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_(1927_film)" title="Napolon (1927 film)">
        Napolon
       </a>
      </i>
      (1927).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Debruge_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Debruge-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nonlinear film emerged from the French
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      in ).[5] in 1924 with Ren Clairs Entracte, Dadast film and then in 1929 with
      <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buuel">
       Luis Buuel
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dal">
       Salvador Dal
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Un_Chien_Andalou" title="Un Chien Andalou">
        Un Chien Andalou
       </a>
      </i>
      (English:
      <i>
       An Andalusian Dog
      </i>
      ). The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrealist" title="Surrealist">
       surrealist
      </a>

      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fullmetal_Alchemist" title="Fullmetal Alchemist">
        Fullmetal Alchemist
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_Powers_Hetalia" title="Axis Powers Hetalia">
        Axis Powers Hetalia
       </a>
      </i>
      , and (partly)
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Boogiepop_Phantom" title="Boogiepop Phantom">
        Boogiepop Phantom
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
       ABC
      </a>
      television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Lost_(TV_series)" title="Lost (TV series)">
        Lost
       </a>
      </i>
      made extensive use of nonlinear story telling, with each episode typically featuring a primary storyline on the island as well as a secondary storyline from another point in a character's life, either past or future.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/FX_(TV_channel)" title="FX (TV channel)">
       FX
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">
       Emmy Award
      </a>
      winning legal drama
      <a href="/wiki/Damages_(TV_series)" title="Damages (TV series)">
       Damages
      </a>
      starring
      <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Close" title="Glenn Close">
       Glenn Close
      </a>
      , begins each season with an intensely melodramatic event taking place and then traveling back six months earlier. Throughout the season, each episode shows events both in the past, present, and future that lead up to and follow said event.
     </p>
     <p>
      The British sitcom
      <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(UK_TV_series)" title="Coupling (UK TV series)">
       Coupling
      </a>
      would often utilize non-linear narratives in which groups of men and women would independently discuss an event, after which (or during) the event would be portrayed.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">
       Video games
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Video games">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_gameplay" title="Nonlinear gameplay">
       Nonlinear gameplay
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiple_endings" title="Multiple endings">
       Multiple endings
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_games" title="Video games">
       video games
      </a>
      , the term "nonlinear" refers to a game that has more than one possible story line and/or ending.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This allows the audience to choose from multiple different paths, that may be compatible with their style of play. This increases replay value, as players must often beat the game several times to get the entire story.
      <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">
       Role-playing video games
      </a>
      , such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fallout_(video_game)" title="Fallout (video game)">
        Fallout
       </a>
      </i>
      , often contain multiple paths which the player may choose from the beginning of the game. Multiple endings also appear in some
      <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">
       adventure games
      </a>
      (such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow_of_Memories" title="Shadow of Memories">
        Shadow of Memories
       </a>
      </i>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Survival_horror" title="Survival horror">
       survival horrors
      </a>
      (such as the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Resident_Evil" title="Resident Evil">
        Resident Evil
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Silent_Hill" title="Silent Hill">
        Silent Hill
       </a>
      </i>
      games),
      <a href="/wiki/Stealth_game" title="Stealth game">
       stealth games
      </a>
      (such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Metal_Gear_Solid" title="Metal Gear Solid">
        Metal Gear Solid
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Platform_game" title="Platform game">
       platform games
      </a>
      (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">
       Sega
      </a>
      's spin-off game,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shadow_the_Hedgehog" title="Shadow the Hedgehog">
        Shadow the Hedgehog
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Some video games mimic film non-linearity by presenting a single plot in a chronologically distorted way instead of letting the player determine the story flow themselves. The
      <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">
       first-person shooter
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tribes:_Vengeance" title="Tribes: Vengeance">
        Tribes: Vengeance
       </a>
      </i>
      is an example of this; another is Sega's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Adventure" title="Sonic Adventure">
        Sonic Adventure
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Often game developers use the idea of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Character_amnesia_(arts)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Character amnesia (arts) (page does not exist)">
       character amnesia
      </a>
      in games. It helps give the game a beginning because the audience only has the understanding that there is a history before the events of the game take place, and it allows the developers more leniency with the paths the player can take. The latter option ultimately results in the development of a nonlinear story. Furthermore, by creating a nonlinear storyline the complexity of game play is greatly expanded. Nonlinear game play allows for greater replay value, allowing the player to put together different pieces of a potentially puzzling storyline. A fitting example of character amnesia is the 2005 video game
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade_(interactive_story)" title="Faade (interactive story)">
        Faade
       </a>
      </i>
      . In
      <i>
       Faade
      </i>
      the player is put into a situation that lasts approximately 10 to 15 minutes in real time, yet the events recalled seem to have a basis in years of dramatic history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="HTML_narratives">
       HTML narratives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_narrative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: HTML narratives">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In contemporary society webpages or to be more correct, hypertexts, have become affluent forms of narratives. Hypertexts have great potential to create non-linear forms of narratives. They allow for individuals to actually interact with the story through links, images, audio and video. An established hypertext narrative is Public Secret.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Public Secret illustrates the reality of being incarcerated in California's Criminal Justice System. It brings to light the way inmates are treated. This functions as a non-linear narrative because it allows for its audience to witness through text and audio the reality of being a female inmate. However, there is no exact beginning or end as there are in comic books or video games. This website consists of multiple subtopics that do not force the audience to make their next selection based on what their previous experiences are.
     </p>
