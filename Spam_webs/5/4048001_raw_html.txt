        </a>
        , managing partner of the San Francisco law firm
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=business&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Prometheus+Partners%22">
         Prometheus Partners
        </a>
        , sees value in such anonymity.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I can ask my former in-house colleagues a question in a way that won't disclose my identity," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Founder and CEO
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=business&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Monica+Zent%22">
         Monica Zent
        </a>
        said her startup recognized a "big demand" for anonymity.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There's an insurmountable need to be able to engage on a more private, more relevant basis," Zent said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Still, legal ethics and attorney malpractice expert
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=business&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Diane+Karpman%22">
         Diane Karpman
        </a>
        of Beverly Hills said Foxwordy's shift "makes me uncomfortable" because lawyers will not be able to tell whether they're giving advice to a friend or an adversary.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I don't see how you can make sure you're not engaged in a conflict of interest," said Karpman of
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=business&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Karpman+%26+Associates%22">
         Karpman &amp; Associates
        </a>
        , which specializes in attorney malpractice and ethics cases.
       </p>
       <p>
        Foxwordy, which has about 2,100 members so far, hopes to become the conduit for lawyer-to-lawyer connections online around the globe as Facebook has for family and friends or LinkedIn has for professionals.
       </p>
       <p>
        Like those social networks, Foxwordy requires members to use their identities to sign up. The company also uses background checks to make sure members are accredited by bar associations.
       </p>
       <p>
        The difference is Facebook and LinkedIn always require the use of real names. (Facebook recently took a step toward anonymity by testing apps that let members sign on without divulging their identities.)
       </p>
       <p>
        For those in the legal profession, however, privacy isn't just an ideal - it's the law.
       </p>
       <h3 class="subhead" style="subhead">
        High ethical standards
       </h3>
       <p>
        "Lawyers are probably held to some of the highest standards of behavior in terms of ethics, consumer privacy and ethical compliance," Zent said. "They're right up there with doctors."
       </p>
       <p>
        But those standards also limit lawyers from the same deep, off-the-record conversations in public online settings that they are already having with colleagues near their office water coolers, at conferences or during cocktail hours.
       </p>
       <p>
        "And those conversations are more fruitful when there's some anonymity," she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Sometimes, lawyers need to move discreetly, without calling attention to themselves, their clients or their employers.
       </p>
       <p>
        In some cases, Quintero said by e-mail, "We need to draw upon the expertise of other legal colleagues who can support our clients in areas in which our firm does not practice. We look to establish friendly cross-referral relationships and Foxwordy gives us the ability to vet out those partners quickly."
       </p>
       <p>
        Closing the doors to everyone who isn't a lawyer helps, he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I don't have to worry about someone trying to sell me insurance for the firm or having a dialogue about sensitive topics in a group or site where literally anyone can listen in," he said. "That's a huge relief."
       </p>
       <p>
        Some Foxwordy members ask help interpreting court rulings and laws. Others request experts on whom they could call to testify.
       </p>
       <p>
        Massachusetts attorney
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=business&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Robert+Ambrogi%22">
         Robert Ambrogi
        </a>
        , who also writes about the Internet and technology, said there is a long list of "now-you-see-them, now-you-don't" networking sites designed for the legal profession, including a high-profile site started by the
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=business&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22American+Bar+Association%22">
         American Bar Association
        </a>
        that "just crashed and burned."
       </p>
       <p>
        "The fact is, lawyers are reluctant to post on public networks," he said. "There's always concern about inadvertently establishing a client relationship with someone who sees one of your posts."
       </p>
       <p>
        But he said "sharing privately in a network like this seems to be appealing."
       </p>
       <p>
        The concept for Foxwordy was born three years ago, when Zent's law firm began looking at how inefficient networking and mentoring were in the legal field. Zent said she began to realize those same inefficiencies existed in other fields.
       </p>
       <h3 class="subhead" style="subhead">
        Solving challenges
       </h3>
       <p>
        "People are still relying on e-mail and trade shows and conferences," she said. "And newsletters on paper. How antiquated. So for us in my firm, Foxwordy was initially created to solve some very pragmatic challenges we had."
       </p>
       <p>
        State and national bar associations have adopted standards covering the use of social media to prevent, for example, a judge from friending attorneys on Facebook. If a lawyer and a judge are Facebook friends, it creates "the appearance of impropriety," Karpman said.
       </p>
       <p>
        But, she wonders, what happens if a Foxwordy member were to post an opinion in a case that helps an opposing attorney during a trial?
       </p>
       <p>
        "You could be working against your client's interests," she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Lawyers also could inadvertently disclose information covered by strict attorney-client confidentiality rules, Karpman said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "You know what you tell a lawyer will always be secret," Karpman said. "It would be a waiver of the fundamental No. 1 obligation a lawyer owes to their client."
       </p>
       <p>
        Zent, however, said the same off-the-record, disclaimer-free conversations lawyers can have on Foxwordy already happen offline.
       </p>
       <p>
        But Foxwordy's cloak of anonymity provides an online "tool that enables them to be discreet" in a way that might be harder in person, she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "At a cocktail lounge, you can't wear a bag over your head," she said.
       </p>

       </p>
