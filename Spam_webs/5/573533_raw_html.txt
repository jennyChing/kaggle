        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c79e74cc970b">

          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/06/for-the-love-of-linen.html">
            For the Love of Linen
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d127d788970c-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="FullSizeRender" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d127d788970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d127d788970c-300wi" style="width: 300px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="FullSizeRender"/>
             </a>
             Who doesn't love Linen Stitch?
            </p>
            <p>
             Oh Linen Stitch, how I love thee.
            </p>
            <p>
             Linen Stitch is a twofer. You know what I mean: You get two in one. First, you get knitting and B, you get weaving. Knitting and weaving, in my estimation is a wonderful combo. And when I knit and weave, aka Linen Stitch, I go so fast and furious that I nearly combust.
            </p>
            <p>
             Seriously, though. It also doesn't hurt to have a little of my favorite Koigu KPPPMmmmmmm around the house. There's something about the hand-painted nature of the yarn that lends itself to a stunning fabric when knit in Linen Stitch.
            </p>
            <p>
             If you're wondering how to knit Linen Stitch, here's how you do it in the round, as I have it here (I can't tell you what I'm making, but if you want to knit a tube-like item in Linen Stitch, here's the pattern):
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Linen Stitch in the Round
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             (odd number of sts; 2-rnd repeat)
            </p>
            <p>
             Rnd 1: *K1, sl 1 wyif; rep from * to last st, k1.
            </p>
            <p>
             Rnd 2: Sl 1 wyif, *k1, sl 1 wyif; rep from * to last st.
            </p>
            <p>
             Repeat Rows 1 and 2 for Linen Stitch.
            </p>
            <p>
             If there is any downside to Linen Stitch, it would be that it isn't stretchy. So, it lends itself to things that just need to be pretty, not too functional, and sit there.
            </p>
            <p>
             P.S. Yes that is cigar smoke in the background. I promise you that I won't sell you any of those skeins on eBay. I don't like getting stinky yarn in the mail, either.
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         April 15, 2015
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c77a25bf970b">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/04/truth-to-knitting.html">
            Pom Pon Etymology and it Happens to us all.
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c77a2518970b-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="Sockscomplete" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c77a2518970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c77a2518970b-300wi" style="width: 285px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="Sockscomplete"/>
             </a>
             Well, they're finished. the one pair of "socks" out of many pair of socks SON (that would be Still on Needles).
            </p>
            <p>
             My guess is, I finished them first because they're little, short ones. Those are the easier ones. The little, short ones. The other socks I have SON are regular socks, you know, the kind that are, well, you know, socks.
            </p>
            <p>
             Remember how I only had 24 grams of yarn left and I was worrying that I'd have to steal from my second skein of Regia Design Line to make the other sock-like item?
            </p>
            <p>
             Turns out I did need to open up that second skein. Not because I ran out of yarn with the other sock-like shortie thingy, but because I decided that they must have pom pons. There was no way around it. I just had to.
            </p>
            <p>
             Plus, those pom pons. Just like tap dancing, you can't frown while making a pom pon. (Or while wearing pom pons, unless you feel stupid.)
            </p>
            <p>
             Which brings me to this: I call them pom pons. Others call them pom poms. I kinda looked it up and the original word was "pom pon" but somehow people misheard the word and started using "pom pom" which has gained ground. Did you know that?
            </p>
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb081e16fc970d-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="Oops" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb081e16fc970d img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb081e16fc970d-200wi" style="width: 200px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="Oops"/>
             </a>
             All I know is, when I hear the word "pom pom" all I get are visions of my seventh-grade year when my friend talked me into trying out for cheerleader with her (and I made it and she didn't). Let me say this: I usually seempretty peppy but I'm not "pom pom" peppy. Oh, those nights on the football field when I had to shake them like I meant it while the seventh-grade boys ran through a giant strip of acrylic-painted super-wide kitchen paper. Oh boy. Whew. Glad that's over. (The big strip of paper usually said something like: "Go Wild Cats, Go!")
            </p>
            <p>
             Maybe that's why I insist on calling the tufts of happy fluff the very correct "pom pon." So, there.
            </p>
            <p>
             Oh, and here's another surprise: Just after I took the first picture, guess what I found?
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         March 31, 2015
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb0813af4d970d">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/03/our-secret-club.html">
            Our Secret Club
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             I took two pictures in the past few days.
            </p>
            <p>
             Awhile back, I bought two skeins of Regia Design Line by Kristen Nicholas (#3314). Each one is 50 grams. That's enough for a pair of regular socks in my size.
            </p>
            <p>
             Since it has been so warm in these parts, I couldn't imagine knitting socks because gosh knows I won't wear full socks any time soon. I mean, I took one of those very accurate online questionnaires to determine whether or not I'm 100 percent "Southern Californian." I answered every, single question honestly (well, except for how many pair of flip-flops I own. I said 3 - 4 but the truth is, I probably have more) and it came back at I'm 100 percent "Southern Californian." Oy.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0f95a7e970c-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="IMG_0501" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0f95a7e970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0f95a7e970c-300wi" style="width: 275px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="IMG_0501"/>
             </a>
             That said--and I'm not saying that knitting a brief-er version of socks isn't too unpalatable based on my workload at the moment--I decided to make little tennis-y socks. You know, the ones we used to wear with pompoms on the backs.
            </p>
            <p>
             For all you young people, yes, we used to wear them. Well, come to think of it, I didn't, but my mom did. Not sure what the pompoms were for except for maybe keeping the little socklets from slipping down your heels and into the inside of your sneakers. Back in the day, by the way, one probably needed things like pompoms to keep things from slipping down. We were seriously in need of elastic-y things. I can't tell you about how many slips I lost during dances, but let's save those stories for later.
            </p>
            <p>
             Anyway.
            </p>
            <p>
             So, I knit the first sock. Then, I took a good look at the leftover yarn from that first skein. Hmmm. Then, I weighed the sock that I just finished knitting.
            </p>
            <p>
             Twenty-four grams. Twenty-four grams with all the ends woven in and cut and discarded. Deep breath.
            </p>
            <p>
             Next move: Grab remaining yarn and weigh it. I held my breath for two days because, dang it, wouldn't you know the 9-volt battery had died in the moments between weighing the first sock and weighing the remaining yarn?
            </p>
            <p>
             And yes, I do have a non-digital scale I use for weighing flour and such for cooking but this one is sensitive enough for a yarny geek like me to eek out the exact weight whether in ounces or in grams!
            </p>
            <p>
             I went to the store once and came back without a battery. Why? Me being me, I got to the checker andasked if they had the batteries because I had looked but did not find one and he said he could have someone look for one. I looked at the long line of unhappy faces waiting behind me and said very politely, "No thank you. I will return another time. It's not a rush." (As if.)
            </p>
            <p>
             Then I had an opportunity to go back again and I scored.
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb0813afa2970d-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="IMG_0502" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb0813afa2970d img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb0813afa2970d-300wi" style="width: 275px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="IMG_0502"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             I ran home and inserted the new battery into unit. I weighed the remaining yarn. And this is what I got.
            </p>
            <p>
             Twenty-four stinking grams.
            </p>
            <p>
             You knitters know what this means.
            </p>
            <p>
             I'm either going to have to dip into that second skein of yarn or I'm going to have to make one sock shorter than the other.
            </p>
            <p>
             Whoever said that knitting wasn't suspenseful was lying.
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>

            My Socks are Going to be Too Big
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0f28abd970c-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="IMG_1849" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0f28abd970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0f28abd970c-300wi" style="width: 285px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="IMG_1849"/>

             Meanwhile, these socks are going to be too big.
            </p>

             The girl with the perfect script in her brain will allow it.
            </p>
            <p>
             Even though, and in spite of it all.
            </p>
            <p>
             And yes, I did point my toes. Otherwise, you'd notice that they look a lot like Fred Flintstone's.

        <h2 class="date-header">
         February 23, 2015
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07f71b43970d">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/02/to-make.html">
            To Make
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7534908970b-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="WBGrandmaGrandpa" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7534908970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7534908970b-350wi" style="width: 350px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="WBGrandmaGrandpa"/>
             </a>
             My grandmother Helen passed away a couple nights ago. Helen was the person who taught me to knit.
            </p>
            <p>
             Although this is not going to be a tribute to my grandmother--gosh knows I've talked about her a lot and you all know how deep for her my feelings are--I wanted to talk about something other than knitting and purling that she taught me.
            </p>
            <p>
             She taught me how to make.
            </p>
            <p>
             She taught me how to make the most perfect, flaky pie crust. (It has to do with how it feels and ice water is a must.)
            </p>
            <p>
             She taught me how to make peace. Gosh knows her husband, my grandpa Herman, spent hours in his shed each day sculpting nudes--and she didn't like it too much. She also didn't like that he displayed them in the mirrored shelves behind the bar in their mobile home--but . . . he was making stuff so it was all good.
            </p>
            <p>
             She taught me how to make a sunny-side up egg.
            </p>
            <p>
             She taught me that when you retire that it is okay to paint other people's pottery and get it fired than struggling to make your own from scratch. That is how she made for me my beloved nativity set.
            </p>
            <p>
             She taught me how to make a Manhattan (although I have never consumed one).
            </p>
            <p>
             She also taught me to cherish what other people have made. I remember seeing little items her three daughters made for her when they were school children still displayed proudly probably more than 30 years after she received them. Notably, a paper weight that my aunt Sandy made for her. It always sat on her kitchen counter. I wonder where it is now.
            </p>
            <p>
             People ask me all the time if I have shown Girlfriend how to knit and if she likes to knit. The answer is yes to the first and the answer is no to the next.
            </p>
            <p>
             I don't feel bad that she doesn't like to knit. But, I would feel bad if she didn't want to make.
            </p>
            <p>
             Making things, whatever they may be, be it a happy sunny-side up egg in an old frying pan, a patchwork pillow from your child's well-used swaddling blankets, a hovercraft (my brother made one when he was Girlfriend's age--12--story to come later), a model railroad, love, peace, jokes, a macrame plant hanger, you name it, is something we need to hang onto. We need to hang onto making dearly.
            </p>
            <p>
             Because if we don't, then all we'll ever have to do again is hit Target and be done with it.
            </p>
            <p>
             The End.
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         February 09, 2015
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07ed85c0970d">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/02/the-lucky-ribs-cable-cowl.html">
            The Lucky Ribs Cable Cowl
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07ed8662970d-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="Luckyribsmain" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07ed8662970d img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07ed8662970d-300wi" style="width: 300px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="Luckyribsmain"/>
             </a>
             There's nothing more satisfying than finding one great skein of yarn and with just that one skein, knitting up a cozy cowl that's fast, easily memorized and versatile.
            </p>
            <p>
             The Lucky Ribs Cable Cowl pattern is both written out and charted. This means, that if you're a "chart person," you'll have a chart to follow. On the other hand, if you're a knitter who likes to read a pattern line by line, it's all written out for you, too.
            </p>
            <p>
             This is a totally reversible cowl. You start by knitting it flat and twisting it just once and then joining the two ends together. If you don't want to join the two ends, you could easily add some fringe and make it a scarf.
            </p>
            <p>
             As long as you can do a simple cable pattern, I'd say that this is pretty much a beginner pattern. But if this is your first time doing cables, it's more of an intermediate beginner pattern.
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0d34503970c-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="Cowlflip" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0d34503970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0d34503970c-200wi" style="width: 200px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="Cowlflip"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             Here are the particulars:
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              FINISHED MEASUREMENTS
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Approximately 6" x 45", after blocking
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         February 04, 2015
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0cf28e7970c">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/02/things-are-not-always-as-they-seem-and-my-cap-has-a-belly-button.html">
            Things are Not Always as They Seem and My Cap Has a Belly Button
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c745bcd9970b-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="MJYarnsHat" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c745bcd9970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c745bcd9970b-300wi" style="width: 300px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="MJYarnsHat"/>
             </a>
             This cap. I will not fix it I will not fix it I will not fix it I will not fix it. I will not fix it. I will not fix it. I will not fix it I will not fix it.
            </p>
            <p>
             I really won't fix it.
            </p>
            <p>
             You know why?
            </p>
            <p>
             Because I like it.
            </p>
            <p>
             There: I said it. I like it.
            </p>
            <p>
             I don't care if the crown looks like it has a belly button or a nipple. I just like it. It was an accident so I will embrace it. It would be very, very easy to fix, but I won't fix it I won't fix it I won't fix it I won't fix it I won't fix it.
            </p>
            <p>
             There are people who would fix it. There are people who will point to it and ask me: "Is it just me, or . . . ?"
            </p>
            <p>
             But I won't fix it.
            </p>
            <p>
             I like knitting caps on the fly by starting with just eight stitches and adding stitches every other round (see the spiral there?) until each piece of the pie adds up to the number of stitches that I need for the proper circumference. Personally, I like a cap that is about the same size as my head--who likes hat head?--and gosh knows, I don't need caps for warmth. So about 21" is perfect for me. The other nice thing about making caps this way is that you never need to do a gauge swatch because, as long as you like your fabric you just keep knitting and when you have enough fabric, you measure a few inches, figure out your goal number of stitches and just knit until you get there.
            </p>
            <p>
             Anyway, I got going and had this grand idea of cinching in the tail from the original cast on of those eight stitches because it was getting in my way. Silly me, I threaded my needle through the cast on stitches without looking at the public side of the cap and cinched it, weaved in my ends and cut the thread. When I turned it to look at the right side I thought: Hey: that looks like a belly button or a nipple. Should I fix it?
            </p>
            <p>
             I will not fix it I will not fix it I will not fix it I will not fix it I will not fix it.
            </p>
            <p>
             I think that we need to cut ourselves some slack when it comes to this kind of thing. Don't you?
            </p>
            <p>
             The yarn is so pretty that no one should notice.
            </p>
            <p>
             Not to mention, I'm good at bobbing and weaving.
            </p>
            <p>
             Yarn:
             <a href="http://mjyarns.com" target="_blank">
              MJ Yarns
             </a>
             . Opulent Fingering Weight in Peasant Colorway.
            </p>
            <p>
             BTW: About the "Things are Not as They Seem" part of the title of this post. Doesn't my picture look like I'm in the snow? I'm not. I'm in my living room sitting on a chair and it's 80 degrees outside. The combo of my running pants, the white carpet and the base of my funky coffee table adds up to what I call Knitting Weather! (Even if it is a total illusion.)
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         January 08, 2015

        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07d5c297970d">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2015/01/hello-there.html">
            Hello There! What I did on my Vacation and a Giveaway.
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             Well, now that THAT's over with, I can get back to "regular" life.
            </p>
            <p>
             Seriously, first you face Halloween, then a tornado sort of begins to swirl and you end up in some sort of angsty vortex until it's New Year's Eve and you just want it all be over with. Then, you spend New Year's Day watching TV, hiking, what have you, and you tell yourself that it's safe to go out and about again and then it hits you like a brick in the face: Valentine's Day is coming! I used to be a marketing professional so I know the shtick,but man. It's outta control nowadays!
            </p>
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0bb79a4970c-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="Placematthings" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0bb79a4970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0bb79a4970c-300wi" style="width: 285px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="Placematthings"/>
             </a>
             Lots of things have been happening. For the past couple of months, I've done a LOT of sewing.
             <a href="http://susanbanderson.blogspot.com" target="_blank">
              Susan B. Anderson
             </a>
             has a blog that I follow. She knits but she also sews. And when I see one of her projects that I must sew, well, I drop the knitting and get out my sewing machine. In this particular case, she was sewing some quickie placemats. I'm a sucker for that kind of thing and I have lots of fabric hanging around so I made some!
            </p>
            <p>
             Quickly, here's how I did it:
            </p>
            <p>
             1. I gathered fabric that I liked, each in lots of about 1/2 yard each. I planned on making six.
            </p>
            <p>
             2. Since I wanted to make six, I used an existing placemat and cut out 12 rectangles roughly 1/2 inch longer and wider than my existing placemat.
            </p>
            <p>
             3. Then, I cut out six pieces of batting that were the SAME size as the existing placemat.
            </p>
            <p>
             4. Next, I just sandwiched each placemat thusly (is thusly a word?): Facedown fabric/batting/fabric right-side up.
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0bb79bd970c-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="Completedpm" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0bb79bd970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0bb79bd970c-300wi" style="width: 285px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="Completedpm"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             5. Last, I went to the sewing machine and simply sewed straight lines, reasonably straight, or crooked (that kind of thing doesn't bug me too much because we dim the lights while eating dinner) and then I sewed all the way around the edges, just to neaten things up.
            </p>
            <p>
             6. Last, last, I clipped notches all around the raw edges.
            </p>
            <p>
             7. Last, last, last, I threw everything in the wash.
            </p>
            <p>
             I love them.
             <a href="http://susanbanderson.blogspot.com/2014/12/hi-knitters-winners-have-been-selected.html" target="_blank">
              <br/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             Now, for some knitting.
            </p>
            <p>
             For those of you who love artisinal yarns, I was introduced to a new company called
             <a href="http://mjyarns.com/default.htm" target="_blank">
              M J Yarns
             </a>
             . This is a yarn that is hand dyed in Colorado. I have two colorways, each with a different base. I'm going to keep one and give one away very soon. I just need to swatch the skein that I'll keep before selecting a winner.
            </p>
            <p>
             Here's my dilemma: I don't know what to make with it!
            </p>
            <p>
             I plan on keeping the golden one. It's obviously fingering weight and it is a blend of merino, cashmere and a bit of nylon. It almost seems a shame to knit socks, but with that nylon content, I'm certainly tempted. Another idea would be armwarmers.
            </p>
            <p>
             What do you think?
            </p>
            <p style="text-align: center;">
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07d5c3b2970d-pi" style="display: inline;">
              <img alt="IMG_0221" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07d5c3b2970d img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201bb07d5c3b2970d-350wi" style="width: 350px;" title="IMG_0221"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             Please tell me what you think in comments, and based on them, I'll make a decision and then pick a RANDOM winner of the
             <a href="http://mjyarns.com/Colorways/green_orchid.htm" target="_blank">
              M J Yarns Simple Sock Fingering Weight in the colorway, Green Orchid
             </a>
             . If you want to name specific patterns in comments, that's cool too. I know people love to read through things like this to get ideas of their own.
            </p>
            <p>
             I'll choose a winner in about a week.
            </p>
            <p>
             Now it's time for me to wind my skein and do a little swatch and wait for your ideas!
            </p>
            <p>
             COMMENTS NOW CLOSED! Congratulations, Catherine, you've won the Green Orchid Simple Sock! I've sent you an email!

        <h2 class="date-header">
         December 08, 2014
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d0a4af38970c">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2014/12/theres-just-too-much-noise.html">
            There's Just Too Much Noise.
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             I've been sort of waiting to write a blog post.
            </p>
            <p>
             First, there was all the lead-up to Thanksgiving.
            </p>
            <p>
             <em>
              By the way, every year, us "girls" take a picture in pretty much the same formation. Although it is a little strange to see yourself age or change throughout the years, this is a fun tradition.
             </em>
            </p>
            <p>
             Here is this year's Thanksgiving picture; I always get to make the potatoes. My sister Dooze is always holding a martini shaker or pouring milk into the mash, and then the third person (my sister's sister-in-law or mother-in-law) gets to do whatever they want to:
            </p>
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c71ad9a0970b-pi" style="display: inline;">
              <img alt="IMG_0209" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c71ad9a0970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c71ad9a0970b-350wi" style="width: 350px;" title="IMG_0209"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             It's nice to have a husband who is a professional photographer. There were special lights hanging from the pots and pans above, giving us such a Thanksgiving-ish "glow." You can't tell by the picture that Loretta had gotten into something and had been throwing up all over the joint. Or, that it was 90 degrees outside and we were sweating like crazy. All in all it was a great day. (P.S. I made my shirt dress. I'll post about it after I talk enough about yarn and knitting.)
            </p>
            <p>
             Then, there was Black Friday. Oy.
            </p>
            <p>
             Then, there was Cyber Monday.
            </p>
            <p>
             And all manner of other things that get in the way this time of year.
            </p>
            <p>
             Things are getting out of control. It's all so noisy.
            </p>
            <p>
             When things get out of control, I shut down.
            </p>
            <p>
             That is just my way of dealing with all the chaos.
            </p>
            <p>
             I just want to get back to the regular day-to-day things. You know: knitting, some sewing, some weaving and such. (And finishing up my next book)I don't like feeling like I need to wait for things to die down in order to reach out or write a post.
            </p>
            <p>
             *************
            </p>
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c71ada14970b-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="IMG_0225" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c71ada14970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c71ada14970b-300wi" style="width: 285px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="IMG_0225"/>
             </a>
             Sort of another subject entirely: You know how I feel about knitting gifts. I usually just don't do it unless it is for Girlfriend or HWWV. They like the hats or socks that I make and they use them. I once knit for my niece who lives in Colorado and I saw that she was wearing what I made for her, which made me smile. Yesterday, my brother whispered in my ear that she'd like a "Wendy Scarf" for Christmas. That made me so happy that I cast on for a simple herringbone scarf with some Pagewood Farms yarn. (Don't remember the color name. Oh wait: It's called "Crayon.") You can look in your favorite stitch dictionary for a herringbone pattern or look at
             <a href="http://www.ravelry.com/patterns/library/favorite-scarf-ever" target="_blank">
              Favorite Scarf Ever
             </a>
             for the pattern. I looked at it, but I read up on other people's modifications before I cast on. I think I'll put some tiny pom poms on the points.
            </p>
            <p>
             What do you do when there's too much noise?
            </p>
            <p>
             The chaos this time of year can kiss my grits.
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         November 13, 2014
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7059276970b">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2014/11/knit-a-custom-dog-sweater-fun.html">
            "Never work with animals or children."
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d08f8001970c-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="Pupcharlie" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d08f8001970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d08f8001970c-350wi" style="width: 350px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="Pupcharlie"/>
             </a>
             That's what W. C. Fields said a long time ago. I guess, in theory, he could have been right. I mean, animals and children are unpredictable. But adults aren't half as cute and adults can be mean.
            </p>
            <p>
             So can dogs.
            </p>
            <p>
             And girls starting at around age six.
            </p>
            <p>
             I know this because I have a girl. The girls at her school started to get a little mean around that age.
            </p>
            <p>
             But Girlfriend doesn't seem to have that trait . . .
            </p>
            <p>
             yet.
            </p>
            <p>
             Just like traffic in Los Angeles, you never comment on how good it is, because if you do, then next thing you know, you're stuck on the freeway for the next six days and seven nights. So I will not brag about Girlfriend's lack of "mean."
            </p>
            <p>
             Back to the subject, well, part of the subject: Animals. And knitting for dogs specifically.
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7059884970b-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="Wes" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7059884970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c7059884970b-300wi" style="width: 300px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="Wes"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             This past July, I went to San Francisco and taped some classes for Creativebug. I did a four-week series on Top-Down Sweater Knitting (see the button on the right-hand sidebar), but one of the classes, the one that goes live today, is about how to knit an easy, custom dog sweater with any yarn and at any gauge, without a pattern. Pup Charlie up there was on set and he's sporting one that I knit for the class. The doggie on the right is Wes. He's a real humdinger. He'll steal your hot dog right out of your hand when you're not looking. True story.
            </p>
            <p>
             To make a custom dog sweater like these, all you have to do is take a few simple measurements and knit some simple shapes, sew a couple things together and you have a nifty dog sweater! What's really cool is that I provide you with a simple worksheet to fill out with the measurements so it's all made very simple and straightforward. You can even knit one of these as a gift for a friend or family member's dog by asking for the measurements over the phone--or shoot--by text messaging!
            </p>
            <p>
             But seriously, even if you don't have a subscription to Creativebug, you can get a free, 14-day trial to see if you like it; just use PROMO CODE:
             <strong>
              KNIT.
             </strong>
             There are tons of classes there. There are knitting classes, crochet classes, illustration, sewing, you name it. Even cake decorating and paper folding.
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         October 21, 2014
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-author-wendy entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c6dc970b">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/knitandtonic/2014/10/magpies-homebodies-and-nomads-a-review-and-giveaway.html">
            Magpies, Homebodies and Nomads, a Review and Giveaway
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d081cf51970c-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="MagpiesHomebodies99535JF" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d081cf51970c img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b8d081cf51970c-250wi" style="width: 250px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="MagpiesHomebodies99535JF"/>
             </a>
             It's no wonder that I like Cirilia Rose's book so much. First, just look at the sweater on that cover! It's a gorgeous shade, one to which I normally gravitate. But beyond her color choices and palette, this is such a fun book to read. Not only is
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Magpies-Homebodies-Nomads-Discovering-Exploring/dp/1584799536/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1413926532&amp;sr=1-1&amp;keywords=magpies+homebodies+and+nomads" target="_blank">
              Magpies, Homebodies &amp; Nomads
             </a>
             a pattern book, in it, Cirilia offers all kinds of useful advice for us knitters who, although we are definitely NOT all the same, we do share a couple important similarities:
            </p>
            <p>
             Our love for fiber
            </p>
            <p>
             Our love to "make"
            </p>
            <p>
             The tension we feel when putting outfits together featuring what we have made
            </p>
            <p>
             So not only do we have 25 patterns--many of them timeless and some of them quirky--we also have a lot of attentive commentary on color selection, styling tips and useful info on substituting yarns.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c5e5970b-pi" style="float: left;">
              <img alt="MagpiesHomebodiesandNomads_p105" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c5e5970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c5e5970b-250wi" style="width: 250px; margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px;" title="MagpiesHomebodiesandNomads_p105"/>
             </a>
             With all of the knitting books out there--have you noticed that it seems like there are millions?--this is one new book that I would highly recommend. Not only for the patterns, but especially for the spirit of the book and the thoughtful way Cirilia Rose approaches herknitting and explains her process.
            </p>
            <p>
             This is one of my favorite patterns, the Jordaan Cape. (I love capes and ponchos and I'm not afraid to yell it from the mountain tops!)
            </p>
            <p>
             From what I understand, Cirilia got to assist with the styling in this book. As you can see, she also did some of the modeling. I can tell you from experience, that this is sort of unusual and I'm so happy to see her fantastic results!
            </p>
            <p>
             The photographs are by Jared Flood, whose eye, if you know him you wouldn't be surprised, is spot on and captures the spirit of Cirilia's creations.
            </p>
            <p>
             I can give away a copy of this book!
            </p>
            <p>
             Cirilia believes that we are all a mixture of magpies, homebodies and Nomads. Me? I'm probably more of a homebody than any of the three and magpie would come in second.
             <a class="asset-img-link" href="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c650970b-pi" style="float: right;">
              <img alt="MagpiesHomebodiesandNomads_p061" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c650970b img-responsive" src="http://www.knitandtonic.net/.a/6a00d83451b50d69e201b7c6f7c650970b-200wi" style="width: 200px; margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px;" title="MagpiesHomebodiesandNomads_p061"/>
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             What about you?
            </p>
            <p>
             In comments leave me one that tells me which of the three overtakes most of your knitting personality and I'll enter you to win a copy of the book. I'm teaching in Chicago at Vogue Knitting Live until Monday, but I'll check in and see how it's all going while I'm there. Shortly after I return, I'll select a winner!
            </p>
            <p>
             BTW: This book will be out in November and was published by Stewart, Tabori &amp; Chang.
            </p>
            <p>
             Good luck!
            </p>
            <p>
             CONGRATULATIONS MEGAN!
            </p>
            <p>
             COMMENTS NOW CLOSED.
            </p>
