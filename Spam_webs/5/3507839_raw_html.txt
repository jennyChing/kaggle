      <p class="lead">
       How do high flyers cope with the pressure? Senior leaders share some of their personal mind-body stress busters. By Thea O'Connor.

       <img alt="Chief executive of foster care organisation Berry Street, Sandie de Wolf." itemprop="image" src="/content/dam/images/1/m/t/x/x/g/image.related.articleLeadNarrow.300x0.1mtxws.png/1430653363917.jpg" title=""/>

        Chief executive of foster care organisation Berry Street, Sandie de Wolf.

      <p>
       Each morning when Brendan Gregor gets in the lift at work, he thinks of four things: be present, have some fun, choose my attitude and make someone's day. This helps him sustain positive energy in his role of human resources director, at software company SAS Australia New Zealand.These four principles form the 'Fish! Philosophy', created by John Christensen in 1998, who observed the behaviour of high-energy, high-sales fishmongers in Seattle.
      </p>
      <p>
       "When I am under pressure I also fall back on the 'three Ws' of leadership I learnt in the '90s.Where am I going? Where am I now? And what next?" says Gregor who has worked in senior human resource roles for the past 20 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       Constant change and unabating pressure are a given in today's workplace, especially for those at the top. Learning how to get the upper hand on stress is essential for senior leaders if they want to fulfil their leadership responsibilities.
      </p>
      <p>
       Staying calm, keeping perspective, thinking creatively and making sound decisions becomes impossible once high levels of stress and anxiety kick in. The stress response sends the brain into short-termreactive thinking, impairs our ability to differentiate between important tasks and irrelevant ones, and renders our mood, which is contagious, short and sharp.
      </p>
      <div class="ad " id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
      </div>
      <p>
       For Sandie de Wolf, chief executive of foster care organisation Berry Street, having a good team around her with whom she can sound out ideas and have a good laugh, is her most important buffer against stress.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I have stamina, resilience and the luck of good health," says de Wolf. "If you don't have good health you will struggle with high-demand jobs. I have never been brilliant at work-life balance, but I walk about four mornings a week, and I build in a work-at-home day, once a month."
      </p>
      <p>
       Jenny Balzarano, chief executiveof technology firm BMS in Wollongong, relies on yoga to keep her sane. "I go to a class after work, two to three times a week, and I come out very relaxed, having let go of the drama of the day," says Balzarano, who also writes a journal about twice a week to download, reflect and learn.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I use pen and paper, as it's more like 'me' writing than if I use a computer which can autocorrect my natural expression." Balzarano's biggest stress reliever since taking up the chief executive position about 12 months ago, has been resetting her expectations about what she gets done in a day, and what constitutes 'achieving'.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I'd be setting myself up for failure if I created a carefully planned day," she says. "My biggest responsibility is to keep everyone moving forward, so my days are about spending time with my staff to enable them to get their job done."
      </p>
      <p>
       The Harvard Business Review's 2010 Leadership Insights agrees that "the leader's job is not to defend a rigid timetable against reality", and that the chief executive's ability to influence a large number of activities through brief contacts or "interruptions", might be a highly leveraged use of time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Knowing when it's time to stop and when to make a change is also key to managing pressure over time. "When my former HR role wasn't sitting well, partly due to lots of travel, I requested a change which my seniors accommodated," says Gregor who this year is making an effort to get more exercise. "I'm working up a sweat two to three times a week and feel better for it already."
      </p>
      <p>
       Have the requirements of leadership changed during the last 20 years?
      </p>
      <p>
       "The essence of leadership has remained the same. What's changed is the complexity of the environment we operate in, and the multiple stakeholders you need to work with," de Wolf says. "As well, the scrutiny of the media has increased the pressure on government departments."
      </p>
      <p>
       "There's been a break away from the more rigid, hierarchical structure, where there was no room to challenge the leader, to more of a matrix structure," Gregor says."The leadership mindset is about enabling others, setting others up for success."
      </p>
      <p>
       The 2010 IBM global survey of 1541 chief executives identified complexity of operating in an increasingly volatile and uncertain world as their No. 1 challenge. Previous surveys found that constant change was the biggest challenge.
      </p>
