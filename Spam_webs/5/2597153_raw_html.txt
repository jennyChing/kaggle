   <b>
    Explanation:
   </b>
   The
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Nebula">
    Great Nebula
in Orion
   </a>
   , also known as M42, is one of the
most famous nebulas in the sky.

The star forming region's glowing gas clouds and hot young stars
are on the right in this sharp and colorful
   <a href="http://www.astrogades.es/index.php?nodo=3titulo=galeria&amp;level=picture&amp;id=720">
    image
   </a>
   that includes the smaller
   <a href="http://seds.org/messier/m/m043.html">
    nebula M43
   </a>
   near center and dusty, bluish reflection nebulae
   <a href="ap071220.html">
    NGC 1977 and friends
   </a>
   on the left.

Located at the edge of an otherwise invisible
   <a href="ap101023.html">
    giant
   </a>
   molecular
   <a href="http://www.seds.org/messier/more/oricloud.html">
    cloud complex
   </a>
   ,
these eye-catching nebulae represent only a small
fraction of this galactic neighborhood's wealth of
   <a href="http://www-ssg.sr.unh.edu/ism/what1.html">
    interstellar material
   </a>
   .

Within the well-studied stellar nursery, astronomers have also
identified what appear to be numerous
   <a href="http://hubblesite.org/newscenter/archive/releases/
2001/13">
    infant planetary systems
   </a>
   .

The gorgeous skyscape spans nearly two degrees or about 45
   <a href="http://chandra.harvard.edu/photo/cosmic_distance.html">
    light-years
   </a>
   at the
   <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9FKZvBB30hg">
    Orion Nebula
   </a>
   's estimated distance of 1,500 light-years.
  </p>
