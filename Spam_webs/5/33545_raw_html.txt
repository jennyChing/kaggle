     7 Great Tips for Cooking the Perfect Pasta
    </h1>

     <p>
      <img alt="pasta dish" src="http://startcooking.com/public/pasta_dish.jpg"/>
     </p>
     <p>
      Kathys video for
      <a href="http://startcooking.com/blog/255/How-to-Cook-Pasta/">
       cooking pasta
      </a>
      is perfect for anyone thats ever struggled with cooking pasta properly. Kathy shows us the 6 rules for cooking the perfect pasta.
      <a href="http://startcooking.com/blog/255/How-to-Cook-Pasta/">
       Go check it out.
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      <em>
       Is that all there is to it?
      </em>
      Sure! But if you want to learn just a bit more about cooking the perfect
      <a href="http://startcooking.com/blog/45/Picking-and-Cooking-with-Different-Types-of-Pasta">
       pasta
      </a>
      , here are an additional 7 tips:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       When the water starts boiling, lower the flame so that the bubbles are small. A big, rolling boil will break more delicate pasta such as ravioli or tortellini.
      </li>
      <li>
       Dont boil pasta in a covered pot, as it will quickly overflow. Quick tip: Just leave your wooden spoon in the pot to prevent spillovers.
      </li>
      <li>
       Never put pasta in a pot that isnt boiling, as this will only leave you with a gooey, overdone noodle. Yuck!
      </li>
      <li>
       Its never a good idea to boil two different varieties of pasta together unless theyre similar in size and shape, due to varied cooking times. It also looks kind of wacky to have spaghetti and penne mixed together.
      </li>
      <li>
       The jury is still debating whether putting a teaspoon of oil into the boiling water before the pasta goes in prevents it from sticking or not. Personally, I always add it in, and Ive never felt it has done any harm to my pasta; and it sure has never stuck together.
      </li>
      <li>
       Dont rinse your pasta after its cooked! This is a terrible myth thats propagated across the cooking world. That wonderful coating of starch thats sitting on your heated pasta will keep the delicious sauce from sticking to it. The only time you might
       <a href="http://www.ilovepasta.org/faqs.html#Q7">
        rinse your pasta
       </a>
       is when using it in a cold dish.
      </li>
      <li>
       Get the pasta sauce on the pasta immediately after you drain it. The noodles cool down fast and start sticking to each other without a sauce or oil to keep them apart. (
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h3>
      Did You Know?
     </h3>
     <p>
      If youve always thought that the Italians gave birth to pasta, youd be mistaken. The idea of the noodle is actually a Chinese invention thats over 4,000 years old. The Romans got hold of the stuff however, and added their own spin by changing the ingredient base from rice flour to semolina flour. Pasta making has changed very little since the early days, except for some automation that was brought about by pasta machines. But dont worry about that, you can just buy it at the store!
     </p>
     <p>
      For more tips on easy, impromptu pasta meals,
      <a href="http://www.ilovepasta.org/recipes.html">
       click here
      </a>
      . Or check out Kathys archive of
      <a href="http://startcooking.com/blog/261/Pasta-Round-up">
       pasta posts
      </a>
      .
     </p>
