            <div class="ap-heading">
             <h4>
              Action Points
             </h4>
            </div>
            <div class="ap-content">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Note that this cohort study of Dutch women with rheumatoid arthritis demonstrated that higher disease activity during pregnancy was associated with a greater likelihood of catch-up growth postnatally.
              </li>
              <li>
               Be aware that no women studied were taking methotrexate or biologics.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           Almost one-third of babies born to women with active rheumatoid arthritis (RA) experience rapid catch-up growth early in life, which places them at risk for later health problems, Dutch researchers warned.
          </p>
          <p>
           For each one point increase in the mother's disease activity score in 28 joints (DAS28),
           <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/art.38519/abstract" target="_blank">
            the odds ratio that the child would have rapid catch-up growth during his or her first year was 1.44 (95% CI 1.07-1.95,
            <em>
             P
            </em>
            =0.02)
           </a>
           , according to Florentien D.O. de Steenwinkel, MD, PhD, and colleagues from the Erasmus Medical Center in Rotterdam.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "Findings from recent studies have shown that rapid catch-up growth in weight postnatally is related to worse cardiovascular and metabolic profiles later in life," the researchers wrote in the July issue of
           <em>
            Arthritis &amp; Rheumatology
           </em>
           <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/art.38519/abstract" target="_blank">
            .
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Women with RA  --  and their physicians  --  may try to avoid medication use during pregnancy due to fears of fetal harm.
          </p>
          <p>
           But factors other than medications, including seropositivity and the degree of disease activity, can contribute to the child's having low birth weight followed by compensatory rapid catch-up in weight, according to the researchers.
          </p>
          <p>
           This rapid weight gain, particularly during the first 3 months, has been shown to have a negative influence on the offspring's cardiovascular and metabolic health predictors at age 21.
          </p>
          <p>
           For instance,
           <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/?term=kerkhof+gf+willemsen+rh+health+profile+rapid+weight" target="_blank">
            in one recent study
           </a>
           , accelerated postnatal weight gain was associated with with increased body fat, waist circumference, cholesterol, and triglycerides, as well as altered insulin responses, in early adulthood.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The current study included 167 children from the Dutch Pregnancy-Induced Amelioration of Rheumatoid Arthritis cohort.
          </p>
          <p>
           Mothers' mean age was 33, and disease duration at the time of delivery was almost 8 years. Mean DAS28 was 3.65 during the first trimester and 3.36 in the third trimester.
          </p>
          <p>
           Slightly more than half of the women continued taking medications for their arthritis during pregnancy.
          </p>
          <p>
           Permitted drugs included prednisone, sulfasalazine, and hydroxychloroquine. None of the women were taking methotrexate or biologics.
          </p>
          <p>
           About 58% of the women were seropositive for either rheumatoid factor or anti-citrullinated protein antibodies. The mean standard deviation score (SDS) for birth weight was -0.07.
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           During the first year, 31% of the children had some degree of catch-up growth, which was defined as an SDS for weight gain above 0.67. Of this group, in 90% the weight gain was classified as rapid, in that the SDS was above 0.5 in the first 3 months. SDS for weight then declined for the remainder of the first year.
          </p>
          <p>
           Unlike weight, the mean SDS for increase in height was similar throughout the first 2 years of life.
          </p>
          <p>
           Among infants whose mothers had low DAS28 scores of 3.2 or less, 22% had rapid weight catch-up, while for those with intermediate scores between 3.2 and 5.1, 32% had rapid catch-up. But for those with high DAS28 scores above 5.1, 50% had rapid catch-up for weight.
          </p>
          <p>
           On a multivariate analysis that adjusted for sex, gestational age, and breast or formula feeding, maternal DAS28 score in the third trimester was the only factor associated with rapid catch-up growth.
          </p>
          <p>
           No association was seen between rapid catch-up growth and medication use, seropositivity, or DAS28 scores in the first or second trimester.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "Our study emphasizes the importance of having low RA disease activity, but more importantly, the study shows that medications for RA, including prednisone, during pregnancy have no influence on the tempo of growth," the researchers observed.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Based on our findings, a goal of achieving low maternal disease activity during pregnancy should be pursued, especially in the third trimester," they stated.
          </p>
          <p>
           They also explained that fetal growth restriction can be classified as symmetric, when both height and weight are equally affected, or asymmetric, when only weight is involved.
          </p>
          <p>
           "
           <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19854107" target="_blank">
            Asymmetric growth restriction
           </a>
           is thought to result from acquired pathologic features affecting the vascularization of the placenta in late pregnancy," they wrote.
          </p>
          <p>
           They also pointed out that many RA patients have impairments of endothelial function, which has been linked with higher disease activity and elevated levels of circulating cytokines.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "It is therefore tempting to speculate that the observed rapid catch-up growth in children born to women with active RA may be related to the impact that certain circulating maternal cytokines may have on the vasculature of the placenta," they noted.
          </p>
          <p>
           Further follow-up of the cohort will be needed to determine what impact the rapid catch-up growth ultimately has on the children's health.
          </p>
          <div style="clear:both;">
          </div>
