     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Ilyushin Il-2
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">
       Cyrillic
      </a>
      :  -2)
      <b>
       Sturmovik
      </b>
      was a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground-attack_aircraft" title="Ground-attack aircraft">
       ground-attack aircraft
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">
       Cyrillic
      </a>
      : ,
      <i>
       turmovk
      </i>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       the Second World War
      </a>
      , produced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      in very large numbers. With 36,183 examples of the Il-2 produced during the war, and in combination with its successor, the
      <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-10" title="Ilyushin Il-10">
       Ilyushin Il-10
      </a>
      , a total of 42,330
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_of_Aviation_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_of_Aviation-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      were built, making it the single most produced military aircraft design in aviation history, as well as one of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_most_produced_aircraft" title="List of most produced aircraft">
       most produced piloted aircraft in history
      </a>
      along with the American postwar civilian
      <a href="/wiki/Cessna_172" title="Cessna 172">
       Cessna 172
      </a>
      and the Soviet Union's own
      <a href="/wiki/Polikarpov_Po-2" title="Polikarpov Po-2">
       Polikarpov Po-2
      </a>
      <i>
       Kukuruznik
      </i>
      biplane, itself sometimes seen side-by-side with the big armored Ilyushin monoplane on the front lines.
     </p>
     <p>
      To Il-2 pilots, the aircraft was simply the diminutive "Ilyusha". To the soldiers on the ground, it was the "Hunchback", the "Flying Tank" or the "Flying Infantryman". Its postwar
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_NATO_reporting_names_for_bombers" title="List of NATO reporting names for bombers">
       NATO reporting name
      </a>
      was "
      <b>
       Bark
      </b>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_Russian_p106_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gunston_Russian_p106-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Il-2 aircraft played a crucial role on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(WWII)" title="Eastern Front (WWII)">
       Eastern Front
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">
       Joseph Stalin
      </a>
      paid the Il-2 a great tribute in his own inimitable manner: when a particular production factory fell behind on its deliveries, Stalin sent an angrily-worded cable to the factory manager, stating "They are as essential to the Red Army as air and bread."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stalinquote_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-stalinquote-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "I demand more machines. This is my final warning!"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Il-2-Krumovo,_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Il-2-Krumovo%2C_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG/220px-Il-2-Krumovo%2C_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Il-2-Krumovo%2C_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG/330px-Il-2-Krumovo%2C_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Il-2-Krumovo%2C_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG/440px-Il-2-Krumovo%2C_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Il-2-Krumovo,_Bulgaria-September_2006.JPG" title="Enlarge">

        </div>
        Il-2M at the National Aviation Museum in Krumovo, Bulgaria
       </div>

     <p>
      The idea for a Soviet armored ground-attack aircraft dates to the early 1930s, when
      <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Pavlovich_Grigorovich" title="Dmitry Pavlovich Grigorovich">
       Dmitry Pavlovich Grigorovich
      </a>
      designed
      <b>
       TSh-1
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       TSh-2
      </b>
      armored biplanes. However, Soviet engines at the time lacked the power needed to provide the heavy aircraft with good performance. Il-2 was designed by
      <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Ilyushin" title="Sergey Ilyushin">
       Sergey Ilyushin
      </a>
      and his team at the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Design_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Central Design Bureau (page does not exist)">
       Central Design Bureau
      </a>
      in 1938.
      <b>
       TsKB-55
      </b>
      was a two-seat aircraft with an armoured shell weighing 700kg (1,540lb), protecting
      <a href="/wiki/Crew" title="Crew">
       crew
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">
       engine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Radiator_(engine_cooling)" title="Radiator (engine cooling)">
       radiators
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">
       fuel tank
      </a>
      . Standing loaded, the Ilyushin weighed more than 4,700kg (10,300lb),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_Russian_p104_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gunston_Russian_p104-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      making the armoured shell about 15% of the aircraft's gross weight. Uniquely for a World War II attack aircraft, and similarly to the forward fuselage design of the World War I-era Imperial German
      <a href="/wiki/Junkers_J.I" title="Junkers J.I">
       Junkers J.I
      </a>
      armored, all-metal biplane, the Il-2's armor was designed as a load-bearing part of the Ilyushin's
      <a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">
       monocoque
      </a>
      structure, thus saving considerable weight. The prototype TsKB-55, which first flew on 2 October 1939,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_Russian_p104_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gunston_Russian_p104-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      won the government competition against
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="Su-6 did not fly at all in 1939, not until 1941 (October 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-6" title="Sukhoi Su-6">
       Sukhoi Su-6
      </a>
      and received
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Force" title="Soviet Air Force">
       VVS
      </a>
      designation
      <b>
       BSh-2
      </b>
      . The prototypes - TsKB-55 and TskB-57 - were built at Moscow plant #39, at that time the Ilyushin design bureau's base.
     </p>
     <p>
      The BSh-2 was overweight and underpowered, with the original
      <a href="/wiki/Mikulin_AM-35" title="Mikulin AM-35">
       Mikulin AM-35
      </a>
      1,022kW (1,370hp) engine designed to give its greatest power outputs at high altitude. Because of this it was redesigned as the
      <b>
       TsKB-57
      </b>
      , a lighter single-seat design, with the more powerful 1,254kW (1,680hp)
      <a href="/wiki/Mikulin_AM-38" title="Mikulin AM-38">
       Mikulin AM-38
      </a>
      engine, a development of the AM-35 optimised for low level operation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilyusha_p2_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ilyusha_p2-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The TsKB-57 first flew on 12 October 1940.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_Russian_p104_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gunston_Russian_p104-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The production aircraft passed State Acceptance Trials in March 1941, and was redesignated
      <b>
       Il-2
      </b>
      in April.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_Russian_p105-6_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gunston_Russian_p105-6-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Deliveries to operational units commenced in May 1941.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilyusha_p._3_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ilyusha_p._3-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 23mm armament of Il-2 was subject to a competition. One of the first 1940 photographs of the Il-2 show it equipped with two MP-6 guns developed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jacob_Taubin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jacob Taubin (page does not exist)">
       Jacob Taubin
      </a>
      (
      <a class="extiw" href="//ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A2%D0%B0%D1%83%D0%B1%D0%B8%D0%BD,_%D0%AF%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B2_%D0%93%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%B3%D0%BE%D1%80%D1%8C%D0%B5%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" title="ru:,  ">
        
      </a>
      ) at OKB-16. The MP-6 gun weighed 70kg and developed an initial muzzle velocity of 900m/s. It operated on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_recoil" title="Short recoil">
       short recoil
      </a>
      principle and had a rate of fire of about 600 rpm. (The development of the MP-6 gun can be traced back to 1937. The initial version was tested in the spring of 1940 on a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bf_110" title="Bf 110">
       Bf 110
      </a>
      bought from Germany, because there was no suitable Soviet aircraft on which to mount it. In the summer of 1940 it was tested on the
      <a href="/wiki/Pashinin_I-21" title="Pashinin I-21">
       Pashinin I-21
      </a>
      .)
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Factory trials of the MP-6 gun on the Il-2 were conducted in August 1940. In the early Il-2 prototypes, these guns were fed by 81-round clips. In flight, these clips sometimes became dislodged because of their large surface, which caused them to experience significant aerodynamic pressure.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Competitive tests were conducted in the spring of 1941 between the MP-6 gun modified to belt-fed and the newly developed, gas-operated
      <a href="/wiki/Volkov-Yartsev_VYa-23" title="Volkov-Yartsev VYa-23">
       Volkov-Yartsev VYa-23
      </a>
      , which had otherwise rather similar characteristics. The VYa-23 was declared winner at this trial. Subsequently, in May 1941, development of the MP-6 gun was terminated and Taubin was arrested
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shirokorad_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Shirokorad-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and summarily executed in October that year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_description">
       Technical description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technical description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Il-2 is a single-engine, propeller-driven, low-wing monoplane of mixed construction with a crew of two (one in early versions), specially designed for assault operations. Its most notable feature was the inclusion of armor in an airframe load-bearing scheme. Armor plates replaced the frame and paneling throughout the nacelle and middle part of the fuselage, and an armored hull made of riveted homogeneous armor steel AB-1 (AB-2) secured the aircrafts engine, cockpit, water and oil radiators, and fuel tanks.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In early 1941, the Il-2 was ordered into production at four factories, and was eventually produced in vast quantities, becoming the single most widely produced military aircraft in aviation history, but by the time
      <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">
       Nazi Germany
      </a>
      invaded the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      on 22 June 1941, only
      <a href="/wiki/Voronezh_Aircraft_Production_Association" title="Voronezh Aircraft Production Association">
       State Aviation Factory 18
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Voronezh" title="Voronezh">
       Voronezh
      </a>
      and Factory 381 at Leningrad had commenced production, with 249 having been built by the time of the German attack.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilyusha_p._3_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ilyusha_p._3-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OKB_p18_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OKB_p18-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Production early in the war was slow because after the German invasion the aircraft factories near Moscow and other major cities in western Russia had to be moved east of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">
       Ural Mountains
      </a>
      . Ilyushin and his engineers had time to reconsider production methods, and two months after the move Il-2s were again being produced. The tempo was not to
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">
       Premier Stalin
      </a>
      's liking, however, and he issued the following telegram to Shenkman and Tretyakov:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       You have let down our country and our Red Army. You have the nerve not to manufacture IL-2s until now. Our Red Army now needs IL-2 aircraft like the air it breathes, like the bread it eats. Shenkman produces one IL-2 a day and Tretyakov builds one or two
       <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-3" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-3">
        MiG-3s
       </a>
       daily. It is a mockery of our country and the Red Army. I ask you not to try the government's patience, and demand that you manufacture more ILs. This is my final warning.
       <p style="text-align: right;">
         Stalin
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stalinquote_5-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-stalinquote-5">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          5
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      As a result, "the production of Shturmoviks rapidly gained speed. Stalin's notion of the Il-2 being 'like bread' to the Red Army took hold in Ilyushin's aircraft plants and the army soon had their Shturmoviks available in quantity."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (June 2015)">
          unreliable source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">
       Operational history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG/220px-Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG/330px-Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG/440px-Ilyushin_Il-2_Warsaw_1.JPG 2x" width="220"/>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_Polish_Army" title="Museum of the Polish Army">

        in

       </div>

     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_use_and_operational_confusion">
       Initial use and operational confusion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Initial use and operational confusion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first use in action of the Il-2 was with the 4th ShAP (Ground Attack Regiment) over the
      <a href="/wiki/Berezina_River" title="Berezina River">
       Berezina River
      </a>
      days after the invasion began.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OKB_p38_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OKB_p38-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The aircraft was so new that the pilots had no training in flight characteristics or tactics, and the ground crew no training in servicing or re-arming. The training received only enabled the pilots to take-off and land; none of the pilots had fired the armament, let alone learned tactics.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      There were 249 Il-2s available on 22 June 1941. In the first three days, 4th ShAP had lost 10 Il-2s to enemy action, a further 19 were lost to other causes, and 20 pilots were killed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 10 July, 4th ShAP was down to 10 aircraft from a strength of 65.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="New_tactics">
       New tactics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: New tactics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Tactics improved as Soviet aircrews became used to the Il-2's strengths. Instead of a low horizontal straight approach at 50 metres altitude, the target was usually kept to the pilot's left and a turn and shallow dive of 30 degrees was utilized, using an
      <a href="/wiki/Echelon_formation" title="Echelon formation">
       echeloned
      </a>
      assault by four to twelve aircraft at a time.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Although the Il-2's RS-82 and RS-132 rockets could destroy armored vehicles with a single hit, they were so inaccurate that experienced Il-2 pilots mainly utilized the cannon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another potent weapon of the Il-2s was the
      <a href="/wiki/PTAB_(bomb)" title="PTAB (bomb)">
       PTAB
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Shaped_charge" title="Shaped charge">
       shaped charge
      </a>
      bomblets (
      <i>
       protivotankovaya aviabomba
      </i>
      , "anti-tank aviation bomb"). They were designated PTAB-2.5-1.5, as they had the size of a 2.5kg (5.5lb) bomb, but weighed only 1.5kg (3.3lb) due to the empty space in the shaped charge. Up to 192 were carried in four external dispensers (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_bomb" title="Cluster bomb">
       cluster bombs
      </a>
      ) or up to 220 in the inner wing panels' internal ventral weapon bays. The HEAT charge could easily penetrate the relatively thin upper armor of all heavy German tanks. PTABs were first used on a large scale in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kursk" title="Battle of Kursk">
       Battle of Kursk
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Thereafter, the Il-2 was widely deployed on the Eastern Front. The aircraft could fly in low light conditions and carried weaponry capable of defeating the thick armor of the
      <a href="/wiki/Panther_tank" title="Panther tank">
       Panther
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tiger_I" title="Tiger I">
       Tiger I
      </a>
      tanks.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effectiveness_as_attack_aircraft">
       Effectiveness as attack aircraft
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effectiveness as attack aircraft">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="414" data-file-width="600" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg/220px-RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg/330px-RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg/440px-RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RIAN_archive_225_IL-2_attacking.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Soviet Il-2 planes attacking a German column during the
        <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kursk" title="Battle of Kursk">
         battle of Kursk
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The true capabilities of the Il-2 are difficult to determine from existing documentary evidence. W. Liss in
      <i>
       Aircraft profile 88: Ilyushin Il-2
      </i>
      mentions an engagement during the Battle of Kursk on 7 July 1943, in which 70 tanks from the German
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9th_Panzer_Division" title="9th Panzer Division">
       9th Panzer Division
      </a>
      were claimed to be destroyed by Ilyushin Il-2s in just 20 minutes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liss_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Liss-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In another report of the action on the same day, a Soviet staff publication states that:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Ground forces highly valued the work of aviation on the battlefield. In a number of instances enemy attacks were thwarted thanks to our air operations. Thus on 7 July enemy tank attacks were disrupted in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kashara" title="Kashara">
        Kashara
       </a>
       region (
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soviet_Thirteenth_Army&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soviet Thirteenth Army (page does not exist)">
        13th Army
       </a>
       ). Here our assault aircraft delivered three powerful attacks in groups of 20-30, which resulted in the destruction and disabling of 34 tanks. The enemy was forced to halt further attacks and to withdraw the remnants of his force north of Kashara.
      </p>
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       Glantz and Orenstein 1999, p. 260.
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In the Battle of Kursk, General V. Ryazanov became a master in the use of attack aircraft
      <i>
       en masse
      </i>
      , developing and improving the tactics of Il-2 operations in co-ordination with infantry, artillery and armoured troops. Ryazanov was later awarded the
      <a href="/wiki/Gold_Star" title="Gold Star">
       Gold Star
      </a>
      of Hero of Soviet Union twice, and the 1st Attack Aircraft Corps under his command became the first unit to be awarded the honorific title of Guards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._296._21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._296.-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1943, one loss corresponded to 26 Sturmovik sorties. About half of those lost were shot down by fighters, the rest falling to anti-aircraft fire.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._296._21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._296.-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The main problem with the Il-2 was the inaccuracy of its attacks.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Towards the end of war the Soviets were able to concentrate massive numbers of Sturmoviks to support their main offensives. However, particularly against dug-in and armored targets, the effect was often more psychological than actual physical destruction of targets.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In the 9 June offensive in the
      <a href="/wiki/Karelian_Isthmus" title="Karelian Isthmus">
       Karelian Isthmus
      </a>
      in Finland, the Finnish AA forces were far too few in numbers to counter the armadas of Pe-2 and Il-2, but they quickly found that the Il-2 attacks generally missed their marks widely, particularly with bombs.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      While some attacks against large unprotected targets such as horse and truck convoys and railyards had devastating results, attacks against dug-in point targets were usually ineffective.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The frequent duels between dug-in 20 and 40mm AA guns and Il-2 attackers never resulted in the complete destruction of the gun, while many Il-2s were brought down in these attacks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biaudet_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-biaudet-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The heavy armor of the Il-2 also meant that it would typically carry only comparatively light bomb-loads, which together with the poor accuracy of its attacks made it a far less deadly attack aircraft than the contemporary Allied fighter-bombers such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_P-47_Thunderbolt" title="Republic P-47 Thunderbolt">
       Republic P-47 Thunderbolt
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Typhoon" title="Hawker Typhoon">
       Hawker Typhoon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The rocket projectiles especially were not effective, even the larger
      <a href="/wiki/RS-82_(rocket_family)" title="RS-82 (rocket family)">
       RS-132
      </a>
      (of which four were carried) having a warhead with only 0.9kg (2.0lb) of explosives, which compared poorly with the P-47's typical load of ten 5 inch (13cm)
      <a href="/wiki/High_Velocity_Aircraft_Rocket" title="High Velocity Aircraft Rocket">
       HVARs
      </a>
      with each having a 21kg (46lb) warhead, or the 8 to 12 "60lb" (27kg) warheads of the Hawker Typhoon's
      <a href="/wiki/RP-3" title="RP-3">
       RP-3
      </a>
      rockets.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Likewise the Shturmovik's bombs were usually only 50kg (rarely 100kg), too small to compensate for the typically wide variation from target point.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      To compensate for the poor accuracy of the Il-2's bombsight, in 1943 the Soviet Command decided to use shaped-charge armor-piercing projectiles against enemy armored vehicles, and the
      <a href="/wiki/PTAB_(bomb)" title="PTAB (bomb)">
       PTAB-2.5-1.5 SCAP
      </a>
      aircraft bomb was put into production. These small-calibre bombs were loaded directly into the bomb bays and were dropped onto enemy vehicles from altitudes up to 100 meters (328ft). As each Il-2 could carry up to 192 bombs, a
      <i>
       fire carpet
      </i>
      70 meters (229ft.) long and 15 meters (49ft) wide covered the enemy tanks, giving a high "kill" probability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._296._21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._296.-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pilots of 291st ShAP were the first to use the PTAB-2.5-1.5 bombs. During one sortie on 5 June 1943, six attack aircraft led by Lt. Col. A. Vitrook destroyed 15 enemy tanks in one attack, and during five days of the enemy advance the 291st Division claimed to have destroyed or damaged 422 enemy tanks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_pp._295-296._23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_pp._295-296.-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22The_Flying_tank.22">
       "The Flying tank"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title='Edit section: "The Flying tank"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Thanks to the heavy armor protection, the Il-2 could take a great deal of punishment and proved difficult for both ground and aircraft fire to shoot down.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      One Il-2 in particular was reported to have returned safely to base despite receiving more than 600 direct hits and having all its control surfaces completely shredded as well as numerous holes in its main armor and other structural damage.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Some enemy pilots favored aiming down into the cockpit and wing roots in diving attacks on the slow, low-flying Il-2 formations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
         <span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (June 2015)">
          unreliable source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Several
      <i>
       Luftwaffe
      </i>
      aces claimed to attack while climbing from behind, out of view of the rear gunner, aiming for the Il-2's non-retractable oil cooler.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      This has been disputed by some Il-2 pilots in postwar interviews,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      since Il-2s typically flew very close to the ground (cruise altitudes below 50m (160ft) were common) and the radiator protruded a mere 10cm (4in) from the aircraft.
     </p>
     <p>
      A major threat to the Il-2 was German ground fire. In postwar interviews, Il-2 pilots reported 20mm (0.79in) and 37mm (1.46in) artillery as the primary threat.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      While the fabled 88mm (3.46in) calibre gun was formidable, low-flying Il-2s presented too fast-moving a target for the 88's relatively low rate of fire, and while occasional hits were scored Soviet pilots apparently did not treat the 88 with the same respect as high-altitude Western heavy bomber crews.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Similarly the attempts in Finland during summer '44 to augment the small numbers of 20/40mm AA in the field army by heavier 76mm guns drawn from homeland defence proved also relatively ineffective and few Il-2s were downed despite attempting different tactics with time-fused fragmentation, contact-fused, and shrapnel ammunition: the heavy guns simply lacked the reaction times to take advantage of the brief firing opportunities presented by the low-altitude Il-2 attacks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biaudet_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-biaudet-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Single-barrel 20mm guns were also found somewhat inadequate due to limited firepower: one or two shells were often not enough to destroy the Il-2, and unless the Il-2 was attacking the gun itself, thus presenting effectively a stationary target, scoring more hits during a firing opportunity was rare.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biaudet_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-biaudet-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (June 2015)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      However, a single hit from a 37mm AA gun was usually enough to bring down an Il-2. Soviet
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_artillery" title="Anti-aircraft artillery">
       anti-aircraft artillery
      </a>
      also frequently mistook it for German aircraft, often with lethal consequences.
     </p>
     <p>
      The armored tub, ranging from 512mm (0.2-0.5in) in thickness and enveloping the engine and the cockpit, could deflect all small arms fire and glancing blows from larger-caliber ammunition. There are reports of the armored windscreen surviving direct hits from 20mm (0.79in) rounds. Unfortunately, the rear gunners did not have the benefit of all-around armor protection, especially from the rear and to the sides and suffered about four times more casualties than the pilots. Added casualties resulted from the Soviet policy of not returning home with unused ammunition which typically resulted in repeated passes on the target.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rastrenin_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rastrenin-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2010)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Soviet troops often requested additional passes even after the aircraft were out of ammunition to exploit the intimidating effect Il-2s had on German ground troops, who gave it the nickname "The Flying Tank".
      <i>
       Luftwaffe
      </i>
      pilots called it
      <i>
       Eiserner Gustav
      </i>
      (Iron Gustav)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
         <span title="WP:CIRC (June 2015)">
          unreliable source?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      or the
      <i>
       Zementbomber
      </i>
      (Concrete bomber).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Finnish nickname
      <i>
       maatalouskone
      </i>
      ("agricultural machine" or "tractor") derived from a word play with
      <i>
       maataistelukone
      </i>
      (ground attack aircraft, literally "ground combat aircraft" where
      <i>
       kone
      </i>
      , literally "machine", in turn is shortened from
      <i>
       lentokone
      </i>
      , aircraft, literally "flying machine")
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rear_gunner">
       Rear gunner
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rear gunner">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Il-2_cabin.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="800" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Il-2_cabin.jpg/220px-Il-2_cabin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Il-2_cabin.jpg/330px-Il-2_cabin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Il-2_cabin.jpg/440px-Il-2_cabin.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Il-2_cabin.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Il-2M cockpit.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_Aviation_in_Belgrade" title="Museum of Aviation in Belgrade">
         Museum of Aviation in Belgrade
        </a>
        , Serbia
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Heavy losses to enemy fighters forced the reintroduction of a rear gunner; early Il-2s were field modified by cutting a hole in the fuselage behind the cockpit for a gunner sitting on a canvas sling armed with a 12.7mm UBT machine gun in an improvised mounting. The semi-turret gun mount allowed the machine gun to be fired at angles of up to 35 upwards, 35 to starboard and 15 to port. Tests showed that maximum speed decreased by between 10 and 20km/h (6.212.4mph) and that the two-seater was more difficult to handle because the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_of_gravity" title="Center of gravity">
       center of gravity
      </a>
      was shifted backwards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._293._28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._293.-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the beginning of March 1942, a production two-seat Il-2 with the new gunner's cockpit began manufacturer tests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._293._28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._293.-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The second cockpit and armament increased all-up weight by 170kg (374lb) so the flaps were allowed to be deployed at an angle of 17 to avoid an over-long takeoff run. The new variant had a lengthened fuselage compartment with an extended canopy offering some protection from the elements. Unlike the well-armoured cockpit of the pilot compartment with steel plating up to 12mm (0.47in) thick behind, beneath and on both sides as well as up to 65mm thick glass sections, the rear gunner was provided with 6mm (.23 in) thick armour, only effective against rifle-calibre rounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._293._28-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._293.-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To improve performance, the Mikulin Design Bureau started work on an uprated AM-38 engine. The new engines produced 1,700hp at takeoff and 1,500hp at 750 meters. They gave an improved takeoff and low-altitude performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._293._28-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._293.-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 30 October 1942, production Il-2s powered by AM38s were used on the Central Front for the first time when they successfully attacked
      <a href="/wiki/Smolensk" title="Smolensk">
       Smolensk
      </a>
      airfield occupied by Germans. The
      <i>
       Shturmovik
      </i>
      rear guns proved to be effective against hostile fighters, and during the service trials alone, gunners shot down seven Bf 109s and repulsed many attacks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._294._29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._294.-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In January 1943 two-seat attack aircraft powered by uprated AM-38F engines (
      <i>
       Forseerovannyy
      </i>
      - uprated) began to arrive at front line units.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nonetheless, the death rate among the air gunners remained exceptionally high and not until late models produced after 1944, was the 13mm (.5 in) rear plate of the armour shell moved rearwards into the (wooden) rear fuselage to allow a gunner to sit behind the fuel tank.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The armour did not extend to the rear or below although side armour panels were riveted to the rear armour plate to protect the ammunition tank for the UBT machine gun, providing some measure of protection. The modifications including adding the rear gunner and gun had added weight behind the center of gravity, resulting in "marginal" stability and handling characteristics that were "barely acceptable". The need to shift the
      <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_center" title="Aerodynamic center">
       aerodynamic center
      </a>
      of the aircraft rearwards due to the weight of the added rear gunner and lengthened cockpit was the reason for the swept back outer wings in later Il-2s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Air-to-air_combat">
       Air-to-air combat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Air-to-air combat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Owing to a shortage of fighters, in 19411942, Il-2s were occasionally used as fighters. While outclassed by dedicated fighters such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Bf_109" title="Messerschmitt Bf 109">
       Messerschmitt Bf 109
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Focke-Wulf_Fw_190" title="Focke-Wulf Fw 190">
       Focke-Wulf Fw 190
      </a>
      , in dogfights, the Il-2 could take on other
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">
        Luftwaffe
       </a>
      </i>
      aircraft with some success.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      German front line units equipped with the
      <a href="/wiki/Henschel_Hs_126" title="Henschel Hs 126">
       Henschel Hs 126
      </a>
      suffered most of all from the ravages of Il-2s. Il-2 pilots also often attacked close formations of
      <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_87" title="Junkers Ju 87">
       Junkers Ju 87s
      </a>
      , as the 7.92mm machine guns of the
      <i>
       Ju 87 Stukas
      </i>
      were ineffective against the heavily armoured
      <i>
       Shturmoviks
      </i>
      . In the winter of 19411942, Il-2s were used against
      <i>
       Luftwaffe
      </i>
      transport aircraft, and became the most dangerous opponent of the
      <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_52" title="Junkers Ju 52">
       Junkers Ju-52/3m
      </a>
      . Pilots of 33rd GvShAP were the most successful in these operations, but other successful units were those operating near Stalingrad. Their targets were not only Ju 52s but also Heinkel He 111 and
      <a href="/wiki/Focke-Wulf_Fw_200_Condor" title="Focke-Wulf Fw 200 Condor">
       Focke-Wulf Fw 200 Condor
      </a>
      bombers, delivering supplies to the besieged German troops.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_p._297._31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon_p._297.-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While the Il-2 was a deadly air-to-ground weapon, and even a fairly effective interceptor against slow bombers and transport aircraft, heavy losses resulted from its vulnerability to fighter attack. Losses were very high, the highest of all types of Soviet aircraft, though given the numbers in service this is only to be expected.
      <i>
       Shturmovik
      </i>
      losses (including Il-10 type), in 1941-1945, were of 10,762 aircraft (533 in 1941, 1,676 in 1942, 3,515 in 1943, 3,347 in 1944 and 1,691 in 1945).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bergstr.C3.B6m_p.132_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bergstr.C3.B6m_p.132-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The main defensive tactic was flying low and power down as the fighters closed in to let the fighter overshoot and fly into the Il-2's firing range.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rastrenin_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rastrenin-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

To his surprise, a German Bf 109 landed near the crash site and the pilot began to scrounge around the wrecked Il-2 possibly to assist Kuznetsov or to look for souvenirs. Thinking quickly, Kuznetsov ran to the German fighter and used it to fly home, barely avoiding being shot down by Soviet fighters in the process.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">
       Variants
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ilyushin_Il-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Variants">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The early two-seater prototype proved to be too heavy for the limited power of the early AM-35 engine. A redesigned single seat version was soon developed and saw combat, particularly in the early phase of the war in the Soviet Union. While the Il-2 proved to be a deadly air-to-ground weapon, heavy losses were caused by its vulnerability to fighter attack. Consequently, in February 1942, the two-seat design was revived. The
      <b>
       Il-2M
      </b>
      , with a rear gunner under the stretched canopy, entered service in September 1942 with the surviving single-seaters eventually modified to this standard. Later changes included an upgrade from 20mm to 23mm or 37mm cannons,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic">
       aerodynamic
      </a>
      improvements, use of wooden outer wing panels instead of metal and increased fuel capacity. In 1943, the
      <b>
       Il-2 Type 3
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Il-2m3
      </b>
      came out with redesigned "arrow-wings" that possessed leading edges that were swept back 15 degrees on the outer panels, and nearly straight trailing edges, resulting in a wing planform somewhat like the
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_T-6_Texan" title="North American T-6 Texan">
       AT-6
      </a>
      trainer. Performance and handling were much improved from the resulting shift of the Il-2's
      <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_center" title="Aerodynamic center">
       aerodynamic center
      </a>
      rearwards with the revised "arrow wing" planform to correct the earlier problem, and this became the most common version of the Il-2. A radial engine powered variant of the Il-2 with the
      <a href="/wiki/Shvetsov_ASh-82" title="Shvetsov ASh-82">
       Shvetsov ASh-82
      </a>
      engine was proposed in 1942 to remedy projected shortages in the Mikulin inline engines. However, the ASh-82 was also used in the new
      <a href="/wiki/Lavochkin_La-5" title="Lavochkin La-5">
       Lavochkin La-5
      </a>
      fighter which effectively secured all available engines to the Lavochkin bureau. The radial engine
      <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-2" title="Sukhoi Su-2">
       Sukhoi Su-2
      </a>
      ground attack aircraft was produced in small quantities, but was generally considered unsuitable due to inadequate performance and lack of defensive armament.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rastrenin_25-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rastrenin-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       TsKB-55
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Two-seat prototype, AM-35 engine, first flight on 2 October 1939.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       BSh-2
      </dt>
      <dd>
       VVS designation for TsKB-55 prototype.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       TsKB-57
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Single-seat prototype, AM-38 engine, first flight on 12 October 1940.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2 (TsKB-57P)
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Single-seat serial aircraft, AM-38 engine, first flight on 29 December 1940, some delivered to combat units in MayJune 1941. Renamed the Il-2 in April 1941. Cannons 20mm
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ShVAK" title="ShVAK">
        ShVAK
       </a>
       or 23mm
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VYa-23" title="VYa-23">
        VYa-23
       </a>
       (depending on which factory the Il-2 was manufactured in).
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2 two seat
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Two-seat version, AM-38 engine, first action on 30 October 1942 near Stalingrad. Maximum bomb load reduced from 600kg to 400kg. Used on edges of flight formations for defense against German fighters. Quickly replaced by the "Il-2 production of 1943".
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2 production of 1943
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Referred in the west as the "Il-2M". Powered by an upgraded AM-38F engine. Delivered to the front units from early 1943. In 1943, the 20mm ShVAK armed Il-2s faded out, leaving only the 23mm VYa variant.
      </dd>
     </dl>

     <dl>
      <dt>
       Il-2 with NS-37
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Referred in the west as the "Il-2 Type 3M". Based on the two seat Il-2, armed with
       <a href="/wiki/Nudelman-Suranov_NS-37" title="Nudelman-Suranov NS-37">
        Nudelman-Suranov NS-37
       </a>
       in conformal gun pods under the wings, instead of the 20/23mm cannons, this version is an attempt to create an anti-tank aircraft, first used in combat during the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kursk" title="Battle of Kursk">
        Battle of Kursk
       </a>
       . However, the combat effectiveness was quite low and production of the variant was limited to about 3,500. Moreover, bomb load was decreased from 600kg to 200kg. It was replaced by the conventional Il-2 attackers armed with cassettes with cumulative bomblets.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2 production 1944 "wing with arrow"
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Referred in West as "Il-2M3" or "Il-2 Type 3". As more
       <a href="/wiki/Duralumin" title="Duralumin">
        duralumin
       </a>
       became available for the Soviet aviation industry, the Il-2 received a set of all-metal wing panels. At the same time, the outer wing planform was swept back, with a straight trailing edge, since the centre of gravity was shifted rearwards after the gunner was added. The wing planform change regained controllability of the two-seat Il-2 back to level of the single-seat Il-2.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2U
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Training version, also known as
       <b>
        UIl-2
       </b>
       .
      </dd>

      </dt>
      <dd>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VYa-23" title="VYa-23">
        VYa-23
       </a>
       cannons removed to save weight, it was able to carry a single 45cm (18in) torpedo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gunston_Russian_p106_4-1">

         </span>

         <span>
          [
         </span>
         36
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Evidently, it was only a design as the 23rd Attack Air Regiment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea_Fleet" title="Black Sea Fleet">
        Black Sea Fleet
       </a>
       flew regular Il-2M-3s fitted with torpedo racks as a field modification, and that no such aircraft were ever noted in the battle sortie logs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.Morozov_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-M.Morozov-37">
         <span>

         </span>
         37
         <span>
          ]
         </span>

       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2I
      </dt>
      <dd>
       Armoured fighter, prototype only. Concept based on several dogfights between Il-2 and
       <i>
        Luftwaffe
       </i>
       bombers. Proved infeasible due to its low speed which causes it to only be able to intercept older
       <i>
        Luftwaffe
       </i>
       bombers.
      </dd>
      <dt>
       Il-2 with M-82
      </dt>
      <dd>
       A backup project prepared while plants producing AM-35/AM-38 were evacuated. Trials demonstrated that with the fighter engine, low-altitude performance and controllability were unacceptable.
      </dd>
