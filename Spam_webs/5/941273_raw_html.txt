         <h1 class="blog-post-title">
          WebRTC: a new game-changer, disrupting Telcos and OTTs
         </h1>
         <div class="blog-post-meta">
          <span class="blog-post-author-name">
           By
           <a class="url fn" href="http://www.visionmobile.com/blog/author/tsahi/" rel="author" title="Posts by Tsahi Levent-Levi">
            Tsahi Levent-Levi
           </a>
          </span>
          <time class="pure-visible-phone blog-post-date-alt" datetime="2001-05-15">
           on Sep 03, 2012
          </time>
          <ul class="blog-post-author-links">
           <li>
            <a class="blog-post-author-email" href="mailto:tsahil@radvision.com" target="_blank">
             <i class="fa fa-envelope">

             E-mail
            </a>
           </li>
           <li style="position:relative; top:7px">

        <div class="blog-post-body entry">
         <p>
          [Championed by Google, WebRTC allows browsers to make calls from your PC or phone  and its disupting both telcos and incumbent VoIP players, from Skype to Viber. Guest author Tsahi Levent-Levi discusses Googles intentions and the trouble ahead for both telcos and OTT players.]
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-3614 alignnone" height="498" src="http://www.visionmobile.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/HiRes.jpg" title="modern cell phone" width="500"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Its been a tough couple of years for carriers (a.k.a. network operators) who have been fighting off competition from over-the-top (OTT) players such as Skype and WhatsApp, offering services such as voice and SMS over the carriers own networks. The impact of these OTT players has been astonishing  whether theyre nimble startups like Viber (with
          <a href="http://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20120724005360/en/Viber-Set-Surge-Text-Messages-Release-Group" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20120724005360/en/Viber-Set-Surge-Text-Messages-Release-Group', 'more than 90 million users']);" target="_blank">
           more than 90 million users
          </a>
          , making over 1.5 billion calls a month and sending over 2 billion text messages), or large corporations such as Apple, whose iMessage reaches 140 million users, sending 1 billion iMessages every day.
          <span id="more-3613">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          But now an even bigger challenge has appeared on the scene:
          <a href="http://bloggeek.me/webrtc-voip/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://bloggeek.me/webrtc-voip/', 'WebRTC']);" target="_blank">
           WebRTC
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          WebRTC is
          <strong>
           a technology that allows developers
          </strong>
          <strong>
           to build real-time communication into web pages
          </strong>
          . And its not just going to affect the carriers  its the OTT vendors who now face a real danger because WebRTC brings down the subscription walls of different OTT players.
         </p>
         <p>
          Right now, when it comes to OTT services, if I want to communicate with someone in real time, theres no way to do so without installing a piece of software  plus, you cant connect across services, e.g. call a Skype user from Viber.
         </p>
         <h3>
          WebRTC is going to change everything
         </h3>
         <p>
          How? It places the ability to use VoIP applications within any browser as its going to be part of the HTML5 standard. You wont need a Skype ID, phone number, email address etc.  it will all take place through the browser, you wont need to subscribe to any service, and youll have Google to thank for it.
         </p>
         <p>
          Google bought Global IP Solutions (GIPS), which provided and licensed voice and video media engines to anyone who wanted to develop a VoIP application (including Yahoo and Skype), reducing the effort considerably by offering the real-time multimedia parts of the application out of the box. Google didnt stop at acquiring the technology  it is now using it to commoditize its competition in the communication space and drive browser sophistication even further.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Heres what Google did
         </h3>
         <p>
          <strong>
            Google created WebRTC by wrapping GIPS up with a set of Java Script APIs targeted at web browser developers
          </strong>
          , which means opening up VoIP technology to millions of developers.
          <br/>
          <strong>
            Google open-sourced WebRTC, under a permissive BSD license
          </strong>
           this made the technology available to reuse, modify and create derivatives; taking it out of the control of real-time media engineers and marginalized competitors like Spirit-DSP
          <br/>
          <strong>
            It took the technology to W3C and IETF standards bodies for standardization
          </strong>
          to make sure it gets adopted and become an ubiquitous and common component in the browser, and in the process, removing any Google-centric connotations from the technology
          <br/>
          <strong>
            It ignored thesignallinglayer
          </strong>
          , allowing vendors to use WebRTC in
          <em>
           any
          </em>
          real time communication settings, regardless of the protocol used forsignallingcall setup
         </p>
         <h3>
          The strategy behind Googles decision
         </h3>
         <p>
          This is a classic economics of complements strategy that is
          <a href="http://www.visionmobile.com/blog/2011/11/flatten-expand-mine-the-three-pillars-of-googles-strategy/" target="_blank">
           commonly used by Google
          </a>
          and its about to change the entire landscape of communication services for both carriers and OTT players.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           WebRTC is all about real-time communication from within the web browser
          </strong>
          , and its a crucial part of Googles strategy because it reduces the barriers of developing rich communication applications by having legions of web developers exposed to WebRTC as a free technology. These web developers will take voice and video services into new domains with new use cases, expanding the richness of communication and
          <strong>
           making it easier than ever before to start your own VoIP service using WebRTC.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          For Google, this decision is simply about strengthening the Web and the web browser to reduce the gap between native application capabilities, whether theyre on the desktop or in the mobile realm. The real value for Google lies in allowing them to serve more ads and mine more insights out of peoples browser behavior  these are things that Google treasures. Such a move can weaken Microsoft along with its Skype acquisition and hurt Apples FaceTime service.
         </p>
         <h3>
          The usual OTT business model
         </h3>
         <p>
          OTT vendors base their strategy around reaching as many users as possible, offering them a compelling free service, locking them into it and then trying to monetize it via four main approaches:
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Advertising, done by ooVoo, Skype and others
          </li>
          <li>
           Connectivity to PSTN (Skype make most of their money out of connectivity to PSTN and the carriers phone numbering scheme)
          </li>
          <li>
           Value-added services, such as multipoint video calling; (done by ooVoo)
          </li>
          <li>
           Cashing out upon acquisition (which is what Viber is hoping to do)
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
          OTT vendors make their money out of mass usage of their system, and for that, they prefer having users work within the boundaries of their service, and not letting them interact with competing OTT offerings: (Just try calling from Viber to Skype. You cant.)
         </p>
         <h3>
          Goodbye, walls  hello to a new way of communicating
         </h3>
         <p>
          WebRTC literally tears down the siloed walls of OTT vendors by removing the need for a physical client for each OTT vendor and for an OTT user ID (such as your skype ID or email address). Since there is no specific signaling, each vendor can decide whether (and how) to use user IDs.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://blogs.amdocs.com/voices/2012/09/03/what-you-haven%e2%80%99t-heard-of-webrtc-yet-you-will%e2%80%a6/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://blogs.amdocs.com/voices/2012/09/03/what-you-haven%e2%80%99t-heard-of-webrtc-yet-you-will%e2%80%a6/', 'It will change the way we communicate']);" target="_blank">
           <strong>
            It will change the way we communicate
           </strong>
          </a>
          , for example:
         </p>
         <p>
           Think of a local insurance agent in Paris looking to lure new customers: he sets up a website, invests in AdWords to bring leads into his sales funnel, and then routes these leads to a contact page  or a phone number. With WebRTC, he can close the loop and have the person at home access his website and contact him directly from the web browser  to wherever the insurance agent is. No OTT vendor required.
          <br/>
           Or a niche social network website for backpackers, trying to connect people planning a trip with one another. They wont need to exchange user IDs or phone numbers, or install anything  with a click of a button they get connected through the social network website itself.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are already startup companies offering services using WebRTC. These include
          <a href="http://bistri.com/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://bistri.com/', 'Bistri']);" target="_blank">
           Bistri
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://www.cloudeo.tv/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.cloudeo.tv/', 'Cloudeo']);" target="_blank">
           Cloudeo
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://www.frisb.com/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.frisb.com/', 'FrisB']);" target="_blank">
           FrisB
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://www.tenhands.net/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.tenhands.net/', 'TenHands']);" target="_blank">
           TenHands
          </a>
          and
          <a href="http://tokbox.com/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://tokbox.com/', 'TokBox']);" target="_blank">
           TokBox
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          As with the current web paradigm of signing in for new services using existing social media accounts, many of the new vendors who will adopt WebRTC technology will also opt for that model, removing the need for a unique service ID.
         </p>
         <h3>
          And what about the carriers?
         </h3>
         <p>
          Is WebRTC a threat or an opportunity? Well, its both  it just depends what the carriers do with it.
         </p>
         <p>
          It does mean that carriers face further disruption to their communication services but in parallel, there are also sizeable WebRTC opportunities. However, in order to seize them, carriers will need to embrace the web developer community and deliver value to WebRTC-based applications and services, curving itself a place in this vibrant ecosystem. Web developers are already looking for WebRTC solutions they can stitch and mesh into their applications. Carriers can actually become a vehicle for innovation vehicle by offering:
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
            Session-based charging for WebRTC
          </strong>
          . As with any carrier service, they can charge customers for the WebRTC sessions they make: WebRTC communication passing through the carriers network can be tracked (through DPI and other means) and then charged for, probably against a bucket of minutes/sessions in the customers plan.
          <br/>
          <strong>
            Merging RCS with WebRTC
          </strong>
          . RCS (also known as Joyn), is the carriers instant messaging solution. By adding WebRTC to RCS, it can offer out-of-the-box programmable multimedia capabilities with no need to look into additional protocols such as VoLTE.
          <br/>
          <strong>
            Quality of service assurance
          </strong>
          . Need the police? Other emergency services? A business-related call? A carrier can assure the quality of service for that call and make sure it gets the proper priority over its network (at a cost, of course).
          <br/>
          <strong>
            Infrastructure
          </strong>
          . WebRTC is just a protocol  making a solution out of it requires a lot of additional components, most of which are server-side. A carrier can offer the server-side infrastructure as a service to customers.
          <br/>
          <strong>
            PSTN connectivity
          </strong>
          . Carriers have their own existing voice communication network, along with connectivity to PSTN landline services. They can offer WebRTC termination to PSTN and GSM, bridging the gap between these voice services.
          <br/>
          <strong>
            WebRTC
          </strong>
          <strong>
           signalling
          </strong>
          . WebRTC offers only the media component with no signaling and you still have to
          <em>
           reach
          </em>
          a person via WebRTC (which is where the carrier comes in  it provides the connectivity for the users).
         </p>
         <p>
          As much as they might want to, carriers are never going to be able to return to the golden revenue days before OTT players arrived on the scene, but WebRTC will allow them to stop the trend, (and maybe even reverse it a little bit), depending on how fast and how far theyre going to act. AT&amp;T, T-Mobile, Deutsche Telekom and Orange are all examples of major carriers who have been quick to recognize and start to investigate the opportunities that WebRTC presents. The question is how long will it take for others to follow?
         </p>

      if(typeof checkbox == 'undefined'){
        checkbox = 1;
      } else {
        checkbox = 2;
      }

      // update user interface
      $('#response').html('Adding email address...');

      $.ajax({
       type : "POST",
       url : "http://www.visionmobile.com/wp-admin/admin-ajax.php",
       data : {
        action: "email_to_mailchimp",
        email: cleaned_email,
        both: checkbox
       },
        beforeSend: function(res){
          $('#response').html('Adding you to the list...');
        },
        success: function(res) {
          logGAEvent("Activation", "Subscribed to newsletter", cleaned_email);
          $('#response').html('Success! Please check your email!');
        },
        error: function(res){
          $('#response').html('Error');
        }
      });

      event.preventDefault();
    });
  })(jQuery);
  </script>
