         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Cycling in the new year
         </h1>
         <p class="headline_meta">
          by
          <span class="author vcard">
           <span class="fn">
            Raymond Parker
           </span>
          </span>
          on
          <abbr class="published" title="2010-12-31">
           December 31, 2010
          </abbr>
         </p>
         <p class="headline_meta">
          in
          <span>
           <a href="http://veloweb.ca/category/cycling/" rel="category tag">
            Cycling
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://veloweb.ca/category/cycling/randonneuring/" rel="category tag">
            Randonneuring
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://veloweb.ca/category/cycling/technical/" rel="category tag">
            Technical
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://veloweb.ca/category/cycling/touring/" rel="category tag">
            Touring
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="format_text entry-content">
         <p>
          <a href="http://veloweb.ca/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/zero-odometer.jpg">
           <img alt="A new start" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4207" height="203" src="http://veloweb.ca/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/zero-odometer.jpg" title="zero-odometer" width="445"/>
          </a>
          For me, every new year is a big zero. Tonight, Ill be resetting my bike computers to naught and replacing batterieswhether they need it or not.
         </p>
         <p>
          As seen in the photo above, I still use basic odometers. But why two? Redundancy, my friends. With two computers on board, if I delete a setting halfway through
          <a href="http://veloweb.ca/2008/08/25/my-hat-trick-with-patrick/">
           a 600 km brevet
          </a>
          , Ive still got two other distance meters to turn to.
         </p>
         <p>
          The right-hand unita Cateye Enduro 8has two trip-distance meters and a separate setting that can be calibrated to a different wheel size. This comes in handy if, like me, you havebikes with different sizedwheelssay, a 700C road bike and a mountain bike or
          <a href="http://veloweb.ca/my-bicycles/rivendell-bleriot/">
           650b randonneuse
          </a>
          . On the left, I have an old Cateye CC-AT 100. Its a reliable old beast that includes an aneroid altimeter that Ive found to be very accurate. Both, of course, include total-distance odometers.
         </p>
         <p>
          Together, these computers give me three trip-distance meters. On the Enduro 8, one can be reset mid-ride to check intermediate distances (make sure its not the main one!), while the CC-AT 100 is my failsafe and lets me know how much torture my quads have endured.
         </p>
         <p>
          While many cyclists now rely on expensive GPS systems to consolidate everythingdistance, heart-rate, position, elevation gain, etc.into one package, Im still a believer in at least one simple backup  and a printed route sheet on
          <a href="http://veloweb.ca/garage/make-a-handy-rando-route-sheet-holder/">
           a holder
          </a>
          , for brevets.
         </p>
         <p>
          I dont reject new technology outright (Id be lying if I claimed I wasnt a gear freak) but theres something about analog navigation that forms an integral part of cyclotouring.
         </p>
         <p>
          Tomorrow, well all be cycling into a new year. Hopefully, that means you can hit the road where you are. Even a short spin announces ones intention to achieve new goals, whether thats touring in a new place, qualifying for
          <a href="http://www.paris-brest-paris.org/pbp2011/index2.php?lang=en&amp;cat=accueil&amp;page=edito" target="_blank">
           Paris-Brest-Paris
          </a>
          , or riding further than last year.
         </p>
         <p>
          Some cyclists find it inspiring to log their distance. Its easy to set up a Word or Excel file to record your rides. Additionally, you could submit your rides at sites like
          <a href="http://www.mapmyride.com/" target="_blank">
           MapMyRide
          </a>
          or register annual distances with
          <a href="http://www.ckap.ca/" target="_blank">
           Canadian Kilometre Achiever Program
          </a>
          (my #1297).
         </p>
         <p>
          Still other road warriors eschew all worldly measurements, preferring to compute their miles in smiles. This is certainly a valid calculation. To sum things up: Whatever turns your cranks into 2011, have a very happy new year.
         </p>

           <p>
            GPS alone is a mistake for brevets, but it can be a useful addition to a cue sheet and bike computer. Ideally Im reading the cue sheet and getting confirmation from the GPS.
           </p>
           <p>
            There are times when the GPS fails and you have the cue sheet to rely on, but there are some times when you make an error with the cue sheet or its very hard to read [rain/dark, etc] when the GPS saves the day.
           </p>
           <p>
            Using both gives you a double check, but if in doubt I go with the cue sheet.
           </p>

          <div class="format_text" id="comment-body-11591">
           <p>
            I have a love/hate relationship with technology. I like collecting the full range of data on my rides, BUT, technology has failed me numerous times. I would never ride a brevet using my new fangled computer. I simply couldnt trust it.
           </p>
           <p>
            The last 600K I rode was guided by my ride buddys GPS. Bad decision. It sent us off in the wrong direction, in hilly country, and we were late to our final control. Ive also read quite a bit about the latest generation of high-end cycling computers, and there seems to be a lot of disappointment among distance riders.
           </p>
           <p>
            On rides that matter, I now go back-to-basics. I have an inexpensive Cateye that has never failed me for distance. I really like the redundancy as illustrated above, and I think maps and/or cue sheets are the way to go. Besides, navigation using the old-school methods somehow makes the adventure more authentic. I also have less to worry about on the rideno tech failureswhich seem to be a common occurrence in life these days.
           </p>
           <p>
            Enjoyed your post, Ray. Thanks.
           </p>
           <p>
            LL
