     <p>
      <b>
       Chesney Henry
      </b>
      "
      <b>
       Chet
      </b>
      "
      <b>
       Baker, Jr.
      </b>
      (December 23, 1929  May 13, 1988) was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
       jazz
      </a>
      trumpeter,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flugelhornist" title="Flugelhornist">
       flugelhornist
      </a>
      and vocalist.
     </p>
     <p>
      Baker earned much attention and critical praise through the 1950s, particularly for albums featuring his vocals (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker_Sings" title="Chet Baker Sings">
        Chet Baker Sings
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/(Chet_Baker_Sings)_It_Could_Happen_to_You" title="(Chet Baker Sings) It Could Happen to You">
        It Could Happen to You
       </a>
      </i>
      ). Jazz historian David Gelly described the promise of Baker's early career as "
      <a href="/wiki/James_Dean" title="James Dean">
       James Dean
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">
       Sinatra
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Bix_Beiderbecke" title="Bix Beiderbecke">
       Bix
      </a>
      , rolled into one."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His well-publicized drug habit also drove his notoriety and fame; Baker was in and out of jail frequently before enjoying a career resurgence in the late 1970s and '80s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_days">
       Early days
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early days">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Baker was born and raised in a musical household in
      <a href="/wiki/Yale,_Oklahoma" title="Yale, Oklahoma">
       Yale, Oklahoma
      </a>
      ; his father, Chesney Baker, Sr., was a professional guitar player, and his mother, Vera (ne Moser) was a talented pianist who worked in a perfume factory. His maternal grandmother, Randi Moser, was
      <a href="/wiki/Norwegians" title="Norwegians">
       Norwegian
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Baker began his musical career singing in a church choir. His father introduced him to brass instruments with a
      <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">
       trombone
      </a>
      , which was replaced with a trumpet when the trombone proved too large.
     </p>
     <p>
      Baker received some musical education at Glendale Junior High School, but left school at age 16 in 1946 to join the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
       United States Army
      </a>
      . He was posted to
      <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
       Berlin
      </a>
      , where he joined the 298th Army band. After leaving the army in 1948, he studied theory and harmony at
      <a href="/wiki/El_Camino_College" title="El Camino College">
       El Camino College
      </a>
      in Los Angeles. He dropped out in his second year, however, re-enlisting in the army in 1950. Baker became a member of the Sixth Army Band at the
      <a href="/wiki/Presidio_of_San_Francisco" title="Presidio of San Francisco">
       Presidio
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      , but was soon spending time in San Francisco jazz clubs such as Bop City and the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_(nightclub)" title="Black Hawk (nightclub)">
       Black Hawk
      </a>
      . Baker once again obtained a discharge from the army to pursue a career as a professional musician.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career_breakthrough">
       Career breakthrough
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career breakthrough">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Baker's earliest notable professional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gig_(musical_performance)" title="Gig (musical performance)">
       gigs
      </a>
      were with saxophonist
      <a href="/wiki/Vido_Musso" title="Vido Musso">
       Vido Musso
      </a>
      's band, and also with
      <a href="/wiki/Tenor_saxophone" title="Tenor saxophone">
       tenor saxophonist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Stan_Getz" title="Stan Getz">
       Stan Getz
      </a>
      , though he earned much more renown in 1952 when he was chosen by
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">
       Charlie Parker
      </a>
      to play with him for a series of West Coast engagements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1952, Baker joined the
      <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">
       Gerry Mulligan
      </a>
      Quartet, which was an instant phenomenon. Several things made the Mulligan/Baker group special, the most prominent being the interplay between Mulligan's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baritone_sax" title="Baritone sax">
       baritone sax
      </a>
      and Baker's trumpet. Rather than playing identical
      <a href="/wiki/Melody" title="Melody">
       melody
      </a>
      lines in unison like
      <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">
       bebop
      </a>
      giants Charlie Parker and
      <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Gillespie" title="Dizzy Gillespie">
       Dizzy Gillespie
      </a>
      , the two would complement each other's playing with
      <a href="/wiki/Counterpoint" title="Counterpoint">
       contrapuntal
      </a>
      touches, and it often seemed as if they had telepathy in anticipating what the other was going to play next. The Quartet's version of "
      <a href="/wiki/My_Funny_Valentine" title="My Funny Valentine">
       My Funny Valentine
      </a>
      ", featuring a Baker solo, was a hit, and became a tune with which Baker was intimately associated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Quartet found success quickly, but lasted less than a year because of Mulligan's arrest and imprisonment on drug charges. Baker formed his own quartet with pianist and composer
      <a href="/wiki/Russ_Freeman_(pianist)" title="Russ Freeman (pianist)">
       Russ Freeman
      </a>
      in 1953, along with bassists
      <a href="/wiki/Carson_Smith" title="Carson Smith">
       Carson Smith
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Mondragon" title="Joe Mondragon">
       Joe Mondragon
      </a>
      , and Jimmy Bond and drummers
      <a href="/wiki/Shelly_Manne" title="Shelly Manne">
       Shelly Manne
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Larry_Bunker" title="Larry Bunker">
       Larry Bunker
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bob_Neel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bob Neel (page does not exist)">
       Bob Neel
      </a>
      . The Chet Baker Quartet found success with their live sets, and they released a number of popular albums between 1953 and 1956. In 1953 and 1954, Baker won the
      <i>
       Down Beat
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Metronome
      </i>
      magazines' Readers Jazz Polls, beating the era's two top trumpeters,
      <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">
       Miles Davis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Clifford_Brown" title="Clifford Brown">
       Clifford Brown
      </a>
      .
      <i>
       Down Beat
      </i>
      readers also voted Baker as the top jazz vocalist in 1954. In 1956, Pacific Jazz released
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker_Sings" title="Chet Baker Sings">
        Chet Baker Sings
       </a>
      </i>
      , a record that increased his profile but alienated traditional jazz fans; he would continue to sing throughout his career.
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to Baker's chiseled features, he was approached by Hollywood studios, and he made his acting debut in the film
      <i>
       Hell's Horizon
      </i>
      , released in the fall of 1955. He declined an offer of a studio contract, preferring life on the road as a musician. Over the next few years, Baker fronted his own combos, including a 1955 quintet featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Francy_Boland" title="Francy Boland">
       Francy Boland
      </a>
      , where Baker combined playing trumpet and singing. He became an icon of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_coast_jazz" title="West coast jazz">
       West Coast "cool school" of jazz
      </a>
      , helped by his good looks and singing talent. Baker's 1956 recording, released for the first time in its entirety in 1989 as
      <i>
       The Route
      </i>
      , with
      <a href="/wiki/Art_Pepper" title="Art Pepper">
       Art Pepper
      </a>
      , helped further the West Coast jazz sound and became a staple of cool jazz.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Drug_addiction_and_decline">
       Drug addiction and decline
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drug addiction and decline">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Baker began using heroin in the 1950s, resulting in an addiction that lasted the remainder of his life. At times, Baker pawned his instruments for money to maintain his drug habit. In the early 1960s, he served more than a year in prison in Italy on drug charges; he was later expelled from both
      <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">
       West Germany
      </a>
      and the UK for drug-related offenses. Baker was eventually
      <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">
       deported
      </a>
      from West Germany to the United States after running afoul of the law there a second time. He settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Milpitas,_California" title="Milpitas, California">
       Milpitas
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">
       northern California
      </a>
      , where he played in
      <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">
       San Jose
      </a>
      and San Francisco between short jail terms served for
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_prescription#Forgeries.2C_thefts_and_prevention" title="Medical prescription">
       prescription fraud
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALLMUSIC_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ALLMUSIC-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1968, Baker was savagely beaten (allegedly while attempting to buy drugs) after a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gig_(musical_performance)" title="Gig (musical performance)">
       gig
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/The_Trident_(restaurant)" title="The Trident (restaurant)">
       The Trident restaurant
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sausalito" title="Sausalito">
       Sausalito
      </a>
      , California sustaining severe cuts on the lips and broken front teeth, which ruined his
      <a href="/wiki/Embouchure" title="Embouchure">
       embouchure
      </a>
      . He stated in the film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Get_Lost_(1988_film)" title="Let's Get Lost (1988 film)">
        Let's Get Lost
       </a>
      </i>
      that an acquaintance attempted to rob him one night but backed off, only to return the next night with a group of several men who chased him. He entered a car and became surrounded. Instead of rescuing him, the people inside the car pushed him back out onto the street, where the chase by his attackers continued, and subsequently he was beaten to the point that his teeth, never in good condition to begin with, were knocked out, leaving him without the ability to play his horn. He took odd jobs, among them pumping gas. Meanwhile he was fitted for dentures and worked on his embouchure. Three months later he got a gig in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Between 1966 and 1974, Baker mostly played
      <a href="/wiki/Flugelhorn" title="Flugelhorn">
       flugelhorn
      </a>
      and recorded music that could mostly be classified as
      <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_jazz" title="West Coast jazz">
       West Coast jazz
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALLMUSIC_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ALLMUSIC-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comeback_and_later_career">
       Comeback and later career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comeback and later career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chet675.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1248" data-file-width="832" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Chet675.jpg/220px-Chet675.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Chet675.jpg/330px-Chet675.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Chet675.jpg/440px-Chet675.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chet675.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Baker in
        <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
         Belgium
        </a>
        , 1983
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      After developing a new embouchure resulting from dentures, Baker returned to the straight-ahead jazz that began his career. He relocated to New York City and began performing and recording again, including with guitarist
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hall_(musician)" title="Jim Hall (musician)">
       Jim Hall
      </a>
      . Later in the 1970s, Baker returned to Europe, where he was assisted by his friend Diane Vavra, who took care of his personal needs and otherwise helped him during his recording and performance dates.
     </p>
     <p>
      From 1978 until his death in 1988, Baker resided and played almost exclusively in Europe, returning to the US roughly once a year for a few performances. This was Baker's most prolific era as a recording artist. However, as his extensive output is strewn across numerous, mostly small European labels, none of these recordings ever reached a wider audience, even though many of them were well received by critics, who maintain that the period was one of Baker's most mature and rewarding. Of particular importance are Baker's quartet featuring the pianist
      <a href="/wiki/Phil_Markowitz" title="Phil Markowitz">
       Phil Markowitz
      </a>
      (197880) and his trio with guitarist
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_Catherine" title="Philip Catherine">
       Philip Catherine
      </a>
      and bassist
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Louis_Rassinfosse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean-Louis Rassinfosse (page does not exist)">
       Jean-Louis Rassinfosse
      </a>
      (198385).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      He also toured with saxophonist Stan Getz during this period.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1983, British singer
      <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">
       Elvis Costello
      </a>
      , a longtime fan of Baker, hired the trumpeter to play a solo on his song "
      <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding_(song)" title="Shipbuilding (song)">
       Shipbuilding
      </a>
      ", from the album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Punch_the_Clock" title="Punch the Clock">
        Punch the Clock
       </a>
      </i>
      . The song exposed Baker's music to a new audience. Later, Baker often featured Costello's song "
      <a href="/wiki/Almost_Blue_(song)" title="Almost Blue (song)">
       Almost Blue
      </a>
      " (inspired by Baker's version of "
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Thrill_Has_Gone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Thrill Has Gone (page does not exist)">
       The Thrill Has Gone
      </a>
      ") in his concert sets, and recorded the song for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Get_Lost_(1988_film)" title="Let's Get Lost (1988 film)">
        Let's Get Lost
       </a>
      </i>
      , a documentary film about his life.
     </p>
     <p>
      The video material recorded by Japanese television during Baker's 1987 tour in Japan showed a man whose face looked much older than he was, but his trumpet playing was alert, lively and inspired. Baker recorded the live album
      <i>
       Chet Baker in Tokyo
      </i>
      with his quartet featuring pianist
      <a href="/wiki/Harold_Danko" title="Harold Danko">
       Harold Danko
      </a>
      , bassist
      <a href="/wiki/Hein_van_de_Geyn" title="Hein van de Geyn">
       Hein van de Geyn
      </a>
      and drummer
      <a href="/wiki/John_Engels" title="John Engels">
       John Engels
      </a>
      less than a year before his death, and it was released posthumously.
      <i>
       Silent Night
      </i>
      , a recording of
      <a href="/wiki/Christmas_music" title="Christmas music">
       Christmas music
      </a>
      , was recorded with Christopher Mason in
      <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">
       New Orleans
      </a>
      in 1986 and released in 1987.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Compositions">
       Compositions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Compositions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Baker's compositions included "
      <a href="/wiki/Chetty%27s_Lullaby" title="Chetty's Lullaby">
       Chetty's Lullaby
      </a>
      ", "Freeway", "Early Morning Mood", "Two a Day", "So Che Ti Perder" ("I Know I Will Lose You"), "Il Mio Domani" ("My Tomorrow"), "Motivo Su Raggio Di Luna" ("Tune on a Moon Beam"), "The Route", "Skidadidlin'", "New Morning Blues", "Blue Gilles", "Dessert", and "Anticipated Blues".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death">
       Death
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chetbakermonument.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="410" height="322" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Chetbakermonument.jpg/220px-Chetbakermonument.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Chetbakermonument.jpg/330px-Chetbakermonument.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/Chetbakermonument.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chetbakermonument.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Plaque at the Hotel Prins Hendrik, in
        <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">
         Amsterdam
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      At about 3:10am on May 13, 1988, Baker was found dead on the Prins Hendrikkade, near the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeedijk_(Amsterdam)" title="Zeedijk (Amsterdam)">
       Zeedijk
      </a>
      , the street below his second-story room of Hotel Prins Hendrik in
      <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">
       Amsterdam
      </a>
      , the Netherlands, with serious wounds to his head. Heroin and
      <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">
       cocaine
      </a>
      were found in his hotel room, and an
      <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">
       autopsy
      </a>
      also found these drugs in his body. There was no evidence of a struggle, and the death was ruled an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accidental_death" title="Accidental death">
       accident
      </a>
      . A plaque outside the hotel memorializes him and the room he was staying in, No. 210, is named "The Chet Baker Room".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Baker is buried at the
      <a href="/wiki/Inglewood_Park_Cemetery" title="Inglewood Park Cemetery">
       Inglewood Park Cemetery
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Inglewood,_California" title="Inglewood, California">
       Inglewood, California
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chet_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Baker was photographed by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Claxton_(photographer)" title="William Claxton (photographer)">
       William Claxton
      </a>
      for his book
      <i>
       Young Chet: The Young Chet Baker
      </i>
      . An
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">
       Academy Award
      </a>
      -nominated 1988 documentary about Baker,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Get_Lost_(1988_film)" title="Let's Get Lost (1988 film)">
        Let's Get Lost
       </a>
      </i>
      , portrays him as a cultural icon of the 1950s, but juxtaposes this with his later image as a drug addict. The film, directed by fashion photographer
      <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Weber_(photographer)" title="Bruce Weber (photographer)">
       Bruce Weber
      </a>
      , was shot in
      <a href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">
       black-and-white
      </a>
      and includes a series of interviews with friends, family (including his three children by third wife Carol Baker), associates and women friends, interspersed with film from Baker's earlier life, and with interviews with Baker from his last years.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Time after Time: The Chet Baker Project
      </i>
      , written by playwright James O'Reilly, toured Canada in 2001 to much acclaim.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <i>

      </i>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oval_House_Theatre" title="Oval House Theatre">
       Oval House Theatre
      </a>
      in February 2007, with further development of the script and performances leading to its revival at the
      <a href="/wiki/606_Club" title="606 Club">
       606 Club
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/London_Jazz_Festival" title="London Jazz Festival">
       London Jazz Festival
      </a>
      of November 2007.
     </p>
     <p>
      Baker was reportedly the inspiration for the character Chad Bixby, played by
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wagner" title="Robert Wagner">
       Robert Wagner
      </a>
      in the 1960 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/All_the_Fine_Young_Cannibals" title="All the Fine Young Cannibals">
        All the Fine Young Cannibals
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>

        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another film, to be titled
      <i>
       Prince of Cool
      </i>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">

        <span>

        </span>

        <span>

      In 1991, singer/songwriter

      </a>

      <i>

      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/K.d._lang" title="K.d. lang">

      The song "Chet Baker", which appears on the 2007 CD
      <i>
       Wally Page and Johnny Mulhern: Live at the Annesley House
      </i>
      , by Irish folk singer-songwriter Wally Page, describes the end of Baker's life in Amsterdam.
     </p>
