           <!--wsa:gooold-->
           Windmills were one of humanitys prime sources of electricity before the technological revolution. Monuments to the technology ofsimpler times, thesestructures come in all shapes in sizes,from massive stone giants to smallsteeltowers. Here is a great collection of amazing shots of abandoned windmill photography!

           Holland

          <p>
           <span class="pibfi_pinterest">

            </span>
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span class="pibfi_pinterest">

          <p>
           Holland is home to quite a few abandoned windmills. Somewhat ominous in appearance, these windmills sit along the roads in and out out of Holland and provide for some excellent scenery along the drive. their massive rotor runs straight through the tall stone shaft and sticks far out the opposite side for momentum and balance.
          </p>

          <p>
           Situated on a hill in Greece, these windmills are quite scenic. Their blades have been removed and they no longer power the area they once did, however they are a part of the countries early history and have thus been preserved quite well. Judging by their size and girth, it is easy to imagine that these windmills most likely provided power to many country-side homes.
          </p>

           Erected in 1787, this windmill served as a replacement for an earlier one that was destroyed in the Revolutionary War. It is located on Watson Farm in Jamestown Rhode Island. It has been very well maintained and retains its classic, colonial look. Hopefully it will continue to be taken care of and enjoyed for years to come.

           Sweden

          <p>
           <span class="pibfi_pinterest">

          <p>

Located in Goatland, Sweden, this structure was most likely used to power a large farm.It appears to have been well maintained over the years, which issuprising for its age.Its blades areof peculiar design, but are efficiantly engineered to capture the wind well. The entire windmill is held sturdy by large wooden support beams extending off of the back, to prevent it from blowing itself over!
          </p>

              Actually, Jon and others, farm windmills pumped water, generated electricity and some powered mills that ground grain. The term windmill was used as a generic for any such wind-driven device by the farmers who used them.
             </p>
             <p>
              The article errs a bit that they were abandoned in favor of modern electricity. The Rural Electrification Authority (REA) set up poles and strung electrical wire throughout rural America, connecting farms to the grid. Farmers were forced, sometimes with deputy sheriffs standing by, to disconnect their wind-powered generators, allow the mounting of the electrical meter, and watch as REA installers hooked them up to the grid. Those who resisted were fined. The Amish, who had religious doctrine behind them, were able to avoid mandatory connection and retained the ability to generate their own electricity. It is ironic that today they are seen as forward-thinking and green, for avoiding the forced abandonment of self-generated electricity while they were ridiculed at the time for being backwards.
             </p>
