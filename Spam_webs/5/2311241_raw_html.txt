      <article about="/content/ditch-debt-and-start-saving" class="node node-infographic see-through clearfix " id="node-93" style="margin-bottom: 0; padding-left: 0; padding-right: 0; padding-bottom: 0;" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">
       <span class="rdf-meta element-hidden" content="Ditch debt and start saving" property="dc:title">
       </span>
       <div class="content">
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           THE BACKGROUND:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Im not proud of it. But there was a time when I seriously considered tossing my credit card into a dark corner of my freezer, letting it harden into an impenetrable block of ice. I was a year out of college with a newfound salary, and in denial that I couldnt go out to dinner and drinks every night and not pay the consequences. Even more daunting than thinking about paying off the credit card was the thought of my empty bank account. How was I supposed to start saving when I was in such a financial hole? Thankfully, before my debt turned into disaster, I got a grip and slowly started digging my way out.
         </p>
         <p>
          Whether it's credit card debt, student loans, car payments, or mortgages, most people deal with debt at some point in their lives. It can be a dangerous thingand if neglected can quickly spiral out of control. Heres the good news: The
          <em>
           Fidelity Viewpoints
          </em>
          <sup>
           
          </sup>
          article 
          <a href="https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/personal-finance/how-to-pay-off-debt">
           How to pay off debtand save too
          </a>
           has six steps you can take to get a handle on your debtand start saving at the same time. Beats the freezer burn, if you ask me.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           THE BREAKDOWN:
          </strong>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="default_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtDT1.png"/>
         </div>
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO1.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           1. Build an emergency fund.
          </strong>
          <br/>
          It might seem counterintuitive, but before you even think about tackling any debt, make sure you have some money socked away to cover necessary living expenses in case of an emergency. You never know when your cars brakes are going to break oryour basements going to flood, or you have some other unexpected expense that throws a wrench into things financially. Fidelity recommends setting aside three to six months to start. You can even set up automatic payments from your checking account to another account to start saving regularly.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO2.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           2. Contribute up to the match on your 401(k).
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Many employers offer a match on your 401(k) or 403(b), meaning, theyll match the amount you contributeoften dollar for dollarup to a certain percentage. Its like getting free money, and you wouldnt pass that up, right? Since retirement could be a long way down the road, Fidelity also suggests you consider investing in equities that may be available in your plan to give your cash a chance to grow. Take a look at this
          <a href="https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/retirement/asset-allocation">
           <em>
            Fidelity Viewpoints
           </em>
           <sup>
            
           </sup>
           article on asset allocation
          </a>
          to learn more.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           On a related note:
          </strong>
          Your employer also may offer another type of tax-advantaged account, called a Health Savings Account, which can be a great way to save for short- and long-term qualified medical expenses.
          <a href="https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/personal-finance/hsa-healthy-habits">
           Read on
          </a>
          to find outwhy you might want to take advantage.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO3.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           3. Pay off high-interest credit cards.
          </strong>
          <br/>
          As I already admitted, it can be easy to run up a high credit card balance. And take it from me: Making only the minimum payment on your balance while paying high interest rates can be a recipe for financial disaster. Do yourself a favor and pay off more than the monthly minimum if you can, and save yourself the stressand the interestlater. The hypothetical example below paints a helpful picture.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="default_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtDT2.png"/>
         </div>
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO2.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <em>
           For illustrative purposes only. Source: Bankrate, Inc.
          </em>
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO4.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           4. Pay off private student loans.
          </strong>
          <br/>
          If you graduated with a degree and a boatload of student loans, tackle the private ones first. They have higher interest rates than government-issued loans (5% to 12% versus 4%, according to FinAid). In some cases, you may be able to deduct the interest on a student loan when paying taxesbut only $2,500 a year if youre earning less than $75,000. In general, it can be a good idea to pay down student debt above 8% interest, especially if that debt is not tax deductible.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO5.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           5. Contribute even
           <em>
            more
           </em>
           to your 401(k).
          </strong>
          <br/>
          I know what youre thinking: Why would I put more away for retirement when I havent finished paying down my debt? Truth is, things like government student loans, car loans, and mortgages tend to have low interest rates. So it can actually be more beneficial to bump up your 401(k) contributions to start saving for the long term, while making minimum payments on your other loans.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO6.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           6. Tackle lower interest loans last.
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Government-issued loans, car payments, and mortgage payments come last of all, because they tend to have lower interest rates (usually 4% and below). As previously discussed, government-issued student loans can even come with tax advantages, so paying only the minimum monthly payment on these types of loanswhile you focus on other financial priorities listed abovemay make the most sense. Keep in mind that college grads can have different types of debt, which can come with various interest rates. For more guidelines on paying them down, take a look at the
          <a href="https://www.fidelity.com/viewpoints/personal-finance/how-to-pay-off-debt">
           original
           <em>
            Fidelity Viewpoints
           </em>
           <sup>
            
           </sup>
           article
          </a>
          .
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic">
         <div class="mobile_text">
          <img src="/content/sites/default/files/firsts-Mobile_ditch_debtMO7.png"/>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="infographic_text">
         <p>
          <strong>
           THE BOTTOM LINE:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Who knew you could tackle debt and start saving at the same time? I wish I did. Follow the simple steps above, and not only will your 401(k) thank you, but youll be well on your way to ditching more of that debt for good.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
