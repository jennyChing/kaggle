          <div class="article-body__content">
           <p>
            Sept. 2 (Bloomberg) -- The Revel Casino Hotel was
envisioned as a playground for Wall Streeters who hated flying
to Las Vegas. Instead, its become a money pit for the banks and
money managers who spearheaded the New Jersey project, and the
losses will keep coming even after closing today.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Atlantic City resort, built at a cost of $2.4 billion,
ceased operations after two bankruptcies and a 10-month search
for a buyer. Barring a sale, the new owners may be Wells Fargo &amp;
Co. and JPMorgan Chase &amp; Co., which provided $125 million in
court-approved funding. Previous backers also included Capital
Group Cos., the third-largest manager of U.S. mutual funds, and
Morgan Stanley, the original investor.
           </p>
           <p>
            The resort fell prey to poor timing, bad design and a
misreading of the local market. The Revel saga shows what can go
wrong when bankers stray from what they know, according to
Charles Geisst, a professor of finance at Manhattan College in
New York and author of the book Wall Street: A History.
           </p>
           <div data-view-uid="1|0_5_1_5">
            <div class="ad-v2 on-mobile" data-position="box1" id="1|0_5_1_5">
             <script type="text/javascript">

           <p>
            The check-in desk at Revel was on the 11th floor, reached
by escalator, Geisst said in an e-mail. Thats not planned
or invested in by someone who understands the hotel business.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its also a case study that has implications for Governor
Chris Christie and other local leaders as they ponder whats
next for Atlantic City in a summit scheduled for Sept. 8. A
future beyond gambling might be the answer.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Name Recognition
           </h2>
           <p>
            Donald Trump, whose brand once topped three Atlantic City
casinos, sued last month to get his name removed from the
remaining two properties.
           </p>
           <p>
            I see whats happening in Atlantic City; its just too
much competition, Trump said in an interview with Bloomberg
Televisions Trish Regan. Theres too much pressure from
Pennsylvania, Maryland, and ultimately everybodys going to do
badly because theyre looking at casinos as a panacea.
           </p>
           <p>
            Revels early champion was Michael Garrity, a principal in
Morgan Stanleys private-equity arm in the mid-2000s, and before
that an analyst at Putnam Investments and an investment banker
at Rothschild Group.
           </p>
           <p>
            Morgan Stanley backed Revel to bring a more upscale casino
to the northeastern U.S., Garrity told New Jersey regulators at
a 2012 licensing hearing, before its April opening.
           </p>
           <p>
            Ive spent a lot of years on Wall Street, Garrity said.
Whats always amazed me, the thousands of traders, analysts,
private-equity guys, bankers that will jump on a plane, fly four
to five hours to Vegas, lose the day on the way back. Because
theyre gamblers? No. Because they want to have fun. And that
was really the premise behind Revel.
           </p>
           <p>
            Garrity couldnt be reached for comment. Wells Fargo,
Capital Group, JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley declined to comment.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Initial Stumble
           </h2>
           <p>
            Revel stumbled before it opened. Morgan Stanley had $800
million in the project when the 2008 financial crisis hit. Kevin
DeSanctis, a casino veteran and the resorts first chief
executive, met with bank officials and told them they would be
in a better position to complete the project down the road if
they invested $480 million to finish the structural work,
according to his testimony at the same 2012 hearing.
           </p>
           <p>
            New York-based Morgan Stanley ultimately put in more than
$1.2 billion, before writing off Revel and selling its interest
for $35.5 million to an investor group led by DeSanctis in 2011.
           </p>
           <p>
            DeSanctis raised $1.15 billion from a new set of lenders
led by JPMorgan to finish the development. To bolster the
financing, New Jersey agreed to provide $261 million in tax
credits as part of Christies push to reverse Atlantic Citys
declining fortunes.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Fresh Capital
           </h2>
           <p>
            With new funds, construction of the 47-story property
resumed. DeSanctis held off finishing 500 of the planned 1,900
hotel rooms, expecting to complete a more-exclusive boutique
section with a separate elevator at a later date.
           </p>
           <p>
            Weve already talked to several folks who are very
interested in doing that, DeSanctis, formerly with Penn
National Gaming Inc. and Mirage Hotel Resort &amp; Casino, told
regulators at the 2012 hearing.
           </p>
           <p>
            DeSanctis, reached by e-mail, declined to comment.
           </p>
           <p>
            More challenges loomed. Cost overruns topped $100 million,
forcing Revel to seek additional funds shortly after opening,
according to a June declaration in bankruptcy court by Shaun
Martin, the companys chief restructuring officer.
           </p>
           <p>
            Revel had to supply its own electricity, and entered into a
costly 20-year agreement requiring it to pay power plant
operating expenses and a 15 percent guaranteed return to a third
party, ACR Energy Partners.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Gastro Pub
           </h2>
           <p>
            The resort, which opened in April 2012, features 2,300 slot
machines, two nightclubs, two theaters and 13 restaurants,
including Amada, an Andalusian tapas bar, and the Mussel Bar &amp;
Grille, a gastro-pub with Belgian flair, according to
marketing materials. The property includes five pools and a
SkyGarden with 20,000 trees and plants.
           </p>
           <p>
            In targeting high-end, overnight guests, Revel skipped the
amenities that Atlantic Citys traditional customers look for,
Martin said. The resort didnt have a lounge for frequent
gamblers or affordable food and beverage options.
           </p>
           <p>
            Hurricane Sandy closed Atlantic City for six days in late
2012. Short of cash and running at a loss before any interest
costs, Revel tapped investors for $150 million that December,
according to a statement, and in March 2013 filed its first
bankruptcy petition.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Debt Erased
           </h2>
           <p>
            In that restructuring, Revel wiped out 82 percent of its
$1.5 billion in debt. Funds affiliated with Chatham Asset
Management LLC, Canyon Capital LLC and Capital Group ended up
with the equity. DeSanctis stepped down and interim CEO Jeff
Hartmann, former president of the Mohegan Sun casino, took over.
           </p>
           <p>
            Canyon Capital declined to comment. A spokesman for Chatham
said it exited the investment in May.
           </p>
           <p>
            In June, Revel filed for bankruptcy a second time. If a
buyer cant be found, some creditors may be wiped out, while
others are left with the cost of maintaining a dormant building.
           </p>
           <p>
            Revel made efforts to broaden its appeal, ending its smoke-free policy, opening two lounges for VIPs and introducing
Relish, a 24-hour cafe with fried shrimp and French fries for
$5.99.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its a beautiful resort, you have to have a seven-day-a-week plan, Hartmann said in a telephone interview last month.
People are looking for quality food at a reasonable price
point.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Gamblers Wanted
           </h2>
           <p>
            Hartmann added the word casino to the property name and
introduced a marketing slogan: Gamblers wanted. He started a
controversial promotion promising to refund slot machine losses
greater than $100 for the month of July. A lawsuit claims Revel
didnt make it clear the refunds would be paid out over 20 weeks
in casino credit.
           </p>
           <p>
            In October, Revels board named Scott Kreeger, a former
executive with Station Casinos, as chief operating officer.
Hartmann left. Kreeger wasnt available for comment, said Lisa
Johnson, a spokeswoman.
           </p>
           <p>
            Design flaws remain a challenge, according to Robert
Heller, chief executive officer of Spectrum Gaming Capital LLC,
an investment bank in New York. They include a bland front
entrance, little pedestrian activity at Revels northern end of
the citys boardwalk and long escalators that older patrons find
intimidating.
           </p>
           <p>
            People want to move quickly through spaces, Heller said
in a telephone interview. Vertical in casinos is never a good
thing.
           </p>
           <p>
            Patrons could access the lobby and casino by elevator said
Johnson.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Citys Decline
           </h2>
           <p>
            Revels failure shows how the best and the brightest, the
smartest guys in the room misjudged the market and the pace of
Atlantic Citys decline, according to Izzy Posner, executive
director of the Lloyd D. Levenson Institute of Gaming,
Hospitality &amp; Tourism at Richard Stockton College of New Jersey.
           </p>
           <p>
            Casino revenue in the city, which introduced gambling as an
economic development tool in 1978, is down more than 40 percent
to $2.86 billion from the 2006 peak as neighboring states
entered the business. Four of the citys 12 casinos have closed
or are slated to close by mid-September. Caesars Entertainment
Corp.s Showboat shut down Aug. 31.
           </p>
           <p>
            Atlantic Citys casino revenue is expected to decline to
around $2 billion annually, Alex Bumazhny, a Fitch Ratings
analyst, said in an Aug. 29 report. About half of Revels
business will transfer to other casinos in the city, with those
located away from the boardwalk -- Boyd Gaming Corp.s Borgata,
Landry Inc.s Golden Nugget and Caesars Entertainment Co.s
Harrahs -- seeing most of the benefit.
           </p>
           <p>
            Local officials, including Mayor Don Guardian and state
Senator Jim Whelan, say someone will pick up Revel for pennies
on the dollar.
           </p>
           <p>
            Hopefully itll be a real casino company, Whelan said.
           </p>

    Sept. 2 (Bloomberg) -- The Revel Casino Hotel was\nenvisioned as a playground for Wall Streeters who hated flying\nto Las Vegas. Instead, it&#x2019;s become a money pit for the banks and\nmoney managers who spearheaded the New Jersey project, and the\nlosses will keep coming even after closing today. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The Atlantic City resort, built at a cost of $2.4 billion,\nceased operations after two bankruptcies and a 10-month search\nfor a buyer. Barring a sale, the new owners may be Wells Fargo &amp;\nCo. and JPMorgan Chase &amp; Co., which provided $125 million in\ncourt-approved funding. Previous backers also included Capital\nGroup Cos., the third-largest manager of U.S. mutual funds, and\nMorgan Stanley, the original investor. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The resort fell prey to poor timing, bad design and a\nmisreading of the local market. The Revel saga shows what can go\nwrong when bankers stray from what they know, according to\nCharles Geisst, a professor of finance at Manhattan College in\nNew York and author of the book &#x201C;Wall Street: A History.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;The check-in desk at Revel was on the 11th floor, reached\nby escalator,&#x201D; Geisst said in an e-mail. &#x201C;That&#x2019;s not planned\nor invested in by someone who understands the hotel business.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;It&#x2019;s also a case study that has implications for Governor\nChris Christie and other local leaders as they ponder what&#x2019;s\nnext for Atlantic City in a summit scheduled for Sept. 8. A\nfuture beyond gambling might be the answer. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Name Recognition &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Donald Trump, whose brand once topped three Atlantic City\ncasinos, sued last month to get his name removed from the\nremaining two properties. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;I see what&#x2019;s happening in Atlantic City; it&#x2019;s just too\nmuch competition,&#x201D; Trump said in an interview with Bloomberg\nTelevision&#x2019;s Trish Regan. &#x201C;There&#x2019;s too much pressure from\nPennsylvania, Maryland, and ultimately everybody&#x2019;s going to do\nbadly because they&#x2019;re looking at casinos as a panacea.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Revel&#x2019;s early champion was Michael Garrity, a principal in\nMorgan Stanley&#x2019;s private-equity arm in the mid-2000s, and before\nthat an analyst at Putnam Investments and an investment banker\nat Rothschild Group. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Morgan Stanley backed Revel to bring a more upscale casino\nto the northeastern U.S., Garrity told New Jersey regulators at\na 2012 licensing hearing, before its April opening. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;I&#x2019;ve spent a lot of years on Wall Street,&#x201D; Garrity said.\n&#x201C;What&#x2019;s always amazed me, the thousands of traders, analysts,\nprivate-equity guys, bankers that will jump on a plane, fly four\nto five hours to Vegas, lose the day on the way back. Because\nthey&#x2019;re gamblers? No. Because they want to have fun. And that\nwas really the premise behind Revel.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Garrity couldn&#x2019;t be reached for comment. Wells Fargo,\nCapital Group, JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley declined to comment. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Initial Stumble &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Revel stumbled before it opened. Morgan Stanley had $800\nmillion in the project when the 2008 financial crisis hit. Kevin\nDeSanctis, a casino veteran and the resort&#x2019;s first chief\nexecutive, met with bank officials and told them they would be\nin a better position to complete the project down the road if\nthey invested $480 million to finish the structural work,\naccording to his testimony at the same 2012 hearing. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;New York-based Morgan Stanley ultimately put in more than\n$1.2 billion, before writing off Revel and selling its interest\nfor $35.5 million to an investor group led by DeSanctis in 2011. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;DeSanctis raised $1.15 billion from a new set of lenders\nled by JPMorgan to finish the development. To bolster the\nfinancing, New Jersey agreed to provide $261 million in tax\ncredits as part of Christie&#x2019;s push to reverse Atlantic City&#x2019;s\ndeclining fortunes. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Fresh Capital &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;With new funds, construction of the 47-story property\nresumed. DeSanctis held off finishing 500 of the planned 1,900\nhotel rooms, expecting to complete a more-exclusive boutique\nsection with a separate elevator at a later date. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;We&#x2019;ve already talked to several folks who are very\ninterested in doing that,&#x201D; DeSanctis, formerly with Penn\nNational Gaming Inc. and Mirage Hotel Resort &amp; Casino, told\nregulators at the 2012 hearing. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;DeSanctis, reached by e-mail, declined to comment. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;More challenges loomed.
