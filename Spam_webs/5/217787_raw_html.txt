     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">
       Wasps
      </a>
      of the genus
      <i>
       <b>
        Sphex
       </b>
      </i>
      (commonly known as
      <b>
       digger wasps
      </b>
      ) are
      <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">
       cosmopolitan
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">
       predators
      </a>
      of the family
      <a href="/wiki/Sphecidae" title="Sphecidae">
       Sphecidae
      </a>
      that sting and paralyze prey insects. There are over 130 known digger wasp species. In preparation for egg laying, they construct a protected "nest" (some species dig nests in the ground, while others use pre-existing holes) and then stock it with captured insects. Typically, the prey are left alive, but
      <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">
       paralyzed
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">
       wasp
      </a>
      toxins. The wasps lay their eggs in the provisioned nest. When the wasp
      <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">
       larvae
      </a>
      hatch, they feed on the paralyzed insects.
     </p>
     <p>
      The well-known species great golden digger wasp (
      <i>
       Sphex ichneumoneus
      </i>
      ) is found in North America. The developing wasps spend the winter in their nest. When the new generation of adults emerge, they contain the genetically programmed behaviors required to carry out another season of nest building. During the summer, a female might build as many as six nests, each with several compartments for her eggs. The building and provisioning of the nests takes place in a
      <a href="/wiki/Stereotypy" title="Stereotypy">
       stereotypical
      </a>
      , step-by-step fashion.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_in_philosophy">
       Uses in philosophy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digger_wasp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses in philosophy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Some writers in the
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind" title="Philosophy of mind">
       philosophy of mind
      </a>
      , most notably
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Dennett" title="Daniel Dennett">
       Daniel Dennett
      </a>
      , have cited the behavior of this animal for their arguments about human and animal free will.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      wasps drop a paralyzed insect near the opening of the nest. Before taking provisions into the nest, the
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      first inspects the nest, leaving the prey outside. During the inspection, an experimenter can move the prey a few inches away from the opening. When the
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      emerges from the nest ready to drag in the prey, it finds the prey missing. The
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      quickly locates the moved prey, but now its behavioral "program" has been reset. After dragging the prey back to the opening of the nest, once again the
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      is compelled to inspect the nest, so the prey is again dropped and left outside during another stereotypical inspection of the nest. This
      <a href="/wiki/Iteration" title="Iteration">
       iteration
      </a>
      can be repeated again and again, with the
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      never seeming to notice what is going on, never able to escape from its programmed sequence of behaviors. Dennett's argument quotes an account of
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      behavior from
      <a href="/wiki/Dean_Wooldridge" title="Dean Wooldridge">
       Dean Wooldridge
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Machinery of the Brain
      </i>
      (1963).
      <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hofstadter" title="Douglas Hofstadter">
       Douglas Hofstadter
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Dennett" title="Daniel Dennett">
       Daniel Dennett
      </a>
      have used this mechanistic behavior as an example of how seemingly thoughtful behavior can actually be quite mindless, the opposite of free will (or, as Hofstadter described it,
      <i>
       sphexishness
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to this seemingly instinctive and programmed behavior, the
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      has been shown, as in some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Henri_Fabre" title="Jean Henri Fabre">
       Jean Henri Fabre
      </a>
      studies, not to count how many crickets it collects for its nest. Although the wasp instinctively searches for four crickets, it cannot take into account a lost cricket, whether the cricket has been lost to ants or flies or simply been misplaced.
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      drags its cricket prey towards its burrow by the antennae; if the antennae of the cricket are cut off, the wasp would not think to continue to pull its prey by a leg.
     </p>
     <p>
      The navigation abilities and other behavior of
      <i>
       Sphex
      </i>
      were studied by the ethologist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niko_Tinbergen" title="Niko Tinbergen">
       Niko Tinbergen
      </a>
      , as explained and demonstrated by
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">
       Richard Dawkins
      </a>
      in the 1991
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution" title="Royal Institution">
       Royal Institution
      </a>
      Christmas Lectures,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Growing_Up_in_the_Universe" title="Growing Up in the Universe">
        Growing Up in the Universe
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
