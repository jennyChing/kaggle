        <h1>
         The realists ask why Rand Paul signed on with the nutty 'neo' conservatives (Mulshine)
        </h1>
        <div class="clearfix">

           A whole lot of conservatives who decided to stand with Rand are now asking why he is going over to the side of liberal internationalists like Bob Menendez, Marco Rubio and Lindsey Graham.

         <div class="entry-content">
          <div class="ndn_embed" data-config-distributor-id="90019" id="ndn-video-player-1">
          </div>
          <p>
           No one was surprised when a character like Marco Rubio signed that silly letter to the Iranians.
          </p>
          <p>
           Rubio is a complete ignoramus when it comes for foreign policy. He made that clear at the Conservative Political Action Conference last month. So did Louisiana Gov. Bobby Jindal.
          </p>
          <p>
           As
           <a href="http://www.theamericanconservative.com/larison/jindal-and-rubios-confused-talking-points/">
            Daniel Larison of the American Conservative
           </a>
           notes, neither of these nutty neocons understands who's fighting whom in the Mideast.
          </p>
          <p>
           Neither understood the painfully obvious fact that the Shiite Iranians are the most bitter enemies the Sunni Islamic State faces. Rubio went to far as to tell  Sean Hannity that "if we wanted to defeat them militarily, we could do it. He [Obama] doesn't want to upset Iran,"
          </p>
          <p>
           Larison observes that "As others have noted already, this criticism makes absolutely no sense. If Obama didn't want to 'upset' Iran, he would probably be committing the U.S. to do far more against ISIS, since the Iranians loathe ISIS and have been fighting them in Iraq. If ISIS were defeated, it would deprive Iran of a hated regional enemy, so they would hardly be 'upset' by this outcome."
          </p>
          <p>
           So that explains Rubio's embrace of the nutty neocon approach seen in that letter. He's simply ignorant.
          </p>
          <p>
           But what about Rand Paul? He was supposed to be the voice of sanity among Washington Republicans. Yet he joined 46 other senators in signing a letter that is based on the most farfetched fantasy ever to take over the Beltway intelligentsia, the idea that breaking off negotiations with Iran would somehow advance the cause of halting nuclear proliferation in the Mideast.
          </p>
          <p>
           In fact, as J
           <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2015/03/11/why-a-bad-deal-with-iran-is-better-than-no-deal-at-all-tom-cotton-letter/">
            effrey Lewis notes here,
           </a>
           it was a similar decision by the Bush administration that gave North Korea the breathing room to develop a nuke:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           There were plenty of reasons to be worried about North Korea's compliance, but the fundamental interest was the same as with Iran: Even an imperfect freeze on plutonium programs put the United States in a stronger, safer position to manage the problem. When intelligence emerged suggesting that North Korea's enrichment program was more advanced than previously thought, the Bush administration walked away from the Agreed Framework, as well as its own policy of a "bold approach" to transform U.S.-DPRK relations. The deal was dead.
           <p>
            What happened next should temper the enthusiasm of anyone who wants to walk away from talks with Iran now. North Korea stockpiled plutonium and tested a nuclear weapon.
            <br/>
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.nj.com/opinion/index.ssf/2015/03/princeton_scientist_penetrates_the_hype_over_iran.html">
            As I noted here,
           </a>
           the leading experts on nuclear nonproliferation argue that the best course for the U.S. is to keep monitors in place and get a deal that has a long "breakout period" during which the U.S. could act against any Iranian program.
           <br/>
          </p>
          <div class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_left" id="asset-15673202">
           <span class="adv-photo-large">
            <a class="full-size-popup" href="http://media.nj.com/new_jersey_opinion/photo/islamic-state-terrorjpg-74d4cb4f05940fee.jpg" target="_blank">
             <img alt="islamic-state-terror.JPG" class="lazy adv-photo" data-original="http://media.nj.com/new_jersey_opinion/photo/15673202-large.jpg" src="/static/common/img/blank.gif"/>
            </a>
            <span class="photo-data">
             <span class="caption">
              Shi'a Muslim fighters allied with Iran  hold a position on the scene of heavy fighting against advancing Jihadist militants and fighters of the Islamic State; the removal of Saddam Hussein led inevitably to the rise of Islamic fundamentalism and greatly strengthened the position of Iran, the very nation the "neo" conservatives were trying to weaken.
             </span>
             <span class="byline">
              Ali Al-Saadiali/Agence France-presse
             </span>
            </span>
           </span>
          </div>
          <p>
           Virtually all traditional conservatives believe this. The baying for war with Iran comes entirely from liberals such as our own Democratic U.S. Senator, Bob Menendez, and the various lefties who write for the Washington Post, one of whom
           <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/war-with-iran-is-probably-our-best-option/2015/03/13/fb112eb0-c725-11e4-a199-6cb5e63819d2_story.html">
            shows just how nutty liberals can be with this piece.
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           (ADD: the link above is to a piece by a certain Joshua Muravchik. One of my liberal friends argued Muravchik is a right-winger. Nonsense. Virtually every neocon intellectual has a left-wing past. Here's his from Wikipedia:
          </p>
          <p>
           <br/>
           "Muravchik was National Chairman of the Young People's Socialist League (YPSL) from 1968 to 1973, and executive director of the Coalition for a Democratic Majority from 1977 to 1979. He was also an aide to the late Congressman James G. O'Hara (D-Mich.) in 1975 and to the late Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan (D-NY) in 1977 and as a campaign aide to the late Senator Henry M. Jackson in his pursuit of the 1976 Democratic presidential nomination.")
           <br/>
          </p>
          <p>
           <br/>
           Why did Rand go over to the side of the Washington Post lefties?
          </p>
          <p>
           Justin Raimondo wants to know.
           <a href="http://original.antiwar.com/justin/2015/03/10/rand-pauls-munich/">
            In this piece
           </a>
           , the prominent libertarian calls this  "Rand Paul's Munich:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           Less than two months ago, speaking at a forum for GOP presidential aspirants, he directly confronted Rubio and Cruz:
           <p>
            "Are you ready to send ground troops into Iran? Are you ready to bomb them? Are you ready to send in 100,000 troops?I'm a big fan of trying to exert and trying the diplomatic option as long as we can. If it fails, I will vote to resume sanctions and I would vote to have new sanctions. But if you do it in the middle of negotiations, you're ruining it."
           </p>
           <p>
            If Sen. Paul is against "ruining it," why did he sign a letter that seeks to do exactly that? Is he now ready to send ground troops into Iran? Is he ready to bomb them?
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Read the entire piece. As Raimondo notes, if Rand thinks he is somehow going to suck up to the left-wing money machine of people like Sheldon Adelson, that's not going to work.
          </p>
          <p>
           He'd be better off leaving that sort of thing to a real professional, like Bob Menendez. Our senator had cleverly positioned himself as the defender of Israel in what looks like an effort to corner contributions for what is going to have to be a big defense fund.
          </p>
          <p>
           As for Rand, he should be the voice out there pointing out to the Wilsonians that their vision for the Mideast has failed miserably. Like that Democratic former governor of New Jersey, the neocons have an almost childlike belief in the power of democracy. It never occurs to them that in a country full of Muslim fundamentalists, any democratic election will create an Islamic fundamentalist state.
          </p>
          <p>
           They tried removing dictators in Iraq, Egypt, Libya and now Syria. And always they profess shock when the newly liberated foreigners pursue their own interests rather than ours.
          </p>
          <p>
           And then when the inevitable happens, they lie about their past views.
          </p>
          <p>
           No one is a worse offender than that former Walter Mondale speechwriter who constantly parrots his liberal views on the pages of the Post.
          </p>
          <p>
           I'm talking of course of Charles Krauthammer. He was at it again just the other day,
           <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/charles-krauthammer-irans-emerging-empire/2015/01/22/c3098336-a269-11e4-903f-9f2faf7cd9fe_story.html">
            wondering out loud why Iran i
           </a>
           s accumulating such power in the Mideast.
          </p>
          <p>
           Uhh, Chuck, get a clue. Iran came to power because people like you prevailed upon George W. Bush to overthrow the dictator who was keeping them in check.
           <br/>
           This has been obvious to us conservatives for more than a decade.
          </p>
          <p>
           In that regard, I've been reposting some of the many columns I wrote in the past in which I interviewed real experts such as ex-CIA guys and former soldiers with decades of experience in the Mideast. They all saw this coming - unlike the liberal on the pages of the Post. Here's one that's perfectly on point. I wrote it in July of 2012, when the Syrian debacle was just getting underway.
          </p>
          <p>
           It was headlined "Israel, and the United States, in a precarious position"
           <br/>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <br/>
           Just a few months ago, Charles Krauthammer, who is the Washington Post's neoconservative-in-residence, was asking, "Why aren't we organizing, training and arming the Syrian rebels in Turkey?"
           <p>
            Now he's giving the answer: "As for Syria, if and when Bashar Assad falls, the Brotherhood will almost certainly inherit power."
           </p>
           <p>
            That's the Muslim Brotherhood. And yes, they or some other Sunni Muslim fundamentalist group would likely rise to power in Syria after a successful revolution.
           </p>
           <p>
            But that was obvious in 2004, when I wrote a column in which I warned: "A democratic Mideast would almost certainly be taken over by the same Islamic fundamentalists we are trying to suppress."
           </p>
           <p>
            I wasn't particularly prescient. I just got a hold of the best sources, ex-CIA agents and others with actual experience in the Mideast. All of them found laughable the idea that liberating Mideasterners would work in our favor.
           </p>
           <p>
            I called one of these guys the other day after he made an interesting point on his blog: We've now reached the point in the "war on terror" that we're tacitly supporting people the neocons would have termed terrorists if they had done the same thing in Iraq.
           </p>
           <p>
            That definition would certainly apply to whoever blew up much of the Syrian leadership last week, using either a suicide bomb or an improvised explosive device, depending on who's describing the attack.
           </p>
           <p>
            "Even inside the Obama administration, I don't hear anyone saying that was terrorism," said
            <a href="http://turcopolier.typepad.com/sic_semper_tyrannis/">
             Pat Lang,
            </a>
            a former Green Beret in Vietnam with an extensive background in the Mideast.
           </p>
           <p>
            But when you consider that the bomber almost certainly had ties to one Islamic fundamentalist group or another, you have to ask yourself why the United States is leaning toward the rebels in this latest war of liberation.
           </p>
           <p>
            For Lang, the answer lies in the extreme naivete of the Republican and Democratic establishments concerning the Mideast.
            <br/>
           </p>
           <div class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_right" id="asset-17193472">
            <span class="adv-photo-large">
             <a class="full-size-popup" href="http://media.nj.com/njcom_photos/photo/2015/03/06/-19dbc3a26fe73146.JPG" target="_blank">
              <img alt="Menendez speaks about alleged corruption charges" class="lazy adv-photo" data-original="http://media.nj.com/njcom_photos/photo/2015/03/06/17193472-large.jpg" src="/static/common/img/blank.gif"/>
             </a>
             <span class="photo-data">
              <span class="caption">
               U.S. Senator Bob Menendez: it makes sense for knee-jerk left-winger like him to support the liberal internationalist view on the Mideast, but what explains Rand Paul's position?
              </span>
              <span class="byline">
               Alexandra Pais | For NJ Advance Media
              </span>
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
           <p>
            "They don't understand the Mideast," said Lang. "They think these uprisings are a Jeffersonian search for good government when what's really going on is the settling of ancient ethnic and religious scores."
           </p>
           <p>
            That score-settling could be particularly nasty in the case of Syria. On "Fox News Sunday," Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said that if the Syrian regime falls, Israel might need to intervene to make sure the regime's weapons don't fall into the wrong hands.
           </p>
           <p>
            "Can you imagine Hezbollah, the people who are conducting with Iran all these terror attacks around the world, can you imagine that they would have chemical weapons?" Netanyahu asked.
           </p>
           <p>
            That would be yet another curious result in what is supposed to be a war on terror, said Lang. Meanwhile, the fall of the Assad regime could have serious strategic consequences for Israel, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            "It has been the goal of the Saudis forever to get Syria under Sunni control," he said.
            <br/>
            With the Assad regime gone, the Saudis could easily exert that control, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            Meanwhile on Israel's other side, the Saudis now have another friendly regime. After their liberation, the Egyptians promptly elected a president from the Muslim Brotherhood.
           </p>
           <p>
            "What's the next neocon move, trying to overthrow the Jordanian king?" Lang joked.
           </p>
           <p>
            That would leave Israel entirely surrounded by hostile fundamentalist regimes. Yet such a move would fit with George W. Bush's 2005 inaugural speech, in which he proclaimed that the goal of U.S. foreign policy should be to "end tyranny in our world."
           </p>
           <p>
            On this point, our neocon friends have a lot of explaining to do. Not long after that speech, Krauthammer said on a talk show, "This whole Arab Spring hinges on Iraq. If we succeed in Iraq, there will be a sweeping of democracy in that region."
           </p>
           <p>
            But the other day, Krauthammer proclaimed that "the Arab Spring is a misnomer. This is an Islamist ascendancy, likely to dominate Arab politics for a generation."
           </p>
           <p>
            I've seen other neocons espousing the same sentiments. After eight years of talking about the wonders of liberating the Mideast, these guys are suddenly realizing that Lang and the other realists were right?
           </p>
           <p>
            I say they can't be that dumb. Lang disagrees. The neocons honestly thought they were aiding Israel with all of these wars of liberation, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            "The Israelis were content to watch all this happen in Iraq and Afghanistan," he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            "Now that it's surrounding Israel with potential hostile regimes, that's a different matter."
           </p>
           <p>
            And it is a matter on which Krauthammer and the rest of the neocons should refrain from commenting.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
