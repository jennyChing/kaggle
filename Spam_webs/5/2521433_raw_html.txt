        <h3 class="entry-header">
         <a href="http://www.dailygalaxy.com/my_weblog/2008/01/the-iss-ufo--in.html">
          The ISS "UFO" -International Space Station to Launch a Paper Spaceplane to Land on Earth
         </a>
        </h3>
        <div class="entry-content">
         <div class="entry-body">
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.dailygalaxy.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/01/21/origami_spacecraft.jpg">
            <img alt="Origami_spacecraft" border="0" height="239" src="http://www.dailygalaxy.com/my_weblog/images/2008/01/21/origami_spacecraft.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="Origami_spacecraft" width="374"/>
           </a>
           We know that without an evil enemy to race against
           <span class="misspell">
            NASA's
           </span>
           budget has fallen in the last few decades, but recent plans make things
sound desperate: they're going to launch a paper plane -an
           <span class="misspell">
            astrogami
           </span>
           spaceplane.
          </p>
          <p>
           Luckily this
isn't the last gasp before we see astronauts panhandling for change
with signs reading "Will mime Zero-G for rocket fuel", but an awesome
expression of the childlike wonder that space travel is meant to have.
          </p>
         </div>
         <a id="more">
         </a>
         <div class="entry-more" style="float:left">
          <p>
           There are a number of reasons for the origami
           <span class="misspell">
            space-plane
           </span>
           ,
an 8 cm long heat-treated paper construction, to be launched from the
International Space Station to coast gently down to Earth. "Proof of
Principle" is one, and I'm sure "Research of alternative construction
strategies" has been typed on some terribly official looking document
somewhere, but it's excellently clear that "Why the hell not" plays a
huge part in this. It's proof that some scientists aren't
number-crunchers, or desk-jobbers, but kids who found an amazingly
exciting world to play in and were delighted to discover they could get
paid for it.
          </p>
          <p>
           The orbital origami has been made in association with the Japanese Origami Airplane Association (
           <span class="misspell">
            JOAA
           </span>
           ),
who must have scored the Make-A-Wish foundation and had every single
members birthday wish happen at once to get the project. This is like
Gary Kasparov riding into your local high school chess club on a giant
motorized rook. Some
           <span class="misspell">
            JOAA
           </span>
           members have taken to walking everywhere coated in cushions on
           <span class="misspell">
            valium
           </span>
           to avoid waking up just in case they're dreaming.
          </p>
          <p>
           The prototype paper airplane, folded to resemble a space shuttle, has been tested in a wind tunnel at
           <span class="misspell">
            airspeeds
           </span>
           up to Mach 7. That bears repeating, but is so awesome that saying it
twice might just break you, so I'm just going to do the highlights. 
Paper Airplane! Mach 7! I'm surprised anyone involved can get
anything done without breaking down in maniacal giggling.
          </p>
          <p>
           You
might have some advanced technical objections to this plan, along the
lines of "even rocks burn up on re-entry" and "I'm fairly sure that
paper burns easier than rocks", a double-certainty that this amazing
voyage is destined to end in a shower of ash. But due to its smaller
mass the plane is expected to fall much more slowly than the sort of
things we're used to crashing in from the stars (meteors, Space
Shuttles,
           <span class="misspell">
            Roswellian
           </span>
           tourist attractions etc) and therefore avoid the massive frictional heating that leads to
           <span class="misspell">
            burnup
           </span>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Some have hoped that the
           <span class="misspell">
            ISS
           </span>
           crew will write a message of peace on the plane, as the scientists have
no idea where it might land (a hands-breadth of paper traveling through
the entire atmosphere is more Chaos Theory than a whole swarm of
butterflies in a storm). It's a good wish - after all, one of the
fondest dreams of the space program is to meet those we've never met
before. Who said they had to be aliens?
          </p>
          <p>
           Posted by Luke McKinney.
          </p>
