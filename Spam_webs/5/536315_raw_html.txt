   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Goldbach's conjecture
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach_conjecture&amp;redirect=no" title="Goldbach conjecture">
       Goldbach conjecture
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Goldbach%27s_weak_conjecture" title="Goldbach's weak conjecture">
       Goldbach's weak conjecture
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Goldbach_partitions_of_the_even_integers_from_4_to_50_rev4b.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="300" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Goldbach_partitions_of_the_even_integers_from_4_to_28_300px.png" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goldbach_partitions_of_the_even_integers_from_4_to_50_rev4b.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The even integers from 4 to 28 as sums of two primes: Even integers correspond to horizontal lines. For each odd prime, there are two oblique lines, one red and one blue. The sums of two primes are the intersections of one red and one blue line, marked by a circle. Thus the circles on a given horizontal line give all partitions of the corresponding even integer into the sum of two primes.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <b>
       Goldbach's conjecture
      </b>
      is one of the oldest and best-known
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_mathematics" title="List of unsolved problems in mathematics">
       unsolved problems
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">
       number theory
      </a>
      and in all of
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
       mathematics
      </a>
      . It states:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Every
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Even_and_odd_numbers" title="Even and odd numbers">
        even
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">
        integer
       </a>
       greater than 2 can be expressed as the sum of two
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">
        primes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoldbachNumber_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GoldbachNumber-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The conjecture has been shown to hold up through 410
      <sup>
       18
      </sup>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but remains unproven despite considerable effort.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Goldbach_number">
       Goldbach number
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach%27s_conjecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Goldbach number">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GoldbachConjecture.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="408" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/GoldbachConjecture.gif/300px-GoldbachConjecture.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/38/GoldbachConjecture.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/38/GoldbachConjecture.gif 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GoldbachConjecture.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The number of ways an even number can be represented as the sum of two primes.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          3
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       Goldbach number
      </b>
      is a positive integer that can be expressed as the sum of two odd primes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoldbachNumber_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-GoldbachNumber-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, another statement of Goldbach's conjecture is that all even integers greater than 4 are Goldbach numbers.
     </p>
     <p>
      The expression of a given even number as a sum of two primes is called a
      <b>
       Goldbach
       <a href="/wiki/Partition_(number_theory)" title="Partition (number theory)">
        partition
       </a>
      </b>
      of that number. The following are examples of Goldbach partitions for some even numbers:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       4 = 2 + 2
      </dd>
      <dd>
       6 = 3 + 3
      </dd>
      <dd>
       8 = 3 + 5
      </dd>
      <dd>
       10 = 3 + 7 = 5 + 5
      </dd>
      <dd>
       12 = 7 + 5
      </dd>
      <dd>
       ...
      </dd>
      <dd>
       100 = 3 + 97 = 11 + 89 = 17 + 83 = 29 + 71 = 41 + 59 = 47 + 53
      </dd>
      <dd>
       ...
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The number of ways in which 2
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      can be written as the sum of two primes (for
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      starting at 1) is:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       0, 1, 1, 1, 2, 1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 3, 2, 3, 2, 4, 4, 2, 3, 4, 3, 4, 5, 4, 3, 5, 3, 4, 6, 3, 5, 6, 2, 5, 6, 5, 5, 7, 4, 5, 8, 5, 4, 9, 4, 5, 7, 3, 6, 8, 5, 6, 8, 6, 7, 10, 6, 6, 12, 4, 5, 10, 3, ... (sequence
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A045917" title="oeis:A045917">
         A045917
        </a>
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">
        OEIS
       </a>
       ).
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach%27s_conjecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="877" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg/200px-Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg/300px-Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg/400px-Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Letter_Goldbach-Euler.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Letter from Goldbach to Euler dated on 7.June 1742 (Latin-German).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          4
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      On 7 June 1742, the German
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">
       mathematician
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_Goldbach" title="Christian Goldbach">
       Christian Goldbach
      </a>
      wrote a letter to
      <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">
       Leonhard Euler
      </a>
      (letter XLIII)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which he proposed the following conjecture:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Every integer which can be written as the sum of two primes, can also be written as the sum of as many primes as one wishes, until all terms are units.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      He then proposed a second conjecture in the margin of his letter:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Every integer greater than 2 can be written as the sum of three primes.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      He considered 1 to be a
      <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">
       prime number
      </a>
      , a convention subsequently abandoned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WolframMathWorld_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WolframMathWorld-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The two conjectures are now known to be equivalent, but this did not seem to be an issue at the time. A modern version of Goldbach's marginal conjecture is:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <b>
        Every integer greater than 5 can be written as the sum of three primes
       </b>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Euler replied in a letter dated 30 June 1742, and reminded Goldbach of an earlier conversation they had (
      <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
       "so Ew vormals mit mir communicirt haben"
      </span>
      ), in which Goldbach remarked his original (and not marginal) conjecture followed from the following statement
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <b>
        Every even integer greater than 2 can be written as the sum of two primes
       </b>
       ,
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      which is, thus, also a conjecture of Goldbach. In the letter dated 30 June 1742, Euler stated:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
        "Dass  ein jeder numerus par eine summa duorum primorum sey, halte ich fr ein ganz gewisses theorema, ungeachtet ich dasselbe nicht demonstriren kann."
       </span>
       ("That  every even integer is a sum of two primes, I regard as a completely certain theorem, although I cannot prove it.")
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorema_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-theorema-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrimeGlossary_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PrimeGlossary-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Goldbach's third version (equivalent to the two other versions) is the form in which the conjecture is usually expressed today. It is also known as the "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_jargon#strong" title="Mathematical jargon">
       strong
      </a>
      ", "even", or "binary" Goldbach conjecture, to distinguish it from a weaker corollary. The strong Goldbach conjecture implies the conjecture that
      <b>
       all odd numbers greater than 7 are the sum of three odd primes
      </b>
      , which is known today variously as the
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Goldbach%27s_weak_conjecture" title="Goldbach's weak conjecture">
        "weak" Goldbach conjecture
       </a>
      </b>
      , the "odd" Goldbach conjecture, or the "ternary" Goldbach conjecture. While the weak Goldbach conjecture appears to have been finally proved in 2013,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfgott_2013_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Helfgott_2013-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfgott_2012_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Helfgott_2012-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the strong conjecture has remained unsolved.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Verified_results">
       Verified results
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach%27s_conjecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Verified results">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For small values of
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      , the strong Goldbach conjecture (and hence the weak Goldbach conjecture) can be verified directly. For instance, Nils Pipping in 1938 laboriously verified the conjecture up to
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      10
      <sup>
       5
      </sup>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With the advent of computers, many more values of
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      have been checked; T. Oliveira e Silva is running a distributed computer search that has verified the conjecture for
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      410
      <sup>
       18
      </sup>
      (and double-checked up to 410
      <sup>
       17
      </sup>
      ). One record from this search is that 3325581707333960528 is the smallest number that has no Goldbach partition with a prime below 9781.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heuristic_justification">
       Heuristic justification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach%27s_conjecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heuristic justification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Statistical considerations that focus on the
      <a href="/wiki/Prime_number_theorem" title="Prime number theorem">
       probabilistic distribution of prime numbers
      </a>
      present informal evidence in favour of the conjecture (in both the weak and strong forms) for
      <a href="/wiki/Sufficiently_large" title="Sufficiently large">
       sufficiently large
      </a>
      integers: the greater the integer, the more ways there are available for that number to be represented as the sum of two or three other numbers, and the more "likely" it becomes that at least one of these representations consists entirely of primes.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:290px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goldbach-1000.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Goldbach-1000.svg/288px-Goldbach-1000.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Goldbach-1000.svg/432px-Goldbach-1000.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Goldbach-1000.svg/576px-Goldbach-1000.svg.png 2x" width="288"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goldbach-1000.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Number of ways to write an even number
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        as the sum of two primes (4
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        1,000), (sequence
        <span class="nowrap">
         <a class="extiw" href="//oeis.org/A002375" title="oeis:A002375">
          A002375
         </a>
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences" title="On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences">
         OEIS
        </a>
        )
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:290px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goldbach-1000000.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="720" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Goldbach-1000000.png/288px-Goldbach-1000000.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Goldbach-1000000.png/432px-Goldbach-1000000.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Goldbach-1000000.png/576px-Goldbach-1000000.png 2x" width="288"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goldbach-1000000.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Number of ways to write an even number
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        as the sum of two primes (4
        <i>

        </i>
        1,000,000)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A very crude version of the
      <a href="/wiki/Heuristic" title="Heuristic">
       heuristic
      </a>
      probabilistic argument (for the strong form of the Goldbach conjecture) is as follows. The
      <a href="/wiki/Prime_number_theorem" title="Prime number theorem">
       prime number theorem
      </a>
      asserts that an integer
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      selected at random has roughly a
      <img alt="1/\ln m\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/b/a/cba5eeba1371c0005a7732980b1a31fb.png"/>
      chance of being prime. Thus if
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      is a large even integer and
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      is a number between 3 and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      /2, then one might expect the probability of
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      simultaneously being prime to be
      <img alt="1 \big / \big [\ln m \,\ln (n-m)\big ]" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/5/be537832bc30c0258e13ff8f33581fad.png"/>
      . If one pursues this heuristic, one might expect the total number of ways to write a large even integer
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      as the sum of two odd primes to be roughly
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\sum_{m=3}^{n/2} \frac{1}{\ln m} {1 \over \ln (n-m)} \approx \frac{n}{2 \ln^2 n}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/7/1/d710dc9096c76aa318c05ace781778f3.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Since this quantity goes to infinity as
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      increases, we expect that every large even integer has not just one representation as the sum of two primes, but in fact has very many such representations.
     </p>
     <p>
      This heuristic argument is actually somewhat inaccurate, because it assumes that the events of
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      being prime are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_independence" title="Statistical independence">
       statistically independent
      </a>
      of each other. For instance, if
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      is odd then
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      is also odd, and if
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      is even, then
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      is even, a non-trivial relation because, besides the number 2, only odd numbers can be prime. Similarly, if
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      is divisible by 3, and
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      was already a prime distinct from 3, then
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      would also be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coprime" title="Coprime">
       coprime
      </a>
      to 3 and thus be slightly more likely to be prime than a general number. Pursuing this type of analysis more carefully,
      <a href="/wiki/G._H._Hardy" title="G. H. Hardy">
       Hardy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Edensor_Littlewood" title="John Edensor Littlewood">
       Littlewood
      </a>
      in 1923 conjectured (as part of their famous
      <i>
       HardyLittlewood prime tuple conjecture
      </i>
      ) that for any fixed
      <i>
       c
      </i>
      2, the number of representations of a large integer
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      as the sum of
      <i>
       c
      </i>
      primes
      <img alt="n=p_1+\cdots +p_c" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/3/a/93a8f769f409c3e0e9b92db215ae7070.png"/>
      with
      <img alt="p_1 \leq \cdots \leq p_c" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/f/3/8f3a2997b92758ee9293a8fc3d95de56.png"/>
      should be
      <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_analysis" title="Asymptotic analysis">
       asymptotically
      </a>
      equal to
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \left(\prod_p \frac{p \gamma_{c,p}(n)}{(p-1)^c}\right)
\int_{2 \leq x_1 \leq \cdots \leq x_c: x_1+\cdots+x_c = n} \frac{dx_1 \cdots dx_{c-1}}{\ln x_1 \cdots \ln x_c}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/6/8/e680219efa0ae515e02db29da9a9f7d9.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where the product is over all primes
      <i>
       p
      </i>
      , and
      <img alt="\gamma_{c,p}(n)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/8/c/08c9400892a883bd29d8b37f5ea81aa4.png"/>
      is the number of solutions to the equation
      <img alt="n = q_1 + \cdots + q_c \mod p" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/e/9/0e9606acded90ad5e34005277e0591d4.png"/>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Modular_arithmetic" title="Modular arithmetic">
       modular arithmetic
      </a>
      , subject to the
      <a href="/wiki/Constraint_(mathematics)" title="Constraint (mathematics)">
       constraints
      </a>
      <img alt="q_1,\ldots,q_c \neq 0 \mod p" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/a/b/cab93d6f2777e033ef68038da8356b64.png"/>
      . This formula has been rigorously proven to be asymptotically valid for
      <i>
       c
      </i>
      3 from the work of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Matveevich_Vinogradov" title="Ivan Matveevich Vinogradov">
       Vinogradov
      </a>
      , but is still only a conjecture when
      <img alt="c=2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/8/4/4847acef124418c705153576282ebd02.png"/>
      . In the latter case, the above formula simplifies to 0 when
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      is odd, and to
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" 2 \Pi_2 \left(\prod_{p|n; p \geq 3} \frac{p-1}{p-2}\right) \int_2^n \frac{dx}{\ln^2 x}
\approx 2 \Pi_2 \left(\prod_{p|n; p \geq 3} \frac{p-1}{p-2}\right) \frac{n}{\ln^2 n}
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/b/0/9b080272c370752c7d869f249f412635.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      when
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      is even, where
      <img alt="\Pi_2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/a/27a2200e7f97b3256e90d44e347542d6.png"/>
      is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twin_prime_constant" title="Twin prime constant">
       twin prime constant
      </a>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \Pi_2 := \prod_{p \geq 3} \left(1 - \frac{1}{(p-1)^2}\right) = 0.6601618158\ldots." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/9/5/895881a75833f9f999a26b4707d035f1.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This is sometimes known as the
      <i>
       extended Goldbach conjecture
      </i>
      . The strong Goldbach conjecture is in fact very similar to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twin_prime_conjecture" title="Twin prime conjecture">
       twin prime conjecture
      </a>
      , and the two conjectures are believed to be of roughly comparable difficulty.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Goldbach partition functions shown here can be displayed as histograms which informatively illustrate the above equations. See
      <a href="/wiki/Goldbach%27s_comet" title="Goldbach's comet">
       Goldbach's comet
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rigorous_results">
       Rigorous results
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach%27s_conjecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rigorous results">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The strong Goldbach conjecture is much more difficult than the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_Goldbach_conjecture" title="Weak Goldbach conjecture">
       weak Goldbach conjecture
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Matveevich_Vinogradov" title="Ivan Matveevich Vinogradov">
       Vinogradov
      </a>
      's method,
      <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Chudakov" title="Nikolai Chudakov">
       Chudakov
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Johannes_van_der_Corput" title="Johannes van der Corput">
       Van der Corput
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Estermann" title="Theodor Estermann">
       Estermann
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      showed that almost all even numbers can be written as the sum of two primes (in the sense that the fraction of even numbers which can be so written tends towards 1). In 1930,
      <a href="/wiki/Lev_Schnirelmann" title="Lev Schnirelmann">
       Lev Schnirelmann
      </a>
      proved
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that any
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_number" title="Natural number">
       natural number
      </a>
      greater than 1 can be written as the sum of not more than C prime numbers, where C is an effectively computable constant, see
      <a href="/wiki/Schnirelmann_density" title="Schnirelmann density">
       Schnirelmann density
      </a>
      .
      <b>
       Schnirelmann's constant
      </b>
      is the lowest number C with this property. Schnirelmann himself obtained C &lt; 800000. This result was subsequently enhanced by many authors; currently, the best known result is due to
      <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Ramar%C3%A9" title="Olivier Ramar">
       Olivier Ramar
      </a>
      , who in 1995 showed that every even number
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      4 is in fact the sum of at most six primes. In fact, the proof (released, but not yet published) of the weak Goldbach conjecture by Harald Helfgott directly implies that every even number
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      4 is the sum of at most four primes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Chen_Jingrun" title="Chen Jingrun">
       Chen Jingrun
      </a>
      showed in 1973 using the methods of
      <a href="/wiki/Sieve_theory" title="Sieve theory">
       sieve theory
      </a>
      that every
      <a href="/wiki/Sufficiently_large" title="Sufficiently large">
       sufficiently large
      </a>
      even number can be written as the sum of either two primes, or a prime and a
      <a href="/wiki/Semiprime" title="Semiprime">
       semiprime
      </a>
      (the product of two primes).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      See
      <a href="/wiki/Chen%27s_theorem" title="Chen's theorem">
       Chen's theorem
      </a>
      for more.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1975,
      <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Montgomery_(mathematician)" title="Hugh Montgomery (mathematician)">
       Hugh Montgomery
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Charles_Vaughan_(mathematician)" title="Robert Charles Vaughan (mathematician)">
       Robert Charles Vaughan
      </a>
      showed that "most" even numbers were expressible as the sum of two primes. More precisely, they showed that there existed positive constants
      <i>
       c
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       C
      </i>
      such that for all sufficiently large numbers
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      , every even number less than
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      is the sum of two primes, with at most
      <img alt="C N^{1-c}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/f/0/6f00be24351162ccc4cf6e7e8a8a7fec.png"/>
      exceptions. In particular, the set of even integers which are not the sum of two primes has
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_density" title="Natural density">
       density
      </a>
      zero.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Linnik" title="Yuri Linnik">
       Linnik
      </a>
      proved in 1951 the existence of a constant
      <i>
       K
      </i>
      such that every sufficiently large even number is the sum of two primes and at most
      <i>
       K
      </i>
      powers of 2.
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Heath-Brown" title="Roger Heath-Brown">
       Roger Heath-Brown
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jan-Christoph_Schlage-Puchta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jan-Christoph Schlage-Puchta (page does not exist)">
       Jan-Christoph Schlage-Puchta
      </a>
      in 2002 found that
      <i>
       K
      </i>
      =13 works.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This was improved to
      <i>
       K
      </i>
      =8 by
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Pintz" title="Jnos Pintz">
       Pintz
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Imre_Z._Ruzsa" title="Imre Z. Ruzsa">
       Ruzsa
      </a>
      in 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As with many famous conjectures in mathematics, there are a number of purported proofs of the Goldbach conjecture, none accepted by the mathematical community.
     </p>
     <p>
      Considerable work has been done on
      <a href="/wiki/Goldbach%27s_weak_conjecture" title="Goldbach's weak conjecture">
       Goldbach's weak conjecture
      </a>
      , culminating in a 2013 claim by
      <a href="/wiki/Harald_Helfgott" title="Harald Helfgott">
       Harald Helfgott
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfgott_2013_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Helfgott_2013-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfgott_2012_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Helfgott_2012-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to fully prove the conjecture for all odd integers greater than 7 (rather than the much larger
      <img alt="e^{3100}\approx 2 \times 10^{1346}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/3/a/53a6a21789bbc73af0824aca1f364113.png"/>
      that was implied by previous results).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_questions">
       Similar questions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goldbach%27s_conjecture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Similar questions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      One can pose similar questions when primes are replaced by other special sets of numbers, such as the squares.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       It was proven by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange" title="Joseph Louis Lagrange">
        Lagrange
       </a>
       that
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrange%27s_four-square_theorem" title="Lagrange's four-square theorem">
        every positive integer is the sum of four squares
       </a>
       . See
       <a href="/wiki/Waring%27s_problem" title="Waring's problem">
        Waring's problem
       </a>
       and the related
       <a href="/wiki/Waring%E2%80%93Goldbach_problem" title="WaringGoldbach problem">
        WaringGoldbach problem
       </a>
       on sums of powers of primes.
      </li>
      <li>
       Hardy and Littlewood listed as their Conjecture I: "
       <b>
        Every large odd number (n &gt; 5) is the sum of a prime and the double of a prime
       </b>
       ." (
       <i>
        Mathematics Magazine
       </i>
       , 66.1 (1993): 45-47.) This conjecture is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Lemoine%27s_conjecture" title="Lemoine's conjecture">
        Lemoine's conjecture
       </a>
       (also called
       <i>
        Levy's conjecture
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       The Goldbach conjecture for
       <a href="/wiki/Practical_number" title="Practical number">
        practical numbers
       </a>
       , a prime-like sequence of integers, was stated by Margenstern in 1984,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         25
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       and proved by
       <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Melfi" title="Giuseppe Melfi">
        Melfi
       </a>
       in 1996: every even number is a sum of two practical numbers.
      </li>
     </ul>
