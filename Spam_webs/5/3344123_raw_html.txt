          MARRIAGE
         </em>
         . Now, truth be told, this topic has come up a few times before, but we have quickly squashed it saying we were both perfectly happy the way things are. Mike has never been married and I went through a not-real-fun divorce, so neither of us were real interested in skipping down the aisle when we met. However thats starting to change.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <em>
          I can hear our mothers squealing already
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         One of the things Mike and I have worked very hard on in the last four years before getting married is how we both are with money. Mike and I are both incredibly independent and both make a very good living independent of one another however he is very much the saver
         <em>
          (unless poker is involved)
         </em>
         and I am very much the spender
         <em>
          (unless poker is involved*cough*)
         </em>
         In 2010, shortly after we moved in together, we divided up the who pays for what and it has stayed like that and worked perfectly for going on three years now. We both treated it like a business deal. He has his accounts, I have my accounts. None of our money is together.  But when we get married does that mean we have to
         <em>
          SHARE?!?!
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <iframe frameborder="0" height="250" marginheight="0" marginwidth="0" scrolling="no" src="http://www.insuranceresourcesforyou.com/e.php?aid=51025a1651ba387b2a000007&amp;wh=300x250&amp;src=" style="width:300px;height:250px;overflow:hidden" width="300">
          Iframe support required to view this content.
         </iframe>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         With money being one of the #1 reasons for divorce in America, we both know and agree that we need to get very clear about our ideas on money before we decide to make a trip down that aisle. Many of our friends have very different financial dynamics in their relationships, with the men basically being the bread winners and the ones in charge of all of the finances. Mike and I make virtually the same amount. Most of the men take care of the entire household tab, giving what looks from the outside as almost an allowance to their wives to spend. Mike and I have literally everything split  with the freedom to do whatever we choose with whatever respective money we have left over. We have everything set up perfectly for long term success.
         <em>
          right?
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Time recently had an interesting article about
         <a href="http://business.time.com/2012/09/18/women-and-money-why-you-need-to-take-control-now/" target="_blank">
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           <strong>
            women and finances
           </strong>
          </span>
         </a>
         in their business section, pointing out that though women are making more these days
         <em>
          (over 1/3 now being the breadwinner of the family *high five ladies!*)
         </em>
         that they still fall into a role of not securing their future through investing or retirement. And I realized
         <em>
          I am one of those women
         </em>
         . I am living quite comfortably now, but I really have nothing solid for the future. When I brought it up to Mike, he explained about how all of his investments work, and how he was already saving for
         <strong>
          <em>
           HIS
          </em>
         </strong>
         retirement.
         <em>
          wwwhhhaaa?
         </em>
         How did I not know this? And why have I not been doing this, too? And what else should we be talking about before we tie the knot?
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <img alt="marriage-and-money" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6939" height="265" src="http://moscatomom.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/03/marriage-and-money.jpg" width="574"/>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <span id="more-6929">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <strong>
          1. Sharing a Last NameBut
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         You will now be sharing a last name but are you going to share a bank account? As things stand right now, Mike and I have literally everything separate. In the past I have shared an account and have not had much success. There are many arguments on this topic, with some people arguing that you have to learn to spend and save together in order for your marriage to work. Personally I think if we were still in our early 20s this might pertain to us. But as two well established, successful thirty-somethings, I do not see anything changing with our setup. Mike and I are very understanding and giving to one another, and really do not keep tabs or any of that craziness. We know that really whats mine is his and whats his is mine even though it has the illusion of being very separate.  This is a conversation to have long before the marriage, though so there are no surprises after.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <strong>
          2. Handling Debt
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Thankfully Mike nor I have any debt to speak of outside of the mortgage and vehicles. But before you tie the knot, it is important to be brutally honest with each other about your debt, financial past, etc. You must also either make a plan for eliminating the debt together, as well as preventing sinking yourselves into further debt in the future. Just like with every other aspect of marriage, communication is key here, and you must be willing to hang up any of your money issues to be honest and open with your spouse to be. Trust me they will eventually find out sooner or later anyway. Better to be honest up front.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <strong>
          3. The Couple That Saves Together
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Since Mike is the saver and I am the spender, we have locked horns a few times when it comes to saving and spending. However, I have been focusing more in the last year on the amount of money I can SAVE instead of the amount I can MAKE and we spent last weekend in Las Vegas with a cruise planned for this Summer. Maybe he was on to something. He has also lightened up with his spending and has stopped analyzing how much I spend on sweet tea versus water eating out for a month.
         <em>
          ..at least out loud.
         </em>
         Point is, its important to discuss how you will save money and what you will save money for, and then work together to make those things happen. Whether it is a new home, a new addition to the family, a 5 day cruise,
         <em>
          (I cant WAIT!)
         </em>
         or even your retirement, communication is key plus it always feels great to know you worked for it and accomplished it  TOGETHER.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <strong>
          4. In Sickness and In Health
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         What if something bad happens? In a perfect world Mike and I would happily continue running our businesses long into our elderly years
         <em>
          (because we both love what we do)
         </em>
         and then spend our final years vacationing and enjoying our hard work. Thats the American Dream, right? But what if something happens?
         <em>
          (God forbid)
         </em>
         Mike has already been working towards his retirement and what if fund, but I really havent. Its important to sit down and get clear on how things would work should anything bad ever happen. The key to success is being fully prepared so you are not taken off guard. This is especially important when sharing a life with someone.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         <strong>
          5. Until Death Do Us Part
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Hopefully you will spend the rest of your life with your spouse to be, and though it is not real romantic, you must talk about what happens after that life as well. In the last year I have mourned the loss of too many friends who have lost their lives too soon, leaving families behind empty handed. Life Insurance was something I thought only old people needed and I was so very, very wrong. Companies like
         <a href="https://www.genworth.com/" target="_blank">
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           <strong>
            Genworth
           </strong>
          </span>
         </a>
         offer Life Insurance and Financial Planning for singles and couples of all ages. Its important to take care of one another in life  and in death  as best you can.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Mike and I are getting more and more excited about the idea of getting married, and feel good about how we are both handling our money for our family. In my past relationship we did not discuss money, and I do admit it was a very large part of our demise. Hopefully by keeping the communication open and honest
         <em>
          (brutally at times)
         </em>
         you can find a strategy that works for you to bring ever lasting love and prosperity to your marriage as will we.
        </p>
        <h4>
         <em>
          How do you handle the finances in your home?
         </em>
        </h4>

Balancing a checkbook drives my fiance crazy and he can intuitively keep track of all the ins and outs of his bank account without balancing it. And that works for him, so thats fine! If we tried to change each other in this very small and inconsequential way, it would cause unnecessary stress.  We are both totally on the same page with what we want for the future and what we want to spend and save on, and everything we do is to build towards our future. People might think it was weird that we have separate accounts, but ironically we are more devoted to one another than some couples who might have one account and then split up.           <div class="comment-content" itemprop="commentText">
           <p>
            I am going to use an oversampled word from the business world.  SYNERGY (or Cynergy).   Pooling your finances places all of the income together synergizing your spending and saving capabilituies.   Having a larger pool of liquid assets improves your ability to pay for items cash, improves the quantity of funds avaible for putting into savings and overall gives everyone a better idea of the household gross income.  These are the main reasons for joining your income streams.  If both parties have access to the accounts and the money is combined then both parties are accountable to the spending and the saving.  I read something recently that said that a couple that goes into a marriage with the idea that the money is seperate based on who makes it has already laid a crumbling foundation for failure when it comes to communication and trust.  If you cannot communite and cooperate over money then how are you to do the same over other subjects.  Morally, spirtiually and legally when you join together in a legally recognized marriage it doesnt matter who made the income unless there is a prenuptuial agreement defining rights to assets in the event of divorce (a whole other discussion regarding setting up for failure in marriage), each person is entitled to half of all existing assets.  All of your points about communicating prior to marrying about spending and savings habits are spot on but they are moot if neither party can act the same way whether they have seperate or joint accounts.  If there are concerns about one persons spending habits before hand they can and should be addressed prior to marriage and corrective actions should be implemented to correct or change those habits.  I personally think that if a couple is not willing to join their money and work together to spend and save jointly then they may want to reconsider marriage all together because there is an underlying seed of distrust already sown (either in this current relationship or carryign over form previous situations) because they are already making assumptions that the relationship has the potential to fail.  Or, someone has a serious control issue that will lead to other disrutpions and problems in the relationship.  Never let the failures of the past be a determining factor for future endeavors.  Instead, learn from that failure and implement changes to prevent it in the future.  Needing to divide assets is a symptom of a greater problem of mistrust not the cause.  If you plan for failure up front then you are destined to fail.  The fact that you and Mike are starting to break down the walls and discuss the need to plan your finances better/differently is a great start and I encourage you to continue it on your path to marriage.
           </p>
