     <p>
      The
      <b>
       High Frequency Active
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        Auroral
       </a>
       Research Program
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       HAARP
      </b>
      ) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
       ionospheric
      </a>
      research program jointly funded by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">
       U.S. Air Force
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       U.S. Navy
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Alaska" title="University of Alaska">
       University of Alaska
      </a>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title='This wlink redirects to the article "University of Alaska system", which is primarily an administrative entity rather than an academic or research entity. (November 2014)'>
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">
       Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency
      </a>
      (DARPA).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_Sheet_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FAQ_Sheet-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Designed and built by
      <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">
       BAE Advanced Technologies
      </a>
      (BAEAT), its purpose is to analyze the ionosphere and investigate the potential for developing ionospheric enhancement technology for radio communications and surveillance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purpose_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Purpose-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The HAARP program operates a major sub-arctic facility, named the HAARP Research Station, on an Air Force-owned site near
      <a href="/wiki/Gakona,_Alaska" title="Gakona, Alaska">
       Gakona, Alaska
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The most prominent instrument at the HAARP Station is the Ionospheric Research Instrument (IRI), a high-power
      <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
       radio frequency
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">
       transmitter
      </a>
      facility operating in the
      <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">
       high frequency
      </a>
      (HF) band. The IRI is used to temporarily excite a limited area of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
       ionosphere
      </a>
      . Other instruments, such as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">
       VHF
      </a>
      and a
      <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">
       UHF
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
       radar
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Fluxgate_compass" title="Fluxgate compass">
       fluxgate
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">
       magnetometer
      </a>
      , a digisonde (an
      <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_sounding" title="Ionospheric sounding">
       ionospheric sounding
      </a>
      device), and an induction magnetometer, are used to study the physical processes that occur in the excited region.
     </p>
     <p>
      Work on the HAARP Station began in 1993. The current working IRI was completed in 2007, and its prime contractor was
      <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Inc." title="BAE Systems Inc.">
       BAE Systems Advanced Technologies
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_Sheet_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FAQ_Sheet-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As of 2008, HAARP had incurred around $250 million in tax-funded construction and operating costs. It was reported to be temporarily shut down in May 2013, awaiting a change of contractors. In May 2014, it was announced that the HAARP program would be permanently shut down later in the year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADNCole2014_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ADNCole2014-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      HAARP was a target of
      <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">
       conspiracy theorists
      </a>
      , who claimed that it was capable of modifying weather, disabling satellites and exerting mind control over people, and that it was being used as a weapon against terrorists. Such theorists blamed the program for causing earthquakes, droughts, storms and floods, diseases such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War_syndrome" title="Gulf War syndrome">
       Gulf War syndrome
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chronic_fatigue_syndrome" title="Chronic fatigue syndrome">
       chronic fatigue syndrome
      </a>
      , the 1996 crash of
      <a href="/wiki/TWA_Flight_800" title="TWA Flight 800">
       TWA Flight 800
      </a>
      , and the 2003
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">
       destruction of the space shuttle
       <i>
        Columbia
       </i>
      </a>
      . Commentators and scientists say that proponents of these theories are "uninformed", because most theories put forward fall well outside the abilities of the facility and often outside the scope of natural science.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopSci_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PopSci-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naiditch_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Naiditch-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The HAARP program began in 1990. A U.S. senator from Alaska, Republican
      <a href="/wiki/Ted_Stevens" title="Ted Stevens">
       Ted Stevens
      </a>
      , helped win approval for the facility,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      whose construction began in 1993.
     </p>
     <p>
      In early May 2013, HAARP was temporarily shut down, awaiting a change between contractors who operated the facility. In July 2013, HAARP program manager James Keeney said, "
      <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">
       Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA)
      </a>
      is expected on site as a client to finish up some research in fall 2013 and winter 2014."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthSky_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EarthSky-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The temporary shutdown was described as being due to "a contractor regime change."
      <a href="/wiki/Ahtna,_Incorporated" title="Ahtna, Incorporated">
       Ahtna, Incorporated
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Alaska_native_corporation" title="Alaska native corporation">
       Alaska Native corporation
      </a>
      serving the region of Alaska where the HAARP site is located, was reportedly in talks to take over the facility administration contract from Marsh Creek, LLC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AK_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AK-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Project_overview">
       Project overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HAARP20l.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/HAARP20l.jpg/220px-HAARP20l.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/HAARP20l.jpg/330px-HAARP20l.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/HAARP20l.jpg/440px-HAARP20l.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

     <p>
      The HAARP project directs a 3.6
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      signal, in the 2.810
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">
       MHz
      </a>
      region of the HF (high-frequency) band, into the ionosphere. The signal may be pulsed or continuous. Then, effects of the transmission and any recovery period can be examined using associated instrumentation, including VHF and UHF radars, HF receivers, and optical cameras. According to the HAARP team, this will advance the study of basic natural processes that occur in the ionosphere under the natural but much stronger influence of solar interaction, and how the natural ionosphere affects radio signals.
     </p>
     <p>
      This will enable scientists to develop methods to mitigate these effects to improve the reliability or performance of communication and navigation systems which would have a wide range of both civilian and military uses, such as an increased accuracy of GPS navigation and advances in underwater and underground research and applications. This may lead to improved methods for submarine communication or an ability to remotely sense and map the mineral content of the terrestrial subsurface, and perhaps underground complexes, of regions or countries, among other things. The current facility lacks range to be used in regions like the Middle East, according to one of the researchers involved, but the technology could be put on a mobile platform.

       <a href="#cite_note-12">

     <p>
      According to HAARP's management, the project strives for openness, and all activities are logged and publicly available. Scientists without security clearances, even foreign nationals, are routinely allowed on site. The HAARP facility regularly (once a year on most years according to the HAARP home page) hosts open houses, during which time any civilian may tour the entire facility. In addition, scientific results obtained with HAARP are routinely published in major research journals (such as

      research lab scientists. Each summer, the HAARP holds a summer school for visiting students, including foreign nationals, giving them an opportunity to do research with one of the world's foremost research instruments.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Research">
       Research
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      HAARP's main goal is basic science research of the uppermost portion of the atmosphere, termed the ionosphere. Essentially a transition between the atmosphere and the
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
       magnetosphere
      </a>
      , the ionosphere is where the atmosphere is thin enough that the sun's X-rays and UV rays can reach it, but thick enough that there are still enough molecules present to absorb those rays. Consequently, the ionosphere consists of a rapid increase in density of free electrons, beginning at ~70km, reaching a peak at ~300km, and then falling off again as the atmosphere disappears entirely by ~1,000km. Various aspects of HAARP can study all of the main layers of the ionosphere.
     </p>
     <p>
      The profile of the ionosphere is highly variable, changing constantly on timescales of minutes, hours, days, seasons, and years. This profile becomes even more complex near Earth's magnetic poles, where the nearly vertical alignment and intensity of earth's magnetic field can cause physical effects like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
       aurorae
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The ionosphere is traditionally very difficult to measure. Balloons cannot reach it because the air is too thin, but satellites cannot orbit there because the air is still too thick. Hence, most experiments on the ionosphere give only small pieces of information. HAARP approaches the study of the ionosphere by following in the footsteps of an ionospheric heater called
      <a href="/wiki/EISCAT" title="EISCAT">
       EISCAT
      </a>
      near
      <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8" title="Troms">
       Troms
      </a>
      , Norway. There, scientists pioneered exploration of the ionosphere by perturbing it with radio waves in the 210MHz range, and studying how the ionosphere reacts. HAARP performs the same functions but with more power and a more flexible and agile HF beam.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of the main scientific findings from HAARP include
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Generating
       <a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">
        very low frequency
       </a>
       radio waves by modulated heating of the auroral
       <a href="/wiki/Electrojet" title="Electrojet">
        electrojet
       </a>
       , useful because generating
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLF" title="VLF">
        VLF
       </a>
       waves ordinarily requires gigantic antennas
      </li>
      <li>
       Generating weak luminous glow (measurable, but below that visible with a naked eye) from absorbing HAARP's signal
      </li>
      <li>
       Generating
       <a href="/wiki/Extremely_low_frequency" title="Extremely low frequency">
        extremely low frequency
       </a>
       waves in the 0.1Hz range. These are next to impossible to produce any other way, because the length of a transmit antenna is dictated by the wavelength of the signal it must emit.
      </li>
      <li>
       Generating
       <a href="/wiki/Whistler_(radio)" title="Whistler (radio)">
        whistler-mode
       </a>
       VLF signals that enter the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       and propagate to the other hemisphere, interacting with
       <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">
        Van Allen radiation belt
       </a>
       particles along the way
      </li>
      <li>
       VLF remote sensing of the heated ionosphere
      </li>
     </ol>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation_and_operation">
       Instrumentation and operation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Instrumentation and operation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The main instrument at HAARP Station is the
      <b>
       I
      </b>
      onospheric
      <b>
       R
      </b>
      esearch
      <b>
       I
      </b>
      nstrument (IRI). This is a high power, high-frequency
      <a href="/wiki/Phased_array" title="Phased array">
       phased array
      </a>
      radio
      <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">
       transmitter
      </a>
      with a set of 180
      <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">
       antennas
      </a>
      , disposed in an array of 12x15 units that occupy a rectangle of about 33 acres (13 hectares). The IRI is used to temporarily energize a small portion of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
       ionosphere
      </a>
      . The study of these disturbed volumes yields important information for understanding natural ionospheric processes.
     </p>
     <p>
      During active ionospheric research, the signal generated by the transmitter system is delivered to the
      <a href="/wiki/Antenna_array_(electromagnetic)" title="Antenna array (electromagnetic)">
       antenna array
      </a>
      and transmitted in an upward direction. At an altitude between 70 to 350km (43 to 217mi) (depending on operating frequency), the signal is partially absorbed in a small volume several tens of kilometers in diameter and a few meters thick over the IRI. The intensity of the
      <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">
       HF
      </a>
      signal in the ionosphere is less than 3W/cm, tens of thousands of times less than the Sun's natural electromagnetic radiation reaching the earth and hundreds of times less than even the normal random variations in intensity of the Sun's natural
      <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
       ultraviolet
      </a>
      (UV) energy which creates the ionosphere. The small effects that are produced, however, can be observed with the sensitive scientific instruments installed at the HAARP Station, and these observations can provide information about the dynamics of
      <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
       plasmas
      </a>
      and insight into the processes of solar-terrestrial interactions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Each antenna element consists of a crossed
      <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">
       dipole
      </a>
      that can be polarized for linear,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinary_mode" title="Ordinary mode">
       ordinary mode
      </a>
      (O-mode), or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraordinary_mode" title="Extraordinary mode">
       extraordinary mode
      </a>
      (X-mode) transmission and reception.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Each part of the two section crossed dipoles are individually fed from a specially designed, custom built transmitter, that operates at very low distortion levels. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effective_Radiated_Power" title="Effective Radiated Power">
       Effective Radiated Power
      </a>
      (ERP) of the IRI is limited by more than a factor of 10 at its lower operating frequencies. Much of this is due to higher antenna losses and a less efficient antenna pattern.
     </p>
     <p>
      The IRI can transmit between 2.7 and 10MHz, a frequency range that lies above the AM radio broadcast band and well below Citizens' Band frequency allocations. The HAARP Station is licensed to transmit only in certain segments of this frequency range, however. When the IRI is transmitting, the bandwidth of the transmitted signal is 100kHz or less. The IRI can transmit in continuous waves (CW) or in pulses as short as 10 microseconds (s). CW transmission is generally used for ionospheric modification, while transmission in short pulses frequently repeated is used as a radar system. Researchers can run experiments that use both modes of transmission, first modifying the ionosphere for a predetermined amount of time, then measuring the decay of modification effects with pulsed transmissions.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are other geophysical instruments for research at the Station. Some of them are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Fluxgate_compass" title="Fluxgate compass">
        fluxgate
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">
        magnetometer
       </a>
       built by the University of Alaska Fairbanks
       <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Institute" title="Geophysical Institute">
        Geophysical Institute
       </a>
       , available to chart variations in the Earth's magnetic field. Rapid and sharp changes of it may indicate a
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_sounding" title="Ionospheric sounding">
        digisonde
       </a>
       that provides ionospheric profiles, allowing scientists to choose appropriate frequencies for IRI operation. The HAARP makes current and historic digisonde information available online.
      </li>
      <li>
       An induction magnetometer, provided by the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">
        University of Tokyo
       </a>
       , that measures the changing geomagnetic field in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra_Low_Frequency" title="Ultra Low Frequency">
        Ultra Low Frequency
       </a>
       (ULF) range of 05Hz.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The Station is powered by a set of five (5) 2500 kilowatt generators being driven by EMD 20-645-E4 diesel locomotive engines.
     </p>

        Developmental Prototype
       </b>
       (DP) had 18 antenna elements, organized in three columns by six rows. It was fed with a total of 360 kilowatts (kW) combined transmitter output power. The DP transmitted just enough power for the most basic of ionospheric testing.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        Filled Developmental Prototype
       </b>
       (FDP) had 48 antenna units arrayed in six columns by eight rows, with 960kW of transmitter power. It was fairly comparable to other
       <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_heater#Current_facilities" title="Ionospheric heater">
        ionospheric heating facilities
       </a>
       . This was used for a number of successful scientific experiments and ionospheric exploration campaigns over the years.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <b>
        Final IRI
       </b>
       (FIRI) is the final build of the IRI. It has 180 antenna units, organized in 15 columns by 12 rows, yielding a theoretical maximum gain of 31
       <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">
        dB
       </a>
       . A total of 3.6MW of transmitter power will feed it, but the power is focused in the upward direction by the geometry of the large
       <a href="/wiki/Phased_array" title="Phased array">
        phased array
       </a>
       of antennas which allow the antennas to work together in controlling the direction. As of March 2007
       <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , all the antennas were in place, the final phase was completed and the
       <a href="/wiki/Antenna_array_(electromagnetic)" title="Antenna array (electromagnetic)">
        antenna array
       </a>
       was undergoing testing aimed at fine-tuning its performance to comply with safety requirements required by regulatory agencies. The facility officially began full operations in its final 3.6 MW transmitter power completed status in the summer of 2007, yielding an effective radiated power (ERP) of 5.1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatts" title="Gigawatts">
        Gigawatts
       </a>
       or 97.1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DBW" title="DBW">
        dBW
       </a>
       at maximum output. However, the site typically operates at a fraction of that value due to the lower
       <a href="/wiki/Antenna_gain" title="Antenna gain">
        antenna gain
       </a>
       exhibited at standard operational frequencies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         24
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_facilities">
       Related facilities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Related facilities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In America, there are two related
      <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_heater" title="Ionospheric heater">
       ionospheric heating
      </a>
      facilities: the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HIPAS" title="HIPAS">
       HIPAS
      </a>
      , near
      <a href="/wiki/Fairbanks,_Alaska" title="Fairbanks, Alaska">
       Fairbanks, Alaska
      </a>
      , which was dismantled in 2009, and (currently offline for reconstruction) one at the
      <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">
       Arecibo Observatory
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
       Puerto Rico
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Incoherent_Scatter_Scientific_Association" title="European Incoherent Scatter Scientific Association">
       European Incoherent Scatter Scientific Association
      </a>
      (EISCAT) operates an ionospheric heating facility, capable of transmitting over 1GW
      <a href="/wiki/Effective_radiated_power" title="Effective radiated power">
       effective radiated power
      </a>
      (ERP), near
      <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8" title="Troms">
       Troms
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
       Norway
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      has the
      <a href="/wiki/Sura_Ionospheric_Heating_Facility" title="Sura Ionospheric Heating Facility">
       Sura Ionospheric Heating Facility
      </a>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Vasilsursk" title="Vasilsursk">
       Vasilsursk
      </a>
      near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nizhniy_Novgorod" title="Nizhniy Novgorod">
       Nizhniy Novgorod
      </a>
      , capable of transmitting 190MW ERP.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conspiracy_theories">
       Conspiracy theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conspiracy theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_conspiracy_theories#Development_of_weapons_technology" title="List of conspiracy theories">
       List of conspiracy theories: Development of weapons technology
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      HAARP is the subject of numerous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theories" title="Conspiracy theories">
       conspiracy theories
      </a>
      . Various individuals have speculated about hidden motives and capabilities of the project. For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Rosalie_Bertell" title="Rosalie Bertell">
       Rosalie Bertell
      </a>
      warned in 1996 about the deployment of HAARP as a military weapon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Chossudovsky" title="Michel Chossudovsky">
       Michel Chossudovsky
      </a>
      stated in a book published by the
      <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Monetary_and_Economic_Reform" title="Committee on Monetary and Economic Reform">
       Committee on Monetary and Economic Reform
      </a>
      that "recent scientific evidence suggests that HAARP is fully operational and has the ability of triggering floods, droughts, hurricanes and earthquakes."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over time, HAARP has been blamed for triggering catastrophes such as
      <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">
       floods
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">
       droughts
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">
       hurricanes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">
       thunderstorms
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
       earthquakes
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
       Iran
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
       Pakistan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
       Haiti
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
       Turkey
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
       Greece
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
       Philippines
      </a>
      , major
      <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
       power outages
      </a>
      , the downing of
      <a href="/wiki/TWA_Flight_800" title="TWA Flight 800">
       TWA Flight 800
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War_syndrome" title="Gulf War syndrome">
       Gulf War syndrome
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Chronic_fatigue_syndrome" title="Chronic fatigue syndrome">
       chronic fatigue syndrome
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naiditch_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Naiditch-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alaska_Dispatch_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alaska_Dispatch-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNews_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-USNews-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Allegations include the following:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A Russian military journal wrote that ionospheric testing would "trigger a cascade of electrons that could flip Earth's magnetic poles".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shachtman_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Shachtman-31">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         31
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">
        European Parliament
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Legislature" title="Alaska Legislature">
        Alaska state legislature
       </a>
       held hearings about HAARP, the former citing "environmental concerns".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physicsworld.com_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-physicsworld.com-32">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         32
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Nick Begich Jr., the son of former
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Representative" title="U.S. Representative">
        U.S. Representative
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Nick_Begich" title="Nick Begich">
        Nick Begich
       </a>
       and brother of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senator" title="U.S. Senator">
        U.S. Senator
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Begich" title="Mark Begich">
        Mark Begich
       </a>
       , author of
       <i>
        Angels Don't Play This HAARP
       </i>
       , has claimed that HAARP could trigger earthquakes and turn the upper atmosphere into a giant lens so that "the sky would literally appear to burn", and maintains a website that claims HAARP is a mind control device.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shachtman_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Shachtman-31">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         31
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bio-33">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         33
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Former
       <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Minnesota" title="Governor of Minnesota">
        Governor of Minnesota
       </a>
       and noted conspiracy theorist
       <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Ventura" title="Jesse Ventura">
        Jesse Ventura
       </a>
       questioned whether the government is using the site to manipulate the weather or to bombard people with mind-controlling radio waves. An Air Force spokeswoman said Ventura made an official request to visit the research station but was rejected- "he and his crew showed up at HAARP anyway and were denied access".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JuneauEmpire_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JuneauEmpire-34">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         34
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Physicist
       <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Eastlund" title="Bernard Eastlund">
        Bernard Eastlund
       </a>
       claimed that HAARP includes technology based on his own patents that has the capability to modify weather and neutralize satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopSci_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-PopSci-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       It has been proposed as a cause of
       <a href="/wiki/The_Hum" title="The Hum">
        low frequency background hums
       </a>
       said to be heard in various locales.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SYSK_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SYSK-35">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         35
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
       Stanford University
      </a>
      professor
      <a href="/wiki/Umran_Inan" title="Umran Inan">
       Umran Inan
      </a>
      told
      <a href="/wiki/Popular_Science" title="Popular Science">
       Popular Science
      </a>
      that weather-control conspiracy theories were "completely uninformed," explaining that "theres absolutely nothing we can do to disturb the Earths [weather] systems. Even though the power HAARP radiates is very large, its minuscule compared with the power of a lightning flashand there are 50 to 100 lightning flashes every second. HAARPs intensity is very small."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopSci_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-PopSci-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Computer scientist David Naiditch characterizes HAARP as "a magnet for conspiracy theorists", saying that HAARP attracts their attention because "its purpose seems deeply mysterious to the scientifically uninformed".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naiditch_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Naiditch-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Weinberger" title="Sharon Weinberger">
       Sharon Weinberger
      </a>
      called HAARP "the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moby_Dick" title="Moby Dick">
       Moby Dick
      </a>
      of conspiracy theories" and said the popularity of conspiracy theories often overshadows the benefits HAARP may provide to the scientific community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nature-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wired-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Austin Baird writing in the
      <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Dispatch" title="Alaska Dispatch">
       Alaska Dispatch
      </a>
      said, "What makes HAARP susceptible to conspiracy criticism is simple. The facility doesn't open its doors in the same way as other federally-funded research facilities around the country, and it doesn't go to great efforts to explain the importance of its research to the public."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alaska_Dispatch_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Alaska_Dispatch-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

           "NRL Scientists Produce Densest Artificial Ionospheric Plasma Clouds Using HAARP"
          </a>
          (Press release). U.S. Naval Research Lab. 2013-02-25

          <q>
           Using the 3.6-megawatt high-frequency (HF) HAARP transmitter, the plasma clouds, or balls of plasma, are being studied for use as artificial mirrors at altitudes 50 kilometers below the natural ionosphere and are to be used for reflection of HF radar and communications signals. Past attempts to produce electron density enhancements have yielded densities of 410 electrons per cubic centimeter (cm) using HF radio transmissions near the second, third, and fourth harmonics of the electron cyclotron frequency. This frequency near 1.44 MHz is the rate that electrons gyrate around the Earth's magnetic field. The NRL group succeeded in producing artificial plasma clouds with densities exceeding 910 electrons cm using HAARP transmission at the sixth harmonic of the electron cyclotron frequency.
