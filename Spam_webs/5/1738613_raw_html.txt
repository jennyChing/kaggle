     <p>
      A
      <b>
       Swashbuckler
      </b>
      (a.k.a.
      <b>
       swasher
      </b>
      ) is a heroic
      <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">
       archetype
      </a>
      in European adventure literature starting in the 16th century
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      typified by use of a sword and chivalric ideals. The archetype also became common as a
      <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
       film
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Genre" title="Genre">
       genre
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_swashbuckler_as_an_archetype">
       The swashbuckler as an archetype
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swashbuckler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The swashbuckler as an archetype">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A 'swashbuckler' is generally a protagonist who is heroic and idealistic and who rescues damsels in distress. Swashbucklers as an archetype are not pirates. His opponent is typically characterised as the dastardly villain. There is a long
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swashbuckler_(list)" title="Swashbuckler (list)">
       list of swashbucklers
      </a>
      who combine outstanding courage,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swordfighting" title="Swordfighting">
       swordfighting
      </a>
      skill, resourcefulness, and a distinctive sense of honor and justice, as for example
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrano_de_Bergerac" title="Cyrano de Bergerac">
        Cyrano de Bergerac
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Three_Musketeers" title="The Three Musketeers">
        The Three Musketeers
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Scarlet_Pimpernel" title="The Scarlet Pimpernel">
        The Scarlet Pimpernel
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood" title="Robin Hood">
       Robin Hood
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Zorro" title="Zorro">
       Zorro
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The hero's activities are primarily chivalaric: rescuing fair maidens, saving the king, or defending the peasants from oppression. Even seventeenth and eighteenth century highwayman and pirate heroes were made to conform to this stereotype.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">
       Historical background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swashbuckler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow,_1933.djvu">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3607" data-file-width="2598" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow%2C_1933.djvu/page1-220px-Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow%2C_1933.djvu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow%2C_1933.djvu/page1-330px-Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow%2C_1933.djvu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow%2C_1933.djvu/page1-440px-Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow%2C_1933.djvu.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stevenson_-_The_Black_Arrow,_1933.djvu" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        R.L. Stevenson -
        <i>
         The Black Arrow
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A possible explanation for the term is that it derives from a fighting style using a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Side-sword" title="Side-sword">
       side-sword
      </a>
      with a
      <a href="/wiki/Buckler" title="Buckler">
       buckler
      </a>
      in the off-hand, which was applied with much "swashing and making a noise on the buckler".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Usually swashbuckling romances are set in Europe from the late
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
       Renaissance
      </a>
      up through the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_Reason" title="Age of Reason">
       Age of Reason
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">
       Napoleonic Wars
      </a>
      . Jeffrey Richards traces the swashbuckling novel to the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
       Romanticism
      </a>
      , and an outgrowth of the historical novel, particularly those of Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">
       Walter Scott
      </a>
      , "... medieval tales of chivalry, love and adventure rediscovered in the eighteenth century".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richards_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Richards-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This type of historical novel was further developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">
       Alexandre Dumas
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/John_Galsworthy" title="John Galsworthy">
       John Galsworthy
      </a>
      said of
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">
       Robert Louis Stevenson
      </a>
      's 1888 swashbuckling romance,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Black_Arrow" title="The Black Arrow">
        The Black Arrow
       </a>
      </i>
      , that it was "a livelier picture of medieval times than I remember elsewhere in fiction."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hope" title="Anthony Hope">
       Anthony Hope
      </a>
      's 1894, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner_of_Zenda" title="The Prisoner of Zenda">
        The Prisoner of Zenda
       </a>
      </i>
      initiated an additional subset of the swashbuckling novel, the
      <a href="/wiki/Ruritanian_romance" title="Ruritanian romance">
       Ruritanian romance
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The perceived significant and widespread role of swordsmanship in civilian society as well as warfare in the renaissance and enlightenment periods led to fencing being performed on theatre stages as part of plays. Soon actors were taught to fence in an entertaining, dramatic manner. Eventually fencing became an established part of a classical formation for actors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Consequently, when movie theatres mushroomed, ambitious actors took the chance to present their accordant skills on the screen. Since silent movies were no proper medium for long dialogues, the classic stories about heroes who would defend their honour with sword in hand were simplified and sheer action would gain priority. This was the birth of a new kind of film hero: the swashbuckler.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Four of the most famous instructors for swashbuckling swordplay are
      <a href="/wiki/William_Hobbs_(choreographer)" title="William Hobbs (choreographer)">
       William Hobbs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_De_Longis" title="Anthony De Longis">
       Anthony De Longis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Anderson_(fencer)" title="Bob Anderson (fencer)">
       Bob Anderson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Diamond_(actor)" title="Peter Diamond (actor)">
       Peter Diamond
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Film">
       Film
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swashbuckler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Swashbuckler_film" title="Swashbuckler film">
       Swashbuckler film
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The genre has, apart from swordplay, always been characterized by influences that can be traced back to the
      <a href="/wiki/Chivalry" title="Chivalry">
       chivalry
      </a>
      tales of Medieval Europe, such as the legends of
      <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood" title="Robin Hood">
       Robin Hood
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">
       King Arthur
      </a>
      . It soon created its own drafts based on classic examples like
      <a href="/wiki/The_Mark_of_Zorro_(1920_film)" title="The Mark of Zorro (1920 film)">
       <i>
        The Mark of Zorro
       </i>
       (1920)
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Three_Musketeers_(1921_film)" title="The Three Musketeers (1921 film)">
        The Three Musketeers (1921)
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scaramouche_(1923_film)" title="Scaramouche (1923 film)">
        Scaramouche (1923)
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Scarlet_Pimpernel_(1934_film)" title="The Scarlet Pimpernel (1934 film)">
        The Scarlet Pimpernel (1934)
       </a>
      </i>
      . Some films did also use
      <a href="/wiki/Motif_(narrative)" title="Motif (narrative)">
       motifs
      </a>
      of pirate stories.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Often these films were adaptations of classic historic novels published by well-known authors such as
      <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">
       Alexandre Dumas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Sabatini" title="Rafael Sabatini">
       Rafael Sabatini
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroness_Emma_Orczy" title="Baroness Emma Orczy">
       Baroness Emma Orczy
      </a>
      , Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">
       Walter Scott
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Johnston_McCulley" title="Johnston McCulley">
       Johnston McCulley
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Rostand" title="Edmond Rostand">
       Edmond Rostand
      </a>
      . Swashbucklers are one of the most flamboyant Hollywood film genres,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      unlike
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinema_verite" title="Cinema verite">
       cinema verite
      </a>
      or modern realistic filmmaking. The genre attracted large audiences who relished the blend of escapist adventure, historic romance, and daring stunts in cinemas before it became a fixture on TV screens.
     </p>
     <p>
      As a first variation of the classic swashbuckler there have also been female swashbucklers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Maureen_O%27Hara" title="Maureen O'Hara">
       Maureen O'Hara
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Against_All_Flags" title="Against All Flags">
        Against All Flags
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Peters" title="Jean Peters">
       Jean Peters
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_the_Indies" title="Anne of the Indies">
        Anne of the Indies
       </a>
      </i>
      were very early action film heroines.
     </p>
     <p>
      Eventually the typical swashbuckler motifs were used up because they had so often been shown on TV screens. Later films such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Princess_Bride" title="The Princess Bride">
        The Princess Bride
       </a>
      </i>
      , the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean_(film_series)" title="Pirates of the Caribbean (film series)">
        Pirates of the Caribbean
       </a>
      </i>
      series and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Mask_of_Zorro" title="The Mask of Zorro">
        The Mask of Zorro
       </a>
      </i>
      include modern takes on the swashbuckler archetype.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Actors">
       Actors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swashbuckler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Actors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Actors notable for their portrayals of swashbucklers include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt-Constant_Coquelin" title="Benot-Constant Coquelin">
        Benot-Constant Coquelin
       </a>
       (1841 1909), was a French
       <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">
        actor
       </a>
       , and "one of the greatest theatrical figures of the age."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       He played "Cyrano de Begerac over 400 times and later toured North America in the role.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In early 1883
       <a href="/wiki/James_O%27Neill_(actor)#The_Count_of_Monte_Cristo" title="James O'Neill (actor)">
        James O'Neill
       </a>
       (1847 - 1920) took over the lead role in "The Count of Monte Cristo" at Booth's Theater in New York. His interpretation of the part caused a sensation with the theater-going public and a company was immediately set up to take the play on tour. O'Neill bought the rights to the play. "Monte Cristo" remained a popular favorite and would continue to make its appearance on tour as regular as clockwork. O'Neill went on to play this role over 6,000 times.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       E. H. Sothern (1859  1933) was especially known for his heroic portrayal of Rudolph Rassendyl in the first stage adaptation of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner_of_Zenda" title="The Prisoner of Zenda">
         The Prisoner of Zenda
        </a>
       </i>
       , which he first played in 1895.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAdnb_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EAdnb-15">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         15
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The role made him a star.
      </li>
     </ul>
