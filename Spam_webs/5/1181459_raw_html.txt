itemprop="articlebody"&gt;&lt;h3&gt;&lt;strong&gt;From passion...&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/h3&gt;&lt;p&gt;Picture this; someone living in a landlocked state, hundreds of miles from salt water powers up their computer, logs on to the internet and within seconds is immersed in&lt;strong&gt; a virtual dive with sea turtles&lt;/strong&gt; in Mexico.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Snow falls outside the window of a student researching the life cycles of tropical corals. Without having to board a plane or pay thousands of dollars in travel expenses, they&rsquo;re able to view &lt;strong&gt;a live coral spawning event in the Cayman Islands&lt;/strong&gt;, and remotely gather data such as water salinity and pH in real-time from the waters around the coral.&nbsp;Click play on this video to view &lt;strong&gt;Weedy Sea Dragon&lt;/strong&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;&lt;iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/6AMTZwJes4M?rel=0" class="article-video" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Sound like an impossible fantasy? One company has made it a reality.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;An online marine science portal called&nbsp;&lt;a href="http://www.oceanclassrooms.com/"&gt;Ocean Classrooms&lt;/a&gt;&nbsp;has created a network of live, underwater webcams that stream high-definition video of popular dive destinations like the Cayman Islands East End and Akumal Bay in Quintana Roo, Mexico.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Ocean Classrooms is the product of a passionate group of professional dive instructors, conservationists and marine educators. Its mission is to improve the level of marine education for everyone-not just the diving community-by making the marine environment accessible to anyone with an Internet connection.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;h3&gt;&lt;strong&gt;...to reality.&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/h3&gt;&lt;p&gt;To develop the Blue Eyes Network, the&nbsp;&lt;u&gt;Ocean Classroom&lt;/u&gt;&nbsp;team locates enthusiastic dive centers, research stations or educational programs that have access to unique marine ecosystems. Once a potential site has been evaluated, a camera is installed and a dedicated Internet connection begins streaming the high-definition video out through the Ocean Classrooms website, allowing viewers to simulate the experience of diving directly from their computer or smart device.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;At the moment, with the service offered by Youtube, you can easily find several live cam videos, streaming on your regular website. Now you can sit back and enjoy the view of jellyfish, shark, ray, coral, reef and other species. Play this video to view &lt;strong&gt;Jelly Fish cam&lt;/strong&gt;, live!&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;&lt;iframe src="//www.youtube.com/embed/ceP_zvyM3A4?rel=0" class="article-video" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;The Blue Eyes network is currently streaming video from Florida, where they capture video of goliath groupers; Grand Cayman, where they streamed the world&rsquo;s first live webcast of a coral spawning event to over 3,000 viewers; Akumal Bay on the Yucatan Peninsula, where a massive seagrass bed provides food and shelter for eight species of endangered sea turtles; and Cooper Island in the British Virgin Islands where viewers watch tarpon hunt in the shallows at night.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;In order to stream the footage live, science nodes are installed&nbsp;alongside cameras to supplement the streaming video. These nodes collect data including pH, water temperature and salinity, and stream the data in real-time next to the video feeds.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;&lt;img alt="Underwater camera used for streaming" src="/Handlers/Image.ashx?tp=art&amp;p=38/original/&amp;n=rtf_9170a7c4-84c8-4128-a812-886e4f93f824.jpg" style="float:right; margin:10px; width:300px" /&gt;&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;The organization&nbsp;believes a complete understanding of the marine world requires spending some real time under the surface of the ocean. This is why they sponsor Teen4Oceans, a non-profit organization whose goal is to create ocean ambassadors out of today&rsquo;s youth. Teens4Oceans chapters achieve this by developing school programs created and implemented by the students that encourage the participants to become scuba certified, develop recycling and sustainability programs for their schools and promote ocean literacy through dive and research expeditions to Blue Eyes Network camera sites.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;h3&gt;&lt;strong&gt;For a good cause.&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/h3&gt;&lt;p&gt;To date,&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Ocean Classrooms&lt;/em&gt;&nbsp;and Teens4Oceans have sent students to tag turtles and study&lt;strong&gt; the impact of tourism on the turtle population in Akumal&lt;/strong&gt;,&lt;strong&gt; built coral nurseries and observed the effect of climate change on coral populations on Grand Cayman&lt;/strong&gt;, and have conducted a number of &lt;strong&gt;fish studies&lt;/strong&gt; in the Virgin Islands and the Florida Keys.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;The Blue Eyes Network is always expanding, with cameras scheduled to be installed in Australia, Hawaii, and possibly even as far away as Dubai in the United Arab Emirates. Each new location provides a fantastic opportunity for viewers of the web cameras, as well as the Teens4Oceans expedition participants.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;To learn more, visit the webcams on&nbsp;&lt;a href="http://oceanclassrooms.com/blue-eyes-media/underwater-webcams"&gt;Ocean Classrooms website&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Nowadays, with camera from California Academy of Sciences, you can have a look at sharks rays and coral reef on their &lt;a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jyWHDIECRYQ&amp;list=PLS14biAqBAtHTw_kaR5UZLdxyMhvEYeI_"&gt;Youtube&lt;/a&gt; account.
