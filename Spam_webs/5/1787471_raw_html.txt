      <p>
       It seems like every decade there is a new vitamin that one needs to make sure they get plenty of. In the late '70s and early '80s it was vitamin C, with the mistaken belief it could prevent colds. In the late '80s and mid-'90s it was vitamin B-12. People believed that if they were tired, all they needed to do was just take some B-12 and that would be the magic elixir to give them energy. Now the vitamin du jour is vitamin D. But before you go drink that gallon of milk or buy some vitamin D over-the-counter, let us first learn a little bit more about the indications and proper use of supplemental vitamin D.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vitamin D is one of the fat-soluble vitamins. Simply stated, this means it is easily absorbed from the gut. There are other soluble vitamins besides vitamin D, notably vitamin A, vitamin E and vitamin K.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are two basic types of vitamin D preparations: vitamin D2 as well as vitamin D3. Vitamin D2 is known as ergocalciferol, and vitamin D3 is known as cholecalciferol. We are now learning that there is a distinct difference between the two. Studies have also shown that vitamin D2 is approximately 30 percent as effective as vitamin D3 in maintaining serum vitamin D levels.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vitamin D, as stated previously, is primarily absorbed from the gut. Humans also require exposure to the sun to further help supplement our vitamin D levels. In the skin vitamin D3 is formed from a precursor that is converted into an active form that we can use. This process also involves going through the liver where a process called 25-hydroxylation takes place.
      </p>
      <p>
       It then moves to the kidney for 1-hydroxylation which then produces the active product known as 1-25-dihydroxy vitamin D. Some individuals may have trouble with this conversion because of dark skin.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a child your mother was partially right, and what I mean is that your mother always told you to drink milk so you can get vitamin D in order to have good strong bones. We now know that vitamin D not only helps with the development of skeletal tissue but is very important in non-skeletal tissue. We now know that too little vitamin D may also assist with the development of certain types of cancers. Vitamin D also helps the nervous, endocrine, immunological, as well as cardiovascular systems do their job more effectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       Heretofore, I have basically concentrated on vitamin D deficiencies. Not to be overlooked are the consequences of vitamin D overdosage which can cause vitamin D intoxication.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vitamin D toxicity can cause a multitude of symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, and increased urinary frequency. It may also cause dizziness, ringing in the ears, constipation, weakness, fatigue, excessive sleepiness, headache, a metallic taste in the mouth, abdominal cramps and diarrhea. It can also raise your calcium levels which can lead to confusion as well as abnormalities with your heart.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before you go out and grab a handful of vitamin D, you need to discuss this with your doctor. There are certain drugs which may interact adversely with vitamin D. One of these is digoxin, which some people take for cardiac arrhythmias. Thiazide diuretics along with vitamin D may also lead to hypercalcemia or an elevated calcium level. Other anti-hypertensive medications known as calcium channel blockers, specifically verapamil and diltiazem, can have their effectiveness reduced with too
       <span class="-newsgate--ccix-command-147-1-0-8">
        much vitamin D.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       Steroids like prednisone can impair vitamin D metabolism, which can lead to bone loss, which in turn can lead to osteoporosis. Taking vitamin D with calcium may be beneficial to the bones, especially for postmenopausal women. However, there is a 17 percent increase in the risk of kidney stones that one should be on the lookout for and be monitored by your physician.
      </p>
      <p>
       For most adults, both male and female over the age of 13,600 international units (IU)  is the recommended dietary daily allowance. For those over 70 years of age, 800 international units are recommended. In addition to supplemental vitamin D as well as getting out into the sunlight, there are many food groups which are high in vitamin D. Although mama preached the milk, the flesh of fatty fish such as salmon, tuna and mackerel are good sources of vitamin D.
      </p>
      <p>
       Small amounts of vitamin D can also be obtained by consuming other dairy products such as cheese, egg yolks as well as beef liver. Vitamin D from these food sources is primarily in the form of vitamin D3. Also, many foods now are fortified with vitamin D. Milk fortification began in the 1930s when the U.S. government identified rickets as a major public health problem.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result of the addition of vitamin D to our dairy products, we very rarely see rickets. Fortification has also been expanded to other food items such as yogurt, margarine, as well as orange juice.
      </p>
      <p>
       If you have any concerns about vitamin D or how much you should be taking, please talk to your doctor. The only way you will know what your level is is to have it checked. Now this is one test you can ask your doctor to perform and he won't get mad.
      </p>
