      <blockquote>
       <span style="color: rgb(221, 230, 180);">
        <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="3">
         <br/>
         Sonnet On A Wet Summer
         <br/>
         <br/>
         All ye who far from town in rural hall,
         <br/>
         Like me, were wont to dwell near pleasant field,
         <br/>
         Enjoying all the sunny day did yield,
         <br/>
         With me the change lament, in irksome thrall,
         <br/>
         By rains incessant held; for now no call
         <br/>
         From early swain invites my hand to wield
         <br/>
         The scythe. In parlour dim I sit concealed,
         <br/>
         And mark the lessening sand from hour-glass fall;
         <br/>
         Or 'neath my window view the wistful train
         <br/>
         Of dripping poultry, whom the vine's broad leaves
         <br/>
         Shelter no more. Mute is the mournful plain;
         <br/>
         Silent the swallow sits beneath the thatch,
         <br/>
         And vacant hind hangs pensive o'er his hatch,
         <br/>
         Counting the frequent drips from reeded eaves.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         ~ John Codrington Bampfylde
         <br/>
        </font>
        <br/>
       </span>
       <br/>
      </blockquote>

     <div class="title">
      <a class="title" href="http://www.topendsports.com/sport/soccer/history.htm" target="_blank">
       Soccer History Timeline
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="text">
      <div style="text-align:center;">
       <br/>
       <a href="http://www.topendsports.com/sport/soccer/history.htm">
        <img src="http://www.topendsports.com/images/header.png"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <br/>
      <div align="center">
       <span style="color: rgb(255, 255, 255);">
        <font face="veranda,arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="2">
         Football  Resources  History
        </font>
       </span>
       <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="4">
        <span style="color: rgb(180, 231, 255);">
         <em>
          <br/>
          A Football History:
         </em>
         <font size="3">
         </font>
        </span>
       </font>
       <span style="color: rgb(180, 231, 255);">
        <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif">
         <em>
          <font size="3">
           <br/>
           from its origin to now
          </font>
         </em>
        </font>
       </span>
       <span style="color: rgb(90, 202, 255);">
        <br/>
        <em>
         <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="3">
          <br/>
          There is no clear documentation stating the date and place of origination of today's most popular sport - world football. However, most historians agree that some type of a ball game has been played for at least over 3000 years. The origins of the game can be seen in games played in
          <br/>
          China, Japan, Egypt, Greece before our modern game developed in England.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Here is a brief outline of some of the events that have shaped
          <br/>
          the game of soccer throughout history.
         </font>
        </em>
       </span>
       <br/>
       <span style="color: rgb(184, 222, 240);">
        <font face="tahoma,arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="4">
         <br/>
         A Football Timeline:
        </font>
       </span>
      </div>
      <br/>
      <span style="color: rgb(61, 192, 255);">
       <font face="verdana,arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="2">
        <strong>
         <br/>
         5000-300 B.C.
        </strong>
        There is evidence in China that military forces around 2nd and 3rd century BC (Han Dynasty) played a game, originally named "Tsu Chu", that involved kicking a leather ball stuffed with fur into a small hole. Like Soccer, no hands were permitted during the play of the game.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         2500 B.C.
        </strong>
        There was possibly a version of a type of ball game played by young women in Egypt during the age of Baqet III, as images of this sport were depicted on his tomb, though there is not much known of this sport except that it was played with a ball.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1000 B.C.
        </strong>
        The Japanese version of 'soccer' is was called Kemari, a game much like modern hackysacks, played with two to twelve players, and played a larger ball stuffed with sawdust. There was also a field designated by four trees (cherry, maple, pine and willow).
        <strong>
         <br/>
         B.C.
        </strong>
        In ancient Greece, they played a game called Episkyros, in which two equally numbered teams would try to throw the ball over the heads of the other team. There was a white line between the teams and another white line behind each team. Teams would change the ball often until one of the team is forced behind the line at their end.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         50 B.C.
        </strong>
        China's Tsu Chu players and Japan's Kemari players were the first to have an "International" game of their versions of soccer, believed to have occurred roughly 50 B.C.. There is a definite date of such a game occurring in 611 A.D.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         600 - 1600 A.D.
        </strong>
        In Mexico &amp; Central America the rubber ball was created, and used in a game on a recessed court 40-50 feet long shaped like a capital "I". In the middle of each wall, was a mounted stone or wooden ring and the object was to project the hard rubber ball through the ring.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         700s
        </strong>
        The first Football games played in Britain was played by the locals of east of England, starting after a 'legendary' game that involved kicking around the severed head of a Danish prince that they have defeated in a war. These games were violent, where injury and death were not uncommon
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1331
        </strong>
        Despite the violence of these games, they were still popular. This led King Edward III of England to pass laws in 1331 to stop the game
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1424
        </strong>
        King James I of Scotland also passed a law banning the game
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1500
        </strong>
        In Italy they played a game called "calcio" with teams of 27+ people. The game involved kicking, carrying or passing a ball across a goal line. In 1580, Giovanni Bardi published a set of rules of the game of calcio.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1572
        </strong>
        Queen Elizabeth I of England, enacted laws that could sentence a football player to jail for a week followed by penance in a church.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1600
        </strong>
        In Alaska and Canada the native Eskimos played a game called aqsaqtuk on ice, using balls
        <br/>
        stuffed with grass, caribou hair, and moss. One legend tells of two villages playing against each other
        <br/>
        with goals 10 miles apart.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1605
        </strong>
        Football became legal again in England
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1620
        </strong>
        In North America, native American Indians in the original Jamestown settlement played a game called pasuckuakohowog, meaning "they gather to play ball with the foot." It was a rough game, played the beach, the field a half-mile wide with goals 1 mile apart, with as many as
        <br/>
        1000 players at a time.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1815
        </strong>
        Eton College of England established a set of rules for the games.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1820
        </strong>
        In the USA, football was played among the Northeastern universities and colleges of Harvard, Princeton, Amherst and Brown.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1848
        </strong>
        The rules were further standardized and a new version was adopted by all the schools, college and universities, known as the Cambridge Rules.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1862
        </strong>
        The first soccer club formed anywhere outside of England was the
        <br/>
        Oneida Football Club, Boston USA.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1863
        </strong>
        October 26 of 1863, the Football Association was formed when eleven London schools and clubs came together at the Freemason's Tavern to establish a single set of rules to administer any football match that were to be played among them. On December 8 1863, Association Football and Rugby Football finally split onto two different organizations. Later in the year, the first ever soccer match was played on Barnes common at Mortlake, London on 19th December 1863 between Barnes Football Club and Richmond Football Club.
        <br/>
        The game ended in a 0-0 draw.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1869
        </strong>
        The Football Association rules were further amended to exclude any handling of the ball.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1872
        </strong>
        The first official international football match was played, between the national teams of Scotland and England, played in Glasgow Scotland. The game was played on  30 November 1872, and finished with a 0-0 draw.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1883
        </strong>
        The four British associations agreed on a uniform code and formed the
        <br/>
        International Football Association Board.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1885
        </strong>
        The first international match played by teams outside of Great Britain was between USA and Canada, played in Newark and ended with Canada winning 1-0.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1888
        </strong>
        Introduction of the penalty kick.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1904
        </strong>
        Establishment of FIFA by delegates from France, Belgium, Denmark, the Netherlands, Spain, Sweden and Switzerland at a meeting in Paris on the 21st of May.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1900
        </strong>
        Soccer played at the Olympic Games for the first time
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1930
        </strong>
        In 1930, The Federation Internationale de Football Association (FIFA) held soccer's first World Cup tournament in Montevideo, Uruguay, with 13 teams.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1932
        </strong>
        Soccer was taken off the program for the Olympic Games in Los Angeles, due to a controversy between FIFA and the IOC over the definition of amateur and the reluctance of many strong soccer countries to travel the US because of the expense involved.
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1991
        </strong>
        The inaugural Women's World Cup in 1991 in China was won by the United States
        <strong>
         <br/>
         1996
        </strong>
        The American women's team won the first-ever women's soccer event at the Olympics.
        <br/>
       </font>
       <br/>
      </span>

      <a class="title" href="http://www.inquisitr.com/2172491/huge-purple-sea-slugs/" target="_blank">
       Huge Purple Sea Slugs Freak Out Beachgoers In California
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="text">
      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
       <br/>
       <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-NqefI2HuXNs/VX5srJO9JFI/AAAAAAABxWs/qx9nyqNuYdY/s1600/inquisitr.bmp" imageanchor="0" style="margin-left: 0em; margin-right: 0em;">
        <img border="0" height="61" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-NqefI2HuXNs/VX5srJO9JFI/AAAAAAABxWs/qx9nyqNuYdY/s1600/inquisitr.bmp" width="215"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <p align="center">
       <br/>
       <span style="color: rgb(141, 73, 110);">
        <em>
         <font face="georgia,times new roman,times,serif">
          <strong>
           <font size="4">
            Huge Purple Sea Slugs Freak Out Beachgoers In California
           </font>
          </strong>
         </font>
        </em>
        <font size="3">
         <br/>
         By Lisa Carley
        </font>
        <font face="georgia,times new roman,times,serif" size="2">
         <br/>
         Posted in: Animal News
         <br/>
         June 14, 2015
        </font>
        <br/>
       </span>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <blockquote>
        <br/>
        <div align="center" style="text-align:center;">
         <a href="http://www.inquisitr.com/2172491/huge-purple-sea-slugs/">
          <img src="http://cdn.inquisitr.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/670X4094-665x385.png"/>
         </a>
        </div>
        <p align="center">
         <span style="color: rgb(151, 63, 131);">
          <font face="georgia,times new roman,times,serif" size="3">
           <em>
            Photo via Contra Costa Times/Morgan Dill
           </em>
           <br/>
           <em>
            <strong>
             <br/>
             California Sea Hares
            </strong>
           </em>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           They arent purple people eaters  theyre giant purple sea slugs. Despite their weird appearances, these blobs showing up on East Bay beaches are more interested in plants than they are in the curious bipedal animals snapping pictures around them.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           According to Contra Costa Times, the purple sea slugs, or California sea hares, have been causing some people to wonder if theyve found purple human hearts on the beach or just, well, weird purple blobs that cant immediately be explained. People have called emergency services wanting to know just what these gooey blobs are and where on earth they come from.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           While the sea slugs are unusually large and abundant, they are perfectly natural to the area. Carolyn Jones, a spokeswoman for the East Bay Regional Park District, explained, Its native to our area. Its not endangered, but they are rarely seen other than an occasional one here or there.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The reason these purple sea slugs are washing up on East Bay beaches may be because the coastal waters are warmer, according to the Associated Press.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Morgan Dill, a naturalist at the Crab Cove Visitor Center in Alameda, said, We cant say for sure why were seeing so many, but the Bay temperatures are definitely warmer this year.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Terry Gosliner, senior curator at the California Academy of Sciences in San Francisco, believes the huge increase in sea slugs could be in tune with a periodic population increase in the mollusks, but he doesnt discount warming in the least.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Were seeing more of these kinds of warming events, and I suspect it may be part of long-term global change. These are signals.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The sea slugs can weigh as much as 15 pounds, but the ones washing up in East Bay arent as big. They are sometimes called sea hares because of their antennae, which can look like rabbit ears.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Most of the time, the purple sea slugs only wash up on shore after they lay their eggs and are dying.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           It is unknown how many of these creatures have washed up on shore since last fall, but some residents have never seen one of these purple blobs until now.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Oakland resident Joel Peter saw the purple sea slugs moving through a canal into Lake Merritt.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I had never seen one before, and then all of a sudden there were 22 of them with these brilliant colors. They really caught my eye.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </span>
         <br/>
        </p>
       </blockquote>

        <br/>
        <br/>
        We rarely invite Tiny Desk alumni back to the confines of Bob Boilen's work space, but we couldn't resist this time. Harpist Yolanda Kondonassis and Grammy-winning guitarist Jason Vieaux have both given solo Tiny Desk performances. Since then they've paired up for concerts and a new album of works composed especially for their combination of instruments.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        Although the harp and guitar are both instruments you pluck and strum, they seem to come from different corners of the classical world. The guitar conjures up the legacy of zesty Spanish music while the harp must contend with the clich of its angelic role in the orchestra. But when Kondonassis and Vieaux are together, the two instruments seem to merge into a singularly evocative voice. ( Kondonassis joked that they could call it a "guitarp" until someone quickly Googled and told us there actually is such a contraption.)
        <br/>
        <br/>
        Music for harp and guitar isn't easy to come by, so Kondonassis and Vieaux have been busy commissioning works like the opening "Elysian," part of a larger suite written for the duo by Gary Schocker.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        Anything that sounds even vaguely non-Western is a good fit for the duo because most cultures enjoy their own versions of the harp and guitar. Alan Hovhaness, an American composer with a global outlook, weaved Asian ideas into his Sonata for Harp and Guitar, subtitled "Spirit of Trees." And the duo ends by beating out the interlocking rhythms of the African candombe, which Argentine composer Mximo Diego Pujol placed in his Suite mgica.
        <br/>
        ~ TOM HUIZENGA

         <img src="http://frontpage.pch.com/images/stories/ap_urn_publicid_ap.org_d22ac40bfbfc49d8aec7679c7b5fcfc8/2-92f45941cd0e44ff9ba0ecc88f707412-preview.jpg"/>

        <span style="color: rgb(150, 187, 255);">
         <font face="georgia,times new roman,times,serif" size="2">

           Walter Thomas poses at his home in Woodstock, Ill., Wednesday, June 3, 2015.
           <br/>
           Thomas can check one thing off his bucket list after backing a car
           <br/>
           through a garage door on purpose with his family's help.
          </em>
         </font>

       <p>
        <font face="georgia,times new roman,times,serif" size="3">
         <span style="color: rgb(255, 0, 0);">
          WOODSTOCK, Ill. (AP)  A suburban Chicago man who says he always wanted to know what it is like to smash through a garage door now knows the feeling.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Ninety-one-year-old Walter Thomas of Woodstock was able to slam an SUV through a garage door with his family's help.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          The garage was slated to be torn down, so Thomas' action was of no consequence. The SUV was donated. It was then up to Thomas to back it through the garage door and check that activity off his bucket list.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Thomas described what he did this way: "I hit the gas, squealed the tires and bang  we went through the door."
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Thomas says he doesn't know what he could do to top that.

       <a class="title" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Little_Prince" target="_blank">
        The Little Prince - Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="text">
       <blockquote>
        <blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <p align="center">
           <br/>
           <span style="color: rgb(194, 210, 161);">
            <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="4">
             <strong>
              The Little Prince
             </strong>
             <br/>
             Author Antoine de Saint-Exupry
            </font>
            <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="3">
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Original title 	Le Petit Prince (as handwritten)
             <br/>
             Translator 	(English editions) Katherine Woods
             <br/>
             Publisher 	Reynal &amp; Hitchcock (U.S.)
             <br/>
             Gallimard (France)
             <br/>
             Publication date  1943 (U.S.: English &amp; French)
             <br/>
             1945 (France: French)
             <br/>
             Preceded by 	Pilote de guerre (1942)
             <br/>
             Followed by 	Lettre  un otage (1944)
             <br/>
             <br/>
             The Little Prince (French: Le Petit Prince; French pronunciation: [l.p.tips]), first published in 1943, is a novella and the most famous work of the French aristocrat, writer, poet and pioneering aviator Antoine de Saint-Exupry (19001944).
            </font>
           </span>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
        </blockquote>
       </blockquote>
       <br/>
       <div style="text-align:center;">
        <br/>
        <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-TCZ4kOX27Fw/VWfJHWnQduI/AAAAAAABxQ8/b9RRlyQn6Cg/s1600/Littleprince.JPG" imageanchor="0" style="margin-left: 0em; margin-right: 0em;">
         <img border="0" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-TCZ4kOX27Fw/VWfJHWnQduI/AAAAAAABxQ8/b9RRlyQn6Cg/s1600/Littleprince.JPG"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <blockquote>
        <blockquote>
         <blockquote>
          <br/>
          <p align="center">
           <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="3">
            <span style="color: rgb(194, 210, 161);">
             The novella is both the most-read and 3rd most-translated book in the world, and was voted the best book of the 20th century in France. Translated into more than 250 languages and dialects (as well as braille), selling nearly two million copies annually with sales totalling over 140 million copies worldwide, it has become one of the best-selling books ever published. After the outbreak of the Second World War Saint-Exupry was exiled to North America. In the midst of personal upheavals and failing health, he produced almost half of the writings for which he would be remembered, including a tender tale of loneliness, friendship, love, and loss, in the form of a young alien prince fallen to Earth. An earlier memoir by the author had recounted his aviation experiences in the Sahara Desert, and he is thought to have drawn on those same experiences in The Little Prince.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Since its first publication in the United States, the novella has been adapted to numerous art forms and media, including audio recordings, radio plays, live stage, film screen, television, ballet, and operatic works.
            </span>
           </font>
          </p>
          <br/>
          <div style="text-align:center;">
           <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-oIdd7JALPMU/VWfDnPByEQI/AAAAAAABxQk/-ja9rKvZj2Y/s1600/Sahara_Crash_-1935-_copyright_free_in_Egypt_3634_StEx_1_321.jpg" imageanchor="0" style="margin-left: 0em; margin-right: 0em;">
            <img border="0" height="386" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-oIdd7JALPMU/VWfDnPByEQI/AAAAAAABxQk/-ja9rKvZj2Y/s1600/Sahara_Crash_-1935-_copyright_free_in_Egypt_3634_StEx_1_321.jpg" width="314"/>
           </a>
           <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-g_HDYo-6XP8/VWfDhdxMxMI/AAAAAAABxQY/r91dvA5JuFY/s1600/Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry123.jpg" imageanchor="0" style="margin-left: 0em; margin-right: 0em;">
            <img border="0" height="258" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-g_HDYo-6XP8/VWfDhdxMxMI/AAAAAAABxQY/r91dvA5JuFY/s1600/Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry123.jpg" width="360"/>
           </a>
           <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-H39_NkT0xn4/VWfDhEld7TI/AAAAAAABxQU/Q27HuWcQxmo/s1600/50++francstexupery.jpg" imageanchor="0" style="margin-left: 0em; margin-right: 0em;">
            <img border="0" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-H39_NkT0xn4/VWfDhEld7TI/AAAAAAABxQU/Q27HuWcQxmo/s1600/50++francstexupery.jpg"/>
           </a>
          </div>
          <p align="center">
           <font face="arial,helvetica,sans-serif" size="3">
            <span style="color: rgb(194, 210, 161);">
             <br/>
             <em>
              Overview:
             </em>
             <br/>
             <br/>
             The Little Prince is a poetic tale, with watercolour illustrations by the author, in which a pilot stranded in the desert meets a young prince fallen to Earth from a tiny asteroid. The story is philosophical and includes social criticism, remarking on the strangeness of the adult world. It was written during a period when Saint-Exupry fled to North America subsequent to the Fall of France during the Second World War, witnessed first hand by the author and captured in his memoir Flight to Arras. The adult fable, according to one review, is actually "...an allegory of Saint-Exupry's own lifehis search for childhood certainties and interior peace, his mysticism, his belief in human courage and brotherhood . . . but also an allusion to the
             <br/>
             tortured nature of their relationship."
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Though ostensibly styled as a children's book, The Little Prince makes several observations about life and human nature. For example, Saint-Exupry tells of a fox meeting the young prince during his travels on Earth. The story's essence is contained in the lines uttered by the fox to the little prince: On ne voit bien qu'avec le cur. L'essentiel est invisible pour les yeux.
             <em>
              ("One sees clearly only with the heart. What is essential is invisible to the eyes.")
             </em>
             Other key thematic messages are articulated by the fox, such as: Tu deviens responsable pour toujours de ce que tu as apprivois.
             <em>
              ("You become responsible, forever, for what you have tamed.")
             </em>
             and C'est le temps que tu as perdu pour ta rose qui fait ta rose si importante.
             <em>
              ("It is the time you have lost for your rose that makes your rose so important.")
             </em>
             The fox's messages are arguably the book's most famous quotations because they deal with human relationships.
            </span>
           </font>
          </p>
