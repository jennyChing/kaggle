     <p>
      <b>
       Alsace wine
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Alsatian wine
      </b>
      (in
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Vin d'Alsace
      </i>
      ) is produced in the
      <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">
       Alsace
      </a>
      region in
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      and is primarily white. Because of its Germanic influence, it is the only
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appellation_d%27Origine_Contr%C3%B4l%C3%A9e" title="Appellation d'Origine Contrle">
        Appellation d'Origine Contrle
       </a>
      </i>
      region in France to produce mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Varietal" title="Varietal">
       varietally labelled
      </a>
      wines, typically from similar grape varieties to those used in
      <a href="/wiki/German_wine" title="German wine">
       German wine
      </a>
      . Along with
      <a href="/wiki/Austrian_wine" title="Austrian wine">
       Austria
      </a>
      and Germany, it produces some of the most noted dry
      <a href="/wiki/Riesling" title="Riesling">
       Rieslings
      </a>
      in the world as well as highly aromatic
      <a href="/wiki/Gew%C3%BCrztraminer" title="Gewrztraminer">
       Gewrztraminer
      </a>
      wines. Wines are produced under three different AOCs: Alsace AOC for white, ros and red wines,
      <a href="/wiki/Alsace_Grand_Cru_AOC" title="Alsace Grand Cru AOC">
       Alsace Grand Cru AOC
      </a>
      for white wines from certain classified vineyards and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A9mant_d%27Alsace_AOC" title="Crmant d'Alsace AOC">
       Crmant d'Alsace AOC
      </a>
      for sparkling wines. Both dry and sweet white wines are produced.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006, vines were grown on 15,298
      <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">
       hectares
      </a>
      (37,800
      <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">
       acres
      </a>
      ) in 119 villages in Alsace, and 111.3 million litres of wine was produced, corresponding to 148.4 million bottles of 750 ml, generating 478.8 million euro in revenue. Of the vineyard surface, 78% was classified for the production of AOC Alsace wines, 4% for AOC Alsace Grand Cru, and 18% for AOC Crmant d'Alsace.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIVA_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CIVA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      About 90% of the wine produced is white.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_p.14_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_p.14-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      25% of the production is exported, and the five largest export markets for still Alsace wine in terms of volume are
      <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
       Belgium
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Netherlands
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg,_Alsace.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="1728" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg%2C_Alsace.jpg/130px-Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg%2C_Alsace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg%2C_Alsace.jpg/195px-Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg%2C_Alsace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg%2C_Alsace.jpg/260px-Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg%2C_Alsace.jpg 2x" width="130"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tokay_Pinot_Gris_from_Kaysersberg,_Alsace.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A 2000 vintage Alsace wine labelled Tokay Pinot gris.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An important influence in the history of Alsace wine has been the repeated changes of nationality of the Alsace region, which has passed from France to Germany and vice versa several times throughout history. In the early history of the Alsace wine industry, they were traded together with other German wines since Rhine provided the means to transport the wines. In much of the postWorld War II era, wine styles in Alsace and Germany diverged, as Alsace wines remained fully fermented, that is dry, to a large extent because they were intended to be paired with food. In the same era, Alsace has also experienced a drive to higher quality, which led to AOC status being awarded. In recent decades, the difference between Alsace and Germany has diminished, since German wines have become drier and more powerful, while many Alsace wines have become sweeter and the late harvest and dessert style wines have been "rediscovered" in Alsace since the VT and SGN designations were introduced in 1983.
     </p>
     <p>
      The total vineyard surface in Alsace has increased over the last decades, although the total French vineyard surface has decreased. In 1967, there were 9,400 hectares (23,000 acres) of Alsace vineyards, in 1982, 11,750 hectares (29,000 acres), and in 2007, 15,300 hectares (38,000 acres).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over the same period of time, among the varieties, Pinot gris has increased the most, from 4% to 15% of the vineyard surface, while Sylvaner has decrased the most.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_name_.22Tokay.22_in_Alsace">
       Use of the name "Tokay" in Alsace
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: Use of the name "Tokay" in Alsace'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The grape variety Pinot gris is believed to have been taken to
      <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">
       Hungary
      </a>
      in the 14th century, where it was named
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sz%C3%BCrkebar%C3%A1t" title="Szrkebart">
       Szrkebart
      </a>
      . It is further believed to have been brought back to Alsace by General
      <a href="/wiki/Lazarus_von_Schwendi" title="Lazarus von Schwendi">
       Lazarus von Schwendi
      </a>
      after his campaign against the Turks in the 16th century. It was planted in
      <a href="/wiki/Kientzheim" title="Kientzheim">
       Kientzheim
      </a>
      under the name "Tokay", taken from Hungarys most famous wine
      <a href="/wiki/Tokaji" title="Tokaji">
       Tokaji
      </a>
      , where it however is not used in the blend. For a long time, the Alsatian wines produced from this variety were labelled Tokay d'Alsace. However, in 1993, an agreement was reached between Hungary and the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      (of which Hungary was not yet a member) to phase out the name Tokay from non-Hungarian wine. In the case of Alsace, Tokay Pinot gris was adopted as an intermediate step, with the "Tokay" part to be eliminated in 2007.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_TdA_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_TdA-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_p.16_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_p.16-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">

        Topgraphic map of Alsace showing the importance of the Vosges to the west and river Rhine to the east. Most vineyards are located on the lower stretches of the Vosges, just above the plains leading down to Rhine. Notice the presence of hills and minor valleys which mean that not just eastern, but also southern and northern slopes can be found in Alsace vineyards.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The geography of the wine growing area in Alsace is determined by two main factors, the
      <a href="/wiki/Vosges" title="Vosges">
       Vosges
      </a>
      mountains in the west and the
      <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">
       Rhine
      </a>
      river in the east. The vineyards are concentrated in a narrow strip, running in a roughly north-south direction, on the lower eastern slopes of the Vosges, at altitudes of 175420 m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_p.14_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_p.14-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Those altitudes provide a good balance between temperature, drainage and sun exposure under Alsace's growing conditions. Because of predominantly westerly winds, the Vosges mountains tend to shelter Alsace from rain and maritime influence, and the region is therefore rather dry and sunny. Rainfall in
      <a href="/wiki/Colmar" title="Colmar">
       Colmar
      </a>
      is 500mm, but can vary greatly between sites, and is the driest city in France. While the slope down the Vosges is generally east-facing, many of the best sites are south-west to south-east facing, and benefit from extra sun exposure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alsace's geology is quite varied, with many different kinds of soils represented in the vineyards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Alsaces soils are a result of its location at a geological
      <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">
       fault
      </a>
      . Alsace as a whole is located on the western part of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhine_Graben" title="Rhine Graben">
       Rhine Graben
      </a>
      , which is the result of two systems of parallel faults, with a dropped down block between the Vosges and the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">
       Black Forest
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wine_styles">
       Wine styles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wine styles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg/220px-Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg/330px-Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg/440px-Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alsatian_Wine_Barrels.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Barrels in Alsace for wines of several notable varieties.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Almost all wines are white, except those made from the
      <a href="/wiki/Pinot_noir" title="Pinot noir">
       Pinot noir
      </a>
      grape which are pale red, often
      <a href="/wiki/Ros%C3%A9" title="Ros">
       ros
      </a>
      , rarely red (e.g.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rouge_d%27Ottrott&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rouge d'Ottrott (page does not exist)">
       Rouge d'Ottrott
      </a>
      <span class="noprint" style="font-size: smaller; font-style: normal;">
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rouge_d%27Ottrott" title="fr:Rouge d'Ottrott">
        fr
       </a>
       )
      </span>
      ).
      <a href="/wiki/Sparkling_wine" title="Sparkling wine">
       Sparkling wines
      </a>
      known as Crmant d'Alsace are also made. Much of the white wines of Alsace are made from aromatic grape varieties, so many characteristic Alsace wines are aromatic, floral and spicy. Since they very seldom have any oak barrel aromas they tend to be very varietally pure in their character. Traditionally all Alsace wines were dry (which once set them apart from German wines with which they share many grape varieties), but an ambition to produce wines with more intense and fruity character has led some producers to produce wines which contain some residual sugar. Since there is no official labeling that differentiates completely dry from off-dry (or even semi-sweet) wines, this has occasionally led to some confusion among consumers. It is more common to find residual sugar in Gewrztraminer and Pinot gris, which reach a higher natural sugar content on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ripeness_(wine)" title="Ripeness (wine)">
       ripeness
      </a>
      , than in Riesling, Muscat or Sylvaner. Usually there is a "house style" as to residual sugar, i.e., some producers only produce totally dry wines, except for their dessert style wines.
     </p>
     <p>
      Almost all production in Alsace is of AOC wine, since there is no
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vin_de_pays" title="Vin de pays">
        Vin de pays
       </a>
      </i>
      region which covers Alsace. Thus, the only alternative to producing AOC wine is to declassify it all the way down to
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vin_de_table" title="Vin de table">
        Vin de table
       </a>
      </i>
      , which generally means that neither grape varieties, region of origin or vintage may be identified. However, this solution is mostly avoided since
      <i>
       edelzwicker
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       gentil
      </i>
      may be blended from several varieties, i.e. varieties that exceed the AOC rules in the concerned season.
     </p>

         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bottles_Alsace.jpg" title="Enlarge">

     <p>
      There is a legal requirement for bottling Alsace wine in tall bottles commonly called
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fl%C3%BBtes_d%27Alsace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fltes d'Alsace (page does not exist)">
        fltes d'Alsace
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size: smaller; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fl%C3%BBte_d%27Alsace" title="fr:Flte d'Alsace">
         fr
        </a>
        )
       </span>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_p.14_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_p.14-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the AOC rules, the bottle type is actually called
      <i>
       vin du Rhin
      </i>
      , i.e., "Rhine wine bottle". Without being mandated by law, this bottle format is also common and traditional in many German regions, particularly for Riesling and other traditional white wine varieties.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Late_harvest_wines">
       Late harvest wines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late harvest wines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are two
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_harvest" title="Late harvest">
       late harvest
      </a>
      classifications,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vendange_Tardive" title="Vendange Tardive">
       Vendange Tardive
      </a>
      (VT) and
      <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9lection_de_Grains_Nobles" title="Slection de Grains Nobles">
       Slection de Grains Nobles
      </a>
      (SGN).
      <i>
       Vendange Tardive
      </i>
      means "late harvest" (which in German would be
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%A4tlese" title="Sptlese">
        Sptlese
       </a>
      </i>
      ), but in terms of
      <a href="/wiki/Must_weight" title="Must weight">
       must weight
      </a>
      requirements, VT is similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Auslese" title="Auslese">
       Auslese
      </a>
      in Germany.
      <i>
       Slection de Grains Nobles
      </i>
      means "selection of noble berries", i.e. grapes affected by
      <a href="/wiki/Noble_rot" title="Noble rot">
       noble rot
      </a>
      , and is similar to a German
      <a href="/wiki/Beerenauslese" title="Beerenauslese">
       Beerenauslese
      </a>
      . For both VT and SGN, Alsace wines tend to be higher in alcohol and therefore slightly lower in sugar than the corresponding German wines. Therefore, Riesling VT and Muscat VT tend to be semi-sweet rather than sweet, while Gewrztraminer and Pinot gris tend to be rather sweet already at VT level. But as is the case with sweetness in other Alsace wines, this depends to a large extent on the house style of the producer.
     </p>
     <p>
      The required level of ripeness of the grapes, which was increased in 2001, are as follows, expressed as sugar content of the must and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potential_alcohol" title="Potential alcohol">
       potential alcohol
      </a>
      :

      </sup>

       </a>
      </sup>

       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-AOC_wines">
       Non-AOC wines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-AOC wines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Almost all Alsace wine is produced under one of the region's three AOC designationsAlsace, Alsace Grand Cru and Crmant d'Alsace. Unlike most other French wine regions, there exists no
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vin_de_pays" title="Vin de pays">
        Vin de pays
       </a>
      </i>
      designation for Alsace. This means that wines that do not qualify for AOC status have to be sold as simple
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vin_de_table" title="Vin de table">
        Vin de table
       </a>
       de France
      </i>
      . This happens in some instances when producers wish to use other grape varieties in their wine, like
      <a href="/wiki/Domaine_Zind-Humbrecht" title="Domaine Zind-Humbrecht">
       Domaine Zind-Humbrecht
      </a>
      which sells its
      <i>
       cuve Zind
      </i>
      , a blend of 65% Chardonnay and 35% Auxerrois.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_structure">
       Industry structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Industry structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Up to 2,000 growers bottle their own wine, but more than 80% of the wine is produced by 175 producers, including many
      <a href="/wiki/Winemaking_cooperative" title="Winemaking cooperative">
       winemaking cooperatives
      </a>
      . Even the largest winemaking companies/ngociants in Alsace tend to be family-owned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_p.14_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_p.14-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2001, approximately 45% of Alsace wine was made by cooperatives.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Producers">
       Producers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alsace_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Producers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Some of the best known producers include
      <a href="/wiki/Maison_Trimbach" title="Maison Trimbach">
       Maison Trimbach
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Domaine_Zind-Humbrecht" title="Domaine Zind-Humbrecht">
       Domaine Zind-Humbrecht
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hugel_%26_Fils" title="Hugel &amp; Fils">
       Hugel &amp; Fils
      </a>
      , Lon Beyer, Weinbach, Josmeyer and
      <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Deiss" title="Marcel Deiss">
       Marcel Deiss
      </a>
      . Many of the larger houses, such as Hugel, sell both wines from their own vineyards and market wines they have produced from purchased grapes, i.e., operate as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%A9gociant" title="Ngociant">
       ngociant
      </a>
      . Producers calling themselves "Domaine", such as Zind-Humbrecht, are supposed to only use grapes from their own vineyards. There are also several winemaking cooperatives, some of which have a rather good reputation.
     </p>
