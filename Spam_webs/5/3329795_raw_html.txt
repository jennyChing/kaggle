           <p>
            Released late last year, Disney's
            <a href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt2294629/" target="_blank" title="Frozen">
             "Frozen"
            </a>
            was a monster hit, perhaps because a story about ice, snow, and perpetual winter made sense to many of us in the midst of a polar vortex. (Well, that, and some pretty catchy songs.) Like Olaf the snowman, we held on to dreams of relaxing in the sun (or at least the wild fantasy of not wearing two sweaters at once), trying to remember "what frozen things do in the summer."
           </p>
           <p>
            At long last, summer has come and we're thawing out and finally putting the memories of winter behind us. Whether you and your kids are also looking ahead to warm weather or just need a new film to watch a million times on vacation, we've rounded up a list of our favorite pre-"Frozen" kids' movies. (We've skipped the popular computer-animated films of recent years, given that most families probably know them already!)
           </p>
           <p>
            Obviously, this is just the tip of the iceberg (pun intended), so feel free to suggest your favorites in the comments below.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Frozen Heart: Choose Your Own Adventure
            </strong>
            <br/>
            Like "Frozen," let's start with tales of adventure. Starring Henry Thomas and Drew Barrymore, Steven Spielberg's
            <strong>
             "E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial" (1982)
            </strong>
            lures you in like a trail of Reese's Pieces. Thomas also appeared with Dabney Coleman in the father-son spy thriller,
            <strong>
             "Cloak and Dagger" (1984)
            </strong>
            . Borrowed from a German novel,
            <strong>
             "The NeverEnding Story" (1984)
            </strong>
            is about the power of imagination. Based on
            <em>
             Basil of Baker Street
            </em>
            ,
            <strong>
             "The Great Mouse Detective" (1986)
            </strong>
            is a Sherlock Holmes story.
            <strong>
             "Mulan" (1998)
            </strong>
            tells the Chinese legend of Hua Mulan, who disguises herself as a man to take her aging father's place in the army. Directed by Brad Bird ("The Incredibles," "Ratatouille"),
            <strong>
             "The Iron Giant" (1999)
            </strong>
            adapts Ted Hughes' story of a boy protecting a visitor from space. The Oscar-nominated
            <strong>
             "The Secret of Kells" (2009)
            </strong>
            is an Irish adventure inspired by the creation of the illuminated Book of Kells.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Do You Want to Build a Snowman? Sisters Doing It for Themselves
            </strong>
            <br/>
            Sisterly love, like the relationship of Elsa and Anna, is the beating heart of this batch of films. Hayley Mills has chemistry with herself as the reunited twin sisters of
            <strong>
             "The Parent Trap" (1961)
            </strong>
            ; the
            <strong>
             1998 remake
            </strong>
            with Lindsay Lohan is also worth the watch. From Hayao Miyazaki's legendary Studio Ghibli comes one of its greatest productions:
            <strong>
             "My Neighbor Totoro" (1988)
            </strong>
            , a tale of two sisters and their adventures with forest spirits. The three major film versions of
            <strong>
             <em>
              Little Women
             </em>
             (1933, 1949, 1994)
            </strong>
            are all terrific; modern audiences will probably prefer the most recent one with Winona Ryder, Susan Sarandon, and Claire Danes.
            <strong>
             "Lilo and Stitch" (2002)
            </strong>
            is about an alien tamed by a girl and her sister (with help from Elvis).
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Love Is an Open Door: Romance with a Twist
            </strong>
            <br/>
            By the end of "Frozen," Anna and Hans' romantic duet has a different, twisty meaning.
            <strong>
             "Pee Wee's Big Adventure" (1985)
            </strong>
            is all about a boy's love for his stolen bike; sensitive viewers may want to skip the scary "Large Marge" scene. Rob Reiner's endlessly quotable
            <strong>
             "The Princess Bride" (1987)
            </strong>
            is a self-reflexive romantic fantasy, a rare adaptation that bests its source material.
            <strong>
             "Ever After" (1998)
            </strong>
            is a revisionist take on the Cinderella story, with Drew Barrymore and Anjelica Huston.
            <strong>
             "Enchanted" (2007)
            </strong>
            sends up Disney romances with its fairy-tale princess (an infectiously enthusiastic Amy Adams) who ends up in Manhattan; Idina Menzel of "Frozen" plays a rival love interest.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Let It Go: Singalong Sessions
            </strong>
            <br/>
            If your kids got through the "fractal" lyric of Menzel's solo, here are other singalong films that we still know all the words to years later. Julie Andrews lights up the musical version of
            <strong>
             "Mary Poppins" (1964)
            </strong>
            with its "practically perfect in every way" soundtrack. Loosely based on Rudyard Kipling's stories,
            <strong>
             "The Jungle Book" (1967)
            </strong>
            is as famous for its songs -- notably Louis Prima on "I Wanna Be Like You" -- as its story. L. Frank Baum's
            <em>
             Oz
            </em>
            books have inspired a number of films, including
            <em>
             the
            </em>
            classic musical,
            <strong>
             "The Wizard of Oz" (1939),
            </strong>
            with Judy Garland, but why not try Sidney Lumet's Motown-produced
            <strong>
             "The Wiz" (1978)
            </strong>
            , with Diana Ross, Michael Jackson, and Lena Horne?
            <strong>
             "Annie" (1982)
            </strong>
            brought the comic strip character to life (with actual eyes, thankfully) with belt-able songs as spunky as its heroine.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Reindeer Are Better Than People: Animal Tales
            </strong>
            <br/>
            If, like Kristoff, you prefer the company of animals, you're in luck. The animated musical of
            <strong>
             "Charlotte's Web" (1973)
            </strong>
            is as classic as E. B. White's tale of "some pig" and his spider friend.
            <strong>
             "Dot and the Kangaroo" (1977)
            </strong>
            adapts an Australian story of a lost child befriended by a kangaroo in the outback. Based on a Newbery Medal winner,
            <strong>
             "The Secret of NIMH" (1982)
            </strong>
            is about a mouse family helped by lab rats. Nominated for a Best Picture Oscar,
            <strong>
             "Babe" (1995)
            </strong>
            , from a British novel, is about a pig who learns to herd sheep. A study in Plasticine stop-motion, the Oscar-winning
            <strong>
             "Wallace and Gromit"
            </strong>
            series about an inventor and his dog includes
            <strong>
             four shorts (1989-2008)
            </strong>
            and a film,
            <strong>
             "The Curse of the Were-Rabbit" (2005)
            </strong>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             In Summer: Travel Fantasies
            </strong>
            <br/>
            Olaf's fantasies of summer have us dreaming of travel. Based on the Norton Juster classic, Chuck Jones'
            <strong>
