       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The Recovery Process  Part 1
       </h1>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="https://www.osc-ortho.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/CM_The_Recovery_Process_Part-I_WhitePaper_2.pdf">
          Full PDF Version of The Recovery Process Part I  663 KB
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         This information will help you get ready for your surgery AND prepare for the days, weeks, and months following your surgery.
         <br/>
         You may need to adjust your work schedule, ask friends or family to help you around the house, and nd time for ongoing physical therapy. Each case is unique and your own commitment to rehabilitation and recovery will accelerate the process and
         <br/>
         improve your outcome.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Preparing for Recovery before Surgery
        </h1>
        <p>
         The Orthopaedic Surgeons at OSC recommend a pre-operative wellness program for our surgical patients to get them into the best physical shape possible before they go into the operating room. Even if this program can be done for only a week or two, it can have tremendous benefits for the patient. Recommendations are as follows:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Quit smoking completely or great reduce the amount you smoke
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Eat healthy, nutritious foods
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Limit junk food and soda
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          limit alcohol consumption
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Do prescribed exercises
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Have any dental problems fixed before surgery
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Take a good multi-vitamin with minerals
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Attend Joint Replacement Classes at the Hospital
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Read all informational materials about your surgery
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Limit contact with individuals who are sick
         </li>
        </ul>
        <h1>
         Preparing Your Home/Life for Recovery
        </h1>
        <p>
         Your home is where you will spend most of your recovery time. Make sure it is safe, comfortable and prepared for your recovery before you have surgery.
        </p>
        <ol style="max-width: 100%;">
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Purchase or prepare foods that can be frozen and xed easily by caregivers during your recovery
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Stock up on the beverages, soft foods and other comfort items you will want on hand
          <ol>
           <li style="color: #555 !important;">
            Throat lozenges or spray for sore throat after anesthesia or cervical procedures
           </li>
           <li style="color: #555 !important;">
            Personal Hygiene wipes
           </li>
           <li style="color: #555 !important;">
            Stool softeners or laxatives for pain medication-induced constipation
           </li>
           <li style="color: #555 !important;">
            Refillable or reusable ice packs for pain control
           </li>
           <li style="color: #555 !important;">
            Comfortable, easily removable clothing, like sweatpants and shirts
           </li>
          </ol>
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Move furniture to allow for easy access of wheelchairs, walkers and canes
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Remove throw rugs and other items that could cause a fall
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Purchase elevated commode seat, walker or other assistive devices if recommended
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Purchase a shower chair or bench or non-skid mats for the tub
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Have designated family members or friends help you for several days after surgery
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Pay bills, take care of the lawn, pet care, childcare arrangements, garbage pickup, etc. so that you wont have to worry about it during the first few days/weeks of recovery
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Take care of legal affairs, living wills, advanced directives, etc
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Arrange transportation home from the hospital
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         <em>
          Stop taking certain medications, such as anti-inflammatories, 7-14 days before surgery. Your physician will advise you.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h1>
         General Surgical Recovery Guidelines
        </h1>
        <h3>
         Pain Management During and Post Surgery
        </h3>
        <p>
         Helping patients reduce or eliminate post-surgical pain is an important aspect of any surgical recovery. While you are in surgery and in recovery, great care will be taken to see that your pain is well-managed. Patients will be given IV pain medication, muscle relaxants and may be given other drugs which help nerve pain or anxiety, which may continue while they remain inpatient at the hospital. The drug cocktails have proven to be more effective than high-doses of narcotics at relieving pain in surgical patients.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Joint Replacement
         </strong>
          Because knee replacement patients will typically experience more post-surgical pain than hip replacementpatients, they may also receive injections of numbing medication at the surgical site. The physician may also choose to place a catheter in place that releases pain medication at the nerve root of the surgical site for several days. Hip Replacement patients typically do not experience a great deal of post-surgical pain
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Spine Surgery
         </strong>
          Upon discharge, patients who are recovering from spine surgery will be given oral pain medication and/or muscle relaxers to take at home during the recovery period, if needed. Many patients are surprised to find that the surgery has helped to greatly relieve their pain and find that they do not need to take much pain medication after the first few days post-op.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Joint Replacement
         </strong>
          All patients who have hip or knee replacements will be started on a DVT (Deep Vein Thrombosis) or blood clot prevention drug beginning the day of surgery to be continued for 6 weeks after surgery. Your Orthopaedic physician willdecide on the appropriate blood thinning medication for you. Examples of these are aspirin or Coumadin (oral medication) or Lovenox (a self-injectable drug). Your physician may require that you get regular lab testing to ascertain how much your blood is thinning so that your dosage of medication can be carefully titrated to your specific needs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Spine Surgery
         </strong>
          Most spine surgery patients will not require DVT Prophyllaxis UNLESS they have a history of DVT. Please discuss any history with your Spine Surgeon.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Surgical Site/Suture Care
        </h3>
        <p>
         All surgical patients will have a surgical site which will require care post-operatively. Your incision may be closed with surgical glue, you may have sutures or you may have surgical staples. Your physician will give you detailed instructions on how to keep the incision site clean, dry and protected during the rst weeks until the incision heals and any sutures or staples can be removed safely.
        </p>
        <p>
         For Spine Surgery Patients, you may have a surgical drain (a small tube with a uid collection bulb on the end) that has been stitched in place inside your incision. If you have a surgical drain, your physician will either give you instructions for drain removal or have a home health nurse come to your home to check the incision and remove the drain for you.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Signs of Infection
         </em>
         : Although most surgical patients are given antibiotic prophyllaxis, occasionally, 1-3 % of patients will develop an infection at their surgical site which will need to be treated. If the surgical site becomes angry, inflamed, swollen, or exudes pus or cloudy fluid, or if the patient has a fever that exceeds 101.5 degrees and chills, this may indicate that the site is infected. Please report any of these signs immediately to your physician.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Recovery from Anesthesia
        </h3>
        <p>
         All spine surgeries performed in a hospital setting and most knee and hip replacements will require that the patient have general anesthesia. During this type of anesthesia, the patient will be given IV sedation to render them unconscious and a breathing tube will be inserted. The breathing tube helps to keep the patient breathing safely during the surgery, as they lose the ability to cough and clear their airways under deep sedation. The anesthesiologist will administer a variety of anesthetic agents to keep the patient asleep and free from pain during the procedure.
        </p>
        <p>
         The goal of the anesthesiologist is to manage the patients unconsciousness through the surgery and to carefully return them to a conscious state after the surgery, breathing safely on their own. This is usually done without any complications, but there are common side-effects that can occur after anesthesia, which are:
        </p>
        <ul style="width: 40%; float: left;">
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          A sore throat, from the breathing tube
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Dry mouth and hoarseness
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Nausea and vomiting
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Shivering
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Sleepiness
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul style="width: 50%; float: right;">
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Confusion
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Emotionality
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Sore muscles
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Difficulty urinating
         </li>
         <li style="color: #555 !important;">
          Itching
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <br style="clear: both;"/>
         <br/>
         The side-effects from anesthesia can be barely noticeable and last for hours or they can be severe and last for days or weeks. If you are concerned about any side-effects you experience, it is important that you notify your physician immediately.
        </p>
        <p>
         If youre preparing for spine, knee, or hip surgery, its important to consult with your physician to have a clear understanding of what to expect during and after the surgery. Part I of this guide gives you a look at what to do to prepare for surgery, and what to expect post-surgery. Be sure to check out The Recovery Process Part II to learn more about individual surgeries and their recovery process. By working closely with your physician, you can learn more about what to expect in your individual case.
        </p>
