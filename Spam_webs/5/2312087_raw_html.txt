      <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">
       orange
      </a>

      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">
       olive
      </a>
      . Kumquat is a fairly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_hardy_citrus" title="Cold hardy citrus">

      </a>
      .

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
       Name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The English name "kumquat" derives from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">
       Cantonese
      </a>
       (
      <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">
       Jyutping
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="yue-jyutping" xml:lang="yue-jyutping">
        gam
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        gwat
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
       </span>
      </i>
      ), literally "golden tangerine".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
       Origin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The plant is native to
      <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
       south Asia
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">
       Asia-Pacific
      </a>
      region. The earliest historical reference to kumquats appears in literature of
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      in the 12th century. They have long been cultivated in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">
       Taiwan
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
       Philippines
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       southeast Asia
      </a>
      . They were introduced to
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      in 1846 by
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fortune" title="Robert Fortune">
       Robert Fortune
      </a>
      , collector for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Horticultural_Society" title="London Horticultural Society">
       London Horticultural Society
      </a>
      , and shortly thereafter into
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_lemon_plant_(Citrus_japonica);_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_lemon_plant_(Citrus_japonica);_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Illustration by
        <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hood_Fitch" title="Walter Hood Fitch">
         Walter Hood Fitch
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      They are slow-growing
      <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">
       evergreen
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">
       shrubs
      </a>
      or short trees, from 2.5 to 4.5 meters (8 to 15ft) tall, with dense branches, sometimes bearing small
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spine_(botany)" title="Spine (botany)">
       thorns
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">
       leaves
      </a>
      are dark glossy green, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">
       flowers
      </a>
      are white, similar to other citrus flowers, borne singly or clustered in the leaf-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axil" title="Axil">
       axils
      </a>
      . Depending on size, the kumquat tree can produce hundreds or even thousands of fruits each year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The tree can be
      <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_plant" title="Aquatic plant">
       hydrophytic
      </a>
      , grown in water, with the fruit often found floating on water near shore during the ripe season.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">
       Varieties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Varieties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Round_kumquat">
       Round kumquat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Round kumquat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      When the kumquats are divided into multiple species, the name
      <i>
       <b>
        Fortunella japonica
       </b>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       Citrus japonica
      </i>
      ) is retained by this group. The
      <b>
       round kumquat
      </b>
      also called
      <b>
       Marumi kumquat
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Morgani kumquat
      </b>
      , is an evergreen tree, producing edible golden-yellow fruit. The round Hawaiian varietal, the "Meiwa kumquat", is eaten raw. The fruit is small and usually round but can be oval shaped. The peel has a sweet flavor but the fruit has a sour center. The fruit can be eaten cooked but is mainly used to make marmalades and jellies. It is grown as an ornamental plant and can be used in
      <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">
       bonsai
      </a>
      . The plant symbolizes good luck in China and other Asian countries, where it is kept as a houseplant and given as a gift during the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">
       Lunar New Year
      </a>
      . Round kumquats are more commonly cultivated than other species due to their cold tolerance.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Oval_kumquat">
       Oval kumquat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oval kumquat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      When the kumquats are divided into multiple species, the name
      <i>
       <b>
        Fortunella margarita
       </b>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       Citrus margarita
      </i>
      ) is used for this group. The
      <b>
       oval kumquat
      </b>
      is also called the
      <b>
       Nagami kumquat
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The unusual feature of the Nagami cumquat is in the eating of the fruit. The fruit is eaten whole, skin and all. The inside is still quite sour, but the skin has the sweeter flavour, when eaten together it produces an unusual refreshing flavour. Fruit ripens mid to late winter and always crops very heavily, making a spectacular display against the dark green foliage. The tree is smaller growing and dwarf in nature, making it ideal for pots and has even been used in bonsai.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jiangsu_kumquat">
       Jiangsu kumquat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jiangsu kumquat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      When the kumquats are divided into multiple species the name
      <i>
       <b>
        Fortunella obovata
       </b>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       Citrus obovata
      </i>
      ) is used for this group. The
      <b>
       Jiangsu kumquat
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Fukushu kumquat
      </b>
      bears edible fruit that can be eaten raw. The fruit can be made into jelly and marmalade. The fruit can be round or bell shaped; it is bright orange when fully ripe. It may be distinguished from other kumquats by its round leaves. It is grown for its edible fruit and as an ornamental plant. It cannot withstand frost.
     </p>

      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">
       Cultivation and uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Kumquats are cultivated in
      <a href="/wiki/Kumquat_production_in_China" title="Kumquat production in China">
       China
      </a>
      , Korea, Japan, Taiwan, Southeast Asia, Nepal, southern Pakistan, Iran, the Middle East, Europe (notably
      <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">
       Corfu
      </a>
      , Greece), and the United States (notably Florida, Alabama, Louisiana, California and Hawaii).
     </p>
     <p>
      They are much
      <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">
       hardier
      </a>
      than other citrus plants such as
      <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">
       oranges
      </a>
      . The 'Nagami' kumquat requires a hot summer, ranging from 25C to 38C (77F to 100F), but can withstand frost down to about 10C (14F) without injury.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Propagation_and_pollination">
       Propagation and pollination
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Propagation and pollination">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kumquats do not grow well from seeds and so are
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">
       vegetatively propagated
      </a>
      , using
      <a href="/wiki/Citrus_rootstock" title="Citrus rootstock">
       rootstock
      </a>
      of another citrus fruit.,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_layering" title="Air layering">
       air layering
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Cutting_(plant)" title="Cutting (plant)">
       cuttings
      </a>
      (using a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rooting_gel" title="Rooting gel">
       rooting gel
      </a>
      /powder) .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They are self-pollinating as are most citrus.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">
       Uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Culinary uses include candying and kumquat preserves,
      <a href="/wiki/Marmalade" title="Marmalade">
       marmalade
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jelly_(fruit_preserves)" title="Jelly (fruit preserves)">
       jelly
      </a>
      . Kumquats can also be sliced and added to salads. In recent years kumquats have gained popularity as a
      <a href="/wiki/Cocktail_garnish" title="Cocktail garnish">
       garnish
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Cocktail" title="Cocktail">
       cocktail
      </a>
      beverages, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Martini_(cocktail)" title="Martini (cocktail)">
       martini
      </a>
      as a replacement for the more familiar olive. A kumquat
      <a href="/wiki/Liqueur" title="Liqueur">
       liqueur
      </a>
      mixes the fruit with
      <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">
       vodka
      </a>
      or other clear spirit. Kumquats are also being used by chefs to create a niche for their desserts
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="a small container for a mousse? (November 2013)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and are common in European countries.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_people" title="Cantonese people">
       Cantonese
      </a>
      often preserve kumquats in
      <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
       salt
      </a>
      or sugar.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      A batch of the fruit is buried in dry salt inside a
      <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">
       glass
      </a>
      jar. Over time, all the juice from the fruit is
      <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">
       diffused
      </a>
      into the salt. The fruit in the jar becomes shrunken, wrinkled, and dark brown in color, and the salt combines with the juice to become a dark brown
      <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">
       brine
      </a>
      . A few salted kumquats with a few teaspoons of the brine/juice may be mixed with hot water to make a remedy for sore throats.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      A jar of such preserved kumquats can last several years and still keep its flavor.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
       Philippines
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">
       Taiwan
      </a>
      , kumquats are a popular addition to
      <a href="/wiki/Green_tea" title="Green tea">
       green tea
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Black_tea" title="Black tea">
       black tea
      </a>
      , either hot or iced.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">
       Vietnam
      </a>
      , kumquat
      <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">
       bonsai
      </a>
      trees (round kumquat plant) are used as a decoration for the
      <a href="/wiki/T%E1%BA%BFt" title="Tt">
       Tt
      </a>
      (Lunar New Year) holiday. Kumquat fruits are also boiled or dried to make a candied snack called
      <i>
       mt qut
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Variants of the kumquat are grown specially in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The kumquat is celebrated annually in
      <a href="/wiki/Dade_City,_Florida" title="Dade City, Florida">
       Dade City, Florida
      </a>
      , U.S.A. with the annual
      <a href="/wiki/Kumquat_Festival" title="Kumquat Festival">
       Kumquat Festival
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
       Composition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Composition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">
       essential oil
      </a>
      of kumquat peel contains much of the aroma of the fruit, and is composed principally of
      <a href="/wiki/Limonene" title="Limonene">
       limonene
      </a>
      , which makes up around 93% of the total.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoyasakoBernhard_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KoyasakoBernhard-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Besides limonene and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-pinene" title="Alpha-pinene">
       alpha-pinene
      </a>
      (0.34%), both
      <a href="/wiki/Monoterpene" title="Monoterpene">
       monoterpenes
      </a>
      , the oil is unusually rich (0.38% total) in
      <a href="/wiki/Sesquiterpene" title="Sesquiterpene">
       sesquiterpenes
      </a>
      such as -
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bergamotene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bergamotene (page does not exist)">
       bergamotene
      </a>
      (0.021%),
      <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllene" title="Caryophyllene">
       caryophyllene
      </a>
      (0.18%), -
      <a href="/wiki/Humulene" title="Humulene">
       humulene
      </a>
      (0.07%) and -
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Muurolene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Muurolene (page does not exist)">
       muurolene
      </a>
      (0.06%), and these contribute to the spicy and woody flavor of the fruit.
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">
       Carbonyl
      </a>
      compounds make up much of the remainder, and these are responsible for much of the distinctive flavor. These compounds include
      <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">
       esters
      </a>
      such as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isopropyl_propanoate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isopropyl propanoate (page does not exist)">
       isopropyl propanoate
      </a>
      (1.8%) and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Terpinyl_acetate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Terpinyl acetate (page does not exist)">
       terpinyl acetate
      </a>
      (1.26%);
      <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">
       ketones
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Carvone" title="Carvone">
       carvone
      </a>
      (0.175%); and a range of
      <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">
       aldehydes
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Citronellal" title="Citronellal">
       citronellal
      </a>
      (0.6%) and
      <a href="/wiki/2-Methylundecanal" title="2-Methylundecanal">
       2-methylundecanal
      </a>
      . Other oxygenated compounds include
      <a href="/wiki/Nerol" title="Nerol">
       nerol
      </a>
      (0.22%) and trans-
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Linalool_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Linalool oxide (page does not exist)">
       linalool oxide
      </a>
      (0.15%).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoyasakoBernhard_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-KoyasakoBernhard-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
