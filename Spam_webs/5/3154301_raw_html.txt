    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Patrick Nagel
      </b>
      (November 25, 1945  February 4, 1984) was an
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Artist" title="Artist">
       artist
      </a>
      . He created popular illustrations on board, paper, and canvas, most of which emphasize the simple grace and beauty of the
      <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">
       female
      </a>
      form, in a distinctive style descended from
      <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">
       Art Deco
      </a>
      . He is best known for his illustrations for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">
        Playboy
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine and the
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">
       pop
      </a>
      group
      <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">
       Duran Duran
      </a>
      , for whom he designed the cover of the best selling
      <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">
       album
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_(Duran_Duran_album)" title="Rio (Duran Duran album)">
        Rio
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Nagel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nagel was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">
       Dayton, Ohio
      </a>
      in 1945, but was raised and spent most of his life in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">
       Los Angeles
      </a>
      area. After serving in the United States Army with the 101st Airborne in
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">
       Vietnam
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nagel attended the
      <a href="/wiki/Chouinard_Art_Institute" title="Chouinard Art Institute">
       Chouinard Art Institute
      </a>
      in Los Angeles in 1969, and in that same year he received his
      <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Fine_Arts" title="Bachelor of Fine Arts">
       Bachelor of Fine Arts
      </a>
      degree from
      <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Fullerton" title="California State University, Fullerton">
       California State University, Fullerton
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1971, Nagel worked as a graphic designer for
      <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">
       ABC Television
      </a>
      , producing graphics for promotions and news broadcasts. The following year, he began work as a freelance artist for major corporations and magazines, including
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Architectural_Digest" title="Architectural Digest">
        Architectural Digest
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper's Magazine">
        Harper's Magazine
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">
       IBM
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/ITT_Corporation" title="ITT Corporation">
       ITT Corporation
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MGM" title="MGM">
       MGM
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Oui_(magazine)" title="Oui (magazine)">
        Oui
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">
       United Artists
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">
       Universal Studios
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1976, Nagel began to regularly contribute images to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">
        Playboy
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine, which improved his exposure and the popularity of "the Nagel Woman" image to a huge audience. In 1978, he made his first poster image for
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mirage_Editions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mirage Editions (page does not exist)">
       Mirage Editions
      </a>
      , with whom he would print many Nagel women images.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nagel's 1982 painting for the album cover of rock group
      <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">
       Duran Duran
      </a>
      's hit album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_(Duran_Duran_album)" title="Rio (Duran Duran album)">
        Rio
       </a>
      </i>
      would become one of his best known images.
     </p>
     <p>
      He also worked for many commercial clients, including
      <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">
       Intel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Strike" title="Lucky Strike">
       Lucky Strike
      </a>
      cigarettes, Ballentine Whiskey, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budweiser_(Anheuser-Busch)" title="Budweiser (Anheuser-Busch)">
       Budweiser
      </a>
      . As his popularity grew he began offering limited edition prints of his work.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Style">
       Style
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Nagel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Style">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nagel would start with a
      <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
       photograph
      </a>
      and work down, always simplifying and removing elements which he felt were unnecessary. The resulting image would look flat, but emphasized those elements which he felt were most important.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Elena G. Millie, curator of the poster collection at the
      <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
       Library of Congress
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       Like some of the old print masters (
       <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Toulouse-Lautrec" title="Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec">
        Toulouse-Lautrec
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bonnard" title="Pierre Bonnard">
        Bonnard
       </a>
       , for example), Nagel was influenced by the
       <a href="/wiki/Woodblock_printing_in_Japan" title="Woodblock printing in Japan">
        Japanese woodblock print
       </a>
       , with figures silhouetted against a neutral background, with strong areas of black and white, and with bold line and unusual angles of view. He handled colors with rare originality and freedom; he forced perspective from flat, two-dimensional images; and he kept simplifying, working to get more across with fewer elements. His simple and precise imagery is also reminiscent of the art-deco style of the 1920s and 1930s- its sharp linear treatment, geometric simplicity, and stylization of form yield images that are formal yet decorative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Nagel's figures generally have black hair, bright white skin, full-lipped mouths, and the distinctive Nagel eyes, which are often squared off in the later works. Because of the intense stylization and reduction of facial features into clean lines, generally the figures resemble each other, though Nagel worked with many models, including
      <a href="/wiki/Playboy_Playmate" title="Playboy Playmate">
       <i>
        Playboy
       </i>
       Playmates
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathy_St._George" title="Cathy St. George">
       Cathy St. George
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tracy_Vaccaro" title="Tracy Vaccaro">
       Tracy Vaccaro
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Tweed" title="Shannon Tweed">
       Shannon Tweed
      </a>
      . Nagel also painted several celebrity portraits including those of
      <a href="/wiki/Joan_Collins" title="Joan Collins">
       Joan Collins
      </a>
      (whose portrait was subsequently released as a limited edition print) and
      <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Cassidy" title="Joanna Cassidy">
       Joanna Cassidy
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death">
       Death
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Nagel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1984, at the age of 38, the artist participated in a 15-minute celebrity "
      <a href="/wiki/Aerobics" title="Aerobics">
       Aerobathon
      </a>
      " to raise funds for the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">
       American Heart Association
      </a>
      . Afterwards, he was found dead in his car, and doctors determined by autopsy that he had suffered a
      <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">
       heart attack
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
