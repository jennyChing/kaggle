       <h1>
        How to Install New Ceramic Floor Tile on Top of Old Ceramic Tile
       </h1>
       <!-- google_ad_section_end -->
       <cite>
        by Darryl Brooks
        <span class="coName">
        </span>
       </cite>
      </header>
      <div id="main">
       <ul class="clearfix" id="sharebar">
       </ul>

        <div class="section" id="section-0">
         <p>
          Installing a new ceramic floor over an existing tile floor is something most DIY homeowners can tackle. Its really not much different than installing tile over cement slab -- with two major caveats. The existing tile floor must be solid and well bonded to the subfloor. If you tile over a floor that is having problems, you may soon have another damaged floor. Second, the new layer of tile adds height that can cause issues with doors and passages to adjoining rooms.
         </p>
        </div>

        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-1">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          1
         </h4>
         <p>
          Check the existing floor for problems. If the grout or any tiles are cracked, that is an indication of a problem that you dont want to tile over. Tap lightly across the floor with your fist or the wood handle of any tool. If there are any hollow sounds, that is an indication of loose tiles or a subfloor problem  another indication of a situation you dont want to tile over.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-2">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          2
         </h4>
         <p>
          Check the height difference the new floor will create. This is the thickness of the tile or very slightly more. The thin-set mortar adds no significant height difference. You need to check how the tile hits the baseboard and does it create any problems. For instance, if the baseboard is curved it can cause a wide grout joint against the base. Check the clearance of any doors. Usually there is plenty of room, but if the floor is out of level, you may need to plane the bottom of the door. Check the flange under the toilet. If the flange is still above or at least flush with the new floor, then you are okay. If not, there are adapters available to raise the toilet slightly.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section clearfix" id="quinstreet">

        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-3">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          3
         </h4>
         <p>
          Mix thin set according to directions and spread a skim coat over the old tile, filling in the grout joints. Allow this to dry. This will give you a smooth hard surface to work on. Mark your starting point. This should be a straight line off of one of the longest walls, preferably in a line of site from a door. This ensures that the tile looks straight even if the room is out of square.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-4">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          4
         </h4>
         <p>
          Mix thin-set mortar according to package directions and begin spreading it with the notched trowel between your line and the wall. The size of the notch is determined by the size and type of tile. Consult with someone at the tile store for the correct size. First spread the thin set with the flat side of the trowel to ensure a good bond and fill in the grout joints of the old floor. Then comb back through it with the notches to get your setting bed.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-5">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          5
         </h4>
         <p>
          Place the tile against the line and work toward the wall. Make sure the grout joints are consistent and straight. Gently twist or tap the tiles into the mortar to ensure a good bond. Make any needed cuts against the wall with the tile cutter.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-6">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          6
         </h4>
         <p>
          Continue working across the floor in this fashion, spreading and tiling a couple of feet at a time. Make cuts as you go with the tile cutter. Allow the floor to set 24 hours when complete.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section clearfix" id="section-7">
         <h4 class="step-header">
          7
         </h4>
         <p>
          Mix grout according to package directions. Spread using the grout float, forcing grout into all joints and pulling the excess off with the edge of the float like a squeegee. Wash grout off of the surface of the tile with a damp sponge while smoothing the joints at the same time. Allow grout to dry to a haze and then wash again with clean water. Once the grout is dry, buff with a clean dry towel if necessary.
         </p>
        </div>

        <div class="section" id="things-needed">
         <h4>
          Things You Will Need
         </h4>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Notched trowel
          </li>
          <li>
           Thin-set mortar
          </li>
          <li>
           Tile cutter
          </li>
          <li>
           Grout
          </li>
          <li>
           Grout float
          </li>
          <li>
           Sponge
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
        <div class="section" id="tips">
         <h4>
          Tip
         </h4>
         <ul>
          <li class="tip">
           <span>
            You could use Type II glue, but thin-set to a well bonded tile floor gives a very strong installation.
           </span>
          </li>
          <li class="tip">
           <span>
            Depending on the type of tile, you may want to use tile spacers to keep the joints straight and consistent. Some tiles have built in spacers and sheet mounted tile is already spaced correctly.
           </span>
          </li>
          <li class="tip">
           <span>
            If you have wood base with quarter-round trim, pull the trim off and reset over the new floor. Youll never be able to tell the difference.
           </span>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
        <div class="section" id="warnings">
         <h4>
          Warning
         </h4>
         <ul>
          <li class="warning">
           <span>
            If there are any loose or cracked tiles, do not tile over them. If only one or two are loose, you could chisel them up and fill the void with mortar. Allow the mortar to dry before continuing with the installation.
           </span>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
