        <h2>
         Meet Pittsburgh Wedding Vendor Judith Browne Calligraphy
        </h2>
        <p>
         I got the chance to meet Judith, a talented British calligrapher who is based in Pittsburgh, Pa. I loved getting to talk with her and see her workspace. She had so many amazing stories of her clients, including the Queen of England (seriously). By the way, if you are interested in learning how to do calligraphy, she teaches classes at CCAC, as well as workshops!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <div>
         <em>
          <strong>
           How did you get started in the wedding business:
          </strong>
         </em>
        </div>
        <p>
         My friends daughter was getting married and asked me to design her wedding invitation. It was a small wedding and each invitation was being sent with a special south sea island shell. So that the shells didnt break she wanted to stuff the envelopes with rose petals. I had the job of sourcing the rose petals and that led me to a wedding invitation store. The two ladies who owned the store wanted to know everything about this wedding and me and when I said I was a calligrapher they asked to see my work and the rest is history.
         <br/>
         I worked with them for many years, addressing envelopes, writing place cards and designing seating plans. Anything that was wedding or event related and could be written on. I met wonderful people, some of them quite famous and I got to do some really unusual stuff.
        </p>

         <em>
          <strong>
           What makes your company different than other business in the field?
          </strong>
         </em>
         <br/>
         Thats hard!! There are only so many ways of addressing an envelope.
         <br/>
         Any creative person brings their own spark to their work, the thing that defines their personality and makes them an individual. I think that shines through in our work.
         <br/>
         I have so many years experience and I have done so many different projects with brides and event planners and I think I also bring all of that to the table.
         <br/>
         I like to work with different things. I love fabric and anything sparkly and I sometimes incorporate those other elements into my work as well as other things.
         <br/>
         I studied art at school and college and although I am not a good painter, it does help to have that background. I love styling things and I like to make my work fit in well with the whole theme of an event. I will have a go at anything!!

          <em>
           From a vendors perspective, what is the best advice you can offer engaged couples?
          </em>
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Start planning early but dont let it take over. Beware the bermuda triangle of Etsy, Pinterest and Instagram!!
         <br/>
         Things dont have to be perfect and your guests are there to see two people they care about exchange lifetime vows. Yes, all those little touches make the day special but in the end they dont matter. You and your families and friends matter. Who cares if the roses are not the perfect shade of pink.

        <p>
         <strong>
          <em>
           What is your biggest accomplishment?
          </em>
         </strong>
         <br/>
         I have two for very different reasons
         <br/>
         In 2002 I was still living in England and the Royal Gamekeepers contacted me. They wanted to commission a piece of calligraphy for the Queens golden jubilee.
         <br/>
         It was very large and I had to write the name of all the gamekeepers and ghillies who had served the Queen during her reign. I had to leave plenty of white space so that a water colour artist could paint pictures of the castles and animals etc. I did see a photo of the finished piece but I wasnt allowed a copy as it is the Queens personal property and hangs in Sandringham Palace.
         <br/>
         I feel very privileged to have worked on that however the other accomplishment means more to me.
        </p>
        <p>
         In 2004 I lived near Liverpool. Liverpool sits at the edge of Liverpool Bay and the River Mersey and the tides are strong and treacherous. The Liverpool River Pilots guide ships around these shipping lanes and they commissioned me to update an old ledger. It was a huge, old leather book with parchment pages and it had been started in the early 19th century. It was meant to record the name of every pilot and their length of service on the river but no one had updated it for over a hundred and fifty years. Working with the Chief Pilot we gathered all the hundreds of names and I started to fill in the ledger. If a pilot had been lost at sea I placed a small gold cross after their name. It was very humbling to realize how many men, some of them just in their teens had died on the river in their attempts to rescue ships in distress.
         <br/>
         Even today the pilots and lifeboat men still go out in appalling weather to rescue large and small vessels and from my studio I could hear the sound of the alarm when the boats were launched. Many people in my community worked as volunteers on the rescue boats and it was always a relief to hear the boats come back safely.
         <br/>
         It was a huge honor to have helped to record their history and probably the thing I am most proud of professionally.
