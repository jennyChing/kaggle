      <h1>
       First in the Nation: Idaho House Passes Health Care Nullification
      </h1>
      <div class="date">
       <div class="dateleft">
        <p>
         <span class="time">
          February 17, 2011
         </span>
         by
         <a href="http://deadlinelive.info/author/jackblood/" rel="author" title="Posts by Jack Blood">
          Jack Blood
         </a>
         <br/>
         Filed under
         <a href="http://deadlinelive.info/category/north-america/" rel="category tag">
          Americas
         </a>
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="dateright">
        <p>
         <span class="icomment">
          <a href="http://deadlinelive.info/2011/02/17/first-in-the-nation-idaho-house-passes-health-care-nullification/#respond">
           Leave a Comment
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <p>
       by:
       <a href="http://blog.tenthamendmentcenter.com/author/michael-boldin/" title="Posts by Michael Boldin">
        Michael Boldin
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="http://blog.tenthamendmentcenter.com/2011/02/first-in-the-nation-idaho-house-passes-health-care-nullification/">
        tenthamendmentcenter.com
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       In an historic vote in the Idaho House of Representatives, the Federal Health Care Nullification Act (originally
       <a href="http://www.tenthamendmentcenter.com/legislation/federal-health-care-nullification-act/">
        authored by the Tenth Amendment Center
       </a>
       ) passed by a vote of 49-20.
      </p>
      <p>
       House Bill 117 (
       <a href="http://www.legislature.idaho.gov/legislation/2011/H0117.htm">
        H117
       </a>
       ) states, in part:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The state of Idaho hereby exercises its sovereign power  to declare the public policy of the state of Idaho regarding the right  of all persons residing in the state of Idaho in choosing the mode of  securing health care services free from the imposition of penalties, or  the threat thereof, by the federal government of the United States of  America relating thereto
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The principle behind such legislation is nullification, which has a  long history in the American tradition. When a state nullifies a  federal law, it is proclaiming that the law in question is void and  inoperative, or non-effective, within the boundaries of that state;  or, in other words, not a law as far as the state is concerned.
      </p>
      <p>
       Implied in any nullification legislation is enforcement of the state  law. In the Virginia Resolution of 1798, James Madison wrote of the  principle of interposition:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        That this Assembly doth explicitly and peremptorily  declare, that it views the powers of the federal government, as  resulting from the compact, to which the states are parties; as limited  by the plain sense and intention of the instrument constituting the  compact; as no further valid that they are authorized by the grants  enumerated in that compact; and that in case of a deliberate, palpable,  and dangerous exercise of other powers, not granted by the said compact,  the states who are parties thereto, have the right, and are in duty  bound, to interpose for arresting the progress of the evil, and for  maintaining within their respective limits, the authorities, rights and  liberties appertaining to them.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       House Bill 117 expressly includes this principle:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The Legislature of the state of Idaho, therefore, on  behalf of the citizens of this state and to secure the blessings of  liberty, hereby
        <strong>
         asserts its legitimate authority to interpose
        </strong>
        between said citizens and the federal government, when it has exceeded  its constitutional authority and declares that the state shall not  participate in and considers void and of no effect the PPACA. [emphasis  added]
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Ten other states are currently considering versions of the Health Care Nullification Act 
       <strong>
        <a href="http://www.tenthamendmentcenter.com/nullification/health-care-nullification-act/">
         track its progress here
        </a>
       </strong>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       All across the country, activists and state-legislators are pressing  for similar legislation, to nullify specific federal laws within their  states.
      </p>
      <p>
       Seven states have passed Health Care Freedom Acts to block health  care mandates from being enforced.  Eight states have passed Firearms  Freedom Acts.  Fifteen states  most recently Arizona  are using the  principles of the 10th Amendment to actively defy federal laws (and a  supreme court ruling, too!) on marijuana. And, massive state resistance  to the 2005 Real ID Act has rendered the law virtually null and void.
      </p>
      <p>
       While some advocates concede that a federal court battle has a slim  chance of success, they point to the successful nullification of the  Real ID Act as a blueprint to resist various federal laws that they see  as outside the scope of the Constitution.  No court battle has been  waged, no federal law has been repealed.  Yet, the 2005 Act sits  virtually null and void due to state-level resistance.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some say that each successful rejection of federal acts will only  embolden others to try the same  resulting in an eventual shift of  power from the federal government to the States and the People  themselves.
      </p>
      <p>

        </a>
        .  He was raised in Milwaukee, WI, and currently resides in Los Angeles, CA.
       </em>
      </p>
