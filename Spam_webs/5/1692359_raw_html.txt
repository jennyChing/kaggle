         <div>
          <p class="bylineBody">
           By
           <span rel="author">
            Alastair Smart
           </span>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p class="publishedDate">
          8:00AM GMT 04 Mar 2012
         </p>
         <meta content="2012-03-04" itemprop="datePublished"/>
         <p class="comments">
          <img alt="Comments" src="/template/ver1-0/i/share/comments.gif"/>
          <a dsqid="9115353" href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/culture/art/art-features/9115353/Damien-Hirst-has-shock-art-lost-its-bite.html#disqus_thread">
           Comments
          </a>
         </p>
         <div class="cl">
         </div>
        </div>
        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           It wasnt your usual London street party. There were no children playing, Victoria sponges or Union Jack bunting. Instead, on this balmy early Nineties afternoon, a group of young artists were taking part in a bizarre get-together in Hoxton called A Fete Worse than Death.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           At one stall, Tracey Emin did palm readings; at another, Gary Hume sold tequila slammers. Yet the most eye-catching contribution came from the ever-entrepreneurial Damien Hirst. Dressed in a clowns costume, for 50p he offered passers-by a sneak peek of his testicles, which hed spot-painted that morning.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           Two decades and an estimated 215million later, over 300 of Hirsts rather more respectable spot paintings are being shown worldwide at all 11 of Larry Gagosians art galleries. In April, he will be given a retrospective at Tate Modern, Londons flagship show during the Olympics. As the world turns its eyes on London, arts biggest showman is taking centre stage. The clown, it seems, is having the last laugh.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           Or is he? Sticking two fingers up at the establishment has of course become Hirsts trademark. Nearly 25 years after the enterprising young Goldsmiths student staged a headline-grabbing show in a disused warehouse, and went on to exhibit a variety of pickled animals in various states of putrefaction, to say that he still divides opinion would be an understatement. Detestable and f------ dreadful is how the
           <i>
            Evening Standard
           </i>
           critic Brian Sewell described one recent show. Simple-minded and sensationalist is Australian critic Robert Hughess verdict on an entire career.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           Then, last month, David Hockney entered the fray. According to the BBCs Andrew Marr, when asked if a poster in his gallery reading, All the works here were made by the artist himself, personally, was a sly dig at Hirst, Hockney nodded. Hockney has since denied attacking Hirst personally. Either way, arts enfant terrible is more than used to swipes at his use of 160 assistants to execute his artistic ideas. As he himself said: I only made five spot paintings myself And the spots I painted are s----. But Hockney went on to make a more telling point. Its a little insulting to craftsmen, skilful craftsmen he told Marr. I used to point out at art school, you can teach the craft, its the poetry you cant teach. Now they try to teach the poetry and not the craft.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class=" related_links_inline" id="tmg-related-links">
          <div class="headerOne styleSix">
           <h2>
            <span>
             Related Articles
            </span>
           </h2>
          </div>

             </a>
            </p>

            </span>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
         <div class="body">
          <p>
           Is he right? Did conceptual art take such a stranglehold in Hirst and his compatriots wake that technique was sacrificed? Just what has Hirsts influence been on British artists? And might there, finally, be a swing away from the form he made so famous and infamous all at once?
          </p>
          <p>
           Hockneys views are echoed by the artist George Shaw, who graduated from Hockneys own alma mater, the Royal College of Art, in 1998. I became incredibly frustrated at art school that you needed a philosophical language to understand contemporary art, rather than a pictorial one, he says. Shaw, 45, a painter in an old-school realist tradition, was Turner-nominated last year for his images of the Coventry housing estate he grew up on. For him, art is about works that an art historian and his mother could both enjoy... But that wasnt what [the tutors] were looking for.
          </p>
          <p>
           Many feel Shaw was scandalously overlooked for last years prize, losing out to installation artist Martin Boyce. But was it down to the Hirst effect? Certainly, there was already a well-established shift away from orthodox art education when Hirst arrived at Goldsmiths College in Camberwell in the late Eighties. In the Sixties  under the influence of minimalism and conceptualism  art schools nationwide had decided intellectual pursuits were as important as technical ones. Painting was dead, the US minimalist Donald Judd declared, and curating, theorising and art history soon became as important as draughtsmanship.
          </p>
          <p>
           Damien didnt create this milieu, but he thrived in it, says Michael Archer, director of Goldsmiths BA in fine art. And Hirst needed little encouragement from Goldsmiths Dean at the time, Jon Thompson, who led this break with tradition. Our philosophy remains the same today, Archer says. We dont break our course down into rigid components of compulsory disciplines. But doesnt this encourage following the path of least resistance? Who can be bothered with the 10,000 hours mastering their craft? If a student wants to learn how to weave or weld, we have the staff to teach it, he says. But we dont force them.
          </p>
          <p>
           The painter Gabriella Boyd, a recent graduate from Glasgow School of Art, cant see what the fuss is about. Drawings still taught and its still popular, says the 24- year-old. I used to love life-drawing class at college and I wasnt the only one. A quick look around Boyds website, indeed, is like being taken in a time-machine back to a Hockney show in the early Sixties.
          </p>
          <p>
           In hard times, we might even expect to see a return to old-school disciplines on cost-grounds. Paints and pencils are a darn sight cheaper than tanks of formaldehyde. Shaws scenes of a depopulated estate  made with model-aeroplane paints  seem in stark contrast. And theres already talk of a new austerity aesthetic, one wholly at odds with Hirsts $100 million, diamond-encrusted skull,
           <i>
            For the Love of God
           </i>
           (2007).
          </p>
          <p>
           Gregor Muir, director of the ICA, isnt sure. Often in times of trouble, you see the rise of fantastists, surrealists and other artists looking to escape it all. In the film world,
           <i>
            The Artist
           </i>
           is a good example, a light-hearted movie perfectly timed to cheer us all up. Muir is an old pal of the YBAs and the author of
           <i>
            Lucky Kunst
           </i>
           , a rollicking chronicle of their rise  yet he insists Hirst hasnt cloned a generation of artists all doing the same thing. Categorically not. Damiens a unique case. A brand unto himself, really.
          </p>
          <p>
           Hirsts sales figures confirm this. According to the Artnet C50 index, arts equivalent of the FTSE 100, Hirsts work has consistently outsold contemporary art in general. At the now infamous 2008 Sothebys sale, when Hirst auctioned off 223 works, his
           <i>
            Golden Calf
           </i>
           (a creature of 18-carat horns and hooves) sold for 10.3million. Now the plentiful supply of spot paintings (1,400 to date), one of which sold last year for 1.8million, ensures Hirst keeps himself within the price range of not-quite-so-rich buyers too.
          </p>
          <p>
           In spite of global economic meltdown, Sothebys sales in contemporary art shot up by 34 per cent in 2011, and Christies by 22 per cent, as investors increasingly turn to art to hedge against volatile markets. But how much of this rise is down to Hirst-style installations? We tend not to take on too many big installation works nowadays, says James Sevier, director specialist in contemporary art at Sothebys. The top lots in our recent sale were abstract paintings by Gerhard Richter, for instance. I wouldnt say Damien has brought about a seismic shift in what were selling. The three canvases by the 80-year-old German painter sold for over 4 million. Evidence that things are looking up for lovers of traditional painting?
          </p>
          <p>
           Fine art dealer Jonathan Clarke certainly thinks so: The most influential dealers are increasingly turning away from conceptual or craft-less art, he says, largely because they cant be sure long-term theres financial security in it. Paintings are a much surer bet.
          </p>
          <p>
           Henry Moores paintings are selling for three times the amount they were expected to just a couple of years ago. So the fact that Hirst is selling less  which he is  is not just down to the economy, but to the shift in the market.
          </p>
          <p>
           And what of those on the ground  the artists themselves? The annual Bloomberg New Contemporaries show, which showcased the early efforts of both Hirst (1989) and Hockney (1960), is a prestigious exhibition of the 40 top works by recent art school graduates. And, interestingly, its video art thats been the most popular medium recently, accounting for 30 per cent of the 1,000 works longlisted last year.
          </p>
          <p>
           Damien Hirst wasnt the slightest point of reference for me, says Yvonne Guzman, a video artist who graduated from Central St Martins in 2011. As Michael Archer explains, most of todays students werent even born when Damien broke on to the scene. To them, hes part of art history. Ed Atkins, James Richards and the Peckham collective Lucky PDF are but three young video acts making a name for themselves. Were the new generation, with a whole new audience, says Lucky PDFs James Early, and were not interested in pandering to a generation that wont be here in 20 years time. Spoken with all the brash, patricidal intent of... a young Damien Hirst.
          </p>
          <p>
           So is what Saatchi described as Hirsts hopeful swagger his real legacy? Muir recalls the late Eighties as a time when recession had brought the nation to its knees But instead of being scared, Damien was motivated. This son of Leeds and scion of Thatcher took advantage of all the empty property around London to stage shows of his Goldsmiths gangs work  most famously,
           <i>
            Freeze
           </i>
           in 1988. This wasnt the way exhibitions were done then, adds Muir. Ever since, though, artists have been showing their work anytime, anywhere: in train carriages, car parks, even their parents kitchens.
          </p>
          <p>
           One of Hirsts strengths was his vitality, says this newspapers art critic, Andrew Graham Dixon. But artistically speaking, he was strongest in his early work. His entrepreneurialism has compromised his art, to some extent.
          </p>
          <p>
           The prime example was the 2008 Sothebys sale, for which Hirst produced new versions of all his old hits (butterfly paintings; medicine cabinets; dead animals in vitrines). Yet the sociologist Sarah Thornton, author of
           <i>
            Seven Days in the Art World
           </i>
           , takes a different view. That sale wasnt about the individual works, she argues. Rather, the sale itself was a daredevil piece of performance art. So it is undoubtedly the art of
           <i>
            selling
           </i>
           that Hirst is a master of these days? Oh, yes. The way he handles the art market is probably his biggest legacy, says Thornton. Particularly his use of series. Hes the direct descendant of Andy Warhol making various unique versions of his Marilyn Monroe silkscreen paintings. The market was nowhere near as global in the Sixties, and Hirst has taken this on to a whole other level.
          </p>
          <p>
           As the performance artist Max Dovey, 23, whose recent work includes
           <i>
            Emotional Stock Market
           </i>
           , a live trading session based on the emotional status of his Twitter followers, observes: Hirst brought an energy, an excitement, a sense that anything was possible.
          </p>
          <p>
           I ask Dovey if Hirsts commercialism might not inspire todays artists to pursue mammon rather than their artistic muse? Absolutely not, says Dovey, a view echoed, refreshingly if inevitably, by every young artist I spoke to. I dont make art for financial gain. But thats not a romantic notion, its a realist one. Times are rough. Only at the very top of the market is there money rolling in.
          </p>
          <p>
           Dovey alludes to the worlds richest collectors (Russian oligarchs, Qatari royals) who continue to purchase the worlds biggest artists as if their oilfields depended on it. According to Guzman, theres a sense on the ground that the mega-rich collectors and artists are all hanging out together on a different universe somewhere. But the vast majority of artists arent a part of that, were just getting on with our art.
          </p>
          <p>

             @TelegraphSeven
            </a>
           </i>
          </p>

        /*
         Read Next Widget
         */
        var readNextWidget = (function(){

            var settings = {
                articleImage: "",
                articleTitle: "",
                articleUrl: "#",
                articleId: "",
                currentArticleId: "",
                articleList: [],
                articleListToSkip: [],
                readNextCookieName: "tmg_read_next",
                openButton: "#readNextOpen a",
                closeButton: "#closeBtn a",
                articleContainer: "#readNextArticleContainer",
                widgetContainer: "#readNextWidget",
                displayThumbnail: false,
                panelOpen: false,
                autoOpen: true
            };

            var renderTemplate = function() {
                var template = '&lt;div id="readNextWidget" style="display:none"&gt;' +
                        '&lt;div id="readNextOpen"&gt;&lt;div class="topBar"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;div id="readNextOpenInner"&gt;&lt;a href="' + settings.articleUrl + '"&gt;&lt;span&gt;Next Story&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="icn"&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;/div&gt;' +
                        '&lt;div id="readNextArticleContainer" style="display:none"&gt;' +
                        '&lt;div id="nextArticle"&gt;&lt;div class="topBar"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;' +
                        '&lt;div id="nextArticleWrapper"&gt;'+
                        '&lt;div id="nextArticleInnerWrapper"&gt;'+
                        '&lt;p id="readNextLabel"&gt;MOST VIEWED IN NEWS&lt;/p&gt;';
                        
				if(settings.articleImage &amp;&amp; settings.articleImage!="null" &amp;&amp; settings.displayThumbnail){
					template = template + '&lt;a id="nextPageThumbnail" href="' + settings.articleUrl + '"&gt;&lt;img src="'+ settings.articleImage +'"/&gt;&lt;/a&gt;';
				}
                        
				template = template + '&lt;a id="nextPageTitle" href="' + settings.articleUrl + '"&gt;&lt;span&gt;' + settings.articleTitle + '&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;/div&gt;' +
                        '&lt;/div&gt;' +
                        '&lt;div id="closeBtn"&gt;&lt;div class="topBar"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;a href="#"&gt;&lt;span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/div&gt;' +
                        '&lt;/div&gt;' +
                        '&lt;iframe style="border:none;position:absolute;top:0;left:0; height:100%;width:100%;z-index:-1;" id="iefix" src="about:blank"&gt;&lt;/iframe&gt;' + //fix for IE bug
                        '&lt;/div&gt;';
                return template;
            };


            var init = function(currentArticleId, articleList, displayThumbnail) {

                settings.articleList = articleList;
                settings.currentArticleId = currentArticleId;
                settings.displayThumbnail = displayThumbnail;

                settings.articleListToSkip = unescape($.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName)).split(',');

                if ($.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName) != null) {
                    chooseNextArticle(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    updateCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                } else {
                    chooseNextArticle(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    createCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                }


                if (settings.articleId != "") {

                    $(renderTemplate()).appendTo("body");

                    $("#nextArticleWrapper a, #readNextOpenInner a").click(function () {
                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'click_headline', 'version_1']);

                        getNavPos($(this).attr("href"), "wayfarerReadNext");
                    });

                    bindUIActions();

                    var displayed = false;

                    setTimeout(function () {
                        if (!displayed) {
                            displayed == true;
                            readNextWidget.display();
                        }

                    }, 10000);

                    $(window).scroll(function () {
                        if (isScrolledIntoView("#tmg-related-links")) {
                            if (!displayed) {
                                displayed == true;
                                readNextWidget.display();
                            }
                        }

                        if(isScrolledIntoView("#shareBottom") || isScrolledIntoView("#newGigyaShareBottom")) {
                            if(settings.autoOpen) {
                                openArticleContainer();
                            }
                        }

                        if($(window).scrollTop() + $(window).height() == $(document).height()) {
                            if(settings.autoOpen) {
                                openArticleContainer();
                            }
                        }
                    });
                }
            };

            var display = function () {
                $(settings.widgetContainer).fadeIn(2000, function () {
                    //animation complete
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_closed', 'version_1']);
                });
            };

            var bindUIActions = function () {
                $(settings.openButton).mouseenter(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'hover_closed', 'version_1']);
                    openArticleContainer();
                });
                $(settings.widgetContainer).mouseleave(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    closeArticleContainer();
                });
                $(settings.closeButton).click(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'click_close', 'version_1']);
                    updateCookie(settings.articleId, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    closeArticleContainer();
                    settings.autoOpen = false;
                });
            };

            var openArticleContainer = function () {
                if (!settings.panelOpen) {
                	$("#readNextArticleContainer").show();//show/hide because height is calculated incorrectly for elements with display:none
                    $("#readNextArticleContainer").hide();
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_open', 'version_1']);
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                    settings.autoOpen = false; //set auto open to false once user has viewed this once
                }
                return false;
            };

            var closeArticleContainer = function () {
                if (settings.panelOpen) {
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                }
                return false;
            };


            var isScrolledIntoView = function(elem) {
                if($(elem).length) { //check if element exists
                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));
                } else {
                    return false;
                }
            };

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {
                    return index == arr.indexOf(el);
                });
            }

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
