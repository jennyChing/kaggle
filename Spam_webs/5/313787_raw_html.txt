     <div class="article-body" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Article">
      <p>
       <a href="/solutions/landing/multiple-sclerosis-symptoms/">
        Multiple sclerosis (MS)
       </a>
       is far from a one-size-fits-all disease. It progresses differently for everyone, and while some people experience only a few symptoms, others must cope with many more. The frequency and severity of
       <a href="/custom/multiple-sclerosis-symptoms/prepare-ms-flare/">
        MS flares
       </a>
       also vary. But one thing is the same for almost everyone with MS: A lot depends on how well you stick with your treatment plan.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are steps you can take to reduce the risk of exacerbations, says Jack Burks, MD, a neurologist and the chief medical officer of the Multiple Sclerosis Association of America. And if youre successful in reducing the number of attacks, you may also alter the progression of the disease in the long run, he says.
      </p>
      <p>
       One way to manage MS attacks is to understand the potential triggers and take steps to keep your symptoms under control.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Why Are MS Symptoms Unpredictable?
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       The immune system and the central nervous system are two major players involved in determining the type and severity of MS symptoms. With MS, your immune system attacks nerve fibers and myelin, the substance that insulates nerve fibers. A lesion, or scar, forms when myelin is damaged. These lesions interrupt and distort the nerve impulses traveling to and from the brain and spinal cord, in turn triggering the various symptoms associated with the disease.
      </p>
      <p>
       Symptoms are different depending on where the myelin is damaged," Dr. Burks says. "And since the location of the myelin damage is unpredictable, symptoms are unpredictable as well." In some cases, lesions occur in an area of the brain that has little or no apparent effect on the body, so symptoms dont appear. This type of disease progression should still be treated, he says, because eventually the attacks can move on to other areas of the brain, with the damage taking its toll at some point.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        How to Manage MS Symptoms Better
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many MS symptoms can be managed effectively with rehabilitation, medication, or other treatment strategies. Thats why its important to be proactive. The key is to start managing symptoms early and surround yourself with a team of professionals who can help you to explore your options.
      </p>
      <p>
       Dont put off talking to your doctor when you experience a new symptom or a flare of familiar symptoms, Burks says. Some MS relapses may not need to be treated, but in many instances there are options such as physical or occupational therapy that your doctor can recommend to help you recover more quickly, he says. Plus, its a good idea to track your symptoms for your medical history.
      </p>
      <p>
       From fatigue to concentration issues, here are solutions for managing some common MS symptoms:
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Fatigue.
       </strong>
       According to the National Multiple Sclerosis Society, about 80 percent of people with MS report having fatigue, which is often a specific type of fatigue related to MS called lassitude. Still, the reasons why fatigue occurs with MS remain a mystery. Fatigue can be severe enough to interfere with the ability to perform necessary tasks at work or at home. Strategies to relieve fatigue include resting when needed and treating other MS symptoms that may be affecting your sleep, such as muscle spasms or bladder dysfunction.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Cognitive changes.
       </strong>
       About half of all people with MS will experience cognitive changes caused by the impact of the disease on certain areas of the brain. Common concerns are
       <a href="/custom/multiple-sclerosis-symptoms/multiple-sclerosis-ways-to-improve-memory/">
        problems with memory
       </a>
       , difficulty concentrating, verbal fluency (finding the right word you want to use), information processing, and so-called executive functions such as planning and prioritizing. Cognitive rehabilitation may help compensate for these changes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Muscle weakness.
       </strong>
       For people with MS, muscle weakness typically affects the muscles used for
       <a href="/custom/multiple-sclerosis-symptoms/multiple-sclerosis-coping-with-walking-problems/">
        walking
       </a>
       . A physical therapy program involving exercise that focuses on stretching and range of motion can help by improving your overall conditioning.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Balance problems.
       </strong>
       MS lesions in certain parts of the brain can cause balance problems. Whats more, other MS symptoms such as tremors, muscle fatigue, and weakness can also affect your balance, so treating these underlying causes may be helpful. Physical therapy is also beneficial, as well as the use of assistive walking devices such as canes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Numbness.
       </strong>
       Numbness or tingling sensations in the body are fairly common for people with MS, often occurring off and on. Most cases dont require treatment, however numbness can become extreme, interfering with the ability to use your hands or feet or even to chew safely. Currently there are no medications available to treat this MS symptom, but steroids may help in severe situations.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Spasticity.
       </strong>
       Muscle stiffness and spasms can be triggered by other MS symptoms such as fatigue, pain, or stress. Physical therapy, orthotics, and medication may be helpful, as well as resolving the underlying causes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Bladder dysfunction.
       </strong>
       About 80 percent of people with MS deal with bladder issues. Damage to nervous system pathways involving the bladder and sphincter can lead to incontinence, voiding dysfunction (during which you have difficulty emptying your bladder), or both. Bladder issues may be managed with medication, a voiding schedule, or dietary changes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Vision problems.
       </strong>
       Blurred vision, double vision, and involuntary eye movement are common during an MS exacerbation. Medication may be prescribed, but often these symptoms will resolve on their own.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pain, depression, and sexual dysfunction are also common MS symptoms. Less common symptoms, which can occur in more advanced cases of MS, include speech problems, difficulty swallowing, difficulty breathing, tremors, and hearing loss. Working with an occupational or physical therapist can help you find ways to overcome these types of symptoms.
      </p>
