               Note that this study was published as an abstract and presented at a conference. These data and conclusions should be considered to be preliminary until published in a peer-reviewed journal.
              </li>
              <li>
               Note that this randomized, controlled trial demonstrated that varenicline was more efficacious than placebo in terms of smoking cessation in HIV positive patients.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           SEATTLE  --  Patients infected with HIV who smoke cigarettes were more likely to quit when they used the smoking cessation agent varenicline (Chantix), researchers said here  --  although fewer than one in five varenicline users succeeded in quitting.
          </p>
          <p>
           After 48 weeks, 17.6% of patients using varenicline remained abstinent compared with 7.2% of patients who were on placebo, reported
           <a href="http://www.pubfacts.com/author/Patrick+Mercie" target="_blank">
            Patrick Mercie, MD,
           </a>
           professor of medicine at Hopital Saint-Andre, Central Hospitalier Universitaire de Bordeaux.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The difference was statistically significant (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.0240), Mercie said at a press conference during the annual
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/MeetingCoverage/CROI/" target="_blank">
            Conference on Retroviruses and Opportunistic Infections
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           In a modified intention-to-treat analysis, 18 of the 102 patients assigned to varenicline were able to abstain from smoking at week 48 compared with eight of the 111 patients who were assigned to the placebo group, Mercie said.
          </p>
          <p>
           "This is about the same results we see in the regular population," Mercie told
           <em>
            MedPage Today
           </em>
           at a press conference. "This was the first randomized, controlled clinical trial of varenicline associated with counseling in people living for HIV."
          </p>
          <p>
           "In France, the prevalence of smoking is between 50% and 60% in HIV-infected subjects, compared with 30% to 40% in the general population," Mercie said. "This is all the more worrying given that HIV infection is associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular disease, cancer, including of the lung, and also infections, notably pulmonary, which are worsened by smoking."
          </p>
          <p>
           He noted that among smoking cessation treatments, varenicline is effective, with a 23% success rate at 52 weeks of treatment in the general population, compared with 10% with a placebo. "Its use is, however, associated with nausea, gastrointestinal disorders, and, more rarely, psychiatric, behavioral, and mood disorders. Its efficacy and safety have, however, never been assessed in people living with HIV. nor have its potential effects on viral replication and CD4-positive T-cell count."
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           In conducting the trial at 30 centers in France, the ANRS 144 Inter-ACTIV study researchers enrolled 248 HIV-infected patients on antiretroviral therapy who were smokers and motivated to quit.
          </p>
          <p>
           The patients were divided into two treatment arms: varenicline given according to recommendations for 12 weeks provided at a dose of 1 mg once daily, then a progressive increase during the first week to twice daily; the second group was given placebo. After the treatment period, the patients were followed up for 36 weeks. Smoking cessation had to be initiated during the first two weeks of treatment.
          </p>
          <p>
           A secondary endpoint was the percentage of people who were able to achieve continuous smoking cessation between weeks 9 and 12, Mercie said. At 12 weeks, 34.3% of patients on varenicline were abstinent, compared with 12.6% in the placebo group, a significant difference (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.0003).
          </p>
          <p>
           "I think the bottom line in this study is that varenicline works in HIV-infected patients as well as it does for the general population," said
           <a href="https://www.uclahealth.org/provider/judith-currier-md" target="_blank">
            Judith Currier, MD
           </a>
           , chief of infectious diseases at the Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center, Los Angeles.
          </p>
          <p>
           "But, that said, it isn't good enough," she told
           <em>
            MedPage Today.
           </em>
           "Clinicians are hesitant about adding another medication to patients already taking a lot of drugs, so at least this study is reassuring that it does work as well in this population. Clinicians may also want to select patients who would not be susceptible to the psychiatric adverse events seen with this drug in the trial."
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "We found that varenicline is safe and well-tolerated," Mercie said. In terms of safety, no significant cardiovascular event was reported in either group. None of the nine cardiovascular adverse events were related to the treatment drug. Among the patients on varenicline, the most frequent events were nausea, abdominal pain and sleep disorders. And there was no negative effect on viral load or CD4-positive cell count, Mercie said.
          </p>
          <p>
           There were nine psychiatric grade 3 or grade 4 drug-related adverse events among the patients on varenicline, he said. Five of those events were depressive episodes. The other were nightmares, he said. But severe psychiatric events were just as common in the placebo group, he noted.
          </p>
          <p>
           Overall, he said, the side effects seen among the HIV patients were the same known adverse events that were seen on patients who were not infected with HIV.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Our study shows that varenicline is effective in helping people living with HIV to quit smoking. To achieve these results, however, certain conditions have to be met: the patient must be motivated, well advised, and followed up regularly by a smoking cessation specialist, must not be in an anxious or depressed state, and must have a well-controlled HIV infection," Mercie said.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Varenicline with appropriate counseling appears to offer some benefit and should be considered in the HIV case management."
          </p>
