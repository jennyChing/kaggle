                            "Meteorological" :P
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            The weather in San Francisco is driven by a couple of different unique features. The most important of these is (as you might guess) the ocean. The California Current is a large current that brings cold ocean water south from the arctic down into Baja California. It's the reason the ocean is so unusually cold year-round.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            In combination with that, we have the Central Valley, the large flat basin between the coastal hills and the Sierra Nevadas. During the day, the cool nighttime Central Valley air is warmed by the Sun (actually, to be precise, it's warmed by the ground, which is warmed by the Sun). As the air heats it expands, and as it expands, it rises. This leaves a low-pressure area over the valley, which sucks in air from over the Pacific Ocean.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Air over the Pacific comes from the tropics and is usually warm and moist from picking up ocean evaporation. However, as this air passes over the cold waters of the California Current, sucked in by the low pressure over the warming Central Valley, it cools down. This reduces its ability to hold water vapor, and the moisture in the air condenses, giving us fog or clouds just offshore.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Now while the arctic sea water is cold, it's still relatively warm compared to ground after a cool night (since the ocean is more resistant to temperature changes than land). So, when this cool moist air is sucked onto land, it gets even colder, to the point where it can sometimes blanket the coastline with fog or clouds.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            What happens next depends entirely on how much ocean air is being sucked up by the Central Valley. If the valley low is weak, the cool air won't be able to make it over Russian Hill, and the Sunset and Richmond will be cold and cloudy, but the rest of San Francisco will be relatively warm and sunny.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            On days when that "central valley suck" is stronger, it can draw the foggy, moist ocean air up over those hills and out into the East Bay, where it usually stops at the Oakland hills. On those days, all of San Francisco is covered in fog or clouds, but the outer East Bay (Walnut Creek, Concord) remain pleasant and warm.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            The Peninsula and the South Bay are often much warmer because The Coast Range that borders the western side south of San Francisco that stop most of the ocean air. The cold air can only really get into the Bay through the only break in the Coast Range -- the Golden Gate. This is why the bridge is almost always fogged in.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Now, as you said, we do also have our unseasonably warm days as well. Most of the time that's because the exact opposite of what I described above is happening. On occasion you can get a high-pressure area over the Central Valley, for one reason or another. When this happens, the high pushes the warm Central Valley air out to the coast. If the push is strong enough, the air can clear the Oakland Hills and roll into San Francisco, creating wonderfully warm weather here.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            An easy way to tell what's going on during a particular day is to check the wind direction. If the wind is coming in from the west, that usually means ocean air is being sucked up into the Central Valley. If the wind is coming in from the east, that usually means there's a high over the valley pushing warm air into the Bay Area.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            California also enjoys a scarcity of thunderstorms, especially during the summer. We owe this pleasure to the location of a semi-permanent high-pressure system in the North Pacific. The Pacific High (as it's called), along with being one of the sources of the California Current, is also the source of most of the West Coast's rain and thunderstorms. Frontal lines spiral outward from a pressure system, like curved bicycle spokes.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            In the summertime, the Pacific High moves further northward, bringing its bad weather with it, which leaves California relatively untouched (while the North Pacific gets to keep its reputation for being perpetually rained upon). In the wintertime it can swing low enough to blanket San Francisco in rain or even the occasional thunderstorm. The intensity of these storms depend on what direction they're coming from -- if the Pacific High is in such a location that the "bicycle spoke" frontal lines (and their associated storm clouds) approach from the southwest, then the storm clouds will be rich with ocean moisture, and can drench the coast, even causing flooding. If the storms are coming from the north or east, they will be comparatively dryer, the Bay Area will see steady but light rain.

                            Mr. Morgan's answer is beautiful (besides the warm air/cold air water vapor slip), but I just wish to add that I once spent a year in a small town on the coast of Oregon - maybe some 500 miles north of the Bay Area - &amp; the weather was nearly identical to my experiences in San Francisco over the last half-decade. The scraggly, sad, out-of-place palm trees growing lonely by the side of the highway seem to bear this out. The difference is Oregon often lacks the hot valley air of California, so it felt more like living off the Great Highway than living in the Mission. The scales of influence were tipped toward the ocean, which were relatively cool in the summer &amp; relatively warm in the winter.
