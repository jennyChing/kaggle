           <h1>
            The History of Data Breaches
           </h1>
           Tuesday April 21, 2015
           <span class="underlined animated full go" data-action="screengo">
           </span>
           <p class="subhead">
           </p>
           <h2>
            By
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/other-posts-by-author/Nate Lord">
             Nate Lord
            </a>
           </h2>
           <p>
            As the wave of data breaches continues to roll on, we take a look back at some of the largest and most damaging data breaches on record. Read on for a historical walk through breaches over time as well as resources for preventing data breaches.
           </p>
          </header>
          <span class="field field-blog-body">
           <p>
            TechTarget
            <a href="http://searchsecurity.techtarget.com/definition/data-breach" target="_blank">
             defines a data breach
            </a>
            as an incident in which sensitive, protected, or confidential data has potentially been viewed, stolen, or used by an individual unauthorized to do so. Data breaches may involve payment card information (PCI), personal health information (PHI), personally identifiable information (PII), trade secrets, or intellectual property. But how long have data breaches been a concern for companies and consumers, and what kind of impact have data breaches had throughout history?
           </p>
           <h3>
            The Origin of Data Breaches
           </h3>
           <p>
            Data breaches have gained widespread attention as businesses of all sizes become increasingly reliant on digital data, cloud computing, and workforce mobility. With sensitive business data stored on local machines, on enterprise databases, and on cloud servers, breaching a companys data has become as simple  or as complex  as gaining access to restricted networks.
           </p>
           <p>
            But data breaches didnt begin when companies began storing their protected data digitally. In fact, data breaches have existed for as long as individuals and companies have maintained records and stored private information. Before computing became commonplace, a data breach could be something as simple as viewing an individuals medical file without authorization or finding sensitive documents that werent properly disposed of. Still, publicly-disclosed data breaches
            <a href="http://searchsecurity.techtarget.com/feature/Data-breach-protection-requires-new-barriers" target="_blank">
             increased in frequency in the 1980s
            </a>
            , and in the 1990s and early 2000s, public awareness of the potential for data breaches began to rise.
           </p>
           <p>
            Laws and
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/blog/what-hipaa-compliance">
             regulations such as HIPAA
            </a>
            or the
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/blog/what-pci-compliance">
             PCI Data Security Standard
            </a>
            are created to provide guidelines for companies and organizations handling certain types of sensitive consumer information. These regulations provide a framework for the required safeguards, storage, and use practices for handling sensitive information. But these rules dont exist in all industries, nor do they definitively stop data breaches from occurring.
           </p>
           <p>
            Most information on data breaches focuses on the time period from 2005 to today. This is largely due to the advancement of technology and proliferation of electronic data throughout the world, making data breaches a top concern for both enterprises and consumers. Todays data breaches can impact hundreds of thousands  often millions  of individual consumers, and even more individual records, all from a single attack on one company.
           </p>
           <h3>
            How Many Data Breaches Occur?
           </h3>
           <p>
            The
            <a href="http://www.privacyrights.org/data-breach" target="_blank">
             Privacy Rights Clearinghouse
            </a>
            , which reports on data breaches impacting consumers, maintains a chronology of data breaches and security breaches dating back to 2005. The data breaches reported by the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse include breaches in which the information compromised includes data elements useful to identity thieves, such as Social Security numbers, account numbers, and driver's license numbers.
           </p>
           <p>
            However, some breaches that do not compromise this type of sensitive information are also reported in order to provide a broad view of the variety and frequency of data breaches. Additionally, the data breaches reported here include only those reported in the United States, not incidents in other countries.
           </p>
           <h3>
            Most Historical Accounts Document Breaches from 2005 Onward
           </h3>
           <p>
            While data breaches were certainly occurring prior to 2005, most of the biggest data breaches recorded in history are reported in 2005 or beyond. This can be attributed to the fact that the worlds volume of data has been growing exponentially year after year, giving cyber criminals a greater opportunity to expose massive volumes of data in a single breach.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a class="colorbox" href="https://digitalguardian.com/sites/default/files/rapid-growth-of-global-data-csc.png">
             <img alt="The Rapid Growth of Global Data, CSC" src="https://digitalguardian.com/sites/default/files/rapid-growth-of-global-data-csc.png"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <center>
            <br/>
            <em>
             Image via
             <a href="http://www.csc.com/insights/flxwd/78931-big_data_universe_beginning_to_explode" target="_blank">
              CSC.com
             </a>
             .
            </em>
           </center>
           <br/>
           <p>
            Consider these projections from a 2012 report by
            <a href="http://www.csc.com/insights/flxwd/78931-big_data_universe_beginning_to_explode" target="_blank">
             CSC
            </a>
            :
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             By 2020, over one-third of all data will live in or pass through the cloud.
            </li>
            <li>
             In 2020, data production is estimated to be 44 times greater than it was in 2009; experts estimate a 4,300 percent increase in annual data generation by 2020.
            </li>
            <li>
             While individuals are responsible for most data creation (70 percent), 80 percent of all data is stored by enterprises.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            In 2005 alone,
            <a href="http://www.privacyrights.org/data-breach" target="_blank">
             136 data breaches were reported
            </a>
            by the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse. More than 4,500 data breaches have been made public since 2005, with more than 816 million individual records breached. In actuality, this number is much higher, as the total number of records breached reported by the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse includes breach reports for which the number of records breached is unknown. Additionally, the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse is not a comprehensive compilation of all breach data, so the actual total records breached as a result of data breaches is likely substantially higher. For instance, the
            <a href="http://www.verizonenterprise.com/DBIR/2015/" target="_blank">
             2015 Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report
            </a>
            covered over 2,100 data breaches in which more than 700 million records were exposed for the year 2014 alone.
           </p>
           <h3>
            Data Breaches Have Become Larger in Number and Impact
           </h3>
           <p>
            Several experts and other media outlets have attempted to name the largest data breaches in history. According to
            <a href="http://www.statista.com/statistics/273550/data-breaches-recorded-in-the-united-states-by-number-of-breaches-and-records-exposed/" target="_blank">
             Statista
            </a>
            , which reports on the number of data breaches and records exposed in the United States since 2005, the number of cyber attacks is on an upward trend. In 2005, for instance, 157 data breaches were reported in the U.S., with 66.9 million records exposed. In 2014, 783 data breaches were reported, with at least 85.61 million total records exposed, representing an increase of nearly 500 percent over 2005. And those are Statistas numbers, which are somewhat conservative when compared to the Verizon DBIR or other industry-standard data breach reports.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a class="colorbox" href="https://digitalguardian.com/sites/default/files/annual-number-of-data-breaches-exposed-records-2005-2014-statista.png">
             <img alt="Annual number of data breaches and exposed records in the United States from 2005 to 2014 (in millions)" src="https://digitalguardian.com/sites/default/files/annual-number-of-data-breaches-exposed-records-2005-2014-statista.png"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <center>
            <br/>
            <em>
             Image via
             <a href="http://www.statista.com/statistics/273550/data-breaches-recorded-in-the-united-states-by-number-of-breaches-and-records-exposed/" target="_blank">
              Statista.com
             </a>
             .
            </em>
           </center>
           <br/>
           <p>
            The trend is not consistently on an uphill slope, however. In 2009, for example, the number of total data breaches reported in the U.S. dropped to 498, from 656 in 2008. That said, the total number of individual records exposed increased sharply, from 35.7 million in 2008 to 222.5 million in 2009. These figures indicate that while fewer breaches occurred, the individual breaches were larger, resulting in a greater number of records exposed per breach.
           </p>
           <p>
            There was also a decline in the number of data breaches reported between 2010 and 2011, with 662 data breaches reported in 2010, and 419 data breaches reported in 2011. Since 2011, however, the number of data breaches reported in the United States has been rising steadily:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             447 data breaches reported in 2012
            </li>
            <li>
             614 data breaches reported in 2013
            </li>
            <li>
             783 data breaches reported in 2014
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/niallmccarthy/2014/08/26/chart-the-biggest-data-breaches-in-u-s-history/" target="_blank">
             Forbes reports
            </a>
            that over the past 10 years, there have been more than 300 data breaches resulting in the theft of 100,000 or more records. And thats just some of the data breaches that were publicly reported.
           </p>
           <h3>
            The Largest Data Breach in History
           </h3>
           <p>
            Interestingly, the largest data breach in history indirectly involved Experian, one of the three main credit reporting agencies. Experian acquired a company called Court Ventures, which gathers and aggregates information from public records, in March 2012. At the time of acquisition, Court Ventures had a contract with a company called U.S. Info Search, enabling customers of U.S. Info Search to access U.S. Info Searchs data to find individuals addresses to determine which court records to review.
           </p>
           <p>
            Court Ventures sold information to a number of third parties, including a Vietnamese fraudster service, which then provided its own customers with the opportunity to look up personal information of Americans, including financial information and Social Security numbers, which was then used for identity theft in many cases.
           </p>
           <p>
            As
            <a href="http://www.experian.com/blogs/news/2014/03/30/court-ventures/" target="_blank">
             Experian explains
            </a>
            , After Experians acquisition of Court Ventures, the U.S. Secret Service notified us that Court Ventures had been and was continuing to resell data from a U.S. Info Search database to a third party, possibly engaged in illegal activity. The suspect in this case posed as a legitimate business owner and obtained access to U.S. Info Search data through Court Ventures prior to the time Experian acquired the company. Experian maintains that no Experian databases were breached; U.S. Info Searchs databases were the source of the consumer information.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some news sources cite 200 million records breached in this incident, which continued for more than 10 months after Experian acquired Court Ventures, although
            <a href="http://www.databreaches.net/experian-sues-court-ventures-to-enforce-indemnification-in-wake-of-court-ventures-breach/" target="_blank">
             DataBreaches.net reports
            </a>
            that 200 million is the figure representing the total number of records stored in the database that was breached. The actual number of records exposed, according to Experian, is unknown, but believed to be much lower.
           </p>
           <h3>
            Other Major Data Breaches
           </h3>
           <p>
            While the Experian/Court Ventures incident is often cited as the largest data breach in history, there are certainly many other data breaches that have gained widespread recognition as having substantial impact, or at minimum, threat of potential impact, on consumers.
           </p>
           <p>
            In 2008 and 2009, Heartland Payment Systems suffered a data breach resulting in the compromise of 130 million records. A New Jersey-based payment processor, Heartland Payment Systems data was breached through malware that was planted on Heartlands network, recording credit card data as it arrived from retailers,
            <a href="http://www.tomsguide.com/us/biggest-data-breaches,news-19083.html" target="_blank">
             Toms Guide reports
            </a>
            . This data, obtained through Heartlands payment processing services for more than 250,000 businesses, was then exposed to cybercriminals. This data breach is regarded as the largest credit card scam in history.
           </p>
           <p>
            Slate.com visualizes the
            <a href="http://www.slate.com/content/dam/slate/blogs/future_tense/2013/08/01/130801_FT_DataBreachFullsize.png" target="_blank">
             largest data breaches in history
            </a>
            , with data since 2005, in an infographic that illustrates whether breaches were the result of hacks, accidental publication, inside jobs, lost or stolen computers, lost or stolen media, poor security, viruses, or unknown causes.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a class="colorbox" href="https://digitalguardian.com/sites/default/files/biggest-data-breaches-of-all-time-slate.png">
             <img alt="The Biggest Data Breaches of All Time by Records Exposed" src="https://digitalguardian.com/sites/default/files/biggest-data-breaches-of-all-time-slate.png"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <center>
            <br/>
            <em>
             Image via
             <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/future_tense/2013/08/02/infographic_shows_biggest_data_breaches_in_history_of_cybersecurity.html" target="_blank">
              Slate.com
             </a>
             .
            </em>
           </center>
           <br/>
           <p>
            The majority of the largest breaches recorded result from hacking attacks, while one of the earliest reported data breaches, impacting AOL and compromising 92 million records in 2005, is reported as an inside job. Another AOL breach, in 2006, resulted from accidental publishing of sensitive data; this breach compromised 20 million records.
           </p>
           <p>
            A data breach impacting the U.S. military is another notable exception to the rule, with 76 million records compromised in 2009 as the result of lost or stolen media. Other notable major data breaches not previously discussed include:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.informationweek.com/estimates-put-tj-maxx-security-fiasco-at-$45-billion/d/d-id/1054704?" target="_blank">
              TK/TJ Maxx
             </a>
             : 94 million records compromised in 2007
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2011/04/26/us-sony-stoldendata-idUSTRE73P6WB20110426" target="_blank">
              Sony PlayStation Network
             </a>
             : 77 million records compromised in 2010
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/technology/blog/2011/may/03/sony-data-breach-online-entertainment" target="_blank">
              Sony Online Entertainment
             </a>
             : 24.6 million records compromised in 2011
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2013/03/04/tech/web/evernote-hacked/" target="_blank">
              Evernote
             </a>
             : 50 million records compromised in 2013
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.cnet.com/news/livingsocial-hacked-50-million-affected/" target="_blank">
              Living Social
             </a>
             : 50 million records compromised in 2013
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2013/12/sources-target-investigating-data-breach/" target="_blank">
              Target
             </a>
             : 70 million records compromised in 2013
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://finance.yahoo.com/news/hackers-raid-ebay-historic-breach-034232750.html" target="_blank">
              Ebay
             </a>
             : 145 million records compromised in 2014
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="https://corporate.homedepot.com/mediacenter/pages/statement1.aspx" target="_blank">
              Home Depot
             </a>
             : 56 million records compromised in 2014
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/larrymagid/2014/10/02/jp-morgan-chase-warns-customers-about-massive-data-breach/" target="_blank">
              JP Morgan Chase
             </a>
             : 76 million records compromised in 2014
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="https://www.anthemfacts.com/ceo" target="_blank">
              Anthem
             </a>
             : 80 million records compromised in 2015
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Among these major data breaches are a multitude of other breaches affecting companies of all sizes, with varying levels of impact. This data, particularly in light of the fact that most of these breaches are carried out by hackers, illustrates that data breaches are becoming more widespread  and having a much larger impact, compromising millions of records containing sensitive consumer, company, or user data.
           </p>
           <p>
            With even the worlds largest companies suffering massive data breaches impacting millions of consumers, modern enterprises require a comprehensive, full-circle approach to data protection and security. The reactive approaches of yesterday simply dont cut it in the modern threat landscape.
           </p>
           <h3>
            Data Breach Defense and Prevention Resources
           </h3>
           <p>
            With the ever-increasing number of data breaches and increased threats to security  enterprise, small business, and personal security alike  data breach defense and prevention has advanced accordingly, offering greater protections and a more proactive approach to security to ensure the safety of sensitive information. The following resources offer additional information on the advancement of data protection, as well as valuable tips for preventing data breaches.
           </p>
           <p>
            Members of the U.S. House of Representatives House Energy and Commerce Committee have
            <a href="http://energycommerce.house.gov/press-release/committee-advances-bills-protect-consumers-and-environment" target="_blank">
             advanced a bill
            </a>
            that addresses the increased threat of cyber crime to consumers. The
            <a href="https://energycommerce.house.gov/hearing/discussion-draft-hr-data-security-and-breach-notification-act-2015" target="_blank">
             Data Security and Breach Notification Act
            </a>
            is a bipartisan solution to address the growing problem of cyber crimes and protect vulnerable information from criminals. The legislation establishes a nationwide safety regime for data protection and breach notification.
           </p>
           <p>
            Verizons annual
            <a href="http://www.verizonenterprise.com/DBIR/2015/" target="_blank">
             Data Breach Investigations Report
            </a>
            has become a security industry favorite for its assessments of thousands of data breaches that were investigated by Verizon, the U.S. Secret Service, and several partnering security organizations. Every year the DBIR sets out to analyze major trends in data breaches, victims being targeted, attack patterns, and more.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://datalossdb.org/" target="_blank">
             DataLossDB
            </a>
            is an open source, community-maintained research project that covers publicly-disclosed data breaches across the globe. The site provides details around data loss incidents as well as analysis of historical data breach trends.
           </p>
           <p>
            IT Business Edge provides a slideshow illustrating
            <a href="http://www.itbusinessedge.com/slideshows/show.aspx?c=79585" target="_blank">
             8 Ways to Prevent Data Breaches
            </a>
            , including tips such as instituting end user security awareness, performing regular vulnerability assessments, and other helpful tactics.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.databreachtoday.com/" target="_blank">
             Data Breach Today
            </a>
            is a multi-media news resource and a robust source of information on the latest data breaches, their impact, and strategies for preventing your company from falling victim to cyber criminals.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.databreachwatch.org/" target="_blank">
             Data Breach Watch
            </a>
            is another resource reporting on discovered data breaches, news, and trends impacting both consumers and companies in every vertical.
           </p>
           <p>
            The
            <a href="http://www.globalprivacyblog.com/" target="_blank">
             Global Privacy &amp; Security Compliance Law Blog
            </a>
            is an excellent resource for companies faced with stringent and ever-changing security regulations and compliance requirements.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://thehill.com/blogs/congress-blog/technology/232041-data-breach-prevention-is-dead" target="_blank">
             The Hill
            </a>
            takes a critical look at data breach prevention practices in light of the alarming statistics on the number of data breaches and their subsequent fallouts in 2014. As this article points out, the number of data breaches that involved 100 million customer records or more doubled in 2014. In fact, one billion data records were either lost or stolen in 2014, a 71 percent increase over 2013. Its time that executives and information security professionals accept the fact that their companies will be breached and start thinking outside the box when it comes to data security. To be in denial of this truth is to not accept reality.
           </p>
           <p>
            This article from
            <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/01/26/opinion/sunday/preventing-the-next-data-breach.html?_r=0" target="_blank">
             The New York Times
            </a>
            discusses strategies for minimizing the risk of a data breach in light of the major breaches impacting some of the worlds largest companies in recent history. One suggestion, for instance, is minimizing or eliminating the unnecessary storage of data. Companies also need to think carefully about what data they are collecting and storing. By keeping lots of sensitive information, they place themselves and their customers at considerable  and in some cases unnecessarily greater  risk than if they had deleted the data or never collected it. To take one startling example, security experts say there was absolutely no reason for Target to have stored the four-digit personal identification numbers, or PINs, of their customers debit cards.
           </p>
           <p>
            Toms IT Pro reports on
            <a href="http://www.tomsitpro.com/articles/data-breach-prevention-technologies,1-1662.html" target="_blank">
             new tools and technologies for preventing data breaches
            </a>
            following the 2014 SecureWorld Expo.
           </p>
           <p>
            While no company wants to suffer a data breach, healthcare providers and their business associates stand to lose even more from a data breach, with penalties under HIPAA for inadequately protecting personal health information. CIO takes a look at the risks of a data breach in the healthcare industry, offering
            <a href="http://www.cio.com/article/2368702/healthcare/12-tips-to-prevent-a-healthcare-data-breach.html" target="_blank">
             12 tips to prevent a healthcare data breach
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            According to
            <a href="http://searchsecurity.techtarget.com/feature/Data-breach-protection-requires-new-barriers" target="_blank">
             this article
            </a>
            from TechTarget, Assumption of breach is the new norm. The article goes on to describe how this critical shift in thinking is being used to create more stringent, comprehensive data breach protection policies and protocols.
           </p>
           <p>
            As many of these sources point out, 2014 was a record-breaking year for data breaches, renewing the focus on the need for better methods for both preventing and responding to the (inevitable) data breach. This 60-minute webinar training from Bank Info Security discusses 
            <a href="http://www.bankinfosecurity.com/webinars/changing-landscape-data-breaches-consumer-protection-in-2015-w-612" target="_blank">
             The Changing Landscape of Data Breaches &amp; Consumer Protection in 2015
            </a>
            . The full webinar will be available on-demand soon.
           </p>
           <p>
            Experian, itself subjected to the perils of the aftermath of a data breach, has released its second annual
            <a href="http://www.experian.com/assets/data-breach/white-papers/2015-industry-forecast-experian.pdf" target="_blank">
             Data Breach Industry Forecast for 2015
            </a>
            . The report provides an overview of data breach trends and the outlook for 2015, and also emphasizes the need for companies to have a data breach response plan.
           </p>
           <p>
            Holland &amp; Knight takes a look at 
            <a href="http://www.hklaw.com/PrivacyBlog/How-Recent-Data-Breaches-are-Changing-the-Face-of-Cybersecurity-for-the-Private-Sector-02-13-2015/?utm_source=Mondaq&amp;utm_medium=syndication&amp;utm_campaign=View-Original" target="_blank">
             How Recent Data Breaches are Changing the Face of Cybersecurity for the Private Sector
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            We at Digital Guardian regularly cover the topic of data breaches and provide insights on both preventing and responding to a data breach. In this
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/blog/data-breach-experts-share-most-important-next-step-you-should-take-after-data-breach-2014-2015">
             expert roundup
            </a>
            , we ask 30 data security experts to share the most important next step you should take following a data breach. In
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/blog/startups-data-breaches-how-startup-can-protect-itself-data-breach-2014-beyond">
             another roundup
            </a>
            , we asked 27 data security experts for their insights on the most cost-effective ways startups can protect themselves from data breaches. Be sure to follow
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/blog" target="_blank">
             our blog
            </a>
            for updates on the latest data security information, research, and discussions, and visit our
            <a href="https://digitalguardian.com/resources">
             resources section
            </a>
            for analyst reports, case studies, data sheets, and other resources on data breach prevention and data security.
           </p>
          </span>
