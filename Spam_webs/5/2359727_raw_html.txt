         <h1 class="title">
          See an Abandoned Bus Station Transform Into an Idea Lab
         </h1>
         <div class="headline">
          The makeover aims to build a community of tech entrepreneurs who can shape the future of Tijuana.
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="section">
         <div class="row row-remove-xs">
          <div class="main-image">
           <img alt="" class="main-media" src="http://www.takepart.com/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/Buenrostro00000.jpg?itok=1808ft-6"/>
           <div class="caption">
            <p>
             The interior of an abandoned bus station in Tijuana, Mexico, before it was remodeled as a collaborative workspace. (Photo: Hernan Cazares)
            </p>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="section">
         <div class="row">
          <div class="author-teaser col-xxs-12 col-xs-10 col-xs-offset-1">
           <div class="line line-style-2">
           </div>
           <div class="row author">
            <div class="published-at col-xxs-10 col-xxs-offset-2">
             Feb 27, 2015
            </div>
            <div class="image col-xxs-2">
             <img class="img-circle" src="http://www.takepart.com/sites/default/files/styles/70x70_thumbnail/public/profiles/photos/EnriqueGili%5B3%5D.jpg?itok=eGbR64Ze"/>
            </div>
            <div class="about col-xxs-10 col-xs-7">
             Enrique Gili is a freelance journalist in San Diego covering science, nature, and technology.
            </div>
            <div class="links col-xxs-10 col-xxs-offset-2 col-xs-3 col-xs-offset-0">
             <ul class="menu horizontal-menu">
              <li>
               <a class="btn btn-secondary" href="/author/enrique-gili" id="author_bio">
                Bio
               </a>
              </li>
              <li>
               <a class="btn btn-secondary" href="/www.twitter.com/gili92107" target="_blank">
                <span class="icon i-twitter">
                </span>
               </a>
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
           </div>
           <div class="line line-style-1">
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="main-content">
          <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
           <div class="field-items">
            <div class="field-item even">
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               Put the words
               <em>
                <a href="http://www.takepart.com/world/technology" target="_self">
                 innovation
                </a>
               </em>
               and
               <em>
                Tijuana
               </em>
               together, and theres bound to be
              </span>
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               a few raised eyebrows. But in pockets of one of Mexicos most vibrant yet historically violent cities,
              </span>
              a tech scene is starting to emergeone led by entrepreneurs who hope to reform the citys reputation. Their goal? To turn whats known as a destination for cheap thrills and discount drugs into one with solid footing in the global digital economy.
             </p>
             <p>
              One such entrepreneuris Miguel Buenrostro, founder of
              <a href="https://www.facebook.com/ReactivandoEspacios" target="_blank">
               Reactivando Espacios
              </a>
              , a venture that develops creative spaces by reviving underused and forgotten buildings by fusing commerce with art and culture. One unlikely building it has turned into a modern, bustling workspace is an abandoned bus station.
             </p>
             <p>
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               Built in 1983, the building had been abandoned and fell into disrepair over the years. It was officially closed in 2009. But Buenrostro, with partners Miguel Marshall and Marco Soto, saw its potential and
              </span>
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               began renovating the space.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span>
               During the
              </span>
              80s, the bus depot was designed to accommodate the Mexican diaspora, and in the decades that followed, the station thrived. At the time, Tijuana was the destinationof hundreds of thousands of Mexican migrants who had left the countryside in search of better jobs, and it was their starting point for work in the maquiladorasfree-trade factoriesalong the U.S.-Mexico border.
             </p>
             <figure class="row align-center">
              <div class="inline-feature-image">
               <img alt="" class="inline-image" src="http://www.takepart.com/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/Edificio55.jpg?itok=8uKJxtXX" title=""/>
               <div class="caption col-xxs-12">
                <p>
                 <em>
                  Exterior of the former bus station. (Photo: Hernan Cazares)
                 </em>
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
             </figure>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span>
               At one point, the station handled 20,000 people per week, but that all
              </span>
              came to an end, said Buenrostro. A drug war peaked with the arrest of the mayor in 2007 for possessing weapons tied to murders, along with the murder of 32 police officers in 2010. Subsequent economic forces and
              <a href="http://maryafischer.com/baghdad-on-the-border/" target="_blank">
               the decline of tourism
              </a>
              sapped the city of its vitality. The people stopped coming, Buenrostro said.
             </p>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               But he and his team breathed life into the station. In spring 2014, it opened its doors as
              </span>
              <a href="https://www.facebook.com/hubstn" target="_blank">
               Hub Stn
              </a>
              . It operates with the credence that like-minded entrepreneurs can benefit from working in proximity to one another. A collaborative workspace, Hub Stn caters
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               to Tijuanas thousands of tech workers, a bilingual workforce as fluent in
              </span>
              database management and C++ as it is in English. Many more are set to arrive. More than 83,000 students in the city are enrolled across 35 public and private universities, 14 technical schools, and 57 high schools, and Tijuana has 13 nationally accredited university programs in engineering, information sciences, and business administration, according to
              <a href="http://www.creativeclass.com/rfcgdb/articles/CCG_report_fullpour_f3.pdf" target="_blank">
               a report
              </a>
              by Creative Class Group.
             </p>
             <figure class="row align-center">
              <div class="inline-feature-image">
               <img alt="" class="inline-image" src="http://www.takepart.com/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/5AnaBlanco.jpg?itok=LQ7eLcdG" title=""/>
               <div class="caption col-xxs-12">
                <p>
                 <em>
                  Interior area of the station before it was remodeled by Reactivando Espacios. (Photo: Hernan Cazares)
                 </em>
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
             </figure>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               Hub Stn has 11 tenants, including Reactivando Espacios, totaling 50 entrepreneurs focused on technology and corporate branding. The second floor is modern and open, offering office amenities such as a conference room, Wi-Fi access, and a common area with enough seating to accommodate dozens comfortably.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               Tijuana has always been a very creative city out of necessity, said Buenrostro. You have to improvise in order to survive.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               The airy space appeals to Tijuanas skilled technical workers, many of whom are looking for their own version of Silicon Valley.
              </span>
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               Damian Rivas is a self-taught
              </span>
              Web designer who began his career painting murals of Tijuanas gritty cityscape. These days, his main focus is working out of Hub Stn, handling the digital presence of corporate clients based in the United States and Mexico. He sees Tijuana as a test lab and a proving ground for companies that want to establish themselves not just in Mexico but also abroad. Tijuana is the gateway to Latin America, he said.
             </p>
             <figure class="row align-center">
              <div class="inline-feature-image">
               <img alt="" class="inline-image" src="http://www.takepart.com/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/transformaci%C3%B3n.jpg?itok=fDKyWQsz" title=""/>
               <div class="caption col-xxs-12">
                <p>
                 <em>
                  The newly renovated space now includes a common area with Wifi access and enough seating for dozens of people. (Photo: Hernan Cazares)
                 </em>
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
             </figure>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               His latest project is an app designed to
              </span>
              alleviate parking congestion on Tijuanas streets, which could also prove useful in San Francisco or Mexico City. Rather than drive blind hoping to find a parking spot, users would be able to reserve a slot in one of the citys commercial parking structures. Data collected on the shopping and traffic patterns of consumers could be valuable as well. As it is, parking-lot owners know next to nothing about their customers, he said.
             </p>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-d6b2ae4e-c1e3-8575-a8ec-0de6750b4d78">
               Some could say thats the same level of knowledge many have about Tijuana. But
              </span>
              behind its tough image, the city is starting to prove its ready to move on.
             </p>
             <p dir="ltr">
              <span id="docs-internal-guid-3b5e1bb9-c1e1-6ee3-2eff-cec72c506f15">
               You can look at Tijuana and think its lacking some basic things, or you can look at it as a place of opportunity, Buenrostro said. At least for the creative and the entrepreneurial community, its our job to create this culture and look for opportunities.
              </span>
             </p>
