     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Tibet Autonomous Region
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       TAR
      </b>
      ),
      <b>
       Tibet
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Xizang
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_alphabet" title="Tibetan alphabet">
       Tibetan
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="bo" style="display:inline-block; font-family:'DDC Uchen', Kailash, Jomolhari,'Tibetan Machine Uni'; font-weight:normal; font-size:130%; vertical-align:top;">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Xzng
       </span>
      </i>
      ) for short, also called the
      <b>
       Xizang Autonomous Region
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_China" title="Provinces of China">
       province
      </a>
      -level
      <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_regions_of_China" title="Autonomous regions of China">
       autonomous region
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       People's Republic of China
      </a>
      (PRC). It was created in 1965 on the basis of Tibet's
      <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_of_Tibet_into_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Incorporation of Tibet into the People's Republic of China">
       Incorporation
      </a>
      by the PRC in 1951.
     </p>
     <p>
      Within China, Tibet is identified as an Autonomous Region. The current borders of Tibet were generally established in the 18th century
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and include about half of
      <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">
       ethno-cultural Tibet
      </a>
      . The Tibet Autonomous Region is the second-largest
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Administrative divisions of the People's Republic of China">
       province-level division of China
      </a>
      by area, spanning over 1,200,000 square kilometres (460,000sqmi), after
      <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">
       Xinjiang
      </a>
      , and mostly due to its harsh and rugged terrain, is the least densely populated provincial-level division of the PRC.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Tibet" title="History of Tibet">
       History of Tibet
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Modern scholars
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_sovereignty_debate" title="Tibetan sovereignty debate">
       still debate
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_relations_during_the_Ming_dynasty" title="Sino-Tibetan relations during the Ming dynasty">
       exact nature of relations
      </a>
      between Tibet and the Chinese
      <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">
       Ming dynasty
      </a>
      (13681644) and whether the Ming had sovereignty over Tibet
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wylie_2003_470_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wylie_2003_470-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wang_nyima_1997_1_40_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wang_nyima_1997_1_40-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laird_2006_106_107_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-laird_2006_106_107-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      after the
      <a href="/wiki/Mongol_conquest_of_Tibet" title="Mongol conquest of Tibet">
       Mongol conquest of Tibet
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tibet_under_Yuan_administrative_rule" title="Tibet under Yuan administrative rule">
       Yuan administrative rule
      </a>
      in the 13th and 14th centuries. While Tibet has formally been a
      <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">
       protectorate
      </a>
      of China and
      <a href="/wiki/Tibet_under_Qing_administrative_rule" title="Tibet under Qing administrative rule">
       under administrative rule
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">
       Qing dynasty
      </a>
      (1644-1912) since 1720, from 1912 to 1950 Tibet was dissolved of suzerainty under
      <a href="/wiki/China_proper" title="China proper">
       China proper
      </a>
      as a result of the
      <a href="/wiki/Xinhai_Revolution" title="Xinhai Revolution">
       Xinhai Revolution
      </a>
      and Japanese occupation during the
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . Other parts of ethno-cultural Tibet (eastern
      <a href="/wiki/Kham" title="Kham">
       Kham
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Amdo" title="Amdo">
       Amdo
      </a>
      ) have also been under the administration of the Chinese dynastic government since the mid-eighteenth century;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      today they are distributed among the provinces of
      <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">
       Qinghai
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">
       Gansu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">
       Sichuan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">
       Yunnan
      </a>
      . (See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Xikang" title="Xikang">
       Xikang
      </a>
      province)
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1950, the People's Liberation Army defeated the Tibetan army in a
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chamdo" title="Battle of Chamdo">
       battle
      </a>
      fought near the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Chamdo" title="Chamdo">
       Chamdo
      </a>
      . In 1951, the Tibetan representatives signed a
      <a href="/wiki/Seventeen_Point_Agreement_for_the_Peaceful_Liberation_of_Tibet" title="Seventeen Point Agreement for the Peaceful Liberation of Tibet">
       seventeen-point agreement
      </a>
      with the Chinese
      <a href="/wiki/Central_People%27s_Government_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China_(1949%E2%80%9354)" title="Central People's Government of the People's Republic of China (194954)">
       Central People's Government
      </a>
      affirming China's
      <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">
       sovereignty
      </a>
      over Tibet and the
      <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_of_Tibet_into_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Incorporation of Tibet into the People's Republic of China">
       incorporation of Tibet
      </a>
      . The agreement was ratified in Lhasa a few months later.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although the 17-point agreement had provided for an autonomous administration led by the
      <a href="/wiki/Dalai_Lama" title="Dalai Lama">
       Dalai Lama
      </a>
      , a "Preparatory Committee for the Autonomous Region of Tibet" (PCART) was established in 1955 to create a parallel system of administration along Communist lines. The Dalai Lama fled to India in 1959 and renounced the 17-point agreement. Tibet Autonomous Region
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_administrative_divisions_of_China" title="History of the administrative divisions of China">
       was established
      </a>
      in 1965, thus making Tibet an administrative division on the same legal footing as a Chinese province.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
       Geography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Tibet" title="Geography of Tibet">
       Geography of Tibet
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Tibet Autonomous Region is located on the
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">
       Tibetan Plateau
      </a>
      , the highest region on earth. In northern Tibet elevations reach an average of over 4,572 metres (15,000ft).
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">
       Mount Everest
      </a>
      is located on Tibet's border with
      <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">
       Nepal
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The China's provincial-level areas of
      <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">
       Xinjiang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">
       Qinghai
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">
       Sichuan
      </a>
      lie to the north, northeast, and east, respectively, of the Tibet AR. There is also a short border with
      <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">
       Yunnan
      </a>
      province to the southeast. The PRC has border disputes with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_India" title="Republic of India">
       Republic of India
      </a>
      over the
      <a href="/wiki/McMahon_Line" title="McMahon Line">
       McMahon Line
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh" title="Arunachal Pradesh">
       Arunachal Pradesh
      </a>
      , known to the Chinese as "
      <a href="/wiki/South_Tibet" title="South Tibet">
       South Tibet
      </a>
      ". The disputed territory of
      <a href="/wiki/Aksai_Chin" title="Aksai Chin">
       Aksai Chin
      </a>
      is to the west, and its boundary with that region is not defined. The other countries to the south are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">
       Myanmar
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">
       Bhutan
      </a>
      and Nepal.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Everest_North_Face_toward_Base_Camp_Tibet_Luca_Galuzzi_2006_edit_1.jpg">

       </a>

        <div class="magnify">

     <p>
      Physically, the Tibet AR may be divided into two parts, the "lakes region" in the west and north-west, and the "river region", which spreads out on three sides of the former on the east, south, and west. Both regions receive limited amounts of rainfall as they lie in the
      <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">
       rain shadow
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">
       Himalayas
      </a>
      , however the region names are useful in contrasting their
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">
       hydrological
      </a>
      structures, and also in contrasting their different cultural uses which is
      <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">
       nomadic
      </a>
      in the lake region and
      <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
       agricultural
      </a>
      in the river region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On the south the Tibet AR is bounded by the Himalayas, and on the north by a broad mountain system. The system at no point narrows to a single range; generally there are three or four across its breadth. As a whole the system forms the watershed between rivers flowing to the Indian Ocean  the
      <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">
       Indus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_River" title="Brahmaputra River">
       Brahmaputra
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Salween_River" title="Salween River">
       Salween
      </a>
      and its tributaries  and the streams flowing into the undrained salt lakes to the north.
     </p>
     <p>
      The lake region extends from the
      <a href="/wiki/Pangong_Tso" title="Pangong Tso">
       Pangong Tso Lake
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">
       Ladakh
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Rakshastal" title="Lake Rakshastal">
       Lake Rakshastal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yamdrok_Lake" title="Yamdrok Lake">
       Yamdrok Lake
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Manasarovar" title="Lake Manasarovar">
       Lake Manasarovar
      </a>
      near the source of the
      <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">
       Indus River
      </a>
      , to the sources of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salween" title="Salween">
       Salween
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">
       Mekong
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yangtze_River" title="Yangtze River">
       Yangtze
      </a>
      . Other lakes include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dagze_Co" title="Dagze Co">
       Dagze Co
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Namtso" title="Namtso">
       Namtso
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pagsum_Co" title="Pagsum Co">
       Pagsum Co
      </a>
      . The lake region is a wind-swept Alpine grassland. This region is called the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chang_Tang" title="Chang Tang">
       Chang Tang
      </a>
      (Byang sang) or 'Northern Plateau' by the people of Tibet. It is some 1,100km (680mi) broad, and covers an area about equal to that of France. Due to its great distance from the ocean it is extremely arid and possesses no river outlet. The mountain ranges are spread out, rounded, disconnected, separated by relatively flat valleys.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Tibet AR is dotted over with large and small lakes, generally salt or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">
       alkaline
      </a>
      , and intersected by streams. Due to the presence of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discontinuous_permafrost" title="Discontinuous permafrost">
       discontinuous permafrost
      </a>
      over the Chang Tang, the soil is boggy and covered with tussocks of grass, thus resembling the Siberian
      <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">
       tundra
      </a>
      . Salt and fresh-water lakes are intermingled. The lakes are generally without outlet, or have only a small
      <a href="/wiki/Effluent" title="Effluent">
       effluent
      </a>
      . The deposits consist of
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">
       soda
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">
       potash
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Borax" title="Borax">
       borax
      </a>
      and common
      <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
       salt
      </a>
      . The lake region is noted for a vast number of
      <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">
       hot springs
      </a>
      , which are widely distributed between the Himalaya and 34 N, but are most numerous to the west of Tengri Nor (north-west of Lhasa). So intense is the cold in this part of Tibet that these springs are sometimes represented by columns of ice, the nearly boiling water having frozen in the act of ejection.
     </p>
     <p>
      The river region is characterised by fertile mountain valleys and includes the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarlung_Tsangpo_River_(Tibet)" title="Yarlung Tsangpo River (Tibet)">
       Yarlung Tsangpo River
      </a>
      (the upper courses of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmaputra" title="Brahmaputra">
       Brahmaputra
      </a>
      ) and its major tributary, the
      <a href="/wiki/Nyang_River" title="Nyang River">
       Nyang River
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salween" title="Salween">
       Salween
      </a>
      , the Yangtze, the
      <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">
       Mekong
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">
       Yellow River
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarlung_Tsangpo_Canyon" title="Yarlung Tsangpo Canyon">
       Yarlung Tsangpo Canyon
      </a>
      , formed by a horseshoe bend in the river where it flows around
      <a href="/wiki/Namcha_Barwa" title="Namcha Barwa">
       Namcha Barwa
      </a>
      , is the deepest, and possibly longest canyon in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among the mountains there are many narrow valleys. The valleys of
      <a href="/wiki/Lhasa" title="Lhasa">
       Lhasa
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xigaz%C3%AA" title="Xigaz">
       Xigaz
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gyantse" title="Gyantse">
       Gyantse
      </a>
      and the Brahmaputra are free from permafrost, covered with good soil and groves of trees, well irrigated, and richly cultivated.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Tibet_Valley" title="South Tibet Valley">
       South Tibet Valley
      </a>
      is formed by the Yarlung Tsangpo River during its middle reaches, where it travels from west to east. The valley is approximately 1200 kilometres long and 300 kilometres wide. The valley descends from 4500 metres above sea level to 2800 metres. The mountains on either side of the valley are usually around 5000 metres high.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Lakes here include
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Paiku" title="Lake Paiku">
       Lake Paiku
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Puma_Yumco" title="Lake Puma Yumco">
       Lake Puma Yumco
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Government">
       Government
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Government">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Chairmen_of_the_Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="List of Chairmen of the Tibet Autonomous Region">
       List of Chairmen of the Tibet Autonomous Region
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_provincial_leaders_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="List of provincial leaders of the People's Republic of China">
       List of provincial leaders of the People's Republic of China
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Tibet Autonomous Region is a province-level entity of the People's Republic of China. It is governed by a People's Government, led by a Chairman. In practice, however, the Chairman is subordinate to the branch secretary of the
      <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">
       Communist Party of China
      </a>
      . As a matter of convention, the Chairman has almost always been an ethnic Tibetan, while the party secretary has almost always been a non-Tibetan. The current Chairman is
      <a href="/wiki/Losang_Jamcan" title="Losang Jamcan">
       Losang Jamcan
      </a>
      and the current party secretary is
      <a href="/wiki/Chen_Quanguo" title="Chen Quanguo">
       Chen Quanguo
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Indias request, to open a consulate in Lhasa, capital of Tibet has been rejected by Beijing. Beijing, instead has offered Chengdu, the capital of Sichuan province. According to diplomatic sources familiar with the developments, the Chinese dont want more consulates in Lhasa, where only Nepal has one.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">

      <a href="/wiki/List_of_administrative_divisions_of_the_Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="List of administrative divisions of the Tibet Autonomous Region">

      <a href="/wiki/List_of_township-level_divisions_of_the_Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="List of township-level divisions of the Tibet Autonomous Region">

     <p>
      Tibet Autonomous Region is divided into seven
      <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_China#Prefectural_level" title="Administrative divisions of China">
       prefecture-level divisions
      </a>
      : four
      <a href="/wiki/Prefecture-level_city" title="Prefecture-level city">
       prefecture-level cities
      </a>
      and three
      <a href="/wiki/Prefectures_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Prefectures of the People's Republic of China">
       prefectures
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      These in turn are subdivided into a total of sixty-eight
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_(People%27s_Republic_of_China)" title="County (People's Republic of China)">
       counties
      </a>
      , and four
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_China" title="District of China">
       districts
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Lhasa" title="Lhasa">
       Chengguan District
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Samzhubz%C3%AA_District" title="Samzhubz District">
       Samzhubz District
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karub_District" title="Karub District">
       Karub District
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Bayi_District" title="Bayi District">
       Bayi District
      </a>
      ).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">
       Demographics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demographics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      With an average of only 2 people per square kilometer, The Tibet Autonomous Region has the lowest
      <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">
       population density
      </a>
      among any of the Chinese province-level administrative regions, mostly due to its harsh and rugged terrain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thechinaperspective.com_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-thechinaperspective.com-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2011 the Tibetan population was 3 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The ethnic
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">
       Tibetans
      </a>
      , comprising 90.48% of the population,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E8.A5.BF.E8.97.8F.E8.87.AA.E6.B2.BB.E5.8C.BA.E5.B8.B8.E4.BD.8F.E4.BA.BA.E5.8F.A3.E8.B6.85.E8.BF.87300.E4.B8.87_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-.E8.A5.BF.E8.97.8F.E8.87.AA.E6.B2.BB.E5.8C.BA.E5.B8.B8.E4.BD.8F.E4.BA.BA.E5.8F.A3.E8.B6.85.E8.BF.87300.E4.B8.87-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      mainly adhere to
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">
       Tibetan Buddhism
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%B6n" title="Bn">
       Bn
      </a>
      , although there is an
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Muslims" title="Tibetan Muslims">
       ethnic Tibetan Muslim community
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other Muslim ethnic groups such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">
       Hui
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Salar_people" title="Salar people">
       Salar
      </a>
      have inhabited the Region. There is also a tiny Tibetan
      <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
       Christian
      </a>
      community in eastern Tibet. Smaller tribal groups such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Monpa_people" title="Monpa people">
       Monpa
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lhoba" title="Lhoba">
       Lhoba
      </a>
      , who follow a combination of
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">
       Tibetan Buddhism
      </a>
      and spirit worship, are found mainly in the southeastern parts of the region.
     </p>
     <p>
      Historically, the population of Tibet consisted of primarily ethnic
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">
       Tibetans
      </a>
      . According to tradition the original ancestors of the Tibetan people, as represented by the six red bands in the Tibetan flag, are: the Se, Mu, Dong, Tong, Dru and Ra. Other traditional ethnic groups with significant population or with the majority of the ethnic group reside in Tibet include
      <a href="/wiki/Bai_people" title="Bai people">
       Bai people
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Blang_people" title="Blang people">
       Blang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bonans" title="Bonans">
       Bonan
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dongxiang_people" title="Dongxiang people">
       Dongxiang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">
       Han
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">
       Hui people
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lhoba" title="Lhoba">
       Lhoba
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lisu_people" title="Lisu people">
       Lisu people
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Miao_people" title="Miao people">
       Miao
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">
       Mongols
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monguor" title="Monguor">
       Monguor (Tu people)
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Monpa_people" title="Monpa people">
       Menba (Monpa)
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mosuo" title="Mosuo">
       Mosuo
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nakhi" title="Nakhi">
       Nakhi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Qiang_people" title="Qiang people">
       Qiang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nu_people" title="Nu people">
       Nu people
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pumi_people" title="Pumi people">
       Pumi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Salar_people" title="Salar people">
       Salar
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Yi_people" title="Yi people">
       Yi people
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclopdia Britannica Eleventh Edition">
       Encyclopdia Britannica Eleventh Edition
      </a>
      published between 19101911, total population of Tibetan capital of Lhasa, including the lamas in the city and vicinity, was about 30,000, and the permanent population also included Chinese families (about 2,000).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Most
      <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">
       Han people
      </a>
      in the TAR (8.17% of the total population)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      are recent migrants, because all of the Han were
      <a href="/wiki/Population_transfer" title="Population transfer">
       expelled
      </a>
      from "Outer Tibet" (
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%9C-Tsang" title="-Tsang">
       Central Tibet
      </a>
      ) following the
      <a href="/wiki/British_expedition_to_Tibet" title="British expedition to Tibet">
       British expedition
      </a>
      until the establishment of the PRC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Real population of
      <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">
       Han people
      </a>
      is almost 1/3 of TAR population (more than 50% in
      <a href="/wiki/Lhasa" title="Lhasa">
       Lhasa
      </a>
      ). Only 8% of
      <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">
       Han people
      </a>
      have
      <a href="/wiki/Hukou_system" title="Hukou system">
       household registration
      </a>
      in TAR, other keep their
      <a href="/wiki/Hukou_system" title="Hukou system">
       household registration
      </a>
      in place of origin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E8.A5.BF.E8.97.8F.E8.87.AA.E6.B2.BB.E5.8C.BA.E5.B8.B8.E4.BD.8F.E4.BA.BA.E5.8F.A3.E8.B6.85.E8.BF.87300.E4.B8.87_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-.E8.A5.BF.E8.97.8F.E8.87.AA.E6.B2.BB.E5.8C.BA.E5.B8.B8.E4.BD.8F.E4.BA.BA.E5.8F.A3.E8.B6.85.E8.BF.87300.E4.B8.87-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some ethnic Tibetans claim that, with the 2006 completion of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qingzang_Railway" title="Qingzang Railway">
       Qingzang Railway
      </a>
      connecting the TAR to Qinghai Province, there has been an "acceleration" of Han migration into the region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Central Tibetan Administration of the Dalai Lama claims that the PRC has actively swamped Tibet with migrants in order to alter Tibet's demographic makeup.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fifth_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fifth-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Towns_and_villages_in_Tibet">

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Towns and villages in Tibet">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_towns_and_villages_in_the_Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="List of towns and villages in the Tibet Autonomous Region">

     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Comfortable_Housing.22">
       "Comfortable Housing"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: "Comfortable Housing"'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Beginning in 2006, 280,000 Tibetans who lived in traditional villages and as nomadic herdsmen have been forcefully relocated into villages and towns. In those areas new housing was built and existing houses were remodelled to serve a total of 2 million people. Those living in substandard housing were required to dismantle their houses and remodel them to government standards. Much of the expense was borne by the residents themselves often through bank loans. The
      <a href="/wiki/Population_transfer" title="Population transfer">
       population transfer
      </a>
      program, which was first implemented in
      <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">
       Qinghai
      </a>
      where 300,000 nomads were resettled, is called "Comfortable Housing". which is part of the Build a New Socialist Countryside program. Its effect on Tibetan culture has been criticized by exiles and human rights groups.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRW613_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HRW613-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finding employment is difficult for relocated persons who have only agrarian skills. Income shortfalls are made up for by government support programs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT62713_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT62713-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was announced in 2011 that 20,000 Communist Party cadre were to be placed in the new towns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRW613_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HRW613-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">
       Economy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Tibet" title="Economy of Tibet">
       Economy of Tibet
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="593" data-file-width="785" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg/220px-Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg/330px-Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg/440px-Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yaks_still_provide_the_best_way_to_plow_fields_in_Tibet.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Tibetan farmer ploughing a field with
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaks" title="Yaks">
         yaks
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Tibetans traditionally depended upon agriculture for survival. Since the 1980s, however, other jobs such as taxi-driving and hotel retail work have become available in the wake of
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_economic_reform" title="Chinese economic reform">
       Chinese economic reform
      </a>
      . In 2011, Tibet's nominal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">
       GDP
      </a>
      topped 60.5 billion yuan (US$9.60 billion), nearly more than seven times as big as the 11.78 billion yuan (US$1.47 billion) in 2000. In the past five years, Tibet's annual GDP growth has averaged 12%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thechinaperspective.com_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-thechinaperspective.com-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While traditional agriculture and animal husbandry continue to lead the area's economy, in 2005 the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector" title="Tertiary sector">
       tertiary sector
      </a>
      contributed more than half of its GDP growth, the first time it surpassed the area's primary industry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rich reserves of natural resources and raw materials have yet to lead to the creation of a strong secondary sector, due in large part to the province's inhospitable terrain, low population density, an underdeveloped infrastructure and the high cost of extraction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thechinaperspective.com_18-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-thechinaperspective.com-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The collection of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cordyceps_sinensis" title="Cordyceps sinensis">
       caterpillar fungus
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Cordyceps sinensis
      </i>
      , known in Tibetan as
      <i>
       Yartsa Gunbu
      </i>
      ) in late spring / early summer is in many areas the most important source of cash for rural households. It contributes an average of 40% to rural cash income and 8.5% to the TAR's GDP.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The re-opening of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nathu_La" title="Nathu La">
       Nathu La
      </a>
      pass (on southern Tibet's border with India) should facilitate Sino-Indian border trade and boost Tibet's economy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, Chinese news media reported that the
      <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">
       per capita
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disposable_income" title="Disposable income">
       disposable incomes
      </a>
      of urban and rural residents in Tibet averaged 12,482 yuan (US$1,798) and 3,176 yuan (US$457) respectively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/China_Western_Development" title="China Western Development">
       China Western Development
      </a>
      policy was adopted in 2000 by the central government to boost economic development in western China, including the Tibet Autonomous Region.
     </p>

      <li>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Potala_Palace,_August_2009.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Potala_Palace%2C_August_2009.jpg/250px-Potala_Palace%2C_August_2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Potala_Palace%2C_August_2009.jpg/375px-Potala_Palace%2C_August_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Potala_Palace%2C_August_2009.jpg/500px-Potala_Palace%2C_August_2009.jpg 2x" width="250"/>

         Potala Palace

     <p>
      Tourists were first permitted to visit the Tibet Autonomous Region in the 1980s. While the main attraction is the

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Namtso" title="Namtso">
       Namtso Lake

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tashilhunpo" title="Tashilhunpo">
       Tashilhunpo Monastery
      </a>
      .

     <div class="thumb tright">

       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lhasa_airport.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2136" data-file-width="3216" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Lhasa_airport.jpg/250px-Lhasa_airport.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Lhasa_airport.jpg/375px-Lhasa_airport.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Lhasa_airport.jpg/500px-Lhasa_airport.jpg 2x" width="250"/>

     <p>
      The civil airports in Tibet are
      <a href="/wiki/Lhasa_Gonggar_Airport" title="Lhasa Gonggar Airport">
       Lhasa Gonggar Airport

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">
       Railway
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibet_Autonomous_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Railway">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Qinghai%E2%80%93Tibet_Railway" title="QinghaiTibet Railway">
       QinghaiTibet Railway
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Golmud" title="Golmud">
       Golmud
      </a>
      to Lhasa was completed on 12 October 2005. It opened to regular trial service on 1 July 2006. Five pairs of passenger trains run between Golmud and Lhasa, with connections onward to Beijing, Chengdu, Chongqing, Guangzhou, Shanghai, Xining and Lanzhou. The line includes the
      <a href="/wiki/Tanggula_Pass" title="Tanggula Pass">
       Tanggula Pass
      </a>
      , which, at 5,072 m (16,640ft) above sea level, is the world's highest railway.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Lhasa%E2%80%93Xigaz%C3%AA_Railway" title="LhasaXigaz Railway">
       LhasaXigaz Railway
      </a>
      branch from Lhasa to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xigaz%C3%AA" title="Xigaz">
       Xigaz
      </a>
      was completed in 2014. It opened to regular service on 15 August 2014.
     </p>
     <p>
      The construction of first section of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sichuan%E2%80%93Tibet_Railway" title="SichuanTibet Railway">
       SichuanTibet Railway
      </a>
      from Lhasa to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyingchi_County" title="Nyingchi County">
       Nyingchi
      </a>
      . Construction work is expected to start in November 2014, and to take 7 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
