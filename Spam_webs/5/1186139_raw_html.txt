     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-75860 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="75860" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="article-inline-half">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half/public/blogs/69863/2011/10/75860-66760.jpg?itok=fBTIOvjI" title=""/>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            There are so many promises out there that
            <em>
             this
            </em>
            path, or
            <em>
             that
            </em>
            practice will allow you to live in a perpetual state of bliss, achieving all of your
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/motivation" title="Psychology Today looks at goals">
             goals
            </a>
            , becoming rich and thin in the process. And who doesn't want that?
           </p>
           <p>
            Over the years, I've become fascinated with the ways in which we try to feel endlessly good. We've gotten progressively more skillful in our methods: turning away from
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/psychopharmacology" title="Psychology Today looks at drugs">
             drugs
            </a>
            or
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/alcohol" title="Psychology Today looks at alcohol ">
             alcohol
            </a>
            to alter our consciousness and turning towards things like
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/self-help" title="Psychology Today looks at self-help">
             self-help
            </a>
            books,
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/meditation" title="Psychology Today looks at meditation">
             meditation
            </a>
            , yoga, prayer, and special diets. In some ways, we are now spiritually distracting ourselves from our feelings, thinking that we are walking a healthy spiritual path.
           </p>
           <p>
            This experience is called
            <em>
             spiritual bypass
            </em>
            . Spiritual bypass is a defense mechanism. Although the defense looks a lot prettier than other defenses, it serves the same purpose. Spiritual bypass shields us from the truth, it disconnects us from our feelings, and helps us avoid the big picture. It is more about checking
            <em>
             out
            </em>
            than checking
            <em>
             in
            </em>
            and the difference is so subtle that we usually don't even know we are doing it.
           </p>
           <p>
            The shorthand for spiritual bypass is grasping rather than
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/gratitude" title="Psychology Today looks at gratitude">
             gratitude
            </a>
            , arriving rather than being, avoiding rather than accepting. It is spiritual practice in the service of repression, usually because we can not tolerate what we are feeling, or think that we shouldn't be experiencing what we are feeling.
           </p>
           <p>
            There is a shadow side to almost every positive thing we can do for ourselves, including spiritual practice. All spiritual and psychological tools can be used in a "willful" way. For example, sometimes self-care is actually about taking care of ourselves: unplugging from too much work and plugging in to more balance and harmony. But sometimes, under the guise of self-care, we are really just checking out: denying what's happening and how scary it feels to show up for life.
           </p>
           <p>
            At this point, people usually ask me about a particular spiritual practice, hoping that their belief or experience with
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/spirituality" title="Psychology Today looks at spirituality">
             spirituality
            </a>
            does not fall prey to spiritual bypass. Or they tell me that when done properly, their spiritual practice is about connection, greater consciousness, and acceptance. To these people, I reply that spiritual bypass is an equal opportunity defense mechanism. It is more related to what we as human beings do with spiritual practice than it is related to the practice itself.
           </p>
           <p>
            Every tool for spiritual and psychological development has a purpose, and conversely, a place where it's of no help whatsoever. Nothing is a panacea. We know that vitamins will not cure
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/loneliness" title="Psychology Today looks at loneliness">
             loneliness
            </a>
            , but in other less obvious ways we think that one pursuit will give us what we want in every area of life. We think that these practices should afford us freedom from the messiness of life, as though perfection is an attainable standard. We especially feel this way about spirituality. After all, isn't spiritual practice about accessing higher realms of consciousness and transcendence?
           </p>
           <p>
            If you have found a way to transcend the human condition, my hat is off to you. Truly. But for the rest of you who continue to wrestle with emotional growing pains, I am spreading the news about spiritual bypass as a reminder that we are not supposed to rise above it all. We can't out-run our own feet. We can't out-think our own brains. We can't override this human operating system that we live and breath in every hour of every day, freeing ourselves of pain and problems. Not perpetually anyway.
           </p>
           <p>
            We need to remember that spiritual practice and emotional growth are not about achieving a particular quality of feeling ("good"). Being a human being on a spiritual journey isn't about getting cash and prizes all the time, it is about being in the present moment, whatever it happens to look like. What are you experiencing right now? And how about now? Can you be present to all of your feelings without any one of them defining you?
           </p>
           <p>
            There is something very necessary about being who and where you are. I understand that this is a tall order. If I become present to who I am,
            <em>
             all of me
            </em>
            , there is a lot there that I usually don't want to see. For most people this consists of
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/embarrassment" title="Psychology Today looks at shame">
             shame
            </a>
            , anxiety,
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/anger" title="Psychology Today looks at anger">
             anger
            </a>
            , loneliness, self-loathing, our "dark" side, and the list goes on. Come on, who really wants to be present to all of that? But the more that I have tried to rise above it, or turn my back to itthe more it has lingered there, waiting, almost growing in size. So finally, I had to turn around and face it. And the most amazing thing happened (and continues to happen). It didn't swallow me whole like I thought it would. In fact, by recognizing the "dark" stuff that was there, I could finally experience and own what was "light." I could really believe the good stuff once I took responsibility for the stuff that didn't look quite as shiny on the outside. These are the real fruits of spiritual and psychological development. We stop running from ourselves, and start loving ourselves.
           </p>
           <p>
            Can you be a spiritual person and have a bad day? I think the answer is, yes. I would
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/relationships" title="Psychology Today looks at love">
             love
            </a>
            to hear your personal stories of how spiritual bypass has infiltrated your thinking about spirituality. Let's take the shame out of our unrealistic expectations by sharing our experiences. Let's remind others that spirituality can help us rest in the human condition. And let's be kind to one another as we navigate the inherent challenges of being mind, body, and spirit.

                 <p>
                  Thank you for your comment!  Generally speaking, I would agree with you - that when we look at spiritual bypass from a bird's eye view - it is an immature view of spirituality. However, I want to highlight that as a defense mechanism, spiritual bypass is not consciously intended or understood as a defense as it is happening. It is a coping strategy that can embed itself in even the most sophisticated and enlightened person's belief or practice. In fact, spiritual teachers such as Jack Kornfield, John Welwood, and Ram Dass have written that the most sophisticated and mature spiritual practitioners are often the most likely to experience bypass.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  One of the things I write about in my book is that there is no finish line in spiritual or psychological development. This means that we will always be prone to defense mechanisms, no matter how mature we become. As we are constantly growing and changing - there are new feelings and experiences on the path that we are not yet equipped to handle. In some ways, spiritual bypass can be a healthy (adaptive) defense that protects us along the journey, keeping us sheltered from feelings we are not yet ready to face, until we are ready to face them. Believing that one is mature enough to avoid defenses could be seen as a "bypass" in and of itself. We are never free from the subtle ways in which we defend ourselves from being alive. I confess that even after conducting my research, writing the book, and being an expert on the topic - I still fall prey to bypass. I think that the goal is to have compassion for ourselves in the perpetually messy experience of being human and to remain open enough to "catch" ourselves in our defenses (so that we don't stay there permanentlya maladaptive experience of the defense). In other words, maturity in this context is about knowing that we aren't going to be perfect in our spiritual practice and accepting our imperfections.
                 </p>

                  I find myself being in both agreement with you and the author about spiritual bypassing. How can I do that? I think that there is an appropriate time and place to lean HARD on spirituality. Just as there is an appropriate time and place to lean HARD on things like cognitive behavior therapy.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Defense mechanisms can help us to stabilize self-destructive behavior when nothing else can. When we are in extreme pain, or extreme self-destructive cycles, we can't address what's causing our hurt and behaviors, and we can't get any perspective on what's happening to us.                  </p>
                 <p>

                 </p>

If we try to get away from dealing with the darkness we carry using spirituality long-term, we will find that it just doesn't work. We will get bent out of shape one way or another.

             <h3 class="comment__title">
              <a class="permalink" href="/comment/625785#comment-625785" rel="bookmark">
               Find the right spiritual process..
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="submitted subtext">
              Submitted by Anonymous on July 19, 2014 - 3:22pm
             </div>
             <div class="content">
              <div class="field field-name-comment-body field-type-text-long field-label-hidden">
               <div class="field__item">
                <p>
                 It's important to understand that if you are involved in a spiritual process that is shielding you from the truth, disconnecting you from your feelings, and helping you to avoid the big picture then you have not found the right process.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The main problem today is that "everyone" has become an authority on spirituality yet very few of these people have attained self-realization/enlightenment. Only those individuals who have attained self-realization and are in a state of complete union with the reality of life are truly an authority on such matters, PERIOD. Everyone else is just regurgitating something they've read or heard from someone else, through books or education, there is rarely a situation where the knowledge is derived through personal spiritual experience (albeit not always the case).
                </p>
                <p>
                 The spiritual process is not a psychological process, nor is it an intellectual one. The spiritual process is an energetic / physiological change that occurs within the human system by doing specific processes derived in their authentic form originally from India. Once the consciousness is raised over time through these processes...physical, psychological and emotional imbalances eventually dissipate and balance out completely and naturally.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Your statement here: "We need to remember that spiritual practice and emotional growth are not about achieving a particular quality of feeling ("good")." is absolutely false. Everyone is wanting to feel good, that is the whole point of the spiritual process, to find a state of inner peace, bliss and tranquility. The entire planet is wanting to feel good, this is why people continue to choose alcohol, drugs, sex and other means...unfortunately these external methods to feeling good are fleeting, temporal and often harmful. But there are other ways to create blissfulness and balance in the system.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The main difficulty in raising consciousness in society today is that the majority of people do not have the necessary stamina, attention-span and discipline to actually stay involved in an authentic process long enough to bring about any substantial change within themselves. There is also the challenge of finding someone reputable that can impart these processes BUT they are available.
                </p>
                <p>
                 I do appreciate the subject of this article and feel the intention here is good and do agree that there are many people creating a facade of spirituality and not dealing with their issues. I just feel it's important to offer some perspective and create a little more understanding on what spirituality is and isn't.
                </p>
               </div>

             <h3 class="comment__title">
              <a class="permalink" href="/comment/726613#comment-726613" rel="bookmark">
               Something missing for me here ...
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="submitted subtext">
              Submitted by Keolalani on January 5, 2015 - 3:11am
             </div>
             <div class="content">
              <div class="field field-name-comment-body field-type-text-long field-label-hidden">
               <div class="field__item">
                <p>
                 I sincerely appreciate the discussion and its insights, of course. Still, something seems missing.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Do understand the notion of "spiritual bypass" as engaging a spiritual practice in "an attempt to flee unhappiness."
                </p>
                <p>
                 What seems missing from the conversation for me (and yes, I've not read everything here yet) is recognition and affirmation of "being human" as an innate "process" toward a more aware and healthier state of being - versus what is stated at the top here as a "perpetually messy experience of being human."
                </p>
                <p>
                 I may be incorrect in my interpretation, yet a "perpetually messy experience of being human" seems "static" as it fails to affirm that what drives us to reach for a higher state of being - indeed, to improve humanity-at-large if we're ever to survive here - derives from an inner place of knowing "there IS something more to who we are than this 'perpetual experience of what we have conceived 'being human' is about!"
                </p>
                <p>
                 I believe there is a difference between going off in la-la land via any spiritual leader, practice, etc. and engaging as much as possible on a moment-to-moment basis with that larger sense of who we are beyond the myopia of what we think the "human dilemma" is supposed to inevitably be.  The former can be an escape, to be sure.  The latter, however, seems to acknowledge that we can be and "are" vastly more than we think we are - indeed, that the "human dilemma in all of its messiness" might just rightly be a whisper in time akin to the "Matrix" via which "we" collectively engineered an experience of "having forgotten who we are" for a time, replete w/all its dramas.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Why?  Perhaps for the experience of re-membering who we are!  And indeed, if that reality were not already "in is" in some respects, how could we be reaching for something greater than the perpetual messy "pettiness"?
                </p>
                <p>
                 In closing, if there's not something greater to who we are ... indeed, a place of being and creating that is NOT inundated w/perpetual messiness ... then it would seem that we will inevitably "kiss this Earth and humanity good-bye."  For the stark reality, I think, is that we either begin to wake up to a "higher" (to use the term) level of conscious and being w/one another ... or we will literally be "toast."
                </p>
               </div>
