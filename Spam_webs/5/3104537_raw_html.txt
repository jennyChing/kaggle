Now he\u2019s envisioning how it will mature in the coming decades.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Ch2\u003EIn his own words:\u003C\/h2\u003E\u003Cp\u003E\u0022When we turned on the Internet in 1983, having a powerful computer in your pocket was science fiction. Those devices\u2014called the \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.popsci.com\/tags\/internet-things\u0022\u003EInternet of Things\u003C\/a\u003E\u2014now need standard protocols that allow connected products from different companies to work as an ensemble.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003ESoon cars, buildings, cities, and people will have sensors and software that track resources, respond to crime, or take constant vital signs.               Who's Responsible When A Self-Driving Car Crashes?
             </h1>
             <div class="field-subtitle">
              <p>
               Legal expert Bryant Walker Smith talks shop
              </p>
             </div>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">
              <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
              <span class="author">

            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/bryant_walker_smith_web.jpg?itok=gis9zPD6" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/bryant_walker_smith_web.jpg?itok=Wcothpbj" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/bryant_walker_smith_web.jpg?itok=z_kMJaUt" height="678" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="407" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/bryant_walker_smith_web.jpg?itok=Wcothpbj" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Bryant Walker Smith
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                Aaron Greene
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-body">
               <p>
                Smith is the foremost expert on legal issues related to emerging technologies, such as self-driving cars.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              For the first time, in March, an autonomous car drove itself from San Francisco to New York City. How is that even legal? Thats the kind of question Bryant Walker Smith, an assistant professor of law at the University of South Carolina, tries to answer. Hes an expert on the legal implications of self-driving cars. Smith sees a future in which these vehicles all but eliminate crashes, transform liability, and make us look back in horror at the risks we once took on the road.
             </p>
             <h3>
              In his own words:
             </h3>
             <p>
              People ask me, Are you concerned about self-driving cars? And I say, Yes, but Im terrified of todays cars. Thirty thousand people die every year on U.S. roads, and over a million are injured. Ninety-four percent of those crashes are caused by
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/people-keep-crashing-googles-self-driving-cars">
               human error
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              With self-driving cars, decision-making will no longer exist in real time. Its going to shift from a driver sitting behind the wheel, reacting impulsively, to teams of designers sitting behind computers, deliberately anticipating situations the vehicle may need to deal with. A lot of the liability will probably transfer to companies--the manufacturers, designers, and suppliers of the various driving systems. Theyll likely be responsible for a greater share of the (hopefully much smaller) pie of total crash costs.
             </p>
             <aside class="pullquote">
              With automation, people will quickly zone out, so we have to decide what level of human engagement we can legally require.
             </aside>
             <p>
              Cars will learn from experience too--theyll share enormous amounts of data with one another, getting collectively smarter over time. As with cellphones and the Internet, people will accept the technologies benefits for some perceived loss in privacy. Far in the future, youll get into a truly self-driving car and say, I want to go to a good restaurant, and you may not know, or care, where itll take you. Well still be vulnerable, just in a different way. A hacker could infect an entire model of car or corrupt a map or interfere with real-time vehicle communications in traffic.
             </p>
             <p>
              The question now is, how much of that risk are we willing to take in order to advance a technology that could potentially save a lot of lives?
             </p>
             <aside class="pullquote">
              40 percent of car-crash deaths result from drunk or distracted driving--two things an autonomous car could never do.
             </aside>
             <p>
              <em>
               Source: National Highway Traffic Safety Administration
              </em>
             </p>

               under the title, "Bryant Walker Smith On The Future Of Self-Driving Cars."
              </em>
             </p>
