          <div class="single-right">
           <article class="post-24981 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-space tag-extraterrestrial-life tag-mars tag-meteorite tag-nakhla tag-nakhlite" id="post-24981">
            <div class="entry-content">
             <p>
              <strong>
               A team of scientists from the United Kingdom and Greece led by Dr Elias Chatzitheodoridis of the National Technical University of Athens has found a mysterious oval structure embedded deep within a famous Martian meteorite called Nakhla.
              </strong>
             </p>
             <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_24990" style="width: 590px">
              <a href="http://cdn4.sci-news.com/images/enlarge/image_2153_1e-Nakhla-meteorite.jpg">
               <img alt="Two fragments of the Nakhla meteorite. Image credit: NASA." class="size-full wp-image-24990 " height="454" src="http://cdn4.sci-news.com/images/2014/09/image_2153_1-Nakhla-meteorite.jpg" width="580"/>
              </a>
              <p class="wp-caption-text">
               Two fragments of the Nakhla meteorite. Image credit: NASA.
              </p>
             </div>
             <p>
              Multiple fragments of the
              <a href="http://www2.jpl.nasa.gov/snc/nakhla.html" target="_blank">
               Nakhla meteorite
              </a>
              fell to Earth on June 28, 1911 near the village of El-Nakhla in Egypt. Its crystallization age has been determined to be 1.38 billion years.
             </p>
             <p>
              About 11million years ago, an impact event ejected this rock from the Martian surface, after which it traveled through space andcrashed into our planet in 1911.
             </p>
             <p>
              While investigating a polished thin section prepared from the Nakhla sample # BM1911, 369, Dr Chatzitheodoridis and his colleagues found an unusual cell-like oval structure with a concentric wall.
             </p>
             <p>
              In many ways it resembled a fossilized biological cell from Earth but it was intriguing because it was undoubtedly from Mars, said Prof Ian Lyon of the University of Manchester, a team member and the senior author of the
              <a href="http://online.liebertpub.com/doi/abs/10.1089/ast.2013.1069" target="_blank">
               paper published in the journal
               <em>
                Astrobiology
               </em>
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_24992" style="width: 590px">
              <a href="http://cdn4.sci-news.com/images/enlarge/image_2153_2e-Nakhla-meteorite.jpg">
               <img alt="Transmitted light photomicrographs of the cell-like structure in Nakhla. Left: overview photomicrograph; the oval structure is situated inside a small late-forming pocket of mesostasis materials that is interstitial to two clinopyroxene crystals (Cpx); in detail, the oval exhibits a sharp contact with the adjacent amorphous mesostasis phase (M), which is identified as rhyolitic glass; the opaque phases are Ti magnetite (Mt) and rhoenite (Rho). Right: close-up photomicrographs; black arrows indicate conspicuous, orange-stained microchannel features in clinopyroxene rims; white arrows indicate fractures in the mesostasis M that are stained with iron oxide/hydroxide material (orange to yellow hues) and that extend up to the oval structure. Image credit: Chatzitheodoridis Elias et al." class="size-full wp-image-24992 " height="357" src="http://cdn4.sci-news.com/images/2014/09/image_2153_2-Nakhla-meteorite.jpg" width="580"/>
              </a>
              <p class="wp-caption-text">
               Transmitted light photomicrographs of the cell-like structure in Nakhla. Left: overview photomicrograph; the oval structure is situated inside a small late-forming pocket of mesostasis materials that is interstitial to two clinopyroxene crystals (Cpx); in detail, the oval exhibits a sharp contact with the adjacent amorphous mesostasis phase (M), which is identified as rhyolitic glass; the opaque phases are Ti magnetite (Mt) and rhoenite (Rho). Right: close-up photomicrographs; black arrows indicate conspicuous, orange-stained microchannel features in clinopyroxene rims; white arrows indicate fractures in the mesostasis M that are stained with iron oxide/hydroxide material (orange to yellow hues) and that extend up to the oval structure. Image credit: Chatzitheodoridis Elias et al.
              </p>
             </div>
             <p>
              To determine its nature, the scientists carried out a series of chemical and structural investigations at the micrometer and nanometer scales, employing a wide range of imaging and analytical techniques.
             </p>
             <p>
              There is strong textural and chemical evidence that the ovoid structure is indigenous to Nakhla and originated on Mars, the scientists wrote in the paper.
             </p>
             <p>
              The petrographic context of this highly elliptical ovoid structure within late-forming mesostasis glass, coupled with its relatively large size, distinct chemical composition, and complex microtextures, supports this idea and effectively rules out the possibility of an origin by contamination.
             </p>
             <p>
              Prof Lyon said: our research found that it probably wasnt a cell but that it did once hold water  water that had been heated, probably as a result of an asteroid impact.
             </p>
             <p>
              The finding is significant because it adds to increasing evidence that beneath the surface, the Red Planet does provide all the conditions for life to have formed and evolved.
             </p>
             <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_24997" style="width: 590px">
              <a href="http://cdn4.sci-news.com/images/enlarge/image_2153_3e-Nakhla-meteorite.jpg">
               <img alt="SEM images of the surface of a polished thin section of Nakhla, highlighting the petrographic context of the cell-like structure. Top: overview image. Bottom: close-up image, highlighting the structure and some of the surrounding phases. Abbreviations: Ap - apatite; Cpx - clinopyroxene, separated from the Al-rich Cpx with a dotted line; M - amorphous mesostasis phase (rhyolitic glass); Ol - olivine; Rho - rhoenite; S  sulfide. Image credit: Chatzitheodoridis Elias et al." class="size-full wp-image-24997 " height="798" src="http://cdn4.sci-news.com/images/2014/09/image_2153_3-Nakhla-meteorite.jpg" width="580"/>
              </a>
              <p class="wp-caption-text">
               SEM images of the surface of a polished thin section of Nakhla, highlighting the petrographic context of the cell-like structure. Top: overview image. Bottom: close-up image, highlighting the structure and some of the surrounding phases. Abbreviations: Ap  apatite; Cpx  clinopyroxene, separated from the Al-rich Cpx with a dotted line; M  amorphous mesostasis phase (rhyolitic glass); Ol  olivine; Rho  rhoenite; S  sulfide. Image credit: Chatzitheodoridis Elias et al.
              </p>
             </div>
             <p>
              It also adds to a body of evidence suggesting that large asteroids hit Mars in the past and produce long-lasting hydrothermal fields that could sustain life on the planet, even in later epochs, if life ever emerged there.
             </p>
             <p>
              We have been able to show the setting is there to provide life, Prof Lyon said.
             </p>
             <p>
              Its not too cold, its not too harsh. Life as we know it, in the form of bacteria, for example, could be there, although we havent found it yet. Its about piecing together the case for life on Mars  it may have existed and in some form could exist still.
             </p>
             <p>
              Now the scientists are using state-of-the-art techniques to investigate secondary materials in Nakhla and search for possible bio signatures which provide scientific evidence of life, past or present.
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #808080;">

              </span>

             </p>
