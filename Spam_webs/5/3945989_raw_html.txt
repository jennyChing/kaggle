       <h1>
        Midnight Eggsperiments: How to get perfectly boiled eggs
       </h1>
      </div>
      <div class="post-img">
       <a href="http://teafortammi.com/2015/05/how-to-get-perfectly-boiled-eggs/">
        <img alt="Perfect timings for boiled eggs!" class="attachment-full-thumb wp-post-image" height="720" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8207-1080x720.jpg" width="1080"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="post-entry">
       <p>
        The perfect boiled egg is often shrouded in mystery and conflicting advice  old eggs, fresh eggs, room temperature, fridge temperature, boiling water, residual heatso many variables, so much CONFUSION! And what makes it worse, is that eggs are covered in a hard opaque exterior, the shell, that is, preventing us from truly understanding whats going on
        <em>
         inside
        </em>
        the damn things.
       </p>
       <p>
        Well never fear! Because me  and my apparent insomnia  is here to help! Weve done the experiments, so you dont have to. Heres how to get perfectly boiled eggs.
        <br/>
        <span id="more-275">
        </span>
       </p>
       <h3>
        Method and First Notes
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8208.jpg">
         <img alt="How to get perfectly boiled eggs - eggs in the kettle" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-282" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8208.jpg" width="100%"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Ive done many
        <strong>
         MANY
        </strong>
        more experiments than this, but because of a recent marathon of Good Eats, Ive decided to also try
        <strong>
         Alton Browns kettle method
        </strong>
        . This is where you place 4 eggs in your electric kettle, cover with about an inch of water, and put the kettle on to boil. Once the water reaches boiling point, it will shut itself off, leaving your eggs to cook only in residual heat.
       </p>
       <p>
        For the purposes of this experiment, Ive used
        <strong>
         fresh, free-range eggs from the fridge
        </strong>
        , because lets face it: most people keep their eggs in the fridge, and to bring them to room temperature in the morning before breakfast, on top of all the cooking techniques, is just too much work.
       </p>
       <p>
        The most important thing to note at this point, is that the vigorous boiling of the water actually caused the eggs to bounce around violently
        <em>
         inside
        </em>
        the kettle, resulting in cracks, and white bits floating around in the water.
        <em>
         Sexy
        </em>
        . I know that this isnt because of the shock of cold eggs hitting hot water, because the eggs were first covered in cold tap water before it was brought to the boil.
       </p>
       <p>
        Once the kettle clicks off, I set my timer for 5, 8, 10, and 15 minutes. Once the time has passed, I took the egg out with a pair of tongs, and ran it under cold tap water to stop it from cooking any further. I set this aside and label it, and once all the eggs are done, I then peel them all, and cut them, to prevent the eggs from prematurely drying out.
       </p>
       <h3>
        5 minutes
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8206.jpg">
         <img alt="How to get perfectly boiled eggs - 5min" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-281" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8206.jpg" width="100%"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        This produced a
        <strong>
         fairly soft boiled yolk, and tender whites
        </strong>
        . As you can see, the yolk started to firm up around the edges, but you still get your #yolkporn if you so desired. The egg was fairly difficult to peel, mostly because it was so soft in the middle. The egg threatened to split in two the moment I tapped it on the counter to crack the shell, and there was some sticking of the membrane, resulting in small chunks of delicious egg getting torn off in the peeling process.
       </p>
       <h3>
        8 minutes
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8205.jpg">
         <img alt="How to get perfectly boiled eggs - 8min" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-280" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8205.jpg" width="100%"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        The 8 minute mark is where I would start calling an egg hard boiled. The egg is cooked enough for
        <strong>
         the yolk to be completely set, but with a texture that is slightly more creamy than powdery
        </strong>
        still. Personally, this is where I would stop if I wanted hard boiled eggs say, for a potato salad or a Caesar salad, but only because powdery egg yolks bring back bad memories for me.
       </p>
       <h3>
        10 minutes
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8204.jpg">
         <img alt="How to get perfectly boiled eggs - 15min" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-279" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8204.jpg" width="100%"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        This egg is what most people would imagine the typical hard-boiled egg to be.
        <strong>
         White and yolk is firmly set, allowing you to get a clean cut with a knife
        </strong>
        . This is where Alton tells you to stop if you want a hard boiled egg, using this method.
       </p>
       <h3>
        15 minutes
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8203.jpg">
         <img alt="How to get perfectly boiled eggs - 15 min" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-278" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8203.jpg" width="100%"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        So what if you left it another 5 minutes? Well since the direct heat has been turned off, it takes longer to overcook an egg than you would think. The yolks are definitely c
        <strong>
         ooked all the way through
        </strong>
        , no two ways about it, but its
        <strong>
         still lacking the telltale grey ring around the yolk that is a sure sign of an overcooked hard boiled egg
        </strong>
        . Not bad.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Bonus Easter Egg! Geddit?
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8202.jpg">
         <img alt="How to get a perfect boiled egg - 8 min, residual heat" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-277" src="http://teafortammi.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/IMG_8202.jpg" width="100%"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Because boiling your eggs in a kettle might not be such a great idea if you also want tea with your breakfast, I thought it might be a good idea to see what happened when you put the egg in
        <strong>
         after
        </strong>
        youve turned off the heat. This means that you can bring a pot of water to boil on the stove, and then lower the egg in carefully and cover. In this case, I left it in the water for 8 minutes, and it
        <strong>
         produced a slightly softer, runnier result  both with the white and the yolk  than the 5 minute egg from before
        </strong>
        .
       </p>
       <h3>
        Final Notes and Conclusion
       </h3>
       <p>
        So how DO you get the perfectly boiled egg? Well Im not a fan of this kettle method at all, since it messed up the inside of my kettle, and prevents me from getting my cup of tea. Instead I use these timings with a cold, 60g (large), free range egg from the fridge, set into rapidly boiling water:
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         5 minutes, with 30 sec residual heat
        </strong>
         soft boiled egg for soldiers to be dunked into.
        <br/>
        <strong>
         6 minutes 30 seconds
        </strong>
         for set whites and liquid yolks, perfect for
        <a href="http://teafortammi.com/2015/04/vegetarian-shiitake-mushroom-ramen-recipe/">
         marinated eggs to top a ramen.
        </a>
        <br/>
        <strong>
         8 minutes 30 seconds
        </strong>
         hard boiled. For salads, and other hard boiled egg needs.
       </p>
       <p>
        Eggs age 7 times faster outside of the fridge than in (apparently!) and Im really not up for bringing my eggs to room temperature before breakfast and tea. Not happening in my house. So I think its perfectly fine to cook the eggs from the fridge. In fact, for set whites and liquid yolks, its essential that the eggs are from the fridge, or youll get slightly runny whites or slightly set yolks. Not what we want.
       </p>
       <p>
        Now there
        <strong>
         have been concerns that the cold eggs will crack the moment they hit the boiling hot water
        </strong>
        , and to that, I say its so
        <strong>
         very important to get free range eggs
        </strong>
        . And no, its not a moral issue. Free range eggs, Ive found, have
        <em>
         thicker shells
        </em>
        , and this will help prevent the cracking and tell-tale signs of escaping white. Also, if your eggs are fresh, the whites are less watery, meaning that you dont get quite as much fallout, even if your eggs do crack.
       </p>
       <p>
        The other reason why it cracks, is also because some people drop the eggs into the pot of water with their fingers. So I mean DROP, literally. The weight of the egg causes enough impact for the shell to crack, and that doesnt help anyone. What I like to do is use a soup spoon  long handles!  or a pair of tongs to lower the eggs into the water
        <strong>
         carefully
        </strong>
        . Set your timer, and bring the water back to the boil quickly, and you have predictably cooked eggs every time.
       </p>
       <p>
        Also, the reason why older eggs are easier to peel  older eggs are higher in alkalinity, and the age also causes the membrane to separate slightly, allowing you to easily peel your eggs. I dont know about you, but Im
        <em>
         not waiting a couple of weeks just to eat my boiled eggs
        </em>
        , so there are a couple of ways to counter this. Food geek legend
        <strong>
         Harold Mcgee
        </strong>
        says to add baking soda to the water to increase the alkalinity. But really, by dropping the eggs into boiling water, the shock of heat helps the membrane separate anyway, making it easy to peel.
       </p>
       <p>
        And finally, whether youre cooking it at a rolling boil or not, I dont really taste a discernible texture difference regarding how rubbery whites can get if theyre overcooked. Unless youre hellbent on overcooking your eggs, I dont think that water at a rolling boil is going to affect it that much, except give you more time in the morning!
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Do you have any egg questions? Id love to help out! Just leave me a comment below.
        </em>
       </p>
