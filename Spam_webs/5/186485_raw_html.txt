        <h1 class="title">
         13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics
        </h1>
        <div class="byline">
         By
         <a class="ovr" href="/writers?name=various">
          Various
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="published">
         October 27, 2014 | 9:12am
        </div>
       </div>
       <b class="article-shares-links">
        <a class="social-stub social-share facebook" data-href="http://www.pastemagazine.com/articles/2014/10/13-terrifying-modern-horror-comics.html" data-shares="818" data-title="13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics" href="javascript:void(0);">

        </a>
        <a class="social-stub social-share twitter" data-href="http://www.pastemagazine.com/articles/2014/10/13-terrifying-modern-horror-comics.html" data-shares="54" data-title="13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics" href="javascript:void(0);">
         Tweet
        </a>
        <a class="social-stub social-share google-plus" data-href="http://www.pastemagazine.com/articles/2014/10/13-terrifying-modern-horror-comics.html" data-shares="3" data-title="13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics" href="javascript:void(0);">
         Share
        </a>
       </b>
       <div id="article-shares-links-mobile">
        <a class="non button facebook" data-href="http://www.pastemagazine.com/articles/2014/10/13-terrifying-modern-horror-comics.html" data-shares="818" data-title="13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics" href="javascript:void(0);">
         <i class="icon-facebook">
          Share
         </i>
        </a>
        <a class="non button twitter" data-href="http://www.pastemagazine.com/articles/2014/10/13-terrifying-modern-horror-comics.html" data-shares="54" data-title="13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics" href="javascript:void(0);">
         <i class="icon-twitter">

         </i>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="main-image">
        <img alt="13 Terrifying Modern Horror Comics" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/GhostsRuins_Main.jpg?635724807918495044"/>
       </div>
       <div class="copy entry">
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Horror comics hit such a spectacular stride during the 40s and 50s, under imprints like EC (
         <i>
          Tales From the Crypt
         </i>
         ) before segueing to Warren (
         <i>
          Creepy, Eerie
         </i>
         ), that one cant help but wonder why the genre has never  risen to those same heights in the ensuing years. The answer is probably Fredric Wertham and the Comics Code that commercially neutered many of these efforts through censorship, but those barriers have been ignored for more than a decade.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Even if there arent quite as many horror comics coming out, comics has produced some of the most introspective, provocative, and horrifying storytelling of any medium in the past few decades. (Could film ever truly capture Ben Templesmiths gorgeously grotesque figures or commercially reproduce the incessant dread of Charles Burns
         <i>
          Black Hole
         </i>
         ?) We thought back to the beginnings of the modern age (mid 80s) to hand pick some of our favorite comics that may have kept us up at into the wee hours of the night. Let us know your favorite horror comics in the comments.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/30Days.jpg">
          <img alt="30Days.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="695" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/30Days-thumb-450x695-125879.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="450"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          1. 30 Days of Night
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer:
         </b>
         Steve Niles
         <br/>
         <b>
          Artist:
         </b>
         Ben Templesmith
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publisher:
         </b>
         IDW
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Every time I read Steve Niles and Ben Templesmiths grisly vampire yarn
         <i>
          30 Days of Night
         </i>
         , I say the same thing over and over: How did no one ever think of this genius story? The setting? Barrow, Alaska  the top of the world. True to the title, a small population experiences 30 days of continual night during the winter. The vampire horror story writes itself. However, this script comes from the hands of narrative stalwart Steve Niles, one of the masters of modern horror comics, and Templesmiths cold, brutal pencils fit perfectly. I still get chills every time the coven of vampires make their slow, vicious descent on the townsfolk, unaware of the true horror thats come for them.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest Scene:
         </b>
         Theres a chilling moment early on in the second issue when chief vamp Niles bluntly states the hopelessness of the townspeople: This is how its meant to be. Humans like bottles waiting for their caps to be popped. All the while, human blood washes away into the inky depths of vampire shadow.
         <i>
          Darren Orf
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/BlackHole.jpg">
          <img alt="BlackHole.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="620" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/BlackHole-thumb-450x620-125883.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="450"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          2. Black Hole
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer &amp; Artist:
         </b>
         Charles Burns
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publishers:
         </b>
         Kitchen Sink Press/Fantagraphics/Pantheon
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Charles Burns masterwork is perhaps the apex of body horror, tracing the spread of an STD that mutates its victims. Theres little overt violence and few conventional scares, but the book worms its way into your mind with Burns starkly beautiful, woodcut-like images of depravity and teenage confusion, remaining with the reader long after the lights turn off. Picture old school publisher ECs horror titles without the narrative neatness or the veneer of humor.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest Scene:
         </b>
         Its hard to isolate smaller moments from a work that focuses on sustained, slow-burn horror, but the second half of the chapter Windowpane comes the closest. Protagonist Keith goes for a walk in the woods seeking refuge, and falls into a natural landscape that distorts into a writhing, squishy fever dream.
         <i>
          Hillary Brown
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/GhostsRuins.jpg">
          <img alt="GhostsRuins.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="395" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/GhostsRuins-thumb-500x395-125903.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          3. Ghosts and Ruins
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer &amp; Artist:
         </b>
         Ben Catmull
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publisher:
         </b>
         Fantagraphics
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         OK, we may be cheating a bit here.
         <i>
          Ghosts and Ruins
         </i>
         doesnt strictly work in panels and word balloons  writer/artist Ben Catmull opts for full-page illustration accompanied by prose  but its content is far and away some of the most creeping, haunting, regal spook strata released by a comics publisher. The book portrays 13 (ha) gorgeously textured, shaded portraits of domiciles where something very, very wrong happened long ago. The plain-spoken descriptions pave a path to the gorgeous visuals where the readers mind fills in the horrifying gaps. Entry The Secluded House features a rectangular farm house rooted in an endless meadow, roiling clouds pregnant with rain high above. The description? All that is known is that any person who ventures closer than this does not come back. This is the definition of less-is-more storytelling, with terrifying results. Catmull also breaks the morose undertones with wickedly funny asides, including a ghost who kicks men in the crotch and watches unfortunate souls clip their toe nails.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest Moment:
         </b>
         This is a consistently excellent work, but The Disgusting Garden delivers a haunting sensorial experience rooted around a home fertilized by a dismembered family. Aside from the smell of blooming flowers, rotting fruit, web grass, broken stems, crushed insects, and mud and the sound of sick mourning doves, this entrys illustrations remain the most inspired and otherworldly out of the entire volume, painting a dense oasis of the macabre.
         <i>
          Sean Edgar
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/OriginalSins.jpg">
          <img alt="OriginalSins.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="700" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/OriginalSins-thumb-450x700-125885.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="450"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          4. Hellblazer
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer:
         </b>
         Jamie Delano, Garth Ennis, Brian Azzarello, Others
         <br/>
         <b>
          Artists:
         </b>
         Various
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publisher:
         </b>
         Vertigo
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Sardonic mage John Constantine has summoned gods, battled demons and conquered cancer. But, for all of its horror and occultist elements,
         <i>
          Hellblazer
         </i>
         found its scariest moments rooted in reality. Even at its most satirical, Constantines exploits always maintained a through-line of realistic subtext. Whether its Warren Ellis grisly descriptions of domestic decay or Brian Azzarello dropping the the smart-ass mage into the American prison system, Constantines most frightening encounters have hewn close enough to that kernel of truth to remind readers that real life can be as terrifying as any succubus or demon spawn.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest Scene:
         </b>
         Constantines niece Gemma is almost lured to her death by ghosts. In one of the most gripping vignettes from the first collection by Jamie Delano,
         <i>
          Original Sins
         </i>
         , the adolescent befriends three girls who invite her home to meet their husband (
         <i>
          red flag
         </i>
         ). She naps with them in bed until a very quiet, very adult man finally arrives home. Delano builds sustained tension, jumping from scenes of Constantines search for the girl to scenes of Gemma kneeling down in a satanic marriage ritual. In the most classic of ghost story tropes, Constantine finally storms the scene to find that Gemmas three friends had been strangled long ago, decomposing in bed before they coaxed other kids into supernatural polygamy. Totally eerie.
         <i>
          Robert Tutton
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/Hellboy_The_Corpse_and_the_Iron_Shoes.jpg">
          <img alt="Hellboy_The_Corpse_and_the_Iron_Shoes.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="693" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/Hellboy_The_Corpse_and_the_Iron_Shoes-thumb-450x693-125887.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="450"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          5. Hellboy
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer and Artist:
         </b>
         Mike Mignola
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publisher:
         </b>
         Dark Horse
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Through
         <i>
          Hellboy
         </i>
         , Mike Mignola explores every inch of gothic heritage, weaving an ornate tapestry of fiction that stretches from Lovecraft to Shelley to Milton and beyond. What other series can meld mechanical gorillas with Rasputin and Baba Yaga? At the center, one very conflicted, heroic demon battles his destiny as the biggest antagonist of all: the biblical Beast of Revelations. Postmodern awesome aside, Mignolas art breaths with majesty and mood, etching a profound sense of history and loss through stray panels of crumbling castles and wayward wildlife.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest Scene:
         </b>
         <i>
          Hellboy
         </i>
         supports a colorful bestiary packed with vicious, mythological creeps, but short story The Corpse takes on a personal, panicked tone. The story features a couple whose baby mysteriously mocks and antagonizes its parents, leading Big Red to discover a scheme by faeries to kidnap human children and replace them with shapeshifters. The set up rings with pain and pathos until Hellboy finally reveals the changeling behind the traumatic mischief. Also:
         <i>
          Jenny Greenteeth
         </i>
         no more needs said. Artist Richard Corbens collaborations on
         <i>
          Hellboy: The Crooked Man
         </i>
         also introduced some Appalachian folky dread that needs to be seen firsthand.
         <i>
          Sean Edgar
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/Ifeelsick1.jpg">
          <img alt="Ifeelsick1.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="687" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/Ifeelsick1-thumb-450x687-125889.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="450"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          6. I Feel Sick
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer &amp; Artist
         </b>
         : Jhonen Vasquez
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publisher:
         </b>
         Slave Labor Graphics
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Technically closer to an absurdist dark comedy than a conventional horror story,
         <i>
          I Feel Sick
         </i>
         chronicles the misadventures of Devi, one of the only supporting cast members to survive
         <i>
          Johnny The Homicidal Maniac
         </i>
         . Devi quits her bookstore job to paint sci-fi novel covers. Unfortunately, the more-lucrative employment situation doesnt pan out smoothly. Devil starts receiving uncontrollable and inexplicable static shocks, and an unfinished painting of a purple-haired doll starts telling her to do bad stuff. Making matters worse, she cant meet a dude without him turning out to be a mass murderer or a chronic pants shitter.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest (or Weirdest) Scene:
         </b>
         While attempting to leave the building after years as a shut-in, the psychic obese woman who lives below Devis apartment gets stuck in the hallway, rendering it unpassable for her fellow tenants. While struggling to squeeze by the fat lady, Devi learns that it is the fat, not the lady herself, that foresees the future.
         <i>
          Barry Thompson
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="1linebreakdiamond.png" class="mt-image-center" height="43" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/blogs/lists/1linebreakdiamond.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="323"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/2014/10/27/JohnnyHomicidal.jpg">
          <img alt="JohnnyHomicidal.jpg" class="mt-image-center" height="698" src="http://cdn.pastemagazine.com/www/articles/assets_c/2014/10/JohnnyHomicidal-thumb-450x698-125891.jpg" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="450"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <big>
          7. Johnny the Homicidal Maniac
         </big>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Writer &amp; Artist
         </b>
         : Jhonen Vasquez
         <br/>
         <b>
          Publisher:
         </b>
         Slave Labor Graphics
         <br/>
        </p>
        <p>
         At its core,
         <i>
          JTHM
         </i>
         is a coming-of-age story in which the titular Johnny (Nny, for short) ultimately curbs his all-encompassing, homicidal fury. The final page of
         <i>
          JTHM: The Directors Cut
         </i>
         collection hints that Nny could grow into a somewhat-adjusted, productive member of the world. That said, he kills many,
         <i>
          many
         </i>
         innocent-ish people in unspeakably gruesome, though inspired, ways before experiencing anything resembling a  epiphany. He also has to die, inadvertently unmake all of existence, and take underwhelming sojourns to both Heaven and Hell. The success of this 7-issue series  required reading for depressed teenagers in the 90s  led to Nickelodeon inviting Vasquez to create the far-less-grisly
         <i>
          Invader Zim
         </i>
         cartoon. Subsequently, Vasquez lost his mind working for corporate childrens programming, but contrary to what
         <i>
          JTHM
         </i>
         might lead one to expect, the creator did not consequently embark on a blood-frenzied rampage.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <b>
          Scariest Scene:
         </b>
         In the conclusion of issue #5, characters Tess R. and Krik escape from Nnys basement of soul-annihilating, Lovecraftian terror and human misery only to discover that the entire universe outside has vanished into nothingness. Then a tentacle monster slices Krik in half.
         <i>
          Barry Thompson
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
