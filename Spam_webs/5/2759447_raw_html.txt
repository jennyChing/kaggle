     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_communication">
       Identity and communication
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jester_(hacktivist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identity and communication">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Jester first appeared on Twitter where he announced his attack on the Taliban website alemarah.info on January 1, 2010. On June 26, 2010 he established his
      <a href="/wiki/WordPress" title="WordPress">
       WordPress
      </a>
      blog "Jester's Court".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Jester also communicates via his
      <a href="/wiki/I2P" title="I2P">
       I2P
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_Relay_Chat" title="Internet Relay Chat">
       IRC
      </a>
      channel #jester and cautions these are the only three authentic methods of communication from him: "As per usual, because of the large amount of imposters trying to pass themselves off as me I will only speak in THREE places, here via this blog, my twitter and the i2p IRC network outlined above where my nick (th3j35t3r) is registered to myself. If you see a jester anywhere else its not me."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Military_service">
       Military service
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jester_(hacktivist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Military service">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Jester had stated that he was a former soldier and had served in
      <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">
       Afghanistan
      </a>
      and elsewhere.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xerxes_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-xerxes-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel1_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-spiegel1-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A former defense operative claimed that The Jester was a former military contractor involved in
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Special_Operations_Command" title="United States Special Operations Command">
       US Special Operations Command
      </a>
      projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes1_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes1-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On April 10, 2012, The Jester gave a live chat interview to a class of Computer Science students at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_Maine" title="University of Southern Maine">
       University of Southern Maine
      </a>
      where he confirmed his military service and stated he served four "operational tours".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland5_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland5-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="XerXeS_and_other_tools">
       XerXeS and other tools
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jester_(hacktivist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: XerXeS and other tools">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Jester claims to have originally developed his
      <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">
       DoS script
      </a>
      as a means to test and
      <a href="/wiki/Hardening_(computing)" title="Hardening (computing)">
       harden
      </a>
      servers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland1_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland1-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After learning from an article that Jihadists were using the Internet to recruit and coordinate terror
      <a href="/wiki/Clandestine_cell_system" title="Clandestine cell system">
       cells
      </a>
      , The Jester resolved to disrupt online communications between Jihadists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland2_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland2-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He weaponized his script and created a front-end known as "XerXeS"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourcecode_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sourcecode-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in order to solve the script's usability problems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland1_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland1-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hacking_history">
       Hacking history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jester_(hacktivist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hacking history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On January 1, 2010, The Jester began a campaign against
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jihadist" title="Jihadist">
       Jihadist
      </a>
      websites. His first target was alemarah.info, which was the
      <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">
       Taliban
      </a>
      's website at the time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-welt1_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-welt1-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On November 28, 2010, The Jester posted several tweets claiming to be responsible for the downtime WikiLeaks was experiencing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailycaller1_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-dailycaller1-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He justified his alleged attacks by claiming that WikiLeaks was "attempting to endanger the lives of our [US] troops, 'other assets' &amp; foreign relations."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xerxes_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-xerxes-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In retaliation to The Jester's reported efforts, hacktivists including a group named
      <a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(group)" title="Anonymous (group)">
       Anonymous
      </a>
      in support of WikiLeaks were reported as
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Payback" title="Operation Payback">
       temporarily disrupting the website of MasterCard
      </a>
      as well as attacking websites of Amazon and PayPal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On November 29, 2010, someone claiming to be The Jester stated that he had been raided by the U.S. and attempted to solicit money for legal fees. The Jester purported that the person was an impostor, though writers at InfoSecIsland believe the hoax was created by The Jester himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On December 28, 2010, a DoS attack targeted
      <a href="/wiki/4chan" title="4chan">
       4chan.org
      </a>
      . On that same day, The Jester tweeted "4chan.org that looks like a TANGO DOWN (not) maybe you guys pissed off the wrong person trying to (wrongly) ID me?"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailycaller1_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-dailycaller1-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This tweet is believed to be a reference to claims by 4chan users that The Jester was a man from
      <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">
       Montana
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dailycaller1_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-dailycaller1-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-praetorianprefect1_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-praetorianprefect1-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On February 21, 2011, The Jester began a DoS attack on several sites belonging to the
      <a href="/wiki/Westboro_Baptist_Church" title="Westboro Baptist Church">
       Westboro Baptist Church
      </a>
      for celebrating the death of homosexual U.S. service men.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In March 2011, The Jester employed a different style of attack by using an
      <a href="/wiki/Cross-site_scripting" title="Cross-site scripting">
       XSS
      </a>
      vulnerability to make it appear as if fabricated articles were inserted online Libyan newspapers
      <i>
       The Malta Independent Online
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       Tripoli Post
      </i>
      . On March 28, 2011 he tweeted links to the forged articles. The articles were not visible in search, or to viewers of those websites, and viewable only via the inserted links. These tweets drew the attention of Anthony M. Freed, who examined the articles and discovered they were anomalies not contained in the newspapers' respective archives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland3_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland3-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Further inspection by Freed revealed The Jester left a watermark of his signature Harlequin avatar on the articles he created, which can only be seen by tilting the computer monitor back at an angle. The fabricated articles reported degradation in troop morale among fighters loyal to
      <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">
       Muammar Gaddafi
      </a>
      and incidents of his soldiers abandoning their posts. Freed concluded The Jester's objective was a "psyops campaign aimed at breaking the spirit of the troops loyal to Libyan strongman Muammar Gaddafi."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland3_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland3-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Jester confirmed this in a subsequent interview later the same year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland4_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland4-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 2011 The Jester vowed to find and expose members of
      <a href="/wiki/LulzSec" title="LulzSec">
       LulzSec
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damon_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-damon-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He attempted to obtain and publish
      <a href="/wiki/Personally_identifiable_information" title="Personally identifiable information">
       personally identifiable information
      </a>
      of key members within group, whom he described as "childish".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enemies_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-enemies-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On June 24, 2011, he claimed to have revealed the identity of LulzSec leader Sabu as Xavier Kaotico, an
      <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_consulting" title="Information technology consulting">
       information technology consultant
      </a>
      possibly from
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In July of the same year he falsely accused Hugo Carvalho, a Portuguese IT professional, of also being Sabu, leaving The Jester's outing claims to be considered suspect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, in a post on his blog in November 2011, The Jester retracted his prior identifications for "Sabu", issued an apology and correctly identified "Sabu" as Hector Xavier Monsegur, 28, of New York.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sabu's identity was confirmed on March 6, 2012 when Monsegur was arrested by the FBI and it was revealed that he had been acting as an FBI informant in the interim.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 2011, at the
      <a href="/wiki/Hacker_Halted#Hacker_Halted_USA" title="Hacker Halted">
       Hackers Halted USA
      </a>
      conference, The Jester gave a surprise live presentation and fielded questions through an online chat with presenter Jeff Bardin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infosecisland4_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-infosecisland4-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His identity was authenticated via his Twitter account. Jester answered questions about XerXeS and other tools in development, and discussed his motivations for attacking militant jihadi recruiting websites. On August 26, 2012, Bardin hosted a similar presentation at
      <a href="/wiki/Utica_College" title="Utica College">
       Utica College
      </a>
      for the college's Cybersecurity Master's program.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Late November 2011, th3j35t3r claimed to take down multiple jihadist sites permanently, with his newest tool known as 'Saladin'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Saladin is claimed to be similar to other 'Apache Killer' tools used by hackers. Critics have claimed Saladin does not exist, and that he is relying on domain expiration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On May 14, 2012 The Jester's Twitter account (@th3j35t3r) appeared to have been deleted, along with all posts on his WordPress blog.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the Twitter account and WordPress blog were merely temporarily deactivated and were subsequently restored May 16, 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">
       Ecuadorean
      </a>
      stock exchange and the country's tourism website, and promised to attack any other governments considering granting asylum to
      <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">
       NSA
      </a>
      leaker
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">
       Edward Snowden
      </a>
      . In a June blog post, he wrote that Snowden "is not a goddam hero, here to save Americans from 'the government' because of privacy infringements and breaches of the 4th amendment, he is a traitor and has jeopardized all our lives." In tweets, the Jester also alluded to a plan to seize control of the fire alarms at the Ecuadorean embassy in
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      , which would force WikiLeaks founder
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_Assange" title="Julian Assange">
       Julian Assange
      </a>
      to set foot on U.K. soil and face potential extradition to
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_SANS_Report:_The_Jester:_A_Lesson_in_Asymmetric_Warfare">
       The SANS Report: The Jester: A Lesson in Asymmetric Warfare
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jester_(hacktivist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The SANS Report: The Jester: A Lesson in Asymmetric Warfare">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In December 2011, TJ O'Connor, a research analyst in the Information Technology and Operations Center (ITOC),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      produced a comprehensive report for the
      <a href="/wiki/SANS_Institute" title="SANS Institute">
       SANS Institute
      </a>
      detailing the history of The Jester's hacking campaigns titled "The Jester Dynamic: A Lesson in Asymmetric Unmanaged Cyber Warfare".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The paper examines the history, motives and impact of two years worth of The Jester's hacking, and provides a detailed analysis of the timeline of his attacks, a speculative analysis of the tools he may use, and review of his use of social media and public relations through his blog.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="QR_code_attack">
       QR code attack
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Jester_(hacktivist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: QR code attack">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On March 5, 2012 The Jester changed his Twitter account @th3j35t3r avatar from his signature Jester icon to a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QR_Code" title="QR Code">
       QR Code
      </a>
      without comment or explanation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      QR Codes are bar codes that can be scanned by mobile phones and are most often used in advertising. Scanning a QR Code redirects a browser to a website.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scanning The Jester's icon led to a URL where he had an image of his signature Jester icon and an embedded, hidden code that allegedly exploited a vulnerability that affects
      <a href="/wiki/Safari_(web_browser)" title="Safari (web browser)">
       Safari
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">
       Chrome
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">
       Android
      </a>
      browsers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "When anyone scanned the original QR code using an
      <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">
       iPhone
      </a>
      or Android device, their device would silently make a
      <a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">
       TCP
      </a>
      shell connection back to my remote server," The Jester wrote. "Like a phone call, if you like."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snd_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-snd-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Apparently, once connected to the Jester's server, the network diagnostic tool
      <a href="/wiki/Netcat" title="Netcat">
       Netcat
      </a>
      checked the connecting mobile device to see if it had Twitter software installed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Twitter account information was then harvested and checked against a list that The Jester allegedly maintains of known Twitter accounts associated with the hacking groups
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anonymous_Operation" title="Anonymous Operation">
       Anonymous
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/LulzSec" title="LulzSec">
       LulzSec
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_AntiSec" title="Operation AntiSec">
       AntiSec
      </a>
      , with Wikileaks, Al Qaeda and Islamic Jihadi recruiting sites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Twitter accounts that did not hit a match to the list were ignored. Possibly, when a Twitter account registered a hit on the list, the account permissions were transferred to The Jester, he elevated his permissions to allow him to exploit the entire mobile device. It is possible that in this manner The Jester was able to obtain access to
      <a href="/wiki/Short_Message_Service" title="Short Message Service">
       SMS
      </a>
      text messages, voicemail, call logs, and email on the phone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snd_44-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-snd-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Jester's QR code ran quietly for five days before a Twitter user going by the moniker @RootDial noticed the embedded code and mentioned it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In those five days, The Jester wrote "Over 1,200 curious netizens scanned the QR code . . . Of those, over 500 devices reverse-shelled back to the listening server. Of those, a significant number were on the '[hit]-list' and as such treated as valid targets."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msncom_42-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-msncom-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On March 12, The Jester posted an encrypted file containing the data harvested from his QR Code.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snd_44-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-snd-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a private communication, SecurityNewsDaily asked The Jester why he'd encrypted the information rather than post it in regular, plain text. "I encrypt my [data] dumps as a matter of course because I am not the same as my detractors who drop personal info all the time," he replied. "The right people have the plain text dump. It would be highly irresponsible of me to be dropping anything in the open."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snd_44-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-snd-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
