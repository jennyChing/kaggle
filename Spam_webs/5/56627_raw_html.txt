         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           After 145 years of featuring elephants in its circus acts, Ringling Bros. and Barnum &amp; Bailey
           <a href="http://www.elephantcenter.com/about-us/media-and-news/">
            announced Thursday
           </a>
           that it will retire its elephant herd by 2018.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           "This decision was not easy, but it is in the best interest of our company, our elephants and our customers," said Kenneth Feld, chairman and CEO of Ringling Bros. and Barnum &amp; Bailey's parent company, Feld Entertainment, in a
           <a href="http://www.elephantcenter.com/about-us/media-and-news/">
            press release
           </a>
           .
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The company said it made the "unprecedented" decision to focus its elephant work on conservation programs. "No other institution has done or is doing more to save this species from extinction," Feld said, "and that is something of which I and my family are extremely proud."
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The circus will send its 13 traveling Asian elephants to the
           <a href="http://www.elephantcenter.com/">
            Ringling Bros. and Barnum &amp; Bailey Center for Elephant Conservation
           </a>
           in central Florida.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           In an
           <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/aponline/2015/03/05/us/ap-us-ringling-bros-circus-elephants.html?partner=rss&amp;emc=rss&amp;_r=0">
            interview with the Associated Press
           </a>
           , a Feld Entertainment executive vice president acknowledged a "mood shift" among the circus's consumers.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           "A lot of people aren't comfortable with us touring with our elephants," said the executive, Alana Feld.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The company's press release said that "the circus will continue to feature other extraordinary animal performers, including tigers, lions, horses, dogs and camels."
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            Intelligentand Endangered
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The Asian elephant (
           <i>
            <a href="http://animals.nationalgeographic.com/animals/mammals/asian-elephant/">
             Elephas maximus
            </a>
           </i>
           ) is the second largest land mammal on Earth, behind its cousin the
           <a href="http://animals.nationalgeographic.com/animals/mammals/african-elephant/">
            African elephant
           </a>
           .
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Intelligent and strong, the Asian elephant is known for being a highly social animal capable of problem solving, grief, and empathy. (Related: "
           <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2014/02/140221-elephants-poaching-empathy-grief-extinction-science/">
            It's Time to Accept That Elephants, Like Us, Are Empathetic Beings
           </a>
           .")
          </p>

            </iframe>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="media__caption ">
            <div class="media__caption--text">
            </div>
           </figcaption>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <i>
            See how elephants in the wild have distinct signals and gestures. Elephant biologist Joyce Poole explains.
           </i>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           There are only
           <a href="http://www.iucnredlist.org/details/7140/0">
            41,000 to 52,000 Asian elephants left in the wild
           </a>
           , according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature estimates, earning the animals an Endangered designation.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Habitat loss, conflict with humans, and the illegal wildlife trade drives the population a little lower each year. (Related:
           <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2014/08/140818-elephants-africa-poaching-cites-census/">
            "100,000 Elephants Killed by Poachers in Just Three Years, Landmark Analysis Finds."
           </a>
           )
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           A
           <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2011/10/ringling-bros-elephant-abuse?page=1">
            2011 investigation
           </a>
           by the magazine
           <i>
            Mother Jones
           </i>
           found that "Ringling elephants spend most of their long lives either in chains or on trains, under constant threat of the bullhook, or ankusthe menacing tool used to control elephants."
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Despite abuse allegations raised by the Humane Society of the United States and other animal welfare groups, Ringling Bros. prevailed in a long legal battle against the groups in 2014, collecting nearly $16 million in a settlement.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.peta.org">
            People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals
           </a>
           on Thursday called for the Ringling Bros. to expedite the elephants' retirement.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           "Three years is too long for a mother elephant separated from her calf, too long for a baby elephant beaten with the sharp fireplace-poker-like weapons called bull hooks that Ringling handlers use routinely, too long for an animal who roams up to 30 miles a day in the wild to be kept in shackles," PETA president Ingrid E. Newkirk said in a statement.
          </p>
         </div>

          <div class="media--small left " style="padding-top: 0px; padding-bottom: 0px;">

            </iframe>
