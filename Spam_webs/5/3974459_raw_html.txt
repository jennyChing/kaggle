         <h1 class="title">
          New Delhi calling to faraway students
         </h1>
         <div class="tabs">
         </div>
        </div>
        <!-- /#content-header -->
        <div id="content-area">
         <div class="node node-type-article" id="node-1269">
          <div class="node-inner">
           <em>
            <a href="/volume-9/number-4">
             Volume 9 Number 4 April 8 - May 12 2013
            </a>
           </em>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <div class="image">
            <div class="image-with-caption" style="width:550px">
             <div class="image">
              <img alt="Camilla Orr-Thomson in the grounds of Fatehpur Sikri, a city outside Agra founded by Mughal emperor Akbar in 1569. Photo: Richard Hamilton" height="413" id="imceimage-field_article_img-" src="/sites/voice.unimelb.edu.au/files/voice/v9n04/Camilla-in-India.jpg" width="550"/>

             <div class="caption" style="font-style:italic">
              Camilla Orr-Thomson in the grounds of Fatehpur Sikri, a city outside Agra founded by Mughal emperor Akbar in 1569. Photo: Richard Hamilton
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <div class="teaser">
            <b>
            </b>
            <p>
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Last month, Masters of Global Media Communication student, and University Media and PR Intern,
             <strong>
              Camilla Orr-Thomson
             </strong>
             , travelled to India with a group of students to participate in the Universitas 21 Advanced International Study at the University of Delhi. She reflects on what she saw there, and the people she met during this international education experience.
            </p>
            <p>
            </p>
           </div>
           <div class="media">
           </div>
           <div class="content">
            <p class="p1">
             In 1965, Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, then Foreign Minister of Pakistan said: If India builds the bomb, we will eat grass or leaves, even go hungry, but we will get one of our own. We have no other choice.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             I wonder if Mr Bhutto knew that almost 50 years later, discussions of national security would be as much concerned about the nuclear bomb as they are about the security threat of people suffering while their government tries to obtain one.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             It was an issue I and other students came back to time and again during an intensive subject in India this February.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             We joined students from Melbourne, the University of Delhi and the University of Birmingham (UK) to undertake the subject Traditional and New Security Challenges: South Asia in Global Perspective, jointly developed by the three universities through Universitas 21.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             As I am completing a degree in Global Media Communication, my interest in diplomacy and security was somewhat different from the other students. While the majority of the group was studying international relations, development or public policy, I wanted to understand security to articulate it in media representations.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             The University of Delhi in itself was worth the visit with its expansive, beautiful gardens and diverse range of historical buildings. The most significant building was perhaps the Vice-Chancellors Office, once the home of the last Viceroy of India, Lord Mountbatten.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Each day, we would meet at the University for two sessions that considered fundamental regional security issues including nuclear proliferation, terrorism, regional governance, and South Asian relations.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Subject co-ordinators Dr Pradeep Taneja (University of Melbourne) and Dr Edward Newman (University of Birmingham) hosted guest lecturers including Uday Bhaskar, a retired Commodore, and one of Indias leading experts on security and strategic affairs. Professor Naunita Behera of Delhi University also played an important role in co-ordinating the program.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             We also were asked to consider new contributions to the field of security theory, which contend that a broader range of internal and external issues should be defined as security threats. These included forced migration, the gendered experience of war and environmental changes.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Where once security experts would have debated the effects of war on the environment, they are also currently debating the effects of environmental changes on the likelihood of war.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             In smaller groups, we were asked to prepare presentations about specific South Asian countries and the security challenges they faced. Our multinational student cohort articulated a range of regional issues including poor regional collaboration, humanitarian crises, competition for natural resources and the ongoing threat of terrorism.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             The intensive also highlighted the need for young people to consider how we can contribute to making the broader region in which we live a less violent place in the future.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             One of my class members from the University of Delhi, Krishnamurari Mukherjee, said that it was important for international students to have such opportunities to study together.
            </p>
            <p class="p1">
             Such collaboration leads to intensive and stimulating academic exchanges and it makes us realise we are not so different, he said.
            </p>
            <p class="p2">
             www.universitas21.com
            </p>
           </div>
          </div>

          <h2 class="title">
           About Voice
          </h2>
          <div class="content">
           <div class="node clear-block" id="node-265">
            <div class="meta">
            </div>
            <div class="content">
             <p>
              Voice is the newspaper of the University of Melbourne. It is published monthly and is distributed on the Universitys campuses, published as a supplement to the Age on the second Monday of each month and is available on line and by free email or hard copy subscription.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Each month Voice explores a major social issue in which University academics and researchers contribute to public debate. Voice also contains research news, informed opinion and analysis, alumni news and profiles, stories on student life and activities, Arts reviews and previews, sport, previews of multimedia programs and listings of Public Lectures and other events.
