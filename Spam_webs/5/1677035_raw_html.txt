      <div id="entrybody">
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/svrdesignco/5614871190/">
         <img alt="Thornton Creek water channel, with Thornton Place &amp; Aljoya developments in background (by: SvR Design)" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2694/5800521880_40e0fb5ac7_d.jpg" title="Thornton Creek water channel, with Thornton Place &amp; Aljoya developments in background (by: SvR Design)"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Leave it to a city famous for coffee and rain to produce possibly the best example of transit-oriented urbanism, natural public space, and green stormwater infrastructure I have ever seen. This Seattle redevelopment is green in so many ways that it is hard to know where to start.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://lafoundation.org/research/landscape-performance-series/case-studies/case-study/137/">
         <img alt="the site before redevelopment (via Landscape Architecture Foundation)" height="213" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3298/5800673310_055373c583_m_d.jpg" title="the site before redevelopment (via Landscape Architecture Foundation)" width="266"/>
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/svrdesignco/3679102672/">
         <img alt="site plan for Thornton Creek restoration (by: SvR Design)" height="213" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5061/5800521420_bd810cabf4_m_d.jpg" title="site plan for Thornton Creek restoration (by: SvR Design)" width="221"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Maybe we should start with the parking lot, because thats what the whole nine-acre site was before redevelopment began. Ugly. Horrible for the environment. A complete waste of urban space.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/mapei/5801102395/">
         <img alt="the site in context (via Google Earth)" height="246" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5181/5801102395_e8b2745d18_d.jpg" title="the site in context (via Google Earth)" width="500"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        The site is in an area that is transitioning from automobile-oriented and suburban in feel to walkable and lively. It sits just south of the Northgate shopping mall, northeast of a community college, a couple of blocks east of the I-5 freeway and justwest of an area of single-family homes. It is transit-rich, however, justa block froma major bus transfer station (with planned light rail access). You can see the current bus lines in orange on the satellite image. With better amenities and some investment, the potential for smart, green redevelopment is immense.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/index.html">
         <img alt="Thornton Creek water channel (by: Thornton Place)" height="352" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2440/5800260555_48f58cfbed_d.jpg" title="Thornton Creek water channel (by: Thornton Place)" width="500"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Enter the city of Seattle, with
        <a href="http://www.seattle.gov/util/About_SPU/Drainage_&amp;_Sewer_System/Projects/COS_002477.asp">
         a sophisticated initiative
        </a>
        to bring long-buried
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thornton_Creek">
         Thornton Creek
        </a>
        back to life above the surface, where it belongs. Add lots of carefully planned nature (not really an oxymoron when were dealing with a city), arranged to be highly walkable and educational as well as environmentally beneficial. The creek restoration was led under contract to the city by Seattles
        <a href="http://www.svrdesign.com/index.html">
         SvR Design
        </a>
        , which previously designed the landscape and green infrastructure features of the much-celebrated, mixed-income
        <a href="http://switchboard.nrdc.org/blogs/kbenfield/green_affordable_walkable_beau.html">
         High Point
        </a>
        development, also in Seattle.
       </p>
       <p>
        Heres how
        <a href="http://lafoundation.org/research/landscape-performance-series/case-studies/case-study/137/">
         the Landscape Architecture Foundation puts it
        </a>
        :
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          Carved out of an abandoned parking lot, the Thornton Creek Water Quality Channel treats urban stormwater runoff
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/svrdesignco/3923884355/">
           <img align="right" alt="Thornton Creek water channel (by: SvR Design)" class="image-right" height="180" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2147/5799969469_3f2628e2b4_m_d.jpg" title="Thornton Creek water channel (by: SvR Design)" width="240"/>
          </a>
          from 680 acres within a necklace of channels, pools, and terraces designed to mimic the performance of a natural creek. Its lush plantings, overlooks, and paths have added 2.7 acres of public open space to the Northgate Urban Center and catalyzed surrounding redevelopment. The facility is a model for how multi-functional landscapes can be integrated into the dense urban fabric.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        The redevelopment not only added 530 units of housing (net density: 96 units per acre) and 50,000 square feet of retail space; it simultaneously accomplished the following, according to the LAF:
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Increased open space within the Northgate Urban Center by about 50%.
        </li>
        <li>
         Provided pedestrian links from adjacent commercial and residential neighborhoods, shortening walking distances by 50%.
        </li>
        <li>
         Reduced impervious surfaces by 78%.
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://lafoundation.org/research/landscape-performance-series/case-studies/case-study/137/photos/additional-78/">
          <img align="right" alt="Thornton Creek water channel (via Landscape Architecture Foundation)" class="image-right" height="180" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2755/5800120205_e6f27e5ba3_m_d.jpg" title="Thornton Creek water channel (via Landscape Architecture Foundation)" width="240"/>
         </a>
         Designed to remove an estimated 40-80% of total suspended solids from 91% of the average volume of annual stormwater runoff from the 680-acre drainage basin.
        </li>
        <li>
         Created new habitat within this heavily paved commercial area. Within one month after opening, native birds were observed at the project. A variety of desirable native volunteer plants have migrated into the site and begun to establish.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        85 percent of the projects plant palette are native species, including 172 native trees, 1,792 native shrubs, and 49,000 native perennials, herbs, grasses, rushes, and sedges. The planting mix and the channels alignment will be allowed to evolve naturally over time. When was the last time you saw this much additional urban density, increased natural space and reduced imperviousness on the same site? (For more of the technical details, see
        <a href="http://www.seattle.gov/util/groups/public/documents/webcontent/spu01_006146.pdf">
         the citys extensive report
        </a>
        on the project.)
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.bensonshaw.com/bensonshaw.com/BadBuoys.html">
         <img alt="floating public art (by: Benson Shaw)" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3622/5800752764_c9f7c84557_m_d.jpg" title="floating public art (by: Benson Shaw)" width="245"/>
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.bensonshaw.com/bensonshaw.com/FallingWater.html">
         <img alt="they glow at night (by: Benson Shaw)" height="163" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5073/5800752676_c341dfd6f2_m_d.jpg" title="they glow at night (by: Benson Shaw)" width="245"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        The space also includes
        <a href="http://www.bensonshaw.com/bensonshaw.com/T_CREEK.html">
         some cool public art
        </a>
        (by
        <a href="http://www.bensonshaw.com/bensonshaw.com/Home.html">
         Benson Shaw
        </a>
        ),along with educational signage (example below) about the projects environmental benefits.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="signage: What is a Biofiltration Swale? (by: City of Seattle)" height="488" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5150/5800752540_84d4a3b912_d.jpg" title="signage: What is a Biofiltration Swale? (by: City of Seattle)" width="488"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        The city is understandably pleased with the results. Note the credit to community involvement:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          The [Thornton Creek Water Channel] Facility is designed to remove pollutants from stormwater by slowing urban runoff before these flows enter the creek. The Facility, in conjunction with improvements to surrounding roadways, Northgate Mall, Northgate Library and the Northgate Community Center, is part of a larger strategy to revitalize the Northgate Urban Center. Community involvement was instrumental in making the Facility successful from a number of perspectives. A stakeholder group, made up of a broad balance of community, environmental and business interests, helped drive the Facilitys design, which resulted in a Facility design that integrates environmental and commercial concern . . . The Facility was constructed by Seattle Public Utilities with funding support from Washington State Department of Ecology via a Washington State Water Pollution Control Revolving Fund Loan.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Nice job.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/retail.html">
         <img alt="Thornton Place plaza (by: Thornton Place)" height="346" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5118/5800635262_28aca03c5f_d.jpg" title="Thornton Place plaza (by: Thornton Place)" width="500"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        This leads us to the urbanist part of the redevelopment, which is every bit as impressive as the improvements to public space and water quality. Much of the credit for this accomplishment belongs to a new development called
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/index.html">
         Thornton Place
        </a>
        , comprising 109 condos, 278 apartments (20 percent priced below market), a 14-screen cinema, 50,000 square feel of retail, and a very appealing plaza that gives the block a great urban public space in counterpoint to the natural one. Thornton Place was developed by the Swedish-founded
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/docs/media/downloads/Company_Overview.pdf">
         Stellar Holdings
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        According to
        <a href="http://seattletimes.nwsource.com/html/businesstechnology/2008918753_thornton25.html">
         a story by Eric Pryne in
         <em>
          The Seattle Times
         </em>
        </a>
        , Stellar actually offered prospective condo buyers a layoff-protection plan in hopes of spurring sales in this sour market. Reflecting the anxiety of current circumstances, the developers agreed to make mortgage payments for up to six months if a condo buyer loses his or her job within the first year.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://a5.sphotos.ak.fbcdn.net/photos-ak-snc1/v2482/175/115/54797656305/n54797656305_1452896_7442008.jpg?dl=1">
         <img alt="Creekside at Thornton Place (by: Thornton Place)" height="373" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2646/5800260673_9e8fa0580d_d.jpg" title="Creekside at Thornton Place (by: Thornton Place)" width="500"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Writing
        <a href="http://hugeasscity.com/2009/04/14/seattles-first-tod/">
         in the tastefully titled blog
         <em>
          hugeasscity
         </em>
        </a>
        , Dan Bertolet says that Thornton Place is really Seattles first transit-oriented development, and that the developer deserves credit for taking some risks:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          Northgate has been targeted for growth by city planners for decades: It is a designated Urban Center, a bus transit hub and future light rail station area. But the existing car-oriented, single-use built environment around the mall is a highly unappealing site for small-scale mixed-used residential development  what developer would risk being the lonely pioneer amidst a sea of big box and parking lots? In contrast, a large project like Thornton Place creates a center of gravity powerful enough both to keep itself alive, and to be a catalyst for future adjacent development.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/creeksideapts.html">
         <img alt="Creekside at Thornton Place (by: Thornton Place)" height="173" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3026/5800634996_b3803fe398_m_d.jpg" title="Creekside at Thornton Place (by: Thornton Place)" width="245"/>
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/creeksideapts.html">
         <img alt="Creekside at Thornton Place (by: Thornton Place)" height="173" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2546/5800634880_3e69117c59_m_d.jpg" title="Creekside at Thornton Place (by: Thornton Place)" width="245"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Bertolet writes (and I agree) that the walkability of the project would have been even better if it had been broken up into two blocks with a through street in the center, taking advantage of an opportunity to restore lost
        <a href="http://switchboard.nrdc.org/blogs/kbenfield/being_connected_is_a_very_good.html">
         street connectivity
        </a>
        . That would have required some serious design thought to avoid compromising the natural area, of course. But (sigh) neighbors objected, fearing additional traffic (actually, a through street would have reduced, not increased, congestion), and the result is still outstanding for its context.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.yelp.com/biz_photos/0tpigLtIUSONS8V9riKJ_Q?select=-niSIiSGNCtNma6P2oviqQ">
         <img align="left" alt="Northgate Transit Center (by: Mark M, via Yelp)" class="image-left" height="240" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3165/5801102459_33d2faa576_m_d.jpg" title="Northgate Transit Center (by: Mark M, via Yelp)" width="180"/>
        </a>
        In addition to the environmental benefits derived from its land recycling, transit access (transit center, photo left)and urbanism,
        <a href="http://www.thornton-place.com/docs/media/downloads/GreenSheet.pdf">
         Thornton Place has
        </a>
        a district heating system, energy-efficient and resource-conserving buildings (LEED-certified), recycled construction waste and preferred parking for alternative-fueled vehicles. It registered for the
        <a href="http://switchboard.nrdc.org/blogs/kbenfield/knowing_when_its_green_a_citiz.html">
         LEED-ND
        </a>
        pilot program, but as far as I can tell has not yet fully submitted its documentation to be certified.
       </p>
       <p>
        Across the creek fromThornton Placeis a laudable project called
        <a href="http://www.eraliving.com/senior_living/Seattle_Northgate_WA/zip_98125/era_living/2754">
         Aljoya
        </a>
        , comprising 143 units of assisted-living housing for seniors,on the southeast cornerof the site (background, photo below). Aljoya was developed by Era Living. The city also brought more investment to the area, adding a new neighborhood library and community center just northeast of the redevelopment block.
       </p>
       <p>
        The citys report on the water channel project highlights the overall redevelopments outstanding mix of nature and urbanism:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          The Facility united a divided community, fulfilling collective goals of providing a
         </em>
         <em>
          diverse housing mix, jobs, retail,
          <a href="http://ofparamount.blogspot.com/2011/05/thornton-creek-water-quality-channel.html">
           <img align="right" alt="looking across the water channel to Aljoya (by: Janet Way, via Of Paramount Importance)" class="image-right" height="180" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3121/5801575987_c1bddac0b2_m_d.jpg" title="looking across the water channel to Aljoya (by: Janet Way, via Of Paramount Importance)" width="240"/>
          </a>
          recreation, public open space, raising environmental awareness, and improving Thornton Creek water quality. The Facility serves as the front yard for the private commercial and residential development that flanks it. Indeed, the Facilitys development and construction, which cost approximately $14.7 million is believed to have generated over $200 million in adjacent private development. It has catalyzed the Northgate neighborhoods emergence as a growing urban center for the City of Seattle.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Lets raise a glass (or cup) to that. I have a feeling that this project is going to start showing up in a lot of case studies and presentations, including my own, and deservedly so.
       </p>
