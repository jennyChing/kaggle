       The Idea of History

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Collingwood was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Cartmel" title="Cartmel">
       Cartmel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Grange-over-Sands" title="Grange-over-Sands">
       Grange-over-Sands
      </a>
      , in
      <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">
       Lancashire
      </a>
      , the son of the artist and archaeologist
      <a href="/wiki/W._G._Collingwood" title="W. G. Collingwood">
       W. G. Collingwood
      </a>
      . He was educated at
      <a href="/wiki/Rugby_School" title="Rugby School">
       Rugby School
      </a>
      , and at
      <a href="/wiki/University_College,_Oxford" title="University College, Oxford">
       University College, Oxford
      </a>
      , where he gained a First in Classical Moderations [Greek and Latin] in 1910 and a congratulatory First in Greats [Ancient History and Philosophy] in 1912.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Prior to graduation he was elected a fellow of
      <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_College,_Oxford" title="Pembroke College, Oxford">
       Pembroke College, Oxford
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Collingwood was a fellow of
      <a href="/wiki/Pembroke_College,_Oxford" title="Pembroke College, Oxford">
       Pembroke College, Oxford
      </a>
      , for some 15 years until becoming the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waynflete_Professor_of_Metaphysical_Philosophy" title="Waynflete Professor of Metaphysical Philosophy">
       Waynflete Professor of Metaphysical Philosophy
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_College,_Oxford" title="Magdalen College, Oxford">
       Magdalen College, Oxford
      </a>
      . He was the only pupil of
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_J._Haverfield" title="Francis J. Haverfield">
       F. J. Haverfield
      </a>
      to survive World War I. Important influences on Collingwood were the Italian Idealists
      <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Croce" title="Benedetto Croce">
       Benedetto Croce
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Gentile" title="Giovanni Gentile">
       Giovanni Gentile
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guido_de_Ruggiero" title="Guido de Ruggiero">
       Guido de Ruggiero
      </a>
      , the last of whom was also a close friend. Other important influences were
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G.W.F._Hegel" title="G.W.F. Hegel">
       Hegel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
       Kant
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Vico" title="Giambattista Vico">
       Giambattista Vico
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/F._H._Bradley" title="F. H. Bradley">
       F. H. Bradley
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Alexander_Smith" title="John Alexander Smith">
       J. A. Smith
      </a>
      . His father
      <a href="/wiki/W._G._Collingwood" title="W. G. Collingwood">
       W. G. Collingwood
      </a>
      was a leading archaeologist and artist, being a student of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">
       Ruskin
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Work">
       Work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Collingwood is most famous for his book
      <i>
       The Idea of History
      </i>
      (1946), a work collated from various sources soon after his death by his pupil T. M. Knox. The book came to be a major inspiration for philosophy of history in the English-speaking world, and is extensively cited, leading one commentator to ironically remark that Collingwood is coming to be "the best known neglected thinker of our time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Collingwood held history as "recollection" of the "thinking" of a historical personage. Collingwood considered whether two different people can have the same thought and not just the same content, concluding that "there is no tenable theory of personal identity" preventing such a doctrine.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       The Principles of Art
      </i>
      Collingwood held (following Croce) that works of art are essentially expressions of emotion. He portrayed art as a necessary function of the human mind, and considered it collaborative activity. In politics Collingwood defended the ideals of what he called liberalism "in its Continental sense":
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The essence of this conception is ... the idea of a community as governing itself by fostering the free expression of all political opinions that take shape within it, and finding some means of reducing this multiplicity of opinions to a unity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Collingwood_as_archaeologist">
       Collingwood as archaeologist
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._G._Collingwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Collingwood as archaeologist">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Collingwood was not just a philosopher of history, but also a practising historian and archaeologist, being during his time a leading authority on Roman Britain: he spent his term time at Oxford teaching philosophy but devoted his long vacations to archaeology.
     </p>
     <p>
      He began work along Hadrians Wall. The family home was at Coniston in the Lake District and his father was a leading figure in the Cumberland and Westmorland Archaeological Society. Collingwood was drawn in on a number of excavations and put forward the theory that Hadrians Wall was not so much a fighting platform but an elevated sentry walk.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also put forward the suggestion that Hadrians defensive system also included a number of forts along the Cumberland coast. He was very active in the 1930 Wall Pilgrimage for which he prepared the ninth edition of Bruces Handbook.
     </p>
     <p>
      His final and most controversial excavation in Cumbria was that of a circular ring ditch near Penrith known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Arthur%27s_Round_Table_(henge)" title="King Arthur's Round Table (henge)">
       King Arthur's Round Table (henge)
      </a>
      in 1937. This would appear to be a Neolithic henge monument and Collingwoods excavations, while failing to find conclusive evidence of Neolithic activity, nevertheless found the base of two stone pillars, a possible cremation trench and some post holes. Sadly his subsequent ill health prevented him undertaking a second season, so the work was handed over to the German prehistorian
      <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Bersu" title="Gerhard Bersu">
       Gerhard Bersu
      </a>
      who queried some of Collingwoods findings. However recently Grace Simpson, the daughter of the excavator F.G. Simpson, has queried Bersus work and largely rehabilitated Collingwood as an excavator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He also began what was to be the major work of his archaeological career, preparing a corpus of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Inscriptions_of_Britain" title="Roman Inscriptions of Britain">
        Roman Inscriptions of Britain
       </a>
      </i>
      , which involved travelling all over Britain to see the inscriptions and draw them; he eventually prepared drawings of nearly 900 inscriptions. It was finally published in 1965 by his student R.P. Wright.
     </p>
     <p>
      He also published two major archaeological works. The first, somewhat surprisingly for a philosopher was
      <i>
       The Archaeology of Roman Britain
      </i>
      , a handbook in sixteen chapters covering first the archaeological sites - fortresses, towns and temples, and then the portable antiquities  inscriptions, coins, pottery and brooches. Mortimer Wheeler in a review ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      remarked that "it seemed at first a trifle off beat that he should immerse himself in so much museum-like detail  but I felt sure that this was incidental to his primary mission to organise his own thinking".
     </p>
     <p>
      But his most important work was his contribution to the first volume of the Oxford History of England 
      <i>
       Roman Britain and the English Settlements
      </i>
      of which he wrote the major part 
      <a href="/wiki/Nowell_Myres" title="Nowell Myres">
       Nowell Myres
      </a>
      adding the second smaller part on English Settlements. The book was in many ways revolutionary, for it set out to write the story of Roman Britain from an archaeological rather than a historical viewpoint, putting into practice his own belief in Question and Answer archaeology.
     </p>
     <p>
      The result was alluring and influential. However as Ian Richmond wrote: The general reader may discover too late that it has one major defect. It does not sufficiently distinguish between objective and subjective and combines both in a subtle and apparently objective presentation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The most notorious passage is that on Romano-British art where he says: "The impression that constantly haunts the archaeologist, like a bad smell, is that of an ugliness that plagues the place like a London fog".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Collingwoods most important contribution to British archaeology was his insistence on Question and Answer archaeology, that excavations should only take place if there is a question to be answered. It is a philosophy which, as
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Birley" title="Anthony Birley">
       Anthony Birley
      </a>
      points out,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      has been incorporated by English Heritage into the conditions for Scheduled Monuments Consent. But it has always been surprising that the proponents of the New archaeology in the 1960s and the 70s should have entirely ignored the work of Collingwood, the one major archaeologist who was also a major professional philosopher.
     </p>
     <p>
      Outside archaeology and philosophy, he also published
      <i>
       The First Mate's Log (1940)
      </i>
      , an account of a yachting voyage in the Mediterranean, in the company of several of his students.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Ransome" title="Arthur Ransome">
       Arthur Ransome
      </a>
      was a family friend, and learned to sail in their boat, subsequently teaching his sibling's children to sail. Ransome loosely based
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_characters_in_Arthur_Ransome_books#The_Swallows" title="List of characters in Arthur Ransome books">
       the Swallows
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Swallows_and_Amazons_series" title="Swallows and Amazons series">
        Swallows and Amazons series
       </a>
      </i>
      on his sibling's children.
     </p>
     <p>
      After several years of increasingly debilitating strokes Collingwood died at
      <a href="/wiki/Coniston,_Cumbria" title="Coniston, Cumbria">
       Coniston
      </a>
      , Lancashire, in January 1943. He was a practising Anglican throughout his life.
     </p>
