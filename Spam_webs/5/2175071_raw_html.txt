  She tells Nicholas Shakespeare how she came to write  and draw  the

          <p class="bylineBody">
           By
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/journalists/nicholas-shakespeare/" rel="author" title="Nicholas Shakespeare">

           </a>

           <span>
            Prague

        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           At Helga Weisss first concentration camp, a boy of her age, 14, wrote a poem she has never forgotten. It ends:
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           <i>
            Butterflies dont live here,
           </i>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           <i>
            In the ghetto
           </i>
           .
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           Helga got out of the ghetto and eventually back to Prague, where she lives in a short street of flat-fronted apartment blocks. I speak into an intercom, and she lets me in, not taking her finger off the buzzer. The buzzing pursues me up four flights of granite steps, all the way to her open door, breaking off only as I step inside. She stands, waiting, in the hallway, an old woman no taller than a girl: black trousers, blue eyes, short grey hair  and, hanging on a silver chain around her neck, a stone in the shape of a butterfly wing.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           This is the apartment I was born in, she says, leading the way into a small drawing room crammed with books and paintings. Its not the most comfortable, not the most beautiful, but I have become attached to it. From this apartment we were deported.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class=" related_links_inline" id="tmg-related-links">

            </span>
           </li>

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           I look around, quickly taking in a piano (on the same spot where her father, a bank official, used to play Chopin), a brown sofa scattered with knitted cushions (saved by our non-Jewish friends during the war) and a contemporary poster of a young female cellist, her granddaughter. Its much later that I notice the butterflies.
          </p>
          <p>
           I have a lot of butterflies, she agrees, and points at the stained-glass orange one embedded in the window, four dangling in the net curtain, a blue one pinned to the wall. Because butterflies mean freedom.
          </p>
          <p>
           She finds it interesting that there were none at Terezn  only lice and bedbugs. It was while watching a butterfly settle on a flower as she stumbled up the hill to her fourth and final concentration camp, Mauthausen, that Helga reached a simple decision: she wanted to live. Of the 15,000 Jewish children under the age of 15 deported to Terezn, Helga was one of a mere 100 ever to see Prague again.
          </p>
          <p>
           Helga  now Helga Hoskov-Weissov, her married name  is, incredibly, a survivor of Terezn, Auschwitz, Freiberg and Mauthausen; an artist, a widow and a great-grandmother; and, in her 84th year, the published author of a remarkable journal describing her girlhood as a prisoner of the Nazis.
          </p>
          <p>
           If it seems surprising that such a diary should surface at this point, it should be explained that parts of it were published in the Sixties, in a Czech anthology, and Helga had no idea anyone was interested beyond that. In Czechoslovakia, no one cared about the Jews: First of all, no one figured on us returning, she explains, and in 1948 there was the Communist coup, at which point, the situation for Jews here was pretty ugly. Yet talking to Helga, it becomes possible to imagine what it might be like chatting with Anne Frank, also born in 1929, had she lived.
          </p>
          <p>
           We were sitting here and heard the knocking on the door. It always happened at night. There were already rumours that this evening someone from the Jewish community would distribute the summons, so we expected it. After 8pm it was forbidden to go outside. No Jew would come up those stairs, nor an Aryan. So when there was a knock, it couldnt be anyone else. It was December 4 1941.
          </p>
          <p>
           While they waited with a huge crowd at the Trade Fair Palace, Helga experienced her first period. A German officer promised that they were going to a safe place where they would not be persecuted. In the event, they were among 45,000 Prague Jews who were transported by train to the fortress of Terezn, three hours away. It was snowing. Men were immediately separated from women, parents from children. Helga was put in a room with about 30 girls her age. She tried to see her parents for an hour each day, and made a pact with her father to think of each other every evening at 7pm. Sometimes it was not possible. Soon after, she sent him a childish drawing of a snowman. His reaction: Draw what you see. So she did.
          </p>
          <p>
           The sole footage that exists is a propaganda film promoting Terezn as a spa town for the benefit of a visit by the Red Cross. Education was forbidden, but the Germans wanted to show it was a normal place. A building was tarted up to look like a school, with a sign reading Holidays to explain the absence of teachers and pupils. More signs pointed the way to parks and public baths. We were not allowed to go outside to see the Red Cross. The Germans chose a group of young people and gave them a basket with vegetables, and they had to walk along and sing. It was all a stage, a performance, a humbug.
          </p>
          <p>
           Helga depicted the reality in her drawings, and because the Germans forbade photographs, these drawings, which were recovered after the war along with her Terezn journal, have become an important testimony. She flicks through a book of them, pausing over an ink sketch of a man checking his blanket for bedbugs or blechy. She says: I killed them by hand. They were small, but when they had eaten or drunk they bloated up  and what is most terrible, full of blood.
          </p>
          <p>
           Her favourite drawing is of a funeral cart loaded with bread, and bearing the logo Welfare for the young. She says: It was the only vehicle at Terezn. They wanted to decimate us psychologically too. Everything was carried on it  luggage, old people, dirty linen  except dead bodies. As for those famous baskets of vegetables, which Helga and other children spent their days cultivating, she snorts: It was all for the Germans.
          </p>
          <p>
           Hunger drove her to hide cabbages and lettuce in her underwear, but she had to be alert in case the Germans searched her, ready to throw the food away or gulp it down. Once it happened to me that I was walking under a plum tree and I took a plum and shared it, and some worker saw me  because we were watched by the Jews too. And after that we were going to be included in a transport, until my father managed to stop it. For one plum, you could pay with your life.
          </p>
          <p>
           In October 1944, Helgas father was loaded on to a railway truck. The Germans explained that the men were going ahead to prepare another ghetto. We were not sure about anything. We wanted to believe it. He hugged her head to his chest. She could distinctly hear his heart beating. Till today, I see him. He climbed on to the steps of the truck. He was waving and trying to smile, but it was not a smile.
          </p>
          <p>
           Three days later, it was the turn of Helga and her mother. Their destination  Auschwitz-Birkenau  meant nothing to them. We had no idea of extermination camps. We had no idea gas chambers existed. We didnt know till the moment we got there. The train stopped and we saw a big area with barracks and barbed wire and smoking chimneys. We supposed it was a factory. She waits for it to sink in. Everything was terrible, but everything was much worse than we supposed.
          </p>
          <p>
           She stepped on to the ramp with her mother. Prisoners in striped jackets whispered instructions, helping them. Dont say you are ill, dont say you are young, or tomorrow you will go through the chimney. A German officer stood at the head of the queue directing people right and left. I am often asked if this was Josef Mengele. Probably it was. I dont remember his face, only his gloves, but his face is not important. The important thing was his finger, because with his finger he pointed right, left, right, left. On the right side, he let go those younger and able to work. On the other side, mothers and small children and the old. At 15, Helga was too young to be a worker. She decided to lie, say that she was 18. I am not religious, but in that moment I start to pray. I ask God to leave me and my mother together. For whatever reason, the man lifted his finger, snarled at both of them: Rechts!
          </p>
          <p>
           She was led into a room with 500 others to be shaved. And not only from the head  from everywhere. After that, we couldn't recognise each other. I had a lot of hair, dark blonde, long enough, and I remember my mother finished earlier than me. I was looking for her, I couldnt recognise her  my mother, the same. We were looking for voices. Young SS men stood watching and smiling. It was very funny for them.
          </p>
          <p>
           Naked, Helga was ushered into a hut as big as this room full of coats, dresses, shoes, and ordered quickly to pick one item of each. She snatched a thin red dress (I would stand in it for hours in the rain, for roll-calls, the red dye flowing down my leg), two high-heeled shoes (right and left, but not a pair) and a long green coat. I felt happy after I received such a coat, so cold were we. Such things can make a woman happy.
          </p>
          <p>
           Happiness is not an emotion very often admitted at Auschwitz. Guileless and largely uncontaminated by retrospective knowledge, Helgas account is vital with these human surprises. Does she remember any laughter during her short stay at Auschwitz? There was laughter between prisoners. I was all the time with my mother. I was lucky. You had to have somebody. Otherwise, for those on their own, there was the gallows humour of the prisoner who pointed at the smoking crematorium, and told her: Tomorrow the soot you find on your nose will be mine.
          </p>
          <p>
           Helga was in Auschwitz 10 days, not long enough to be tattooed, but long enough to have seared into her memory the number 54391, which she had to call out at every roll-call. She can still only utter it in German: Vier und fnfzig drei hundert ein und neunzig.
          </p>
          <p>
           An SS man called Beck was in charge at the end. He knew the transport we were on was destined for the gas chamber at Mauthausen. He could have left us somewhere on the way, but it was his duty to bring us to death, and thats what he did. She recalls the last morning at Mauthausen, on 5 May 1945. Up until then, we were watched from all sides and corners. But on that morning they ran away, threw off their uniforms, dressed in civilian clothes and ran.
          </p>
          <p>
           Interestingly, the first word that Helga heard murmured was peace rather than freedom. It is war and peace. War is over. It is peace. And inside peace is freedom, the end of hunger, the finish of cruelty. But peace was not such a wonderful thing. It came too late and we were too sick. We were glad it was over, but my first thought was for my father: we survived, but did he?
          </p>
          <p>
           She often thought she saw him in the street. There and in dreams. I always saw him walking and coming towards me. It lasted a whole year. Probably he had died on the very day he left Terezn, at Auschwitz. I hope he didnt know until that moment. I hope it was over quickly.
          </p>
          <p>
           The war over, she returned with her mother to Prague, and eventually to their fourth-floor apartment, where in July 1945 her uncle Josef turned up with Helgas drawings and two yellow notebooks of diaries. Entrusted with them on the eve of her transportation to Auschwitz, he had bricked the whole lot into a wall in his barracks at Terezn.
          </p>
          <p>
           Helga started to write after rereading the Terezn entries, continuing from where she broke off. I needed to get it out of me. It was still inside me. Thats why I wrote it in the present. Thats why when I speak about it, I am present. It is still present  as very much is her father. I am still in touch with him. In many situations, I ask him what he would say, what he would do, and I remember his words, everything he taught.
          </p>
          <p>
           Her best friend at Terezn wrote in Helgas journal: Forget the hours of suffering, but never the lessons they taught you.
          </p>
          <p>
           What did she learn?
          </p>
          <p>
           The value of life. That people have to be kind to each other.
          </p>
          <p>
           And never to throw away food or clothes. I saw some shoes in the street the other day, and I remember how shoes or a piece of bread would save somebodys life. And very often if I have difficulty climbing the steps to the fourth floor, in my thoughts I see the steps in the quarry at Mauthausen, and the prisoners who had to climb to the top, carrying a rock  and then I am able to climb the steps.
          </p>
          <p>
           In the course of a six-hour conversation, we cover her postwar life: her marriage to a Roman Catholic, Jir Hosek, a musician in the Czech Radio Symphony Orchestra; her decision to become an artist, despite being refused a place at the Decorative School of Arts because the new authorities judged her paintings too pessimistic; her endurance of a Communist regime in certain ways just as frightful and anti-Jewish as that of the Nazis. While I am with her, Helgas mobile phone rings twice. One caller is Lisa, 91, who was at Freiberg with her, one of 20 survivors who keep in touch by Skype and meet up in the mountains every year, at a hotel in pindlerv Mln, to talk about the camps.
          </p>
          <p>
           The second call is from her granddaughter, the cellist, with news that her six-month-old daughter Adina has broken her first tooth.
          </p>
          <p>
           Energised, Helga smiles at me: At this moment, I am happy. Isnt it a wonder, after all, to have been granted to a few of us the chance of giving birth? And our children are now older than our parents were, and Im a great-grandmother.
          </p>
          <p>

            </i>
           </a>
          </p>

            var init = function(currentArticleId, articleList, displayThumbnail) {

                settings.articleList = articleList;
                settings.currentArticleId = currentArticleId;
                settings.displayThumbnail = displayThumbnail;

                settings.articleListToSkip = unescape($.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName)).split(',');

                if ($.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName) != null) {
                    chooseNextArticle(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    updateCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);

                        getNavPos($(this).attr("href"), "wayfarerReadNext");

                    bindUIActions();

                    var displayed = false;

                    setTimeout(function () {
                        if (!displayed) {
                            displayed == true;
                            readNextWidget.display();

                        if (isScrolledIntoView("#tmg-related-links")) {

                    settings.autoOpen = false;

                    settings.autoOpen = false; //set auto open to false once user has viewed this once

                        //animation complete

                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                }
                return false;

            var isScrolledIntoView = function(elem) {
                if($(elem).length) { //check if element exists
                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));
                } else {
                    return false;
                }
            };

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {
                    return index == arr.indexOf(el);
                });
            }

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
