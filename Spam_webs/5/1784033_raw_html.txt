   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    U-statistic
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Statistical_theory" title="Statistical theory">
       statistical theory
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       U-statistic
      </b>
      is a class of statistics that is especially important in
      <a href="/wiki/Estimation_theory" title="Estimation theory">
       estimation theory
      </a>
      ; the letter "U" stands for unbiased. In elementary statistics, U-statistics arise naturally in producing
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum-variance_unbiased_estimator" title="Minimum-variance unbiased estimator">
       minimum-variance unbiased estimators
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The theory of U-statistics allows a
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum-variance_unbiased_estimator" title="Minimum-variance unbiased estimator">
       minimum-variance unbiased estimator
      </a>
      to be derived from each
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unbiased_estimator" title="Unbiased estimator">
       unbiased estimator
      </a>
      of an
      <i>
       estimable parameter
      </i>
      (alternatively,
      <i>
       statistical
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Functional" title="Functional">
        functional
       </a>
       <sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=U-statistic&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow">
          <span title="Link needs disambiguation. (March 2014)">
           disambiguation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </i>
      ) for large classes of probability distributions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An estimable parameter is a
      <a href="/wiki/Measurable_function" title="Measurable function">
       measurable function
      </a>
      of the population's cumulative
      <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">
       probability distribution
      </a>
      : For example, for every probability distribution, the population median is an estimable parameter. The theory of U-statistics applies to general classes of probability distributions.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many statistics originally derived for particular parametric families have been recognized as U-statistics for general distributions. In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-parametric_statistics" title="Non-parametric statistics">
       non-parametric statistics
      </a>
      , the theory of U-statistics is used to establish for statistical procedures (such as estimators and tests) and estimators relating to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymptotic_normality" title="Asymptotic normality">
       asymptotic normality
      </a>
      and to the variance (in finite samples) of such quantities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The theory has been used to study more general statistics as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">
       stochastic processes
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Random_graph" title="Random graph">
       random graphs
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Suppose that a problem involves
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically-distributed_random_variables" title="Independent and identically-distributed random variables">
       independent and identically-distributed random variables
      </a>
      and that estimation of a certain parameter is required. Suppose that a simple unbiased estimate can be constructed based on only a few observations: this defines the basic estimator based on a given number of observations. For example, a single observation is itself an unbiased estimate of the mean and a pair of observations can be used to derive an unbiased estimate of the variance. The U-statistic based on this estimator is defined as the average (across all combinatorial selections of the given size from the full set of observations) of the basic estimator applied to the sub-samples.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sen (1992) provides a review of the paper by
      <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Hoeffding" title="Wassily Hoeffding">
       Wassily Hoeffding
      </a>
      (1948), which introduced U-statistics and set out the theory relating to them, and in doing so Sen outlines the importance U-statistics have in statistical theory. Sen says
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "The impact of Hoeffding (1948) is overwhelming at the present time and is very likely to continue in the years to come". Note that the theory of U-statistics is not limited to
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the case of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically-distributed_random_variables" title="Independent and identically-distributed random variables">
       independent and identically-distributed random variables
      </a>
      or to scalar random-variables.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=U-statistic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term U-statistic, due to Hoeffding (1948), is defined as follows.
     </p>
     <p>
      Let
      <img alt="f\colon R^r\to R" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/8/1/a815a560d805cd9f1758a6e040ee530c.png"/>
      be a real-valued or complex-valued function of
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      variables. For each
      <img alt="n\ge r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/a/4/da466b5864500bfee2d3be9e2593c65b.png"/>
      the associated U-statistic
      <img alt="f_n\colon R^n \to R" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/c/0/ec0f52905170c7db5eb3e6d0c7bb970b.png"/>
      is equal to the average over ordered samples
      <img alt="\varphi(1),\ldots, \varphi(r)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/9/e/79e4c09540b9b6f590c333f258932a62.png"/>
      of size
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      of the sample values
      <img alt="f(x_\varphi)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/0/4/7045a0b4785d99c5ae018cc379c1d01f.png"/>
      . In other words,
      <img alt="f_n(x_1,\ldots, x_n) = \operatorname{ave} f(x_{\varphi(1)},\ldots, x_{\varphi(r)})" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/0/920ee9ec3ba9a5b37ca4c7086c2fe42f.png"/>
      , the average being taken over distinct ordered samples of size
      <img alt="r" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png"/>
      taken from
      <img alt="\{1,\ldots, n\}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/2/6/02617b75af4d512caf92071211ac3d6a.png"/>
      . Each U-statistic
      <img alt="f_n(x_1,\ldots, x_n)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/c/6/4c6186c4efb5e3a895987c215caab671.png"/>
      is necessarily a
      <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_function" title="Symmetric function">
       symmetric function
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      U-statistics are very natural in statistical work, particularly in Hoeffding's context of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically-distributed_random_variables" title="Independent and identically-distributed random variables">
       independent and identically-distributed random variables
      </a>
      , or more generally for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exchangeable_sequence" title="Exchangeable sequence">
       exchangeable sequences
      </a>
      , such as in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_random_sampling" title="Simple random sampling">
       simple random sampling
      </a>
      from a finite population, where the defining property is termed `inheritance on the average'.
     </p>
     <p>
      Fisher's
      <i>
       k
      </i>
      -statistics and Tukey's
      <a href="/wiki/Polykay" title="Polykay">
       polykays
      </a>
      are examples of
      <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial" title="Homogeneous polynomial">
       homogeneous polynomial
      </a>
      U-statistics (Fisher, 1929; Tukey, 1950). For a simple random sample
      <i>
       
      </i>
      of size
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      taken from a population of size
      <i>
       N
      </i>
      , the U-statistic has the property that the average over sample values
      <i>
       
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        n
       </i>
      </sub>
      (
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      ) is exactly equal to the population value
      <i>
       
      </i>
      <sub>
       <i>
        N
       </i>
      </sub>
      (
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
       Examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=U-statistic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Some examples: If
      <img alt="f(x) = x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/8/a/f8abf34599677984dfe91b4f300389f6.png"/>
      the U-statistic
      <img alt="f_n(x) = \bar x_n = (x_1 + \cdots + x_n)/n" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/e/e/8ee8096b101a2f6eaa5f683474e6b6ba.png"/>
      is the sample mean.
     </p>
     <p>
      If
      <img alt="f(x_1, x_2) = |x_1 - x_2|" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/b/d/ebdcc0cf54df7d8ce548639cb5ddfcc4.png"/>
      , the U-statistic is the mean pairwise deviation
      <img alt="f_n(x_1,\ldots, x_n) = \sum_{i\neq j} |x_i - x_j| / (n(n-1))" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/0/9/60969e50f02ebcc3832ab7ed6a7c8ee0.png"/>
      , defined for
      <img alt="n\ge 2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/d/5/5d5f448f1528508f27683f57c3c34111.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      If
      <img alt="f(x_1, x_2) = (x_1 - x_2)^2/2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/e/0/ae0c1b7c5fa1e035e7abc57b9f5a4325.png"/>
      , the U-statistic is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_variance" title="Sample variance">
       sample variance
      </a>
      <img alt="f_n(x) = \sum(x_i - \bar x_n)^2/(n-1)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/f/5/2f53024c402c9f1dbf77ba4576ba0f53.png"/>
      with divisor
      <img alt="n-1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/4/3/a438673491daae8148eae77373b6a467.png"/>
      , defined for
      <img alt="n\ge 2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/d/5/5d5f448f1528508f27683f57c3c34111.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The third
      <img alt="k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/c/e/8ce4b16b22b58894aa86c421e8759df3.png"/>
      -statistic
      <img alt="k_{3,n}(x) = \sum(x_i - \bar x_n)^3 n/((n-1)(n-2))" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/e/d/2ed54280ecddd12139a0165e4d28c614.png"/>
      , the sample
      <a href="/wiki/Skewness" title="Skewness">
       skewness
      </a>
      defined for
      <img alt="n\ge 3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/8/2/f8233aa1fafe3ee7992984a6e13b8257.png"/>
      , is a U-statistic.
     </p>
     <p>
      The following case highlights an important point. If
      <img alt="f(x_1, x_2, x_3)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/e/e/aee5767fde53863a87491d68ae407fe4.png"/>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">
       median
      </a>
      of three values,
      <img alt="f_n(x_1,\ldots, x_n)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/c/6/4c6186c4efb5e3a895987c215caab671.png"/>
      is not the median of
      <img alt="n" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/8/7b8b965ad4bca0e41ab51de7b31363a1.png"/>
      values. However, it is a minimum variance unbiased estimate of the expected value of the median of three values and in this application of the theory it is the population parameter defined as "the expected value of the median of three values" which is being estimated, not the median of the population. Similar estimates play a central role where the parameters of a family of probability distributions are being estimated by probability weighted moments or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-moments" title="L-moments">
       L-moments
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">
       See also
