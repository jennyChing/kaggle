       <td class="tb1">
        <div id="technique">
         <b>
          Lacing Technique:
         </b>
         <p>
           Begin straight across on the outside (grey section) and in through the bottom eyelets.
         </p>
         <p>
           The left (blue) end runs all the way up the inside and out through the top left eyelet.
         </p>
         <p>
           The right (yellow) end runs straight up on the inside and out through the next higher eyelet, then
	straight across on the outside and in through the adjacent eyelet on the other side. Repeat until the end reaches
	the top right eyelet.
         </p>
        </div>
        <br/>
        <div id="features">
         <b>
          Features:
         </b>
         <div class="tick">
          Even no. of eyelets = neat
         </div>
         <div class="cross">
          Odd no. of eyelets = messy
         </div>
         <div class="cross">
          End lengths shift
         </div>
         <div class="plus">
          28% longer ends (approx.)
         </div>
        </div>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td class="tb2" colspan="2">
        <div id="notes">
         <b>
          Notes:
         </b>
         <p>
          Straight Easy Lacing only works correctly on shoes with
          <b>
           even numbers
          </b>
          of eyelet pairs (eg. 8pairs = 16eyelets). This is because the lace must cross the
	shoe an even number of times so that the ends will meet and can be tied together. On shoes with an
          <b>
           odd number
          </b>
          of eyelet pairs (eg. 7pairs = 14eyelets), the lace ends finish diagonally opposite
	each other at the top of the shoe.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a class="ma2" href="#workarounds" title="Workarounds for odd numbers of eyelet pairs">
           See below
          </a>
          for some workarounds for shoes with odd numbers of eyelet pairs.
         </p>
        </div>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td class="tb1" colspan="2">
        <div class="morenotes">
         <b>
          Sports / Military Advice:
         </b>
         <p>
          Like other straight lacing methods, Straight Easy Lacing has an additional benefit for sporting or military
	use: The upper horizontal sections of shoelace can be quickly cut through with a knife or scissors in order to
	more easily remove a boot from a broken, sprained or otherwise injured ankle or foot.
         </p>
         <p>
          Note that most military forces have regulations for just about everything, so I'd recommend that military
	personnel check before they adopt this, or any other, possible non-regulation lacing method!
         </p>
        </div>

     <table class="maintable i100x160 il">
      <colgroup>
       <col>
       </col>
      </colgroup>
      <tr>
       <td class="th2">
        Odd Workarounds
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb1">
       <td>
        <p>
         <a class="ma8" href="#notes" title="Notes about even / odd numbers of eyelet pairs">
          As mentioned above
         </a>
         , Straight Easy Lacing only works correctly on shoes with
         <b>
          even numbers
         </b>
         of eyelet pairs. Here's several common workarounds for shoes with
         <b>
          odd numbers
         </b>
         of eyelet pairs, using sample diagrams with seven pairs of eyelets.
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb2">
       <td>
        <img alt="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 1" src="../Dont_Link/StraightEasyLacing7am.gif" title="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 1"/>
        <b>
         Ends Tucked In
        </b>
        <p>
         Firstly, you can accept the fact that the lace ends don't meet and simply tuck them into the shoe.
        </p>
        <p>
         For a tighter fit, you could also knot each lace end so that they don't pull through the eyelets, then tuck them in.
	However, those knots can feel uncomfortable.
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb1">
       <td>
        <img alt="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 2" src="../Dont_Link/StraightEasyLacing7bm.gif" title="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 2"/>
        <b>
         Skip One Eyelet Pair
        </b>
        <p>
         Another solution is to simply not use either the top or the bottom pair of eyelets. This avoids the odd limitation
	by using only an even number of the available eyelet pairs.
        </p>
        <p>
         It's also possible to skip a pair of eyelets somewhere in the middle, which breaks the lacing into two sections.
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb2">
       <td>
        <img alt="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 3" src="../Dont_Link/StraightEasyLacing7cm.gif" title="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 3"/>
        <b>
         One Diagonal
        </b>
        <p>
         Use a single diagonal pass somewhere in the lacing. At the top, it's less noticeable due to the bows and shoelace
	ends. At the bottom, you can even run the diagonal around the inside of the tongue, making it invisible at the
	expense of some slight discomfort. Near the middle of the lacing, a diagonal may be positioned to line up with
	and run through a tongue centering loop (if the shoe has one).
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb1">
       <td>
        <img alt="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 4" src="../Dont_Link/StraightEasyLacing7dm.gif" title="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 4"/>
        <b>
         One Crossover
        </b>
        <p>
         Instead of trying to hide a single diagonal, this alternative makes a feature out of a single crossover. This
	crossover can be placed at either the top or bottom of the lacing, but unlike the above "One Diagonal" variation,
	it cannot be placed in the middle of the lacing.
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb2">
       <td>
        <img alt="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 5" src="../Dont_Link/StraightEasyLacing7em.gif" title="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 5"/>
        <b>
         Across and Back
        </b>
        <p>
         The second from bottom straight section runs across right-to-left (on top), then left-to-right (underneath),
	passing under itself on the right side. The underlying section is fairly well hidden by the straight section on
	top.
        </p>
        <p>
         This is the preferred method for dress shoes with three pairs of eyelets, with the lacing running across and back
	under the middle pair of eyelets.
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr class="tb1">
       <td>
        <img alt="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 6" src="../Dont_Link/StraightEasyLacing7fm.gif" title="Straight Easy Lacing Odd Variation 6"/>
        <b>
         Twice Through Top Eyelet
        </b>
        <p>
         The end that runs straight up the left side (blue end) and the end that snakes left and right through all the
	remaining eyelets (yellow end)
         <b>
          both
         </b>
         emerge through the top left eyelet. One of those ends then runs straight across and out through the top
	right eyelet.
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
