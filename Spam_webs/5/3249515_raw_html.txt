       In south Asia, only the wealthy complete a formal education. Ben Doherty meets some girls from poor families trying to buck the trend.

       <img alt="Farha Khan, seven, (at left) studies in the tiny one room family home in Govindpuri she shares with her parents and six-year-old sister Farhana." src="/content/dam/images/3/a/y/m/3/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.3axz3.png/1403888401000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Farha Khan, seven, (at left) studies in the tiny one room family home in Govindpuri she shares with her parents and six-year-old sister Farhana.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Graham Crouch
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       In the afternoon quiet in Bhai Kot, school is in on the cool concrete floor of Shumaila's family home. She is seated, her covered head bowed over a page of simple maths equations, red pen in hand. Fifteen-year-old Shumaila is the teacher of this barefoot class.
      </p>
      <p>
       Outside, in the unpaved streets of this Punjab village near Lahore, in Pakistan's north-east, there are children at work. They are in fields helping with harvests, on rubbish dumps scavenging for plastics to sell, working alongside parents doing stitching piecework, or shelling nuts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pakistan has one of the highest rates of out-of-school children in the world, especially of girls. It's estimated that more than nine million children have dropped out of school in this country, or have never been. Shumaila is trying to keep the children in her class from becoming another statistic of Pakistan's failing education system. And she is trying to avoid becoming one herself.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Farhana Khan walks her five-year-old neighbour Aaliya home from school to the tiny one room family home in Govindpuri she shares with her parents and sister Farhana." src="/content/dam/images/3/a/y/m/1/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.3axz3.png/1403888401000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Farhana Khan walks her five-year-old neighbour Aaliya home from school to the tiny one room family home in Govindpuri she shares with her parents and sister Farhana.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Graham Crouch
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       Shumaila is student and teacher at once, not out of choice, but out of necessity. Two years ago, her parents told her they could no longer afford to send her to school. The peppercorn school fees were not prohibitive; it was the cost of simply getting there.
      </p>
      <div class="ad" id="adspot-300x250-pos3">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script type="text/javascript">
        titan.requestConditionalAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       Finding the 2000 rupees ($22) each month so Shumaila could ride the daily rickshaw with her classmates to school 10 kilometres away was beyond the family's limited means. She was, her parents said, to stay home and work, stitching embroidery in clothes to earn money for the family.
      </p>
      <p>
       Shumaila resisted. "I threatened to run away from home, to my grandmother's house. I said, 'I will get an education there. I will pay for my own education, I will teach to make money.'" Every morning, between 8.30am and 2pm, Shumaila is a student for the first session of classes at Bado Ki Sani school (overwhelmed and under- resourced, most schools in Pakistan run two sessions a day). At the end of each day, she packs up her books and clambers with her classmates into a rickshaw for the ride home. There, she has barely 15 minutes for a modest lunch before her first lessons, as teacher, begin.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Girls take an exam at Bado Ki Sani school in Pakinstan's Punjab." src="/content/dam/images/3/a/y/m/0/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.3axz3.png/1403888401000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Girls take an exam at Bado Ki Sani school in Pakinstan's Punjab.
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       For two hours, she tutors five children, aged between 10 and 12, in English, maths and science, subjects she is still learning herself. From 5pm to 6pm she teaches another group of 18 students lessons from the Koran. For every student in her charge, she earns 150 rupees a month - enough, just, to make school affordable for now, and to contribute a little to the household income. In winter, it is dark before she has time to turn to her own homework. If it's not too cold, she studies by the moonlight on the terrace of her house. If it is, she works inside by the glow of a battery-powered lamp, for there is no electricity. This afternoon, Shumaila has set her students a series of simple maths equations. No one, she says, as she corrects the work of a small boy whose tattered bag bears the Pakistani flag, taught her to be a teacher. "I just follow my books and the example of my teachers at school.
      </p>
      <p>
       Shumaila is one of six siblings. Her older sister left school young. No one in her family has ever progressed as far as she has, to year 10. But for Shumaila and her younger siblings to go to school - her brother recently won a trophy and a collection of books for his achievement in year 4 - her family goes without. "Fruit and milk and meat, they are only in our imagination now," Shumaila says. "The choice we have is between eating and going to school. Often we are hungry.
      </p>
      <p>
       Uncles tell her she should not be at school at all, that girls are unfit for education. They tell Shumaila's mother, Jamila, that the right and proper thing is for her to save money for her daughter's dowry, so she can be married off, rather than waste it on education for a girl. Shumaila ignores the hostility. "They cannot stop me from going," she says.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imagePortrait">
       <img alt="Shahajadi (at bottom) with friends in Pairabondh, Bangladesh." itemprop="image" src="/content/dam/images/3/a/y/l/z/image.related.articleLeadNarrow.300x0.3axz3.png/1403888401000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Shahajadi (at bottom) with friends in Pairabondh, Bangladesh.
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       But regardless of her resistance, Shumaila's family face a reality that she will soon have to stop going to school. "I want my daughter to have a respectable position in life," Jamila says. "And it is a matter of pride that my daughter goes to school. The problem is having enough money to send her.
      </p>
      <p>
       Jamila's husband is a daily wage labourer. The work comes and goes with the seasons, the crops, and the vicissitudes of a fragile rural economy. It pays poorly even when it can be found. "Shumaila wants to be a doctor. Soon, I regret, I will have to tell her that she cannot. She must stop her education because we cannot afford it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Shumaila, previously a picture of seraphic calm as she moves about her "classroom", is visibly upset by this. "I just want my parents to say to me once, 'We want you to be a doctor, too.' I will study harder, I will find a way, but I need them to support me.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bado Ki Sani school is the reason Shumaila can even dream of being a doctor. The school is run by non-government-organisation CARE, which took over the failed state-run school a decade ago and has steadily transformed it since. Fewer than 2 per cent of people pay tax in Pakistan, so there is little money for governments to pay for anything (save the notable exception of a massive, well-resourced army). Hospitals are ignored, infrastructure projects are abandoned and, perhaps most of all, schools suffer.
      </p>
      <p>
       When CARE arrived at Bado Ki Sani, the school had only two run-down classrooms and three overwhelmed teachers for 112 children. There were new classrooms, but they were locked because the contractor who built them thought he hadn't been paid enough. So the kids sat outside, through Pakistan's short, sharp winters and long, baking summers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Few lessons were ever taught - teaching was more a form of crowd control - and besides, there were precious few books to teach from, anyway. Students had neither pencils nor notebooks.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the community's urging, and with the government's blessing, CARE took over the school (smashing open the locks on the closed classrooms) and began teaching. A decade on, there are now 33 teachers and nearly 1000 students, who come from nine villages, some as far as 20 kilometres away.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bado Ki Sani boasts one of the best records in Punjab state in government-run exams, and Shumaila is one of its most promising students. For now. Across the three largest countries of south Asia - India, Pakistan and Bangladesh - more than 22 million primary-school aged children are not in school. With public attention and legislative change over the last decade, enrolment rates have improved dramatically. But being enrolled is not the same as going to school: the challenge now is keeping children in school, and giving them a reason to be there. In Pakistan, while 95 per cent of children enrol in year 1, only 45 per cent will still be in class at the end of their primary years. In Bangladesh, the figure is 55 per cent; in India, approaching 60 per cent.
      </p>
      <p>
       Across the subcontinent, somewhere near half of all primary schoolchildren drop out, most because the quality of education, especially outside of cities, is abysmal. After five years of primary school, 50 per cent of children cannot count or read to a year 2 level, and families, weighing the earning potential of a 12-year-old girl or boy against costly, apparently wasted, days in school, opt for putting their kids to work.
      </p>
      <p>
       The benefits of schooling on employment and income are immediately apparent. But the less obvious, broader impacts of an education, especially on the lives of girls in the developing world, are extraordinarily profound. Girls in schools saves lives. In south Asia, a girl born to a literate mother is 50 per cent more likely to survive to the age of five. For every extra year a girl can stay in school, her average income increases by 20 per cent. Girls who finish eight years of school are four times less likely to be married as children (there were 14 million child marriages of girls in south Asia last year). When girls are educated, birth rates fall, child health and nutrition improves, household incomes rise and are better spent on food and further education, and domestic violence drops markedly.
      </p>
      <p>
       Says Gordon Brown, the former British PM and now the UN's special envoy on education, "Progress towards securing girls' education has stalled because we have failed to reach the most marginalised: the child labourer, the child bride, the trafficked child, and the girl discriminated against for her gender.
      </p>
      <p>
       In her home in a Delhi slum, a six-year-old who we will call "Roshni" plays absent-mindedly with the scar on her right foot, a burn that stubbornly refuses to heal. She was burned by the man who raped her. He held a knife over a stove flame before pressing it against her skin, threatening her that if she told anyone about what he was about to do he would come back and harm her again. He then drugged her, and dragged her to the upstairs terrace of the crowded slum tenement where they both live and raped her.
      </p>
      <p>
       Roshni talks matter-of-factly about the attack she endured: "On that day, my mother went to buy vegetables, that man hurt me with the knife, then he gave me some medicine and took me on the roof. Then he beat me and did the wrong thing.
      </p>
      <p>
       The report filed with police is far more graphic. The attack has left her badly injured, and suffering herpes, which returns when her family runs out of money to pay for the medicines she needs. But she is scarred beyond her physical wounds. "She is different now. She never leaves me," Roshni's mother, Chand Biwi, says. "She wakes up in the night-time screaming.
      </p>
      <p>
       Roshni cannot hope for professional treatment for the physical and bodily trauma she has endured. But her family are desperately trying to restore some normality to her life. They want her back in school. "She is fearful of going to school," says Biwi. "Before this incident she used to go fearlessly and was enjoying her lessons. But now she does not take any interest in study.
      </p>
      <p>
       Her teacher at Govindpuri's MCD School, Australian volunteer Ravneet Mundi, says Roshni, when she can be coaxed into going to school, won't leave the side of her eight-year-old sister, Shabana. Roshni refuses to sit in her classroom or participate in lessons. Despite her fears, Roshni wants to be able to return: "I want to be a police [officer]. Police beat people and put them in jail." Says Biwi, "I want my daughters to be educated. I am illiterate. I want them to have a better life than me.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Roshni's crowded slum neighbourhood, her schoolmates always walk the kilometre-and-a-half to school, through the dark, narrow alleyways, in the company of their mothers. Ansri Khatun and her family are migrants to Delhi, from the poor eastern state of Jharkhand. They are five, sharing a single room with a concrete floor, and a lone wooden bed. Ansri's husband works as a tailor - the money he can earn in the capital is far greater than anything he could hope to make at home, and the chances for their children are greater here, too. But rents are expensive and, without land, so is food. They are alone here.
      </p>
      <p>
       If the family is forced back to return to Jharkhand, seven-year-old daughter Farha's education will end. "It is too costly here," Ansri says. "Farha is a good student, but I don't think she can go to school any more when we go there. In our village, no girls go to school.
      </p>
      <p>
       Farha proudly speaks the fundamental English she has learnt: her name, her parents' names, her village. She goes to school every day, she explains, because she wants to be a doctor: "I want to save people's lives.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bangladesh has long known strong women. Two women have dominated the prime ministership for decades, but as long as a century ago, the issue of girls' education was a touchstone for those seeking equality for women. In 1909, feminist writer and social activist Begum Rokeya established the first school for Muslim girls in what was then British India. She spent a lifetime championing girls' education, telling the mullahs who opposed girls in schools that they didn't know the religion they professed to espouse, and accusing them of corrupting the faithful.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rokeya was born in the village of Pairabondh, in a poor part of rural northern Bangladesh, and the school there bears her name. But the village is also a demonstration of how the battle for female education is not yet won. It is a holiday today, and Shahajadi is climbing a tree with her friends in the playground. "I am in 5th class," she says in English, which she learns at school, "but my favourite lesson is Bangla class.
      </p>
      <p>
       Shahajadi's mother never went to school, and her older sister was married at 13, dropping out as soon as her engagement was finalised. Shahajadi expects the same will happen to her. "I would like to stay in school to pass class 10, but I don't know if I will be allowed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Farak Duj, a project officer with Save The Children, has worked in girls' education in Bangladesh for 15 years. "Child marriage is still our major problem, in all areas of Bangladesh, but it is in the rural areas we see it most often." Two-thirds of girls are married before 18, one-third before 15. "Parents prefer boys for higher education. Parents will spend money on a boy, because if he has education he can get a better job and earn money for the family. Girls will get married and they will leave the house, they will live with another family.
      </p>
      <p>
       Gordon Brown says the most substantial change in places like Bangladesh will come from within the country itself, and from those most affected. "In terms of cultural barriers, we find it is very often in supporting young people themselves that progress can be made, so we must look at encouraging, for example, the growth of the so-called 'wedding busters', girls who have created 'child-marriage-free zones' in Bangladesh.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most powerful advocates for girls' education will be girls themselves: the Shumailas who will teach the girls of her village to read, the Shahajadis who will resist the pressure on her to abandon school for an early marriage. "What is most important is that girls themselves ... must have a say in this process," says Brown. "The voices of young people can become louder and more influential than those of the adults charged with protecting them."
      </p>
