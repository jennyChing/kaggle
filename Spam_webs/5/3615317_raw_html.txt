        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Can a small Bavarian town with a population of a little more than 20 000 people be the birthplace of two large world-famous sports brands? Both of them owned by two brothers who get into a fight, resulting in a bitter rivalry that lasts long after their deaths?
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         The answer to both questions is yes. Lets go back to 1922 in the town of Herzogenaurach (dont bother, you cant pronounce that) and follow the story of the Adidas brand.
        </p>
        <dl class="wp-caption foter-photo" id="foter-photo-figure" style="color: #888888; position: relative; font-size: 11px; font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; overflow: hidden; zoom: 1; padding: 4px; border: 1px solid #dddddd; border-top-left-radius: 3px; border-top-right-radius: 3px; border-bottom-right-radius: 3px; border-bottom-left-radius: 3px; width: 450px; float: none; margin: 5px auto 10px;">

        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The Family Business
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Success stories rarely get any more inspirational (albeit punctuated by controversies) than that. In the early 1920s Adolf and Rudolf Dassler launched a shoe company in their mothers laundry. Their first shoes were made out of whatever materials they found in post-World War I Europe, mostly war helmets and other military gear. Electricity was also scarce, so occasionally the two brothers had to use the power, generated by a stationary bicycle. None of that stopped them, however, and their business was soon on the rise. When Hitler came to power in 1933, both of them joined his party, but they always put their business first. In 1936 they persuaded African-American sprinter
         <a href="http://www.jesseowens.com/" target="_blank">
          Jesse Owens
         </a>
         to wear and compete with their shoes to the Olympics. He won four gold medals, giving international recognition to the Dassler shoes. A great push ahead for the company and a huge blow for Hitler, who wanted to use the event to demonstrate the superiority of the Aryan race. But when I said the story was controversial, I meant it.
        </p>
        <dl class="wp-caption foter-photo" id="foter-photo-figure" style="color: #888888; position: relative; font-size: 11px; font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; overflow: hidden; zoom: 1; padding: 4px; border: 1px solid #dddddd; border-top-left-radius: 3px; border-top-right-radius: 3px; border-bottom-right-radius: 3px; border-bottom-left-radius: 3px; width: 450px; float: none; margin: 5px auto 10px;">

        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The Split
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         The brothers lived in the same house, but their wives didnt get along, so there was constant tension in the air. They had numerous disputes but the last drop fell during World War II, when the town of Herzogenaurach came under Allied bombing. Adolf and his wife climbed into the bomb shelter which was already hosting Rudolf and his family. Thats when Adolf said: The dirty bastards are back again, referring to the Allied forces, but Rudolf apparently took that the wrong way. Soon after, the two brothers split the Dassler Brothers Shoe Company into two parts. Adi Dassler combined the first letters of his names to form the Adidas brand, but dont feel sorry for Rudolf, because he soon established Puma. This would be the beginning of a large-scale feud that was only resolved in 2009 when the two companies met each other for a friendly
         <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/business/8267709.stm" target="_blank">
          game of soccer
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The Evolution of the Three Stripes
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Adolf Dassler was facing a big challenge. The shoes he made were already popular, but with the company division and just 47 employees at hand, he had to establish a new brand that is both easily recognized and benefiting from the trust the previous one had built. He knew that a simplistic but distinctive logo design can aid in this endeavor, so the company started putting three stripes on all of its shoes. That way, whenever people used them in competitions, the logo would be visible from afar. The company also emphasized the now popular The Brand with the 3 stripes, recognizing how such a comparison was working to its benefit.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         By the beginning of the 1970s Adidas had already moved beyond footwear and was manufacturing casual shoes, sports apparel, gear, watches and looking to expand even more. Naturally, the growing number of products had to be reinforced by changing the logo. That brought the birth of the Trefoil logo in 1972, whose main purpose was to reflect the diversity of the Adidas brand. It displays three leaves, each shooting in a different direction, as if to point out the growth of the brand. Adolf Dassler and his wife Kathe, however, decided they wanted to keep the balance between expansion and tradition, so they intersected the leaves with the three horizontal stripes. Later the Trefoil logo became the corporate logo for Adidas and it is now used for its heritage products, such as the
         <a href="http://www.adidas.com/com/originals/" target="_blank">
          Adidas Originals
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <dl class="wp-caption foter-photo" id="foter-photo-figure" style="color: #888888; position: relative; font-size: 11px; font-family: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif; overflow: hidden; zoom: 1; padding: 4px; border: 1px solid #dddddd; border-top-left-radius: 3px; border-top-right-radius: 3px; border-bottom-right-radius: 3px; border-bottom-left-radius: 3px; width: 450px; float: none; margin: 5px auto 10px;">
         <dt class="wp-caption-dt">
          <a href="http://foter.com/photo/trefoil/">
           <img alt="trefoil l The Story of a Brand: Adidas" class="foter-photo mceItem" src="http://photos.foter.com/140/trefoil_l.jpg" style="display: block; width: 100%;" title="The Story of a Brand: Adidas"/>
          </a>
         </dt>
         <dd class="wp-caption-dd" style="padding: 0; margin: 0;">
          <span style="display: block; float: right;">
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/jakescreations/100216569/">
            jbelluch
           </a>
           /
           <a href="http://foter.com">
            Foter
           </a>
           /
           <a href="http://www.eduteka.pl/doc/cc-by-nc-nd">
            CC BY-NC-ND
           </a>
          </span>
         </dd>
        </dl>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Adidas present logo is another variation on the three-stripe design, showing remarkable dedication to its roots. It was first used for the Equipment line of products and designed by then Creative Director Peter Moore. The logo shows three slanted stripes, which look like a mountain when combined. The symbolism behind the mountain can be interpreted in many ways  the mountain is the aims, the goals and the challenges that lie ahead. Its as if Adidas is pushing its customers to set higher and higher goals for themselves.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 460px">
         <img alt="Adidas Logo The Story of a Brand: Adidas" class=" " height="250" src="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/20/Adidas_Logo.svg" title="The Story of a Brand: Adidas" width="450"/>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Source: Wikipedia
         </p>
        </div>
        <p style="text-align: justify;">
         Whatever the intention behind the logo, the fact is that you will be able to recognize it wherever you see it and that still remains among the best indicators of a brands success. Go pick a fight with your brother and launch that start-up!
        </p>
