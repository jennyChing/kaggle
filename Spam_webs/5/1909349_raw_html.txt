           <p>
            As the brain-boggling bombast of summer movie season fades, it's once again time to check in on the most interesting specimens currently squirming in the petri dish of potential Hollywood productions found in the latest adaptation news. The range of possible adaptations, as always, provokes surprise and skepticism as much as delight and excitement. Regardless, it's all just plain fun to think about, so here are nine of the most intriguing projects in the mix right now.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              <b>
               <i>
                <b>
                 <i>
                  The Stand
                 </i>
                </b>
                <br/>
               </i>
              </b>
             </i>
            </b>
            We snatch up any morsel of casting and/or production scuttlebutt on the long-gestating adaptation of Stephen King's epic novel
            <em>
             The Stand
            </em>
            like the desperate scavengers that we are. And this is a very tasty tidbit: Apparently, brand-new Oscar winner Matthew McConaughey has been offered the key role of Randall Flagg, the Devil-ish antagonist who corrals the country's last bad men to do battle with the healthier remnants of humanity after a plague has wiped out most of the population. "The Fault in Our Stars" director Josh Boone is helming.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              <b>
               <i>
                The Emperor's Children
               </i>
              </b>
              <br/>
             </i>
            </b>
            Claire Messud's 2006 bestseller has
            <a href="http://www.wordandfilm.com/2011/10/noah-baumbach-abandons-adaptation-of-claire-messuds-the-emperors-children-to-crazy-heart-director-scott-cooper/">
             passed through several sets of worthy filmmaking hands
            </a>
            , including those of writer-directors Noah Baumbach ("The Squid and the Whale") and Scott Cooper ("Crazy Heart"). The adaptation has suddenly landed with actress-turned-filmmaker Lake Bell ("In a World..."), who reportedly plans to keep Baumbach's script. Though the story of three highly educated thirty-something Manhattanites stalled out in the months before the September 11 terrorist attacks at one point had several stars attached, casting is again wide open.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              The Land of Steady Habits
             </i>
            </b>
            <br/>
            As a writer-director, Nicole Holofcener ("Enough Said," "Lovely &amp; Amazing") has thus far stuck to her own original ideas (though she did adapt Laura Lippman's 2003 novel
            <i>
             Every Secret Thing
            </i>
            for someone else to direct). That may change with Ted Thompson's recently published debut novel about a family man in his sixties questioning his comfortable life in suburban Connecticut, which Holofcener may now adapt for Fox Searchlight ("
            <a href="http://www.wordandfilm.com/2014/03/wes-andersons-grand-budapest-hotel-pays-homage-to-author-stefan-zweig/">
             The Grand Budapest Hotel
            </a>
            ," "Wild").
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              Fantastic Beasts &amp; Where to Find Them
             </i>
            </b>
            <br/>
            Harry Potter may temporarily be exhausted as a film character, but his world holds magic yet. J.K. Rowling's 2001 spin-off book, which purports to be a Hogwarts textbook, written by one Newt Scamander decades before Mr. Potter entered the world, is now getting its own film adaptation. Already scheduled for November 2016, the film has acquired a trusted hand to direct it: David Yates, who so expertly orchestrated four of the Potter films, including the final two "Deathly Hallows" installments.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             "Untitled Blade Runner Sequel"
            </b>
            <br/>
            The androids are apparently still dreaming ... The terribly prolific Ridley Scott has declared that he has a strong script (by Michael Green) ready for his long-discussed sequel to "Blade Runner," the 1982 sci-fi classic that featured Harrison Ford as an ex-cop bounty hunter assigned to exterminate human-seeming "replicants." This is exciting (if nerve-wracking) news for fans of the original, which was based on Philip K. Dick's 1968 novel
            <i>
             Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?
            </i>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             "The Destroyer"
            </b>
            <br/>
            Very few people are familiar with the 1985 action comedy "Remo Williams: The Adventure Begins," which was based on characters drawn from Warren Murphy and Richard Sapir's series of pulp novels about a New Jersey cop "murdered," abducted, and repurposed as an ass-kicking vigilante by a super-secret government agency. This terrible injustice may finally be remedied by "Kiss Kiss Bang Bang" auteur Shane Black, who has been tasked with directing a reinvention of the material for a new audience, with a screenplay co-written by "Fight Club" adapter Jim Uhls.
           </p>
           <p>
            "
            <b>
             Knights Of The Roundtable: King Arthur
            </b>
            "
            <br/>
            Warner Bros.' and director Guy Ritchie's plans to give the revamped-and-modernized "Sherlock Holmes" treatment to the legends of King Arthur are continuing apace. The Camelot-centered franchise hopeful, which already has a Summer 2016 release date, is in the process of nailing down tentpole vets Charlie Hunnam ("Pacific Rim") and Elizabeth Olsen ("Godzilla") as Arthur and Guinevere to join Idris Elba as Belvedere.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             "Ashecliffe"
            </b>
            <br/>
            Dennis Lehane's 2003 novel
            <i>
             Shutter Island
            </i>
            , which became a high-grossing Martin Scorsese-Leonardo DiCaprio thriller in 2010, is now being spun into a television show at HBO. Titled "Ashecliffe," after the hospital for the criminally insane where all of the novel's action takes place, the prequel series boasts Scorsese back in the director's chair for the pilot, which Lehane is writing himself.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              The Jungle Book(s)
             </i>
            </b>
            <br/>
            Oscar winners Christian Bale and Cate Blanchett have been cast, along with Tom Hollander, Naomie Harris, Eddie Marsan, and Peter Mullan, in "Jungle Book: Origins," to be directed by Andy Serkis ("Dawn of the Planet of the Apes"). Benedict Cumberbatch has already signed on to voice Shere Khan in the CGI/live-action hybrid being produced by Warner Bros. for an October 2016 release. Meanwhile, Jon Favreau's live-action/animated Disney version of the 1894 Rudyard Kipling classic, scheduled for release a year earlier, has lined up its own heavy hitters, including Scarlett Johansson, Sir Ben Kingsley, and Bill Murrary.
           </p>
