     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_folk_religion" title="Mesoamerican folk religion">
       Mesoamerican folk religion
      </a>
      , a
      <i>
       <b>
        Nagual
       </b>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       <b>
        Nahual
       </b>
      </i>
      (both pronounced [na'wal]) is a human being who has the power to transform either spiritually or physically into an animal form: most commonly
      <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">
       jaguar
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">
       puma
      </a>
      but also other animals such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">
       mules
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">
       birds
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">
       dogs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">
       coyotes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Such a Nagual is believed to use their powers for good or evil according to their personality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Specific beliefs vary, but the general concept of nagualism is pan-Mesoamerican. Nagualism is linked with pre-Columbian shamanistic practices through Preclassic Olmec depictions which are interpreted as human beings transforming themselves into animals. The system is linked with the
      <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_calendars" title="Mesoamerican calendars">
       Mesoamerican calendrical system
      </a>
      , used for divination rituals. The birth date often determines if a person will be a Nagual. Mesoamerican belief in
      <a href="/wiki/Tonal_(mythology)" title="Tonal (mythology)">
       tonalism
      </a>
      , wherein every person has an animal counterpart to which his life force is linked, is also part of the definition of nagualism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In English the word is often translated as "transforming witch", but translations without the negative connotations of the word
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Witch" title="Witch">
       witch
      </a>
      would be "transforming
      <a href="/wiki/Trickster" title="Trickster">
       trickster
      </a>
      " or "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shape_shifter" title="Shape shifter">
       shape shifter
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nagual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       nagual
      </i>
      derives from the
      <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
       Nahuatl
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       nahulli
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA for Nahuatl">
        /nawali/
       </a>
      </span>
      , an indigenous religious practitioner, identified by the Spanish as a 'magician'. The nagual is acquired along with the other characteristics of a person's birth day at birth. Each day is associated with an animal which has strong and weak aspects. A person born on "The Dog Day" would have both strong and weak 'Dog' aspects. In Nahuatl the word
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tonalli" title="Tonalli">
        tonalli
       </a>
      </i>
      was used to refer both to a day and to the animal associated with that day. The nagual is different, where the
      <i>
       tonal
      </i>
      is the day spirit proper, the nagual is the spirit familiar of the day. It is probable that the tonal represents the daytime aspect and the nagual the nighttime aspect of the tonalli, 'the things of the day'. Because practitioners of powerful magic were normally born on certain days related to animals with a strong or harmful aspect they would often have specific tonals such as the jaguar or puma. In Aztec mythology the God
      <a href="/wiki/Tezcatlipoca" title="Tezcatlipoca">
       Tezcatlipoca
      </a>
      was the protector of nagualism, because his tonal was the jaguar and he governed the distribution of wealth.
     </p>
     <p>
      In modern rural Mexico,
      <i>
       nagual
      </i>
      is sometimes synonymous with
      <i>
       brujo
      </i>
      ("witch"): one who is able to shapeshift into an animal at night, (normally into an
      <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">
       owl
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">
       bat
      </a>
      , or turkey) drink blood from human victims, steal property, cause disease, and the like.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some indigenous communities the position of Nagual is integrated into the religious hierarchy. The community knows who is a
      <i>
       Nagual
      </i>
      , tolerating, fearing and respecting them.
      <i>
       Nagualli
      </i>
      are hired to remove curses cast by other
      <i>
       nagualli
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In other communities the accusation of nagualism may result in violent attacks by the community towards the accused.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Western study of Nagualism was initiated by noted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist">
       archaeologist
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguist
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnologist" title="Ethnologist">
       ethnologist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Garrison_Brinton" title="Daniel Garrison Brinton">
       Daniel Garrison Brinton
      </a>
      who published
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.gutenberg.org/files/26426/26426-0.txt" rel="nofollow">
       "Nagualism: A Study in Native-American Folklore and History"
      </a>
      which chronicled historical interpretations of the word and those who practiced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nagualism" title="Nagualism">
       nagualism
      </a>
      in Mexico in 1894. He identified various beliefs associated with nagualism in some modern Mexican communities such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixe" title="Mixe">
       Mixe
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Nahua_peoples" title="Nahua peoples">
       Nahua
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Zapotec_peoples" title="Zapotec peoples">
       Zapotec
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Mixtec" title="Mixtec">
       Mixtec
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Subsequently many studies have described Nagualism in different Mesoamerican cultures such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoque" title="Zoque">
       Zoques
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Jakaltek_people" title="Jakaltek people">
       Jakaltek
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_people" title="K'iche' people">
       K'iche'
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Q%27eqchi%27_people" title="Q'eqchi' people">
       Q'eqchi'
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Tzeltal_people" title="Tzeltal people">
       Tzeltal Maya
      </a>
      . Among the Jacaltec, Naguals reinforce indigenism by punishing those who collaborate with non Mayas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Discussion continues on which degree nagualism represents a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">
       pre-Columbian
      </a>
      belief system or is modelled on European popular religion. Gustavo Correa,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      suggests nagualism is not
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">
       pre-Columbian
      </a>
      , arguing that it was wholly imported from Europe, where he compares it to the medieval belief in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Werewolves" title="Werewolves">
       werewolves
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      However, Indigenous sources of pre-Columbian origin such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh" title="Popol Vuh">
       Popol Vuh
      </a>
      describe concepts clearly related to modern Nagualism.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement">
         <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (January 2009)">
          dubious
         </span>
        </a>
        <span class="metadata">
         
         <a href="/wiki/Talk:Nagual#Dubious" title="Talk:Nagual">
          discuss
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . Nonetheless, the only version of this document which has survived was transcribed well after the Spanish Conquest and may contain concepts introduced by European missionaries.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Kaplan
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      concludes that, in Oaxaca, the belief in Naguals as evil shape shifting witches is common in both indigenous and mestizo populations. According to Kaplan, the belief in animal spirit companions is exclusively indigenous. These is certain for some groups and communities, but for others, as the mixes, chinantecos, triquis or tacuates, the who can can control their nahual or alterego are also protectors of the people and the natural resources and culture of the community, and highly revered but also feared.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       Nagual
      </i>
      was popularized in books by author
      <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Castaneda" title="Carlos Castaneda">
       Carlos Castaneda
      </a>
      .
     </p>
