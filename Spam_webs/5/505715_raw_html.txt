     <h1 class="article_title p-name">
      Art Directing with Looking Room
     </h1>
     <p class="article_byline p-author h-card">
      <a class="u-url" href="#author">
       <span class="avatar">
        <img alt="Mark Boulton" class="article_image u-photo" height="160" src="http://cloud.24ways.org/authors/markboulton280.jpg" width="160"/>

     <h3>
      Using photographic composition techniques to start to art direct on the template-driven web.
     </h3>
     <p class="lede">
      Think back to last night. There you are, settled down in front of the TV, watching your favourite soap opera, with nice hot cup of tea in hand. Did you notice  whilst engrossed in the latest love-triangle  that the cameraman has worked very hard to support your eyes natural movement on-screen? Hes carefully framed individual shots to create balance.
     </p>
     <p>
      Think back to last week. There you were, sat with your mates watching the big match. Did you notice that the cameraman frames the shot to go with the direction of play? A player moving right will always be framed so that he is on the far left, with plenty of room to run into.
     </p>
     <p>
      Both of these cameramen use a technique called Looking Room, sometimes called Lead Room. Looking Room is the space between the subject (be it a football, or a face), and the edge of the screen. Specifically, Looking Room is the negative space on the side the subject is looking or moving. The great thing is, its not just limited to photography, film or television; we can use it in web design too.
     </p>
     <h2>
      Basic Framing
     </h2>
     <p>
      Before we get into Looking Room, and how it applies to web, we need to have a look at some basics of photographic composition.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many web sites use imagery, or photographs, to enhance the content. But even with professionally shot photographs, without a basic understanding of framing or composition, you can damage how the image is perceived.
     </p>
     <p>
      A simple, easy way to make photographs more interesting is to fill the frame.
     </p>
     <p>
      Take this rather mundane photograph of a horse:
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <a href="http://flickr.com/photos/mikepedroncelli/114987037/">
       <img alt="Horse" height="525" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/1_horse.jpg" width="700"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      A typical point and click affair. But, we can work with this.
     </p>
     <p>
      By closely cropping, and filling the frame, we can instantly change the mood of the shot.
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <a href="http://flickr.com/photos/mikepedroncelli/114987037/">
       <img alt="cropped image of a horse" height="486" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/2_horsecropped.jpg" width="700"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ive also added Looking Room on the right of the horse. This is space that the horse would be walking into. It gives the photograph movement.
     </p>
     <h2>
      Subject, Space, and Movement
     </h2>
     <p>
      Generally speaking, a portrait photograph will have a subject and space around them. Visual interest in portrait photography can come from movement; how the eye moves around the shot. To get the eye moving, the photographer modifies the space around the subject.
     </p>
     <p>
      Look at this portrait:
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <a href="http://flickr.com/photos/laenulfean/548688957/">
       <img alt="Portrait" height="509" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/3_portrait.jpg" width="700"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      The photography has framed the subject on the right, allowing for whitespace, or Looking Room, in the direction the subject is looking. The framing of the subject (1), with the space to the left (2)  the Looking Room  creates movement, shown by the arrow (3).
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <img alt="Annotated portrait" height="509" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/4_annotatedportrait.gif" width="700"/>
     </p>
     <p>
      Note the subject is not framed centrally (shown by the lighter dotted line).
     </p>
     <p>
      If the photographer had framed the subject with equal space either side, the resulting composition is static, like our horse.
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <img alt="Centred portrait" height="509" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/5_centredportrait.gif" width="700"/>
     </p>
     <p>
      If the photographer framed the subject way over on the left, as she is looking that way, the resulting whitespace on the right leads a very uncomfortable composition.
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <img alt="Left portrait" height="509" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/6_leftportrait.gif" width="700"/>
     </p>
     <p>
      The root of this discomfort is what the framing is telling our eye to do. The subject, looking to the left, suggests to us that we should do the same. However, the Looking Room on the right is telling our eye to occupy this space. The result is a confusing back and forth.
     </p>
     <h2>
      How Looking Room applies to the web
     </h2>
     <p>
      We can apply the same theory to laying out a web page or application. Taking the three same elements  Subject, Space, and resulting Movement  we can guide a users eye to the elements we need to. As designers, or content editors, framing photographs correctly can have a subtle but important effect on how a page is visually scanned. Take this example:
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <img alt="BBC home" height="397" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/7_bbchome.jpg" width="699"/>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk">
       <span class="caps">
        BBC
       </span>
      </a>
      homepage uses great photography as a way of promoting content. Here, they have cropped the main photograph to guide the users eye into the content.
     </p>
     <p>
      By applying the same theory, the designer or content editor has applied considerable Looking Room (2) to the photograph to create balance with the overall page design, but also to create movement of the users eye toward the content (1)
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <img alt="BBC home 2" height="397" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/8_bbchome2.jpg" width="699"/>
     </p>
     <p>
      If the image was flipped horizontally. The Looking Room is now on the right. The subject of the photograph is looking off the page, drawing the users eye away from the content. Once again, this results in a confusing back and forth as your eye fights its way over to the left of the page.
     </p>
     <p class="image">
      <img alt="BBC home 3" height="397" src="http://media.24ways.org/2008/05/9_bbchome3.jpg" width="699"/>
     </p>
     <h2>
      A little bit of Art Direction
     </h2>
     <p>
      Art Direction can be described as the act or process of managing the visual presentation of content. Art Direction is difficult to do on the web, because content and presentation are, more often than not, separated. But where there are images, and when we know the templates that those images will populate, we can go a little way to bridging the gap between content and presentation.
     </p>
     <p>
      By understanding the value of framing and Looking Room, and the fact that it extends beyond just a good looking photograph, we can start to see photography playing more of an integral role in the communication of content.
     </p>
     <p>
      We wont just be populating templates. Well be art directing.
     </p>
