  <p>
   <b>
    Explanation:
   </b>
   Look up into
   <a href="http://science.nasa.gov/headlines/y2004/
19mar_planets.htm">
    the sky tonight
   </a>
   and without a telescope
or binoculars you might have
   <a href="http://astronomy.swin.edu.au/staff/gmackie/billions.html">
    a
view
   </a>
   like this one of Moon, planets and stars.
   <a href="http://www.pikespeakphoto.com/planets.html">
    The lovely
photo
   </a>
   was taken on March 23rd,
and captures the crescent Moon on the horizon with Venus above it.

Both brilliant celestial bodies are over-exposed.

Farther above Venus is the tinted glow of Mars with
the Pleiades star cluster just to the red planet's right.

The V-shaped arrangement
   <a href="ap040304.html">
    of
stars
   </a>
   to the left of Mars is the Hydaes star cluster.

Bright red giant
   <a href="http://www.astro.uiuc.edu/~kaler/sow/
aldebaran.html">
    Aldebaran
   </a>
   , not itself a member of the Hyades
cluster, marks the top left of the V.
   <a href="http://www.griffithobs.org/planetsgather.html">
    During
the next week
   </a>
   , all five naked-eye planets,
Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn, along with
the Moon
   <a href="http://skyandtelescope.com/observing/objects/planets/
article_1226_1.asp">
    will grace the evening sky
   </a>
   together - a
lunar and planetary spectacle that can be
   <a href="http://www.spaceweather.com/">
    enjoyed by skygazers
   </a>
   around the world.

But look just after sunset, low on the western horizon,
to see
   <a href="ap030412.html">
    Mercury
   </a>
   before it sets.

The next similar
   <a href="ap000524.html">
    gathering
   </a>
   of the planets will be in 2008.
  </p>
