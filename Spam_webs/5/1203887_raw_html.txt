        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         Finding time and space for songwriting
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         By
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.songwritingscene.com/author/sharon-goldman/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Sharon Goldman
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <div class="shareaholic-canvas" data-app="share_buttons" data-app-id="13095816" data-link="http://www.songwritingscene.com/2010/02/finding-time-and-space-for-songwriting/" data-summary="" data-title="Finding time and space for songwriting">
        </div>
        <p>
         Im sitting on an Amtrak train to Washington, D.C., thinking about how the vast majority of us have jobs. Whether youre a high-powered executive, a stay-at-home mom, a touring musician, a waitress or an administrative assistant, we all do what it takes to pay the bills and take care of ourselves and those we love.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unfortunately, that doesnt always leave as much time for songwriting as wed like, right? Sure, Id love to break out my guitar and digital recorder here in the Quiet Car, but Im pretty sure my laptop-loving neighbors wouldnt be thrilled. Plus, theres several work papers I need to read and a meeting to prepare forshould I even be writing this blog post?
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-181">
         </span>
         In any case, I asked some SongwritingScene.com readers how they find the time to get their songwriting going amidst the busy hum of lifeheres what they had to say:
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Willis T. Spaggwater:
         </strong>
         Patience, and lack of sleep.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Petra Cook:
         </strong>
         My day job at the moment is homeschooling momso I either work around whats shes doing or include her in it if she wants.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Martin Rodriguez:
         </strong>
         Having a job where I can work on lines WHILE I work.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Ronnie Gainey:
         </strong>
         I take my guitar into the bathroom and play very quietly so my kids dont hear me.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Brian Kendig
         </strong>
         : Usually start them at night, sometimes in the morning and then I work on songs driving to and from work.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          John Murray
         </strong>
         : Avoid as many responsibilities as possible without alienating loved ones.
        </p>
        <p>
         For myself, I feel incredibly lucky that I work at home these daysso during my breaks Ill often pick up my guitar and noodle around with some chord progressions or write a few lines. But I know it can be tough when youre juggling job, family and, well, life!
        </p>
        <p>
         What are some ways you work songwriting into your busy schedule?
        </p>
