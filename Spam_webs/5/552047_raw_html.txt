      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        Untold amounts of concrete cover the planet, and at least six billion cubic yards of it are produced annually. If thats not alarming enough, most concrete only has a lifespan of 60 to 80 years.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        That means all those buildings, bridges, and other infrastructure will have to be fortified or outright replaced sooner or later.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        To do this would require a more deft approach than normal demolition, which maximizes wreckage, resulting in a whole lot of waste and mess.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot offers a possible solution: "smart deconstruction."
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        A conceptual project by designer Omer Haciomerog, the robot would make it possible to gently deconstruct and recycle concrete right on-site.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        The robot is fitted with an articulate arm and head, which spits out water jets strong enough to break down sections of concrete.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        It separates the water from the cement and aggregate, and funnels it for reuse. The aggregate is pulverized into pebbles and packaged to be sent off to concrete pre-cast stations.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        The concealed rebar structure is excavated and cleaned of rust, then recycled.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>
     <figcaption>
      <div class="title">
       The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot
      </div>
      <div class="caption">
       <p>
        The most ingenious aspect of the design is that it allows the recycling process to be done in real-time and on-site, ensuring that the materials are packaged immediately following deconstruction. A fleet of EROs could take down a building in no time, while not wasting any of its structure.
       </p>
      </div>
     </figcaption>

             <div class="twelve columns">
              <h1 class="title" itemprop="title headline">
               Recycling Robot Of The Future "Erases" Concrete Buildings
              </h1>
             </div>
             <span class="deck pull-three" itemprop="deck description">
              <p>
               A conceptual robot makes it possible to intelligently deconstruct a building, without any of the fuss or mess.
              </p>
             </span>
            </div>
            <div class="tools row collapse">
             <div class="twelve columns hide-on-print">
              <div class="share-box">
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </header>
           <div class="row collapse">
            <div class="body prose twelve columns" itemprop="body articleBody">
             <p>
              Theres too much concrete in the world to accurately quantify it, though estimates put
              <em>
               annual
              </em>
              production of the stuff at about 6 billion cubic yards. Its an alarming situation, to be sure, with potentially devastating environmental effects. Consider that concrete under normal conditions has a lifespan of just 60 to 80 yearsmeaning that a significant number of the worlds buildings and bridges will have to be upgraded, if not entirely rebuilt, within our lifetimes.
             </p>
             <p>
              How, exactly, will that be accomplished? Surely not through normal demolition, which would entail untold sums of waste, noise, mess, and expense. The ERO Concrete Recycling Robot, a portable concrete-eating machine, proposes an answer.
             </p>
             <p>
              Developed by designer
              <a href="http://www.omerh.com/" target="_blank">
               Omer Haciomeroglu
              </a>
              , the conceptual robot "erases" concrete structures piece meal without any of the waste or dust associated with cut-and-dry demolition methods. It has the ability to separate concrete from rebar and recycle the materials right on-site before cataloging them for reuse.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//d.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/codesign/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2013/07/1673081-inline-ero-mid-closed-open-grey.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              In order to achieve the most efficient method of demolition, Haciomeroglu writes, "the process had to start with separation on the spot." The difference lay in transitioning from "brutal pulverizing to smart deconstruction." The intelligent way, according to Haciomeroglu, is to gradually pick apart the structure from the top down.
             </p>
             <p>
              That starts with a very enlightened device. The EROs design looks like a next-generation Dyson appliance, only larger and built of presumably more durable material. The robot features a highly articulate mechanical arm fitted with a concrete-guzzling head, and a "vacuum" chamber made portable by Dyson-like balls. The latter enable the ERO to nimbly traverse a construction site as it scans the ground in search for optimal entry routes.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//d.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/codesign/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2013/07/1673081-inline-ero-final-back-02-logolu.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              A hose plugs right into the base of the ERO, pumping highly pressurized jets of water through the robots head that break concrete down into its constituent elements. The aggregate is separated from the cement slurry and filtered; its pulverized into pebbles, packaged, labeled, and shipped off to concrete pre-cast stations. The water extracted from the concrete is captured and reused to clean excavated steel rebar of dust and rust.
             </p>
             <p>
              The goal: "Every bit of the load-bearing structure is [rendered] reusable for new building blocks." Ambitious, and for an untested design, perhaps overly so. Even so, the potential built into the ERO is huge, and if realized in the "near feature," as Haciomeroglu predicts, it would prove groundbreaking.
             </p>
            </div>
           </div>
