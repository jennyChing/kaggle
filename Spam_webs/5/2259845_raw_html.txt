            Discover the wonders of winter snow
           </h1>

          <div class="article-content clearfix">
           <p>
            Its winter and that means snow for many northern climes. And while all snow is cold and white, the winter wonders it creates are pretty diverse. Be it the towering frozen walls of Japans Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route, high altitude spiked fields of the snow formations called penitentes
            <em>
             ,
            </em>
            or plains of wandering snow rollerssnow is much more than just flakes. Check out the assorted snow-pourri below.
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/40/410640.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            These snow walls along the Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route are upwards of 65 feet high. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/zipckr/">
             Pietro Zanarini
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/38/410638.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            First opened in 1971, the route connects many scenic areas in Japans North Alpine mountains, but the snow walls are a big draw in themselves. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/25259860/">
             Digicacy
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/43/410643.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            Buses and pedestrians share the road so visitors can get up close to the cold stuff. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/tsuda/">
             Tsuda
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/44/410644.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            This region receives one of the heaviest snow falls in the world, 23 feet on average per year. Terrain and snow drift assure that the snow wall area gets more than triple that amount. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/jsteph/">
             Jsteph
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/45/410645.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            The snow spikes above, called penitentes, are naturally formed. They can occur anywhere theres snow and sun but are seen most often high in the mountains. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/ruesch/">
             DreamX
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/46/410646.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            Penitentes are made when the sun hits a snow field and causes the snow to transform directly to vapor, skipping the liquid stage. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/ponyboy101/">
             Houston Marsh
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/47/410647.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            The snow spires can range from less than an inch up to 16 feet high. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/redteam/">
             RedTeam
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/48/410648.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            The frosty doughnuts above are a rare, natural phenomena called snow rollers. Image by
            <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org">
             WikiCommons
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/50/410650.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            Gravity or wind sets the snow roller creation in motion. Just like making a good snow ball, the snow must be slightly wet to stick. Image by
            <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/gordothegeek/">
             Gord Campbell
            </a>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <img alt="" src="http://static.grindtv.com/images/1/00/41/06/49/410649.jpg"/>
            <br/>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            Snow rollers can be ball-like or noodle-shaped. As long as theyre cylindrical with a hollow center they earn the name. Image by
            <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org">
             WikiCommons
            </a>
           </blockquote>
