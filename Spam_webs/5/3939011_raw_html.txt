       A Letter to My Younger Self

    <div class="article__content">
     <p>
      <em>
       <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:1858_Bf._Orleans-Frankreich.JPG" style="background-color: initial;">
        Image:
       </a>
       <span style="background-color: initial;">
        overseas letter, 1858
        <br/>
       </span>
      </em>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dear 2009 Me,
     </p>
     <p>
      Well you've done it. You started graduate school and, at two months in, you are just beginning to get the hang of things. By now you've figured out that the coffee from the cafe downstairs is terrible, but at least this end of campus has all the good food trucks (spoiler alert: a Thai food truck is about to open that will blow your mind). You're meeting people who will turn out to be incredible colleagues and friends. They will be there for you in a few months when you go through a rough breakup, and will dance at your wedding in just a few years (and dog-sit for your
      <a href="https://chroniclevitae.com/news/698-want-work-life-balance-get-a-puppy">
       awesome puppy
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      You've also developed a few organizational habits already, even though it's been a chaotic start to the semester. I'm writing to let you know how unbelievably helpful those habits are going to be over the next five years. Now that I'm sitting here in 2014 writing the dissertation -- editing it, actually -- I've been thinking about how much time and grief some of them have saved us (you and me) over the years.
     </p>
     <p>
      So I just wanted to take a break from all the red ink to say thanks for a few key things:
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Thank you for summarizing everything.
      </strong>
      You know how you are spending what seems like an hour trying to boil down each scholarly article you read to just three or four sentences? And then you saved the articles and the summaries on your computer? THANK YOU. I've been rereading so many of those summaries lately. I really can't recall the details of those first-semester readings, but when I had to write the lit-review section of my dissertation last month, it was so awesome to pull up those old theory pieces and have handy summaries right there. They were also a huge help while studying for comprehensive exams, and again when prepping for orals. It might feel like it takes an hour to write one of those summaries, but really it only takes a few minutes. And it has made such a huge difference.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Thanks for tagging every article the same way.
      </strong>
      By the time you start writing the diss, you will have exactly one gazillion articles stored on your computer. I know that right now you are organizing everything into folders, but that system isn't going to work out. Eventually you are just going to start dumping every PDF into a folder called "readings." But since you tagged everything by the author's last name and the publication date, I can still find stuff with a quick Spotlight search on the beautiful iMac you're going to treat yourself to after you do your fieldwork.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Thank you (sort of) for picking Zotero.
      </strong>
      If memory serves, you are just now weighing the decision to start using Zotero, a free and open-source software program for managing research materials. There certainly are a lot of choices when it comes to citation managers -- Endnote, Refworks, Mendeley. And you know what? Those others might be better than Zotero; its certainly not perfect. But just picking a citation manager early on, and sticking to it, has meant that every works cited section that you write -- including the mother of them all, the one for your dissertation, which I'm working on now in between revisions -- will only take a few minutes. (OK so it takes a little longer than that, since consistent formatting is not Zotero's forte. But still it's better than nothing). Moreover, it means that I now have five years' worth of citations all in the same place. And I didnt lose any over the years by trying to switch from one program to another.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Thanks for backing up your documents.
      </strong>
      You've already gotten into the habit of emailing drafts to yourself, and that's great. In a few months, you'll get religious about maintaining a full hard-drive backup. Well guess what? It's going to save your butt. You're going to have three major hard-drive crashes in the next five years. The first one is going to be really painful (you'll have those email copies, but this first crash is the reason you join the Church of Time Machine, a backup software application for Apple computers). But the two hard-drive crashes after that won't be so bad because you'll have a full backup of your entire computer. And now that I'm writing the dissertation, I know that I won't lose five years' worth of work, including all those summaries and that cumulative Zotero library, because it's all on an external disk. Which is great, because with the defense date looming, I don't have time to recreate a week's worth of work, let alone five years' worth.
     </p>
     <p>
      I know graduate school is overwhelming right now. It's going to get harder, so buck up. And you're going to make a bunch of mistakes. But these are a few things that worked out great. So thanks for that. Now it's back to editing. It's hard to believe that in just a few weeks it will all be over.
     </p>
     <p>
      XO,
      <br/>
      2014 Me
     </p>

      </em>
     </p>
