     <h1>
      History and Taxonomy of DistilledSpirits
     </h1>
     <p class="uppercase centered" style="margin-bottom:30px">
      June 10, 2002

      <a class="text_color" href="/misc">
       Miscellaneous
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      For nearly 3,000 years people have been making potent spirits for medicinal and recreational purposes. In recent history, worldwide distribution has made it possible for us to taste beverages from around the world. What follows is a surface-level overview of the evolution and classification these products. See
      <a href="#spirits-glossary">
       the glossary
      </a>
      for help with any unfamiliar terms.
     </p>
     <h2>
      The Distillation Process
     </h2>
     <p>
      The basic distillation procedure is the same regardless of the spirit. Alcohol is not "created" by distillation, just concentrated. A weak alcoholic beverage such as wine or beer is heated to boiling in a still (see
      <a href="#spirits-glossary">
       illustrations
      </a>
      below). Since the various constituents of the resulting vapor (like water, ethyl, methyl, and isopropyl alcohols) will vaporize and condense at different temperatures, they may be selectively extracted to create a new mixture which may then be further aged and/or flavored by the distiller. The different kinds of stills (e.g.,
      <a href="#glossary-potstill">
       pot still
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="#glossary-columnstill">
       column still
      </a>
      ) function in different ways and result in products of vastly different makeup and taste, but the basic process is the same: slowly boil the liquid and keep the vapors you want.
     </p>
     <div id="spirits-table">
      <div class="level-1 first" id="primitives">
       <h3>
        The Primitive Distillates
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        800
        <span class="smallcaps">
         BC
        </span>
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        various ferments
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        Around 800
        <span class="smallcaps">
         BC
        </span>
        distilled alcoholic beverages were being made in Asia. These included:
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <span class="label">
          Skhou:
         </span>
         In the Caucasus, from kefir (mare's milk)
        </li>
        <li>
         <span class="label">
          Sochou/Shochu:
         </span>
         In Japan, from sake (rice)
        </li>
        <li>
         <span class="label">
          Saut/Sautchoo:
         </span>
         In China, from tehoo (rice, millet)
        </li>
        <li>
         <span class="label">
          Arrack:
         </span>
         In India, from toddy (rice with molasses or palm sap)
        </li>
        <li>
         <span class="label">
          Arika:
         </span>
         By Tartars, from koumiss (mare's milk)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="mead">
       <h3>
        Distilled Mead
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Britain, 500
        <span class="smallcaps">
         AD
        </span>
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        mead (honey)
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="aquavini">
       <h3>
        Aqua Vini
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Spain, 1200s
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        wine
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        Spanish forerunner of brandy.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="brandy">
       <h3>
        Brandy
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Italy, 1000s
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        fruit wine (unaged)
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        The word comes from the Dutch "brandewijn" meaning "burnt wine."
       </p>
       <div class="level-2 first" id="grapebrandy">
        <h3>
         Grape Brandy
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         likely the first Western distilled beverage
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         grape wine
        </p>
        <div class="level-3 first" id="cognac">
         <h3>
          Cognac
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Cognac, France, 1500s
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          wine (7.5%
          <span class="smallcaps">
           ABV
          </span>
          , high acidity)
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Pot double-distill to 70%
          <span class="smallcaps">
           ABV
          </span>
          , dilute to 50%, age in oak 2 years (minimum) to 5 years or more (Very Superior Old Pale--
          <span class="smallcaps">
           VSOP
          </span>
          ). Dilute (if necessary) to 40%
          <span class="smallcaps">
           ABV
          </span>
          , color with caramel, add a small amount of sugar for taste.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="armagnac">
         <h3>
          Armagnac
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Armagnac region, France, 1411 (first mention)
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          wine
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Pot single-distill to 53%
          <span class="smallcaps">
           ABV
          </span>
          , age in sappy, strong black oak. Outside of France, armagnac is sometimes made in a column still, and with caramel syrup added.
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          Armagnac is generally drier than Cognac.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="brandydejerez">
         <h3>
          Brandy de Jerez
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Jerez de la Frontera, Spain
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          primarily the Airen grape of La Mancha and Extremadura
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Column distill (often elsewhere in Spain, then ship to Jerez) and age in sherry casks for minimums of 6 months, one year for Reserva, three years for Gran Reserva. In practice the best Reservas and Gran Reservas are aged 12-15 years.
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          Grapes from other regions are usually used as the local sherry grapes are too valuable for brandy production.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="metaxa">
         <h3>
          Metaxa
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Greece, 1888 (invented by Spyros Metaxa)
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          wine
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Pot distill, flavor with Muscat wine, age at least three years, flavor with anise, rose leaves and other spices.
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          Grapes from other regions are usually used as the local sherry grapes are too valuable for brandy production.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 first" id="fruitbrandy">
        <h3>
         Fruit Brandy
        </h3>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         non-grape fruit wine
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Age minimally, and rarely in wood (fine calvados is an exception).
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         Not to be confused with fruit-flavored grape brandy.
        </p>
        <div class="level-3 first" id="eaudevie">
         <h3>
          Eau-de-vie
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Alsace region, France, 1553 (first mention)
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          non-grape fruit wine
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Do not age.
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          "Water of life" in French, eau-de-vie is colorless. The term is also used in France to refer to brandy in general.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="calvados">
         <h3>
          Calvados
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Lower Normandy region, France
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          apple cider
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Age in oak casks for minimum of two years.
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          The best-known fruit brandy, calvados is made from the local small, tart apples.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="kirschwasser">
         <h3>
          Kirschwasser
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Germany
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          black cherry
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="applejack">
         <h3>
          Applejack
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          United States, colonial period
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          hard cider (fermented apples)
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          The word comes from "jacking," a nickname for the
          <a href="#glossary-freezedistillation">
           freeze distillation
          </a>
          procedure originally used.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="rakija">
         <h3>
          Rakija
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Balkans
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          various fruits
         </p>
         <p class="comments">
          Often spelled "rakia", many varieties are popular on the Balkan peninsula where it may be made from grapes, plums (known as slivovitz, usually from the same sloe plum used to flavor "sloe gin"), apricots, pears, quince, figs, and walnuts.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 first" id="pomace">
        <h3>
         Pomace
        </h3>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         pomace wine (made from grape pulp, skins, stems and seeds)
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         The "poor man's brandy," pomace was originally something for wine makers to do with their grapes after pressing.
        </p>
        <div class="level-3 first" id="grappa">
         <h3>
          Grappa
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          Bassano del Grappa, Italy
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          pomace wine
         </p>
         <p class="process">
          <span class="label">
           Process:
          </span>
          Don't age, or age 2-4 years.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="level-3 middle" id="marc">
         <h3>
          Marc
         </h3>
         <p class="origin">
          <span class="label">
           Origin:
          </span>
          France
         </p>
         <p class="ingredients">
          <span class="label">
           Ingredients:
          </span>
          pomace wine
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="vodka">
       <h3>
        Vodka
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Russia, 1400s
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        rye or wheat, sometimes potato or another grain (traditionally the cheapest starch available)
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        Probably produced since the 12th century for medicinal purposes, the word comes from the Russian diminutive for "water." In the 1930s Vodka became popular in the United States where it is government-defined as a neutral spirit without distinctive characteristics.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="whiskey">
       <h3>
        Whiskey
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Ireland and Scotland, around 1500; known as
        <em>
         aqua vitae
        </em>
        ,
        <em>
         uisge beatha
        </em>
        or
        <em>
         usquebaugh
        </em>
        (all meaning "water of life") until the 1700s (name shortened from "usquebaugh" to "usky" and eventually "whiskey").
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        barley, corn, rye
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        Spelled "whisky" in Scotland, Canada, Japan, and New Zealand.
       </p>
       <div class="level-2 first" id="singlemaltscotch">
        <h3>
         Single-Malt Scotch
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Scotland, 1500
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         100% barley malt
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Under the Scotch Whiskey Act 1988: pot distill to no more than 94.8%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         , age in oak for at least three years, dilute with water and add nothing but caramel for color.
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         Currently there are only about 115 operating distilleries producing single-malt scotch.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="blendedscotch">
        <h3>
         Blended Scotch
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Scotland, 1860s
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         single malt scotch (30-60%), grain alcohol (from corn)
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Mix several malt whiskies from different distillers with grain spirits (more neutral in flavor) produced in column stills.
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         Legal since the 1860s, the motivation for blending was originally economic but it also met the demand outside of Scotland for a milder spirit.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="irishwhiskey">
        <h3>
         Irish Whiskey
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Ireland
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         barley (60%), barley malt (40%)
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Dry the malt (don't roast), and triple distill.
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         Milder than single malt and some blended scotch.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="rye">
        <h3>
         Rye
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         North America
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         rye (minimum 51%), barley malt
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         By law: distill at less than 80%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         and age for at least two years in new charred barrels.
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         Some rye is bottled and marketed directly, but most is blended into other whiskies for character and structure.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="bourbon">
        <h3>
         Bourbon
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Bourbon County, Kentucky, United States
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         corn (minimum 51%), barley malt
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         By law: distill at less than 80%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         and age for at least two years in new charred barrels. In practice, aged at least four years.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="tennesseewhiskey">
        <h3>
         Tennessee Whiskey
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Tennessee, United States
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         corn (minimum 51%), barley malt
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         By law: distill at less than 80%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         , filtered through bed of sugar maple charcoal, age for at least two years in new charred barrels.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="canadianwhiskey">
        <h3>
         Canadian Whiskey
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Canada
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         corn or wheat with rye, barley, or barley malt
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Usually: blend from different whiskies of different ages; by law: age in used oak barrels for minimum three years (four to six in practice). Export in barrel or bottle in Canada at 43.4%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         .
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="rum">
       <h3>
        Rum
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Americas, 1500s
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        molasses or sugar cane
       </p>
       <p class="process">
        <span class="label">
         Process:
        </span>
        Distill in continuous still to about 85%
        <span class="smallcaps">
         ABV
        </span>
        , filter, blend, and leach through charcoal for "silver" rum, age for 1 year (light-bodied) to 6 years (heavy), add caramel (if desired) to deepen color and flavor. For Jamaican Rum ferment longer and pot distill to lower purity.
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        Settlers in the New World began to make rum because barley had not yet been cultivated and molasses was plentiful.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="gin">
       <h3>
        Gin
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
        </span>
        Holland
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
        </span>
        juniper berries, various grains
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        The word come from the Dutch "genever" which means juniper. The number of botanicals in a gin ranges from 4 to 20.
       </p>
       <div class="level-2 first" id="genever">
        <h3>
         Genever
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Holland
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         malt wine (malted barley, wheat, corn, and rye)
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Pot double- or triple-distill at low proof, distill once more with juniper to about 48%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         .
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         A few genevers from Holland and Belgium are distilled directly from juniper berries.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="londondry">
        <h3>
         London Dry Gin
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         England
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         grain (corn, barley, malt, wheat, or rye), juniper berries, botanicals
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Column distill to 90 or 94%
         <span class="smallcaps">
          ABV
         </span>
         , pot distill with juniper and botanicals, dilute, bottle. Generally, don't age. For some high quality gins juniper and botanicals go in a chamber in the still which the vapor passes through before condensation.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="level-2 middle" id="plymouth">
        <h3>
         Plymouth Gin
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Plymouth, England
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Copper pot distill.
        </p>
        <p class="comments">
         Originally the local gin style of the English Channel port of Plymouth, now produced only by Plymouth, Coates &lt; Co., which also controls the rights to the term Plymouth Gin.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="level-1 first" id="mezcal">
       <h3>
        Mezcal
       </h3>
       <p class="origin">
        <span class="label">
         Origin:
        </span>
        Mexico
       </p>
       <p class="ingredients">
        <span class="label">
         Ingredients:
        </span>
        juice of any species of agave plant (a.k.a. maguey, pronounced muh-GAY)
       </p>
       <p class="process">
        <span class="label">
         Process:
        </span>
        Cultivate agave 8-10 years, depending on the type, cut off flower stalk when it appears at plant's sexual maturity--growth is redirected into central stalk which swells with sweet, juicy pulp. After swelling, cut plant from roots and remove the long sword-shaped leaves, leaving the pia ("pineapple," so-called because it resembles a giant green and white pineapple) which weighs 25-100 pounds. Quarter pia, bake in underground oven heated with wood charcoal (for mezcal's distinctive smoky taste), and crush and shred to extract the sweet juice
        <em>
         aguamiel
        </em>
        ("honey water"). Ferment and pot distill agave juice to 55%
        <span class="smallcaps">
         ABV
        </span>
        with water only (high-quality) or add sugar ("mixto"). If needed add natural flavorings and caramel for color.
       </p>
       <p class="comments">
        The famous "worm" found in some bottles ("con gusano") is actually the larva of one of two moths that live on the agave plant. Reason for the worm is obscure, but it does serve as proof of high alcohol content--if worm remains intact, percentage of alcohol in the spirit is high enough to preserve the pickled worm. Top-quality mezcals do not include a worm in the bottle.
       </p>
       <div class="level-2 first" id="tequila">
        <h3>
         Tequila
        </h3>
        <p class="origin">
         <span class="label">
          Origin:
         </span>
         Jalisco, Mexico
        </p>
        <p class="ingredients">
         <span class="label">
          Ingredients:
         </span>
         juice of blue agave (
         <em>
          agave tequiliana
         </em>
         )
        </p>
        <p class="process">
         <span class="label">
          Process:
         </span>
         Similar to mezcal but bake pia in steam oven or autoclave (oversized pressure cooker) until all starch is converted to sugar.
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <span class="label">
           Silver/Blanco:
          </span>
          Age 60 days, less, or not at all.
         </li>
         <li>
          <span class="label">
           Gold (usually a mixto):
          </span>
          Unaged silver tequila plus caramel for color.
         </li>
         <li>
          <span class="label">
           Reposado:
          </span>
          Age in wooden tanks or casks for legal two months (three to nine months for better quality brands). Best-selling tequila in Mexico.
         </li>
         <li>
          <span class="label">
           Aejo:
          </span>
          Age in wood at least one year (one and a half to three years for high-quality mixtos, up to four years for high-quality agaves).
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p class="comments">
         By Mexican law all 100% agave or aged Tequila is bottled in Mexico. All 100% agave tequila is labeled as such (otherwise it is a "mixto," made from fermented agave juice and other sugars, usually cane sugar with water). Aging tequila more than four years is controversial; most Tequila producers oppose it to protect the distinctive earthy and vegetal agave flavor.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>

     <div class="article" id="spirits-glossary">
      <h2>
       Glossary
      </h2>
      <dl>
       <dt id="glossary-barleymalt">
        barley malt
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Barley kernels which have been soaked and which have begun to germinate. When used in beer production, the malt is dried to stop enzyme activity and prevent sprouting which would consume sugar intended for the yeast.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-botanicals">
        botanicals
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Fruits, spices, and herbs used to flavor gin. Botanicals are either mixed into the
        <a href="#glossary-wash">
         wash
        </a>
        prior to boiling or (usually for premium-brand gins) placed at the top of the still so that the alcohol can pass through in vapor form.
       </dd>
       <div class="spirits-glossary-ill">
        <img class="content_image" src="http://assets.alexreisner.com/uploads/image/file/211/illustration.columnstill-1360549859.gif"/>
        <p class="caption-title">
         The column still.
        </p>
        <p>
         The plates in each column are hottest at the bottom and coolest at the top. Liquids with low boiling points are concentrated in the vapor that leaves the first column and rises in the second. As is the case with the pot still, the impurities are recycled to extract their fraction of desirable ingredients.
        </p>
        <p class="caption-credit">
         Illustration from Harold McGee's excellent book
         <em>
          On Food and Cooking
         </em>
         . Modified slightly for readability at small size.
        </p>
       </div>
       <dt id="glossary-columnstill">
        column still
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Created in the early 1800s to avoid the process of draining and cleaning the
        <a href="#glossary-potstill">
         pot still
        </a>
        between batches, it is also known as the
        <em>
         patent still
        </em>
        or
        <em>
         continuous still
        </em>
        because it can be run constantly, without need for maintenance between batches. Column stills allow the distiller less control of the resulting flavor constituents and produce a characterless spirit relative to the pot still. It also allows the distiller to use cheaper ingredients since less of the original ingredients' flavor is preserved.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-continuousstill">
        continuous still
       </dt>
       <dd>
        See
        <a href="#glossary-columnstill">
         column still
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-freezedistillation">
        freeze distillation
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Like distillation by heating, the objective is to separate the various constituents of a liquid, but freeze distillation uses cooling, rather than heating, to exploit varying freezing, rather than boiling points. Since water freezes at zero degrees Celcius and ethyl alcohol at -114, a fermented beverage is cooled and solid chunks of ice removed. Freeze distillation is rarely used for distilled spirits (outside of
        <a href="#applejack">
         applejack
        </a>
        ). The process is not as efficient as normal distillation and is illegal in many countries due to an increased risk of high concentrations of the poisonous fusel oils the pot distiller must carefully remove.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-greenbeer">
        green beer
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Beer in the stage of production immediately after the yeast has been removed. It has yet to be conditioned, clarified, pasteurized and packaged.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-kefir">
        kefir
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Milk-based fermented beverage of the Balkans.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-kumiss">
        koumiss
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Milk-based fermented beverage of the Tartars whose nomadic lifestyle did not allow them to cultivate grains.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-mead">
        mead
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Honey-based fermented beverage from Europe. The name comes from the Sanskrit word for honey.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-patentstill">
        patent still
       </dt>
       <dd>
        See
        <a href="#glossary-columnstill">
         column still
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
       <div class="spirits-glossary-ill">
        <img class="content_image" src="http://assets.alexreisner.com/uploads/image/file/212/illustration.potstill-1360549842.gif"/>
        <p class="caption-title">
         Pot distillation.
        </p>
        <p>
         The container at center receives both the distillate from the first pot, which must be further purified, and the undesirable portions--the foreshots and feints--of the second distillation, more of whose alcohol can thereby be extracted.
        </p>
        <p class="caption-credit">
         Illustration from Harold McGee's excellent book
         <em>
          On Food and Cooking
         </em>
         . Modified slightly for readability at small size.
        </p>
       </div>
       <dt id="glossary-potstill">
        pot still
       </dt>
       <dd>
        The first type of still and the only one used until the early 1800s, a pot still actually consists of two pots, the first of which contains the
        <a href="#glossary-wash">
         wash
        </a>
        . When the first pot is heated to between 173 and 212 degree Fahrenheit (hot enough to boil alcohol but not water), alcohol vapor rises up and out, into a coiled tube where it is cooled, collected, then re-heated and further purified. (Moonshine is the term for alcohol which has forgone the second distillation and which is potentially deadly due to its high concentration of poisonous constituents.) Because the pot still allows for precise control (when wielded skillfully), it is the still of choice for any spirit without additives in which the pure flavor is important (e.g., any good whiskey or brandy).
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-sake">
        sake
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Rice-based fermented beverage.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-toddy">
        toddy
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Fermented beverage made from rice with molasses or palm sap.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-tehoo">
        tehoo
       </dt>
       <dd>
        Fermented beverage made from rice and millet.
       </dd>
       <dt id="glossary-wash">
        wash
       </dt>
       <dd>
        The raw ingredient of distillation (in liquid form), usually fermented fruit or grains.
       </dd>
      </dl>
