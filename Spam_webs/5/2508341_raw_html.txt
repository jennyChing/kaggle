         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          A long-standing practice of administering
          <a href="http://www.healthandsuppliments.com/" target="_blank" title="anabolic androgenic steroids">
           anabolic androgenic steroids
          </a>
          to patients during cardiopulmonary bypass surgery for reducing inflammation failed to assist patients and actually increased the risk of heart attacks, according to results of a large clinical trial.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          <img alt="Anabolic Steroids Can Hurt Patients In Bypass Surgery" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1792" height="298" src="http://www.healthandsuppliments.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/Anabolic-Steroids-Can-Hurt-Patients-In-Bypass-Surgery.jpg" width="448"/>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          This study shows that administering steroids during cardiac surgery requiring bypass can cause harm, said Dr. Richard Whitlock, a cardiologist with McMaster University in Hamilton, Ontario, who led the international trial. Based on these results, we suggest that steroids should not be used prophylactically during
          <a href="http://www.heart.org/HEARTORG/Conditions/HeartAttack/PreventionTreatmentofHeartAttack/Cardiac-Procedures-and-Surgeries_UCM_303939_Article.jsp" target="_blank" title="cardiac surgeries">
           cardiac surgeries
          </a>
          that require the use of cardiopulmonary bypass.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Twenty percent of patients undergoing open heart surgery in the North America are given steroids, said Whitlock, who presented his findings on Monday at the annual scientific sessions of the American College of Cardiology in Washington. But the steroid use is much higher in Europe, he said, particularly in the Netherlands, Denmark and Germany. In the Netherlands its almost the standard of care.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          In the new study, half the patients during surgery received a widely used steroid to prevent inflammation (
          <a href="http://www.nlm.nih.gov/medlineplus/druginfo/meds/a682795.html" target="_blank" title="methylprednisolone">
           methylprednisolone
          </a>
          ) while the remaining received a placebo. The trial failed to achieve both its primary goals. While the overall death rate was no better for those receiving the steroid than those taking placebo, there was also no significant benefit from the steroid on a combination of measurements including death, heart attack, stroke, new renal failure or respiratory failure, compared with placebo.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: justify;">
          Whitlock said heart surgeons became interested in steroids in the 1940s and 1950s and have been playing with this idea ever since.
         </p>
