         <h1>
          What Usain Bolt can teach us about the sporting gene
         </h1>
         <div class="ct-meta-details">
          <dl>
           <dt>
            First published:
           </dt>
           <dd>
            Tuesday 17 September 2013 8:43AM
           </dd>
           <dt>
            By:
           </dt>
           <dd>
            <strong>
             Amanda Smith
            </strong>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </div>
         <div class="section rich-text">
          <div class="inline-content photo full">
           <a href="/radionational/programs/bodysphere/image/4960998">
            <img alt="" src="http://www.abc.net.au/radionational/image/4960998-3x2-700x467.jpg" title=""/>
           </a>
           <a href="/radionational/programs/bodysphere/image/4960998">
            <strong>
             Image:
            </strong>
            Kenyan long-distance runner, Daniel Kipngetich Komen training in the Rift Valley, Kenya. The Kelenjin tribe that he belongs to is known as 'the running tribe' for their elite sporting performances
            <span class="source">
             (Simon Bruty/Getty Images)
            </span>
           </a>
          </div>
          <p>
           <em>
            When the human genome was mapped ten years ago, among the many possibilities it promised was the hope of finding the 'gene for sport'. It turns out to be a lot more complicated than that, with freak athletes like Usain Bolt and Finnish endurance skiing champion Eero Mntyranta revealing that choosing the right sport and the right training is also important, as
            <strong>
             Amanda Smith
            </strong>
            discovers.
           </em>
          </p>
          <iframe frameborder="no" height="166" scrolling="no" src="https://w.soundcloud.com/player/?url=http%3A%2F%2Fapi.soundcloud.com%2Ftracks%2F110693578&amp;color=b33116&amp;auto_play=false&amp;show_artwork=true" width="100%">
          </iframe>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           In 2000, David Epstein was a member of Columbia University's varsity track squad. One of his high school training partners, a state champion, dropped dead on the track after running a mile race.
          </p>
          <p>
           'That was actually one of the things that propelled me to write about sports and genetics,' Epstein says. 'I really wondered how someone who was the picture of health and athleticism could, with no previous recognisable symptoms, drop dead'.
          </p>
          <p>
           It turns out that Kevin Richards had a single switch in the 3 billion letters that make up his DNA code, in precisely the place to cause hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. This is a genetic disease that causes the heart muscle cells to be built in a way that makes them prone to electrical misfiring during exercise. Epstein says it was a time bomb in the chest of his friend and it's by far the most common cause of sudden death in young athletes.
          </p>
          <div class="inline-content quote right">
           <blockquote class="pullquote">
            <div class="quote">
             <p>
              Genes have gotten a bad reputation by being portrayed as destiny all the time, but many genes are just predisposition.
             </p>
            </div>
            <div class="cite">
             David Epstein, sport science writer
            </div>
           </blockquote>
          </div>
          <p>
           This was a case of a genetic mutation that no athlete wants to have. A far more rare, and desirable variant is the one discovered in a ruddy-faced Finnish reindeer farmer. Back in the 1960s, Eero Mntyranta was probably the best endurance athlete in the world. He was a cross-country skiier and winner of seven Olympic medals, three of them gold. In the 15 kilometre race at the 1964 Winter Olympics he won by a margin that's never been equalled. Twenty years after Mntyranta retired, a group of scientists found him to have a rare gene mutation that runs in his family. Epstein says that it causes their bodies to be over-responsive to their own EPO hormone, the hormone that cues the body to produce red blood cells. 'So the people in the family with that mutation have tremendous endurance.'
          </p>
          <p>
           Was it genetics alone that made Mntyranta a champion? Epstein says that he's the Arctic skier version of a Kenyan runner. 'He grew up skiing across a frozen lake to school, he used cross-country skiing as a way to get out of poverty and improve his life, and he would train 50 kilometres at a time.' At the same time, Epstein adds, 'it was his incredible oxygen carrying capacity that allowed him to undergo such training'. Effectively, Eero Mntyranta's body did naturally what numerous Tour de France cyclists have been caught doing artificially to improve their endurance.
          </p>
          <p>
           This kind of genetic variant, however, is a rarity. So is there such a thing as the perfect athlete anywhere on the planet right now? David Epstein says genetically speaking, no. He quotes the work of two British scientists, Allun Williams and Jonathan Folland, in relation to endurance athletes. 'They gathered up all the research on genes that seemed to be associated with good endurance performance and then they looked at how prevalent they are in humans.' Williams and Folland considered only two dozen genes, but for one person to have all the advantageous variants of just these two dozen is close to statistically impossible. 'They'd have to be luckier than literally winning the lottery two weeks in a row,' Epstein says.
          </p>
          <p>
           So in what ways should genetic information be used in sport? How useful is it to know which sports you're genetically best suited to? In the future should parents be able to select for sporting traits in their children? Epstein is cautious. 'Genes have gotten a bad reputation by being portrayed as destiny all the time,' he says. 'But many genes are just predisposition'.
          </p>
          <p>
           Epstein also warns against the way genetics-as-destiny can be used to stereotype population groups. For example, in the 1930s American sportswriters observed a preponderance of Jewish players in college basketball and so concluded that Jews had a genetic disposition for basketball. Epstein says this was tied to perceived characters traits such as being sneaky and good at stealing the ball. 'So part of the very understandable reason that genetic explanations have gotten a bad reputation is because they've been used foolishly and to enforce stereotypes without the evidence being there.' He is, however, in support of genetic information being used to help people make informed choices. 'My hope is that instead of genetic science saying "we can engineer your kid" or "your kid has to play this sport because they have this gene", they will say "you can do what you want but you have genes that would cause you to be a high responder to this type of training, so if you are not getting what you want from this other sport, maybe you should give this one a try".'
          </p>
          <p>
           Its this kind of genetic information that can also be used to direct the training that an individual athlete should follow. Epstein cites a study of Danish soccer players, in relation to those with fast-twitch muscle fibres and those with slow-twitch muscle fibres. 'To put it crudely, the fast-twitch are the ones that you use for explosive exercise; sprinting and jumping, and they tire out very quickly'.
          </p>
          <div>
           <h3>
           </h3>
           <div class="article-index section ct-teaser-view">
            <ul class="article-index">
             <li>
              <div class="cs-teaser ct-teaser-feature">
               <div class="figure">

               </div>
               <div class="header">
                <h3 class="title">
                 <a href="/radionational/programs/bodysphere/the-sports-gene/4945060">
                  The sports gene
                 </a>
                </h3>
                <ul class="cs-audio-links">
                 <li>
                  <a class="ico ico-download" href="http://mpegmedia.abc.net.au/rn/podcast/2013/09/bse_20130915.mp3" title="Download this item as MP3(12.13MB)">
                   Download
                  </a>
                 </li>
                </ul>
                <p class="time">
                 Sunday 15 September 2013
                </p>
               </div>
               <div class="summary">
                <p>
                 Listen to

                 to find out more about genes and extraordinary sporting performance.
                </p>

           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           The Danish physiologist Jesper Andersen tested the proportion of fast and slow twitch fibres in athletes through muscle biopsies, and advised on the kind of training that suited any individual's particular make up. Those with a higher proportion of fast-twitch muscle fibres were advised to train less. But soccer coaches make all the players train the same, and so often the athletes with the fast-twitch muscle fibres end up hurt. This has resulted in a preponderance of players at elite level who don't have fast-twitch muscle fibres, even though it's a desirable thing in a soccer player. They have all been lost along the way to chronic injuries.
          </p>
          <p>
           'So at the top level of sports, because we are not paying attention to individual biological variation and we give everyone the same cookie cutter training plan, you are losing some of the people who have the most desirable traits,' Epstein says. For him, Usain Bolt is a paradigmatic example of training for body type. 'If you read his biography it's like a testament to how lazy he is, by his own admission, but when you have the proportion of fast-twitch muscle fibres he probably does and can contract your muscles that violently, having training breaks and then training short and intense is really the best. I think he really has that figured out'.
          </p>
