     <p>
      An
      <b>
       open standard
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">
       standard
      </a>
      that is publicly available and has various rights to use associated with it, and may also have various properties of how it was designed (e.g. open process). There is no single definition and interpretations vary with usage.
     </p>
     <p>
      The terms
      <i>
       open
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       standard
      </i>
      have a wide range of meanings associated with their usage. There are a number of definitions of open standards which emphasize different aspects of openness, including the openness of the resulting specification, the openness of the drafting process, and the ownership of rights in the standard. The term "standard" is sometimes restricted to technologies approved by formalized committees that are open to participation by all interested parties and operate on a consensus basis.
     </p>
     <p>
      The definitions of the term
      <i>
       open standard
      </i>
      used by academics, the European Union and some of its member governments or parliaments such as Denmark, France, and Spain preclude open standards requiring fees for use, as do the New Zealand, South African and the Venezuelan governments. On the standard organisation side, the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Consortium" title="World Wide Web Consortium">
       World Wide Web Consortium
      </a>
      (W3C) ensures that its specifications can be implemented on a
      <a href="/wiki/Royalty-free" title="Royalty-free">
       royalty-free
      </a>
      basis.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many definitions of the term
      <i>
       standard
      </i>
      permit patent holders to impose "reasonable and non-discriminatory licensing" royalty fees and other licensing terms on implementers or users of the standard. For example, the rules for standards published by the major internationally recognized standards bodies such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_Engineering_Task_Force" title="Internet Engineering Task Force">
       Internet Engineering Task Force
      </a>
      (IETF),
      <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
       International Organization for Standardization
      </a>
      (ISO),
      <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">
       International Electrotechnical Commission
      </a>
      (IEC), and
      <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">
       ITU-T
      </a>
      permit their standards to contain specifications whose implementation will require payment of patent licensing fees. Among these organizations, only the IETF and ITU-T explicitly refer to their standards as "open standards", while the others refer only to producing "standards". The IETF and ITU-T use definitions of "open standard" that allow
      <a href="/wiki/Reasonable_and_non-discriminatory_licensing" title="Reasonable and non-discriminatory licensing">
       "reasonable and non-discriminatory" patent licensing
      </a>
      fee requirements.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are those in the open-source software community who hold that an "open standard" is only open if it can be freely adopted, implemented and extended.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While open standards or architectures are considered non-proprietary in the sense that the standard is either unowned or owned by a collective body, it can still be publicly shared and not tightly guarded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langlois.2C_Richard_N_1999_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Langlois.2C_Richard_N_1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The typical example of open source that has become a standard is the personal computer originated by IBM and now referred to as Wintel, the combination of the Microsoft operating system and Intel microprocessor. There are three others that are most widely accepted as open which include the GSM phones (adopted as a government standard), Open Group which promotes UNIX and the like, and the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) which created the first standards of SMTP and TCP/IP. Buyers tend to prefer open standards which they believe offer them cheaper products and more choice for access due to network effects and increased competition between vendors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Open standards which specify formats are sometimes referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Open_format" title="Open format">
       open formats
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Many specifications that are sometimes referred to as standards are proprietary and only available under restrictive contract terms (if they can be obtained at all) from the organization that owns the copyright on the specification. As such these specifications are not considered to be fully
      <i>
       open
      </i>
      . Joel West has argued that "open" standards are not black and white but have many different levels of "openness". Ultimately a standard needs
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">
         <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2015)">
          according to whom?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      to be open enough that it will become adopted and accepted in the market, but still closed enough that firms can get a return on their investment in developing the technology around the standard. A more open standard tends to occur when the knowledge of the technology becomes dispersed enough that competition is increased and others are able to start copying the technology as they implement it. This occurred with the Wintel architecture as others were able to start imitating the software. Less open standards exist when a particular firm has much power (not ownership) over the standard, which can occur when a firms platform wins in standard setting or the market makes one platform most popular.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_IEEE.2C_ISOC.2C_W3C.2C_IETF_and_IAB_Definition">
       Joint IEEE, ISOC, W3C, IETF and IAB Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Joint IEEE, ISOC, W3C, IETF and IAB Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On August 12, 2012, the Institute for Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE), Internet Society (ISOC), World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) and Internet Architecture Board (IAB),
      <a class="external text" href="https://open-stand.org/about-us/affirmation/" rel="nofollow">
       jointly affirmed
      </a>
      a set of principles which have contributed to the exponential growth of the Internet and related technologies. The
      <a class="external text" href="https://open-stand.org/about-us/principles/" rel="nofollow">
       OpenStand Principles
      </a>
      define open standards and establish the building blocks for innovation. Standards developed using the OpenStand principles are developed through an open, participatory process, support interoperability, foster global competition, are voluntarily adopted on a global level and serve as building blocks for products and services targeted to meet the needs of markets and consumers. This drives innovation which, in turn, contributes to the creation of new markets and the growth and expansion of existing markets.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       There are five, key OpenStand Principles, as outlined below:
      </b>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       1. Cooperation
      </b>
      Respectful cooperation between standards organizations, whereby each respects the autonomy, integrity, processes, and intellectual property rules of the others.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       2. Adherence to Principles
      </b>
      Adherence to the five fundamental principles of standards development:
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Due process.
      </b>
      Decisions are made with equity and fairness among participants. No one party dominates or guides standards development. Standards processes are transparent and opportunities exist to appeal decisions. Processes for periodic standards review and updating are well defined.
      <b>
       Broad consensus.
      </b>
      Processes allow for all views to be considered and addressed, such that agreement can be found across a range of interests.
      <b>
       Transparency.
      </b>
      Standards organizations provide advance public notice of proposed standards development activities, the scope of work to be undertaken, and conditions for participation. Easily accessible records of decisions and the materials used in reaching those decisions are provided. Public comment periods are provided before final standards approval and adoption.
      <b>
       Balance.
      </b>
      Standards activities are not exclusively dominated by any particular person, company or interest group.
      <b>
       Openness.
      </b>
      Standards processes are open to all interested and informed parties.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       3. Collective Empowerment
      </b>
      Commitment by affirming standards organizations and their participants to collective empowerment by striving for standards that:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       are chosen and defined based on technical merit, as judged by the contributed expertise of each participant;
      </li>
      <li>
       provide global interoperability, scalability, stability, and resiliency;
      </li>
      <li>
       enable global competition;
      </li>
      <li>
       serve as building blocks for further innovation; and
      </li>
      <li>
       contribute to the creation of global communities, benefiting humanity.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <b>
       4. Availability
      </b>
      Standards specifications are made accessible to all for implementation and deployment. Affirming standards organizations have defined procedures to develop specifications that can be implemented under fair terms. Given market diversity, fair terms may vary from royalty-free to fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms (FRAND).
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       5. Voluntary Adoption
      </b>
      Standards are voluntarily adopted and success is determined by the market.
     </p>
     <p>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="ITU-T_definition">
       ITU-T definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: ITU-T definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">
       ITU-T
      </a>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">
       standards development organization
      </a>
      (SDO) that is one of the three sectors of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ITU" title="ITU">
       International Telecommunications Union
      </a>
      (a specialized agency of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
       United Nations
      </a>
      ). The ITU-T has a Telecommunication Standardization Bureau director's
      <i>
       Ad Hoc
      </i>
      group on IPR that produced the following definition in March 2005, which the ITU-T as a whole has endorsed for its purposes since November 2005:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">
        ITU-T
       </a>
       has a long history of open standards development. However, recently some different external sources have attempted to define the term "Open Standard" in a variety of different ways. In order to avoid confusion, the ITU-T uses for its purpose the term "Open Standards" per the following definition:
      </dd>
      <dd>
       "Open Standards" are standards made available to the general public and are developed (or approved) and maintained via a collaborative and consensus driven process. "Open Standards" facilitate
       <a href="/wiki/Interoperability" title="Interoperability">
        interoperability
       </a>
       and data exchange among different products or services and are intended for widespread adoption.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Other elements of "Open Standards" include, but are not limited to:
       <ul>
        <li>
         Collaborative process  voluntary and market driven development (or approval) following a transparent consensus driven process that is reasonably open to all interested parties.
        </li>
        <li>
         Reasonably balanced  ensures that the process is not dominated by any one interest group.
        </li>
        <li>
         Due process - includes consideration of and response to comments by interested parties.
        </li>
        <li>
         Intellectual property rights (IPRs)  IPRs essential to implement the standard to be licensed to all applicants on a worldwide, non-discriminatory basis, either (1) for free and under other reasonable terms and conditions or (2) on reasonable terms and conditions (which may include monetary compensation). Negotiations are left to the parties concerned and are performed outside the
         <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">
          SDO
         </a>
         .
        </li>
        <li>
         Quality and level of detail  sufficient to permit the development of a variety of competing implementations of interoperable products or services. Standardized interfaces are not hidden, or controlled other than by the SDO promulgating the standard.
        </li>
        <li>
         Publicly available  easily available for implementation and use, at a reasonable price. Publication of the text of a standard by others is permitted only with the prior approval of the SDO.
        </li>
        <li>
         On-going support  maintained and supported over a long period of time.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">
       ITU-T
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/ITU-R" title="ITU-R">
       ITU-R
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
       ISO
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">
       IEC
      </a>
      have harmonized on a common patent policy
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      under the banner of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Standards_Cooperation" title="World Standards Cooperation">
       WSC
      </a>
      . However, the ITU-T definition should not necessarily be considered also applicable in ITU-R, ISO and IEC contexts, since the Common Patent Policy
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      does not make any reference to "open standards" but rather only to "standards."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="IETF_definition">
       IETF definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: IETF definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In section 7 of its
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 2026
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IETF" title="IETF">
       IETF
      </a>
      classifies specifications that have been developed in a manner similar to that of the IETF itself as being "open standards," and lists the standards produced by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANSI" title="ANSI">
       ANSI
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
       ISO
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">
       IEEE
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/ITU-T" title="ITU-T">
       ITU-T
      </a>
      as examples. As the IETF standardization processes and IPR policies have the characteristics listed above by ITU-T, the IETF standards fulfill the ITU-T definition of "open standards."
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the IETF has not adopted a specific definition of "open standard"; both
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 2026
      </a>
      and the IETF's mission statement (
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3935" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 3935
      </a>
      ) talks about "open process," but
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 2026
      </a>
      does not define "open standard" except for the purpose of defining what documents IETF standards can link to.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 2026
      </a>
      belongs to a set of RFCs collectively known as BCP 9 (Best Common Practice, an IETF policy).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2026" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 2026
      </a>
      was later updated by BCP 78 and 79 (among others). As of 2011 BCP 78 is
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5378" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 5378
      </a>
      (Rights Contributors Provide to the IETF Trust),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and BCP 79 consists of
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3979" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 3979
      </a>
      (Intellectual Property Rights in IETF Technology) and a clarification in
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc4879" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 4879
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The changes are intended to be compatible with the "Simplified
      <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Software_Distribution" title="Berkeley Software Distribution">
       BSD
      </a>
      License" as stated in the IETF Trust Legal Provisions and Copyright FAQ based on
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5377" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 5377
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2012, the IETF combined with the W3C and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">
       IEEE
      </a>
      to launch OpenStand
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpenStand_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OpenStand-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and to publish The Modern Paradigm for Standards. This captures "the effective and efficient standardization processes that have made the Internet and Web the premiere platforms for innovation and borderless commerce". The declaration is then published in the form of
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6852" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 6852
      </a>
      in January 2013.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="European_Interoperability_Framework_for_Pan-European_eGovernment_Services">
       European Interoperability Framework for Pan-European eGovernment Services
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: European Interoperability Framework for Pan-European eGovernment Services">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      defined the term for use within its European Interoperability Framework for Pan-European eGovernment Services, Version 1.0
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although it does not claim to be a universal definition for all
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      use and documentation.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       To reach interoperability in the context of pan-European eGovernment services, guidance needs to focus on open standards.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The word "open" is here meant in the sense of fulfilling the following requirements:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The standard is adopted and will be maintained by a not-for-profit organization, and its ongoing development occurs on the basis of an open decision-making procedure available to all interested parties (consensus or majority decision etc.).
       </li>
       <li>
        The standard has been published and the standard specification document is available either freely or at a nominal charge. It must be permissible to all to copy, distribute and use it for no fee or at a nominal fee.
       </li>
       <li>
        The intellectual property - i.e. patents possibly present - of (parts of) the standard is made irrevocably available on a
        <a href="/wiki/Royalty-free" title="Royalty-free">
         royalty-free
        </a>
        basis.
       </li>
       <li>
        There are no constraints on the re-use of the standard
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          15
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </blockquote>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Danish_government_definition">
       Danish government definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Danish government definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Danish government has attempted to make a definition of open standards,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which also is used in pan-European software development projects. It states:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <ul>
       <li>
        An open standard is accessible to everyone free of charge (i.e. there is no discrimination between users, and no payment or other considerations are required as a condition of use of the standard)
       </li>
       <li>
        An open standard of necessity remains accessible and free of charge (i.e. owners renounce their options, if indeed such exist, to limit access to the standard at a later date, for example, by committing themselves to openness during the remainder of a possible patent's life)
       </li>
       <li>
        An open standard is accessible free of charge and documented in all its details (i.e. all aspects of the standard are transparent and documented, and both access to and use of the documentation is free)
       </li>
      </ul>
     </blockquote>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Government_Definition">
       Indian Government Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Indian Government Definition">

     <p>
      A clear Royalty Free stance and far reaching requirements case is the one for India's Government

        4.1.1 Specification document of the Identified Standard shall be available with or without a nominal fee.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bruce_Perens.27_definition">
       Bruce Perens' definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bruce Perens' definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One of the most popular definitions of the term "open standard," as measured by Google ranking, is the one developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Perens" title="Bruce Perens">
       Bruce Perens
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His
      <a class="external text" href="http://perens.com/OpenStandards/Definition.html" rel="nofollow">
       definition
      </a>
      lists a set of principles that he believes must be met by an open standard:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Availability: Open Standards are available for all to read and implement.
      </li>
      <li>
       Maximize End-User Choice: Open Standards create a fair, competitive market for implementations of the standard. They do not lock the customer into a particular vendor or group.
      </li>
      <li>
       No Royalty: Open Standards are free for all to
       <i>
        implement
       </i>
       , with no royalty or fee.
       <i>
        Certification
       </i>
       of compliance by the standards organization may involve a fee.
      </li>
      <li>
       No Discrimination: Open Standards and the organizations that administer them do not favor one implementor over another for any reason other than the technical standards compliance of a vendors implementation. Certification organizations must provide a path for low and zero-cost implementations to be validated, but may also provide enhanced certification services.
      </li>
      <li>
       Extension or Subset: Implementations of Open Standards may be extended, or offered in subset form. However, certification organizations may decline to certify subset implementations, and may place requirements upon extensions (see
       <i>
        Predatory Practices
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
      <li>
       Predatory Practices: Open Standards may employ license terms that protect against subversion of the standard by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Embrace,_extend_and_extinguish" title="Embrace, extend and extinguish">
         embrace-and-extend
        </a>
       </i>
       tactics. The licenses attached to the standard may require the publication of reference information for extensions, and a license for all others to create, distribute, and sell software that is compatible with the extensions. An Open Standard may not otherwise prohibit extensions.
      </li>
     </ol>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Open_Source_Initiative.27s_definition">
       Open Source Initiative's definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Open Source Initiative's definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Open_Source_Initiative" title="Open Source Initiative">
       Open Source Initiative
      </a>
      defines the requirements and criteria for open standards as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       The Requirement
      </b>
     </p>
     <p>
      An "open standard" must not prohibit conforming implementations in open source software.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       The Criteria
      </b>
     </p>
     <p>
      To comply with the Open Standards Requirement, an "open standard" must satisfy the following criteria. If an "open standard" does not meet these criteria, it will be discriminating against open source developers.
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       No Intentional Secrets: The standard MUST NOT withhold any detail necessary for interoperable implementation. As flaws are inevitable, the standard MUST define a process for fixing flaws identified during implementation and interoperability testing and to incorporate said changes into a revised version or superseding version of the standard to be released under terms that do not violate the OSR.
      </li>
      <li>
       Availability: The standard MUST be freely and publicly available (e.g., from a stable web site) under royalty-free terms at reasonable and non-discriminatory cost.
      </li>
      <li>
       Patents: All patents essential to implementation of the standard MUST:
       <ul>
        <li>
         be licensed under royalty-free terms for unrestricted use, or
        </li>
        <li>
         be covered by a promise of non-assertion when practiced by open source software
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       No Agreements: There MUST NOT be any requirement for execution of a license agreement, NDA, grant, click-through, or any other form of paperwork to deploy conforming implementations of the standard.
      </li>
      <li>
       No OSR-Incompatible Dependencies: Implementation of the standard MUST NOT require any other technology that fails to meet the criteria of this Requirement.
      </li>
     </ol>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      As an important provider of Web technology
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_and_Communications_Technology" title="Information and Communications Technology">
       ICT
      </a>
      Standards, notably
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensible_Markup_Language" title="Extensible Markup Language">
       XML
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol" title="Hypertext Transfer Protocol">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Consortium" title="World Wide Web Consortium">

      </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Looking at the end result, the spec alone, up for adoption, is not enough. The participative/inclusive process leading to a particular design, and the supporting resources available with it should be accounted when we talk about Open Standards:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <ul>
       <li>
        transparency (due process is public, and all technical discussions, meeting minutes, are archived and referencable in decision making)
       </li>
       <li>

       </li>
       <li>
        openness (anybody can participate, and everybody does: industry, individual, public, government bodies, academia, on a worldwide scale)
       </li>
       <li>
        impartiality and consensus (guaranteed fairness by the process and the neutral hosting of the W3C organization, with equal weight for each participant)
       </li>
       <li>
        availability (free access to the standard text, both during development, at final stage, and for translations, and assurance that core Web and Internet technologies can be implemented Royalty-Free)

       <li>
        maintenance (ongoing process for testing, errata, revision, permanent access, validation, etc.)

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_Standards_Organization_definition">
       Digital Standards Organization definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Digital Standards Organization definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Standards_Organization" title="Digital Standards Organization">
       Digital Standards Organization
      </a>
      (DIGISTAN) states that "an open standard must be aimed at creating unrestricted competition between vendors and unrestricted choice for users."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defining_Open_Standard_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Defining_Open_Standard-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its brief definition of "open standard" (or "free and open standard") is "a published specification that is immune to vendor capture at all stages in its life-cycle." Its more complete definition as follows:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "The standard is adopted and will be maintained by a not-for-profit organization, and its ongoing development occurs on the basis of an open decision-making procedure available to all interested parties.
       </li>
       <li>
        The standard has been published and the standard specification document is available freely. It must be permissible to all to copy, distribute, and use it freely.
       </li>
       <li>
        The patents possibly present on (parts of) the standard are made irrevocably available on a royalty-free basis.
       </li>
       <li>
        There are no constraints on the re-use of the standard.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A key defining property is that an open standard is immune to vendor capture at all stages in its life-cycle. Immunity from vendor capture makes it possible to improve upon, trust, and extend an open standard over time."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         33
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      This definition is based on the EU's EIF v1 definition of "open standard," but with changes to address what it terms as "vendor capture." They believe that "Many groups and individuals have provided definitions for 'open standard' that reflect their economic interests in the standards process. We see that the fundamental conflict is between vendors who seek to capture markets and raise costs, and the market at large, which seeks freedom and lower costs... Vendors work hard to turn open standards into franchise standards. They work to change the statutory language so they can cloak franchise standards in the sheep's clothing of 'open standard.' A robust definition of "free and open standard" must thus take into account the direct economic conflict between vendors and the market at large."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defining_Open_Standard_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Defining_Open_Standard-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Free_Software_Foundation_Europe.27s_definition">
       Free Software Foundation Europe's definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Free Software Foundation Europe's definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Free_Software_Foundation_Europe" title="Free Software Foundation Europe">
       Free Software Foundation Europe
      </a>
      (FSFE) uses a
      <a class="external text" href="http://fsfe.org/projects/os/def.html" rel="nofollow">
       definition
      </a>
      which is based on the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Interoperability_Framework" title="European Interoperability Framework">
       European Interoperability Framework
      </a>
      v.1, and was extended after consultation with industry and community stakeholders. FSFE's standard has been adopted by groups such as the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.selfproject.eu/" rel="nofollow">
       SELF
      </a>
      EU Project, the 2008 Geneva Declaration on Standards and the Future of the Internet, and international
      <a href="/wiki/Document_Freedom_Day" title="Document Freedom Day">
       Document Freedom Day
      </a>
      teams.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to this definition an Open Standard is a format or protocol that is:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Subject to full public assessment and use without constraints in a manner equally available to all parties;
      </li>
      <li>
       Without any components or extensions that have dependencies on formats or protocols that do not meet the definition of an Open Standard themselves;
      </li>
      <li>
       Free from legal or technical clauses that limit its utilisation by any party or in any business model;
      </li>
      <li>
       Managed and further developed independently of any single vendor in a process open to the equal participation of competitors and third parties;
      </li>
      <li>
       Available in multiple complete implementations by competing vendors, or as a complete implementation equally available to all parties.
      </li>
     </ol>

     <blockquote>
      <p>
       A specification that is public, the standard is inclusive and it has been developed and is maintained in an open standardization process, everybody can implement it without any restriction, neither payment, to license the IPR (granted to everybody for free and without any condition). This is the minimum license terms asked by standardization bodies as W3C. Of course, all the other bodies accept open standards. But specification itself could cost a fair amount of money (ie. 100-400Eur per copy as in ISO because copyright and publication of the document itself).
      </p>
     </blockquote>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Patents">
       Patents
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Patents">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2002 and 2003 the controversy about using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reasonable_and_non-discriminatory" title="Reasonable and non-discriminatory">
       reasonable and non-discriminatory
      </a>
      (RAND)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Licensing" title="Licensing">
       licensing
      </a>
      for the use of patented technology in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_standard" title="Web standard">
       web standards
      </a>
      increased.
      <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Perens" title="Bruce Perens">
       Bruce Perens
      </a>
      , important associations as
      <a href="/wiki/Free_Software_Foundation" title="Free Software Foundation">
       FSF
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Foundation_for_a_Free_Information_Infrastructure" title="Foundation for a Free Information Infrastructure">
       FFII
      </a>
      and others have argued that the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">
       patents
      </a>
      restricts who can implement a standard to those able or willing to pay for the use of the patented technology. The
      <a href="/wiki/Requirement" title="Requirement">
       requirement
      </a>
      to pay some small amount per user, is often an insurmountable problem for free/open source software implementations which can be redistributed by anyone.
      <a href="/wiki/Royalty-free" title="Royalty-free">
       Royalty free
      </a>
      (RF) licensing is generally the only possible license for free/open source software implementations. Version 3 of the
      <a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">
       GNU General Public License
      </a>
      includes a section that enjoins anyone who distributes a program released under the GPL from enforcing patents on subsequent users of the software or derivative works.
     </p>
     <p>
      One result of this controversy was that many governments (including the Danish, French and Spanish governments singly and the EU collectively) specifically affirmed that "open standards" required royalty-free licenses. Some standards organizations, such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W3C" title="W3C">
       W3C
      </a>
      , modified their processes to essentially only permit royalty-free licensing.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oasis-Open" title="Oasis-Open">
       Oasis-Open
      </a>
      allows committees to operate either on a RAND basis or a royalty-free basis, but OASIS does say to grant "open standards" when they are not royalty-free.
     </p>
     <p>
      Patents for
      <a href="/wiki/Software_patent" title="Software patent">
       software, formulas and algorithms
      </a>
      are currently enforceable in the US but not in the EU. The
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.european-patent-office.org/legal/epc/e/ar52.html#A52_2_c" rel="nofollow">
       European Patent Convention Article 52 paragraph (2)(c)
      </a>
      expressly prohibits algorithms, business methods and software from being covered by patents. The US has only allowed them since 1989 and there has been growing controversy in recent years as to either the benefit or feasibility.
     </p>
     <p>
      A standards body and its associated processes cannot
      <i>
       force
      </i>
      a patent holder to give up its right to charge license fees, especially if the company concerned is not a member of the standards body and unconstrained by any rules that were set during the standards development process. In fact, this element discourages some standards bodies from adopting an "open" approach, fearing that they will lose out if their members are more constrained than non-members. Few bodies will carry out (or require their members to carry out) a full patent search. Ultimately, the only sanctions a standards body can apply on a non-member when patent licensing is demanded is to cancel the standard, try to rework around it, or work to invalidate the patent. Standards bodies such as W3C and OASIS require
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      that the use of required patents be granted under a royalty-free license as a condition for joining the body or a particular working group, and this is generally considered enforceable.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples of patent claims brought against standards previously thought to be open include
      <a href="/wiki/JPEG#Patent_issues" title="JPEG">
       JPEG
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Rambus" title="Rambus">
       Rambus
      </a>
      case over
      <a href="/wiki/DDR_SDRAM" title="DDR SDRAM">
       DDR SDRAM
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC#Patent_licensing" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">
       H.264 video codec
      </a>
      is an example of a standards organization producing a standard that has known, non-royalty-free required patents.
     </p>
     <p>
      Often the scope of the standard itself determines how likely it is that a firm will be able to use a standard as patent-like protection. Richard Langlois argues that standards with a wide scope may offer a firm some level of protection from competitors but it is likely that Schumpeterian creative destruction will ultimately leave the firm open to being "invented around" regardless of the standard a firm may benefit from.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langlois.2C_Richard_N_1999_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Langlois.2C_Richard_N_1999-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
