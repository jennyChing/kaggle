       <h1>
        The Good Listener: When You Need To Concentrate, Which Music Is Best?
       </h1>
       <input id="title355243180" type="hidden" value="The Good Listener: When You Need To Concentrate, Which Music Is Best?"/>
       <input id="modelShortUrl355243180" type="hidden" value="http://n.pr/1qAiRLP"/>
       <input id="modelFullUrl355243180" type="hidden" value="http://www.npr.org/sections/allsongs/2014/10/11/355243180/the-good-listener-when-you-need-to-concentrate-which-music-is-best"/>

      <div class="storytext storylocation linkLocation" id="storytext">
       <p>
        We get a lot of mail at NPR Music, and alongside the promotional ghost peppers we unwittingly spilled on our pants is a slew of smart questions about how music fits into our lives  and, this week, a request for music to fill your head with sound, but not distractions.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          Quint Smith writes via Twitter: "I'd love to see that editing playlist with Stars Of The Lid you mentioned on [Pop Culture Happy Hour] posted somewhere. I'm in need of ideas."
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <div class="bucketwrap image large" id="res355245962" previewtitle="With Twin Peaks coming back in 2016, it's a perfect time to let Angelo Badalamenti's score return to your headphones.">
        <div class="imagewrap" data-crop-type="">

         <a class="enlargebtn" href="#" title="Enlarge">

       <p>
        Okay, quick backstory: On
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/monkeysee/2014/09/26/351705779/pop-culture-happy-hour-fall-books-and-great-detectives">
         a recent episode
        </a>
        of
        <em>
         Pop Culture Happy Hour
        </em>
        , I spoke at length about an iTunes playlist I maintain called "Ambient For Editing," which contains instrumental albums by everyone from
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=123807475">
         Stars Of The Lid
        </a>
        (a double-length collection of cinematic orchestral music called
        <em>
         And Their Refinement Of The Decline
        </em>
        ) to
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/artists/124207021/pantha-du-prince">
         Pantha Du Prince
        </a>
        (dance music with bells, on a record called
        <em>
         Elements Of Light
        </em>
        ).
       </p>
       <p>
        I was describing the playlist in passing, as context for my praise of three recent additions: Aphex Twin's
        <em>
         Syro
        </em>
        (the
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/2014/09/23/350882007/a-call-from-the-weird-fringes-aphex-twins-syro">
         first album in 13 years
        </a>
        by weirdo Richard D. James), Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross'
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/2014/09/25/350948108/first-listen-trent-reznor-atticus-ross-gone-girl-motion-picture-soundtrack">
         <em>
          Gone Girl
         </em>
         soundtrack
        </a>
        (pretty, but a little hard on the nerves in spots), and
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/2011/12/05/143077187/a-winged-victory-for-the-sullen-intense-clarity">
         A Winged Victory For The Sullen
        </a>
        's
        <em>
         Atomos
        </em>
        (see above, re: Stars Of The Lid). But I also triggered an avalanche of requests for yet more recommendations. It turns out a lot of people spend their days wearing headphones and staring at computer screens, looking for music to fill their heads without distracting them from the task at hand.
       </p>
       <p>
        In response to those queries, I rattled off a long list of names and selections on this week's
        <em>
         Pop Culture Happy Hour
        </em>
        (start at
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/monkeysee/2014/10/10/355094138/pop-culture-happy-hour-gone-girl-and-what-pop-culture-can-teach">
         the 37-minute mark
        </a>
        ), but here's a synopsis of the stuff I recommend and use most often: anything by
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/event/music/316654406/music-by-arvo-p-rt-live-from-the-met-museums-temple-of-dendur">
         Arvo Prt
        </a>
        , Bing &amp; Ruth's
        <em>
         Tomorrow Was The Golden Age
        </em>
        (terrific, and
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/2014/10/05/352735195/first-listen-bing-ruth-tomorrow-was-the-golden-age">
         available for a few more days
        </a>
        through our First Listen series),
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=130019740">
         Brian McBride
        </a>
        's
        <em>
         The Effective Disconnect
        </em>
        (he's from Stars Of The Lid and it shows), anything by
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/2011/03/18/134652356/the-calm-blue-sea-beautiful-buildup-pummeling-payoff">
         The Calm Blue Sea
        </a>
        (wonderfully stormy instrumental rock, in the spirit of
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/artists/15396244/explosions-in-the-sky">
         Explosions In The Sky
        </a>
        ),
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/artists/125495328/flying-lotus">
         Flying Lotus
        </a>
        '
        <em>
         Until The Quiet Comes
        </em>
        (cluttered and zingy, but fun), Metavari's song "
        <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8lD0X78gWhk">
         Pacific Lights
        </a>
        " played over and over again (has non-distracting sampled dialogue, and also a harp),
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/event/music/151842109/nathan-salsburg-tiny-desk-concert">
         Nathan Salsburg
        </a>
        's
        <em>
         Affirmed
        </em>
        (lilting, horse-racing-themed solo acoustic guitar),
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/event/music/247205959/kexp-presents-nils-frahm">
         Nils Frahm
        </a>
        's
        <em>
         Screws
        </em>
        (slow, gorgeous solo piano),
        <a href="http://jonsiandalex.com/">
         Riceboy Sleeps
        </a>
        ' self-titled album (an ambient project from Adam Somers and
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/artists/123604659/jonsi">
         Jonsi
        </a>
        of
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/artists/15189623/sigur-r-s">
         Sigur Rs
        </a>
        ), the Latvian Radio Choir's
        <em>
         Rachmaninoff: Vespers
        </em>
        (
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/deceptivecadence/2013/04/07/176353983/vespers-habaneras-and-early-morning-walks-new-classical-albums">
         Deceptive Cadence-approved
        </a>
        choral music) and Angelo Badalamenti's
        <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pXrjMaVoTy0">
         <em>
          Twin Peaks
         </em>
         soundtrack
        </a>
        (though sense memory makes me think I'm studying for finals in college).
       </p>
       <p>
        Then, I mentioned  and swear by  two massively slowed-down versions of well-known songs. One, a
        <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bidHnEekXpE">
         35-minute version
        </a>
        of Justin Bieber's "U Smile," swept the Internet for a day or two a few years back; you can read more about it
        <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/allsongs/2010/08/17/129254788/the-pop-artistry-of-justin-bieber-celestial-choirboy">
         here
        </a>
        . The other, a gorgeous
        <a href="https://soundcloud.com/birdfeeder/jurassic-park-theme-1000-slower">
         54-minute elongation
        </a>
        of the
        <em>
         Jurassic Park
        </em>
        theme, was recommended to me in the wake of the Bieber song. Both fill my headphones on a regular basis, to the point where I probably don't even notice half the time Bob Boilen strikes the accursed gong on his desk.
       </p>
       <p>
        Because people are
        <em>
         still
        </em>
        asking me for more recommendations, and because I always welcome new music for editing, let's crowd-source this thing: Please list your own concentration soundtrack in the comments section. Trust me, people are looking for the tips.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Got a music-related question you want answered? Leave it in the comments, drop us an email at
         <a href="mailto:allsongs@npr.org?subject=Question">
          allsongs@npr.org
         </a>
         or tweet
         <a href="http://www.twitter.com/allsongs">
          @allsongs
         </a>
        </em>
        .
       </p>
