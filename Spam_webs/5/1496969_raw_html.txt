      <h1 class="asset-headline" itemprop="headline">
       DEQ tightens rules on residential oil-gas drilling
      </h1>
     </section>
     <section class="storytopbar-bucket story-byline-module" id="module-position-OQoCkbgcGwk">

      <p>
       Oil and gas drilling in highly populated residential areas will require more protections for neighbors under new instructions announced Tuesday by the state Department of Environmental Quality's Supervisor of Wells.
      </p>
      <p>
       But residents of metro Detroit townships  like Shelby and Scio  who already have endured round-the-clock noise, bright lights and other nuisances from oil well construction near their homes last summer said the regulatory changes don't do enough to protect communities.
      </p>
      <p>
       The DEQ's new instructions for oil and gas developers include requirements to provide notice to local governments before projects commence, groundwater monitoring, containing drill cuttings and fluids in tanks, noise reduction and ceasing drilling-related truck traffic during overnight hours. However, the new regulation only applies in a county with a population of 750,000 people or more. Only three counties in Michigan meet that population figure: Wayne, Oakland and Macomb. The regulation also only applies when the drilling location is zoned for residential activity and  40 or more structures for public or private occupancy exist within 1,320 feet of the well location.
      </p>

      <p>
       The changes come from a task force convened after public outcry caused by an oil well built in a zoned residential area of Macomb County's Shelby Township last August  only about 500 feet from a neighborhood. Noisy drilling  occurred on a 24-7 basis for about three weeks, along with bright lights at night and truck and heavy equipment traffic.
      </p>
      <p>
       The new rules "require a lot of protections in terms of noise, lighting, berming and screening," said DEQ Supervisor of Wells Hal Fitch.
      </p>
      <p>
       "It provides transparency, notice to residents. It requires a thorough analysis of alternate drilling locations  a company's got to tell us why there's not a better location to drill from that's farther from residential development."
      </p>
      <p>
       But what's missing from the regulation change  increased setbacks between homes and wells  is "unacceptable," said Laura Krempa, whose home is less than 600 feet from the well installed by West Bay Exploration in Shelby Township last summer.
      </p>

      <p>
       "Our real issue isn't, 'You can't do drilling in residential areas.' It's 'You need to be at least 1,000 feet away from a house,'
       <span class="-newsgate--ccix-command-133">
        " she said, adding the DEQ "would never address the setback issue."
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       The instruction comes after several meetings of a task force consisting of representatives from state government, the Michigan Townships Association, the Michigan Oil and Gas Association, and members of the public, Fitch said. He said he recognizes the new instruction to oil and gas developers will not satisfy everyone.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There are some who would just like to prohibit oil and gas development," he said. "The problem with that is, the mineral owners and the companies leasing the minerals have constitutional rights that you have to honor. You can't just deny people their property rights, unless you pay them for it."
      </p>
      <p>
       Laura Robinson, an organizer of the nonprofit group Citizens for Oil-Free Backyards, convened after a similar oil well controversy in Scio Township last August, welcomed some of the changes, but said they don't go far enough.
      </p>

      <p>
       The  counting of homes within 1,320 feet of a well location "is a completely arbitrary number  noise doesn't respect boundaries like that," she said.
      </p>
      <p>
       The rule only applying when a well is near 40 or more homes in a residentially zoned area is also problematic, she said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Obviously that doesn't allow for less-dense areas to be covered," she said.  "There's no reason it should just apply to a particular region. And that doesn't allow for this to affect anything in mixed-used areas  that's what primarily exists in townships."
      </p>
      <p>
       Though requiring an analysis of alternative well sites farther from homes is a positive, "an evaluation is meaningless without any kind of follow-through and guidelines," Robinson said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Fitch confirmed that the new rules would not have affected  the controversial Scio Township well, which was built in an agricultural area that also doesn't meet the nearby home density requirement.
      </p>
      <p>
       The new rules take effect for all oil and gas development that starts  in the state after Feb. 24.
      </p>
      <p>
       "One of the advantages of an instruction, as opposed to more formal rule-making, is that we can turn it around in a short period of time," Fitch said. "We're going to look at how this works, examine it and make adjustments as the need arises."
      </p>

      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-paragraph-cci-infobox-head-">
        <b>
         Highlights of new state oil and gas drilling rules in residential areas
        </b>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       New instructions apply to Michigan counties with a population of 750,000 or more people. Only Wayne, Oakland and Macomb counties meet that threshold.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Applies to oil or gas well locations in areas zoned residential.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Applies to areas where 40 or more homes or public occupancy buildings are within 1,320 feet of the well site.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Township supervisor and residents within 1,320 feet of a proposed well location must be notified before work starts.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Permit applications must include an analysis showing why alternative drilling locations farther  from homes aren't feasible.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Actions must be taken to reduce nuisance noise, and non-emergency truck traffic and heavy equipment movement must be stopped between 8 p.m. and 8 a.m.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Drilling operations must have shielded rig lighting, perimeter fencing, site security and an on-site supervisor qualified in well safety.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Material Safety Data sheets must be posted for chemicals used or stored at the well site.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-macro-cci-z-sym-square-bullet-">
        
       </span>
       Gases must be captured or burned.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="-newsgate-paragraph-cci-infobox-endnote-">
        <i>
         Source: Michigan Department of Environmental Quality
        </i>
       </span>
