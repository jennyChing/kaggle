        <div class="body viziwyg-editable viziwyg-field-2237134-BODY viziwyg-section-7878">
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           Texas job growth continued to be pulled down by low oil prices in February as the state reported the smallest monthly job gain since October 2011.
          </p>
          <p>
           Texas added 7,100 jobs in February, the second straight month of slower growth, according to government data released Friday. Job losses in oil and related industries offset large gains in other areas.
          </p>
          <p>
           This is consistent with my view that the economy of Texas already is feeling the drag of lower oil prices, said Robert A. Dye, chief economist for Dallas-based Comerica Bank.
          </p>
          <p>
           The strong U.S. dollars impact on exports also hurt oil and manufacturing jobs last month.
          </p>
          <p>
           Texas January number was revised downward to 13,100 new jobs. That means the state has generated an average of 10,100 a month so far this year vs. an average 34,000 a month last year. The employment growth rate declined to 3.1 percent for the 12 months through February, down from 3.5 percent for the 12 months through January.
          </p>
          <p>
           Nationwide, February employment rose in 36 states plus Washington, D.C., fell in 13 states and was the same in Wyoming, the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reported Friday. California ranked No. 1 with 29,400 jobs; Texas was No. 18.
          </p>
          <p>
           Oil prices have declined more than 50 percent since July. While low energy prices help consumers by putting more money in their pockets, it hurts oil-heavy states like Texas as companies trim production, reduce capital spending and lay off workers.
          </p>
          <p>
           In 2014, Texas added 407,400 jobs, the second most nationally after California. But economists expected the states economy and job growth to slow this year because of low oil prices.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Texas oil industry lost 3,500 jobs in February after falling a revised 4,300 jobs in January  the sectors first job losses since fall 2009.
          </p>
          <p>
           Houston-based oil company Baker Hughes said it would cut 7,000 jobs globally, most by the end of this month, as it faces projected declines in product sales and oil well construction by customers. In February, Baker Hughes laid off 350 people in Texas, according to documents filed with the state.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Houston, Dallas, Fort Worth, Midland and Odessa areas showed gains in oil, gas and construction jobs last month, according to data from the Texas Workforce Commission. But economists note that those numbers are volatile because theyre not adjusted for seasonal variations and some of the job losses may be in rural areas without detailed data.
          </p>
          <p>
           Eagle Ford isnt in an MSA [metropolitan statistical area]; its out in the boonies, said Bud Weinstein, an energy economist at Southern Methodist Universitys Maguire Energy Institute in Dallas. The good news is that ... [the Dallas-Fort Worth area] ought to be relatively insulated from the oil and gas slowdown because we have a much smaller concentration of oil and gas.
          </p>
          <p>
           While Texas is the nations No. 1 oil-producing state, oil and gas jobs make up less than 3 percent of total employment.
          </p>
          <p>
           In February, Texas manufacturing lost 9,500 jobs  more than any other state  and professional and business services lost 4,000 jobs, according to BLS data. Economists say both, at least in part, are related to the oil industry and support services.
          </p>
          <p>
           In addition to less demand for manufactured goods in the oil and gas industry, a strong U.S. dollar and a weaker trade environment in Latin America are making American-made products more expensive, said Boyd Nash-Stacey, an economist for BBVA Compass bank. Texas exports have declined in the last few months.
          </p>
          <p>
           Despite the slower job growth, Texas seasonally adjusted unemployment rate dipped from 4.4 percent in January to 4.3 percent in February as more people found work or dropped out of the labor force. The Dallas-Fort Worth areas jobless rate, which is not seasonally adjusted, fell from 4.4 percent in January to 4.1 percent
          </p>
          <p>
           The states labor force shrank by 380 workers in February, the first decline since March 2007, said Comericas Dye. It shows that job growth is no longer pulling people into the workforce, especially with layoffs looming, he explained.
          </p>
          <p>
           Although six of 11 Texas industries lost jobs, certain areas continued to see fast growth. Trade, transportation and utilities added the most jobs in February  16,300, its largest monthly increase in more than 20 years. Leisure and hospitality added 9,700 jobs in February. Construction added 1,600 jobs but at a slower than usual pace for the second straight month.
          </p>
          <p>
           Dallas-based AT&amp;T is looking to hire more than 500 people for retail, technician and call center jobs in North Texas.
          </p>
          <p>
           We consistently hire in our growth areas like wireless, video, cloud services and mobility applications, said AT&amp;T spokesman Marty Richter. Last year, the telecommunications company hired nearly 29,000 people nationwide, including about 5,300 in Texas, he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           While more companies are expected to expand or relocate to North Texas and hire workers, economists expect oil industry woes to keep job growth this year to below 2 percent, down from 3.6 percent last year.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its still a matter of time, said Cheryl Abbot, a BLS regional economist in Dallas. How long will oil prices stay low? Thats the key to when the impact goes beyond [oil industry] jobs and starts impacting the entire economy.
          </p>
          <p>

          </p>
