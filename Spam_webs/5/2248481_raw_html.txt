             <div class="article-body">
              <p>
               Being a working mother is tough. Being a working mother whos also a leader (and many of us
               <em>
                do
               </em>
               lead in some capacity, even if its not officially part of our job description) is even tougher. We must not only do a great job and inspire others to do the same, we must also do that while also caring for colicky babies or finicky toddlers or know-it-all tweens and teens.
              </p>
              <p>
               It will never be easy. But if we embrace our feminine strengths rather than trying to man up in the workplace, it gets a lot more doable. When we lead like a girl (to paraphrase the Always campaign) were not fighting our natural tendencieswhich leaves us the energy we need to devote to those we love the most.
              </p>
              <p>
               Its true. Women
               <em>
               </em>
               think and communicate differently, which means we also lead differently. And because our natural skill set is increasingly valued in the global economy, were perfectly positioned to become todays and tomorrows leaders.
              </p>
              <p>
               I have always felt women have the raw materials to be exceptional leaders. And when I collaborated with my 19 amazing co-authors to write
               <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Leading-Women-Influential-Leadership-Business/dp/1440584176/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1421869900&amp;sr=1-1" target="_blank">
                <em>
                 Leading Women
                </em>
               </a>
               <em>
                : 20 Influential Women Share Their Secrets to Leadership, Business, and Life,
               </em>
               that belief was only reinforced.
              </p>
              <p>
               Based on what I learned from them, here are 10 ways to use your feminine strengths to lead like a girl:
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                1. Re-think what power means to you.
               </strong>
               It doesnt have to be command and control leadership. My co-author Gloria Feldt explains that instead of seeking power over, women are more comfortable seeking the power to. Feminine power is the ability to accomplish our goals, provide for our families and make the world a better placeand to help others do the same.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                2. Dont try to go it alone.
               </strong>
               Asking for help is an efficient and effective way to get things done. Women have long realized the benefits of tapping into the resources and expertise of others, from Will you watch the kids? to Whats your advice? to Can we work together on this? We dont mind admitting what we dont know and are willing to share the credit, which means we are good at spotting problems and making sure they get fixed.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                3.
               </strong>
               <strong>
                Dont ask, What can you do for me? Instead ask, What can we create together?
               </strong>
               This moves you away from networkingthe mile-wide-inch-deep world of social media and insincere business card exchangesand toward the deep, mutually fulfilling connections that women crave. This is Connecting 2.0its the powerful force behind the women-helping-women movement thats changing the playing field for women in business, government, education, philanthropy and other fields. It feels good and it works.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                4. When appropriate, get a little personal.
               </strong>
               We show empathy and caring when we deal with our kids, of course. But with employees and colleagues? Sure. Womens ability to nurture relationships can actually be a huge business asset. The quality of a leaders relationships with peers and employees has a major impact on company culture and morale, and thus productivity and growth.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                5. Reach out and help someone.
               </strong>
               Women know the significance of a helping hand, mutual support and mentorship, and we value the satisfaction and meaning that come from aiding others. In the workplace, this ability can mean the difference between being a boss and being a leadera distinction that creates employee buy-in and engagement.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                6. Go ahead and talk. Its okay.
               </strong>
               Because women are seen as talkative and chatty, many of us make a conscious effort to hold their tongues in professional settings. Not necessary. Claire Damken Brown, PhD (another co-author), says research has found that women talk to exchange information and establish cohesion, which actually makes us better leaders.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                7. Try your hand at public speaking.
               </strong>
               Lois Phillips, PhD, says women have a natural affinity for it. We tend to provide information to help listeners achieve their goals, rather than to establish dominance over the group or negotiate status. We also want to connect to our audience and have an innate ability to read and respond to their nonverbal cues.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                8. Choose to see
               </strong>
               <strong>
                obstacles as teachable moments and stepping stones.
               </strong>
               Women have an ability to reframe what happens to us. (M. Bridget Cook-Burch tackles this subject in
               <em>
                Leading Women
               </em>
               .) If your company is failing in one area, you might see that failure as a springboard to move in a fresh new direction. Being able to shift your focus away from what you dont want to the things youd like to create can help your entire organization become more future-focused and productive.
               <strong>
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
               </strong>
               <strong>
                9.
               </strong>
               <strong>
                Unlock your teams collective intelligence.
               </strong>
               This calls for
               <em>
                true
               </em>
               collaboration, which
               <strong>
               </strong>
               Birute Regine, EdD, says requires participants to read nonverbal cues and others emotions, to use empathy, to put ego aside and to be sensitive to fairness and turn-taking. All are feminine skills. Without them, collaboration can easily devolve into group-think and follow-the-leader.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                10. Listen to your instincts.
               </strong>
               Society loves telling women how we should think, feel or live. (Consider, for example, the disapproval working mothers have long faced.) It can be easy to internalize these voices. Dont let it happen.
               <strong>
               </strong>
               When facing opposition or making a decision, tune in to how youre feelingphysically, spiritually and emotionally. If youre headed in a good direction, you should feel alive and energized.
              </p>
              <p>
               As women and as working mothers, it truly is our time to step up and take our place as leaders. When we focus and hone our feminine skills, we can make a positive impact not just on our companies but also on our communities and our worldwhich is the best thing we can do for our children.
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
              <p>
