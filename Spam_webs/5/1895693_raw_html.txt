         Coaches Gathering: Best Strength Training Exercises For Runners
        </h1>

        <div class="entry-content">
         <div class="post-picture">
          <img alt="" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/themes/OptimizePress/timthumb.php?src=http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Types-Of-Exercises-e1412790942496.jpg&amp;h=200&amp;w=200&amp;zc=1" style="border:1px solid #c0c0c0;float:right;"/>

         <p>
          We have talked about strength training a lot in recent weeks. We have covered its benefits, from helping runners to be stronger and less prone to injuries to perfecting running form and improve performance. We have also provided several examples of strength training routines that you can do at home or in the gym.
         </p>
         <p>
          Well, a couple of days ago, we decided to gather some running coaches from all over the world and ask them this simple question:
         </p>
         <p>
          If you can recommend acouple ofstrength training exercises for runners, what would they be and why?
         </p>
         <p>
          These coaches are kind enough to share some of their insights with us and now, we will share them with you, our readers, as well. You can bet that every single information from these coaches is a real gem as they are not some wannabe coaches. They are coaches in real life and have worked with thousands of runners and athletes. They have also regularly written articles for authoritative running magazine, such as Runners World.
         </p>
         <p>
          Without further ado, let us begin.
          <em>
           note: order based on the firstcoach who replied to our email.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coach Janet Hamilton
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_659" style="width: 177px">
          <img alt="Janet Hamilton" class="wp-image-659 size-full" height="167" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Janet-Hamilton.png" width="167"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Janet Hamilton
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Janet Hamilton, Exercise Physiologist, Certified Strength and Conditioning Specialist and Running Coach with
          <a href="http://www.runningstrong.com/" target="_blank">
           RunningStrong.com
          </a>
          recommends the following:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            Strength training exercises for runners should mimic the biomechanical demands of running. I recommend starting with body weight only, and working up to a point where a set of 25-30 repetitions of the exercise is easily accomplished, then if additional challenge is desired the athlete can use light weights for resistance. Two of my favorites include:
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Squats 
            </strong>
            stand with feet a shoulders distance apart and weight evenly distributed, there should be a chair or weight bench behind you . While keeping your feet flat on the floor, flex your knees and hips so that your buttocks just barely brush the seat of the chair, then come back up to standing. If you keep your heels down on the floor as instructed, youll be forced to flex your hips and knees properly. Keeping your back straight does not mean keeping it vertical! Keep your knees lined up straight, dont let them cave in toward each other. If you cant get to chair depth without lifting your heels, then stop at whatever depth you can go  and gradually work your way into the full range of motion as you are able to. A proper squat position has your chest over your knees, and your knees over your feet, and your feet flat on the floor.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Lunges 
            </strong>
            Stand with your feet side-by-side, then take a large step forward with your right leg and simultaneously flex the right hip and knee so that you lower your body toward the floor. Your left knee will flex and depending on your strength and balance, you may be able to go low enough to actually brush that left knee to the floor. Push back up to return to the starting position with your feet side-by-side. Repeat several times on one leg, then shift and do the same with the other leg. At the deepest point of the lunge its important to keep your torso erect, your hips stable, and your knee lined up with the hip (not caved in!). Work your way into the full-depth lunge by doing shallow lunges first and insuring that you have the strength and balance to accomplish the motion with no complaint from your knees. Form is important!
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coach Jay Johnson
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_664" style="width: 130px">
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Jay-Johnson.png">
           <img alt="Jay Johnson" class="wp-image-664 size-full" height="120" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Jay-Johnson.png" width="120"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Jay Johnson
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Jay from
          <a href="http://www.coachjayjohnson.com/" target="_blank">
           CoachJayJohnson.com
          </a>
          has at least three students who have won the United States Track and Field national titles in indoor track, cross country and the roads. In his email to us he said:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            In my coaching there would be many more than three exercises and many would be
            <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
             <strong>
              mobility exercises
             </strong>
            </span>
            .
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Jay then shared his
          <a href="http://www.coachjayjohnson.com/2011/11/eight-week-general-strength-progression/" target="_blank">
           Eight Week General Strength Progression Plan
          </a>
          to answer our question and share his strength training exercises.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coach Mike Hamberger
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_669" style="width: 133px">
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Mike-Hamberger.jpg">
           <img alt="Mike Hamberger" class=" wp-image-669" height="130" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Mike-Hamberger.jpg" width="123"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Mike Hamberger
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Mike is the operator of
          <a href="http://www.dcrunningcoach.com" target="_blank">
           DC Running Coach
          </a>
          . He is a former multi-sport athlete whose run-splits consistently ranked at the top of the field. In 2010, Mike finished his 3rd Boston Marathon, setting a new PR at 2:46:33, and at the Army 10-Miler he finished in the top-100 overall for the second time.
         </p>
         <p>
          In operating DC Running Coach since 2006, Mike has been correcting running form in athletes training from 5k to marathon, from couch potato to Olympic hopeful, and from ages 8 to 80, including scholastic athletes of all ages, sports, and experience. In Mikes email to answer our question, he said:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Single-leg squats (for which I will broaden the definition to include walking lunges)
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Running is a continuous series of single-leg squats, even if they are not full squats, or at least its supposed to be if we are running correctly with a midfoot strike (most runners are heel striking and therefore not hitting the ground with the proper gentle bend in the knee). The principle of specificity of training requires that the strength training be run-specific, so single-leg exercises are the best bet for a runner (double-leg exercises are old school). Assuming flawless technique and done with shoes off, single-leg squats will primarily target the glutes and feet, with secondary activation in the quads, hips, abs and back. Theres lots of variation in how this exercise can be done.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            The advantage of walking lunges is that they follow all of the same principles of technique as single-leg squats, but they allow the athlete to increase the range of motion and therefore hip, glute and hamstring activation are increased. Advanced ways to do walking lunges include slowly rotating a medicine ball side to side while walking, or by holding a 20- to 50-pound bar overhead (depending on the overall strength of the person). The latter is very run-specific, as core engagement is now near its peak.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Single-leg planks
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Similar to the rationale of single-leg squats, this exercise is very run-specific. If the average runner is taking 15,000  20,000 single-leg steps in a half-marathon, then we need to train in a single-leg stance fashion. Place the forearms on the ground parallel to each other (holding the hands together is cheating, as it steals the ab activation from the exercise and defeats the whole purpose) and perform single-leg reps by raising one leg up to a 30-45 degree angle in slow-motion while keeping this moving leg straight. You will notice that the leg moving in the air requires glute engagement, but the primary purpose of this exercise is to strengthen the hip and quad that are bracing on the ground. Ab and back activation are included. The number of reps will range from 2  15 per set, as this is a difficult exercise if done with perfect/proper form.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Single-leg bridges
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            This is the best way to strengthen the hamstrings for runners. The precise technique is difficult to explain in a type-written fashion, as there is an exponential difference between flawless form and good form. Begin by laying on your back and feet in the ground about 6-10 inches away from your butt (keep only 2-4 inches between the feet). Raise the back and butt off the ground so that only the shoulder blades, head and feet (and arms) are touching the ground. Now extend one leg at a 45-degree angle to the ground and hold it there as long as you can without pulling a hamstring in the leg thats on the ground. This eccentric (resisting) action of the hamstring of the leg on the ground is the same way we activate our hammies while running (during the swing phase/action of the stride), especially the faster we run (assuming proper run mechanics, like midfoot strike and optimum stride length). Be sure to keep the entire foot and toes pressed into the ground. The glutes are activated as a secondary muscle group. A set on each leg will last ~4-30 seconds. If someone can do them longer than this, than I would suggest a more advanced style (or check their form).
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coach Rory Coleman
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_672" style="width: 138px">
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Rory-Coleman.jpg">
           <img alt="Rory Coleman" class="wp-image-672 size-full" height="128" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Rory-Coleman.jpg" width="128"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Rory Coleman
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Rory is acoachbased in Cardiff, Wales. Hespecializes in coaching athletes for marathons, ultra-marathons and beyond.He hascompleted over 830 marathons, 225 Ultra-marathons and 9 Guinness World Records for treadmill running. He shares his thoughts and coaching online at
          <a href="http://www.rorycoleman.co.uk" target="_blank">
           http://www.rorycoleman.co.uk
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Rory hasa Diploma in Personal Training and qualified as an Advanced Level Three Instructor with additional qualifications in Nutrition Management, Ante/Post Natal, Obesity, Training in Special Environments and Health Screening. In his email to us, Rory did not only share about the three strength exercises he thought runners should do but also other crucial pieces of information, such as when is the right time to do them. Here is Rorys full answer:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            I believe that runners should try and make themselves as physically strong as possible as the misconception is just to runs lots of miles 
            <strong>
             Press ups, Seated Row and Shoulder Presses
            </strong>
            are ideal to improve upper body strength and weights that can be lifted 10-20 times to failure.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Always do them
            <strong>
             BEFORE
            </strong>
            [a run] as after a run or treadmill session as you are always too sweaty and can get very cold too quickly and psychologically more likely
            <strong>
             NOT
            </strong>
            to do them.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Its another way of training your body as ULTRA-runners need a break from all those miles but still need the buzz from exercise. All of my clients have strength work built into their macro-cycles three times a week and they all say they now
            <strong>
             LOVE
            </strong>
            the gym.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            My Coaching Ethos is more of a Holistic approach to preparing the body for the Ultimate tests that folk self-impose. All over fitness especially in mega-day races is a pre-requisite in my book and being a lean mean fighting machine that looks the part helps.
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coach Amanda Loudin
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_676" style="width: 130px">
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Amanda-Loudin.jpg">
           <img alt="Amanda Loudin" class="wp-image-676 size-full" height="120" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Amanda-Loudin.jpg" width="120"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Amanda Loudin
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Amanda is a mom, writer, and RRCA certified running coach. She debutedin endurance sports in 1998 andsince then have completed an Ironman, 12 marathons, and literally hundreds of races of every distance under the sun.
         </p>
         <p>
          Amanda iscommitted to natural running. She isa former orthotic/stability shoe wearer and since early 2011, have changed herways. Shenow believe in strong feet and less shoe. Youll find plenty of information about natural running on her website
          <a href="http://misszippy1.com" target="_blank">
           MissZippy1
          </a>
          . Here is Amandas answer:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Single-leg deadlifts:
            </strong>
            I love this move. You get so much out of itbalance, a great glute activation, and all around runner muscle recruitment.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Push-ups:
            </strong>
            Another bang for your buck move. You get upper body activity along with great abdominal recruitment. Make them harder by using a stability ball and pulling ball into chest using lower abs after each push up.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Hamstring curls with a pike using a stability ball:
            </strong>
            Place feet on top of stability ball in a bridge position and pull the ball toward you using hamstrings and core. Then lift (pike) hips into higher bridge and straighten legs back out while keeping hips lifted. Great for core, stability and hamstrings.
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Coach John Goldthorp
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_678" style="width: 131px">
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/John.jpg">
           <img alt="John" class=" wp-image-678" height="197" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/John.jpg" width="121"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           John Goldthorp
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          John is afitness professional in Center City, Philadelphia and the owner of
          <a href="http://www.fixyourrun.com" target="_blank">
           Fix YourRun
          </a>
          . He has been in the businessfor over thirteen years.He holdsbachelors degree in Kinesiology from Penn State University which formed the foundation of hismovement education.
         </p>
         <p>
          As an ACE Certified Personal Trainer, Johnworks with clients of all ages and diverse backgrounds. John has also beencertified by the Road Runners Club of America in order to help new and experienced runners achieve their own bragging rights by developing safe, yet effective training programs. Here is Johns answer to us:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           <em>
            If I HAD to pick 3 strength training exercises for runners, Id pick the
            <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
             <strong>
              Hip Thrust, Single Leg Deadlift, and the Suitcase Carry
             </strong>
            </span>
            . Im sure theres PLENTY of room for debate here, too!
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Now, before I discuss why I honed in on those, we have to realize that strength training is a skill and you absolutely must demonstrate competency in basic moves before more advanced exercises are attempted. So, be patient, and work up to more advanced movements as you become proficient and confident. That said, I picked two relatively easy moves and one that is harder.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Since the average runner is typically sedentary a majority of the day, Ill focus on strengthening the hips and core. But make no mistake, one can develop a decent amount of strength throughout the whole body by doing these.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Hip Thrust
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            This relatively basic movement pattern is nearly unrivaled for backside development and simplicity. Starting with your upper back across a bench, you simply hinge at the hips to lower yourself then squeeze your butt muscles hard to rise up and finish. The Hip Thrust and the Single Leg Deadlift (described below) are fantastic choices for runners because they develop strength in your hip extensors (your butt!) which are so crucial for producing a powerful stride AND controlling unwanted motion at the knee.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            I like to focus on building some endurance and coordination first before progressing to strength. Try 2 sets of 15-20 reps in the first month before progressing down to 3 sets of 6-10 reps.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/cBrHdatPj9g?rel=0&amp;showinfo=0" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Single Leg Deadlift
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            I chose this type of deadlift because I feel that if a runner can demonstrate beautiful, symmetrical form, then they are likely someone who is functioning at a high level. By that I mean that they have excellent mobility AND control over their body.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            While I love the classic deadlift for the total body strength it builds, the single leg version is more specific to runners because there is a strong rotational component and we need to be able to control that rotation. Additionally, when you hold a weight in the non-weight bearing arm you amplify the anti-rotation core challenge; the weight wants to pull you into a flexed, twisted spine. Your challenge is to resist that.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Once youve mastered the form (and that may take a while  start unweighted at first), shoot for 2-3 sets of 6-8 reps per side.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/7uA1PLNIWK8?rel=0&amp;showinfo=0" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Suitcase Carry
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Finally, I love the suitcase carry for its simplicity (just hold a weight in one hand at your side and walk!) and awesome core challenge to the side of the body which is a plane of motion in which runners are notoriously weak. A couple of easy to follow rules here: 1) Maintain perfect posture as you walk 2) Crush your grip and 3) Do NOT allow your feet to cross paths. Sometimes Ill lay down a long stretch of rope and straddle the rope as I walk to remind myself not to cross my steps.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Try walking for 30-50 paces or 20-30 seconds. The goal is to carry as much weight as you can with perfect form.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: center;">
           <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/tLFaKhew3To?rel=0&amp;showinfo=0" width="560">
           </iframe>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p style="text-align: left;">
          <strong>
           Coach Brendan
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_689" style="width: 131px">
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/brendan.jpg">
           <img alt="brendan" class="wp-image-689 " height="156" src="http://ultrarunners.info/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/brendan.jpg" width="121"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Coach Brendan
          </p>
         </div>
         <p style="text-align: left;">
          Coach Brendan shares his coaching at
          <a href="http://www.coachbrendan.com" target="_blank">
           CoachBrendan.com
          </a>
          and he says:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <div class="gmail_default">
           <em>
            Three great strengthening training exercises for my runners are core exercises of planks (hold for 20-30 seconds) and hold in normal front position and side plank position; beach runs where the runner runs for 15 seconds to one minute on the sand; and a circuit workout using a variety of resistance bands and weights to improve strength and fitness
           </em>
          </div>
         </blockquote>
         <p style="text-align: left;">
          So, these are the top strength training tips for runners from highly reputable coaches all over the world. The conclusion that we can summarize is that all coaches agree that incorporating strength exercisesinto your running training program can and will benefit you as a runner. Somecoaches also suggest that if you do not have access to weights or gym classes, you can always do strength training exercises using
          <a href="http://ultrarunners.info/strength-training-for-runners-top-4-bodyweight-exercises/" target="_blank">
           your own body weight
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: left;">
          We hope you enjoy our first coaches gathering and there will be more of this in the future covering other topics, such as nutrition, injury recovery among others.
         </p>
