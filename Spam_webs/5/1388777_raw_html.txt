     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Etoro
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       Edolo
      </b>
      , are a tribe and ethnic group of the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Highlands_Province" title="Southern Highlands Province">
       Southern Highlands Province
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">
       Papua New Guinea
      </a>
      . Their territory comprises the southern slopes of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mt._Sisa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mt. Sisa (page does not exist)">
       Mt. Sisa
      </a>
      , along the southern edge of the central mountain range of
      <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">
       New Guinea
      </a>
      , near the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papuan_Plateau" title="Papuan Plateau">
       Papuan Plateau
      </a>
      . They are well known among
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropologists
      </a>
      because of ritual
      <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">
       homosexual acts
      </a>
      practised between the young boys and men of the tribe. The Etoro believe that young boys must
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semen_ingestion" title="Semen ingestion">
       ingest the semen
      </a>
      of their elders daily from the age of 7 until they turn 17 to achieve adult male status and to properly mature and grow strong.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Etoro believe that they each contain a certain amount of
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_(esotericism)" title="Energy (esotericism)">
       life force
      </a>
      , the highest concentrations of which are contained in semen. This life force passes to others through sexual relations. Women are seen to waste the life force if they do not get pregnant after sexual intercourse. As people get older, and their bodies weaken, this is attributed to a depletion of their life force.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2009, the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">
       National Geographic Society
      </a>
      reported an estimate that there were less than 1668 speakers of the Etoro/Edolo language
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://travel.nationalgeographic.com.au/travel/enduring-voices/png-expedition/" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">
       Marriage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Etoro_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Marriage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      O'Neil and Kottak agree that most men marry and have heterosexual relations with their wives. The fear that heterosexual sex causes them to die earlier and the belief that homosexual sex prolongs life means that heterosexual relations are focused towards reproduction.
     </p>
