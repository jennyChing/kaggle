    <div class="box" id="section_0">
     <span>
      <h3 class="pagetitle">
       <img alt="Sports and Action" height="20" src="images/img_macro_label_01.png" width="205"/>
      </h3>
     </span>
     <img alt="Photo @ Chase Jarvis" height="350" src="images/macro/img_01.jpg" width="710"/>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
    </div>
    <span class="credit">
     Photo  Chase Jarvis
    </span>
    <div class="intro">
     <p>
      In general, macro photography is simply the close up photos of subjects.  The apparent magnification of subjects involved with macro photography can really open your eyes to the wonderful textures and details of objects that we come across every day. Macro photographs can fascinate, surprise, and even frighten viewers through the details they reveal.
     </p>
    </div>
    <div class="box" id="section_1">
     <span>
      <h3 class="secondary_title">
       <img alt="Macro: Setting Your Camera For Macro" src="images/img_macro_label_02.png"/>
      </h3>
     </span>
     <img alt="Labybug shot using 100mm macro lens" height="350" src="images/macro/img_02.jpg" width="710"/>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <p class="caption">
      This is a typical macro photo of a ladybug shot using a 100mm macro lens. Note the following characteristics of this photo that are shared by just about all macro photos: 1) the subject appears magnified beyond what you are used to seeing 2) the depth of field is extremely shallow, and 3) The metering is set to expose the subject well.
     </p>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="desc">
      <p>
       Setting your camera for a macro shot generally involves:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Opening the lens aperture to its largest setting, and selecting spot focus.
       </li>
       <li>
        A high shutter speed must be also be set as macro  subjects are so small, any tiny camera movement has the potential to cause blurring in the resulting photo.
       </li>
       <li>
        So consider setting a higher ISO to achieve a higher shutter speed as well.
       </li>
       <li>
        Most cameras have a macro preset mode that helps you to automatically optimize them for macro shots.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="box" id="section_2">
     <span>
      <h3 class="secondary_title">
       <img alt="Macro: Macro Subject Selection and Settings" src="images/img_macro_label_03.png"/>
      </h3>
     </span>
     <div class="mutiple_rows">
      <div class="row">
       <span class="caption" style="padding-top:40px;">
        Flowers are a perennial favourite macro subject for their intricate details that can be revealed through macro photography.
       </span>
       <img alt="Macro shot of a flower" height="620" src="images/macro/img_03_a.jpg" width="470"/>
      </div>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
      <div class="row">
       <span class="caption">
        Just about anything with an interesting shape or texture can make for a good abstract macro subject  like this close up of a one centimetre wide Japanese wall ornament.
       </span>
       <img alt="A japanese wall ornament" height="620" src="images/macro/img_03_b.jpg" width="470"/>
      </div>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
      <div class="row">
       <span class="caption">
        Insects are popular among master macro photographers for the greater degree of difficulty involved in shooting them and their alien-like appearance when photographed close-up.
       </span>
       <img alt="Closeup of grasshooper" height="310" src="images/macro/img_03_c.jpg" width="470"/>
      </div>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="desc">
      <p>
       Just about any small object with an interesting texture or appearance can be a good macro subject. However, the most popular subjects are the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <h4>
         Flowers and plants
        </h4>
        Flowers and plants are popular macro subjects as they are motionless and easy   
      to shoot, yet they offer a broad range of textures and shapes that make for a 
      good macro photo.
       </li>
       <li>
        <h4>
         Still life objects with interesting shapes or textures
        </h4>
        Any small object with an interesting shape or texture can make a good macro  
      subject. However, for greatest effect, a macro photographer should present that 
      subject in a way that surprises viewers at its latent details  that they never 
      knew existed.
       </li>
       <li>
        <h4>
         Insects
        </h4>
        Insects are perhaps the most challenging, yet gratifying macro subjects to shoot.    
      They require not only the patience to either stalk them or wait for them to appear 
      in proper shooting distance at just the right place, they also need quick reflexes
      to set your camera, focus on them and shoot before they move again.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="box" id="section_3">
     <span>
      <h3 class="secondary_title">
       <img alt="Macro: Manual Focusing May Be Better" src="images/img_macro_label_04.png"/>
      </h3>
     </span>
     <div class="mutiple_rows">
      <div class="row">
       <span class="caption" style="padding-top:40px;">
        This Madressilva flower bud took up such a small portion of the frame that the photographers lens kept focusing on the background instead. For macro shots like these, it is best to mount your camera on a tripod and carefully focus manually.
       </span>
       <img alt="Drifting car" height="330" src="images/macro/img_04_a.jpg" width="470"/>
      </div>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
      <div class="row">
       <span class="caption">
        This fly would have been difficult to shoot using a manual lens that kept on searching for focus as it would scare it away. In such cases, carefully approaching an insect and manually focusing may be a better approach.
        <br/>
       </span>
       <img alt="Fly" height="350" src="images/macro/img_04_b.jpg" width="470"/>
      </div>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="desc">
      <p>
       Many macro subjects are so small or take up such a small portion of the frame that a cameras lens may have trouble focusing on it. In these cases, setting your camera to a manual focus mode and doing the focusing work yourself may be better.
      </p>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="box" id="section_4">
     <span>
      <h3 class="secondary_title">
       <img alt="Macro: Background Selection" src="images/img_macro_label_05.png"/>
      </h3>
     </span>
     <img alt="Orchids taken with a large and small apertures" height="350" src="images/macro/img_05.jpg" width="710"/>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <p class="caption2col">
      <span class="leftcol">
       When these orchids were shot at a very small aperture using a normal lens, the relatively closely positioned background elements in this photo are clearly visible and cause distraction.
      </span>
      <span class="rightcol">
       When reshot using a macro lens, the large aperture involved, as well as the lens magnification blurs the background out to complement the subject.
      </span>
     </p>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="desc">
      <p>
       As macro photography involves such shallow depth of field, the distance between the subject and the background becomes less important. Instead, the compatibility of the background colour tone and shade to your subject becomes a more important consideration as this example shows.
      </p>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="box" id="section_5">
     <span>
      <h3 class="secondary_title">
       <img alt="Macro: Flash Use in Macro" src="images/img_macro_label_06.png"/>
      </h3>
     </span>
     <img alt="Little Lizard" height="350" src="images/macro/img_06.jpg" width="710"/>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <p class="caption">
      This little lizard had to be shot with a diffused flash in order to increase the shutter speed required while still properly exposing it.
      <br/>
     </p>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
     <div class="desc">
      <p>
       In many macro shots, the introduction of some additional light by a flash will help expose the subject better and increase the shutter speed to prevent photo blurring. Even in shaded areas in bright daylight, you may still need a flash. However, as macro subjects are typically positioned very close to the lens, you probably need to lower your flash output power if you are using an external flash, or diffuse your flash if you are using an inbuilt flash to avoid over exposing the subject and to create a more even illumination. A simple trick you can use to diffuse your cameras in-built flash is to cover it with a sheet of tissue paper or two.
      </p>
     </div>
     <div class="clear">
     </div>
    </div>
