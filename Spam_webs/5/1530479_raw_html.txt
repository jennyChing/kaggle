     <p>
      A
      <b>
       non-governmental organization
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       NGmO
      </b>
      ) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">
       organization
      </a>
      that is neither a part of a
      <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">
       government
      </a>
      nor a conventional for-profit
      <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">
       business
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Usually set up by ordinary citizens, NGOs may be funded by governments, foundations, businesses, or private persons. Some avoid formal funding altogether and are run primarily by volunteers. NGOs are highly diverse groups of organizations engaged in a wide range of activities, and take different forms in different parts of the world. Some may have charitable status, while others may be registered for tax exemption based on recognition of social purposes. Others may be fronts for political, religious or other interest.
     </p>
     <p>
      The number of NGOs in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      is estimated at 1.5 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      has 277,000 NGOs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      is estimated to have had around 2 million NGOs in 2009, just over one NGO per 600 Indians, and many times the number of primary schools and primary health centres in India.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NGOs are difficult to define, and the term '
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/NGO_(disambiguation)" title="NGO (disambiguation)">
       NGO
      </a>
      ' is rarely used consistently. As a result, there are many different classifications in use. The most common focus is on 'orientation' and 'level of operation'. An NGO's orientation refers to the type of activities it takes on. These activities might include human rights, environmental, or development work. An NGO's level of operation indicates the scale at which an organization works, such as local, regional, national or international.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term "non-governmental organization" was first coined in 1945, when the United Nations (UN) was created.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The UN, itself an inter-governmental organization, made it possible for certain approved specialized international non-state agencies
      <i>
       i.e.
      </i>
      , non-governmental organizationsto be awarded observer status at its assemblies and some of its meetings. Later the term became used more widely. Today, according to the UN, any kind of private organization that is independent from government control can be termed an "NGO", provided it is not-for-profit, non-criminal and not simply an opposition political party.
     </p>
     <p>
      One characteristic these diverse organizations share is that their non-profit status means they are not hindered by short-term financial objectives. Accordingly, they are able to devote themselves to issues which occur across longer time horizons, such as climate change, malaria prevention or a global ban on landmines. Public surveys reveal that NGOs often enjoy a high degree of public trust, which can make them a useful  but not always sufficient  proxy for the concerns of society and stakeholders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
       Types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      NGO/GRO (governmental related organisations) types can be understood by their orientation and level of operation.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="By_orientation">
       By orientation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: By orientation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Charitable orientation
       </i>
       often involves a top-down paternalistic effort with little participation by the "beneficiaries". It includes NGOs with activities directed toward meeting the needs of the poor peoples.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Service orientation
       </i>
       includes NGOs with activities such as the provision of health, family planning or education services in which the programme is designed by the NGO and people are expected to participate in its implementation and in receiving the service.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Participatory orientation
       </i>
       is characterized by self-help projects where local people are involved particularly in the implementation of a project by contributing cash, tools, land, materials, labour etc. In the classical community development project, participation begins with the need definition and continues into the planning and implementation stages.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Empowering orientation
       </i>
       aims to help poor people develop a clearer understanding of the social, political and economic factors affecting their lives, and to strengthen their awareness of their own potential power to control their lives. There is maximum involvement of the beneficiaries with NGOs acting as facilitators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawry_2009_29.E2.80.9330_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lawry_2009_29.E2.80.9330-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="By_level_of_operation">
       By level of operation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By level of operation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Track_II_diplomacy">
       Track II diplomacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Track II diplomacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Track_II_diplomacy" title="Track II diplomacy">
       Track II diplomacy
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Track II dialogue, or Track II diplomacy, is transnational coordination that involves non-official members of the government including
      <a href="/wiki/Epistemic_community" title="Epistemic community">
       epistemic communities
      </a>
      as well as former policy-makers or analysts. Track II diplomacy aims to get policymakers and policy analysts to come to a common solution through discussions by unofficial means. Unlike the Track I diplomacy where government officials, diplomats and elected leaders gather to talk about certain issues, Track II diplomacy consists of experts, scientists, professors and other figures that are not involved in government affairs. The members of Track II diplomacy usually have more freedom to exchange ideas and come up with compromises on their own.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">
       Activities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Activities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are also numerous classifications of NGOs. The typology the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      uses divides them into Operational and Advocacy:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-definition_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-definition-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NGOs do vary in their methods. Some act primarily as lobbyists, while others primarily conduct programs and activities. For instance, an NGO such as
      <a href="/wiki/Oxfam" title="Oxfam">
       Oxfam
      </a>
      , concerned with poverty alleviation, might provide needy people with the equipment and skills to find food and clean
      <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">
       drinking water
      </a>
      , whereas an NGO like the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FFDA" title="FFDA">
       FFDA
      </a>
      helps through investigation and documentation of human rights
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      violations and provides legal assistance to victims of human rights abuses. Others, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan_Information_Management_Services" title="Afghanistan Information Management Services">
       Afghanistan Information Management Services
      </a>
      , provide specialized technical products and services to support development activities implemented on the ground by other organizations.
     </p>
     <p>
      NGOs were intended to fill a gap in government services, but in countries like India and China, NGOs are slowly gaining a position in decision making. In the interest of sustainability, most donors require that NGOs demonstrate a relationship with governments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      State Governments themselves are vulnerable because they lack economic resources, and potentially strategic planning and vision. They are therefore sometimes tightly bound by a nexus of NGOs, political bodies, commercial organizations and major donors/funders, making decisions that have short term outputs but no long term affect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In India, for instance, NGOs are under regulated, political, and recipients of large government and international donor funds. NGOs often take up responsibilities outside their skill ambit. Governments have no access to the number of projects or amount of funding received by these NGOs. There is a pressing need to regulate this group while not curtailing their unique role as a supplement to government services.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operational">
       Operational
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operational">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Operational NGOs seek to "achieve small-scale change directly through projects."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WillettsCS-NTWKS_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WillettsCS-NTWKS-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They mobilize financial resources, materials, and volunteers to create localized programs. They hold large-scale
      <a href="/wiki/Fundraising" title="Fundraising">
       fundraising
      </a>
      events and may apply to governments and organizations for grants or contracts to raise money for projects. They often operate in a hierarchical structure; a main headquarters being staffed by professionals who plan projects, create budgets, keep accounts, and report and communicate with operational fieldworkers who work directly on projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WillettsCS-NTWKS_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-WillettsCS-NTWKS-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Operational NGOs deal with a wide range of issues, but are most often associated with the delivery of services or environmental issues, emergency relief, and public welfare. Operational NGOs can be further categorized by the division into relief-oriented versus development-oriented organizations; according to whether they stress service delivery or participation; whether they are religious or secular; and whether they are more public- or private-oriented. Although operational NGOs can be community-based, many are national or international. The defining activity of operational NGOs is the implementation of projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WillettsCS-NTWKS_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-WillettsCS-NTWKS-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Campaigning">
       Campaigning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Campaigning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Campaigning NGOs seek to "achieve large-scale change promoted indirectly through influence of the political system."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WillettsCS-NTWKS_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-WillettsCS-NTWKS-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Campaigning NGOs need an efficient and effective group of professional members who are able to keep supporters informed, and motivated. They must plan and host demonstrations and events that will keep their cause in the media. They must maintain a large informed network of supporters who can be mobilized for events to garner media attention and influence policy changes. The defining activity of campaigning NGOs is holding demonstrations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WillettsCS-NTWKS_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-WillettsCS-NTWKS-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Campaigning NGOs often deal with this issues relating to human rights, women's rights, children's rights. The primary purpose of an Advocacy NGO is to defend or promote a specific cause. As opposed to operational project management, these organizations typically try to raise awareness, acceptance and knowledge by lobbying, press work and activist event.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Both_operational_and_campaigning">
       Both operational and campaigning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Both operational and campaigning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It is not uncommon for NGOs to make use of both activities. Many times, operational NGOs will use campaigning techniques if they continually face the same issues in the field that could be remedied through policy changes. At the same time, Campaigning NGOs, like human rights organizations often have programs that assist the individual victims they are trying to help through their advocacy work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WillettsCS-NTWKS_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-WillettsCS-NTWKS-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_relations">
       Public relations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public relations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Non-governmental organizations need healthy relationships with the public to meet their goals. Foundations and charities use sophisticated public relations campaigns to raise funds and employ standard lobbying techniques with governments. Interest groups may be of political importance because of their ability to influence social and political outcomes. A code of ethics was established in 2002 by The World Association of Non Governmental Organizations.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Project_management">
       Project management
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Project management">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There is an increasing awareness that management techniques are crucial to project success in non-governmental organizations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Generally, non-governmental organizations that are private have either a community or environmental focus. They address varieties of issues such as religion, emergency aid, or humanitarian affairs. They mobilize public support and voluntary contributions for aid; they often have strong links with community groups in developing countries, and they often work in areas where government-to-government aid is not possible. NGOs are accepted as a part of the international relations landscape, and while they influence national and multilateral policy-making, increasingly they are more directly involved in local action.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_structure">
       Corporate structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Corporate structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Staffing">
       Staffing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Staffing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Some NGOs are highly professionalized and rely mainly on paid staff. Others are based around voluntary labour and are less formalized. Not all people working for non-governmental organizations are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volunteer" title="Volunteer">
       volunteers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Many NGOs are associated with the use of international staff working in 'developing' countries, but there are many NGOs in both North and South who rely on local employees or volunteers. There is some dispute as to whether
      <a href="/wiki/Expatriate" title="Expatriate">
       expatriates
      </a>
      should be sent to developing countries. Frequently this type of personnel is employed to satisfy a
      <a href="/wiki/Donation" title="Donation">
       donor
      </a>
      who wants to see the supported project managed by someone from an
      <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">
       industrialized country
      </a>
      . However, the expertise of these employees or volunteers may be counterbalanced by a number of factors: the cost of
      <a href="/wiki/Alien_(law)" title="Alien (law)">
       foreigners
      </a>
      is typically higher, they have no
      <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">
       grassroot connections
      </a>
      in the country they are sent to, and local expertise is often undervalued.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-definition_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-definition-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The NGO sector is an essential employer in terms of numbers.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      For example, by the end of 1995,
      <a href="/wiki/Concern_Worldwide" title="Concern Worldwide">
       CONCERN worldwide
      </a>
      , an international Northern NGO working against poverty, employed 174 expatriates and just over 5,000 national staff working in ten developing countries in
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
       Asia
      </a>
      , and in
      <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
       Haiti
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">
       Funding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Funding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Whether the NGOs are small or large, various NGOs need budgets to operate. The amount of budget that they need would differ from NGOs to NGOs. Unlike small NGOs, large NGOs may have annual budgets in the hundreds of millions or billions of dollars. For instance, the budget of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Retired_Persons" title="American Association of Retired Persons">
       American Association of Retired Persons
      </a>
      (AARP) was over US$540 million in 1999.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Funding such large budgets demands significant fundraising efforts on the part of most NGOs. Major sources of NGO funding are membership dues, the sale of
      <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">
       goods
      </a>
      and services, grants from international institutions or national governments, and private
      <a href="/wiki/Donation" title="Donation">
       donations
      </a>
      . Several EU-grants provide funds accessible to NGOs.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even though the term "non-governmental organization" implies
      <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">
       independence
      </a>
      from governments, many NGOs depend heavily on governments for their funding.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globall_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-globall-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A quarter of the US$162 million income in 1998 of the
      <a href="/wiki/Famine_relief" title="Famine relief">
       famine-relief
      </a>
      organization
      <a href="/wiki/Oxfam" title="Oxfam">
       Oxfam
      </a>
      was donated by the British government and the EU. The Christian relief and development organization
      <a href="/wiki/World_Vision_United_States" title="World Vision United States">
       World Vision United States
      </a>
      collected US$55 million worth of goods in 1998 from the American government.
     </p>
     <p>
      Government funding of NGOs is controversial, since, according to David Rieff, writing in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">
        The New Republic
       </a>
      </i>
      , "the whole point of humanitarian intervention was precisely that NGOs and civil society had both a right and an obligation to respond with acts of aid and solidarity to people in need or being subjected to repression or want by the forces that controlled them, whatever the governments concerned might think about the matter."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some NGOs, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">
       Greenpeace
      </a>
      do not accept funding from governments or intergovernmental organizations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBS_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HBS-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GP_Annual_report_2008_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GP_Annual_report_2008-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overhead_costs">
       Overhead costs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Overhead costs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Overhead_(business)" title="Overhead (business)">
       Overhead
      </a>
      is the amount of money that is spent on running an NGO rather than on projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overhead1_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overhead1-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This includes office expenses,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overhead1_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overhead1-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      salaries, banking and bookkeeping costs. What percentage of overall budget is spent on overhead is often used to judge an NGO with less than 4% being viewed as good.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overhead1_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Overhead1-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The World Association of Non-Governmental Organizations states that ideally more than 86% should be spent on programs (less than 20% on overhead).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/The_Global_Fund_to_Fight_AIDS,_Tuberculosis_and_Malaria" title="The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria">
       The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria
      </a>
      has specific guidelines on how high overhead can be to receive funding based on how the money is to be spent with overhead often needing to be less than 5-7%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      typically allows 37%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A high percentage of overhead to total expenditures can make it more difficult to generate funds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      High overhead costs may also generate criticism with some claiming the certain NGOs with high overhead are being run simply to benefit the people working for them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While overhead costs can be a legitimate concern, a sole focus on them can be counterproductive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Research published by the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.urban.org/publications/311055.html" rel="nofollow">
       Urban Institute
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.ssireview.org/articles/entry/the_ratings_game/" rel="nofollow">
       Center for Social Innovation
      </a>
      at Stanford University have shown how rating agencies create incentives for nonprofits to lower and hide overhead costs, which may actually reduce organizational effectiveness by starving organizations of the infrastructure they need to effectively deliver services. A more meaningful rating system would provide, in addition to financial data, a qualitative evaluation of an organizations transparency and governance: (1) an assessment of program effectiveness; (2) and an evaluation of feedback mechanisms designed for donors and beneficiaries; and (3) such a rating system would also allow rated organizations to respond to an evaluation done by a rating agency.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      More generally, the popular discourse of nonprofit evaluation should move away from financial notions of organizational effectiveness and toward more substantial understandings of programmatic impact.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring_and_control">
       Monitoring and control
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Monitoring and control">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the March 2000 report on United Nations Reform priorities, former U.N. Secretary General Kofi Annan wrote in favor of international humanitarian intervention, arguing that the international community has a "right to protect"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      citizens of the world against ethnic cleansing, genocide, and crimes against humanity. On the heels of the report, the Canadian government launched the Responsibility to Protect R2P
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      project, outlining the issue of humanitarian intervention. While the R2P doctrine has wide applications, among the more controversial has been the Canadian government's use of R2P to justify its intervention and support of the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.zmag.org/content/showarticle.cfm?SectionID=55&amp;ItemID=9238" rel="nofollow">
       coup
      </a>
      in Haiti.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Years after R2P, the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Federalist_Movement" title="World Federalist Movement">
       World Federalist Movement
      </a>
      , an organization which supports "the creation of democratic global structures accountable to the citizens of the world and call for the division of international authority among separate agencies", has launched Responsibility to Protect  Engaging Civil Society (R2PCS). A collaboration between the WFM and the Canadian government, this project aims to bring NGOs into lockstep with the principles outlined under the original R2P project.
     </p>
     <p>
      The governments of the countries an NGO works or is registered in may require reporting or other monitoring and oversight. Funders generally require reporting and assessment, such information is not necessarily publicly available. There may also be associations and watchdog organizations that research and publish details on the actions of NGOs working in particular geographic or program areas.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In recent years, many large corporations have increased their
      <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">
       corporate social responsibility
      </a>
      departments in an attempt to preempt NGO campaigns against certain corporate practices. As the logic goes, if corporations work
      <i>
       with
      </i>
      NGOs, NGOs will not work
      <i>
       against
      </i>
      corporations. Greater collaboration between corporations and NGOs creates inherent risks of co-optation for the weaker partner, typically the nonprofit involved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2007, The United States Department of Defense Assistant Secretary of Defense (Health Affairs)
      <a href="/wiki/S._Ward_Casscells" title="S. Ward Casscells">
       S. Ward Casscells
      </a>
      established an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Health" title="International Health">
       International Health
      </a>
      Division under Force Health Protection &amp; Readiness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Part of International Health's mission is to communicate with NGOs in areas of mutual interest. Department of Defense Directive 3000.05,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in 2005, requires DoD to regard stability-enhancing activities as a mission of importance equal to combat. In compliance with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geneva_conventions" title="Geneva conventions">
       international law
      </a>
      , DoD has necessarily built a capacity to improve essential services in areas of conflict such as
      <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">
       Iraq
      </a>
      , where the customary lead agencies (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Department" title="State Department">
       State Department
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAID" title="USAID">
       USAID
      </a>
      ) find it difficult to operate. Unlike the "co-option" strategy described for corporations, the OASD(HA) recognizes the neutrality of health as an essential service. International Health cultivates collaborative relationships with NGOs, albeit at arms-length, recognizing their traditional independence, expertise and honest broker status. While the goals of DoD and NGOs may seem incongruent, the DoD's emphasis on stability and security to reduce and prevent conflict suggests, on careful analysis, important mutual interests.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      International non-governmental organizations have a history dating back to at least the late eighteenth century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been estimated that by 1914, there were 1083 NGOs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      International NGOs were important in the
      <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism" title="Abolitionism">
       anti-slavery movement
      </a>
      and the movement for
      <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">
       women's suffrage
      </a>
      , and reached a peak at the time of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Disarmament_Conference" title="World Disarmament Conference">
       World Disarmament Conference
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the phrase "non-governmental organization" only came into popular use with the establishment of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Organization" title="United Nations Organization">
       United Nations Organization
      </a>
      in 1945 with provisions in Article 71 of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_10_of_the_United_Nations_Charter" title="Chapter 10 of the United Nations Charter">
       Chapter 10 of the United Nations Charter
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for a consultative role for organizations which are neither governments nor member statessee
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consultative_Status" title="Consultative Status">
       Consultative Status
      </a>
      . The definition of "international NGO" (INGO) is first given in resolution 288 (X) of ECOSOC on February 27, 1950: it is defined as "any international organization that is not founded by an international treaty". The vital role of NGOs and other "major groups" in
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">
       sustainable development
      </a>
      was recognized in Chapter 27
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">
       Agenda 21
      </a>
      , leading to intense arrangements for a consultative relationship between the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
       United Nations
      </a>
      and non-governmental organizations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been observed that the number of INGO founded or dissolved matches the general "state of the world", rising in periods of growth and declining in periods of crisis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Rapid development of the non-governmental sector occurred in western countries as a result of the processes of restructuring of the
      <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">
       welfare state
      </a>
      . Further
      <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
       globalization
      </a>
      of that process occurred after the fall of the communist system and was an important part of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_consensus" title="Washington consensus">
       Washington consensus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globall_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-globall-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Globalization during the 20th century gave rise to the importance of NGOs. Many problems could not be solved within a nation.
      <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">
       International treaties
      </a>
      and international organizations such as the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">
       World Trade Organization
      </a>
      were centered mainly on the interests of capitalist enterprises. In an attempt to counterbalance this trend, NGOs have developed to emphasize
      <a href="/wiki/Humanitarianism" title="Humanitarianism">
       humanitarian issues
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developmental_aid" title="Developmental aid">
       developmental aid
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">
       sustainable development
      </a>
      . A prominent example of this is the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Social_Forum" title="World Social Forum">
       World Social Forum
      </a>
      , which is a rival convention to the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">
       World Economic Forum
      </a>
      held annually in January in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davos,_Switzerland" title="Davos, Switzerland">
       Davos
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      . The fifth World Social Forum in
      <a href="/wiki/Porto_Alegre" title="Porto Alegre">
       Porto Alegre
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
       Brazil
      </a>
      , in January 2005 was attended by representatives from more than 1,000 NGOs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In terms of environmental issues and sustainable development, the
      <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">
       Earth Summit
      </a>
      in Rio in 1992 was the first to show the power of international NGOs, when about 2,400 representatives of NGOs came to play a central role in deliberations. Some have argued that in forums like these, NGOs take the place of what should belong to popular movements of the poor. Whatever the case, NGO transnational networking is now extensive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another issue which has brought NGOs to develop further is the inefficiency of some top-heavy, global structures. For instance, in 1994, former
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
       UN
      </a>
      envoy to
      <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">
       Somalia
      </a>
      Mohamed Sahnoun published a book entitled "Somalia: The Missed Opportunities",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which he clearly shows that when the United Nations tried to provide humanitarian assistance, they were totally outperformed by NGOs, whose competence and dedication sharply contrasted with the United Nations' excessive caution and bureaucratic inefficiencies, their main Somalia envoys operating from the safety of their desks in
      <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">
       Nairobi
      </a>
      . The refusal of
      <a href="/wiki/Boutros_Boutros-Ghali" title="Boutros Boutros-Ghali">
       Boutros Boutros-Ghali
      </a>
      , then
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Secretary_General" title="UN Secretary General">
       UN Secretary General
      </a>
      to accept this criticism led to the early end of Mohamed Sahnoun's mission in Somalia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">
       Legal status
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Legal status">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The legal form of NGOs is diverse and depends upon homegrown variations in each country's laws and practices. However, four main family groups of NGOs can be found worldwide:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Unincorporated and
       <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_association" title="Voluntary association">
        voluntary association
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trusts" title="Trusts">
        Trusts
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charities" title="Charities">
        charities
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foundation_(nonprofit_organization)" title="Foundation (nonprofit organization)">
        foundations
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Companies not just for profit
      </li>
      <li>
       Entities formed or registered under special NGO or
       <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">
        nonprofit
       </a>
       laws
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">
       Council of Europe
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">
       Strasbourg
      </a>
      drafted the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_the_Recognition_of_the_Legal_Personality_of_International_Non-Governmental_Organizations" title="European Convention on the Recognition of the Legal Personality of International Non-Governmental Organizations">
       European Convention on the Recognition of the Legal Personality of International Non-Governmental Organizations
      </a>
      in 1986, which sets a common legal basis for the existence and work of NGOs in Europe. Article 11 of the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">
       European Convention on Human Rights
      </a>
      protects the right to freedom of association, which is also a fundamental norm for NGOs.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="How_NGOs_Influence_World_Affairs">
       How NGOs Influence World Affairs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: How NGOs Influence World Affairs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Some argue that NGOs have great influence and power in global affairs. Service-delivery NGOs provide public goods and services that governments from developing countries are unable to provide to society, due to lack of resources. Service-delivery NGOs can serve as contractors or collaborate with democratized government agencies to reduce cost associated with public goods. Capacity-building NGOs influence global affairs differently, in the sense that the incorporation of accountability measures in Southern NGOs affect "culture, structure, projects and daily operations."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Advocacy and public education NGOs affect global affairs in its ability to modify behavior through the use of ideas. Communication is the weapon of choice used by advocacy and public-education NGOs in order to change people's actions and behaviors. They strategically construct messages to not only shape behavior, but to also socially mobilize communities in promoting social, political, or environmental changes.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques">
       Critiques
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Critiques">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Issa_G._Shivji" title="Issa G. Shivji">
       Issa G. Shivji
      </a>
      is one of Africa's leading experts on law and development issues as an author and academic. His critique on NGOs is found in two essays: "Silences in NGO discourse: The role and future of NGOs in Africa" and "Reflections on NGOs in Tanzania: What we are, what we are not and what we ought to be". Shivji argues that despite the good intentions of NGO leaders and activists, he is critical of the "objective effects of actions, regardless of their intentions".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shivji argues also that the sudden rise of NGOs are part of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoliberal" title="Neoliberal">
       neoliberal
      </a>
      paradigm rather than pure altruistic motivations. He is critical of the current manifestations of NGOs wanting to change the world without understanding it, and that the
      <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">
       imperial
      </a>
      relationship continues today with the rise of NGOs.
     </p>
     <p>
      James Pfeiffer, in his case study of NGO involvement in Mozambique, speaks to the negative effects that NGO's have had on areas of health within the country. He argues that over the last decade, NGO's in Mozambique have "fragmented the local health system, undermined local control of health programs, and contributed to growing local social inequality".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He notes further that NGO's can be uncoordinated, creating parallel projects among different organizations, that pull health service workers away from their routine duties in order to serve the interests of the NGO's. This ultimately undermines local primary health care efforts, and takes away the governments' ability to maintain agency over their own health sector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      J. Pfeiffer suggested a new model of collaboration between the NGO and the DPS (the Mozambique Provincial Health Directorate). He mentioned the NGO should be 'formally held to standard and adherence within the host country', for example reduce 'showcase' projects and parallel programs that proves to be unsustainable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Mathews" title="Jessica Mathews">
       Jessica Mathews
      </a>
      wrote in
      <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Affairs" title="Foreign Affairs">
       Foreign Affairs
      </a>
      in 1997: "For all their strengths, NGOs are special interests. The best of them  often suffer from tunnel vision, judging every public act by how it affects their particular interest".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since NGOs have to worry about policy trade-offs, the overall impact of their cause might bring more harm to society.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Vijay_Prashad" title="Vijay Prashad">
       Vijay Prashad
      </a>
      argues that from the 1970s "The World Bank, under Robert McNamara, championed the NGO as an alternative to the state, leaving intact global and regional relations of power and production."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Others argue that NGOs are often imperialist
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in nature, that they sometimes operate in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racialized" title="Racialized">
       racialized
      </a>
      manner in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_world" title="Third world">
       third world
      </a>
      countries, and that they fulfill a similar function to that of the clergy during the high colonial era. The philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hallward" title="Peter Hallward">
       Peter Hallward
      </a>
      argues that they are an aristocratic form of politics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also points to the fact that NGOs like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Action_Aid" title="Action Aid">
       Action Aid
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_Aid" title="Christian Aid">
       Christian Aid
      </a>
      "effectively condoned the [2004 US backed] coup" against an elected government in Haiti and argues that they are the "humanitarian face of imperialism."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Popular movements in the global South such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape_Anti-Eviction_Campaign" title="Western Cape Anti-Eviction Campaign">
       Western Cape Anti-Eviction Campaign
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
       South Africa
      </a>
      have sometimes refused to work with NGOs arguing that this will compromise their autonomy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has also been argued that NGOs often disempower people by allowing funders to push for stability over social justice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another criticism of NGOs is that they are being designed and used as extensions of the normal foreign-policy instruments of certain Western countries and groups of countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Russian President
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">
       Vladimir Putin
      </a>
      made this accusation at the 43rd
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munich_Conference_on_Security_Policy" title="Munich Conference on Security Policy">
       Munich Conference on Security Policy
      </a>
      in 2007, concluding that these NGOs "are formally independent but they are purposefully financed and therefore under control."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, Michael Bond wrote "Most large NGOs, such as Oxfam, the Red Cross, Cafod and Action Aid, are striving to make their aid provision more sustainable. But some, mostly in the US, are still exporting the ideologies of their backers."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NGOs have also been accused of using white lies or misinformed advise to enact their campaigns, i.e., accusations that NGOs have been ignorant about critical issues because, as chief scientist at
      <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">
       Greenpeace
      </a>
      Doug Parr said, these organizations appear to have lost their efforts in being truly scientific and now seem to be more self-interested. Rather than operating through science so as to be rationally and effectively practical, NGOs have been accused of abusing the utilization of science to gain their own advantages. In the beginning, as Parr indicated, there was "a tendency among our critics to say that science is the only decision-making tool  but political and commercial interests are using science as a cover for getting their way."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the same time, NGOs can appear to not be cooperative with other groups, according to the previous policy-maker for the German branch of
      <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">
       Friends of the Earth
      </a>
      , Jens Katjek. "If NGOs want the best for the environment, he says, they have to learn to compromise."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_to_legitimacy">
       Challenges to legitimacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-governmental_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Challenges to legitimacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The issue of the legitimacy of NGOs raises a series of important questions. This is one of the most important assets possessed by an NGO, it is gained through a perception that they are an independent voice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2002aHD_66-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-2002aHD-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their representation also emerges as an important question. Who bestows responsibilities to NGOs or INGOs and how do they gain the representation of citizens and civil society is still not scrutinized thoroughly. For instance, in the article, it is stated, "To put the point starkly: are the citizens of countries of the South and their needs represented in global civil society, or are citizens as well as their needs constructed by practices of representation? And when we realize that INGOs hardly ever come face to face with the people whose interests and problems they represent, or that they are not accountable to the people they represent, matters become even more troublesome."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Moreover, the legitimacy and the accountability of NGOs on the point of their true nature are also emerging as important issues.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Various perceptions and images on NGOs are provided, and usually implemented in an image as 'non-state actors' or 'influential representatives of civil society that advocate the citizen.' Accountability may be able to provide this and also be able to assist activities by providing focus and direction
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As non-state actors with considerable influence over the governance in many areas, concerns have been expressed over the extent to which they represent the views of the public and the extent to which they allow the public to hold them to account.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1996HD_70-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-1996HD-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The origin of funding can have serious implications for the legitimacy of NGOs. In recent decades NGOs have increased their numbers and range of activities to a level where they have become increasingly dependent on a limited number of donors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1996HD_70-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1996HD-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Consequently, competition has increased for funding, as have the expectations of the donors themselves.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This runs the risk of donors adding conditions which can threaten the independence of NGOs; for example, an over-dependence on official aid has the potential to dilute the willingness of NGOs to speak out on issues which are unpopular with governments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2002aHD_66-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-2002aHD-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In these situations NGOs are being held accountable by their donors, which can erode rather than enhance their legitimacy, a difficult challenge to overcome. Some commentators have also argued that the changes in NGO funding sources has ultimately altered their functions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2002aHD_66-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2002aHD-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Corporate funding has also brought criticism. Joel Binda writes that corporations seek to partner with NGOs as part of their Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) programs. This provides marketing and public relations benefits for businesses. These partnerships between corporations and NGOs allows corporations to claim some credit for the work of NGOs. Since NGOs receive funding from corporations, they may be reluctant to criticize corporations. In order to satisfy corporate donors and attract more funding, NGOs may tailor their activities to serve corporate interests. For example, by focusing on projects that look good to a corporation's consumers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        74
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NGOs have also been challenged on the grounds that they do not necessarily represent the needs of the developing world, through diminishing the so-called Southern Voice. Some postulate that the
      <a href="/wiki/North%E2%80%93South_divide" title="NorthSouth divide">
       NorthSouth divide
      </a>
      exists in the arena of NGOs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linden_75-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-linden-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They question the equality of the relationships between Northern and Southern parts of the same NGOs as well as the relationships between Southern and Northern NGOs working in partnerships. This suggests a division of labour may develop, with the North taking the lead in advocacy and resource mobilisation whilst the South engages in service delivery in the developing world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linden_75-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-linden-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The potential implications of this may mean the needs of the developing world are not addressed appropriately as Northern NGOs do not properly consult or participate in partnerships. The real danger in this situation is that western views may take the front seat and assign unrepresentative priorities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The flood of NGOs has also been accused of damaging the public sector in multiple developing countries, e.g. accusations that NGO mismanagement has resulted in the breakdown of public health care systems. Instead of promoting equity and alleviating poverty, NGOs have been under scrutiny for contributing to socioeconomic inequality and disempowering services in the public sector of third world countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patrick-Cockburn_78-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Patrick-Cockburn-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The scale and variety of activities in which NGOs participate has grown rapidly since the 1980s, witnessing particular expansion in the 1990s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79">
       <a href="#cite_note-79">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        79
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This has presented NGOs with a need to balance the pressures of centralisation and decentralisation. By centralising NGOs, particularly those that operate at an international level, they can assign a common theme or set of goals. Conversely it may also be advantageous to decentralise as this can increase the chances of an NGO responding more flexibly and effectively to localised issues by implementing projects which are modest in scale, easily monitored, produce immediate benefits and where all involved know that corruption will be punished.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patrick-Cockburn_78-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Patrick-Cockburn-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80">
       <a href="#cite_note-80">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        80
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
