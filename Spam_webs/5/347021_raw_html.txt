            <img alt="running-a-b-tests" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24648" height="406" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/running-a-b-tests.jpg" width="560"/>

          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           Running these A/B tests on your welcome emails will help keep you on top of your conversion game.
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surf_lifesaving" target="_blank">
            Image source
           </a>
           .
          </div>
          <p>
           When it comes to conversion rate optimization, we typically begin by optimizing the top of the funnel  social media posting times, calls to action, landing page headlines and images.
          </p>
          <p>
           But as we are becoming proficient at CRO in these areas,
           <strong>
            we need to start looking further down the funnel.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Whys that?
          </p>
          <p>
           People who have taken that first step and qualified themselves by signing up for your mailing list or for a free trial are incredibly valuable to your business. And a series of well-timed, well-written emails can be an incredibly powerful tool for welcoming new users and
           <strong>
            converting them into lifelong customers
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           A/B testing can help you optimize your welcome messages  but you need to test more than just your subject line.
          </p>
          <p>
           Here are seven more advanced A/B tests you should be running to optimize your welcome emails.
          </p>
          <h2>
           1. Test timing and frequency
          </h2>
          <p>
           A new user has just signed up for a 14-day free trial. Your service and your emails now have 14 days to make them excited about becoming a paying customer.
          </p>
          <p>
           So how many emails do you send?
          </p>
          <p>
           One per day? Thats a bit spammy, isnt it?
          </p>
          <p>
           One per week? Theyll probably forget who you are by the time the second email arrives.
          </p>
          <p>
           Heck, why not send five emails in the first day? Or three in the first three days, then none for a week?
          </p>
          <p>
           As you start to ask questions like this, youll realize that
           <strong>
            these are just assumptions (or worse,
           </strong>
           <a href="http://unbounce.com/a-b-testing/the-hippos-have-left-the-building-ab-testing-is-back/" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             HIPPOs!
            </strong>
           </a>
           <strong>
            ) around email frequency.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           And very often, your assumptions around what is too much or too little will be way off the mark.
          </p>
          <p>
           This, of course, is where some good old-fashioned A/B testing comes in.
          </p>
          <h3>
           How to test frequency
          </h3>
          <p>
           Suggesting, Why dont we send five emails in the first day? is just throwing spaghetti at the wall and seeing what might stick. Sure, you may find some conversion uplift, but it teaches you very little.
          </p>
          <p>
           A better approach is to
           <strong>
            start with a hypothesis like this
           </strong>
           :
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Insight
            </strong>
            : People who sign up to our free trial are trying to solve an urgent problem. So urgent, in fact, that its the number one thing on their to-do list this week. Theyll be evaluating our service and competitors intensely over the next 48 hours and running the first live trial within the next 72 hours.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Hypothesis
            </strong>
            <strong>
             :
            </strong>
            Heavily loading our welcome messaging within the first 72 hours will be welcome, timely and relevant for these users and will increase conversion rates.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Insight driven and very testable. Lovely.
          </p>
          <p>
           This is better than the spaghetti approach because it helps you figure out the best timing
           <em>
            and
           </em>
           it teaches you something about your users. As an added bonus, the results from your test
           <strong>
            give you insight that can help develop new hypotheses and further tests and experiments
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           In
           <a href="http://unbounce.com/a-b-testing/how-to-get-the-most-out-of-a-b-testing-case-study/" target="_blank">
            this article
           </a>
           , Michael Aagaard talks about using insight from other case studies to generate hypotheses. His examples are for landing pages but the process is just the same.
          </p>

          <h2>
           2. Test urgency
          </h2>
          <p>
           Testing the timing of your messages is a great first test to run, but youll also want to start testing the content of these messages. Were not going to discuss the words you can test in a subject line or on a CTA, because
           <a href="http://unbounce.com/email-marketing/7-proven-ways-to-increase-your-email-open-click-thru-rates/" target="_blank">
            we all know
           </a>
           <a href="http://unbounce.com/email-marketing/perfect-subject-line/" target="_blank">
            how to do that
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Instead, lets look at a tactic that many companies use in their emails to increase conversions: adding a sense of
           <strong>
            urgency
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Referencing time limits in your welcome emails can create a sense of hurry in your users and encouraging them to act
           <em>
            now.
           </em>
          </p>
          <h3>
           How Doggyloot uses urgency
          </h3>
          <p>
           Take a look at what happened when
           <a href="https://doggyloot.com/" target="_blank">
            Doggyloot
           </a>
           tested urgency in their shopping cart abandonment emails. In the screenshot below, you can see that they tell you that the Items you added to your cart are almost sold out.
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="doggyloot-urgency" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24464" height="292" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/doggyloot-urgency.png" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           <a href="http://www.marketingsherpa.com/article/case-study/email-personalization-ctr-ecommerce-site" target="_blank">
            Image source
           </a>
           .
          </div>
          <p>
           When they added a sense of urgency and a clear call to action, Doggyloots abandonment emails became
           <a href="http://www.marketingsherpa.com/article/case-study/email-personalization-ctr-ecommerce-site" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             responsible for an additional days worth of revenue each month
            </strong>
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Any time I want to optimize a set of automated emails, I often start by experimenting with urgency. Most often, it produces the biggest early uplifts.
          </p>
          <h3>
           How to test urgency
          </h3>
          <p>
           If youre not sure where to start, you can start testing urgency in your emails by sending reminder emails at different time intervals.
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="how-to-test-urgency" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24465" height="283" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/how-to-test-urgency.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           Test sending Your trial is almost up! emails at intervals of 48, 24 and 12 hours.
          </div>
          <p>
           A few things you should know before you get started:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Always include a control group
            </strong>
            that doesnt receive the email youre testing. This control will act as a baseline and help you be certain that any reminder email  regardless of the timing  is better than no email at all. It also lets you isolate and measure the exact impact of each email on your conversion rate.
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             The number of variations in your test should depend on the number of new signups you get
            </strong>
            . If youre not getting many, start with two variations and use a statistical significance calculator. I usually let tests run for two or three weeks, and I pick the number of variations that will give me (roughly) 100 conversions each.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <h2>
           3. Test personalization
          </h2>
          <p>
           Doggyloot has seen a lot of success from their email marketing, in part because of their decision to run
           <a href="http://www.marketingsherpa.com/article/case-study/email-personalization-ctr-ecommerce-site" target="_blank">
            personalization and segmentation tests
           </a>
           based on one very important point of doggy data:
           <strong>
            the size of the owners dog (small, medium or large)
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           They did this because they figured that Chihuahua owners wouldnt be buying St. Bernard chew toys which were probably bigger than the dogs themselves!
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="doggyloot-segmentation" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24466" height="339" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/doggyloot-segmentation.png" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           These 3-foot duck chews are no match for a Chihuahua.
           <a href="http://www.marketingsherpa.com/article/case-study/email-personalization-ctr-ecommerce-site" target="_blank">
            Image source
           </a>
           .
          </div>
          <p>
           Segmenting emails based on this data produced results that were pretty astounding. They found that
           <strong>
            emails that were targeted to large dog owners had
           </strong>
           <a href="http://www.marketingsherpa.com/article/case-study/email-personalization-ctr-ecommerce-site" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             click-through rates 410% higher
            </strong>
           </a>
           <strong>
            than the average.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           This single piece of data was so powerful that its now the only question they ask of every new user on their homepage (in addition to email):
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="Doggyloot-Homepage" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24467" height="252" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/Doggyloot-Homepage.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           The form on the left asks, How big are your dogs?
          </div>
          <p>
           Knowing and using one simple data point about your recipients can drastically improve your conversion rates from emails.
          </p>
          <h3>
           How to test personalization
          </h3>
          <p>
           What other personal details can you capture to experiment with in your welcome emails?
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            You could start with details that are explicitly captured  like
            <strong>
             name, date of birth and company name
            </strong>
            .
           </li>
           <li>
            You could also experiment with information that is implicitly captured  like
            <strong>
             location, device type or level of product usage
            </strong>
            .
           </li>
           <li>
            If you want to try something a little more advanced, try personalizing based on
            <strong>
             the ad campaign that attracted them, the keyword they searched for or the landing page they signed up on.
            </strong>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           All of these data points are fertile grounds for testing. The important thing is to get started!
          </p>

          <h2>
           4. Test asking for feedback
          </h2>
          <p>
           In early 2012, I read
           <a href="http://socialtriggers.com/what-people-buy/" target="_blank">
            this post
           </a>
           by Derek Halpern, which lead to one of the most valuable emails I ever wrote.
          </p>
          <p>
           In the post, Derek recommends engaging new users (or blog subscribers) with an automatic email containing
           <strong>
            one simple question
           </strong>
           :
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            What are you struggling with?
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Since his post was first published, Ive used this technique again and again and Ive found it to be extremely valuable.
          </p>
          <h3>
           How Groove asks for feedback
          </h3>
          <p>
           Customers who have already converted have extremely valuable insight to offer. With my
           <a href="http://www.sparkpage.com/" target="_blank">
            SaaS company
           </a>
           , asking questions such as, Whats the biggest part of your marketing day job that youre struggling with right now? played a big part in
           <strong>
            shaping our product roadmap
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://groovehq.com/" target="_blank">
            Groove
           </a>
           , a customer support SaaS company, started including a similar question in their confirmation email to all new trial users:
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="groove-autoresponder-feedback1" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24470" height="366" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/groove-autoresponder-feedback1.png" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           Groove asks new users, Why did you sign up for Groove?
           <a href="http://groovehq.com/blog/email-onboarding-optimization" target="_blank">
            Image source
           </a>
           .
          </div>
          <p>
           This automated email has proven quite useful to Groove:
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            This email gets a
            <strong>
             41% response rate and has given us more business insight than any email weve ever sent.
            </strong>
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Here are some of the
           <a href="http://groovehq.com/blog/email-onboarding-optimization" target="_blank">
            responses

           they got:

          <h2>
           5. Test delivering unexpected value
          </h2>
          <p>
           In a past life at a marketing consultancy, I had many clients who were musicians and record labels. Some of the musicians had large mailing lists and sent monthly newsletters.
          </p>
          <p>
           These typically performed well but there was one problem: Whether youd been a fan for 10 days or 10 years, youd get the same monthly email. And because most people on the list werent new, the newsletter content tended to be geared to long-term fans  which was off-putting for a new subscriber.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            When we started acknowledging new subscribers by putting basic welcome campaigns in place, the results were dramatic.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Instead of getting a stream of monthly newsletters, every new fan would get a series of emails introducing them to the artist and ultimately
           <strong>
            building an emotional connection.
           </strong>
           After a few weeks, new subscribers were presented the opportunity to support the artist by buying the latest album or merch.
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="musician-welcome-email-flow" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24472" height="141" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/musician-welcome-email-flow.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
          </div>
          <p>
           With one Irish artist, we started testing different motivating factors we could implement before the ask. If we could send them one email 24-48 hours before asking them to purchase,
           <strong>
            what type of email would be most likely to get them to subsequently convert
           </strong>
           ?
          </p>
          <p>
           Something with strong social proof, showing the thousands of others enjoying the music? Or a reminder about how people who purchase are supporting an artist?
          </p>
          <p>
           It turns out that
           <strong>
            the single biggest boost (which doubled the subsequent purchase rate) was an unexpected gift.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           About four weeks after joining the mailing list, a fan would get an out of the blue email with three free, exclusive-to-the-mailing-list acoustic songs and a personal note from the musician.
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="musician-welcome-email-unexpected-gift" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24473" height="139" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/musician-welcome-email-unexpected-gift.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
          </div>
          <p>
           This was a true gift  it wasnt contingent on any actions, which I think is what made it so successful.
          </p>
          <p>
           It was just nice.
          </p>
          <p>
           And people who got this gift were
           <strong>
            twice as likely to buy the album or a t-shirt when they got the ask two days later
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           This is applicable to any business.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ive seen businesses send free ebooks or white papers or even just free access to an old webinar.
          </p>
          <p>
           Theres lots of
           <a href="http://sethgodin.typepad.com/seths_blog/2010/02/the-hidden-power-of-a-gift.html" target="_blank">
            good psychology
           </a>
           to suggest why this works (gifting begets
           <a href="http://unbounce.com/landing-pages/six-principles-of-persuasion-landing-pages/" target="_blank">
            reciprocation
           </a>
           ), but its easy to see how unexpected delight and value would make a potential customer happier to buy from you.
          </p>
          <h2>
           6. Test email design
          </h2>
          <p>
           By now, we know the importance of the email subject line and content. But what about the design of the email?
          </p>
          <p>
           Should your welcome emails look like flashy ezine newsletters? Or like slightly toned down emails with brand colors, a logo and a few product images? What about a plain text email, with no branding or design at all?
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="email-styles" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24474" height="282" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/email-styles.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
           Should your welcome emails be flashy and packed with images, sleek with a little branding, or plain text? The only way to find out is to test.
          </div>
          <h3>
           Testing email design
          </h3>
          <p>
           The Obama 2012 campaign team found that the
           <strong>
            top-earning emails were consistently personal-style emails in plain text
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Heres what Amelia Showalter, Obamas director of analytics
           <a href="http://www.marketingexperiments.com/blog/general/email-marketing-how-ab-testing-raised-500-million-for-obama-for-america.html" target="_blank">
            had to say about it
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            I think it gave the impression that there were real people writing these emails, that it wasnt focus-grouped to death. It was something more off the cuff.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           On the other hand, Wishpond
           <a href="http://blog.wishpond.com/post/72879592554/a-b-split-testing-your-email-marketing-campaigns" target="_blank">
            recently blogged
           </a>
           about tests they ran which found the opposite.
          </p>
          <p>
           They had
           <a href="http://www.forbes.com/forbes/2005/1017/071.html" target="_blank">
            read a study
           </a>
           which said that, 67% of consumers consider clear, detailed images to carry more weight than product information or customer ratings, so they decided to test that in their emails.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sure enough, they found that
           <strong>
            emails with images had a 60% higher click through rate than text-only emails
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Now, I didnt include both counter examples to confuse you!
          </p>
          <p>
           I included them because they illustrate that you
           <strong>
            cant trust your gut, industry standards or what competitors are doing. You just have to test.
           </strong>
          </p>

          <h2>
           7. Test winning users back
          </h2>
          <p>
           What about when your customers free trial is over? What should you do if youve brought them through a well-timed, personalized and valuable welcome sequence and they still havent converted into a customer?
          </p>
          <p>
           Just because they havent acted yet doesnt mean theyre not interested in what you have to offer. It doesnt hurt to try to win them back.
          </p>
          <h3>
           How Squarespace wins users back
          </h3>
          <p>
           Lets take a look at two emails Squarespace sends to users who havent converted from trial to paying.
          </p>
          <p>
           This first email, sent two days after the free trial ends, offers seven more days and a second chance for the customer to re-engage  a good combination of urgency and win back.
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="squarespace-winback-1" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24475" height="384" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/squarespace-winback-1.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
          </div>
          <p>
           The second email, which comes a few days later, has the subject, What could we have done better? Even though its an email asking for feedback, it still gives the
           <strong>
            user the option to extend their trial and get back in
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="squarespace-winback-2" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24476" height="394" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/squarespace-winback-2.jpg" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
          </div>
          <h3>
           How Groove wins users back
          </h3>
          <p>
           Groove also experimented with win back emails as part of their onboarding flow. If a user hadnt yet converted, they sent emails
           <a href="http://groovehq.com/blog/email-onboarding-optimization" target="_blank">
            7, 21 and 90 days
           </a>
           after the end of their free trial.
          </p>
          <div class="blog-photo">
           <img alt="groove-90-days-later" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-24477" height="304" src="http://unbounce.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/photos/groove-90-days-later.png" width="560"/>
          </div>
          <div class="blog-photo-annotation">
          </div>
          <p>
           90 days may seem far too long (will users even remember Groove?)  but when they tested it, they found
           <strong>
            that email
            <a href="http://groovehq.com/blog/email-onboarding-optimization" target="_blank">
             <strong>
              gets a steady 2% conversion rate
             </strong>
            </a>
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Not a massive number, but 2% is better than the 0% youd get if you had let them go forever!
          </p>

          <h2>
           Have hypothesis, will test
          </h2>
          <p>
           The key here isnt to copy the tactics used by the winning tests verbatim, but to find inspiration for running powerful tests to run in your own welcome messaging.
          </p>
          <p>
           To get started
           <strong>
            you need some customer insight
           </strong>
            either from your existing support emails, your ongoing customer development or by simply asking subscribers what theyre struggling with. This insight will help you
           <strong>
            create hypotheses about where you can add messages that motivate, that remove barriers, that build relationships and that create urgency
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           If you couple the landing page youve worked so hard on with an equally awesome welcome email series, youll find that more subscribers will happily convert. But the only way to make your welcome emails awesome is to start testing today. You can use

           </a>
           or
           <a href="http://www.sparkpage.com/" target="_blank">
            SparkPage
           </a>
           to build your first welcome sequence  they all have free trials.

                <p>
                 Dave, great point. This is acually the subject of a follow up post Im writing, so Ill try give you a succint, quick reply now.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Aboslutely, if you pick a winner with no statistical significance, you might as well be flipping a coin. So we base this 2-3 weeks on 3 key factors:
                </p>
                <p>
                 1. With welcome emails, I know roughly how many we send each week (i.e. how many people sign up), and therefore I know how long it takes to see a winner that will give a decent uplift. In our case, thats 21 days. (For us, decent = &gt;2%)
                </p>
                <p>
                 2. This cadence really helps from an administrative point of view. Were more likely to run more tests if we have a testing schedule and it becomes part of our routine.
                </p>
                <p>
                 3. And possibly the most important part: if, after 2-3 weeks (or the period you pick), there isnt a statistically significant difference then you should stop the test. The winning variation isnt going to produce a conversion uplift big enough to keep going (&gt;2% is the bar I set), so you should cut your losses and start experimenting with a different variation or in a different area.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Waiting for significance (as in the post you linked) has an opprotunity cost that is often overlooked. That cost is the uplift from the tests youre not running.
                </p>
