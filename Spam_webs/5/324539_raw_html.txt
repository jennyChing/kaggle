        <div class="pf-content">
         <p>
          <img alt="What Not to Wear" class="right" height="209" src="http://netdna.copyblogger.com/images/what-not-to-wear.jpg" title="Image of What Not to Wear" width="200"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          What in the world does the cheeky cable fashion show
          <em>
           What Not to Wear
          </em>
          have to do with effective blogging?
         </p>
         <p>
          Have I finally pushed this analogy thing too far?
         </p>
         <p>
          Nope this one is easy. But youre going to have to read the rest to see for yourself.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span id="more-856">
          </span>
          OK, lets proceed. But just to make sure were all on the same page, lets start with a quick summary of what
          <em>
           What Not to Wear
          </em>
          is all about.
         </p>
         <p>
          Depending on where you live,
          <em>
           What Not to Wear
          </em>
          is either a fashion show on
          <em>
           The Learning Channel
          </em>
          featuring Stacy London and Clinton Kelly, or a fashion show on the BBC starring Lisa Butcher and Mica Paris. The show, which originated in the UK, is an ambush-style reality program where fashion victims are critiqued on their current clothing choices, and then (often brutally) coached on buying an entire new wardrobe, all for the delight of the viewing audience.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Why Wrong Beats Right
         </h3>
         <p>
          Truth is, the approach taken by
          <em>
           What Not to Wear
          </em>
          actually helps both its victims and the audience make smarter fashion choices. And by adopting the reason the show is an effective educational program into your own blogging, youll be able to truly get through to your readers, which is a benefit to everyone involved.
         </p>
         <p>
          The key is to focus on mistakes, or
          <em>
           what not to do
          </em>
          , instead of focusing only on what to do. So, if youre writing a how to post that will do well in social media, your examples should focus on what not to do in order to best illustrate the
          <em>
           right
          </em>
          thing to do.
         </p>
         <p>
          Dont believe me? Well, theres actual psychological research that backs this up.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Fighting Fire With Mistakes
         </h3>
         <p>
          Wendy Joung performed behavioral training research on firefighters in 2006, and the results are published in
          <a href="http://www.blackwellpublishing.com/journal.asp?ref=0269-994X">
           Applied Psychology
          </a>
          . She and her colleagues found that firefighters trained with case studies that focused on others who had made poor decisions and suffered adverse consequences ultimately showed better judgment and better adaptive thinking than a control group provided with case studies that focused on positive results.
         </p>
         <p>
          Bottom linemistakes teach better than successes. You might already know this from your own life.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Increase Attention, Engagement and Retention
         </h3>
         <p>
          Training programs and content marketing face the same natural enemieslack of attention, poor engagement, and unmemorable approaches. Focusing on mistakes naturally attracts attention, increases engagement, and produces lessons that are vividly memorable its just the way were wired.
         </p>
         <p>
          This is much of what youre aiming for with blogging. Because no matter your ultimate goals, a lack of attention, engagement or retention will not allow you to get there.
         </p>
         <p>
          So, to create breakthrough blog posts, you should create vivid illustrations with the mistakes others have made, or simply mistakes in general. Youll find that you defeat the demons that a lack of attention, poor engagement, and bad retention represent.
         </p>
         <p>
          Still not convinced?
         </p>

         <p>
          Should you make this strategy the focus of every post? No, and I certainly dont. But keep in mind that for any type of how to or instructional content, adding in examples of what not to do will make you a more effective communicator.
