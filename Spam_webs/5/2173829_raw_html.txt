     <p>
      <b>
       Percy Mayfield
      </b>
      (August 12, 1920  August 11, 1984) was an
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Songwriter" title="Songwriter">
       songwriter
      </a>
      famous for the songs "
      <a href="/wiki/Hit_the_Road_Jack" title="Hit the Road Jack">
       Hit the Road Jack
      </a>
      " and "
      <a href="/wiki/Please_Send_Me_Someone_to_Love" title="Please Send Me Someone to Love">
       Please Send Me Someone to Love
      </a>
      ", as well as a successful
      <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">
       rhythm and blues
      </a>
      artist known for his smooth vocal style.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Percy_Mayfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mayfield was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Minden,_Louisiana" title="Minden, Louisiana">
       Minden
      </a>
      , the seat of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webster_Parish" title="Webster Parish">
       Webster Parish
      </a>
      in northwestern
      <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">
       Louisiana
      </a>
      . As a youth, his talent for
      <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">
       poetry
      </a>
      led him into songwriting and singing. He began his performing career in
      <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
       Texas
      </a>
      , and then moved to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">
       Los Angeles
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      in 1942; success as a singer continued to elude him. In 1947, a small
      <a href="/wiki/Record_label" title="Record label">
       record label
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Swing_Time_Records" title="Swing Time Records">
       Swing Time
      </a>
      , signed him to
      <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">
       record
      </a>
      his song "Two Years of Torture." The song sold steadily over the next few years, prompting
      <a href="/wiki/Art_Rupe" title="Art Rupe">
       Art Rupe
      </a>
      to sign Mayfield to his label,
      <a href="/wiki/Specialty_Records" title="Specialty Records">
       Specialty Records
      </a>
      in 1950.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mayfield_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mayfield-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although his vocal style was influenced by such stylists as
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Brown_(musician)" title="Charles Brown (musician)">
       Charles Brown
      </a>
      , Mayfield did not focus on the white market, unlike many West Coast bluesmen. Rather, he sang
      <a href="/wiki/Blues_ballad" title="Blues ballad">
       blues ballads
      </a>
      , mostly his own songs, in a gentle vocal style. His most famous recording, "
      <a href="/wiki/Please_Send_Me_Someone_to_Love" title="Please Send Me Someone to Love">
       Please Send Me Someone to Love
      </a>
      ", a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chart-topper" title="Chart-topper">
       number one
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">
       R&amp;B
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hit_single" title="Hit single">
       hit single
      </a>
      in 1950, was widely influential and recorded by many other singers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gillett_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gillett-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 1952 automobile accident left Mayfield seriously injured, including a facial disfigurement that limited his performing. However, that did not stop his prolific songwriting. Mayfield continued to write and record for Specialty until 1954, and then recorded for
      <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">
       Chess Records
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Records" title="Imperial Records">
       Imperial Records
      </a>
      . His career continued to flourish with songs like "Strange Things Happening", "Lost Love", "What a Fool I Was", "Prayin' for Your Return"' "Cry Baby", and "Big Question".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1961, he came to the attention of
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">
       Ray Charles
      </a>
      with his song "
      <a href="/wiki/Hit_the_Road_Jack" title="Hit the Road Jack">
       Hit the Road Jack
      </a>
      ". Charles signed him to his
      <a href="/wiki/Tangerine_Records_(1963)" title="Tangerine Records (1963)">
       Tangerine Records
      </a>
      label, primarily as a songwriter; there he wrote "Hide Nor Hair", "At The Club", "Danger Zone", and "On the Other Hand, Baby".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mayfield_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mayfield-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The period following the RCA recordings in the early 70's saw Percy Mayfield once again slip into obscurity and poverty; there was, however, one final chapter to his career. In the early 80's Bay Area blues musician
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Naftalin" title="Mark Naftalin">
       Mark Naftalin
      </a>
      discovered that Percy was living in the East Bay somewhere and was able to provide Percy with a band for live performances around several Marin County and East Bay clubs. The exposure also led to 1982 Timeless studio date with the excellent
      <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Walker" title="Phillip Walker">
       Phillip Walker
      </a>
      Blues Band.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death">
       Death
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Percy_Mayfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      When Mayfield died of a
      <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">
       heart attack
      </a>
      in 1984, the day before his 64th birthday,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      he had fallen back into obscurity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Percy_Mayfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Percy Mayfield married three times. The identity of his first wife is unknown; his second wife was Willie Mae Atlas Mayfield; his third wife was Tina Mayfield. With his second wife, he had one child, daughter Pamela, and three grandchildren.
     </p>
