          <p>
           I remember my first digital camera as if it was yesterday, a Nikon Coolpix 775, 2.1 glorious megapixels that was going to forever change my life. At the time in fall of 2001, this camera cost well over $300, nearly what I paid for my used Canon EOS A2 film camera a year earlier! It was a great camera but fell short in so many places where my film SLR was clearly better, like
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Horrible low light performance
           </li>
           <li>
            Digital noise across the board was terrible
           </li>
           <li>
            Shutter lag
           </li>
           <li>
            Focus speed
           </li>
           <li>
            Frames per second, make that frame per second
           </li>
           <li>
            Wide angle and telephoto options, well they werent options!
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           What it did do was revolutionize how I shot, because I could now photograph anything, anywhere without the cost of film. Even with the limitations it greatly expanded my photography abilities.
          </p>
          <p>
           10 years later point and shoots are nearly disposable, the cost has decreased while the performance has increased beyond mosts wildest dreams. Sadly, these wonderful devices still take a back seat to the more flexible and SLR digital cameras of today. That doesnt mean that they cant and dont take amazing photographs though!
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/9171182@N02/5685720878/" target="_blank" title="Fog by subadei, on Flickr">
            <img alt="Fog" height="375" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5297/5685720878_93b552bf80.jpg" width="500"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/9171182@N02/5685720878/" target="_blank" title="Fog by subadei, on Flickr">
            Photo By subadei
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Great shots do take a little more planning simply because there are still some limitations from point and shoots. The stunning, spooky fog was captured on a Canon G12, most likely with the help of a tripod.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/nickbrokalakis/4297214200/" target="_blank" title="022 by nickb_rock, on Flickr">
            <img alt="022" height="375" src="http://farm5.static.flickr.com/4061/4297214200_fb5ec11e76.jpg" width="500"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/nickbrokalakis/4297214200/" target="_blank" title="022 by nickb_rock, on Flickr">
            Photo By nickb_rock
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Studio portraits, sure why not? Three flashes and a Canon G11 make this photograph possible.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/siddharthmenon/3494752358/" target="_blank" title="Runing on waters by Siddharth Menon, on Flickr">
            <img alt="Runing on waters" height="375" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3657/3494752358_6f0b8abff6.jpg" width="500"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/siddharthmenon/3494752358/" target="_blank" title="Runing on waters by Siddharth Menon, on Flickr">
            Photo By Siddharth Menon
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           As evident in this photo, freezing action shots are no longer impossible with point and shoot cameras!
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/intherough/3088579845/" target="_blank" title="Golden Blue by ...-Wink-..., on Flickr">
            <img alt="Golden Blue" height="375" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3179/3088579845_e425b4f709.jpg" width="500"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/intherough/3088579845/" target="_blank" title="Golden Blue by ...-Wink-..., on Flickr">
            Photo By -Wink-
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Wonderful landscapes and HDR, produced by a Panasonic point and shoot.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/ashleyrosex/2529813630/" target="_blank" title="past the point of love, (made it to #2 explore !) [10,00streamviews!] by ashley rose,, on Flickr">
            <img alt="past the point of love, (made it to #2 explore !) [10,00streamviews!]" height="375" src="http://farm4.static.flickr.com/3201/2529813630_b2124b701f.jpg" width="500"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/ashleyrosex/2529813630/" target="_blank" title="past the point of love, (made it to #2 explore !) [10,00streamviews!] by ashley rose,, on Flickr">
            Photo By ashley rose
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           One place that point and shoots have always shined was with macro. Im not sure why, but the focal distance has always been really close and for a fraction of the cost of a macro lens, shots like this can be captured. Done with a Canon Powershot SD750 the sharpness rivals that of the best macro lenses on DSLRs today.
          </p>
          <p>
           Hopefully this collection of photos showing the wide range of shots will inspire you to utilize your point and shoot more. For me, Ive upgraded from the 775 several times over the year and on any given day youre more likely to catch me with a point and shoot than my DSLR. The pocket-ability, ease of use and quality are the main reasons I shoot with it. The other thing Ive noticed is that doing street photography with a point and shoot seems to be so much easier. Strangers are less likely to look at you as anything other than a tourist instead of trying to discretely use that long lens on a DSLR body. Ive also started to use my point and shoot (Canon G10) in the studio, the manual control and hot shoe allow me to do everything my DSLR can do, the focal length is perfect and the images are always crisp. Best of all, I never have to worry about sensor dust!
          </p>

              <section class="comment-content comment">
               <p>
                I treasure what my DSLR can do.  But  the ability of advanced point and shoot cameras to operate in total silence have allowed me to get images that would have been far more difficult with a DSLR.  Ive shot over 5 weddings and several studio portrait sessions and professional events with advanced point and shoot cameras (i.e. those with full manual modes and raw)  and gotten fantastic results.
               </p>
               <p>
                Equipment is important  but skill and vision is 1000 times more important.  Every camera has advantages and limitations.  Adapting to both is the key.  Magnum photographer Alex Majoli used a point and shoot because it reminded him of his rangefinder more than his DSLRs did  he shot the Iraq War and the presidential elections for Time and Newsweek with a point and shoot  and won the award for magazine photographer of the year (google the article Alex Majoli Points and Shoots).
               </p>
               <p>
                I used to pick on a friend (in a friendly way of course) who would use nothing but disposable cameras.  She could afford to buy a Leica without a second thought  yet she insisted on using the disposable cameras  because she enjoyed using them.  After seeing her photographs, lets just say I dont pick on her anymore.  Like Majoli and my friend, I learned that the key is using what allows you to enjoy making pictures your way.
               </p>
               <p>
                Thanks for the excellent article.
               </p>
              </section>
