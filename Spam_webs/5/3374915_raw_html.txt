           <span content="http://www.fastcodesign.com/3046264/this-crazy-way-to-paint-patterns-on-3-d-objects-is-like-a-cartoon-come-to-life" itemprop="permalink">

             <div class="twelve columns">
              <h1 class="title" itemprop="title headline">
               Crazy Way To Paint Patterns On 3-D Objects Is Like A Cartoon Come To Life
              </h1>
             </div>
             <span class="deck pull-three" itemprop="deck description">
              <p>
               Thanks to new technology, even the most elaborate object can be precisely painted just by dipping it in a swirling vat of rainbow colors.
              </p>
             </span>
            </div>
            <div class="tools row collapse">
             <div class="twelve columns hide-on-print">
              <div class="share-box">
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </header>
           <div class="row collapse">
            <div class="body prose twelve columns" itemprop="body articleBody">
             <p>
              In old Warner Bros. cartoons, one common gag is a single can of paint that contains an elaborate pattern, like plaid, which can be applied to a surface with a single brush stroke. It's funny because it totally defies physics.
             </p>
             <p>
              The closest thing we've had to this up until now is a technology called hydrographic printing, which would apply a patterened film layer to an object when you dunked it in a pool of water. But the problem, though, was that it wasn't precise. How a piece of plastic film stretched across an object when it was dunked yielded inconsistent results. Until now, that is.
             </p>
             <p>
              Thanks to a jaw-dropping new breakthrough in hydrographic printing, researchers have figured out how to precisely paint multi-colored patterns on an object just by dipping it into a pool of water.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//a.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/05/3046264-inline-i-1-this-crazy-way-to-paint-3-d-objects-is-like-a-cartoon-come-to-life-copy.gif"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              A team comprised of researchers from Hangzhou's Zheijiang University and NYC's Columbia University to figure out a solution to the problem, which they call computational hydrographic printing. Essentially, what they do is 3-D scan whatever object they want to print on
              <em>
               before
              </em>
              they dunk it. Algorithms then take whatever pattern you want to paint on it, and print it on the layer of transparent film in such a way that, when lowered into the water bath by a robotic arm, the pattern will be applied perfectly, every time.
             </p>
             <p>
              According to the researchers, this technique could be used to easily paint 3-D printed objects, or even more traditionally manufactured items, like a mug. And by dunking an object multiple times, you could even apply three-dimensional paint patterns to an object, making it look painted by hand.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-video inline">
              <iframe frameborder="0" height="340" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/YlUhPrAqiY0?rel=1&amp;autoplay=0" width="560">
              </iframe>
             </figure>
             <p>
              Since this is still in the research stage, there's no telling when, or if, this technology will truly change how we paint 3-D objects.              </p>
