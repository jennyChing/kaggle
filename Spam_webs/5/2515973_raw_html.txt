               <img alt="Ettore Sottsass Book" src="/blogs/daily/2014/06/ettore-sottsass-philippe-thome/_jcr_content/par/cn_contentwell/par-main/cn_blogpost/cn_image_1.size.ettore-sottsass-book-01-book-cover-philippe-thome.jpg" title=""/>
               <p class="body credits">

                 Ettore Sottsass
                </i>
                by Philippe Thom. Photo courtesy of Phaidon
               </p>

             <div class="parbase cn_text">
              <div class="body ">
               <p>
                Ettore Sottsass (19172007) is probably best known for founding the Memphis Group, a Milan-based collective of young designers who produced brightly colored, asymmetrical furniture, lamps, and ceramics in the early 1980s. But those were just a few years in a prolific career that spanned decades and disciplines. Now a new book,
                <i>
                 <a href="http://www.phaidon.com/store/design/ettore-sottsass-9780714865843/" target="_blank">
                  Ettore Sottsass
                 </a>
                </i>
                (Phaidon, $150) by Philippe Thom, puts a spotlight on the Italian architect and designers entire oeuvre, from his iconic electronics for Olivetti, such as the Valentine typewriter, to his unique glass and ceramics.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="parbase image image-center cn_image">
              <figure class="media image-center">
               <img alt="Lora Totino villa, Italy" src="/blogs/daily/2014/06/ettore-sottsass-philippe-thome/_jcr_content/par/cn_contentwell/par-main/cn_blogpost/cn_image_3.size.ettore-sottsass-book-02-lora-totino-villa-italy.jpg" title=""/>
               <p class="body credits">
                A drawing of the Lora Totino villa, in Turin, Italy, circa 1951.
                <br/>
                Photo courtesy of Archivio Ettore Sottsass
               </p>
              </figure>
             </div>
             <div class="parbase cn_text">
              <div class="body ">
               <p>
                Sottsass, who studied architecture in Turin, Italy, after immigrating from his native Austria, possessed a playfulness that was integral to his design. In reaction to the rationalist architecture of his fathers generation, Sottsass once said, When I was young, all we ever heard about was functionalism, functionalism, functionalism. Its not enough. Design should also be sensual and exciting. Throughout the volume, Thom, a scholar of the history of architecture and design, draws parallels between life and art by juxtaposing Sottsasss biography with his boundary-crossing creations.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="parbase image image-center cn_image">
              <figure class="media image-center">
               <img alt="Valentine typewriter" src="/blogs/daily/2014/06/ettore-sottsass-philippe-thome/_jcr_content/par/cn_contentwell/par-main/cn_blogpost/cn_image.size.ettore-sottsass-book-03-valentine-typewriter.jpg" title=""/>
               <p class="body credits">
                The Valentine typewriter conceived by Sottsass for Olivetti, in 1969.
                <br/>
                Photo courtesy of Alberto Fioravanti
               </p>
              </figure>
             </div>
             <div class="parbase cn_text">
              <div class="body ">
               <p>
                Alternating between personal history and critical analysis clearly links Sottsasss influencesstudying architecture at Turin Polytechnic University, serving in WWII, being held as a prisoner of war, traveling to the far reaches of Asiawith his work. The tome also includes five short essays, written by design experts such as Francesca Picchi and Emily King, that explore Sottsasss work in architecture, graphic design, photography, industrial design, and collectors editions.
               </p>
              </div>
