        <p>
         <u>
          <strong>
           Fire
          </strong>
         </u>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Wild fires destroy millions of dollars worth of property every year and have the potential to take lives. Communities surrounded by forests are most at risk. Wild fires can start easily and spread quickly. All it takes is a discarded cigarette or a campfire left unattended.
        </p>
        <p>
         <br/>
         Few fires spread faster than 1 km/hour, and most move at less than 0.5 km/hour. Leaf litter, dead grass, dry moss, or lichen, dead or live brush, and tree foliage are all forms of highly combustible matter.
        </p>
        <p>
         <br/>
         House fires are always a possible danger and could be an added peril in the event of a disaster. It is imperative that you know and follow the safety measures for fire prevention, detection and escape. Having this knowledge is extremely important, especially after a disaster, as fire fighters may not be able to get to you quickly.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <u>
          <strong>
           Types of Fires and Response
          </strong>
         </u>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Electrical Fires
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Be sure to shut off the electricity FIRST. NEVER USE WATER on an electrical fire! Use baking soda, dry chemicals, or carbon dioxide.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Oil or Grease Fires
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Smother the flames. Use a proper fire extinguisher, a lid, baking soda, or sand. DO NOT USE WATER. It will only spread the flammable liquid.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Natural Gas Fires
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Shut off the gas supply. Then use an extinguisher, sand, or water to put out the fire.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Prevention
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Most accidental fires can be avoided by using common sense and a little care. Good housekeeping is the first line of defense against house fires.
         <br/>
         Safe Storage of Hazardous Materials
         <br/>
         Hazardous materials (flammables, pesticides, cleaning supplies, etc.) should be handled with care and respect. Keep supplies stored in your home at a minimum, with flammable liquids stored in metal containers, away from heat sources. To reduce the possibility of additional problems:
         <br/>
         Purchase hazardous substances in unbreakable containers and store them in appropriate areas with secure latches,
         <br/>
         Store away from children, foodstuffs and emergency supplies,
         <br/>
         Avoid storing materials together that create toxic or combustible combinations when mixed. (e.g. Liquid chlorine bleach and ammonia).
         <br/>
         Should a spill occur, extreme caution should be used when determining the method and extent of containment, and cleanup:
         <br/>
         Ensure the area is well ventilated before entering,
         <br/>
         Ensure there are no sources of ignition (smoke, sparks, etc.),
         <br/>
         Avoid direct contact with hazardous materials (wear protective clothing),
         <br/>
         Contain liquids with absorbent material (sand, kitty litter) to prevent spreading, and
         <br/>
         Remember to follow the manufacturer's directions (on labels) for usage, storage, and disposal.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Detection
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Statistics suggest that most fatal fires in homes occur while the family is sleeping. Smoke and toxic gases are the killers in most home fires, rather than the flames. Smoke detectors can alert your family while the fire is still small and can make the difference between life and death.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         For minimum protection, there should be a smoke detector between the bedrooms and the rest of the house, and at the top of each stairway. The amount of detection equipment required in your residence, depends on the size and floor plan of your home, your life style and how much money you are willing' to spend. Your local fire department can assist you with selecting a suitable fire detection system.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Response
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         When a fire occurs under normal conditions, it is essential to call the fire department first. In a disaster, phone lines may not be in service.
         <br/>
         If the fire is small, try to control the spread of the flames yourself, BUT only if it is safe to do so. Always keep an open escape route between you and the fire.
         <br/>
         If a fire does occur, your home might be saved if you know how to fight fires, act promptly and have firefighting tools on hand. In a major disaster, there is always a risk of fire. Work closely with your neighbours to keep the flames from spreading.
         <br/>
         Basic Firefighting Tools
         <br/>
         Garden hose
         <br/>
         Bucket of sand
         <br/>
         Ladder
         <br/>
         Knowledge of how to extinguish all types of fires
         <br/>
         Fire extinguisher. Have an all-purpose ABC fire extinguisher in your home and in your car. Check them regularly to be sure they are fully charged and ready for use.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Fire extinguishers
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         There are several classes of fire extinguishers:
         <br/>
         Class A puts out fires from ordinary combustibles such as wood and paper.
         <br/>
         Class B puts out fires from flammable liquids such as grease, oil, and gasoline.
         <br/>
         Class C are used for electronic fires, and
         <br/>
         Class D is used for flammable metals. A good fire extinguisher is one that is multi-class and can put out several types of fires. Dry chemical extinguishers, which are multi-class, are commonly available.
         <br/>
         When fighting a small fire with a fire extinguisher, remember PASS.
         <br/>
         1.Pull the pin at the top of the extinguisher that keeps the handle from being accidentally pushed.
         <br/>
         2.Aim the nozzle toward the base of the fire.
         <br/>
         3.Squeeze the handle to discharge the extinguisher.
         <br/>
         4.Sweep the nozzle back and forth at the base of the fire.
         <br/>
         Note: Once the fire appears to be out, watch it carefully as it may reignite.
         <br/>
         If your clothes catch fire, STOP, DROP, and ROLL!
         <br/>
         If the fire is beyond your ability to contain
         <br/>
         GET OUT FAST and STAY OUT.
         <br/>
         Do not stop to dress, gather pets, or collect valuables. Smoke and toxic fumes can kill you within minutes.
         <br/>
         Have a pre-determined meeting place outside where all family members will report as soon as possible. When two people are at the meeting place, one should run to the nearest phone (neighbors) and phone the fire department. The second person stays to report to the others that help is on the way.
         <br/>
         Include fire escape routes in your family disaster drills.
         <br/>
         Remember, smoke, toxic fumes and heat RISE. To avoid these hazards:
         <br/>
         oDrop to the floor.
         <br/>
         oCrawl to the nearest wall and follow it around to the door.
         <br/>
         oKeep your head about 18 inches above the floor and take short, small breaths while escaping.
         <br/>
         oCover your mouth with a cloth if possible.
         <br/>
         oTeach your family not to open any doors if they feel hot when touched.
         <br/>
         oKeep doors and windows closed to limit the rapid spreading of the flames.
         <br/>
         oFor children, a fire and escape drill should be a wise mixture of seriousness and a game - never a scary situation. Children who have had fire escape practice at home will automatically do the right thing in a real emergency. Without practice, they will most likely hide under a bed or in a closet, which unfortunately can result in death.
