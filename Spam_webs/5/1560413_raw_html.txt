    The pot calling the kettle black
   </h1>

     <p>
      The phrase "
      <b>
       The pot calling the kettle black
      </b>
      " is an
      <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">
       idiom
      </a>
      used to claim that a person is guilty of the very thing of which they accuse another.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations_and_origins">
       Interpretations and origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_pot_calling_the_kettle_black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interpretations and origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As generally understood, the person accusing (the "pot") is understood to share some quality with the target of their accusation (the "kettle"). The pot is mocking the kettle for a little soot when the pot itself is thoroughly covered in it.
     </p>
     <p>
      An alternative interpretation, recognized by some,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but not all,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      sources is that the pot is sooty (being placed on a fire), while the kettle is clean and shiny (being placed on coals only), and hence when the pot accuses the kettle of being black, it is the pots own sooty reflection that it sees: the pot accuses the kettle of a fault that only the pot has, rather than one that they
      <i>
       share
      </i>
      . The point is illustrated by a poem that appeared anonymously in an early issue of the
      <a href="/wiki/St._Nicholas_Magazine" title="St. Nicholas Magazine">
       St. Nicholas Magazine
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       "Oho!" said the pot to the kettle;
       <br/>
       "You are dirty and ugly and black!
       <br/>
       Sure no one would think you were metal,
       <br/>
       Except when you're given a crack."
       <br/>
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       "Not so! not so!" kettle said to the pot;
       <br/>
       "'Tis your own dirty image you see;
       <br/>
       For I am so clean  without blemish or blot 
       <br/>
       That your blackness is mirrored in me."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_themes_in_antiquity">
       Similar themes in antiquity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_pot_calling_the_kettle_black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Similar themes in antiquity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Ancient Greece
       </a>
       , mention of 
       <a href="/wiki/The_Snake_and_the_Crab" title="The Snake and the Crab">
        the Snake and the Crab
       </a>
        signified much the same idiom. The first instance of this is in a drinking song (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Skolion" title="Skolion">
         skolion
        </a>
       </i>
       ) dating from the late 6th or early 5th century BCE.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The fable ascribed to
       <a href="/wiki/Aesop%27s_Fables" title="Aesop's Fables">
        Aesop
       </a>
       concerns a mother crab and its young, where the mother tells the child to walk straight and is asked in return to demonstrate how that is done.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The same theme differently expressed occurs in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">
        Aramaic
       </a>
       version of the story of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahiqar" title="Ahiqar">
        Ahiqar
       </a>
       , dating from about 500 BCE. 'The bramble sent to the pomegranate tree saying, "Wherefore the multitude of thy thorns to him that toucheth thy fruit?" The pomegranate tree answered and said to the bramble, "Thou art all thorns to him that toucheth thee".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">
        Gospel of Matthew
       </a>
       7:3,
       <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
        Jesus
       </a>
       is quoted as asking, during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discourse_on_judgmentalism" title="Discourse on judgmentalism">
        discourse on judgmentalism
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">
        Sermon on the Mount
       </a>
       , "Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother's eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye?"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
