     <h1>
      Will The Retirement Age Change In The Future?
     </h1>
     <div class="layout-body-topbar clear">
      <span class="by-author">
       By
       <a href="/contributors/53717/">
        Anne M. Russell
       </a>
       | May 19, 2015
      </span>
      <span class="font-size">
       <span class="f-small">
        A
       </span>
       <span class="f-medium">
        A
       </span>
       <span class="f-large">
        A
       </span>
       |
       <a class="print_button" href="#">
        <img alt="" height="15" src="http://i.investopedia.com/public/img/icon_print.png"/>
       </a>
      </span>

        <div class="content">
         <div class="layout-body layout-body-article box clear">
          <div class="content-image">
           <img alt="" onerror="this.onerror='';this.src='http://i.investopedia.com/dimages/graphics/thinkstockphotos-495056669.jpg';return true;" src="http://i.investopedia.com/medium/dimages/graphics/thinkstockphotos-495056669.jpg" title=""/>
          </div>
          <div class="content-box">
           <p>
            Worried that the age of retirement will change? Put your mind to rest: It already has  the traditional retirement age of 65 is history for most of us. That change was initiated during the first
            <a href="http://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/socialsecurity.asp">
             Social Security
            </a>
            benefits crisis  in the early 1980s when Ronald Reagan was President.
           </p>
           <p>
            At this point, only people born in 1937 or before can begin receiving full Social Security benefits when they turn 65. For the rest of us, the age of eligibility has crept upward, hitting those who are now 45 and younger the hardest. Those people, born in 1960 or later, arent eligible for full benefits until they turn 67. For most of the Boomers, its age 66. (You are, of course, allowed to claim benefits earlier, but doing so permanently reduces the amount youll receive each payment. See
            <a href="http://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/031715/want-retire-early-think-again.asp">
             <em>
              Want To Retire Early? Think Again
             </em>
            </a>
            .)
           </p>
           <h2>
            A Trend?
           </h2>
           <p>
            But is this upward aging a one-time adjustment or the foreshadowing of a trend? Are we looking at a retirement age of 70 in the near future? The bulge in the population chart known as the baby boomers moving into retirement, combined with a smaller cohort of younger workers, has pushed Social Security into deficit spending. The good news, however, is that the agency set aside $2.7 trillion in the so-called
            <a href="http://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/102814/social-security-trust-fund-solvent.asp">
             Trust Fund
            </a>
            for this foreseeable event. But those extra dollars will be completely disbursed by 2033  or sooner  as longevity means more retirees claim benefits for a longer time.
           </p>
           <p>
            Even the Social Security Administration itself acknowledges this reality in its most recent Trustees Report, stating bluntly, Neither Medicare nor Social Security can sustain projected long-term program costs in full under currently scheduled financing.
           </p>
           <div class="ad-textlink ad-incontent-wrap">
            <div id="AdSlot_IC-Rectangle">
             <script type="text/javascript">
              &lt;!--//--&gt;&lt;![CDATA[// &gt;&lt;!--

             </script>
            </div>
           </div>
           <!-- .ad-textlink -->
           <h2>
            Avoiding the Worst-Case Scenario
           </h2>
           <p>
            There are a number of ways to save Social Security from
            <a href="http://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/insolvency.asp">
             insolvency,
            </a>
            ranging from increasing payroll taxes to curtailing benefits through reduction in cost-of-living increases or cutting benefits for high-income recipients. Obviously, these different options are going to vary in disfavor depending on the constituencies: Wage earners are going to be less enthusiastic about higher payroll taxes while retirees will fight against limiting benefits. Or, in a worst-case scenario, if the government takes no action at all, payments would actually drop by 25% of what they are currently to match lower incoming revenue. (For further reading, see
            <em>
             <a href="http://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/071514/why-social-security-running-out-money.asp">
              Why is Social Security running out of money?
             </a>
             )
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
            One of the easiest and least painful options  at least for those who are currently 45 and over  is continuing to push the age of eligibility upward for future generations, since it would affect only the youngest members of the population who are the farthest away from collecting, while grandfathering in those who are currently in or near retirement. Presidential candidate Senator Rand Paul (R-Kentucky) has repeatedly suggested in public comments that as part of his larger Social Security reform plan, he would start eligibility changes with those who are currently 55, but its unlikely that unpopular notion would gain enough support to pass Congress.
           </p>
           <h2>
            The Bottom Line
           </h2>
           <p>
            If no changes are made to Social Security, it will exhaust the Trust Fund money in about 15 years and benefits for everyone will be cut. The worst burden will fall on the youngest Americans, who may eventually face a shortfall so severe that the entitlement program would effectively cease to exist. So, in lieu of that, the federal government needs to act to increase worker contributions and reduce benefits payouts. One of the most likely tactics to accomplish that goal is to continue to push upward the age at which benefits can be claimed, from the current high of 67, to age 70, as the United Kingdom as already done for its youngest citizens. (See
            <em>
             <a href="http://www.investopedia.com/financial-edge/0710/raising-the-retirement-age-5-countries-testing-the-waters.aspx">
              Raising The Retirement Age: 5 Countries Testing The Waters
             </a>
             .)
            </em>
           </p>
