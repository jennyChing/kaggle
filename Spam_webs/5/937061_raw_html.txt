     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-163100 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="163100" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
            <iframe frameborder="0" height="338" scrolling="no" src="https://embed.gettyimages.com/embed/144716558?et=re9-mM-0Q6pqU1HQNrRcSg&amp;similar=off&amp;sig=sEK_JSNG-8y85y1gd8_lLS9VHySsAu3I5_pm-E0YjI0=" width="507">
            </iframe>
           </p>
           <p>
            Psychology seeks to improve our
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/empathy" title="Psychology Today looks at understanding">
             understanding
            </a>
            of human minds and behavior in a way that is consistent with the facts. That's the idea, anyway. But what happens when our best answers become, in a sense, unbelievable?
           </p>
           <p>
            There are various challenges in psychological research. Recent debates over replication and publication practices highlight some. The simple possibility that at some point it all gets too complex for mere mortals to understand is another. Yet we're making tangible progress on these fronts all the time.
           </p>
           <p>
            But here's another problem that hasn't been generally acknowledgedone that may be fast approaching and that involves centrally the public: What if it turns out that people mostly believe what they want to believe, and that some of the questions we address have answers that almost no one wants to believe?
           </p>
           <p>
            Rob Kurzban and I have recently run into this problem
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Hidden-Agenda-Political-Mind-Self-Interest/dp/0691161119" target="_blank" title="The Hidden Agenda of the Political Mind">
             when studying the public's political views
            </a>
            . We took a large database of political and demographic information and sought out the strongest connections. The results provided a pretty clear pattern. It turns out that people are often driven by what can be generally (though not fully) described as self-interest.
           </p>
           <p>
            Who tends to really like the idea of robust
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/politics" title="Psychology Today looks at government">
             government
            </a>
            safety nets? Poorer people with limited access to private social support. Who tends to think that the government ought to stay out of the income redistribution business? Richer, white, Christian men. Who's especially liberal on
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/religion" title="Psychology Today looks at religious">
             religious
            </a>
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/bias" title="Psychology Today looks at discrimination">
             discrimination
            </a>
            ? People who aren't Christian. The conservatives on these issues? Christians who don't excel at meritocracy (because of less
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/education" title="Psychology Today looks at education">
             education
            </a>
            , etc.). Who's especially liberal on immigration? Immigrants. Who doesnt like immigration? Native-born whites who don't excel at meritocracy. Who tends to want family planning tools available? People who spend long periods of their lives being sexually active but not wanting to have children. And so on.
           </p>
           <p>
            But our findings go against people's introspective beliefs. Hardly anyone points to self-interest in accounting for their own views. Instead, people generally claim that they and their political allies are just smart people looking out for what's best for society as a whole (or for some popular subset of society, like women, children, or the middle class). If there's self-interest at work here, most people think it's limited to their political opponents.
           </p>
           <p>
            Psychologists, though, are often unbothered by disconnects between the data patterns and people's self-descriptions. Theres a long line of research in social psychology and
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/cognition" title="Psychology Today looks at cognitive">
             cognitive
            </a>
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/neuroscience" title="Psychology Today looks at neuroscience">
             neuroscience
            </a>
            showing that people often arent aware of their own motives.
           </p>
           <p>
            <iframe frameborder="0" height="300" scrolling="no" src="https://embed.gettyimages.com/embed/170616675?et=gR4yCpKrSBFkXyYx5omE8A&amp;similar=on&amp;sig=xvLvnjno6x70FmKEMOhgtJde-3iRauraJJiGciXc4jY=" width="450">
            </iframe>
           </p>
           <p>
            It's worse than that, really. Not only are people often ignorant of their own motives, they're also masters of self-deceptive self-presentation. The introspective "mistakes" are often strategic maneuvers aimed at convincing others that the person is smart, nice, competent,
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/altruism" title="Psychology Today looks at altruistic">
             altruistic
            </a>
            , principled, and so on. People believe nice-sounding stories about themselves so that they can more effectively sell them to other people.
           </p>
           <p>
            In explaining the political mind, then, we were ultimately able to arrive at an account that is consistent with psychology and public opinion data. In short, people often prefer political positions that advance their own interests. Yet people also engage in self-deceptive spinthey consciously believe that their own political views stem from high and mighty motives. And it hardly does any good to point this out, because people usually prioritize their preferred policy outcomes and strategic narratives over such bloodless luxuries as empirical coherence.
           </p>
           <p>
            While the self-deceptive stories appeal to their various constituencies, our account, as far as we can tell, will please no one, including our own political selves (we have political views typical of our researcher demographic). Part of our defense of our efforts, in fact, is that our account is unwelcome to pretty much every conceivable political orientationright, left, center, libertarian, or whatever.
           </p>
           <p>
            And so arises a practical problem. We're left telling people something that our own account predicts they probably won't believe. In fact, if our account ends up changing the way lots of people talk about their own political views, then it's probably wrong.
           </p>
           <p>
            The broader issue is that much of the best social science is trending in this direction, towards explanations that reveal and undermine people's self-presentational biases. When these explanations have no natural constituencywhen there's no compelling reason why people ought to believe things they find unpleasant to believethen we're approaching a different kind of practical limit in social science.
           </p>
           <p>
            You wouldn't believe us if we told you.
           </p>

                <p>
                 "I only know what I read in the papers." Political opinions tend to be poorly informed and when people look at sketchy data they tend to see what they know. People will project themselves even on the starry sky and see Orion the hunter and not an automobile if they lived thousands of years ago. It may be partially incorrect to conflate self projection with selfishness. I have observed that people readily change their views when they are exposed to competent presenters such as philosophers in a debate. College students will be persuaded by philosopher "A" after her presentation, then they will agree with "B" who refutes "A" and then go back to agreeing with "A" after her rebuttal of "B". Sure minds are somewhat closed but they are also quite malleable. I suspect that the unbelievable selfishness of political opinion is largely an artifact of ignorance. A Catholic cardinal once said he was a liberal on issues where he was well informed and a conservative on issues about which he knew little. The prefrontal cortex will not be able to overrule the instinctual parts of the brain without abundant facts.
