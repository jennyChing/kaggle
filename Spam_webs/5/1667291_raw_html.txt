        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Lets See What Happens, Part Five: Making Connections
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="author vcard">
          By
          <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?author=2" title="View all posts by Rick Neal">
           Rick Neal
          </a>
          <span class="bl_sep">
           |
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="onDate">
          <a href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?p=865" rel="bookmark" title="1:50 pm">
           <span class="entry-date">
            November 16, 2011
           </span>
           <span class="entry-time">
            - 1:50 pm
           </span>
          </a>
          <span class="bl_sep">
           |
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="bl_categ">
          <a href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?cat=6" rel="category">
           What's he playing?
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p>
          So, the past few posts in this series have been laying the groundwork for this stage. The
          <a href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?p=821">
           Secrets Deck
          </a>
          and
          <a href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?p=846">
           Sandboxes
          </a>
          give you the raw materials, and
          <a href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?p=853">
           Watching Their Eyes
          </a>
          lets you assess those materials. Now, you start making decisions and building the rough shape of the campaign arc that is emerging from your play.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Look at What You Have
         </h3>
         <p>
          First thing you need to do is take a look at what youve got. Now, if youre like me
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_0_865" id="identifier_0_865" title="And god help you if you are.">
            1
           </a>
          </sup>
          , you probably intend to take careful notes of all the information youve gathered through the play of the campaign, and you may have done so every now and then, but then life gets in the way, and you wind up with not as much stuff written down as you might want
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_1_865" id="identifier_1_865" title="Quick tip: if you havent taken notes of what went on in a game session  say, by publishing it on a blog  take a look at your adventure notes to jog your memories. Itll also help to remind you what you didntuse.">
            2
           </a>
          </sup>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Well, now you get to make up for the lack. I recommend sitting down and making a list of what youve got. What goes on the list? Here are some ideas:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Villains the PCs hated
          </li>
          <li>
           Places that were cool
          </li>
          <li>
           Storylines that the players liked
          </li>
          <li>
           Things the PCs bypassed
          </li>
          <li>
           NPCs that the PCs liked
          </li>
          <li>
           Things that the players said they wanted to do but you havent got to yet
          </li>
          <li>
           Ideas you had for adventures that you never fleshed out
          </li>
          <li>
           Questions, themes, and situations that you laid in that never got explored
          </li>
          <li>
           Secrets the players are getting close to
          </li>
          <li>
           Sandbox elements that never came up
          </li>
          <li>
           Anything else that interests you or you have a question about
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          When I make my list, it usually starts as a compilation of the few times Ive remembered to keep notes, written down on a piece of paper in no particular order. I try to scatter them all over the page
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_2_865" id="identifier_2_865" title="Actually, I use two different approaches here. One is to do the scatter list, and the other is to put them in a numbered list so that I can randomly pick elements later using dice. The goal is to use some method to help you make associations and links you might not otherwise.">
            3
           </a>
          </sup>
          , for reasons Im going to talk about next. Once I get the things from my notes written down, I start adding other stuff, brainstorming style  I write down whatever comes to mind , no judgment, no evaluation.
         </p>
         <p>
          Often I find that one element sparks a memory of something else  these I try to group nearby on the paper, but I dont worry too much about that. Sometimes it almost cascades, as one idea leads to another, and then another, and so on.
         </p>
         <p>
          The page can get pretty crowded
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_3_865" id="identifier_3_865" title="Seriously, use a bigpiece of paper. Youre making a mind map with lots of different elements, and will be adding to it as you go, so give yourself room to sprawl.">
            4
           </a>
          </sup>
          , but thats okay. You want a rich collection of elements to choose from, but youre going to be picking and choosing later, so try not to pre-judge.
         </p>
         <p>
          How much stuff do you want on your list? As much as is fun. As much as is useful. The amount is going to vary depending on your temperament and how long the campaign has been running. You want enough stuff that not everything fits together neatly. Dont worry, you can ignore stuff that doesnt quite work for you later, and you can add other stuff you think of.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now, lets talk about what to do with that list.
         </p>
         <h3>
          The Glass Bead Game
         </h3>
         <p>
          First, go read
          <a href="http://rdonoghue.blogspot.com/2010/11/learn-this-game.html">
           this blog post
          </a>
          . Now, read the follow-up
          <a href="http://rdonoghue.blogspot.com/2010/11/glass-bead-fate.html">
           here
          </a>
          . It was this pair of blog posts by Rob Donoghue that helped me understand the structure of what I was doing, so thanks for that, Rob!
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_4_865" id="identifier_4_865" title="Another great example of this kind of adventure-building tool is in Jeremy Kellers Technoir, which bakes in this kind of scenario construction right from the start.">
            5
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
         <p>
          What I do next is look for something on my list that catches my eye: an NPC that I thought could have got more play, a location everyone thought was cool, an adventure whose outcome still bugs the players
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_5_865" id="identifier_5_865" title="And by this I dont mean they all went, Man, that ending sucked. I mean one where they went, Okay, we stopped the bad guys here, but I just knowtheres more of them, and theyre up to something.">
            6
           </a>
          </sup>
          . The goal is to grab something  anything, really  to use as the nucleus for the next couple of steps.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once youve picked your starting point, look at whats arrayed around it. If you followed my suggestions about creating the list, the elements you have near your starting point should be largely unrelated to the nucleus, and to each other
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_6_865" id="identifier_6_865" title="If, on the other hand, youre going with my numbered list suggestion, then just roll a few random items on the list and scatter them on a piece of paper.">
            7
           </a>
          </sup>
          . Draw some lines between the elements, and play the Glass Bead game, as suggested in Robs blog posts.
         </p>
         <p>
          This is an exercise in directed creativity, similar to William Burroughs cut-up text and the core ingredients on Iron Chef. Youre taking a number of arbitrary elements, and combining them into something new, coherent, and interesting. Its a way of coming up with connections that wouldnt have occurred to you normally. When youre playing the Glass Bead game with the elements youve chosen, youre not really looking for similarities. Youre looking for connections  ways to fit the elements together into a story.
         </p>
         <p>
          I tend to work with groups of five
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_7_865" id="identifier_7_865" title="Hail Eris! All Hail Discordia!">
            8
           </a>
          </sup>
          or six elements in the group. Thats plenty to build all sorts of interesting connections, but not so much that it gets muddy and (overly) complicated. Dont try to connect every element to every other element, but try and build a network that connects everything. When youre satisfied that youve got an interesting and coherent skeleton, put it aside
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_8_865" id="identifier_8_865" title="If the end result is not interesting and/or coherent, abandon it and start again. No pressure, no fault. This is a tool, not a test.">
            9
           </a>
          </sup>
          , maybe transferring the little diagram youve made to a separate sheet of paper.
         </p>
         <p>
          And then do it again. And again. And again, as often as is fun and useful, each time with different elements. I recommend putting together four to eight skeletons in this manner, giving you fodder for the next stage.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Summarize
         </h3>
         <p>
          At this point, I like to write a simple sentence or two to summarize the relationships within the skeleton that outlines the basic core of the adventure it presents. For example, in my old
          <a href="http://www.rickneal.ca/?p=91">
           Broken Chains
          </a>
          campaign, I had elements of anti-psionic sentiment in a nation, a conspiracy of psions, a powerful diplomat who was secretly a demon, elves agitating for an independent nation, and corruption within the church. These came together into a skeleton, and I wrote the following outline:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           Lady Elorowyr, representative to the High Seat from NATION
           <sup>
            <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_9_865" id="identifier_9_865" title="Whaddaya want? It was years ago, and I dont have my notes anymore. I cant remember the name.">
             10
            </a>
           </sup>
           , is actually a demon in the service of Lady Spite
           <sup>
            <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_10_865" id="identifier_10_865" title="The big evil goddess of the trinity of evil gods.">
             11
            </a>
           </sup>
           , and is working to exacerbate NATIONs isolationist tendencies, playing on their anti-psionic sentiment. She knows of the Legacy, the remnants of the former psionic ruling families, who secretly control the non-psionic government of NATION, and encourages them to work against the elven populace, who wish to form their own anion within NATIONs borders. Her contact is a secretary for the Prelate of the Mother.
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Now, thats kind of a long outline
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_11_865" id="identifier_11_865" title="It was built entirely of items from the Secrets Deck of for the campaign, which already has some strong narrative elements in place.">
            12
           </a>
          </sup>
          , but it sums up the situation, and gives you the foundation you need to start building adventures. The other thing writing the summary does is show you the stress points of the skeleton  the things that dont quite fit and dont quite work.
         </p>
         <p>
          When you spot a bit that doesnt quite work  like the idea of the contact in the summary above  you need to decide if youre going to fix it or cut it out. Fixing it takes some rethinking, but if the element adds cool things to the overall skeleton, its worth the work. On the other hand, sometimes the problematic piece doesnt add anything
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_12_865" id="identifier_12_865" title="More to the point, you cant think of a way to use it to add anything thats cool and isnt brought in by another element.">
            13
           </a>
          </sup>
          , cut it out of the skeleton and route around it.
         </p>
         <p>
          At the end of this process, you should have a small stack of little mind-maps that lay out the relationship of your selected elements, and a couple of sentences summarizing each skeleton.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Mix It Up
         </h3>
         <p>
          So, now youve got a small stack of skeletons that you can use to build a campaign arc. Its time to take a look at them, and see how you can mash them together.
         </p>
         <p>
          Look for similarities and overlap in the skeletons, both in the elements and in the relationships between them. See if there are recurring themes within the summaries. Look to see if there are ways that you can combine two or more skeletons into a larger one that still makes sense and is cool. Check to see if there are weaknesses in one skeleton that another can shore up, or strengths in one that another can enhance.Draw new mind-maps. Write new summaries. Play with the ideas until something clicks and you find the ultimate cool factor.
         </p>
         <p>
          One of the things I like to do at this point is look for a twist in a skeleton: something that I can use to pull a bit of a bait-and-switch on the players
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_13_865" id="identifier_13_865" title="Not maliciously. Not a gotcha. But a real twist: you thought A, but now you see its really B. What are you gonna do now?">
            14
           </a>
          </sup>
          . For example, in the situation from Broken Chains I outlined above, it looks like the nobility of NATION is really anti-psionic and oppressive to prevent the abuses that ancient psions committed in the past. But really, the non-psionic nobility are manipulated by a secret society of psions to continue the oppression and prevent powerful psions from usurping the societys power and influence. See? Twist!
         </p>
         <p>
          What youre aiming for is one solid skeleton to carry the campaign arc. When you think youve got that, move on to the next step.
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_14_865" id="identifier_14_865" title="What about the leftover skeletons? Hang on to them! Theyre good idea fodder for smaller adventures, or expanding the skeleton, or even just for the next campaign.">
            15
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
         <h3>
          Adjusting Scope
         </h3>
         <p>
          Now youve got your rough plan. Time to see if it fits into the rest of your campaign.
         </p>
         <p>
          One of the nice things about using this method is that all the elements came from your campaign, so the arc you come up with is pretty much guaranteed to fit into the game theme-wise. Its still a good idea to check to see if youre maintaining the mood and feel of the game so far, or that youre changing it in a direction that you and your players will be comfortable with. This is just a gut-check, but its a worthwhile thing to think about.
         </p>
         <p>
          The other fit you need to think about is whether its going to absorb as much of the remaining game as you want it to. Think about how much longer the campaign is going to run
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_15_865" id="identifier_15_865" title="Im assuming a finite campaign, here. If the campaign is not finite, think about how much campaign time you want this arc to take.">
            16
           </a>
          </sup>
          , and how much you want the game to focus on the campaign arc  that is, do you want everything to be about the arc from now on, or do you want to have some unrelated adventures sprinkled in?
         </p>
         <p>
          The nice thing about the summary and the skeleton is that they can expand or contract as you require, and as your desired pace indicates. If you want things tight and short, look at removing some of the elements and complexity. If you want things to be longer and more complex, look at adding some elements to extend things and act as screening elements and red herrings.
         </p>
         <p>
          Only you can decide when youve got the right amount of stuff and detail in the skeleton for your campaign arc. Build in as much or as little as you need. Then on to the actual structure of the adventures.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Making Your Map
         </h3>
         <p>
          At this point, youve got a solid idea about what the arc is. Time to get things ready for the players to come and mess stuff up  which is another way of saying that you need to rough in the adventures.
         </p>
         <p>
          Look at your skeleton, and find a good adventure. It doesnt need to be the first one  though thats where I often start  or the last one  which is the other place I most frequently start  but it needs to be something you think is cool. Write a couple of sentences summarizing the plot, adversaries, outcomes, etc.
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_16_865" id="identifier_16_865" title="I like to use index cards for this.">
            17
           </a>
          </sup>
          Then find another adventure and do the same. Keep doing it until you have all the adventures you want or need for the arc.
         </p>
         <p>
          Two adventures are critical: the adventure that hooks the players in, and the climax adventure. As noted above, I usually start from one end or the other, and usually do both before I start trying to fill in the middle part. The main goal of the hook adventure is to be cool enough that the characters are interested in continuing to follow the threads you give them, and the main goal of the climax adventure is to be cool enough to put a satisfying cap on the entire sequence. Thats why I like to have a solid handle on those two adventures before the middle ones.
         </p>
         <p>
          With the middle adventures, you need to decide if the arc is going to be linear or sandboxy
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_17_865" id="identifier_17_865" title="Its a word if I say its a word.">
            18
           </a>
          </sup>
           whether the adventures run in a straight line, or if the characters get to pick and choose a bit where they go next. I tend to make mine a combination of the two. By putting the summaries for the adventures on index cards, you can move them around and see which arrangements work best for your arc and your players.
         </p>
         <p>
          Keep playing with the arrangement until you have a sequence that you like, and then take a good look at the hook and climax summaries, to see if anything needs to change, to be added, or to be removed. Look at the arc as a whole, and make sure its doing what you want. If its not, play with it some more. As I said previously, this is a tool, not a test. You get as many do-overs as you want
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_18_865" id="identifier_18_865" title="And remember, this is all supposed to be fun. Its a game. If youre not having fun at any point, put it aside and walk away for a couple days. And also? This isnt the onlyway to do this kind of thing. Just one way that I do it.">
            19
           </a>
          </sup>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          When youre happy with the overall arc, youre done. I recommend writing down the final form of things, because I always manage to forget something. This process gives you a map of adventures that youll need to flesh out for actual play, but you dont need to do that right away
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_19_865" id="identifier_19_865" title="Except for maybe the hook adventure, if youre in a time crunch.">
            20
           </a>
          </sup>
          . I suggest leaving the thing alone for a couple of days so that you come back to it with fresh eyes before starting to build the adventures
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_20_865" id="identifier_20_865" title="This also gives you a little time to bask in the well-deserved satisfaction of having built a campaign arc based on you players choices and preferences, tailoring the game to them. You rock as a GM. Enjoy that feeling, because itll probably go away five minutes into playing the first adventure. Sorry.">
            21
           </a>
          </sup>
          .
         </p>
         <h3>
          On The Fly
         </h3>
         <p>
          The above process assumes youve got a good chunk of time to do your prep, and that you like having things mapped out in a pretty concrete manner. Neither of these assumptions may be true, so Im going to give a quick rundown about doing a lot of this stuff on the fly.
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           Still make your list, but only put the coolest stuff on it. During play, add the coolest elements that come up.
          </li>
          <li>
           Make just one skeleton based on the coolest element on your list.
          </li>
          <li>
           Come up with a good hook and an idea about the climax.
          </li>
          <li>
           Build the hook adventure and, as you run it, look for a logical next step to take you towards the climax.
          </li>
          <li>
           Add elements to your list during play, and adjust your ideas about the climax based on the coolest of these.
          </li>
          <li>
           When you get to the climax adventure, give it your all!
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats about all
          <sup>
           <a class="footnote-link footnote-identifier-link" href="#footnote_21_865" id="identifier_21_865" title="Like Im not running off at the mouth.">
            22
           </a>
          </sup>
          Ive got to say about Making Connections. Weve got two more posts to go: Give Your Head a Shake and Discard Liberally. I hope to have the series wrapped up by Christmas.
         </p>
         <p>
          See you next time.
         </p>
