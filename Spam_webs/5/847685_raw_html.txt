    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Greenwashing
      </b>
      (a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_word" title="Compound word">
       compound word
      </a>
      modelled on "
      <a href="/wiki/Whitewash_(censorship)" title="Whitewash (censorship)">
       whitewash
      </a>
      "), or "green sheen,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Spin_(public_relations)" title="Spin (public relations)">
       spin
      </a>
      in which
      <a href="/wiki/Green_PR" title="Green PR">
       green PR
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Green_marketing" title="Green marketing">
       green marketing
      </a>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Deception" title="Deception">
       deceptively
      </a>
      used to promote the perception that an organization's products, aims or policies are
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">
       environmentally friendly
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Evidence that an organization is greenwashing often comes from pointing out the spending differences: when significantly more money or time has been spent advertising being "green" (that is, operating with
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">
       consideration for the environment
      </a>
      ), than is actually spent on environmentally sound practices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Greenwashing efforts can range from changing the name or label of a product to evoke the
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">
       natural environment
      </a>
      on a product that contains harmful chemicals to multimillion dollar advertising campaigns portraying highly polluting energy companies as eco-friendly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While greenwashing is not new, its use has increased over recent years to meet consumer demand for
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">
       environmentally friendly
      </a>
      goods and services. The problem is compounded by lax enforcement by regulatory agencies such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">
       Federal Trade Commission
      </a>
      in the United States, the
      <a href="/wiki/Competition_Bureau" title="Competition Bureau">
       Competition Bureau
      </a>
      in Canada, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Advertising_Practice" title="Committee of Advertising Practice">
       Committee of Advertising Practice
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_Committee_of_Advertising_Practice" title="Broadcast Committee of Advertising Practice">
       Broadcast Committee of Advertising Practice
      </a>
      in the United Kingdom. Critics of the practice suggest that the rise of greenwashing, paired with ineffective regulation, contributes to consumer skepticism of all green claims, and diminishes the power of the consumer in driving companies toward greener solutions for manufacturing processes and business operations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many corporate structures use greenwashing as a way to repair public perception of their brand. The structuring of corporate disclosure is often set up so as to maximize perceptions of legitimacy. However, there is a growing body of social and environmental accounting research finds corporate posturing and deception in the absence of external monitoring and verification.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
         <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">
          clarification needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
       Usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenwashing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenwashingcard.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Greenwashingcard.JPG/220px-Greenwashingcard.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Greenwashingcard.JPG/330px-Greenwashingcard.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Greenwashingcard.JPG/440px-Greenwashingcard.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenwashingcard.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hotel "greenwashed" laundry card
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       greenwashing
      </i>
      was coined by
      <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">
       New York
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">
       environmentalist
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay_Westervelt" title="Jay Westervelt">
       Jay Westervelt
      </a>
      in a 1986 essay regarding the
      <a href="/wiki/Hospitality_industry" title="Hospitality industry">
       hotel industry
      </a>
      's practice of placing placards in each room promoting reuse of towels ostensibly to "save the environment." Westervelt noted that, in most cases, little or no effort toward reducing energy waste was being made by these institutionsas evidenced by the lack of cost reduction this practice effected. Westervelt opined that the actual objective of this "green campaign" on the part of many hoteliers was, in fact, increased profit. Westervelt thus labeled this and other outwardly environmentally conscientious acts with a greater, underlying purpose of profit increase as
      <i>
       greenwashing
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition, the political term "linguistic detoxification" describes when, through
      <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">
       legislation
      </a>
      or other
      <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">
       government
      </a>
      action, the definitions of
      <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">
       toxicity
      </a>
      for certain substances are changed, or the name of the substance is changed, so that fewer things fall under a particular classification as toxic. The origin of this phrase has been attributed to environmental activist and author
      <a href="/wiki/Barry_Commoner" title="Barry Commoner">
       Barry Commoner
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Similarly, introduction of a
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission_trading" title="Carbon emission trading">
       Carbon Emission Trading Scheme
      </a>
      may feel good, but may be counterproductive if the cost of carbon is priced too low, or if large emitters are given "free credits." For example,
      <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">
       Bank of America
      </a>
      subsidiary
      <a href="/wiki/MBNA" title="MBNA">
       MBNA
      </a>
      offers an Eco-Logique
      <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" title="MasterCard">
       MasterCard
      </a>
      for Canadian consumers that rewards customers with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_offsets" title="Carbon offsets">
       carbon offsets
      </a>
      as they continue using the card. Customers may feel that they are nullifying their
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">
       carbon footprint
      </a>
      by purchasing polluting goods with the card. However, only 0.5 percent of purchase price goes into purchasing carbon offsets, while the rest of the
      <a href="/wiki/Interchange_fee" title="Interchange fee">
       interchange fee
      </a>
      still goes to the bank.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CashingIn_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CashingIn-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenwashing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the mid 1960s, the environmental movement gained momentum. This popularity prompted many companies to create a new green image through advertising. Jerry Mander, a former Madison advertising executive, called this new form of advertising "ecopornography."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first
      <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">
       Earth Day
      </a>
      was held on April 22, 1970. This encouraged many industries to advertise themselves as being friendly to the environment. Public utilities spent 300 million dollars advertising themselves as clean green companies. This was eight times more than the money they spent on pollution reduction research.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tglo_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tglo-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfs_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gfs-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1985, the
      <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">
       Chevron Corporation
      </a>
      launched one of the most famous greenwashing ad campaigns in history. Chevron's "People Do" advertisements were aimed at a "hostile audience" of "societally conscious" people. Two years after the launch of the campaign, surveys found people in California trusted Chevron more than other oil companies to protect the environment. In the late 1980s The
      <a href="/wiki/American_Chemistry_Council" title="American Chemistry Council">
       American Chemistry Council
      </a>
      started a program called
      <a href="/wiki/Responsible_Care" title="Responsible Care">
       Responsible Care
      </a>
      , which shone light on the environmental performances and precautions of the group's members. The loose guidelines of responsible care caused industries to adopt self-regulation over government regulation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tglo_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tglo-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfs_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gfs-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1991, a study published in the Journal of Public Policy and Marketing (
      <a href="/wiki/American_Marketing_Association" title="American Marketing Association">
       American Marketing Association
      </a>
      ) found that 58% of environmental ads had at least one deceptive claim. Another study found that 77% of people said the environmental reputation of company affected whether they would buy their products. One fourth of all household products marketed around Earth Day advertised themselves as being green and environmentally friendly. In 1998 the
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">
       Federal Trade Commission
      </a>
      created the "Green Guidelines," which defined terms used in environmental marketing. The following year the FTC found that the Nuclear Energy Institute claims of being environmentally clean were not true. The FTC did nothing about the ads because they were out of their jurisdiction. This caused the FTC to realize they needed new clear enforceable standards. In 1999, according to environmental activist organizations, the word "greenwashing" was added to the
      <i>
       Oxford English Dictionary
      </i>
      .

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenwashing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: USA">

     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">
       Federal Trade Commission
      </a>
      (FTC) provides voluntary guidelines for environmental marketing claims. These guidelines give the FTC the right to prosecute false and misleading advertisement claims. The green guidelines were not created to be used as an enforceable guideline but instead were intended to be followed voluntarily. Listed below are the green guidelines set by the FTC.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Qualifications and disclosures: The Commission traditionally has held that in order to be effective, any qualifications or disclosures such as those described in these guides should be sufficiently clear, prominent and understandable to prevent deception. Clarity of language, relative type size and proximity to the claim being qualified, and an absence of contrary claims that could undercut effectiveness, will maximize the likelihood that the qualifications and disclosures are appropriately clear and prominent.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gft_27-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-gft-27">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          27
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Distinction between benefits of product, package and service: An environmental marketing claim should be presented in a way that makes clear whether the environmental attribute or benefit being asserted refers to the product, the product's packaging, a service or to a portion or component of the product, package or service. In general, if the environmental attribute or benefit applies to all but minor, incidental components of a product or package, the claim need not be qualified to identify that fact. There may be exceptions to this general principle. For example, if an unqualified "recyclable" claim is made and the presence of the incidental component significantly limits the ability to recycle the product, then the claim would be deceptive.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gft_27-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-gft-27">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          27
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Overstatement of environmental attribute: An environmental marketing claim should not be presented in a manner that overstates the environmental attribute or benefit, expressly or by implication. Marketers should avoid implications of significant environmental benefits if the benefit is in fact negligible.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gft_27-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-gft-27">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          27
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Comparative claims: Environmental marketing claims that include a comparative statement should be presented in a manner that makes the basis for the comparison sufficiently clear to avoid consumer deception. In addition, the advertiser should be able to substantiate the comparison.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gft_27-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-gft-27">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          27
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </blockquote>

       University of Oregon
      </a>
      in partnership with EnviroMedia Social Marketing, allows examples of greenwashing to be uploaded and rated by the public.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The British Code of Advertising, Sales Promotion and Direct Marketing has a specific section (section 49) targeting environmental claims.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to some organizations opposing greenwashing, there has been a significant increase in its use by companies over the last decade. TerraChoice Environmental Marketing, an advertising consultancy company issued a report denoting a 79% increase in the usage of corporate greenwashing between 2007 and 2009. Additionally, it has begun to manifest itself in new varied ways. Within the non-residential building products market in the United States, some companies are beginning to claim that their environmentally minded policy changes will allow them to earn points through the U.S. Green Building Council's Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design rating program. This point system has been held up as an example of the "gateway effect" that the drive to market products as environmentally friendly is having on company policies. Jim Nicolow, AIA, LEED Fellow, leader of architecture and planning firm Lord, Aeck &amp; Sargent's sustainable design initiative has claimed that the greenwashing trend may be enough to eventually effect a genuine reduction in environmentally damaging practices. According to the Home and Family Edition, 95% consumer products claiming to be green were discovered to commit at least one of the "Sins of Greenwashing". The Seven Sins of Greenwashing are as follows:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Sin of the Hidden Trade-off, committed by suggesting a product is "green" based on an unreasonably narrow set of attributes without attention to other important environmental issues.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sin of No Proof, committed by an environmental claim that cannot be substantiated by easily accessible supporting information or by a reliable third-party certification.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sin of Vagueness, committed by every claim that is so poorly defined or broad that its real meaning is likely to be misunderstood by the consumer.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sin of Worshiping False Labels is committed when a claim, communicated either through words or images, gives the impression of a third-party endorsement where no such endorsement exists.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sin of Irrelevance, committed by making an environmental claim that may be truthful but which is unimportant or unhelpful for consumers seeking environmentally preferable products.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sin of Lesser of Two Evils, committed by claims that may be true within the product category, but that risk distracting consumers from the greater environmental impact of the category as a whole.
      </li>
      <li>
       Sin of Fibbing, the least frequent Sin, is committed by making environmental claims that are simply false.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         50
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      In 2008, Ed Gillespie identified "ten signs of greenwashing", which are similar to the
      <i>
       Seven Sins
      </i>
      listed above, but with three additional indicators.
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Suggestive pictures - Images that imply a baseless green impact, such as flowers issuing from the exhaust pipe of a vehicle.
      </li>
      <li>
       Just not credible - A claim that touts the
       <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">
        environmentally friendly
       </a>
       attributes of a dangerous product, such as cigarettes.
      </li>
      <li>
       Gobbledygook - The use of
       <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">
        jargon
       </a>
       and/or information that the average person can not readily understand or be able to verify.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         51
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Companies may pursue
      <a href="/wiki/Environmental_certification" title="Environmental certification">
       environmental certification
      </a>
      to avoid greenwashing through independent verification of their green claims. For example, the
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Trust" title="Carbon Trust">
       Carbon Trust
      </a>
      Standard launched in 2007 with the stated aim "to end 'greenwash' and highlight firms that are genuine about their commitment to the environment".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
