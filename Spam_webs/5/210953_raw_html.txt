     <p>
      <b>
       Dennis Coles
      </b>
      (born May 9, 1970),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      better known by his
      <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">
       stage name
      </a>
      <b>
       Ghostface Killah
      </b>
      , is an American
      <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">
       rapper
      </a>
      and prominent member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">
       Wu-Tang Clan
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After the group achieved breakthrough success in the aftermath of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">
        Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)
       </a>
      </i>
      , the members went on to pursue solo careers to varying levels of success. Ghostface Killah debuted his solo-career with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ironman_(Ghostface_Killah_album)" title="Ironman (Ghostface Killah album)">
        Ironman
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1996, which was well received by music critics. He has continued his success over the following years with critically acclaimed albums such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Clientele" title="Supreme Clientele">
        Supreme Clientele
       </a>
      </i>
      (2000) and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FishScale" title="FishScale">
        FishScale
       </a>
      </i>
      (2006). His stage name was taken from one of the characters in the 1979
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kung_fu" title="Kung fu">
       kung fu
      </a>
      film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mystery_of_Chessboxing" title="Mystery of Chessboxing">
        Mystery of Chessboxing
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newyorker-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is the founder of his own label Starks Enterprises.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ghostface Killah is critically acclaimed
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [

       stream-of-consciousness

      <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">
       slang

      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Non_sequitur_(literary_device)" title="Non sequitur (literary device)">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">
       Early work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A roommate of Wu-Tang founder
      <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">
       RZA
      </a>
      , Ghostface helped bring together the other seven members. In 1995, Ghostface guest-starred extensively on fellow Clan member
      <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">
       Raekwon
      </a>
      's debut album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Only_Built_4_Cuban_Linx..." title="Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...">
        Only Built 4 Cuban Linx...
       </a>
      </i>
      , appearing on almost every song and receiving nearly equal billing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also contributed songs to the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Park_(film)" title="Sunset Park (film)">
        Sunset Park
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Be_a_Menace_to_South_Central_While_Drinking_Your_Juice_in_the_Hood" title="Don't Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood">
        Don't Be a Menace to South Central While Drinking Your Juice in the Hood
       </a>
      </i>
      soundtracks, which would be included on his first solo LP,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ironman_(Ghostface_Killah_album)" title="Ironman (Ghostface Killah album)">
        Ironman
       </a>
      </i>
      , in 1996. The album, which debuted at #2 on the
      <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">
       <i>
        Billboard
       </i>
       200
      </a>
      , had a more pronounced
      <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">
       soul
      </a>
      influence (particularly 1970s soul) than previous Wu-Tang releases, and Ghostface's future albums would continue to feature this stylistic trait.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2000, he released his second studio album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Clientele" title="Supreme Clientele">
        Supreme Clientele
       </a>
      </i>
      . The album was well received by critics and peaked #7 on the
      <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200">
       <i>
        Billboard
       </i>
       200
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It included "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Kids_(Ghostface_Killah_single)" title="Apollo Kids (Ghostface Killah single)">
       Apollo Kids
      </a>
      ", a popular single which featured
      <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">
       Raekwon
      </a>
      and had a sample of "Cool Breeze" by
      <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Burke" title="Solomon Burke">
       Solomon Burke
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherchez_LaGhost" title="Cherchez LaGhost">
       Cherchez LaGhost
      </a>
      ", another single off the album, became a minor club hit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Supreme Clientele
      </i>
      would be a turning point in RZA's influence on his sound, as only six songs are produced by the
      <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">
       RZA
      </a>
      , compared to
      <i>
       Ironman
      </i>
      , its predecessor where every song but one is produced by him. Though he contributed fewer beats to the project, RZA personally oversaw the mixing and production of the album as a whole, contributing to
      <i>
       Supreme Clientele'
      </i>
      s unified sound.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B" title="R&amp;B">
       R&amp;B

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bulletproof_Wallets" title="Bulletproof Wallets">

      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Thomas_(singer)" title="Carl Thomas (singer)">
       Carl Thomas
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Raekwon" title="Raekwon">
       Raekwon
      </a>
      . He had another minor club hit with "Flowers", which featured guest vocals from fellow Wu-Tang members
      <a href="/wiki/Method_Man" title="Method Man">
       Method Man
      </a>
      and Raekwon, and a popular single "
      <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Showers" title="Ghost Showers">
       Ghost Showers
      </a>
      " which featured Madame Majestic, who also sung on the popular Wu-Tang track "
      <a href="/wiki/Gravel_Pit" title="Gravel Pit">
       Gravel Pit
      </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">

      <a href="/wiki/Jadakiss" title="Jadakiss">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">
       Controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghostface_Killah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In July 2011, Coles was sued by Jack Urbont for copyright violation, stemming from the improper use of Urbont's 1960's "Iron Man" theme song. Urbont has also taken issue with Coles' appropriation of the Iron Man brand name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In April 2015 Coles was granted a summary judgement on the lawsuit stating that
      <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Entertainment" title="Marvel Entertainment">
       Marvel Entertainment
      </a>
      , owners of the Iron Man character and brand, owned the "Iron Man" theme song not Urbont.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2011, Coles filed a lawsuit against Universal Music for unpaid royalties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aliases">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      Ghostface Killah, like most members of the Wu-Tang clan, rapped under several personae, each with their own name, mythology and influences. Some recurring aliases:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Ghostface Killah
       </b>
       (occasionally spelled
       <b>
        Ghostface Killer
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        Ghost Face Killah
       </b>
       , or abbreviated
       <b>
        GFK
       </b>
       )
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <b>
          Ghostface
         </b>
         (shortened stage name during
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/The_Pretty_Toney_Album" title="The Pretty Toney Album">
           The Pretty Toney Album
          </a>
         </i>
         era)
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
          <a href="#cite_note-45">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           45
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
          <a href="#cite_note-46">
           <span>
            [
           </span>
           46
           <span>
            ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </sup>
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          Ghostdini
         </b>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Ironman
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        Tony Starks
       </b>
       , or just
       <b>
        Starks
       </b>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Ghostface has frequently assumed the names of both Ironman and Tony Starks, a reference to the
       <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">
        Marvel Comics
       </a>
       character
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">
        Iron Man
       </a>
       and his true identity, billionaire industrialist
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">
        Tony Stark
       </a>
       (note the variant spellings). He released a 1996 album titled
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ironman_(Ghostface_Killah_album)" title="Ironman (Ghostface Killah album)">
         Ironman
        </a>
       </i>
       . His song, "Slept On Tony With Dirt," appears in the
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Man_(2008_film)" title="Iron Man (2008 film)">
        2008 film
       </a>
       , and he even appears in a deleted scene on the DVD.
