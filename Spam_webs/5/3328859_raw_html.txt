     <p>
      In 19th-century
      <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">
       psychiatry
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       monomania
      </b>
      (from Greek
      <i>
       monos
      </i>
      , one, and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mania" title="Mania">
        mania
       </a>
      </i>
      , meaning "madness" or "frenzy") was a form of partial insanity conceived as single pathological preoccupation in an otherwise sound mind.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :155
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eigen_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eigen-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :26
      </sup>
      Partial insanity, variations of which enjoyed a long pre-history in jurisprudence, was in contrast to the traditional notion of total insanity, exemplified in the diagnosis of mania, as a global condition affecting all aspects of understanding and which reflected the position that the mind or soul was an indivisible entity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eigen_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eigen-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :256, 31, 39
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BerriosSymptoms_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BerriosSymptoms-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :243
      </sup>
      Coined by the French
      <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrist" title="Psychiatrist">
       psychiatrist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-%C3%89tienne_Dominique_Esquirol" title="Jean-tienne Dominique Esquirol">
       Jean-tienne Dominique Esquirol
      </a>
      (17721840) around 1810,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :153
      </sup>
      monomania was a new disease-concept characterised by the presence of an expansive
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Id%C3%A9e_fixe_(psychology)" title="Ide fixe (psychology)">
        ide fixe
       </a>
      </i>
      in which the mind was diseased and deranged in some facets but otherwise normal in others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :157
      </sup>
      Esquirol and his circle delineated three broad categories of monomania coherent with the traditional tripartite classification of the mind into intellectual, emotional and volitional faculties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valverde_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Valverde-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :46
      </sup>
      Emotional monomania is that in which the patient is obsessed with only one emotion or several related to it; intellectual monomania is that which is related to only one kind of delirious idea or ideas. Although monomania was retained as one of seven recognized categories of mental illness in the 1880 US census,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disorders0_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-disorders0-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      its importance as a psychiatric diagnostic category was in decline from the mid-19th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berrios_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berrios-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_monomania">
       Types of monomania
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Monomania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of monomania">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Monomania may refer to:
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Clerambault%27s_syndrome" title="De Clerambault's syndrome">
        de Clerambault's syndrome
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Erotomania" title="Erotomania">
        erotomania
       </a>
       ): Delusion that a particular man or woman is in love with the patient. This can occur without reinforcement or even acquaintanceship with the love object.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Id%C3%A9e_fixe_(psychology)" title="Ide fixe (psychology)">
        Ide fixe
       </a>
       : Domination by an overvalued idea, for example, "staying thin" in
       <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">
        anorexia nervosa
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Kleptomania" title="Kleptomania">
        Kleptomania
       </a>
       : Irresistible urge to steal
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Pyromania" title="Pyromania">
        Pyromania
       </a>
       : Impulse to deliberately start fires
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lypemania" title="Lypemania">
        Lypemania
       </a>
       : Early elaboration later to become modern concept of
       <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">
        depression
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Monomania_in_popular_culture">
       Monomania in popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Monomania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Monomania in popular culture">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature_and_film">
       In literature and film
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Monomania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In literature and film">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honor de Balzac">
       Honor de Balzac
      </a>
      describes monomania in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A9nie_Grandet" title="Eugnie Grandet">
        Eugnie Grandet
       </a>
      </i>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balzac_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Balzac-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       As if to illustrate an observation which applies equally to misers, ambitious men, and others whose lives are controlled by any dominant idea, his affections had fastened upon one special symbol of his passion. The sight of gold, the possession of gold, had become a monomania.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Additionally, in Balzac's novel
      <i>
       Lucien De Rubempre
      </i>
      , the title character is referred to as in a
      <a class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=0mJEAAAAIAAJ&amp;pg=PA362" rel="nofollow">
       hallucinatory state
      </a>
      similar to that of a monomaniac.
     </p>
     <p>
      Monomaniacal fear is explored in great depth in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._E._Braddon" title="M. E. Braddon">
       M. E. Braddon
      </a>
      's novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Lady_Audley%27s_Secret" title="Lady Audley's Secret">
        Lady Audley's Secret
       </a>
      </i>
      , through the protagonist Robert Audley, whom the guilty woman accuses of monomania in his relentless attempt to prove her guilt. She describes monomania thus:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braddon_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Braddon-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       What is one of the strangest diagnostics of madnesswhat is the first appalling sign of mental aberration? The mind becomes stationary; the brain stagnates; the even current of reflection is interrupted; the thinking power of the brain resolves itself into a monotone. As the waters of a tideless pool putrefy by reason of their stagnation, the mind becomes turbid and corrupt through lack of action; and the perpetual reflection upon one subject resolves itself into monomania.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Bront">
       Emily Bront
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights" title="Wuthering Heights">
        Wuthering Heights
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Heathcliff_(Wuthering_Heights)" title="Heathcliff (Wuthering Heights)">
       Heathcliff
      </a>
      is described as a monomaniac, obsessing over his reunion with Cathy in the final chapters of the novel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tytler_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tytler-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Crime_and_Punishment" title="Crime and Punishment">
        Crime and Punishment
       </a>
      </i>
      , by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoevsky">
       Fyodor Dostoevsky
      </a>
      , the main character,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raskolnikov" title="Raskolnikov">
       Raskolnikov
      </a>
      , is said to be a monomaniac on numerous occasions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dostoyevsky_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dostoyevsky-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is said that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flaubert" title="Flaubert">
       Flaubert
      </a>
      's hatred of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourgeois" title="Bourgeois">
       bourgeois
      </a>
      and their btise (willful idiocy), that began in his childhood, developed into a kind of monomania.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chisholm-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a 1993
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Outside_(magazine)" title="Outside (magazine)">
        Outside
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine article about
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_McCandless" title="Christopher McCandless">
       Christopher McCandless
      </a>
      that he later expanded into the best-selling book,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Into_the_Wild_(book)" title="Into the Wild (book)">
        Into the Wild
       </a>
      </i>
      (which was also made into a
      <a href="/wiki/Into_the_Wild_(film)" title="Into the Wild (film)">
       film
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Jon_Krakauer" title="Jon Krakauer">
       Jon Krakauer
      </a>
      summarizes the portrait of Christopher painted by friends, family, and schoolmates thusly: "McCandless could be generous and caring to a fault, but he had a darker side as well, characterized by monomania, impatience, and unwavering self-absorption, qualities that seemed to intensify throughout his college years."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Moby-Dick" title="Moby-Dick">
        Moby-Dick
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">
       Herman Melville
      </a>
      (1851), Captain Ahab is a monomaniac, as shown by his quest to kill Moby Dick. One particular situation where he is shown as a monomaniac is in the crew's first encounter with the whale, stating:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melville_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Melville-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       But, as in his narrow-flowing monomania, not one jot of Ahab's broad madness had been left behind; so in that broad madness, not one jot of his great natural intellect had perished. ... so that far from having lost his strength, Ahab, to that one end, did now possess a thousand-fold more potency than ever he had sanely brought to bear upon any reasonable object.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In the original French text of Jules Vernes'
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Around_the_World_in_80_Days" title="Around the World in 80 Days">
        Around the World in 80 Days
       </a>
      </i>
      , the main character, Phileas Fogg is viewed by his servant Passepartout as a "monomane" for his fixation with winning his bet to complete his tour of the world in the prescribed 80 days.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 19th-century writer
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">
       Edgar Allan Poe
      </a>
      would often write tales in which the narrator and protagonist would suffer some form of monomania, becoming excessively fixated on an idea, an urge, an object, or a person, often to the point of mental and/or physical destruction. Poe uses the theme of monomania in:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Berenice_(short_story)" title="Berenice (short story)">
        Berenice
       </a>
       " (about a madman who gets obsessed with the teeth of his cousin and fiancee Berenice)
      </li>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Cat_(short_story)" title="The Black Cat (short story)">
        The Black Cat
       </a>
       " (a man fears his cat and kills it, adopts another cat, kills his wife, and is then punished by the cat)
      </li>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Fall_of_the_House_of_Usher" title="The Fall of the House of Usher">
        The Fall of the House of Usher
       </a>
       " (the main character Usher is obsessed with the fear of death)
      </li>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Masque_of_the_Red_Death" title="The Masque of the Red Death">
        The Masque of the Red Death
       </a>
       " (a prince fears a terrible disease but finally gets ill from the red death and dies)
      </li>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Oval_Portrait" title="The Oval Portrait">
        The Oval Portrait
       </a>
       " (about a painter who is obsessed with painting his wife)
      </li>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tell-Tale_Heart" title="The Tell-Tale Heart">
        The Tell-Tale Heart
       </a>
       " (a madman is obsessed with an elderly man's "vulture eye")
      </li>
      <li>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Man_of_the_Crowd" title="The Man of the Crowd">
        The Man of the Crowd
       </a>
       " (the narrator follows an old man wherever he goes)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.G._Wells" title="H.G. Wells">
       H.G. Wells
      </a>
      '
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Time_Machine" title="The Time Machine">
        The Time Machine
       </a>
      </i>
      , the time traveller states that "To sit among all those unknown things before a puzzle like that is hopeless. That way lies monomania."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wells-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Tomb_(short_story)" title="The Tomb (short story)">
       The Tomb
      </a>
      ", a 1917 short story by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.P._Lovecraft" title="H.P. Lovecraft">
       H.P. Lovecraft
      </a>
      , the pledge by the character of Jervas Dudley to break into an ancient family crypt is said by a physician to have been "the beginning of a pitiful monomania."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovecraft_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lovecraft-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Last_of_Ch%C3%A9ri" title="The Last of Chri">
        The Last of Chri
       </a>
      </i>
      , by
      <a href="/wiki/Colette" title="Colette">
       Colette
      </a>
      , Pal considers Chri to be a monomaniac.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cherie_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cherie-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_West_Wing" title="The West Wing">
        The West Wing
       </a>
      </i>
      , incoming White House Chief of Staff Joshua Lyman is described as being monomaniacal in his dedication to public service, as displayed by singularly focusing on his political work at the expense of his personal life and relationships.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Six_Napoleons" title="The Six Napoleons">
       The Six Napoleons
      </a>
      ", by Arthur Conan Doyle, where the criminal was thought to be suffering from monomania because he was repeatedly smashing the busts of Napoleon.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Count_of_Monte_Cristo" title="The Count of Monte Cristo">
        The Count of Monte Cristo
       </a>
      </i>
      , by French author
      <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">
       Alexandre Dumas
      </a>
      , Abbe Faria is believed to suffer from a monomania regarding the treasure he always spoke of to the persons of
      <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27If" title="Chteau d'If">
       Chteau d'If
      </a>
      , the political prison in which he was detained.
     </p>
