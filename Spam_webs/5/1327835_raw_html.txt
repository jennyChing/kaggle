       <h1>
        An environmentally friendly battery made from wood
       </h1>
       <h5 class="data">
        July 24, 2013
       </h5>
      </header>
      <section class="content-holder content-table content-story">
       <!--begin:content-->
       <section class="news-content">
        <!--begin:article-block-->
        <section class="content article-block">
         <article>
          <div class="first-block">
           <p>
            Taking inspiration from trees, scientists have developed a battery made from a sliver of wood coated with tin that shows promise for becoming a tiny, long-lasting, efficient and environmentally friendly energy source. Their report on the device1,000 times thinner than a sheet of paperappears in the journal
            <i>
             Nano Letters
            </i>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
          <!-- 1st block -->
          <div class="article-banner first-banner gtext-ad">
           <script language="JavaScript">
            &lt;!-- 
            var google_adnum = 0;
            var ad_channel_cmn = '+0559369967', google_hints="";
            
            OA_show(61);
        --&gt;
           </script>
          </div>
          <p>
           Liangbing Hu, Teng Li and colleagues point out that today's batteries often use stiff, non-
           <a class="textTag" href="http://phys.org/tags/flexible+substrates/" rel="tag">
            flexible substrates
           </a>
           , which are too rigid to release the stress that occurs as ions flow through the battery. They knew that wood fibers from trees are supple and naturally designed to hold mineral-rich water, similar to the electrolyte in batteries. They decided to explore use of wood as the base of an experimental sodium-ion battery. Using sodium rather than lithium would make the device environmentally friendly.
          </p>
          <p>
           Lead author Hongli Zhu and other team members describe lab experiments in which the device performed successfully through 400 charge-discharge cycles, putting it among the longest-lasting of all sodium-ion nanobatteries. Batteries using the new technology would be best suited for large-scale
           <a class="textTag" href="http://phys.org/tags/energy+storage/" rel="tag">
            energy storage
           </a>
           applications, such as wind farms or solar energy installations, the report indicates.
          </p>
