      <div class="post-2392 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-christianity tag-trinity entry">
       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The Long, Strange Story of the Trinity
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2013-06-10T12:00:34+00:00">
         June 10, 2013
        </span>
        by
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/crossexamined/author/bseidensticker/" rel="author" title="Bob Seidensticker">
           Bob Seidensticker
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="post-comments">
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/crossexamined/2013/06/the-long-strange-story-of-the-trinity/#respond">
          <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="2392 http://www.patheos.com/blogs/crossexamined/?p=2392">
           Leave a Comment
          </span>
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-2393" height="200" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/crossexamined/files/2013/06/Borromean-Rings.jpg" title="Borromean Rings, interlocked only when all three are present" width="200"/>
         The Christian doctrine of the Trinity claims one God in three persons. The
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.newadvent.org/cathen/15047a.htm" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Catholic Encyclopedia
         </a>
         defines it: In the unity of the Godhead there are Three Persons, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, these Three Persons being truly distinct one from another.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unity but also distinct? Three but also one? That makes no sense, so lets go to the source and read about it in the Bible.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          And the Bible says 
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Though the Trinity is one of the most fundamental doctrines of Christianity, the Bible says nothing about it directly. Did Paul and the apostles define God in a trinitarian fashion? If the Trinity is essential to a proper understanding of Christianity as the modern church claims, the ancients silence on the matter suggests that it is a later invention.
        </p>
        <p>
         Thats not to say that one cant use the Bible to form arguments in favor of
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinity#History" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          various relationships
         </a>
         between God, Jesus, and the Holy Spirit. Several interpretations competed in the early centuries of the church.
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Was Jesus merely a good man, adopted by God (Adoptionism)?
         </li>
         <li>
          Are Father, Son, and Holy Spirit just labels for the different roles of one being (Sabellianism or Modalism)?
         </li>
         <li>
          Was Jesus created by God and subordinate to him (Arianism)?
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         These are all plausible interpretations, justifiable with Bible passages, but they are heresies today. It took about two centuries for the doctrine of the Trinity to enter the debate (
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinity#History" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          through Tertullian
         </a>
         ), and it took almost two more centuries of haggling for the doctrine to mature into its present form and sweep away its competitors at the First Council of Constantinople (381).
        </p>
        <p>
         While still a cardinal, the man who would become Pope Benedict XVI was asked if he was bothered by many Catholics ignoring papal dictates.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.nybooks.com/blogs/nyrblog/2013/mar/10/does-pope-matter/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          He said that he was not
         </a>
         , because truth is not determined by a majority vote. But a majority vote is exactly how doctrines like the Trinity came into being.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Comma Johanneum
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         You know how I said that the Bible says nothing directly about the Trinity? For completeness, we should address this:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          For there are three that testify
          <em>
           in heaven: the Father, the Word and the Holy Spirit, and these three are one.
          </em>
          (1 John 5:7)
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         The part in italics is called the
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma_Johanneum" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Comma Johanneum
         </a>
         (a comma is a short clause). The oldest and most reliable manuscripts do not show the Comma.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma_Johanneum#Manuscript_evidence" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          It appears first
         </a>
         in a few 7th-century Latin manuscripts and only centuries later in Greek manuscripts. Unlike much of the rest of the New Testament, it doesnt appear in the letters of early church fathers, many of whom wouldve delighted in supporting their position with such a quote.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is agreed by scholars to be an addition to the original.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          What
          <em>
           is
          </em>
          the Trinity?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Lots of analogies have been proposed for the Trinity. Maybe its like water, which has the three states of solid, liquid, and gas. Or like a person who can be spouse, parent, and employer. But this is modalismGod acts in different modes at different times.
        </p>
        <p>
         Okay, then maybe its like an egg, which has shell, white, and yolk. Or like time, which has past, present, and future. Or like the Borromean rings in the picture above that only compose a linked whole when all three rings are present. But this is Partialism, the claim that the three persons of God are three separate parts.
        </p>
        <p>
         Given the clear history of conflict on this question and the many discarded explanations, youd think that heretical analogies wouldnt be offered.
        </p>
        <p>
         Most Christians simply say that its a mystery and admit that we cant understand it. Contrast that with the monotheism celebrated by Islam. The shahadah, the basic creed of Islam, says, There are no deities but Allah, and Mohammed is his prophetsimple and unambiguous.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          A few questions raised by the Trinity doctrine
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Instead of the convoluted and unintelligible Trinity, why not simply embrace the polytheism? My guess is that first-century Christians so valued Jewish monotheism that this tenet couldnt be dropped. As the stature of Jesus increased over time, from a good man adopted as messiah by God (as told in Mark) to a being who was there at the beginning (John 1:1), they were stuck with fitting the square peg of the divinity of Jesus into the round hole of monotheism.
        </p>
        <p>
         Why not then have a duality, Yahweh + Jesus? The problem is that two is the number for male and female, which was not the symbolism they were going for. Perhaps the Holy Spirit, initially just a bit player or merely a synonym for God, was elevated into the Trinity. And even this is flexible. While the idea of Mary as
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-Redemptrix" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Co-redemptrix
         </a>
         is not Catholic doctrine, it has threatened to become so at various periods in the churchs history.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          And now let us close 
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The typical Christian response to a contradiction is to find a way to make both claims true. This is never clearer than with the Trinity. The Bible says that there is one god, but it also says that Jesus existed since the beginning of time. So they must
         <em>
          both
         </em>
         be true! But what first-century Christian would rationalize this with the doctrine of the Trinity?
        </p>
        <p>
         Or, take this from the other direction. Explain the Trinity to first-century Christians and ask if they buy it. If you imagine that they do, you have a new problem: why the vitally important doctrine of the Trinity wasnt explained in the New Testament.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Trinity is a Christian
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_mysteries#Christian_mysteries" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          mystery
         </a>
         something that cant be explained by reason alone. A supernatural explanation is necessary. (This raises the question: If it doesnt make sense, why accept it? But lets set that aside.) Apologists often admit that they will just have to ask God about it when they get to heaven.
        </p>
        <p>
         That humility is laudable, but how about some of that in other areas? If you dont trust yourself to make sense of the Trinity, why imagine that you correctly understand Gods position on polygamy, slavery, and genocide when the Old Testament gives clear support for them? Why imagine that your evaluation of abortion and gay marriage is correct when the Bible doesnt address these topics directly?
        </p>
        <p>
         If only the Trinity were a frequent reminder for Christians to be humble in their claims, it would be valuable for everyone.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          See also:
         </em>
         
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/crossexamined/2013/06/god-has-many-names-but-do-we-need-one-more/" title="God Has Many Names, But Do We Need One More?">
          God Has Many Names, But Do We Need One More?
         </a>
         
        </p>
        <p align="right">
         <em>
          It is too late in the day for men of sincerity
         </em>
         <br/>
         <em>
          to pretend they believe in the Platonic mysticism
         </em>
         <br/>
         <em>
          that three are one and one is three,
         </em>
         <br/>
         <em>
          and yet, that the one is not three, and the three are not one.
         </em>
         <br/>
          Thomas Jefferson (letter to John Adams, 1813)
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Photo credit:
         </em>

          Johansson
         </a>
        </p>
