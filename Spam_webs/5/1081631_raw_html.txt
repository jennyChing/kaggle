            <hgroup>
             <h1>
              A Major Shift in Multiple Myeloma Diagnosis
             </h1>
            </hgroup>
           </header>
           <div class="byline">
            By Carole Alison Chrvala, PhD
            <br/>
            Reviewed by Alan S. Weinstein, MD, FACP, Senior Physician Advisor, Virtua Fox Chase Cancer Program, Marlton, NJ
           </div>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <aside class="sidebar">
            <div class="media infographic">
             <img alt="image" src="http://clf1.medpagetoday.com/assets/images/cuso/cs_velcade_doctor_studying_300px_225px.jpg"/>
            </div>
            <section class="actionpoints">
             <header>
              <h2>
               Take Note
              </h2>
             </header>
             <div class="content">
              <ul>
               <li>
                The updated criteria for the diagnosis of multiple myeloma include any one of 3 new validated markers which can substitute for the current requirement for attributable features of hypercalcemia, renal failure, anemia, and bone lesions
               </li>
               <li>
                Implementation of these new diagnostic criteria has the potential for initiation of earlier treatment and improved prognosis
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
            </section>
           </aside>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
            </strong>
            In October 2014, the International Myeloma Working Group (IMWG) released updated criteria for the diagnosis of multiple myeloma that elevates the diagnosis beyond clinicopathological findings alone. These new criteria have wide-reaching implications for the clinical management of patients with multiple myeloma as well as individuals with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS) and smoldering myeloma.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            Prior to this update, the diagnosis of multiple myeloma required clinical evidence of serious end-organ damage including osteolytic bone lesions and renal failure.
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
            These criteria tended to preclude initiation of early treatment when the disease was at an early stage--and most susceptible to therapeutic interventions that could prevent serious organ damage.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            Multiple myeloma is a cytogenetically heterogeneous clonal plasma cell proliferative disorder, with most cases arising from asymptomatic MGUS. Approximately 80% of multiple myeloma develops as non-immunoglobulin MGUS and 20% from light chain immunoglobulin MGUS.
           </p>
           <p>
            Smoldering myeloma is an intermediate-stage disease between MGUS and multiple myeloma, with the annual risk of progression to multiple myeloma estimated at 10%.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            For the past decade, the diagnosis of MGUS and smoldering myeloma has been based on the absence of classic symptoms of end-organ or tissue impairment including hyperCalcemia, Renal insufficiency, Anemia, and osteolytic Bone lesions (CRAB).
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
            The identification of patients who transition from MGUS to myeloma without these features has been a challenge for physicians because organ or tissue damage and functional impairment were not clearly defined. The result was often a delay in the initiation of treatment. Patients were managed with close follow-up until evidence of end-organ damage was clearly established.
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
            Such damage can be serious and irreversible for many patients.
           </p>
           <p>
            The IMWG felt that a disease definition requiring severe end-organ damage was unreasonable for many patients, especially with recent treatment advances for multiple myeloma that are more effective and have more favorable safety profiles compared with earlier interventions that relied on alkylating agents and corticosteroids.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            The updated diagnostic criteria for multiple myeloma add 3 markers as myeloma-defining events to the classic CRAB features including:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Clonal bone marrow percentage 60%
            </li>
            <li>
             Involved/uninvolved serum free light chain ratio 100
            </li>
            <li>
             &gt;1 focal lesion on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) that is 5 mm or greater in size
             <sup>
              1
             </sup>
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Two or more independent studies have confirmed that each of these 3 markers was associated with 80% risk of progression to the development of myeloma-related organ damage within 2 years. The presence of at least one of these 3 markers is considered sufficient to establish a diagnosis of multiple myeloma, regardless of the presence or absence of CRAB features.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            The biggest changes can be described as a philosophical/conceptual change about when to start treatment for myeloma and specific changes in the criteria. For a long time we have been operating under the assumption that there have to be symptoms or signs indicating end-organ damage before we start treatment. This was understandable, as the vast majority of patients with MGUS would not need therapy during their lifetime--and treatments had definite side effects, said Shaji K. Kumar, MD, Professor of Medicine, Division of Hematology and Blood and Marrow Transplantation at the Mayo Clinic in Minnesota.
           </p>
           <p>
            However, we now have safer drugs and also biomarkers and sensitive tests to discern very early evidence of end-organ damage. With these tools, we are able to predict the onset of the end-organ damage. As a result, now we are ready to treat patients based on their probability of developing end-organ damage rather than actual damage, which sometimes can be permanent.
           </p>
           <p>
            This represents a paradigm shift in our thinking, continued Dr. Kumar.
           </p>
           <p>
            According to Jo Caers, MD, PhD, Department of Hematology, CHU University of Lige, Lige, Belgium, Physicians still need to evaluate the need for treatment and not follow this definition too dogmatically. For some patients, you can still wait before starting a treatment. However, these current definitions allow an early intervention if you are convinced that this can help your patient.
           </p>
           <p>
            The IMWG also highlighted promising biomarkers that may further refine the diagnostic process pending further research. These include:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             High levels of circulating plasma cells
            </li>
            <li>
             Abnormal plasma cell immunophenotype 95% and immunoparesis (depressed levels of normal immunoglobulins)
            </li>
            <li>
             Progression of smoldering myeloma assessed by 10% increases in serum monoclonal protein on each of 2 successive evaluations obtained within a 6-month interval
            </li>
            <li>
             Cytogenetic subtypes t(4;14), 1q amp, or del 17p
            </li>
            <li>
             A high rate of bone marrow plasma cell proliferation
            </li>
            <li>
             Unexplained decreases 25% in creatinine clearance plus elevations in urinary monoclonal protein or serum free light chain concentrations
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            The IMWG noted that evidence from more than 2 independent studies that confirms the association of any of these biomarkers with at least an 80% risk of progression from smoldering myeloma to multiple myeloma within 2 years will support their future inclusion as diagnostic criteria for multiple myeloma.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            Note that smoldering myeloma is now defined by both serum protein &gt;30 g/L or urinary monoclonal protein &gt;500 mg/24 hours and/or clonal bone marrow plasma cells 10-60% AND the absence of myeloma defining events or amyloidosis.
           </p>
           <p>
            The current version of the updated diagnostic criteria for multiple myeloma and the potential for additional biomarkers holds the promise of earlier treatment and improved prognosis in patients with multiple myeloma and those at high risk for myeloma.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            Jens Hillengass, MD, University of Heidelberg, Faculty of Medicine Heidelberg and Clinic Heidelberg commented optimistically that, Patients with an earlier stage of the disease can be treated. Hopefully, this will prevent them from developing end-organ damage or at least postpone complications and will lead to an improved survival.
           </p>

           </div>
