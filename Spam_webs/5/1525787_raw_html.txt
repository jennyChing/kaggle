        <h1>
         Three Poems from Yannis Ritsos
         <em>
          Diaries of Exile
         </em>
        </h1>
        <div class="details">
         <p>
          by
          <a href="http://www.sampsoniaway.org/author/olivia-rose-mancing/" title="Posts by Olivia Rose Mancing">
           Olivia Rose Mancing
          </a>
          / July 24, 2014 /
          <a href="http://www.sampsoniaway.org/literary-voices/poetry/2014/07/24/three-poems-from-yannis-ritsos-diaries-of-exile/#respond" title="Comment on Three Poems from Yannis Ritsos Diaries of Exile">

        <p>
         The celebrated Greek poet
         <a href="http://www.poetryfoundation.org/bio/yannis-ritsos">
          Yannis Ritsos
         </a>
         was both lauded and hated for his writing during his lifetime. Though he was nominated for the Nobel Prize twice and won the Lenin Peace Prize, his work was burned and banned several times between 1936 and 1970. It also put him in exile and, later, under house arrest.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Diaries of Exile
         </em>
         , which was released in English this year, is a series of three diaries-in-poetry that Ritsos wrote between 1948 and 1950 while a political prisoner of the Greek Civil War, first on the island of Limnos and then at the infamous camp on the island of
         <a href="http://www.thecommononline.org/dispatches/propaganda-greek-island">
          Makronisos
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         This volume has been translated by
         <a href="http://www.pen.org/interview/pen-ten-karen-emmerich">
          Karen Emmerich
         </a>
         and Edmund Keeley and is
         <a href="http://www.pen.org/literature/2014-pen-award-poetry-translation">
          short-listed
         </a>
         for the 2014 PEN Award for Poetry in Translation.
        </p>
        <p>
         Today
         <em>
          Sampsonia Way
         </em>
         presents three poems from
         <em>
          Diaries of Exile
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          November 6
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Evening. The bell for the evening meal.
         <br/>
         Shouts from the boys playing soccer.
         <br/>
         Was it yesterday?-I dont remember;-a stunning sunset
         <br/>
         so violet, so gold, so rosy.
         <br/>
         We stood there. We watched. We talked
         <br/>
         alone, alone, tossing our voices into the wind
         <br/>
         so as to tie things together, to unbind our hearts.
        </p>
        <p>
         A letter arrived in the yard:
         <br/>
         Panousiss son was killed.
         <br/>
         Our talk nestled against the walls.
         <br/>
         The sunset suddenly nothing.
        </p>
        <p>
         The night had no hours. The knot loosened.
         <br/>
         Panousiss aluminum plate grew cold on the table.
         <br/>
         We lay down. We covered ourselves. We loved one another
         <br/>
         around that untouched plate that no longer steamed.
        </p>
        <p>
         Around midnight the black cat came in through the window
         <br/>
         and ate some of Panousiss food.
         <br/>
         Then the moon came in
         <br/>
         and hung motionless over the plate.
         <br/>
         Panousiss arm on the blanket
         <br/>
         was a severed plane tree.
        </p>
        <p>
         Well then-must we really be so sad
         <br/>
         in order to love one another?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          January 25
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         For a moment we took refuge
         <br/>
         against the latrine wall.
         <br/>
         The wind was cutting.
         <br/>
         An old man stared at a cloud.
         <br/>
         I looked at him smiling
         <br/>
         in the light of that cloud-so peaceful,
         <br/>
         so far removed from desire and pain-
         <br/>
         I was jealous.
        </p>
        <p>
         Old people agree with the clouds.
         <br/>
         And its taking us a long time to get old.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          May 11
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         After the rain the buildings and the stones
         <br/>
         change colors.
        </p>
        <p>
         Two old men sit on the bench. They dont talk.
         <br/>
         So much shouting and so much silence remains.
         <br/>
         The newspapers age in an hour.
        </p>
        <p>
         Stressed, unstressed, stressed, unstressed
         <br/>
         the monotony of change-stressed;
         <br/>
         unstressed, stressed, strophe, antistrophe
         <br/>
         and neither rage nor sorrow.
        </p>
        <p>
         Evening lights out;
         <br/>
         just as heavy for the one who struck
         <br/>
         as for the one he struck.
        </p>
        <p>
         The men sit on the stones
         <br/>
         pare their nails.
         <br/>
         The others died.
         <br/>
         We forgot them.
        </p>

           <li class="heading">
            About the Translators
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Edmund Leroy Keeley
            </strong>
            is an author, translator, educator, critic, and Professor Emeritus of English at Princeton University. He was born in Damascus, Syria, in 1928 and lived in Greece from ages 8 to 11. He is a prize-winning novelist and a noted expert on Greek poets C. P. Cavafy, George Seferis, Odysseus Elytis and Yannis Ritsos, and on post-Second World War Greek history.
            <a href="https://www.pen.org/edmund-keeley?tmember=1">
             Read more here.
            </a>
           </li>
           <li>
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Karen Emmerich
            </strong>
            is an assistant professor of comparative literature at the University of Oregon, and a
            <a href="http://wordswithoutborders.org/contributor/karen-emmerich">
             translator
            </a>
            of Modern Greek poetry and prose. Her translations include
            <em>
             Rien ne va plus
            </em>
            by Margarita Karapanou,
            <em>
             Landscape with Dog and Other Stories
            </em>
            by Ersi Sotiropoulos, and
            <em>
             Poems
            </em>
            (19451971) by Miltos Sachtouris. She is the recipient of translation grants and awards from the NEA, PEN, and the Modern Greek Studies Association.
            <a href="http://complit.uoregon.edu/faculty/emmerich.html">
             Read more here.
            </a>
           </li>
