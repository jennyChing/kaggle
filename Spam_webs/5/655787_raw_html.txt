      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Introverts Lay 'Corrective' Landmines For Extrovert Peers, Says Study
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">
        Comment Now
       </a>
       <div class="comments_follow followable_block">
        <div class="followable_block initializing" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Introverts Lay 'Corrective' Landmines For Extrovert Peers, Says Study&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/1464-9818" data-target-name="" data-target-type="article" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow" data-type-advoice="false">
         <div class="follow_unireg">
          <a class="follow_unireg_link" href="javascript://follow">

         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
     </hgroup>
     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         Susan Cains 2013 book,
         <a href="http://www.thepowerofintroverts.com/about-the-book/">
          Quiet: The Power of Introverts in a World That Cant Stop Talking
         </a>
         , came marching onto the scenelike a cavalry of affirmation to the rescue of introverts everywhere, and the march has barely slowed since. The groundswell support for the books thesisthat were dramatically undervaluing the contributions of the stereotypical quiet personality typeresulted in a sort of cultish affinity for introversion.
        </p>
        <p>
         Cains points are generally well-taken, but as with all movement-making arguments, theyve also fueled a few too many shallow conclusions  namely, that introverts have a clearer shot at personal integrity than others. You know, like Honest Abe, one of Cains historical models of quiet virtue.
        </p>
        <p>
         The truth, however, is that theres nothing inherently virtuous about introversion, nothing predisposing this personality type (or any other) toward more ethical decision-making. Perhaps it is true, as Cain argues, that introverts are more sensitive than their extroverted peers, but its also true that sensitivity is a trait of two faces.
        </p>
        <p>
         A recent
         <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.5465/amj.2011.0214">
          study
         </a>
         challenged the virtue-of-introversion argument by showing that elevated personality sensitivity within organizations can manifest as bias against other personality types. Researchers from the
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/colleges/university-of-florida/">
          University of Florida
         </a>
         Warrington
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/colleges/university-of-connecticut/school-of-business/">
          School Of Business
         </a>
         wanted to know if this sort of bias would turn up in performance appraisals.
        </p>
        <p>
         In two experiments, the researchers tested how introverts and extroverts would appraise peers of the opposite personality type after having worked together on teams for the previous few months. The results showed that introverts were more likely to give negative performance appraisals to extroverts, particularly if the extroverted peer had an over-bearing personality style, even if the extroverts performance was comparable to other team members. Introverts were also six times less likely to recommend extroverted peers for performance bonuses.
        </p>
        <p>
         Extroverts, on the other hand, didnt appear to bias their reviews based on personality types. Their appraisals of introverted and extroverted peers were more or less consistent.
        </p>
        <p>
         So whats going on here? Of a few possible interpretations, the one the researchers think most likely is that the introverts used the performance appraisals as a corrective measure against peers they viewed as over-bearinganddisagreeablesorts of people.
        </p>
        <p>
         The researchers think theres a takeaway warning in these results for extroverts in organizations: Individuals high in extroversion and disagreeableness should be made aware that their trait-relevant behaviors may have a profoundly negative impact on how introverted individuals experience theirencounters and may lead to reduced performance evaluation or reward giving for collective accomplishments.
        </p>
        <p>
         Partly, these results may come down to how different personality types focus their attention in interpersonal situations. Introverts tend to feel the personalities of others more deeply (a trait Cain discusses in her book), while an extroverts attention is less prone to fixate on personality differences. What this study suggestsis that introverts are less liable to make an issue of personality differences in day-to-day situations, but are taking plenty of mental notes to unload in more intimate settings, like performance appraisals.
        </p>
        <p>
         One thing worth keeping in mind is that our notions of purely introverted and extroverted personality styles arent holding up well in recent research on the
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/daviddisalvo/2013/04/10/move-over-extroverts-here-come-the-ambiverts/">
          ambivert
         </a>
         phenomena. According to researchers like
         <a href="https://mgmt.wharton.upenn.edu/profile/1323/">
          Adam Grant
         </a>
         from the
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/colleges/university-of-pennsylvania/wharton-school/">
          Wharton School
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/business/">
          Business
         </a>
         , a high percentage of us fall between introversion and extroversion, describing us more accurately as ambiverts (more on that
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/daviddisalvo/2013/04/10/move-over-extroverts-here-come-the-ambiverts/">
          here
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <p>
         In any case, the metalesson from studies like this is that we should be careful about ascribing virtue based on personality styles. Whether were introverts, extroverts or ambiverts, theres always plenty of blame to go around.
        </p>
        <p>
         The study was published in the
         <em>
          <a href="http://amj.aom.org/content/early/2014/11/20/amj.2011.0214">
           Academy of Management Journal
          </a>
          .
         </em>
         H/T: the always excellent
         <a href="http://digest.bps.org.uk/2015/01/why-you-might-want-to-beware-introvert.html">
          BPS Research Digest
         </a>
         .
        </p>

          <p>
           Dear Dave, you are articles are interesting and detailed (unlike some blogs which as too short and sometimes copied and pasted). I appreciate your passion for personality psychology.
          </p>
          <p>
           I wonder if you ever heard of the Personality Assessment System developed by Dr. John Gittinger, former Chief Psychologist for the Central Intelligence Agency in the 1950-60s? I think that you would love this. Its not well known because it was once only used for research within government agencies. He adjusted IQ testing and WAIS subtests (Weschler Adult Intelligence Scale) to show a link between personality development and ones individual abilities. You cant fake this test or influence generic questions about yourself by your own perceptions because its a test of ABILITIES.
          </p>
          <p>
           Turns out that personality actually is (as we all know) more complex than merely introvert vs extrovert and ambivert. Gittinger discovered 4 very distinct yet interconnected dimensions to personality that manifest during 3 major stages of development throughout our lifetimes. Examples: Genetic or primitive ages 0-2, parental &amp; environmental influences known as basic adjustments from ages 2-12, and finally, he saw social or surface adjustments during the 13 to 28 age range.
          </p>
          <p>
           The PAS model shows internalizers &amp; externalizers as only 1 of 4 dimensions of personality and slightly different than the popularized introvert/extroverts which limits or generalizes personality traits and can be faked.
          </p>
          <p>
           - The Externalizer is perceptually dominant, environmentally sensitive, and more responsive to external than to internal cues more interested in doing than in thinking. His perception is relatively specific and concrete. His emotionality is directed outward, he is primarily environmentally dependent, and he seeks his major psychological satisfactions in relationships
          </p>
          <p>
           - The Internalizer on the other hand, is ideationally dominant, self-sufficient, and more responsive to internal than to external cues. The I individual will seem primarily passive, tending toward withdrawal, and is usually more inclined to thinking than to doing. He perceives in abstract rather than concrete terms. His emotionality is directed inward, he is primarily self-contained, and he seeks his major psychological satisfactions in the privacy of his own experiences.   PASF.org
          </p>
          <p>
           http://www.pasf.org/model1.htm
           <br/>
           http://www.pasf.org/summary.htm
           <br/>
           http://www.pasf.org/gitt.htm
          </p>

         <div class="comment_content">
          <p>
           One underlying challenge to all this discussion is the definition of introvert and extrovert. People are impressionistally (the impression they leave on an observer) introvert can come from a variety of actual underlying personality profiles. John Gitteringers Personality Assessment System (pasf.org or see the Wikipedia article), used in the CIA in the cold war years, included three levels of development of personality dimensions, a primitive (possibly genetic), a nearly permanent adjustment confirming or compensating the primitive by late teens and a surface confirming or modifying in young adulthood. Even a primitive Externalizer who is not compensated (and thus very externally oriented and dependent on relating) can impress as introvert if highly modified (especially if the social dimension is a certain way). This person is a very different introvert than an primitive Internalizer who has remained introvert as seen from the outside. There are many other interesting combinations which is one reason research tends to get so confused  one study group might be dominated by one kind of introverts and another a different one.
          </p>

         <div class="comment_content">
          <p>
           What studies like this, purporting to unveil prejudices, fail to take into account are (a) the prior experiences of the subjects with the characteristic being tested against, and (b) the externalities implied by that experience beyond the immediate task. Imagine the same experiment, but with the trait tested against being whether or not the person identified as a drug addict rather than an extravert.
          </p>
          <p>
           While it may not be fair to evaluate two equal performances of the same micro-task differently, if the experience of the introvert tells them that extraverts consistently bring negative practical factors to the larger process which are external to the immediate task (for example, the massive inefficiencies that result from their obsessive social grooming) the differential evals are perfectly rational and practical, in their end-goals. After all, this is why we perform drug tests and background checks on prospective employees: to see if the person in question is bringing problematic personality traits into the process. When the job isnt people-oriented (sales and service) extraversion can be a resource sink rather than an asset.
          </p>
          <p>
           Is it fair to judge a self-identified addicts performance differently? Perhaps not. Is it reasonable to do so, particularly if the addict doesnt see their addiction as a problem, but even as a virtue? Now, thats a different question.
