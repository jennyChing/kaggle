        <h1>
         5 Things You Didn't Know About Smuggling Drugs into Prison
        </h1>
       </header>
       <footer>
        <div class="meta">
         <span class="by">

       <section>
        <p>
         Growing up, I never once thought that I would ever intentionally commit a serious crime. I was a good kid with good morals. Life, though, will sometimes put you in a position that forces you to take crazy risks. My risk just happened to involve sneaking drugs into jail by tying them to my penis.
        </p>
        <p>
         No, I wasn't smuggling in recreational stuff that would let me start my own narcotics empire on the cell block. This was medication that my doctor said I needed, but that jail wouldn't allow me to have. And that's how I wound up going from a (mostly) law-abiding citizen to committing a felony overnight. By tying a bundle of pain medication to my dick.
        </p>
        <p>
         If this all sounds like a very stupid story, I'm glad I have so far accurately characterized the events. Here's how it happened:
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #5. Jail Has Stupid Rules
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/personalexperience/4/6/4/328464.jpg?v=1" height="203" width="610"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 610px; text-align: right;">
          angelo gilardelli/iStock/Getty Images
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Through a series of bad decisions and disastrous luck, I wound up getting sentenced to a
         <a href="http://www.journal-news.com/news/news/weekend-only-jail-inmates-are-on-the-decline/nP7qB/" target="c">
          weekends-only
         </a>
         jail term, which I'm guessing most of you didn't even know was a thing.
        </p>
        <p>
         Basically, the police found a baggie of marijuana in my car (which was parked in my driveway -- the cop just randomly walked up, as if he was bored and figured it looked like a weed car). Since it was my first offense, I was given one year of probation, drug and alcohol treatment classes, 40 hours of community service, and three full weekends in the slammer. So, yes, they sometimes do jail terms like detention in
         <i>
          The Breakfast Club
         </i>
         .
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/personalexperience/1/1/0/329110_v1.jpg" height="216" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          Universal Pictures
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Only in this case it's your cellmate who gives you the horns.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Later, I was riding my bike, enjoying my last hours of freedom and rethinking my life choices. That's the last thing I remember before I woke up in the middle of the street, covered in blood. I'd been hit by a car, my arm broken at a complete right angle. The courts actually gave me an extra week before starting my jail time in order to undergo the necessary surgery to fix my obliterated limb. The weekend after my surgery, I was in a cast and under lockdown. And herein lies the problem:
        </p>
        <p>
         I was on a regimen of about six Oxycontin a day as well as a muscle relaxer in order to manage the pain of recently having six screws, two bolts, and a metal plate shoved inside my arm. I thought I would be fine just bringing my pills and my prescriptions in with me to show that I needed them, and the guards or whoever would dole them out to me as needed during my stay. Imagine my delight when the officer informed me that I wouldn't be getting my medication until they heard from my physician -- who, like most doctors, was not in the office on weekends. I was so fucked that the officer actually said, "Looks like you're kinda fucked."
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/personalexperience/4/7/0/328470_v1.jpg" height="233" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          Thinkstock Images/Stockbyte/Getty Images
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "None of you have a future, and you will all die alone. Thus concludes today's motivational speech."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         See, when you go to jail, they can't just accept prescriptions and pills
         <a href="http://www.spokesman.com/stories/2013/jul/21/jails-policy-on-prescription-medication-can-leave/" target="c">
          that you bring in yourself
         </a>
         , because for all they know you forged the scripts and those pills are frozen cocaine coated with super acid. Checking with your doctor and ordering the meds themselves is standard procedure. This sounds fantastic on paper, but it isn't exactly a fast process -- just ask all the people
         <a href="http://www.denverpost.com/news/ci_24988770/family-questions-womans-death-kansas-jail" target="c">
          who have died in prison
         </a>
         waiting for their medication to be authorized. (You can't actually ask them, because they
         <i>
          died
         </i>
         .)
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/personalexperience/4/6/8/328468_v1.jpg" height="239" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          Stephen Mcsweeny/Hemera/Getty Images
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "'Waah, I'm lifeless and can't move or speak, waaah.' God, you corpses are such divas."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         The jail where I was staying threw in an additional hurdle requiring me to be physically present when they actually received my doctor's authorization, which was difficult because, as I mentioned, I was in prison only on the weekends, and my doctor was in the office only on weekdays. Sometimes jails have their own doctors who can look you over (who, wouldn't you know it, also don't work on the weekends), but if they decide you don't really need painkillers, you're pretty much stuck with frontier medicine, which is another way of saying "biting nearby objects and screaming into the callous night." Some facilities
         <a href="http://www.news-journal.com/news/local/state-inmates-not-guaranteed-own-prescription-medications/article_c7c246fb-f3b8-5f59-9ddb-c712ec6e57c8.html" target="c">
          refuse to dispense narcotics at all
         </a>
         , regardless of what any big-city doctors say.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/personalexperience/9/7/0/328970_v1.jpg" height="283" width="350"/>
         <font size="1" style="color: #909090; display:block; margin-top: -10px; height: 5px; width: 350px; text-align: right;">
          Hitoshi Nishimura/The Image Bank/Getty Images
         </font>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "Vicodin? Please -- a steady diet of
          <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/thesalt/2014/01/02/256605441/punishing-inmates-with-the-loaf-persists-in-the-u-s" target="c">
           prison loaf
          </a>
          is all your patient needs to bounce back."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         That whole first weekend was one of the most painful and exhausting experiences of my entire life.
