     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <b>
        baculum
       </b>
      </i>
      (also
      <b>
       penis bone
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       penile bone
      </b>
      or
      <i>
       <b>
        os penis
       </b>
      </i>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">
       bone
      </a>
      found in the
      <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">
       penis
      </a>
      of many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placental_mammal" title="Placental mammal">
       placental mammals
      </a>
      . It is absent in the
      <a href="/wiki/Human_penis" title="Human penis">
       human penis
      </a>
      , but present in
      <a href="/wiki/Penis#Primates" title="Penis">
       the penises of other primates
      </a>
      , such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">
       gorilla
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">
       chimpanzee
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-patterns-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The bone is located above the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male_urethra" title="Male urethra">
       male urethra
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PerrinWursig2009_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PerrinWursig2009-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and it aids
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">
       sexual reproduction
      </a>
      by maintaining sufficient stiffness during
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_penetration" title="Sexual penetration">
       sexual penetration
      </a>
      . The female equivalent is the
      <b>
       baubellum
      </b>
      or
      <i>
       <b>
        os clitoridis
       </b>
      </i>
       a bone in the
      <a href="/wiki/Clitoris" title="Clitoris">
       clitoris
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

       <a href="#cite_note-Ewer1973-2-4">

        4

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baculum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The phallic meaning of the term is unique to English; however, similar words exist in other languages with the same meaning, such as the Slovenian word
      <i>
       bakulum
      </i>
      . The word
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      originally meant "stick" or "staff" in
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">
       homologue
      </a>
      to the
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Female_mammals" title="Female mammals">
       female mammals
      </a>
      is known as the
      <i>
       baubellum
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       os clitoridis
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">
       Purpose
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baculum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purpose">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The baculum is used for copulation and varies in size and shape by
      <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">
       species
      </a>
      . Its characteristics are sometimes used to differentiate between similar species. A bone in the penis allows a male to mate for a long time with a female,

        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      in some
      <a href="/wiki/Mating_system" title="Mating system">

     <p>
      Such a wide distribution among placental mammals suggests that the bone evolved early in the history of these mammals, and was subsequently lost in certain groups. The baculum is an exclusive characteristic of placentals and closely related
      <a href="/wiki/Eutheria" title="Eutheria">
       eutherians
      </a>
      , being absent in other mammal clades, and it has been speculated that it is derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Epipubic_bone" title="Epipubic bone">
       epipubic bones
      </a>
      more widely spread across mammals, but notoriously absent in placentals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szalay2006_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Szalay2006-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Among the primates the
      <a href="/wiki/Marmoset" title="Marmoset">
       marmoset
      </a>
      , weighing around 500 g, has a
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      measuring around 2mm, while the tiny 63 g
      <a href="/wiki/Galago" title="Galago">
       galago
      </a>
      has one around 13mm long. The great apes, despite their size, tend to have very small penis bones, and humans are the only ones to have lost them altogether.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWB_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WWB-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      In some mammalian species, such as the raccoon (
      <i>
       Procyon lotor
      </i>
      ), the baculum can be used to determine relative age. If the baculum tip is made up of uncalcified cartilage, has a porous base, masses less than 1.2 g, and measures less than 90mm long, then the baculum belongs to a juvenile male.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvy2012_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Silvy2012-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>

        </span>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Absence_in_humans">
       Absence in humans
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baculum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Absence in humans">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Unlike other primates,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_(genus)" title="Homo (genus)">
       humans
      </a>
      lack an os penis or os clitoris; however, this bone is present but is much reduced among the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_ape" title="Great ape">
       great apes
      </a>
      : in many ape species it is a relatively insignificant 1020mm structure. There are reported cases of
      <a href="/wiki/Human_penis" title="Human penis">
       human penis
      </a>
      ossification following trauma,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and one reported case of a congenital os penis surgically removed from a 5-year-old boy, who also had other developmental abnormalities, including a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambiguous_genitalia" title="Ambiguous genitalia">
       cleft scrotum
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Clellan_S._Ford" title="Clellan S. Ford">
       Clellan S. Ford
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Frank_A._Beach" title="Frank A. Beach">
       Frank A. Beach
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Patterns_of_Sexual_Behavior" title="Patterns of Sexual Behavior">
        Patterns of Sexual Behavior
       </a>
      </i>
      (1953), p.30 say "Both gorillas and chimpanzees possess a penile bone. In the latter species the os penis is located in the lower part of the organ and measures approximately three-quarters of an inch in length."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-patterns-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In humans, the rigidity of the
      <a href="/wiki/Erection" title="Erection">
       erection
      </a>
      is provided entirely through blood pressure in the
      <a href="/wiki/Corpus_cavernosum_penis" title="Corpus cavernosum penis">
       corpora cavernosa
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been speculated that the loss of the bone in humans, when it is present in our nearest related species the
      <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">
       chimpanzee
      </a>
      , is because humans "evolved a
      <a href="/wiki/Mating_system" title="Mating system">
       mating system
      </a>
      in which the male tended to accompany a particular female all the time to try to ensure paternity of her children"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWB_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-WWB-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which allows for frequent matings of short duration. Observation suggests that primates with a
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      only infrequently encounter females, but engage in longer periods of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_sexual_behavior" title="Animal sexual behavior">
       copulation
      </a>
      that the
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      makes possible, thereby maximizing their chances of fathering the female's offspring. Human females exhibit
      <a href="/wiki/Concealed_ovulation" title="Concealed ovulation">
       concealed ovulation
      </a>
      also known as 'hidden estrus', meaning it is almost impossible to tell when the female is fertile, so frequent matings would be necessary to ensure paternity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWB_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-WWB-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">
       evolutionary biologist
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">
       Richard Dawkins
      </a>
      speculated in 1989 that the loss of the bone in humans, when it is present in our nearest related species the
      <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">
       chimpanzee
      </a>
      , is a result of
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_selection" title="Sexual selection">
       sexual selection
      </a>
      by females looking for
      <a href="/wiki/Signalling_theory" title="Signalling theory">
       honest signals
      </a>
      of good health in prospective mates. The reliance of the human penis solely on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">
       hydraulic
      </a>
      means to achieve a rigid state makes it particularly vulnerable to blood pressure variation. Poor erectile function betrays not only physical states such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">
       diabetes
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurological_disorders" title="Neurological disorders">
       neurological disorders
      </a>
      but mental states such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(biological)" title="Stress (biological)">
       stress
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">
       depression
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A third view is that its loss in humans was a side-effect of
      <a href="/wiki/Neoteny" title="Neoteny">
       neoteny
      </a>
      during human evolution; it is noted that late-stage fetal chimpanzees lack a baculum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bednarik_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bednarik-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">
       Cultural significance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baculum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The existence of the
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      is unlikely to escape the notice of
      <a href="/wiki/Pastoralism" title="Pastoralism">
       pastoralist
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">
       hunter-gatherer
      </a>
      cultures.
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been argued that the "
      <a href="/wiki/Rib" title="Rib">
       rib
      </a>
      " in the biblical story of
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">
       Adam and Eve
      </a>
      is actually a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mistranslation" title="Mistranslation">
       mistranslation
      </a>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Hebrew" title="Biblical Hebrew">
       Biblical Hebrew
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">
       euphemism
      </a>
      for
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      , and that its removal from Adam in the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">
       Book of Genesis
      </a>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">
       creation myth
      </a>
      to explain this absence (as well as the presence of the
      <a href="/wiki/Perineal_raphe" title="Perineal raphe">
       perineal raphe
      </a>
       as a resultant '
      <a href="/wiki/Scar" title="Scar">
       scar
      </a>
      ') in humans.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Oosik">
       Oosik
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baculum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oosik">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <b>
       Oosik
      </b>
      is a term used in Native
      <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">
       Alaska
      </a>
      cultures to describe the
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Walrus" title="Walrus">
       walruses
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phocidae" title="Phocidae">
       seals
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">
       sea lions
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">
       polar bears
      </a>
      . Sometimes as long as 60cm (2ft), fossilized bacula are often polished and used as a handle for
      <a href="/wiki/Knife" title="Knife">
       knives
      </a>
      and other tools. The oosik is a polished and sometimes carved
      <i>
       baculum
      </i>
      of these large northern carnivores.
     </p>
     <p>
      Oosiks are also frequently sold as souvenirs to tourists by
      <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Natives" title="Alaska Natives">
       Alaska Natives
      </a>
      . In 2007 a 4.5-foot (1.4m) long
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
       fossilized
      </a>
      penis bone from an extinct species of walrus, believed by the seller to be the largest in existence, was sold for $8,000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4.5foot_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-4.5foot-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
