    <p>
     <strong>
      Update
     </strong>
     : After about three hours of delay caused by stuck fuel valves, the Delta IV Heavy rocket boosted by the new RS-68A rocket engine made a successful maiden flight this morning at 9:15 Eastern. "It was a great day. It was a thrill to see it get off," said Steve Bouley, vice president of Launch Vehicle and Hypersonic Systems for PWR. The holds in the countdown that delayed the launch had nothing to do with the engines, he said, which appeared to perform flawlessly.
    </p>
    <div class="embedded-recirc--container norewrite">
     <a class="embedded-recirc--link link">
      <div class="embedded-recirc--image-container">
       <img class="embedded-recirc--image" nopin="nopin"/>
      </div>
      <div class="embedded-recirc--inner">
       <div class="embedded-recirc--label">
        We Recommend
       </div>
       <div class="embedded-recirc--title link-txt">
       </div>
      </div>
     </a>
    </div>
    <p>
     <span>
      <strong>
       If all goes well, the world's most powerful
      </strong>
     </span>
     hydrogen and liquid oxygen fueled rocket engine will take flight for the first time tomorrow at 6:13 am EDT. The Pratt &amp; Whitney Rocketdyne (PWR) RS-68A adds 39,000 pounds of thrust to the first-gen RS-68 for 702,000 total pounds of thrust. That's 284,000 more pounds of thrust than the space shuttle's main engine, which was also hydrogen powered. Three of the engines will loft a United Launch Alliance Delta IV Heavy from the Cape Canaveral Air Force Station in Florida on a mission to carry a classified National Reconnaissance Office satellite, the NROL-15, into orbit.
    </p>
    <div class="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
     <div class="gpt-tag" id="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
     </div>
    </div>
    <p>
     Here's the reason you should care: Tomorrow's planned flight represents a significant advancement of the tech that is most likely to power our future space exploration and settlement efforts. "Liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen is the best combination when you're looking simply at performance," says Futron Corporation senior space analyst Jeff Foust of PWR.
    </p>
    <p>
     Not only does this fuel combo deliver a big bang for its weight, but it also uses the elemental constituents of plain old water. That makes it all the more important to any plan to exploit space-based resources and eventually settle the solar system. Water is present in quantity on the moon, on Mars, and on asteroids. And plans by Planetary Resources to
     <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/science/space/news/billionaires-aim-to-make-trillions-mining-asteroids-8310208">
      mine asteroids
     </a>
     , for example, project that water will be one of the most valuable commodities to be claimed. Instead of having to lift rocket fuel out of Earth's gravity well, future spacecraft will be able to dock with space-based refueling depots to be topped off with
     <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/science/space/news/should-space-miners-target-the-moon-or-asteroids-analysis-8534419">
      fuel made in space
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <p>
     But beyond the most obvious challengeactually developing the technology to mine an asteroidhydrogen-powered craft face two major hurdles. First, hydrogen must be kept very cold to keep it a liquid: minus 423.17 F, or about 125 degrees colder than liquid oxygen needs to be. That makes it difficult to handle. Liquid hydrogen also has a very low density, requiring bigger propellant tanks to contain a larger volume of fuel.
    </p>
    <p>
     "With denser fuels like kerosene that also happen to be noncryogenic, you can use smaller tanks that help with other aspects of vehicle engineering as well," Foust says. "If liquid hydrogen didn't have those disadvantages I think you'd see it in use a lot more, simply because of the performance boost that it provides."
    </p>
    <p>
     For PWR, the tradeoff for using hydrogen fuel is worth it. Steve Bouley, vice president of Launch Vehicle and Hypersonic Systems for PWR, says that yes, it's exceeding difficult to handle a deep cryogen like liquid hydrogen. But for RS-68A, the increased performance allowed engineers to design the rocket more for reliability and manufacturability than efficiency. "Having the ability to give up some of the propellant combination's performance capability, we were able to use a very simple operating cyclethe gas-generator cyclefor the RS-68 engine, and that allowed us to make it also a very robust engine." In other words, the power of hydrogen allows them to build a heavier engine than they could with kerosene.
    </p>
    <p>
     The RS-68 was designed to be a cheaper single-use alternative to Space Shuttle Main Engines (SSME). The engine was developed at 20 percent of the cost of the SSME in about half the time, thanks in part to having 80 percent fewer parts. After the inaugural flight, PWR and ULA hope to make the RS-68A the new standard power plant for the Delta IV Heavy.
    </p>
    <p>
     For tomorrow's launch, the Delta IV Heavy will be driven by a single RS-68A in its first stage, and one each in two strap-on side boosters. The boosters will drop away about 4 minutes into the flight, leaving the single main first-stage booster to push the rest of the vehicle to high altitude before itself dropping off about 5-1/2 minutes into the flight. This will allow the second stage rocket with its single RL-10 hydrogen fuel rocket engine to fire and send the payload the rest of the way into orbit.
    </p>
    <p>
     The launch was rescheduled from Thursday to give Tropical Storm Debby time to clear the launch site. Watch for updates, including a live webcast of the launch beginning at 5:53 am EDT Friday,
     <a href="http://www.ulalaunch.com/site/pages/Launch.shtml#/29/" target="_blank">
      on the ULA website
     </a>
     .
    </p>

      and is a frequent contributor to Popular Mechanics
     </em>
     .
    </p>
