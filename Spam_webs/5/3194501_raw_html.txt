   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Ackermann function
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Computability_theory" title="Computability theory">
       computability theory
      </a>
      , the
      <b>
       Ackermann function
      </b>
      , named after
      <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ackermann" title="Wilhelm Ackermann">
       Wilhelm Ackermann
      </a>
      , is one of the simplest
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and earliest-discovered examples of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_function" title="Total function">
       total
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Computable_function" title="Computable function">
       computable function
      </a>
      that is not
      <a href="/wiki/Primitive_recursive_function" title="Primitive recursive function">
       primitive recursive
      </a>
      . All primitive recursive functions are total and computable, but the Ackermann function illustrates that not all total computable functions are primitive recursive.
     </p>
     <p>
      After Ackermann's publication
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ack_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ack-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of his function (which had three nonnegative integer arguments), many authors modified it to suit various purposes, so that today "the Ackermann function" may refer to any of numerous variants of the original function. One common version, the two-argument
      <b>
       AckermannPter function
      </b>
      , is defined as follows for nonnegative integers
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" A(m, n) =
\begin{cases}
n+1 &amp; \mbox{if } m = 0 \\
A(m-1, 1) &amp; \mbox{if } m &gt; 0 \mbox{ and } n = 0 \\
A(m-1, A(m, n-1)) &amp; \mbox{if } m &gt; 0 \mbox{ and } n &gt; 0.
\end{cases}
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/a/e/0ae4053de098cc9554752b190a38bc56.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Its value grows rapidly, even for small inputs. For example
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      (4,2) is an integer of 19,729 decimal digits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ackermann_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the late 1920s, the mathematicians
      <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Sudan" title="Gabriel Sudan">
       Gabriel Sudan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ackermann" title="Wilhelm Ackermann">
       Wilhelm Ackermann
      </a>
      , students of
      <a href="/wiki/David_Hilbert" title="David Hilbert">
       David Hilbert
      </a>
      , were studying the foundations of computation. Both Sudan and Ackermann are credited
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with discovering
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_function" title="Total function">
       total
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Computable_function" title="Computable function">
       computable functions
      </a>
      (termed simply "recursive" in some references) that are not
      <a href="/wiki/Primitive_recursive_function" title="Primitive recursive function">
       primitive recursive
      </a>
      . Sudan published the lesser-known
      <a href="/wiki/Sudan_function" title="Sudan function">
       Sudan function
      </a>
      , then shortly afterwards and independently, in 1928, Ackermann published his function
      <img alt="\varphi\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/5/c/75cd063929964414770b038221f33ea3.png"/>
      . Ackermann's three-argument function,
      <img alt="\varphi(m, n, p)\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/1/ee1ca6ac3bd2ce6b85d336ff41b45905.png"/>
      , is defined such that for
      <i>
       p
      </i>
      = 0, 1, 2, it reproduces the basic operations of
      <a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">
       addition
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">
       multiplication
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">
       exponentiation
      </a>
      as
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\varphi(m, n, 0) = m+n,\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/f/2/8f2c82e93a63986a24f7460dc5f54a33.png"/>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\varphi(m, n, 1) = m\cdot n,\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/a/4/1a4d833fa8b152f21d83ba9a3162e9d3.png"/>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\varphi(m, n, 2) = m^n,\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/2/f/62fe4b440c27016ec831d2b114c13c0b.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      and for
      <i>
       p
      </i>
      &gt; 2 it extends these basic operations in a way that can be compared to the
      <a href="/wiki/Hyperoperation" title="Hyperoperation">
       hyperoperations
      </a>
      : (Aside from its historic role as a total-computable-but-not-primitive-recursive function, Ackermann's original function is seen to extend the basic arithmetic operations beyond exponentiation, although not as seamlessly as do variants of Ackermann's function that are specifically designed for that purposesuch as
      <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Goodstein" title="Reuben Goodstein">
       Goodstein's
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hyperoperation" title="Hyperoperation">
       hyperoperation
      </a>
      sequence.)
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       On the Infinite
      </i>
      , David Hilbert hypothesized that the Ackermann function was not primitive recursive, but it was Ackermann, Hilberts personal secretary and former student, who actually proved the hypothesis in his paper
      <i>
       On Hilberts Construction of the Real Numbers
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       On the Infinite
      </i>
      was Hilberts most important paper on the foundations of mathematics, serving as the heart of
      <a href="/wiki/Hilbert%27s_program" title="Hilbert's program">
       Hilbert's program
      </a>
      to secure the foundation of
      <a href="/wiki/Transfinite_number" title="Transfinite number">
       transfinite numbers
      </a>
      by basing them on finite methods.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ack_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ack-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B3zsa_P%C3%A9ter" title="Rzsa Pter">
       Rzsa Pter
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raphael_Robinson" title="Raphael Robinson">
       Raphael Robinson
      </a>
      later developed a two-variable version of the Ackermann function that became preferred by many authors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_properties">
       Definition and properties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ackermann_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition and properties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ackermann's original three-argument function
      <img alt="\varphi(m, n, p)\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/1/ee1ca6ac3bd2ce6b85d336ff41b45905.png"/>
      is defined
      <a href="/wiki/Recursion" title="Recursion">
       recursively
      </a>
      as follows for nonnegative integers
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       p
      </i>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \varphi(m,n,p) = \begin{cases}
\varphi(m, n, 0) = m + n \\
\varphi(m, 0, 1) = 0 \\
\varphi(m, 0, 2) = 1 \\
\varphi(m, 0, p) = m &amp;\text{ for } p &gt; 2 \\
\varphi(m, n, p) = \varphi(m, \varphi(m, n-1, p), p - 1) &amp;\text{ for } n &gt; 0 \text{ and } p &gt; 0.
\end{cases}\,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/f/92feaf94957d767e3642420f54159ff5.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Of the various two-argument versions, the one developed by Pter and Robinson (called "the" Ackermann function by some authors) is defined for nonnegative integers
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      as follows:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" A(m, n) =
\begin{cases}
n+1 &amp; \mbox{if } m = 0 \\
A(m-1, 1) &amp; \mbox{if } m &gt; 0 \mbox{ and } n = 0 \\
A(m-1, A(m, n-1)) &amp; \mbox{if } m &gt; 0 \mbox{ and } n &gt; 0.
\end{cases}
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/a/e/0ae4053de098cc9554752b190a38bc56.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      It may not be immediately obvious that the evaluation of
      <img alt=" A(m, n)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/d/e/dde8d7bb476fb3deb6486551f734162e.png"/>
      always terminates. However, the recursion is bounded because in each recursive application either
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      decreases, or
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      remains the same and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      decreases. Each time that
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      reaches zero,
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      decreases, so
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      eventually reaches zero as well. (Expressed more technically, in each case the pair (
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      ) decreases in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexicographic_order" title="Lexicographic order">
       lexicographic order
      </a>
      on pairs, which is a
      <a href="/wiki/Well-order" title="Well-order">
       well-ordering
      </a>
      , just like the ordering of single non-negative integers; this means one cannot go down in the ordering infinitely many times in succession.) However, when
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      decreases there is no upper bound on how much
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      can increaseand it will often increase greatly.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pter-Ackermann function can also be expressed in terms of various other versions of the Ackermann function:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       the indexed version of
       <a href="/wiki/Knuth%27s_up-arrow_notation" title="Knuth's up-arrow notation">
        Knuth's up-arrow notation
       </a>
       (extended to integer indices  -2):
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <img alt="A(m,n)=2\uparrow^{m-2} (n+3) - 3." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/1/5/b15db2392a08317d5de564935532082d.png"/>
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The part of the definition
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       , 0) = A(
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       -1, 1) corresponds to
       <img alt="2\uparrow^{m+1} 3=2\uparrow^m 4." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/b/3/ab316c1584b26dba8fbe462456304f9a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyper_operator" title="Hyper operator">
        hyper operators
       </a>
       :
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <img alt="A(m,n)=2[m](n+3)-3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/4/e/54e99ce0125a6859cb06d1e8bbfc2069.png"/>
         .
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Conway_chained_arrow_notation" title="Conway chained arrow notation">
        Conway chained arrow notation
       </a>
       :
      </li>
     </ul>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <img alt="A(m,n)=(2\rightarrow(n+3)\rightarrow(m-2))-3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/8/e/08e743dde20b3019020ea764bbcbcaa2.png"/>
         for
         <img alt="m\geq3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/e/dee5246ee5ca8d877039dec471229700.png"/>
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       hence
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <img alt="2\rightarrow n\rightarrow m=A(m+2,n-3)+3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/6/8/e686f86ce98c63dfd0d9d2c34e30586a.png"/>
         for
         <img alt="n&gt;2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/b/67b347d189c69e57ba64952ced1cde5a.png"/>
         .
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       (
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       =1 and
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       =2 would correspond with
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       ,2)=1 and
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       ,1)=1, which could logically be added.)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      For small values of
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      like 1, 2, or 3, the Ackermann function grows relatively slowly with respect to
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      (at most
      <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">
       exponentially
      </a>
      ). For
      <i>
       m
      </i>
       4, however, it grows much more quickly; even
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      (4,2) is about 2
      <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
       
      </span>
      10
      <sup>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap">
        19
        <span style="margin-left:.25em">
         728
        </span>
       </span>
      </sup>
      , and the decimal expansion of
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      (4,3) is very large by any typical measure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Logician
      <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Friedman" title="Harvey Friedman">
       Harvey Friedman
      </a>
      defines a version of the Ackermann function as follows:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       For n = 0: A(m, n) = 1
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       For m = 1: A(m, n) = 2n
      </li>
      <li>
       Else: A(m, n) = A(m - 1, A(m, n - 1))
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      He also defines a single-argument version A(n) = A(n, n).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A single-argument version A(k) = A(k, k) that increases both
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      at the same time dwarfs every primitive recursive function, including very fast-growing functions such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Exponential_function" title="Exponential function">
       exponential function
      </a>
      , the factorial function, multi- and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfactorial" title="Superfactorial">
       superfactorial
      </a>
      functions, and even functions defined using Knuth's up-arrow notation (except when the indexed up-arrow is used). It can be seen that A(n) is roughly comparable to f
      <sub>
       
      </sub>
      (n) in the fast-growing hierarchy.
     </p>
     <p>
      This extreme growth can be exploited to show that
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      , which is obviously computable on a machine with infinite memory such as a
      <a href="/wiki/Turing_machine" title="Turing machine">
       Turing machine
      </a>
      and so is a
      <a href="/wiki/Computable_function" title="Computable function">
       computable function
      </a>
      , grows faster than any primitive recursive function and is therefore not primitive recursive. In a category with exponentials, using the isomorphism
      <img alt="A \times B \rightarrow C \cong A \rightarrow (B \rightarrow C)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/4/8/c48b8b538a7f04e9b14ad47292191451.png"/>
      (in computer science, this is called
      <a href="/wiki/Currying" title="Currying">
       currying
      </a>
      ), the Ackermann function may be defined via primitive recursion over higher-order functionals as follows:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="
\begin{array}{lcl}
\operatorname{Ack}(0) &amp; = &amp; \operatorname{Succ} \\
\operatorname{Ack}(m+1) &amp; = &amp; \operatorname{Iter}(\operatorname{Ack}(m))
\end{array}
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/1/5/015cb53ecadf3a639f16329972b1a7d0.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
      <i>
       Succ
      </i>
      is the usual
      <a href="/wiki/Successor_function" title="Successor function">
       successor function
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       Iter
      </i>
      is defined by primitive recursion as well:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="
\begin{array}{lcl}
\operatorname{Iter}(f)(0) &amp; = &amp; f(1) \\
\operatorname{Iter}(f)(n+1) &amp; = &amp; f(\operatorname{Iter}(f)(n)).
\end{array}
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/9/b/59b7cc1896d4f42c688de2f3c7a0b9fb.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      One interesting aspect of the Ackermann function is that the only arithmetic operations it ever uses are addition and subtraction of 1. Its properties come solely from the power of unlimited recursion. This also implies that its running time is at least proportional to its output, and so is also extremely huge. In actuality, for most cases the running time is far larger than the output; see below.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">
       Expansion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ackermann_function&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion">

     <p>
      To see how the Ackermann function grows so quickly, it helps to expand out some simple expressions using the rules in the original definition. For example, we can fully evaluate
      <img alt="A(1, 2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/4/c/64cbc4a6527170daf63e5b00ea8e1819.png"/>

     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\begin{align}
A(1,2) &amp; = A(0, A(1, 1)) \\
&amp; = A(0, A(0, A(1, 0))) \\
&amp; = A(0, A(0, A(0, 1))) \\
&amp; = A(0, A(0, 2)) \\
&amp; = A(0, 3) \\
&amp; = 4.
\end{align}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/7/f/97f1bec3cc025321efb263394ca62957.png"/>
      </dd>

      To demonstrate how
      <img alt="A(4, 3)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/b/6/1b6d643eee3c13dc7360245f1c3beaa7.png"/>
      's computation results in many steps and in a large number:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\begin{align}
A(4, 3) &amp; = A(3, A(4, 2)) \\
&amp; = A(3, A(3, A(4, 1))) \\
&amp; = A(3, A(3, A(3, A(4, 0)))) \\
&amp; = A(3, A(3, A(3, A(3, 1)))) \\
&amp; = A(3, A(3, A(3, A(2, A(3, 0))))) \\
