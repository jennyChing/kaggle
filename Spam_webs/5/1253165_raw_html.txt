        <h1 class="posttitle">
         Domain Name Registration Triggers C&amp;D From Instagram... Which Triggers A Weirdly Wonderful Backlash Filled With Duck Drawings`
        </h1>
        <h3>
         from the
         <i>
          domain-name-fun
         </i>
         dept
        </h3>
        <div class="postbody">
         <p>
          If you're going to allow the corporate finger to rest heavily on the "
          <a href="https://www.techdirt.com/articles/20130619/11310623533/epic-response-to-bogus-cease-desist-letter-bravo-your-legal-satire.shtml" target="_blank">
           Release the Lawyers
          </a>
          " button, you need to be braced for the backlash. As backlashes go, this particular incident is light on
          <a href="https://www.techdirt.com/blog/?company=kleargear" target="_blank">
           one-star reviews
          </a>
          and
          <a href="https://www.techdirt.com/articles/20130307/14574822243/simcity-backlash.shtml" target="_blank">
           widespread excoriation
          </a>
          . But it is dripping with sarcasm masquerading as wide-eyed innocence and shows just how quickly a handful of internet denizens can make someone wish they'd never bothered trying to "right" a "wrong."
          <br/>
          <br/>
          A redditor registered the domain slutsofinstagram.com, as one does when presented with the available tools and the inclination to make snap judgements for the amusement of one's self and (hopefully) others. Shortly after that,
          <a href="http://www.reddit.com/r/funny/comments/2hhasi/so_i_got_a_cease_and_desist_letter_from_instagram/" target="_blank">
           the legal department of Instagram got involved
          </a>
          , as one does when elbowing others for trademark breathing space and possessing the inclination to host photos without worrying about third-party hecklers.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <center>
          <img alt="" src="https://i.imgur.com/Ycj4JV1.png" style="width: 500px; height: 425px;"/>
         </center>
         <br/>
         The letter notes that slutsofinstagram.com "contains" the trademarked word "Instagram." It then talks about its 30 million users and being a "worldwide leader" in photo uploading and justifiably famous for doing so.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         It also points out that it must police the internet for uses/abuses of its trademark, ensuring that consumers aren't confused and its mark remains tarnish-free. Finally, the letter notes that the registrant may not be familiar with trademark law, but that's the registrant's fault and he should immediately cease all use of the Instagram trademark, disable any site at that address and not attempt to trade/sell the domain name to another party.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         That bit of officiousness prompted this completely ridiculous (but in the more positive sense) response:
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <center>
          <img alt="" src="https://i.imgur.com/f0hT5UF.png" style="width: 499px; height: 250px;"/>
         </center>
         <blockquote>
          <i>
           Hi
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Wow that sounds like you guys have a cool videogame. 30 million members. Holy moly! I was confused at first but you must be referring to my online fantasy series Sltsof in Stgram. It's a really cool project I'm working on, you should check it out. It's about a magical goat and a duck princess who journey across the enchanted land of Stagram, many adventures are had
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I'm sorry if it sounds close to your company name but I don't think you own the alphabet, that would be funny imagine? Have a good day.
          </i>
         </blockquote>
         Two pages from slutsofinstagram were attached (completely SFW):
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <center>
          <img alt="" src="https://i.imgur.com/3B4w4rY.jpg" style="width: 500px; height: 935px;"/>
         </center>
         <br/>
         Soon,
         <a href="http://www.slutsofinstagram.com/" target="_blank">
          more images were posted
         </a>
         , some by the site owner but many more from others, fleshing out the Stgram world.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <center>
          <img alt="" src="https://i.imgur.com/hMDji1U.png" style="width: 500px; height: 400px;"/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="" src="https://i.imgur.com/f9N0jMw.jpg" style="width: 500px; height: 403px;"/>
         </center>
         <br/>
         As the site owner notes, he has yet to hear back from Instagram. Some questions have been raised, like
         <a href="http://abovethelaw.com/2014/09/instagram-gets-trolled-after-sending-cease-and-desist-letter-to-sluts-of-instagram/" target="_blank">
          what kind of legal rep only signs her first name
         </a>
         on a C&amp;D and who the hell
         <a href="http://www.reddit.com/r/KarmaCourt/comments/2hidxu/the_people_vs_ueditormatt_for_posting_something/" target="_blank">
          actually posts funny content to r/Funny
         </a>
         ? There's also a lack of clarity on the chicken-and-egg problem. Did this redditor create the site with artwork in place simply to troll Instagram, or did he actually have a more unsavory destination in mind before Instagram stepped in?
         <br/>
         <br/>
         Either way, the resulting silence (if legit) is growing rather loud. The redditor apparently posted this in early August, even though it was only in the past few days that any attention has been paid to it. Instagram's purported actions have pretty much ensured this site will remain filled with hand-drawn ducks and goats (and Stgram maps), rather than the more titillating content unwary surfers (and "Edith") may be expecting. So, in a way, it's still a win for Instagram, which won't have its name associated with the word "sluts." On the other hand, the content swiftly filling up slutsofinstagram.com definitely makes it a bit harder to legally claim this redditor should have to abandon his registered domain.
        </div>

         <h3 class="commentsubject">
          Re:
         </h3>
         <div class="cbody">
          As near as I can tell from my admittedly brief research, simply registering the domain does not infringe on trademarks, and there is no legal cause for action as a result. So, no, Instagram isn't on solid legal ground here. In order for trademark infringement to occur, the trademark must be used in commerce. In the case of domain names, this can occur in two ways: the domain leads to a site that is engaging in commerce, or the domain itself is sold ("cybersquatting").
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Just registering a domain name that includes a trademark does not infringe unless you do something with it that makes it infringing.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          (See
          <a href="http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/property00/domain/CaseLaw.html" rel="nofollow">
           http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/property00/domain/CaseLaw.html
          </a>
          )

         <h3 class="commentsubject">
          Re: Funny but disingenuous
         </h3>
         <div class="cbody">
          The domain owner's reply can be submitted in a UDRP response but would most likely factor little in the decision. A UDRP Complainant has to make three points of a case:
          <br/>
          <br/>
          (i) the domain name is identical or confusingly similar to a trademark or service mark in which the complainant has rights; and
          <br/>
          <br/>
          (ii) the domain owner has no rights or legitimate interests in respect of the domain name; and
          <br/>
          <br/>
          (iii) the domain name has been registered and is being used in bad faith.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          The first point would easily go in Instagram's favor. The actual use of the website as something that obviously has not relation to Instagram would, however, factor greatly as a defense against a bad faith registration and show legitimate interest in the domain name. Assuming this domain actually went to a UDRP (I suspect it won't), if the domain owner actually submitted a defense I believe he'd have a very good chance of keeping the domain.
