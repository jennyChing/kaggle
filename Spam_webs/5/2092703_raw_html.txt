     <p>
      <b>
       The Man from Another Place
      </b>
      (played by
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._Anderson" title="Michael J. Anderson">
       Michael J. Anderson
      </a>
      ) is a character from the television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Twin_Peaks" title="Twin Peaks">
        Twin Peaks
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      created by
      <a href="/wiki/David_Lynch" title="David Lynch">
       David Lynch
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Frost" title="Mark Frost">
       Mark Frost
      </a>
      . He is an inhabitant of the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_and_White_Lodges" title="Black and White Lodges">
       Black Lodge
      </a>
      , a realm of pure
      <a href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">
       evil
      </a>
      . Early on in the series, The Man gives Agent
      <a href="/wiki/Dale_Cooper" title="Dale Cooper">
       Dale Cooper
      </a>
      clues to apprehending The Man's nemesis,
      <a href="/wiki/Killer_BOB" title="Killer BOB">
       BOB
      </a>
      . He later makes recurring appearances in relation to the Black Lodge.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Twin_Peaks">
       Twin Peaks
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_from_Another_Place&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Twin Peaks">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Man from Another Place first appears in the series' third episode, in a dream experienced by Cooper. Although a spirit, he appears to Cooper as a
      <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">
       dwarf
      </a>
      in a red
      <a href="/wiki/Suit_(clothing)" title="Suit (clothing)">
       suit
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Dress_shirt" title="Dress shirt">
       dress shirt
      </a>
      . In the dream, The Man gives Cooper a series of esoteric clues which ultimately prove helpful in determining the identity of
      <a href="/wiki/Laura_Palmer" title="Laura Palmer">
       Laura Palmer
      </a>
      's killer, The Man's fellow Black Lodge spirit,
      <a href="/wiki/Killer_BOB" title="Killer BOB">
       BOB
      </a>
      . One of these clues is a strange 1940s-style
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz_dance" title="Jazz dance">
       jazz dance
      </a>
      , a sequence which makes repeated appearances throughout the course of the series. The series never made clear The Man's reasons for wanting to help Cooper, or his true identity. However, despite his linkage to MIKE and the enmity and struggle for Garmonbozia between
      <a href="/wiki/MIKE" title="MIKE">
       MIKE
      </a>
      and BOB, it is unlikely that The Man aids Agent Cooper in an effort to thwart BOB.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      There are a handful of scenes throughout the series that suggest that The Man and BOB are allies; this is also consistent with MIKE removing his arm, as typically this is an action taken when the appendage causes harm. Following Cooper's dream, The Man appears only a few more times: once with BOB, appearing to Cooper following the death of
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Twin_Peaks_characters#Josie_Packard" title="List of Twin Peaks characters">
       Josie Packard
      </a>
      , and again at the end of the series when Cooper ventures into the Black Lodge. It is also questioned whether The Man from Another Place is indeed Mike's severed arm in human form in the Black Lodge.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_Walk_with_Me">
       Fire Walk with Me
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_from_Another_Place&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fire Walk with Me">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Twin_Peaks:_Fire_Walk_with_Me" title="Twin Peaks: Fire Walk with Me">
        Twin Peaks: Fire Walk with Me
       </a>
      </i>
      expands upon The Man from Another Place's identity and his reasons for wanting to help Cooper.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">
       Identity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_from_Another_Place&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Identity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Fire Walk with Me
      </i>
      explains that The Man from Another Place is connected to MIKE, the faceless spirit entity who
      <a href="/wiki/Demonic_possession" title="Demonic possession">
       possesses
      </a>
      Philip Gerard, The One Armed Man, in the series. The script explicitly states that the Man from Another Place is, in fact, MIKE's severed arm. In the series, the one-armed Gerard tells Cooper a story about having been BOB's partner until he cut off his own arm in an effort to relieve himself of a tattoo on the left shoulder that BOB also had. In
      <i>
       Fire Walk With Me
      </i>
      , The Man from Another Place tells Cooper, "I Am the Arm, and I sound like this." He then makes a whooping noise with his hand and mouth. Later, a similar-sounding siren accompanies the first appearance of Gerard. At the end of the film, when BOB enters the Black Lodge and stands beside his host,
      <a href="/wiki/Leland_Palmer" title="Leland Palmer">
       Leland Palmer
      </a>
      , The Man From Another Place appears, standing beside Philip Gerard. At one point The Man From Another Place puts his hand where Gerard's arm used to be, linking up the Arm with its owner and making MIKE whole.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the film
      <i>
       Fire Walk With Me
      </i>
      , after placing his hand on MIKE's shoulder, the Man from Another Place and MIKE speak in unison to BOB, stating "I want all my garmonbozia". BOB then removes blood from Lelands shirt and throws it to the ground. The Man from Another Place is then seen slowly eating creamed corn, interspersed with close-up night vision footage of a monkey staring into the camera, which appears to be the same type of monkey as was earlier shown to be the face of Mrs. Tremond's grandson when he temporarily removed his mask.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reverse_speech">
       Reverse speech
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_from_Another_Place&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reverse speech">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The strange cadence of the Mans dialogue was achieved by having Anderson speak into a recorder. This was then played in reverse, and Anderson was directed to repeat the reversed original. This "reverse-speech" was then reversed again in editing to bring it back to the normal direction. This created the strange rhythm and accentuation that set Coopers dream world apart from the real world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-test-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anderson recalls that his reverse-speech was not difficult to master as, coincidentally, he had used it as a secret language with his junior high school friends. Series creator David Lynch was unaware of this when he cast Anderson in the part, and had hired a trainer to help Anderson with enunciation. When he found out Anderson could already talk backwards, he canceled the trainer and wrote more difficult lines of dialogue for Anderson to read.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
