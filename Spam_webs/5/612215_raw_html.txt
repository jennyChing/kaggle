               <p dir="ltr">
                Alright ladies. Lets talk that time of the month  you know that week or so you spend a little extra sleepy, maybe a little more hungry, maybe a little more powerful in the weight room? In an
                <a href="http://www.womenshealthmag.com/fitness/period-workout#." target="_blank">
                 interview
                </a>
                with Womens Health magazine, Stacy Sims, Ph.D. shares that during our periods, and the week after, we can feel more powerful due a drop in certain hormones. This isnt the only hormonal change that can impact our performance. Our hormones and menstrual cycle can really affect how we lift. In order to really understand how hormones affect our workouts and how we lift, we need to understand what hormonal shifts are taking place and how they affect our body overall.
                <span id="more-225">
                </span>
               </p>
               <h4 dir="ltr">
                A Quick Rundown on Your Cycle
               </h4>
               <p dir="ltr">
                When youre gearing up for the gym during that time of the month, chances are you wont feel like yourself entirely. You may feel sluggish, be experiencing cramps, have lower back pain, or be craving copious amounts of the delicious nectar of goddesses known as chocolate.
                <a href="https://www.womenshealth.gov/publications/our-publications/fact-sheet/menstruation.html">
                 WomensHealth.gov
                </a>
                (and of course Wikipedia) provides a pretty detailed and easy-to-read explanation of what is actually happening inside our bodies when its that time of the month. In the first half of our cycle, levels of estrogen start to rise. In fact, according to the article linked above, it is the rise and fall of levels of hormones during the month control the menstrual cycle. Progesterone and estrogen level drops cause the uterus to shed its lining and egg in a process termed menstruation. Our hormones shift naturally during different phases of the cycle.
               </p>
               <h4 dir="ltr">
                What About the Effects on Aerobic Exercise?
               </h4>
               <p dir="ltr">
                A 2003 study conducted by The University of Sydney found that regularly menstruating female athletes, competing in strength-specific sports and intense anaerobic/aerobic sports, do not need to adjust for menstrual cycle phases to maximize performance [1]. This is exciting news, isnt it?! Citing several other studies, however, Dr. Xanne Janse De Jonge notes that during prolonged exercise in hot conditions, a decrease in exercise time to exhaustion is shown during the mid-luteal phase (the latter phase of the menstruation cycle, aka the time youre on your period), when body temperature is elevated. Therefore, the mid-luteal phase has a potential negative effect on prolonged exercise performance through elevated body temperature and potentially increased cardiovascular strain. What this means is that for female endurance athletes, adjustment of competition schedules to your menstrual cycle, especially in hot or humid conditions, may be worth consideration.
               </p>
               <h4 dir="ltr">
                What About the Effects on Anaerobic Exercise?
               </h4>
               <p dir="ltr">
                The difference between anaerobic capacity between the follicular phase and the luteal phase is significant [2]. The researchers also found that anaerobic power was greater during the luteal phase than during the follicular phase. The fatigue index was greater during the follicular phase than during the luteal phase. The women in this study demonstrated greater anaerobic capacity, produced greater peak power, and were less fatigued by the end of the exercise during the luteal phase than during the follicular phase. Keep in mind, though, there are studies that have seen no reduction in performance across different menstrual phases.
               </p>
               <h4 dir="ltr">
                Phases of Your Cycle and Your Performance
               </h4>
               <p dir="ltr">
                Our workout programs often consist of some form of
                <a href="http://smartgirlslift.com/2014/03/04/what-is-periodization/">
                 lifting periodization
                </a>
                or nutrition cycles. Our bodies go through cycles as well and women are all too familiar about how these cycles can make us feel. When it comes to how we lift, train, and perform in competition, our menstrual cycle can have an impact on how we feel during workouts. When it comes to athletic performance, a 2006 study concluded that physical performance was not affected by the menstrual period, but that the pain of menstruation decreased during the training and competition. 71% of the athletes in the study felt worst just before the menstruation period, 62.2% of the athletes said that their performance was same during the menstruation, and 21.2% said that their performance got worse [3]. Most of the athletes said that they have a painful menstruation period, but that during the competition their pain decreased.
               </p>
               <h4 dir="ltr">
                Can My Birth Control Affect My Performance?
               </h4>
               <p dir="ltr">
                To make it brief, the answer is likely yes. However, how your performance is impacted is not set in stone. In retrospective surveys on the menstrual cycle and performance, from 37 to 63% of athletes did not report any cycle phase detriment, while 13 to 29% reported an improvement during menstruation [4]. This survey found that the best performances were generally in the immediate post-menstrual days, with the worse performances during the premenstrual interval and the first few days of menstrual flow. However, because this is a survey-based, this study has an inherent built-in bias, and is limited by the lack of substantiation of cycle phase.
               </p>
               <p dir="ltr">
                The survey also concluded that oral contraceptives appear to have little impact on strength, but can reduce musculoskeletal injuries for women, possibly because of a reduction of dysmenorrhoea and premenstrual symptoms. In general, the effects of oral contraceptives on athletic performance diminish with the lower dosages. When talking with your friends, its clear that not all women suffer to the same level with symptoms related to their menstrual cycles. Not every woman has the same fitness level nor will every woman be impacted in the same way or to the same extent.
               </p>
               <h4 dir="ltr">
                What Does All This Mean for Me?
               </h4>
               <p dir="ltr">
                In case hitting the gym, trail, or dance floor for the endorphins and a healthy, fit body werent motivation enough, working out can help your body all times of the month. For some, working out and getting in exercise while menstruating can decrease the pain they experience. This is what happens for me  I sometimes have to drag myself through the doors of my gym, but I always feel so much better once Im warmed up and working out. It also means you need to be aware of how your body handles birth control (if youre taking it) and the shifts in hormone levels. Body awareness is a must. If you need a day off, take it. If you are able to push yourself especially hard during a certain week or day, make sure you hit the gym or spend time doing your favorite activity during that time.
               </p>
               <p dir="ltr" style="text-align: center;">

                </em>
