        <h1 class="title">
         Travel Inspiration  Bernini: Sculpting in Clay
        </h1>
        <div id="triberr_endorsement">
        </div>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Update: This article has made the short list for
         <strong>
          Best Single Post Art and Culture,
         </strong>
         in the
         <em>
          Italy Magazine
         </em>
         2014 Blogger Awards. Please click on the link and vote for Adventures of a Carry-on. Grazie!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres the link:
         <a href="http://www.italymagazine.com/blog-awards/2014?field_blog_category_tid=44504" target="_blank" title="Adventures of a Carry-on Best Single Post Art &amp; Culture">
          Adventures of a Carry-on Best Single Post Art and Culture
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Italy Magazine Blogger Awards 2014" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-7314" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/www.adventuresofacarryon.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/italy-blogger-square-2.jpg?resize=313%2C308"/>
        </p>
        <h2>
         Bernini Sculpting In Clay
        </h2>
        <p>
         How many times have you walked by a building, a garden, a storefront, even the produce aisle in your favorite marketand
         <strong>
          never really saw it
         </strong>
         ? Im willing to bet it happens every day, because I do it too. We become laser focused on taking care of the mundane details of life, often with our heads down reading our iPhones, or headphones on at the gym, rushing through the grocery store at the end of a long day so we can get what we need and get out as quickly as possible.
        </p>
        <p>
         Earlier this week I went to the exhibition
         <strong>
          Bernini: Sculpting in Clay
         </strong>
         at the
         <strong>
          Kimbell Museum of Art
         </strong>
         in Ft. Worth, Texas. Though Ive been to
         <strong>
          Rome
         </strong>
         many times and Ive seen many of Berninis real sculptures and fountains in their home environment, I was curious to see the clay models up close, at The Kimbell Museum, a beautiful space filled with natural light.
        </p>
        <p>
         Bernini is all over Rome. Even if you never stepped inside a church or museum, you could not possibly visit Rome and without seeing at least one fountain by Bernini. The guy was prolific and very successful, working for Popes and the nobility of the day. Hes often credited with Rome becoming known as a city of fountains. The most widely known of these fountains is the
         <strong>
          Fountain of the Four
         </strong>
         <strong>
          Rivers
         </strong>
         in Piazza Navona, a top tourist spot in Rome.
        </p>
        <p>
         Born in Naples in 1598 to a father who was also a sculptor, Bernini was a natural. In a biography of Bernini, it is said that he carved a bust of stone when he was only 8 years old that was the marvel of everyone. Bernini died in Rome, in 1680, at the age of 81, after becoming one of the most influential artists of all time and transforming the look of seventeenth century Rome.
        </p>
        <p>
         The exhibition at The Kimbell consists of approximately forty small terracotta sculptures (called bozzetti) and thirty artists drawings and sketches. On the walls behind the clay models hang huge black and white photographs of the actual marble sculptures, in Rome.
        </p>
        <p>
         My favorite part of the exhibition was the gallery devoted to the
         <strong>
          Ponte SantAngelo
         </strong>
         . In 1667, Pope Clement IX, commissioned Bernini to restore the bridge. Berninis vision was to sculpt ten larger than life angels carrying a symbol of the passion to adorn the bridge and create a procession leading to
         <strong>
          Piazza San Pietro
         </strong>
         , another Bernini masterpiece.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_3109" style="width: 510px">
         <a href="http://www.adventuresofacarryon.com/2013/03/10/travel-inspiration-bernini-sculpting-in-clay/bernini-in-clay_/#main" rel="attachment wp-att-3109">
          <img alt="Bernini Sculpting in Clay at the Kimbell Musuem of Art." class="size-full wp-image-3109 " data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/adventuresofacarryon.files.wordpress.com/2013/03/bernini-in-clay_.jpg?resize=500%2C489"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Photo from the exhibition
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         The angels are beautiful  so beautiful that when Pope Clement saw the two angels that Bernini himself had sculpted, he declared they were to beautiful to be outside, and commanded Bernini to sculpt replacements (the other eight were sculpted by his assistants). The original angels live at Sant Andrea della Fratte.
        </p>
        <p>
         Standing in a museum, in Texas, I was struck by the fact that, as many times as Ive been to Rome, Ive never even walked across the Ponte SantAngelo or seen the angels up close  and I realized I have missed a very important piece of the history of Rome, and some amazing art.
        </p>
        <p>
         Who would have thought that an exhibition of small clay models could inspire a trip to Rome, to study the completed sculptures?
         <strong>
          Knowledge creates understanding and understanding creates interest
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         I gained a new appreciation for Bernini and the influence he had on the design of Rome, the home of the Catholic church. St. Peters Square is like a mini Bernini museum, and he designed the sweeping colonade that defines Piazza San Pietro. I could have easily missed going to the exhibition and justified that by the fact that Ive been to Rome, Ive seen the real thing. Instead, I plan to go back to Rome and this time, I will consciously seek Bernini with my head up and my eyes open.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://totallyhistory.com/gian-lorenzo-bernini/" title="Totally History">
          Totally History
         </a>
         has a nice bio on Bernini.
        </p>
        <p>
         A biography:
         <a href="http://www.barnesandnoble.com/w/bernini-and-the-birth-of-baroque-portrait-sculpture-andrea-bacchi/1009235089?ean=9780892369317&amp;r=1&amp;" target="_blank" title="Bernini and the Birth of Baroque Portrait Sculpture">
          Bernini and The Birth of Baroque Portrait Sculpture
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Visit Bernini at Santa Maria
         <a href="http://www.vatican.va/various/basiliche/sm_maggiore/index_en.html" target="_blank" title="Santa Maria Maggiore">
          Maggiore
         </a>
         .
        </p>
