     <p>
      <b>
       Mount Tambora
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       Tamboro
      </b>
      ) is an active
      <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">
       stratovolcano
      </a>
      which is a peninsula of the island of
      <a href="/wiki/Sumbawa" title="Sumbawa">
       Sumbawa
      </a>
      , Indonesia. Sumbawa is flanked both to the north and south by
      <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">
       oceanic crust
      </a>
      , and Tambora was formed by the active
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">
       subduction zone
      </a>
      beneath it. This raised Mount Tambora as high as 4,300m (14,100ft),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stothers1984_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stothers1984-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      making it, in the 18th century, one of the tallest peaks in the Indonesian archipelago. After a large
      <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
       magma chamber
      </a>
      inside the mountain filled over the course of several decades, volcanic activity reached a historic climax in the eruption of 10 April 1815.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degens1989_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Degens1989-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This eruption was about a
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_explosivity_index" title="Volcanic explosivity index">
       volcanic explosivity index
      </a>
      (VEI) of 7, the only unambiguously confirmed VEI-7 eruption since the
      <a href="/wiki/Hatepe_eruption" title="Hatepe eruption">
       Lake Taupo
      </a>
      eruption in about AD 180.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oppenheimer2003_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Oppenheimer2003-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (The
      <a href="/wiki/Heaven_Lake" title="Heaven Lake">
       Heaven Lake
      </a>
      eruption of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baekdu_Mountain" title="Baekdu Mountain">
       Baekdu Mountain
      </a>
      around AD 969 might have also been VEI-7.)
     </p>
     <p>
      With an estimated ejecta volume of 160km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (38cumi), Tambora's 1815 outburst is the largest volcanic eruption in recorded history. The explosion was heard on
      <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">
       Sumatra
      </a>
      , more than 2,000km (1,200mi) away. Heavy
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">
       volcanic ash
      </a>
      falls were observed as far away as
      <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">
       Borneo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">
       Sulawesi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">
       Java
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">
       Maluku Islands
      </a>
      . Most of the deaths from the eruption were from starvation and disease, as the eruptive fallout ruined agricultural productivity in the local region. The death toll was at least 71,000 people, of whom 11,00012,000 were killed directly by the eruption;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oppenheimer2003_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Oppenheimer2003-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the often-cited figure of 92,000 people killed is believed to be an overestimation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tanguy1998_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tanguy1998-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The eruption caused global climate anomalies that included the phenomenon known as "
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">
       volcanic winter
      </a>
      ": 1816 became known as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">
       Year Without a Summer
      </a>
      " because of the effect on North American and European weather. Crops failed and livestock died in much of the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
       Northern Hemisphere
      </a>
      , resulting in the worst
      <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">
       famine
      </a>
      of the 19th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oppenheimer2003_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Oppenheimer2003-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During an
      <a href="/wiki/Excavation_(archaeology)" title="Excavation (archaeology)">
       excavation
      </a>
      in 2004, a team of
      <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">
       archaeologists
      </a>
      discovered
      <a href="/wiki/Tambora_culture" title="Tambora culture">
       cultural remains
      </a>
      buried by the 1815 eruption.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-URI_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-URI-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were kept intact beneath the 3m-deep pyroclastic deposits. At the site, dubbed the "
      <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">
       Pompeii
      </a>
      of the East", the artifacts were preserved in the positions they had occupied in 1815.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_setting">
       Geographical setting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographical setting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sumbawa_Topography.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1860" data-file-width="2440" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Sumbawa_Topography.png/220px-Sumbawa_Topography.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Sumbawa_Topography.png/330px-Sumbawa_Topography.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Sumbawa_Topography.png/440px-Sumbawa_Topography.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sumbawa_Topography.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Topographic map of Tambora and Sumbawa
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tambora_volc.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="527" data-file-width="799" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Tambora_volc.jpg/220px-Tambora_volc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Tambora_volc.jpg/330px-Tambora_volc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Tambora_volc.jpg/440px-Tambora_volc.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tambora_volc.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Mt. Tambora and its surroundings as seen from space
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mount Tambora is on
      <a href="/wiki/Sumbawa" title="Sumbawa">
       Sumbawa
      </a>
      Island, part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Sunda_Islands" title="Lesser Sunda Islands">
       Lesser Sunda Islands
      </a>
      . It is a segment of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Arc" title="Sunda Arc">
       Sunda Arc
      </a>
      , a string of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_islands" title="Volcanic islands">
       volcanic islands
      </a>
      that forms the southern chain of the Indonesian
      <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">
       archipelago
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foden1986_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Foden1986-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tambora forms the Sanggar
      <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">
       peninsula
      </a>
      on Sumbawa. At the north of the peninsula is the
      <a href="/wiki/Flores_Sea" title="Flores Sea">
       Flores Sea
      </a>
      , and at the south is
      <a href="/wiki/Saleh_Bay" title="Saleh Bay">
       Saleh Bay
      </a>
      , 86km (53mi) long and 36km (22mi) wide. At the mouth of Saleh Bay is a 30,000-hectare
      <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">
       islet
      </a>
      called Moyo (Indonesian:
      <i>
       Pulau Moyo
      </i>
      ) which has a guest shelter or luxurious resort where celebrities such as Princess Diana once stayed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asita-ntb_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-asita-ntb-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Besides its interest for
      <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
       seismologists
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanology" title="Volcanology">
       volcanologists
      </a>
      , who monitor the mountain's activity, Mount Tambora is an area of scientific studies for archaeologists and
      <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">
       biologists
      </a>
      . It also attracts tourists for hiking and wildlife activities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinar_Harapan_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sinar_Harapan-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prodant_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-prodant-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The two nearest cities are Dompu and Bima. Three concentrations of villages are around the mountain slope. At the east is Sanggar village, to the northwest are Doro Peti and Pesanggrahan villages, and to the west is Calabai village.
     </p>
     <p>
      Three ascent routes are used to reach the caldera. The first route starts from Doro Mboha village south of the mountain, and follows a paved road through a cashew plantation until it reaches 1,150m (3,770ft)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" title="Above sea level">
       above sea level
      </a>
      . The end of this route is the southern part of the caldera at 1,950m (6,400ft), reachable by a hiking track.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_main_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_main-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This location is usually used as a base camp to monitor the volcanic activity, because it only takes one hour to reach the caldera. The second route is southwest of the mountain, starting from Doro Peti village; the Tambora volcanic monitoring station is in Doro Peti. The third route starts from Pancasila village northwest of the mountain, and passes through a coffee plantation. Using the third route, the caldera is accessible only by foot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_main_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_main-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The highest point of Tambora is on a hill near the westen rim of the caldera.
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2011, the alert level for the volcano was raised from level I to level II after increasing activity was reported in the caldera, including earthquakes and smoke emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portal.vsi.esdm.go.id_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-portal.vsi.esdm.go.id-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In September 2011, the alert level was raised to level III after further increases in activity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GodLikeProductions_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GodLikeProductions-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_history">
       Geological history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geological history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
       Formation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Tambora is 340km (210mi) north of the
      <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Trench" title="Sunda Trench">
       Java Trench
      </a>
      system and 180190km (110120mi) above the upper surface of the active north-dipping
      <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
       subduction zone
      </a>
      . Sumbawa island is flanked to both the north and south by the
      <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">
       oceanic crust
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foden1980_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Foden1980-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The convergence rate is 7.8cm (3.1in) per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigurdsson1989_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sigurdsson1989-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tambora is estimated to have formed around 57,000 years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degens1989_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Degens1989-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Depositing its strata has drained off a large
      <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">
       magma chamber
      </a>
      inside the mountain. The Mojo islet was formed as part of this geological process in which Saleh Bay, collapsing into the caldera of the drained magma chamber, first appeared as a
      <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_basin" title="Oceanic basin">
       sea basin
      </a>
      , about 25,000 years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degens1989_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Degens1989-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to a geological survey before the 1815 eruption, Tambora had the shape of a typical
      <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">
       stratovolcano
      </a>
      , with a high symmetrical volcanic cone soaring up to 4,300 m (14,100ft) above the sea level, and a single central vent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_geology_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_geology-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The diameter at the base is 60km (37mi).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foden1986_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Foden1986-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The central vent emitted lava frequently, which cascaded down a steep slope.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the 1815 eruption, the lowermost portion contains deposits of interlayered sequences of
      <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
       lava
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_rock" title="Pyroclastic rock">
       pyroclastic
      </a>
      materials. The 1 to 4m thick lava flows constitute about 40% of the layers' thickness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_geology_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_geology-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thick
      <a href="/wiki/Scoria" title="Scoria">
       scoria
      </a>
      beds were produced by the fragmentation of lava flows. Within the upper section, the lava is interbedded with scoria,
      <a href="/wiki/Tuff" title="Tuff">
       tuffs
      </a>
      , and pyroclastic flows and falls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_geology_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_geology-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At least 20 subsidiary or
      <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_cone" title="Parasitic cone">
       parasitic cones
      </a>
      are known.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigurdsson1989_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sigurdsson1989-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some of them have names:
      <i>
       Tahe
      </i>
      , 844m (2,769ft);
      <i>
       Molo
      </i>
      , 602m (1,975ft);
      <i>
       Kadiendinae
      </i>
      ;
      <i>
       Kubah
      </i>
      , 1,648m (5,407ft); and
      <i>
       Doro Api Toi
      </i>
      . Most of these parasitic cones have produced
      <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
       basaltic
      </a>
      lavas.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eruptive_history">
       Eruptive history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eruptive history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Tambora_Volcano,_Sumbawa_Island,_Indonesia.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2828" data-file-width="4256" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Mount_Tambora_Volcano%2C_Sumbawa_Island%2C_Indonesia.jpg/220px-Mount_Tambora_Volcano%2C_Sumbawa_Island%2C_Indonesia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Mount_Tambora_Volcano%2C_Sumbawa_Island%2C_Indonesia.jpg/330px-Mount_Tambora_Volcano%2C_Sumbawa_Island%2C_Indonesia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Mount_Tambora_Volcano%2C_Sumbawa_Island%2C_Indonesia.jpg/440px-Mount_Tambora_Volcano%2C_Sumbawa_Island%2C_Indonesia.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Tambora_Volcano,_Sumbawa_Island,_Indonesia.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The summit caldera of the volcano
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">
       Radiocarbon dating
      </a>
      has established the dates of three of Mount Tambora's eruptions before the 1815 eruption. The magnitudes of these eruptions are unknown.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The estimated dates are 3910BC  200 years, 3050BC and 740AD  150 years. They were all explosive central vent eruptions with similar characteristics, but the 740 AD eruption had no pyroclastic flows.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1812, Mount Tambora entered a period of high activity, with its climactic eruption being the catastrophic explosive event of April 1815.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Mount Tambora is still
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_volcano" title="Active volcano">
       active
      </a>
      . Minor lava domes and flows have been extruded on the caldera floor during the 19th and 20th centuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_Tambora_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_Tambora-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The last eruption was recorded in 1967.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory_20-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, it was a very small, non-explosive eruption (VEI = 0).
     </p>
     <p>
      There were reports of a similarly small eruption in 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div style="clear:left;">
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1815_eruption">
       1815 eruption
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1815 eruption">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/1815_eruption_of_Mount_Tambora" title="1815 eruption of Mount Tambora">
       1815 eruption of Mount Tambora
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The VEI-7 eruption had a total tephra ejecta volume of 160km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      (38cumi).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory_20-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was an explosive central vent eruption with pyroclastic flows and a caldera collapse, causing
      <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">
       tsunamis
      </a>
      and extensive land and property damage. It had a long-term effect on global climate. This activity ceased on 15 July 1815.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory_20-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Follow-up activity was recorded in August 1819 consisting of a small eruption (VEI = 2) with flames and rumbling
      <a href="/wiki/Aftershock" title="Aftershock">
       aftershocks
      </a>
      , and was considered to be part of the 1815 eruption sequence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oppenheimer2003_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Oppenheimer2003-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Around 188030 years, Tambora went into eruption again, but only inside the caldera.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory_20-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Global_Volcanism_Program_EruptiveHistory-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Small lava flows and lava dome
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrusive_(geology)" title="Extrusive (geology)">
       extrusions
      </a>
      were formed. This eruption (VEI = 2) created the
      <i>
       Doro Api Toi
      </i>
      parasitic cone inside the caldera.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_history_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_history-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_work">
       Archaeological work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Archaeological work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Tambora_culture" title="Tambora culture">
        Tambora culture
       </a>
       for details about 2004 work on exploring for villages and people lost at the time of the major eruption.
      </i>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem">
       Ecosystem
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ecosystem">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A scientific team led by Swiss
      <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">
       botanist
      </a>
      , Heinrich Zollinger, arrived on Sumbawa in 1847.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zollinger_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zollinger-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zollinger's mission was to study the eruption scene and its effects on the local
      <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">
       ecosystem
      </a>
      . He was the first person to climb to the summit after the eruption. It was still covered by smoke. As Zollinger climbed up, his feet sank several times through a thin surface crust into a warm layer of powder-like sulphur. Some vegetation had re-established itself and a few trees were observed on the lower slope. A
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Casuarina" title="Casuarina">
        casuarina
       </a>
      </i>
      forest was noted at 2,2002,550m (7,2208,370ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zollinger2_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zollinger2-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Imperata_cylindrica" title="Imperata cylindrica">
        imperata cylindrica
       </a>
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">
       grasslands
      </a>
      were also found.
     </p>
     <p>
      Resettlement of the mountain began in 1907. A coffee plantation was started in the 1930s on the northwestern slope of the mountain, in the village of Pekat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boers1995_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boers1995-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A dense
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">
       rain forest
      </a>
      , dominated by the pioneering tree,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Duabanga_moluccana" title="Duabanga moluccana">
        Duabanga moluccana
       </a>
      </i>
      , had grown at an altitude of 1,0002,800m (3,3009,200ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boers1995_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boers1995-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It covers an area up to 80,000ha (200,000 acres). The rain forest was explored by a Dutch team, led by Koster and de Voogd in 1933.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boers1995_25-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boers1995-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From their accounts, they started their journey in a "fairly barren, dry and hot country", and then they entered "a mighty jungle" with "huge, majestic forest giants". At 1,100m (3,600ft), they entered a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_forest" title="Montane forest">
       montane forest
      </a>
      . Above 1,800m (5,900ft), they found
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dodonaea_viscosa" title="Dodonaea viscosa">
        Dodonaea viscosa
       </a>
      </i>
      dominated by
      <i>
       Casuarina
      </i>
      trees. On the summit, they found sparse
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Anaphalis" title="Anaphalis">
        Anaphalis viscida
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wahlenbergia" title="Wahlenbergia">
        Wahlenbergia
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1896, 56 species of birds were found, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Crested_white-eye" title="Crested white-eye">
       crested white-eye
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trainor2002_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trainor2002-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Twelve further species were found in 1981. Several other zoological surveys followed, and found other bird species on the mountain, resulting in over 90 bird species discovered on Mount Tambora.
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow-crested_cockatoo" title="Yellow-crested cockatoo">
       yellow-crested cockatoos
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoothera" title="Zoothera">
       <i>
        Zoothera
       </i>
       thrushes
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_myna" title="Hill myna">
       hill mynas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Green_junglefowl" title="Green junglefowl">
       green junglefowl
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_lorikeet" title="Rainbow lorikeet">
       rainbow lorikeets
      </a>
      are hunted for the cagebird trade by the local people.
      <a href="/wiki/Orange-footed_scrubfowl" title="Orange-footed scrubfowl">
       Orange-footed scrubfowl
      </a>
      are hunted for food. This bird exploitation has resulted in a decline in the bird population. The yellow-crested cockatoo is nearing extirpation on Sumbawa island.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trainor2002_26-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trainor2002-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 1972, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logging_industry" title="Logging industry">
       commercial logging company
      </a>
      has been operating in the area, which poses a large threat to the rain forest. The logging company holds a timber-cutting concession for an area of 20,000ha (49,000 acres), or 25% of the total area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boers1995_25-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boers1995-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another part of the rain forest is used as a hunting ground. In between the hunting ground and the logging area, there is a designated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildlife_reserve" title="Wildlife reserve">
       wildlife reserve
      </a>
      where deer,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_buffalo" title="Domestic buffalo">
       water buffalos
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_pig" title="Wild pig">
       wild pigs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">
       bats
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_fox" title="Flying fox">
       flying foxes
      </a>
      , and various species of reptiles and birds can be found.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boers1995_25-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boers1995-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration_of_the_caldera_floor">
       Exploration of the caldera floor
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exploration of the caldera floor">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg/220px-Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg/330px-Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg/440px-Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calderaboden_des_Tambora.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        On the floor of Tambora's caldera, looking north (GRV 2013)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the Tambora caldera formed by the massive eruption in 1815 an ecosystem has developed largely uninfluenced by human beings because of its isolation. Zollinger (1847) and later P. van Rheden (1913) and W.A. Petroeschevsky (1947) could only observe the caldera floor from the crater rim.
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 2013, a German research team (Georesearch Volcanedo Germany) for the first time carried out a longer expedition into this caldera, about 1300m deep, and with the help of a native team climbed down the southern caldera wall from 2430m to 1340m altitude, reaching the caldera floor while experiencing extreme conditions. A German geoscientist in the team was the first European woman and worldwide the first woman to conquer the almost impassable inner southern wall of this volcano. The team stayed within the Tambora caldera for nine days, researching the caldera floor. People had reached the caldera floor only in a few cases as the descent down the steep walls is difficult and dangerous, subject to earthquakes, landslides and rockfalls. Moreover, only relatively short stays on the caldera floor had been possible due to logistical problems, so that extensive studies had been impossible. The investigation program of Georesearch Volcanedo on the caldera floor included researching the visible effects of the smaller eruptions which had taken place on the caldera floor since 1815, temperature measurements (air, soil, gases), gas measurements, studies of flora and fauna and measurement of weather data. Especially striking was the relatively high activity of Doro Api Toi (
      <i>
       Gunung Api Kecil
      </i>
      means "small volcano")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the southern part of the caldera and the gases escaping under high pressure and loud noises on the lower north-east wall.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg/220px-TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg/330px-TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg/440px-TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TAM_NE-Gas-4966.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Gases escaping under high pressure in the northeastern region of Tambora's caldera (GRV2014)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In July 2014 the same research team from Georesearch Volcanedo Germany carried out a further expedition into the Tambora caldera and set a new record, beating their own: over 12 days the investigations of the previous year were continued and expanded. The results of these two expeditions are to be published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring">
       Monitoring
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Tambora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Monitoring">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3962" data-file-width="3940" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg/220px-Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg/330px-Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg/440px-Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tambora_EFS_highres_STS049_STS049-97-54.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Infrared image of Mount Tambora (north is on the left)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Indonesia's population has been increasing rapidly since the 1815 eruption. As of 2006, the population of Indonesia has reached 222 million people,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BPS_popstats_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BPS_popstats-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of which 130 million are concentrated on
      <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">
       Java
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calder_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Calder-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A contemporary volcanic eruption as large as Tambora's 1815 eruption would cause catastrophic devastation with likely many more fatalities. Therefore, volcanic activity in Indonesia is continuously monitored, including that of Mount Tambora. Seismic activity in Indonesia is monitored by the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Directorate_of_Vulcanology_and_Geological_Hazard_Mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Directorate of Vulcanology and Geological Hazard Mitigation (page does not exist)">
       Directorate of Vulcanology and Geological Hazard Mitigation
      </a>
      , Indonesia. The monitoring post for Mount Tambora is located at Doro Peti village.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_danger_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_danger-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They focus on seismic and
      <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">
       tectonic
      </a>
      activities by using a seismograph. Since the 1880 eruption, there has been no significant increase in seismic activity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_geofisika_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_geofisika-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, monitoring is continuously performed inside the caldera, especially around the parasitic cone Doro Api Toi.
     </p>
     <p>
      The directorate has defined a hazard
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disaster_mitigation" title="Disaster mitigation">
       mitigation map
      </a>
      for Mount Tambora. Two zones are declared: the dangerous zone and the cautious zone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_danger_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_danger-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The dangerous zone is an area that will be directly affected by an eruption: pyroclastic flow, lava flow and other pyroclastic falls. This area, including the caldera and its surroundings, covers up to 58.7km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      (22.7sqmi). Habitation of the dangerous zone is prohibited. The cautious zone includes areas that might be indirectly affected by an eruption:
      <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">
       lahar
      </a>
      flows and other pumice stones. The size of the cautious area is 185km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      (71sqmi), and includes
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pasanggrahan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pasanggrahan (page does not exist)">
       Pasanggrahan
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doro_Peti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doro Peti (page does not exist)">
       Doro Peti
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rao_(Indonesia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rao (Indonesia) (page does not exist)">
       Rao
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Labuan_Kenanga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Labuan Kenanga (page does not exist)">
       Labuan Kenanga
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gubu_Ponda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gubu Ponda (page does not exist)">
       Gubu Ponda
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kawindana_Toi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kawindana Toi (page does not exist)">
       Kawindana Toi
      </a>
      and Hoddo villages. The river
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guwu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guwu (page does not exist)">
       Guwu
      </a>
      at the southern and northwest part of the mountain is also included in the cautious zone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSI_danger_31-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-VSI_danger-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
