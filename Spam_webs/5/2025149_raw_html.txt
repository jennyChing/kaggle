   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Quantum Zeno effect
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       quantum Zeno effect
      </b>
      (also known as the
      <b>
       Turing paradox
      </b>
      ) is a situation in which an
      <a href="/wiki/Particle_decay" title="Particle decay">
       unstable particle
      </a>
      , if observed continuously, will never decay.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sudarshan_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sudarshan-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One can "freeze" the evolution of the system by measuring it frequently enough in its known initial state. The meaning of the term has since expanded, leading to a more technical definition in which time evolution can be suppressed not only by measurement: the quantum Zeno effect is the suppression of unitary time evolution caused by
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">
       quantum decoherence
      </a>
      in quantum systems provided by a variety of sources: measurement, interactions with the environment, stochastic fields, and so on.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakanishi_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nakanishi-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As an outgrowth of study of the quantum Zeno effect, it has become clear that applying a series of sufficiently strong and fast pulses with appropriate symmetry can also
      <i>
       decouple
      </i>
      a system from its decohering environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facchi0_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Facchi0-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The name comes from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeno%27s_arrow_paradox" title="Zeno's arrow paradox">
       Zeno's arrow paradox
      </a>
      which states that, since an arrow in flight is not seen to move during any single instant, it cannot possibly be moving at all.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeno_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zeno-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        note 1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The comparison with Zeno's paradox is due to a 1977 paper by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Sudarshan" title="George Sudarshan">
       George Sudarshan
      </a>
      and Baidyanath Misra.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sudarshan_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sudarshan-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first rigorous and general derivation of this effect was presented in 1974 by Degasperis
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degasperis_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Degasperis-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It had previously been described by
      <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">
       Alan Turing
      </a>
      in 1954:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turing_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Turing-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       It is easy to show using standard theory that if a system starts in an eigenstate of some observable, and measurements are made of that observable
       <i>
        N
       </i>
       times a second, then, even if the state is not a stationary one, the probability that the system will be in the same state after, say, one second, tends to one as
       <i>
        N
       </i>
       tends to infinity; that is, that continual observations will prevent motion 
       <p style="text-align: right;">
         Alan Turing as quoted by A. Hodges in
        <i>
         Alan Turing: Life and Legacy of a Great Thinker
        </i>
        p. 54
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      resulting in the earlier name
      <i>
       Turing paradox
      </i>
      . The idea is contained in the early work by
      <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">
       John von Neumann
      </a>
      , sometimes called the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wave_function_collapse#History_and_context" title="Wave function collapse">
        reduction postulate
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonNeumann_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vonNeumann-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was shown that the quantum Zeno effect of a single system is equivalent to the indetermination of the quantum state of a single system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the reduction postulate, each measurement causes the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction" title="Wavefunction">
       wavefunction
      </a>
      to "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction_collapse" title="Wavefunction collapse">
       collapse
      </a>
      " to a pure
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenstate" title="Eigenstate">
       eigenstate
      </a>
      of the measurement basis. In the context of this effect, an "observation" can simply be the absorption of a particle, without an observer in any conventional sense. However, there is controversy over the interpretation of the effect, sometimes referred to as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Measurement_problem" title="Measurement problem">
       measurement problem
      </a>
      " in traversing the interface between microscopic and macroscopic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zajonc_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Zajonc-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facchi_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Facchi-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another crucial problem related to the effect is strictly connected to the time-energy indeterminacy relation. If one wants to make the measurement process more and more frequent, one has to correspondingly decrease the time duration of the measurement itself. But the request that the measurement last only a very short time implies that the energy spread of the state on which reduction occurs becomes more and more large. However, the deviations from the exponential decay law for small times, is crucially related to the inverse of the energy spread so that the region in which the deviations are appreciable shrinks when one makes the measurement process duration shorter and shorter. An explicit evaluation of these two competing requests shows that it is inappropriate, without taking into account this basic fact, to deal with the actual occurrence and emergence of Zeno's effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghirardi_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ghirardi-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Closely related (and sometimes not distinguished from the quantum Zeno effect) is the
      <i>
       watchdog effect
      </i>
      , in which the time evolution of a system is affected by its continuous coupling to the environment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belavkin-Staszewski_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Belavkin-Staszewski-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watchdog_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-watchdog-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Auletta_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Auletta-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_Zeno_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Unstable quantum systems are predicted to exhibit a short time deviation from the exponential decay law.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khalfin_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Khalfin-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This universal phenomenon has led to the prediction that frequent measurements during this nonexponential period could inhibit decay of the system, one form of the
      <b>
       quantum Zeno effect
      </b>
      . Subsequently, it was predicted that an
      <i>
       enhancement
      </i>
      of decay due to frequent measurements could be observed under somewhat more general conditions, leading to the so-called
      <b>
       anti-Zeno effect
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-definition_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-definition-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        note 2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
       quantum mechanics
      </a>
      , the interaction mentioned is called "measurement" because its result can be interpreted in terms of
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">
       classical mechanics
      </a>
      . Frequent measurement prohibits the transition. It can be a transition of a particle from one half-space to another (which could be used for
      <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mirror" title="Atomic mirror">
       atomic mirror
      </a>
      in an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_nanoscope" title="Atomic nanoscope">
       atomic nanoscope
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nanoscope_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nanoscope-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) as in the time of arrival problem,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allcock_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-allcock-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-echanobe_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-echanobe-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a transition of a photon in a waveguide from one mode to another, and it can be a transition of an atom from one quantum state to another. It can be a transition from the subspace without decoherent loss of a
      <a href="/wiki/Qubit" title="Qubit">
       qubit
      </a>
      to a state with a qubit lost in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computer" title="Quantum computer">
       quantum computer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stolze_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stolze-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this sense, for the qubit correction, it is sufficient to determine whether the decoherence has already occurred or not. All these can be considered as applications of the Zeno effect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fransen_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fransen-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By its nature, the effect appears only in systems with distinguishable quantum states, and hence is inapplicable to classical phenomena and macroscopic bodies.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Various_realizations_and_general_definition">
       Various realizations and general definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_Zeno_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Various realizations and general definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The treatment of the Zeno effect as a
      <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">
       paradox
      </a>
      is not limited to the processes of quantum decay. In general, the term
      <b>
       Zeno effect
      </b>
      is applied to various transitions, and sometimes these transitions may be very different from a mere "decay" (whether exponential or non-exponential).
     </p>
     <p>
      One realization refers to the observation of an object (Zeno's arrow, or any quantum particle) as it leaves some region of space. In the 20th century, the trapping (confinement) of a particle in some region by its observation outside the region was considered as nonsensical, indicating some non-completeness of quantum mechanics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miel94_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-miel94-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even as late as 2001, confinement by absorption was considered as a paradox.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yama01_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-yama01-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, similar effects of the suppression of
      <a href="/wiki/Raman_scattering" title="Raman scattering">
       Raman scattering
      </a>
      was considered an expected
      <i>
       effect
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thun_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-thun-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kouz05_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kouz05-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      not a paradox at all. The absorption of a photon at some wavelength, the release of a photon (for example one that has escaped from some mode of a fiber), or even the relaxation of a particle as it enters some region, are all processes that can be interpreted as measurement. Such a measurement suppresses the transition, and is called the Zeno effect in the scientific literature.
     </p>
     <p>
      In order to cover all of these phenomena (including the original effect of suppression of quantum decay), the Zeno effect can be defined as a class of phenomena in which some transition is suppressed by an interaction  one that allows the interpretation of the resulting state in the terms
      <i>
       transition did not yet happen
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       transition has already occurred
      </i>
      , or
      <i>
       The proposition that the evolution of a quantum system is halted
      </i>
      if the state of the system is continuously measured by a macroscopic device to check whether the system is still in its initial state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pano01_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pano01-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Periodic_measurement_of_a_quantum_system">
       Periodic measurement of a quantum system
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_Zeno_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Periodic measurement of a quantum system">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Consider a system in a state A, which is the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenstate" title="Eigenstate">
       eigenstate
      </a>
      of some measurement operator. Say the system under free time evolution will decay with a certain probability into state B. If measurements are made periodically, with some finite interval between each one, at each measurement, the wave function collapses to an eigenstate of the measurement operator. Between the measurements, the system evolves away from this eigenstate into a
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">
       superposition
      </a>
      state of the states A and B. When the superposition state is measured, it will again collapse, either back into state A as in the first measurement, or away into state B. However, its probability of collapsing into state B, after a very short amount of time
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      , is proportional to
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      , since probabilities are proportional to squared amplitudes, and amplitudes behave linearly. Thus, in the limit of a large number of short intervals, with a measurement at the end of every interval, the probability of making the transition to B goes to zero.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_decoherence" title="Quantum decoherence">
       decoherence theory
      </a>
      , the collapse of the wave function is not a discrete, instantaneous event. A "measurement" is equivalent to strongly coupling the quantum system to the noisy thermal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surroundings_(thermodynamics)" title="Surroundings (thermodynamics)">
       environment
      </a>
      for a brief period of time, and continuous strong coupling is equivalent to frequent "measurement". The time it takes for the wave function to "collapse" is related to the decoherence time of the system when coupled to the environment. The stronger the coupling is, and the shorter the decoherence time, the faster it will collapse. So in the decoherence picture, a perfect implementation of the quantum Zeno effect corresponds to the limit where a quantum system is continuously coupled to the environment, and where that coupling is infinitely strong, and where the "environment" is an infinitely large source of thermal randomness.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments_and_discussion">
       Experiments and discussion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantum_Zeno_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Experiments and discussion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Experimentally, strong suppression of the evolution of a quantum system due to environmental coupling has been observed in a number of microscopic systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1989,
      <a href="/wiki/David_J._Wineland" title="David J. Wineland">
       David J. Wineland
      </a>
      and his group at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIST" title="NIST">
       NIST
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-u0_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-u0-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      observed the quantum Zeno effect for a two-level atomic system that was interrogated during its evolution. Approximately 5000
      <sup>
       9
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">
       Be
      </a>
      <sup>
       +
      </sup>
      ions were stored in a cylindrical
      <a href="/wiki/Penning_trap" title="Penning trap">
       Penning trap
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Laser_cooling" title="Laser cooling">
       laser cooled
      </a>
      to below 250mK. A resonant
      <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
       RF
      </a>
      pulse was applied which, if applied alone, would cause the entire ground state population to migrate into an excited state. After the pulse was applied, the ions were monitored for photons emitted due to relaxation. The ion trap was then regularly "measured" by applying a sequence of
      <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
       ultraviolet
      </a>
      pulses, during the RF pulse. As expected, the ultraviolet pulses suppressed the evolution of the system into the excited state. The results were in good agreement with theoretical models. A recent review describes subsequent work in this area.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leibried_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leibried-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2001,
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_G._Raizen" title="Mark G. Raizen">
       Mark G. Raizen
      </a>
      and his group at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">
       University of Texas at Austin
      </a>
      , observed the quantum Zeno effect for an unstable quantum system,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raizen_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Raizen-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as originally proposed by Sudarshan and Misra.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sudarshan_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sudarshan-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also observed an anti-Zeno effect. Ultracold sodium atoms were trapped in an accelerating optical lattice and the loss due to tunneling was measured. The evolution was interrupted by reducing the acceleration, thereby stopping
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_tunneling" title="Quantum tunneling">
       quantum tunneling
      </a>
      . The group observed suppression or enhancement of the decay rate, depending on the regime of measurement.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Quantum Zeno Effect is used in commercial
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_magnetometer" title="Atomic magnetometer">
       atomic magnetometers
      </a>
      and naturally by birds' magnetic compass sensory mechanism (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetoreception" title="Magnetoreception">
       magnetoreception
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is still an open question how closely one can approach the limit of an infinite number of interrogations due to the Heisenberg uncertainty involved in shorter measurement times. It has been shown, however, that measurements performed at a finite frequency can yield arbitrarily strong Zeno effects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2006, Streed
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      at MIT observed the dependence of the Zeno effect on measurement pulse characteristics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Streed_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Streed-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The interpretation of experiments in terms of the "Zeno effect" helps describe the origin of a phenomenon. Nevertheless, such an interpretation does not bring any principally new features not described with the
      <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrdinger equation">
       Schrdinger equation
      </a>
      of the quantum system.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrosky1_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Petrosky1-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrosky2_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Petrosky2-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Even more, the detailed description of experiments with the "Zeno effect", especially at the limit of high frequency of measurements (high efficiency of suppression of transition, or high reflectivity of a
      <a href="/wiki/Ridged_mirror" title="Ridged mirror">
       ridged mirror
      </a>
      ) usually do not behave as expected for an idealized measurement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nanoscope_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nanoscope-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It should be noted that the Quantum Zeno effect is dependent upon the reductionist postulate for reconciling the measurement problem. Thus, the Quantum Zeno effect does not apply to all interpretations of quantum theory; in particular, the
      <a href="/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation" title="Many-worlds interpretation">
       many-worlds interpretation
      </a>
      (a.k.a. the Multiverse Interpretation) and the Quantum Logic Interpretation. Also, the Quantum Zeno effect may only hold for directly observed quantum systems, meaning that statistically observed systems (i.e. macromolecular systems of approximately 30 or more atoms) might not be affected by the Zeno effect. These qualifications mean that the Zeno effect may possibly be a useful experimental design for testing the Many-Worlds Hypothesis, the Quantum Logic Hypothesis, and various hypotheses related to Quantum Computing and require analysis of the mechanism of the interaction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koshino_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Koshino-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It was shown that the Quantum Zeno effect persists in the many-worlds and relative states interpretations of quantum mechanics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhome_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dhome-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
