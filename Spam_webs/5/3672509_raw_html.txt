   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Mudra
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      This article is about the use of mudra in Indic religion.  For mudra as used in Indian classical music, see
      <a href="/wiki/Mudra_(music)" title="Mudra (music)">
       Mudra (music)
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       mudra
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="noexcerpt">
        <a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/En-us-mudra-2.ogg" title="Listen">
         <img alt="Listen" data-file-height="11" data-file-width="11" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/11px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/17px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Speakerlink-new.svg/22px-Speakerlink-new.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
        </a>
        <sup>
         <span class="IPA" style="color:#00e;font:bold 80% sans-serif;padding:0 .1em">
          <a href="/wiki/File:En-us-mudra-2.ogg" title="File:En-us-mudra-2.ogg">
           i
          </a>
         </span>
        </sup>
       </span>
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="'m' in 'my'">
           m
          </span>
          <span title="/u/ long 'oo' in 'food'">
           u
          </span>
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
           d
          </span>
          <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
           r
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'father'">
           
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       mudr
      </span>
      , "seal", "mark", or "gesture";
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_alphabet" title="Tibetan alphabet">
       Tibetan
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="bo" style="display:inline-block; font-family:'DDC Uchen', Kailash, Jomolhari,'Tibetan Machine Uni'; font-weight:normal; font-size:130%; vertical-align:top;" xml:lang="bo">
       
      </span>
      <a href="/wiki/THL_Simplified_Phonetic_Transcription" title="THL Simplified Phonetic Transcription">
       THL
      </a>
      <i>
       chakgya
      </i>
      ) is a symbolic or ritual gesture in
      <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
       Hinduism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While some mudras involve the entire body, most are performed with the hands and fingers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A mudr is a spiritual gesture and an energetic seal of authenticity employed in the
      <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">
       iconography
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_practice" title="Spiritual practice">
       spiritual practice
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">
       Indian religions
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      One hundred and eight mudras are used in regular
      <a href="/wiki/Tantra" title="Tantra">
       Tantric
      </a>
      rituals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">
       yoga
      </a>
      , mudras are used in conjunction with
      <a href="/wiki/Pranayama" title="Pranayama">
       pranayama
      </a>
      (yogic breathing exercises), generally while seated in
      <a href="/wiki/Lotus_position" title="Lotus position">
       Padmasana
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sukhasana" title="Sukhasana">
       Sukhasana
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Vajrasana_(yoga)" title="Vajrasana (yoga)">
       Vajrasana
      </a>
      pose, to stimulate different parts of the body involved with breathing and to affect the flow of
      <a href="/wiki/Prana" title="Prana">
       prana
      </a>
      in the body.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_and_etymology">
       Nomenclature and etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature and etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Chinese translation is
      <i>
       yin
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        yn
       </span>
      </i>
      ) or
      <i>
       yinxiang
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        ynxing
       </span>
      </i>
      ). The Japanese and Korean pronunciation is "
      <i>
       in
      </i>
      ".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">
       Iconography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Iconography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mudra is used in the
      <a href="/wiki/Iconography" title="Iconography">
       iconography
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
       Hindu
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_art" title="Buddhist art">
       Buddhist art
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
       Indian subcontinent
      </a>
      and described in the scriptures, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Natya_Shastra" title="Natya Shastra">
       Ntyastra
      </a>
      , which lists 24
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        asayuta
       </span>
      </i>
      ("separated", meaning "one-hand") and 13
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        sayuta
       </span>
      </i>
      ("joined", meaning "two-hand") mudras. Mudra positions are usually formed by both the hand and the fingers. Along with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Asana" title="Asana">
        sanas
       </a>
      </i>
      ("seated postures"), they are employed statically in the
      <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">
       meditation
      </a>
      and dynamically in the
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Nya
      </span>
      practice of Hinduism.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hindu and Buddhist iconography share some mudras. In some regions, for example
      <a href="/wiki/Iconography_of_Gautama_Buddha_in_Laos_and_Thailand" title="Iconography of Gautama Buddha in Laos and Thailand">
       in Laos and Thailand
      </a>
      , these are distinct but share related iconographic conventions.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Jamgon_Kongtrul" title="Jamgon Kongtrul">
       Jamgon Kongtrul
      </a>
      in his commentary on the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hevajra" title="Hevajra">
        Hevajra Tantra
       </a>
      </i>
      , the ornaments of
      <a href="/wiki/Wrathful_deities" title="Wrathful deities">
       wrathful deities
      </a>
      and witches made of human bones (Skt:
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       ahimudr
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">
       Wylie
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       rus pa'i rgyanl phyag rgya
      </i>
      ) are also known as mudra "seals".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_classical_dance">
       Indian classical dance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indian classical dance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_mudras_(dance)" title="List of mudras (dance)">
       List of mudras (dance)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_dance" title="Indian classical dance">
       Indian classical dance
      </a>
      , the term "Hasta Mudra" is used. The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Natya_Shastra" title="Natya Shastra">
        Natya Shastra
       </a>
      </i>
      describes 24 mudras, while the
      <i>
       Abhinaya Darpana
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Nandikeshvara" title="Nandikeshvara">
       Nandikeshvara
      </a>
      gives 28.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In all their forms of Indian classical dance the mudras are similar, though the names and uses vary. There are 28 (or 32) root mudras in
      <a href="/wiki/Bharata_Natyam" title="Bharata Natyam">
       Bharata Natyam
      </a>
      , 24 in
      <a href="/wiki/Kathakali" title="Kathakali">
       Kathakali
      </a>
      and 20 in
      <a href="/wiki/Odissi" title="Odissi">
       Odissi
      </a>
      . These root mudras are combined in different ways, like one hand, two hands, arm movements, body and facial expressions. In
      <a href="/wiki/Kathakali" title="Kathakali">
       Kathakali
      </a>
      , which has the greatest number of combinations, the vocabulary adds up to circa 900. Sanyukta mudras are mudras that use both hands and asanyukta mudras are mudras that use only one hand words.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Yogic_mudras">
       Yogic mudras
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yogic mudras">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_mudras_(yoga)" title="List of mudras (yoga)">
       List of mudras (yoga)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The classical sources for the mudras in yoga are the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gheranda_Samhita" title="Gheranda Samhita">
        Gheranda Samhita
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hatha_Yoga_Pradipika" title="Hatha Yoga Pradipika">
        Hatha Yoga Pradipika
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APMB1997_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-APMB1997-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Hatha Yoga Pradipika states the importance of mudras in yoga practice:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       Therefore the goddess sleeping at the entrance of Brahma's door should be constantly aroused with all effort, by performing mudra thoroughly.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In the 20th and 21st centuries, the yoga teacher
      <a href="/wiki/Satyananda_Saraswati" title="Satyananda Saraswati">
       Satyananda Saraswati
      </a>
      , founder of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bihar_School_of_Yoga" title="Bihar School of Yoga">
       Bihar School of Yoga
      </a>
      , continued to emphasize the importance of mudras in his instructional text
      <i>
       Asana, Pranayama, Mudr, Bandha
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APMB1997_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-APMB1997-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_mudra:_Chin_Mudr.C4.81">
       Basic mudra: Chin Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basic mudra: Chin Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JagadguruRamabhadracharya010.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="354" data-file-width="295" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/JagadguruRamabhadracharya010.jpg/200px-JagadguruRamabhadracharya010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/JagadguruRamabhadracharya010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/JagadguruRamabhadracharya010.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JagadguruRamabhadracharya010.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Rambhadracharya" title="Rambhadracharya">
         Rmabhadrcrya
        </a>
        meditating on the banks of the
        <a href="/wiki/Mandakini_River" title="Mandakini River">
         Mandakini River
        </a>
        with fingers folded in the chin mudr.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The thumb and forefinger on each of the hands are jointed, forming a zero. The rest of the fingers are extended. The hands are placed palms-up on the thighs or knees while sitting in
      <a href="/wiki/Vajrasana_(yoga)" title="Vajrasana (yoga)">
       vajrasana
      </a>
      . This mudr activates the
      <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">
       diaphragm
      </a>
      , making for deep "stomach-breathing" as the diaphragm pushes out the internal organs when it descends towards the pelvis on inhalation. Slow rhythmic breathing in a 5-2-4-2 rhythm (5 being the exhalation, and 4 is the inhalation) makes
      <a href="/wiki/Prana" title="Prana">
       prana
      </a>
      flow in the pelvis and in the legs.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_mudra:_Chinmaya_Mudr.C4.81">
       Basic mudra: Chinmaya Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Basic mudra: Chinmaya Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The thumb and forefinger are the same as in Chin Mudr. The rest of the fingers are folded into a fist. The non-folded part of the forefinger and the middle finger should still be touching. As in Chin Mudr, the hands are placed palms-up on the thighs while sitting in
      <a href="/wiki/Vajrasana_(yoga)" title="Vajrasana (yoga)">
       Vajrasana
      </a>
      . This mudra activates the ribs, making them expand sideways on inhalation. Slow rhythmic breathing in a 5-2-4-2 rhythm (5 being the exhalation, and 4 is the inhalation) makes
      <a href="/wiki/Prana" title="Prana">
       prana
      </a>
      flow in the torso and in the throat.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_mudra:_Adi_Mudr.C4.81">
       Basic mudra: Adi Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Basic mudra: Adi Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The thumb is folded into the palm, touching the base of the small finger. The rest of the fingers are folded over the thumb, to create a fist. The hands are placed palms-down on the thighs while sitting in
      <a href="/wiki/Vajrasana_(yoga)" title="Vajrasana (yoga)">
       Vajrasana
      </a>
      . This mudra activates the
      <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_major_muscle" title="Pectoralis major muscle">
       pectoral
      </a>
      muscles, making the chest expand forward on inhalation. Slow rhythmic breathing in a 5-2-4-2 rhythm (5 being the exhalation, and 4 is the inhalation) makes
      <a href="/wiki/Prana" title="Prana">
       prana
      </a>
      flow in the throat and in the head.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_compact_mudra_Brahma_Mudr.C4.81">
       Basic compact mudra Brahma Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Basic compact mudra Brahma Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The hands are as in Adi Mudr, but the palms face upwards and are located at the level of the navel, with the left and right knuckles and first finger joints touching. This is done while sitting in
      <a href="/wiki/Vajrasana_(yoga)" title="Vajrasana (yoga)">
       Vajrasana
      </a>
      . Breathing becomes full: in inhalation, the
      <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">
       diaphragm
      </a>
      descends, the ribs then expand, and then the
      <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_major_muscle" title="Pectoralis major muscle">
       pectoral
      </a>
      muscles move forward. Exhalation works in the same order, which creates a "wave" or ripple effect.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_compact_mudra:_Prana_Mudr.C4.81">
       Advanced compact mudra: Prana Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Advanced compact mudra: Prana Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A complicated mudra combining hand gestures, synchronized movement from gesture to gesture within the breath cycle, and meditation. The mudr is practiced sitting in
      <a href="/wiki/Siddhasana" title="Siddhasana">
       Siddhasana
      </a>
      . Even a single breath cycle of this mudra can significantly stimulate the body. It is described in the book, Theories of the Chakras, by
      <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_Motoyama" title="Hiroshi Motoyama">
       Hiroshi Motoyama
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Common_Buddhist_mudr.C4.81s">
       Common Buddhist mudrs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Common Buddhist mudrs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Abhaya_Mudr.C4.81">
       Abhaya Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Abhaya Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="602" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg/220px-%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg/330px-%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg/440px-%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%EA%B8%88%EB%8F%99%EC%97%B0%EA%B0%807%EB%85%84%EB%AA%85%EC%97%AC%EB%9E%98%EC%9E%85%EC%83%81.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">
         Korea
        </a>
        's National Treasure 119. The right hand shows abhaya mudra while the left is in the varada mudra.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Abhayamudra" title="Abhayamudra">
        Abhayamudra
       </a>
      </i>
      "gesture of fearlessness"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      represents protection, peace, benevolence and the dispelling of fear. In
      <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">
       Theravada Buddhism
      </a>
      , it is usually made with the right hand raised to shoulder height, the arm bent and the palm facing outward with the fingers upright and joined and the left hand hanging down while standing. In Thailand and Laos, this mudra is associated with the walking Buddha, often shown having both hands making a double abhaya mudra that is uniform.
     </p>
     <p>
      The mudra was probably used before the onset of Buddhism as a symbol of good intentions proposing friendship when approaching strangers. In
      <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">
       Gandharan
      </a>
      art, it is seen when showing the action of preaching. It was also used in China during the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">
       Wei
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sui_dynasty" title="Sui dynasty">
       Sui
      </a>
      eras of the 4th and 7th centuries.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The gesture was used by the Buddha when attacked by an elephant, subduing it as shown in several
      <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">
       frescoes
      </a>
      and scripts.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">
       Mahayana Buddhism
      </a>
      , the northern schools' deities often paired it with another mudra using the other hand.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bh.C5.ABmispar.C5.9Ba_Mudr.C4.81">
       Bhmispara Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bhmispara Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3230" data-file-width="3230" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg/220px-Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg/330px-Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg/440px-Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Buddha_sitting-MGR_Lyon-IMG_9878.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Buddha sitting in bhmispara mudr. Birmany. White marble with traces of polychromy.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman_Museum_of_Lyon" title="Gallo-Roman Museum of Lyon">
         Gallo-Roman Museum of Lyon
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       bhmispara
      </i>
      or "earth witness" mudra of
      <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">
       Gautama Buddha
      </a>
      is one of the most common iconic images of Buddhism. It depicts the Buddha sitting in meditation with his left hand, palm upright, in his lap, and his right hand touching the earth. It represents the Buddha asking
      <a href="/wiki/Prithvi" title="Prithvi">
       Prithvi
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Devi" title="Devi">
       devi
      </a>
      of the earth, that she witnessed his enlightenment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dharmacakra_Mudr.C4.81">
       Dharmacakra Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dharmacakra Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Dharmacakra
      </i>
      "wheel-turning"
      <i>
       mudr
      </i>
      represents the moment in the life of Buddha when he preached his first sermon after his Enlightenment in Deer Park in
      <a href="/wiki/Sarnath" title="Sarnath">
       Sarnath
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Dharmachakra" title="Dharmachakra">
       dharmachakra
      </a>
      or "turning of the wheel".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In general, only Gautama Buddha is shown making this mudr except
      <a href="/wiki/Maitreya" title="Maitreya">
       Maitreya
      </a>
      as the dispenser of the Law. Dharmacakra mudr is two hands close together in front of the chest in
      <a href="/wiki/Vitarka" title="Vitarka">
       vitarka
      </a>
      with the right palm forward and the left palm upward, sometimes facing the chest. There are several variants such as in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ajanta_Caves" title="Ajanta Caves">
       Ajanta Caves
      </a>
      frescoes, where the two hands are separated and the fingers do not touch. In the
      <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">
       Indo-Greek style
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">
       Gandhara
      </a>
      , the clenched fist of the right hand seemingly overlies the fingers joined to the thumb on the left hand. In pictorials of
      <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dry%C5%AB-ji" title="Hry-ji">
       Hry-ji
      </a>
      in Japan the right hand is superimposed on the left. Certain figures of
      <a href="/wiki/Amit%C4%81bha" title="Amitbha">
       Amitbha
      </a>
      , Japan are seen using this mudra before the 9th century.
     </p>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="626" data-file-width="850" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg/220px-KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg/330px-KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg/440px-KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KamakuraDaibutsu3969.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hands of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amitabha" title="Amitabha">
         Amitbha
        </a>
        statue at
        <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dtoku-in" title="Ktoku-in">
         Ktoku-in
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Kamakura,_Kanagawa" title="Kamakura, Kanagawa">
         Kamakura
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dhy%C4%81na_in_Buddhism" title="Dhyna in Buddhism">
        Dhyna
       </a>
       mudr
      </i>
      ("meditation mudra") is the gesture of meditation, of the concentration of the Good Law and the
      <a href="/wiki/Sangha" title="Sangha">
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        sagha
       </span>
      </a>
      . The two hands are placed on the lap, right hand on left with fingers fully stretched (four fingers resting on each other and the thumbs facing upwards towards one another diagonally), palms facing upwards; in this manner, the hands and fingers form the shape of a triangle, which is symbolic of the spiritual fire or the
      <a href="/wiki/Three_Jewels" title="Three Jewels">
       Triratna
      </a>
      (the three jewels). This mudra is used in representations of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">
       kyamuni Buddha
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Amit%C4%81bha" title="Amitbha">
       Amitbha
      </a>
      Buddha. Sometimes the Dhyna mudr is used in certain representations of
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
       Bhaiajyaguru
      </span>
      as the
      <a href="/wiki/Bhaisajyaguru" title="Bhaisajyaguru">
       Medicine Buddha
      </a>
      , with a medicine bowl placed on the hands. It originated in India most likely in the
      <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">
       Gandhra
      </a>
      and in China during the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">
       Wei
      </a>
      period. This mudra was used long before the Buddha as
      <a href="/wiki/Yogi" title="Yogi">
       yogis
      </a>
      have used it during their concentration, healing, and meditation exercises. It is heavily used in Southeast Asia in
      <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">
       Theravda
      </a>
      Buddhism; however, the thumbs are placed against the palms. (Dhyna mudr is also known as
      <i>
       Samdhi mudr
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Yoga mudr
      </i>
      ; Japanese:
      <i>
       J-in
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Jkai J-in
      </i>
      ; Chinese:
      <i>
       Ding Yin
      </i>
      .)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Varada_Mudr.C4.81">
       Varada Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Varada Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Varada mudr
      </i>
      "generosity gesture" signifies offering, welcome, charity, giving, compassion and sincerity. It is nearly always shown made with the left hand by a revered figure devoted to human salvation from greed, anger and delusion. It can be made with the arm crooked and the palm offered slightly turned up or in the case of the arm facing down the palm presented with the fingers upright or slightly bent. The Varada mudr is rarely seen without another mudra used by the right hand, typically abhaya mudr. It is often confused with vitarka mudr, which it closely resembles. In China and Japan during the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">
       Northern Wei
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Asuka_period" title="Asuka period">
       Asuka periods
      </a>
      , respectively, the fingers are stiff and then gradually begin to loosen as it developed over time, eventually leading to the
      <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">
       Tang dynasty
      </a>
      standard where the fingers are naturally curved.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      , varada mudra is used in images of
      <a href="/wiki/Avalokite%C5%9Bvara" title="Avalokitevara">
       Avalokitevara
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Gupta_Empire" title="Gupta Empire">
       Gupta Empire
      </a>
      (4th and 5th centuries). Varada mudr is extensively used in the statues of
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vajra_Mudr.C4.81">
       Vajra Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Vajra Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VajraMudra.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="990" data-file-width="667" height="327" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/VajraMudra.JPG/220px-VajraMudra.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/VajraMudra.JPG/330px-VajraMudra.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/VajraMudra.JPG/440px-VajraMudra.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VajraMudra.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Vajra Mudr
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Vajra" title="Vajra">
        Vajra
       </a>
       mudr
      </i>
      "thunder gesture" is the gesture of knowledge.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An example of the application of the Vajra mudr is the seventh technique (out of nine) of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kuji-in" title="Kuji-in">
        Nine Syllable Seals
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vitarka_Mudr.C4.81">
       Vitarka Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Vitarka Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VitarkaMudra.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="251" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/VitarkaMudra.JPG/220px-VitarkaMudra.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/VitarkaMudra.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/VitarkaMudra.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VitarkaMudra.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Vitarka mudr,
        <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">
         Tarim Basin
        </a>
        , 9th century
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Vitarka mudr
      </i>
      "mudra of discussion" is the gesture of discussion and transmission of Buddhist teaching. It is done by joining the tips of the thumb and the index together, and keeping the other fingers straight very much like the abhaya and varada mudrs but with the thumbs touching the index fingers. This mudra has a great number of variants in
      <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">
       Mahayana Buddhism
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">
       Tibetan Buddhism
      </a>
      , it is the mystic gesture of
      <a href="/wiki/Tara_(Buddhism)" title="Tara (Buddhism)">
       Trs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">
       bodhisattvas
      </a>
      with some differences by the deities in
      <a href="/wiki/Yab-Yum" title="Yab-Yum">
       Yab-Yum
      </a>
      . Vitarka mudr is also known as
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
        Prajliganabhinaya
       </span>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Vykhyna mudr
      </i>
      ("mudra of explanation").
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="J.C3.B1.C4.81na_Mudr.C4.81">
       Jna Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Jna Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Jna mudr
      </i>
      ("mudra of wisdom") is done by touching the tips of the thumb and the index together, forming a circle, and the hand is held with the palm inward toward the heart.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Karana_Mudr.C4.81">
       Karana Mudr
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Karana Mudr">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pressapochista14.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Pressapochista14.jpg/220px-Pressapochista14.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Pressapochista14.jpg/330px-Pressapochista14.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Pressapochista14.jpg/440px-Pressapochista14.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pressapochista14.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">
         Joseon Dynasty
        </a>
        figure on the left makes the Karana mudr.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Karana mudr
      </i>
      is the mudra which expels demons and removes obstacles such as sickness or negative thoughts. It is made by raising the index and the little finger, and folding the other fingers. It is nearly the same as the gesture known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corna" title="Corna">
       corna
      </a>
      in many 'western' countries, the difference is that in the Karana mudra the thumb does not hold down the middle and ring finger. (This mudra is also known as
      <i>
       Tarjan mudr
      </i>
      ; Japanese:
      <i>
       Funnu-in
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Acala" title="Acala">
        Fud
       </a>
       -in
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Martial_arts_and_mudra">
       Martial arts and mudra
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mudra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Martial arts and mudra">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Various
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_martial_arts#Asia" title="List of martial arts">
       Asian martial arts
      </a>
      forms contain positions identical to these mudras.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Muromoto (2003) in discussing his experience of mudra in relation to his martial arts training makes reference to
      <a href="/wiki/Mikky%C5%8D" title="Mikky">
       Mikky
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">
       Tendai
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shingon_Buddhism" title="Shingon Buddhism">
       Shingon Buddhism
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       One of the more curious things that I encountered in my martial arts training was the use of mudra in combative arts. Mudra (Japanese: in), for those who aren't familiar with them, are these weird hand gestures that are derived from esoteric Buddhism (mikkyo), particularly the Tendai and Shingon sects. These gestures are supposed to generate spiritual focus and power which then are manifested in some way externally.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Muromoto (2003) states a lineage of mudra in martial arts and evokes
      <a href="/wiki/Kory%C5%AB" title="Kory">
       Kory
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ry%C5%AB_(school)" title="Ry (school)">
       Ry
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kant%C5%8D_region" title="Kant region">
       Kant
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tenshin_Sh%C5%8Dden_Katori_Shint%C5%8D-ry%C5%AB" title="Tenshin Shden Katori Shint-ry">
       Tenshin Shden Katori Shint-ry
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="extiw" href="//fi.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risuke_%C5%8Ctake" title="fi:Risuke take">
       Risuke take
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Donn_F._Draeger" title="Donn F. Draeger">
       Donn F. Draeger
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       In any case, I had known of the use of mudra in koryu ("old" martial arts) since the time I was privy to a discussion with the training master of the Tenshin Shoden Katori Shinto-ryu, Otake Risuke, and the late Donn F. Draeger. Otake sensei described some of the mudra used in his school, which is one of the oldest martial ryu still in existence in Kanto (Eastern) Japan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In relation to charting a historical tributary to mudra within Japanese fighting culture, Muromoto (2003) incorporates
      <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">
       Shint
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">
       Samurai
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">
       Tokugawa shogunate
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Neo-Confucianism" title="Neo-Confucianism">
       Neo-Confucianism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">
       Zen
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">
       Kamakura shogunate
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">
       Edo
      </a>
      period,
      <a href="/wiki/Takuan_S%C5%8Dh%C5%8D" title="Takuan Sh">
       Takuan Sh
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hakuin_Ekaku" title="Hakuin Ekaku">
       Hakuin Ekaku
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       The use of mudra and other aspects of mikkyo are found in many instances in many koryu, because mikkyo and Shinto were the religions of the samurai who founded those ryu that were created before the 1600s. Subsequent ryu developed after the imposition of the Tokugawa government were heavily influenced by Neo-Confucianism, and then later by Zen Buddhism. Although Zen was popularized among the warrior class in the Kamakura period, the 1300s, it did not greatly affect martial arts until the latter part of the Edo Period, with the writings of the Zen priests Takuan and Hakuin. And even at that, Edo Period (1600-1868) martial arts were equally influenced by Neo-Confucianism and even, in the latter part, mystical Shinto.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Muromoto (2003) textually maps the execution of the
      <a href="/wiki/Knifehand_strike" title="Knifehand strike">
       knifehand strike
      </a>
      mudr:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Mikkyo uses mudra most often in combination with various rituals, chants and so on. One common mudra is that of the "knife hand," or shuto. The first two fingers are extended while the thumb and other fingers are clenched. If you look closely, you may see this movement subtlely hidden in some koryu kata, especially by old schools such as the Tenshin Shoden Katori Shinto-ryu, or in statues of divine Buddhist beings. This represents the sword of enlightenment, which cuts away all delusions. Sometimes the tips of the extended fingers are grasped in the fist of the other hand. There is a symbolic meaning for this, derived from mikkyo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007_14-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Muromoto.2C_Wayne_2007-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>

       Origins of Asian martial arts
