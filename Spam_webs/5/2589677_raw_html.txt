       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Ask Not What Your Artist Can Do For You
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/woman_Horizon_Comet_m.jpg">
          <img alt="woman_Horizon_Comet_m" class=" wp-image-566 alignleft" height="108" src="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/woman_Horizon_Comet_m.jpg" width="135"/>
         </a>
         Watchers of the Sky is a 2014 documentary about the work of Raphael Lemnik and Benjamin Ferencz. Lemnik coined the word genocide in order to make state murder a crime. Prior to that it was only a crime to kill citizens of foreign states. Ferencz was chief prosecutor in the Einsatzgruppen trials in Nuremberg.
        </p>
        <p>
         It wouldnt seem on the face of it that such serious matters as genocide would have any relationship to arts funding, but Ferencz told a story at the end of the documentary about Tycho Brahe, the medieval astronomer. Tycho had lobbied for funding, basically, from the King, to study the skies at his leisure. He wanted to map the stars. The King agreed and Tycho was set up in a place where he worked away for 25 years, collecting meticulous records of the movements of the stars and planets. When the King died and a new King came to the throne questions were asked about the funding of the peculiar old man watching the sky and the accountants were sent to ask Tycho to justify himself. Tycho Brahe said that his small contribution to understanding the universe, may not look like much, and may not have much, if any, immediate utilitarian value, but that he believed that some day there would be another person like himself, interested in watching the sky, and that Tychos data would be of enormous help to that person and would actually save them 25 years of labour, the 25 years Tycho had spent on the work at the Kings expense.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/kul_Tycho_16-11-201_697152a.jpg">
          <img alt="Fra: Tycho Brahe museet, Hven." class=" wp-image-555 alignleft" height="182" src="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/kul_Tycho_16-11-201_697152a-218x300.jpg" width="132"/>
         </a>
         Ferencz didnt say if Tycho Braes funding was extended, but the story defined the position Ferencz felt he occupied in his own seemingly pointless work to have war recognized as a crime. The story spoke to me in my own recent thoughts about art and commerce and the value to society, or otherwise, of artistic occupation and, more generally, the life of the imagination. It might seem like a stretch to suggest a connection between these things. Tycho Brahes case the recording of the movement of the stars did have demonstrable scientific utility, but, like the arts, it was also an act of imagination. The arts, far from being a luxury or a dilettantish affectation, are the more entertaining and extravagant expression of the core imaginative human mind.
        </p>
        <p>
         Far from having to defend and justify itself, as exampled in Tycho Brahes case, and as evidenced in current lobbying to have the arts considered important to normal people, the argument as presented between utility and the imagination seems primitive now. And yet at the moment, art and the imaginative life are being represented as a product requiring investment by the public, who become kind of shareholders in what was once a culture but is now a kind of company.
         <a href="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/went-terribly-wrong-edit.jpg">
          <img alt="went-terribly-wrong edit" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-567" height="126" src="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/went-terribly-wrong-edit-300x126.jpg" width="300"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Administrators of funds have offered the suggestion that artists should become more bureaucrattish to more ably compete for funding. Presumably if they dont, all arts funding will go to administrators and all artists will go to the wall, because when it comes to a race in form-filling the money just has to be on the seasoned bureaucrat.
        </p>
        <p>
         It is a little amazing that the worth of day-dreaming is still under question. The idea is maybe best encapsulated by the old story of the Hollywood mogul complaining that when he passed the writers building he heard no typing. Everyone understands the absurdity of that (I hope), and yet when it comes to the funding crunch, the imaginative arts are still regarded as useless, when, arguably, civilisation arose largely, not by the work of bureaucrats, but as a product of the imaginative human mind; the work of those who felt compelled, like Tycho Brahe, to watch the skies. Civilisation
         <em>
          is
         </em>
         a work of art. So, rather than artists becoming bureaucrats to lobby for a stipend, maybe it is time for the community to lobby the artists for some better ideas to help cultivate and expand the proven asset of human ingenuity and maybe have a little more art and a little less bureaucracy in the everyday.
        </p>
        <p>
         Technology has freed many people from labour. Capitalism increases profits by shedding workers, and in so doing it erodes itself when its effects plunge would-be consumers into poverty. But no one can quite get past the notion of paying people to apparently do nothing. But this is exactly what must be done. Those who have been freed by technological progress must be paid a universal basic income, if only to give the capitalist system some funds to chew on. The only thing preventing such a revolutionary change is, ironically, a lack of imagination.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/watchers-of-the-sky.png">
          <img alt="watchers-of-the-sky" class="aligncenter wp-image-554" height="190" src="http://eamonnfkelly.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/watchers-of-the-sky-300x167.png" width="341"/>
         </a>
         in 1969, a copy of the tables of Tycho Brahe accompanied the crew of Apollo 11 to the Moon.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
       </div>
