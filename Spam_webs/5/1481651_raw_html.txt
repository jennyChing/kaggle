           Discussant: Barry A. Franklin, PhD
          </p>

         <ul class="info-bar info-no-author-img">
          <li class="author-desc ">
           <h2>
            <span>
             by
            </span>
            <a href="mailto:p.brown@medpagetoday.com">
             Parker  Brown
            </a>
            <br/>
            <i>
             Staff Writer, MedPage Today
            </i>
           </h2>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div class="body-content">
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad" id="tablet-ad-49613">
           </div>
           <div class="action-points">
            <div class="ap-heading">
             <h4>
              Action Points
             </h4>
            </div>
            <div class="ap-content">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Note that this large meta-analysis demonstrates that increased sedentary time is associated with increased risks of death and hospitalization, regardless of reported physical activity.
              </li>
              <li>
               Be aware that, in all but one of the 41 studies analyzed, physical activity was determined by participant self-report.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           Greater sedentary time was associated with increased risk of adverse health outcomes including early death, even in those who otherwise exercised regularly, a meta-analysis indicated.
          </p>
          <p>
           With data pooled from 41 primary research studies, researchers found that long periods of sitting time were positively correlated with all-cause mortality and other outcomes, after adjustment for levels of physical activity, compared with study participants reporting relatively little sitting time:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            All-cause mortality (hazard ratio 1.240, 95% CI 1.09-1.41)
           </li>
           <li>
            Cardiovascular disease mortality (HR 1.179, 95% CI 1.106-1.257)
           </li>
           <li>
            Cardiovascular disease incidence (HR 1.143, 95% CI 1.002-1.729)
           </li>
           <li>
            Cancer mortality (HR 1.173, 95% CI 1.108-1.242)
           </li>
           <li>
            Cancer incidence (HR 1.130 95% CI 1.053-1.213)
           </li>
           <li>
            Type 2 diabetes incidence (HR 1.910, 95% CI 1.642-2.222)
           </li>
          </ul>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The risk was generally greater at lower levels of physical activity, according to
           <a href="http://www.uhnres.utoronto.ca/researchers/profile.php?lookup=56372" target="_blank">
            David A. Alter, MD, PhD
           </a>
           , at University Health Network in Toronto, and colleagues, writing in the Jan. 20 issue of
           <em>
            Annals of Internal Medicine.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           In an accompanying editorial,
           <a href="https://www.bakeridi.edu.au/research/physical_activity_behavioural_epidemiology/post_doc_fellows/" target="_blank">
            Brigid M. Lynch, PhD
           </a>
           , and
           <a href="http://www.bakeridi.edu.au/research/physical_activity_behavioural_epidemiology/lab_heads/" target="_blank">
            Neville Owen, PhD
           </a>
           , both of the Baker IDI Heart and Diabetes Institute in Melbourne, Australia, wrote, "The implications of these findings are far-reaching. Sedentary behavior is ubiquitous. Society is engineered, physically and socially, to be sitting-centric."
          </p>
          <p>
           "I was not surprised by this study," said
           <a href="http://physiology.med.wayne.edu/profile.php?id=63908" target="_blank">
            Barry Franklin, PhD
           </a>
           , the director of the Cardiac Rehabilitation Program at William Beaumont Hospital in Royal Oak, Mich., who was not affiliated with the work. "Recent studies have shown that for both work-related and household activities, the energy expenditure associated with those has decreased markedly over the last 100 years," he told
           <em>
            MedPage Today
           </em>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            The Future of Sitting
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Governments and professional organizations around the world are now warning that
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/PrimaryCare/ExerciseFitness/33709" target="_blank">
            too much sitting
           </a>
           can be hazardous to health, noted Lynch and Owen. But they also wrote that our understanding of how sitting affects cardiovascular disease is rudimentary.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "Understanding the mechanisms by which sedentary behavior increases risk for disease and the precise amounts of sitting that generate risk may enable us to more effectively characterize the most at-risk groups within the population," they said.
          </p>
          <p>
           To help fill the research gap, they proposed three areas of research. Isotemporal substitution modeling, which looks at the difference in associations when time spent doing one behavior is altered by another activity while time is constant, could be used to look at the relative advantages of doing activities other than sitting.
          </p>
          <p>
           In addition, future research should have objective activity monitoring, Lynch and Owen wrote. Most of the research done has been self-reported and looks at only one measure, like overall hours of sitting or total television viewing time. Researchers could use devices like inclinometers or accelerometers to track activity to reduce measurement error.
          </p>
          <p>
           Finally, future studies should look more closely at population subgroups. "It is unlikely that this risk is uniform across the population," wrote Lynch and Owen. But so far only weak associations between subgroups have been found.
          </p>
          <p>
           The meta-analysis included studies in which the sedentary time of adult participants was correlated to at least one health outcome independent of physical exercise. Sedentary behavior was defined as sitting, watching television, or lying down. Alter and colleagues excluded studies that reported indirect surrogate outcomes with unclear end points, like quality of life, metabolic biomarkers, and weight gain.
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The authors used a quality assessment tool to determine which studies to include. They ended up with 13 studies that analyzed outcomes for all-cause mortality, 14 for cancer incidence and mortality, nine for cardiovascular disease incidence and mortality, and five for type 2 diabetes incidence. No randomized, controlled trials met the selection criteria for the study.
          </p>
          <p>
           Alter and colleagues did find that physical activity moderated the adverse influence of long sedentary periods. In 10 studies that specifically reported joint effects of sedentary time, physical activity and health outcomes, the pooled hazard ratio for all-cause mortality was 1.16 (95% CI 0.84-1.59) with long versus short sedentary time in those with high levels of physical activity. In contrast, among those with low activity, the hazard ratio for all-cause mortality was 1.46 (95% CI 1.22-1.75) for long versus short sedentary periods  --  a 30 percentage-point reduction in risk, the researchers pointed out.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Limitations
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The authors noted several limitations to the study. Physical activity and sitting-time data were self-reported by study participants in all but one of the 41 studies. The authors did not have access to individual-level data and acknowledged the possibility of publication bias in their results which may have undermined the generalizability of their findings. There was statistical evidence of publication bias among studies reporting all-cause mortality and cancer incidence, but none for cardiovascular disease mortality or cancer mortality, the authors found.
          </p>
          <p>
           And the conclusions are limited because of the heterogeneity in how sitting, the primary exposure variable, was measured across the studies they analyzed.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "This has resulted in the pooling of risk estimates," wrote Lynch and Owen. "This may attenuate the overall effect because there is interstudy variation in the upper and lower values of the sedentary behavior categories assigned."
          </p>
          <p>
           Because of this variation, Lynch and Owen wrote, it's not clear how much sedentary behavior puts a person at risk for chronic disease.
          </p>
          <p>
           But Franklin said he thinks that the dangers of sitting should still be part of public health messaging. "When we talk about healthier diets, when we talk about no smoking, when we talk about a regular exercise program, we should also be including avoiding prolonged sedentary behaviors, sitting in front of a computer for hour after hour after hour," he said.
          </p>

          </div>
