      "Atrocious" treatment by medical staff who assumed a Wakatipu man was paralytically drunk when he collapsed after a brain haemorrhage has left his mother outraged  and searching for answers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alex Cottier, 21, was watching the Queenstown National Rugby Sevens tournament on January 8 when he suffered a potentially lethal brain bleed caused by an arteriovenous malformation  a congenital condition that caused acute bleeding in the right frontal lobe of his brain.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alex had been drinking beer and, although he cannot remember the collapse, only minutes before was happily chatting to Taranaki and New Zealand player and friend Ben Souness, who later said Alex was clearly not drunk.
     </p>
     <p>
      The condition first became apparent when another friend saw Alex vomit  a common reaction to an acute brain injury.
     </p>
     <p>
      He did not even know it had happened, but when it was pointed out he stood to clean himself up, and collapsed about 5pm.
     </p>
     <p>
      That was when the diagnosis process  or lack of it, began.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sevens with Altitude chairman Clark Frew yesterday said private doctors and sports medicine specialists were contracted to provide paramedic services to the Queenstown National Rugby Sevens tournament.
     </p>
     <p>
      "From everything I've heard, the sideline treatment procedure was shocking," Vanessa Cottier said. "A nurse who came to see what was happening said there were five people around Alex.
     </p>
     <p>
      "She thought something was seriously wrong and he should be immediately taken to hospital, but she was ignored because it was another 30 minutes until St John ambulance was even contacted to take Alex to hospital."
     </p>
     <p>
      Mrs Cottier, who was contacted by a friend of Alex's, assumed he would be taken to Queenstown's Lakes District Hospital, and left the family home in Arrowtown to go there straight away.
     </p>
     <p>
      "When I got there I was completely shocked to find Alex was still at the sevens. I then left the hospital immediately and still beat the ambulance there. When I got to Alex, there were all these people around him, and he was totally out to it and non-responsive.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Alex's friends who were with him the whole time said a paramedic was actually hitting Alex around the head to try and get a response.
     </p>
     <p>
      "All his friends fronted up and said 'yep, we've all been drinking,' but obviously none of them were paralytic drunk. They were all quiet and stunned over the way Alex had been treated when I got there, and still the paramedic was adamant that it was all drink related."
     </p>

"Thanks to incompetence at Lakes District Hospital, Alex did not get taken to Invercargill Hospital for a CT scan until Monday night, more than a full day after he collapsed. They kept on saying they didn't know what was happening with him and tried to fob me off with a 'most parents don't know what their children get up to' comment.
     </p>
     <p>
      "The assumption that it was alcohol-related was absolutely the issue in causing a huge delay, and it wasn't until the morning after, when Alex's condition hadn't changed, when their attitude started to change.
     </p>
     <p>
      "By noon it was decided to get him to Invercargill, but he wasn't put on an ambulance until 3.30pm and didn't arrive until after 7pm."
