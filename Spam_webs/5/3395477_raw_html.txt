     <p>
      <b>
       Syrah
      </b>
      (also known as
      <b>
       Shiraz
      </b>
      ) is a dark-skinned
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_variety" title="Grape variety">
       grape variety
      </a>
      grown throughout the world and used primarily to produce
      <a href="/wiki/Red_wine" title="Red wine">
       red wine
      </a>
      . DNA profiling in 1999 found Syrah to be the offspring of two obscure grapes from southeastern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/France_(wine)" title="France (wine)">
       France
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dureza" title="Dureza">
       Dureza
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mondeuse_blanche" title="Mondeuse blanche">
       Mondeuse blanche
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrah_WW_Origins_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Syrah_WW_Origins-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Syrah should not be confused with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petite_Sirah" title="Petite Sirah">
       Petite Sirah
      </a>
      , a synonym for
      <a href="/wiki/Durif" title="Durif">
       Durif
      </a>
      , a cross of Syrah with
      <a href="/wiki/Peloursin" title="Peloursin">
       Peloursin
      </a>
      dating from 1880.
     </p>
     <p>
      The style and flavor profile of wines made from Syrah is influenced by the climate where the grapes are grown with moderate climates (such as the northern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhone_Valley_(wine)" title="Rhone Valley (wine)">
       Rhone Valley
      </a>
      and parts of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walla_Walla_AVA" title="Walla Walla AVA">
       Walla Walla AVA
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_State_(wine)" title="Washington State (wine)">
       Washington State
      </a>
      ) tending to produce
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_(wine)" title="Body (wine)">
       medium to full-bodied
      </a>
      wines with medium-plus to high levels of tannins and flavors of blackberry, mint and black pepper notes. In hot climates (such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Barossa_Valley" title="Barossa Valley">
       Barossa Valley
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australia_(wine)" title="Australia (wine)">
       Australia
      </a>
      ), Syrah is more consistently full-bodied with softer tannin, jammier fruit and spice notes of liquorice, anise and earthy leather. In many regions the acidity and tannin levels of Syrah allows the wines produced from the grape to have favorable
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging_potential" title="Aging potential">
       aging potential
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSET_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSET-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Syrah is used as a
      <a href="/wiki/Varietal" title="Varietal">
       varietal
      </a>
      and is also blended. Following several years of strong planting, Syrah was estimated in 2004 to be the world's 7th most grown grape at 142,600 hectares (352,000 acres).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Vine_varieties_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Vine_varieties-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It can be found throughout the globe from France to
      <a href="/wiki/New_World_wine" title="New World wine">
       New World wine
      </a>
      regions such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chile_(wine)" title="Chile (wine)">
       Chile
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Africa_(wine)" title="South Africa (wine)">
       South Africa
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawke%27s_Bay" title="Hawke's Bay">
       Hawke's Bay
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_(wine)" title="New Zealand (wine)">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_(wine)" title="California (wine)">
       California
      </a>
      , Washington and in several Australian wine regions such as Barossa,
      <a href="/wiki/Coonawarra_wine_region" title="Coonawarra wine region">
       Coonawarra
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_Valley_(wine)" title="Hunter Valley (wine)">
       Hunter Valley
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margaret_River_(wine)" title="Margaret River (wine)">
       Margaret River
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/McLaren_Vale" title="McLaren Vale">
       McLaren Vale
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSET_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSET-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
       Origin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Syrah has a long documented history in the
      <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Rhne">
       Rhne
      </a>
      region of southeastern France, but it was not known if it had originated in that region. In 1998, a study conducted by
      <a href="/wiki/Carole_Meredith" title="Carole Meredith">
       Carole Meredith
      </a>
      's research group in the
      <a href="/wiki/UC_Davis_Department_of_Viticulture_and_Enology" title="UC Davis Department of Viticulture and Enology">
       Department of Viticulture and Enology
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis" title="University of California, Davis">
       University of California, Davis
      </a>
      used
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_typing" title="DNA typing">
       DNA typing
      </a>
      and extensive grape reference material from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Nationale_Sup%C3%A9rieure_Agronomique_de_Montpellier" title="cole Nationale Suprieure Agronomique de Montpellier">
       the viticultural research station in Montpellier
      </a>
      , France to conclude that Syrah was the offspring of the grape varieties
      <a href="/wiki/Dureza" title="Dureza">
       Dureza
      </a>
      (father) and
      <a href="/wiki/Mondeuse_blanche" title="Mondeuse blanche">
       Mondeuse blanche
      </a>
      (mother).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrah_WW_Origins_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Syrah_WW_Origins-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oz_pg_247_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oz_pg_247-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhones_Alpes_region_with_Ardeche,_Drome,_Isere_and_Savoie_highlighted.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="189" data-file-width="200" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Rhones_Alpes_region_with_Ardeche%2C_Drome%2C_Isere_and_Savoie_highlighted.png" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhones_Alpes_region_with_Ardeche,_Drome,_Isere_and_Savoie_highlighted.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Rhne-Alpes region. Dureza is believed to have originated in the Ardche (#1) in the southwest and moved east/northeast into Drme (#2) and Isre (#3). Somewhere in this area, most likely in Isre, the vine crossed with Mondeuse blanche, a variety native to the Savoie region (#4), to produce Syrah.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Dureza, a dark-skinned grape variety from the
      <a href="/wiki/Ard%C3%A8che" title="Ardche">
       Ardche
      </a>
      region in France, has all but disappeared from the vineyards, and the preservation of such varieties is a speciality of
      <a href="/wiki/Montpellier" title="Montpellier">
       Montpellier
      </a>
      . Mondeuse blanche is a white grape variety cultivated in the
      <a href="/wiki/Savoy" title="Savoy">
       Savoy
      </a>
      region, and is still found in very small amounts in that region's vineyards today. Both varieties are somewhat obscure today, and have never achieved anything near Syrah's fame or popularity, and there is no record of them ever having been cultivated at long distances from their present homes. Thus, both of Syrah's parents come from a limited area in southeastern France, very close to northern Rhne. Based on these findings, the researchers have concluded Syrah originated from northern Rhne.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrah_WW_Origins_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Syrah_WW_Origins-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The DNA typing leaves no room for doubt in this matter, and the numerous other hypotheses of the grape's origin which have been forwarded during the years all completely lack support in the form of documentary evidence or
      <a href="/wiki/Ampelography" title="Ampelography">
       ampelographic
      </a>
      investigations, be it by methods of classical
      <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">
       botany
      </a>
      or DNA. Instead, they seem to have been based primarily or solely on the name or synonyms of the variety. Because of varying
      <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
       orthography
      </a>
      for grape names, especially for old varieties, this is, in general, very thin evidence. Despite this, origins such as
      <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Sicily" title="Syracuse, Sicily">
       Syracuse
      </a>
      or the famous Iranian city of
      <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">
       Shiraz
      </a>
      have been proposed while the genomic studies yet to be done.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The parentage information, however, does not reveal how old the grape variety is, i.e., when the pollination of a Mondeuse blanche vine by Dureza took place, leading to the original Syrah seed plant. In the year AD 77,
      <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
       Pliny the Elder
      </a>
      wrote in his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">
        Naturalis Historia
       </a>
      </i>
      about the wines of
      <a href="/wiki/Vienne" title="Vienne">
       Vienne
      </a>
      (which today would be called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-R%C3%B4tie" title="Cte-Rtie">
       Cte-Rtie
      </a>
      ), where the
      <a href="/wiki/Allobroges" title="Allobroges">
       Allobroges
      </a>
      made famous and prized wine from a dark-skinned grape variety that had not existed some 50 years earlier, in
      <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">
       Virgil
      </a>
      's age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pliny called the vines of this wine
      <i>
       Allobrogica
      </i>
      , and it has been speculated that it could be today's Syrah. However, the description of the wine would also fit, for example, Dureza,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrah_WW_Origins_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Syrah_WW_Origins-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Pliny's observation that the vines of Allobrogica was resistant to cold is not entirely consistent with Syrah.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_name_Syrah">
       The name Syrah
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The name Syrah">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shiraz_Grapes.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Shiraz_Grapes.jpg/220px-Shiraz_Grapes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Shiraz_Grapes.jpg/330px-Shiraz_Grapes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Shiraz_Grapes.jpg/440px-Shiraz_Grapes.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shiraz_Grapes.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Clusters of Syrah grapes
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      It is called Syrah in its country of origin,
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      , as well as in the rest of
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
       Argentina
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
       Chile
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
       Uruguay
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      and most of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The name Shiraz became popular for this grape variety in
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      , where it has long been established as the most grown dark-skinned variety. In Australia, it was also commonly called
      <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_AOC" title="Hermitage AOC">
       Hermitage
      </a>
      up to the late 1980s, but since that name is also a French
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protected_designation_of_origin" title="Protected designation of origin">
       Protected designation of origin
      </a>
      , this naming practice caused a problem in some export markets and was dropped. The name Syrah for this grape variety is also commonly used in
      <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
       South Africa
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The grape's many other synonyms are used in various parts of the world, including Antourenein noir, Balsamina, Candive, Entournerein, Hignin noir, Marsanne noir, Schiras, Sirac, Syra, Syrac, Serine, and Sereine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jancis_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jancis-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Legends of Syrah's origins come from one of its homonyms - Shiraz.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Shiraz_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Shiraz-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because
      <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">
       Shiraz
      </a>
      , a very old city in
      <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
       Iran
      </a>
      , produced the well-known
      <a href="/wiki/Shirazi_wine" title="Shirazi wine">
       Shirazi wine
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Persia_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Persia-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      legends claim the Syrah grape originated in Shiraz and then was brought to Rhne.
     </p>
     <p>
      At least two significantly different versions of the myth are reported, giving different accounts of how the variety is supposed to have been brought from Shiraz to Rhne, and differing up to 1,800 years in dating this event. In one version, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phocaeans" title="Phocaeans">
       Phocaeans
      </a>
      could have brought Syrah/Shiraz to their colony around
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marseilles" title="Marseilles">
       Marseilles
      </a>
      (then known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marseilles" title="Marseilles">
       Massilia
      </a>
      ), which was founded around 600 BC. The grape would then later have made its way to northern Rhne, which was never colonized by the Phocaeans. No documentary evidence exists to back up this legend, and it also requires the variety to later vanish from the Marseilles region without leaving any trace.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The legend connecting Syrah with the city of Shiraz in Iran may, however, be of French origin.
      <a href="/wiki/James_Busby" title="James Busby">
       James Busby
      </a>
      wrote in
      <i>
       Journal of a recent visit to the principal vineyards of Spain and France
      </i>
      that the 1826 book
      <i>
       nologie Franaise
      </i>
      , "stated that, according to the tradition of the neighbourhood, the plant [Scyras] was originally brought from Shiraz in Persia, by one of the hermits of the mountain".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbjourn81_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jbjourn81-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the name Shiraz has been used primarily in Australia in modern times, while the earliest Australian documents use the spelling "Scyras", the name Shiraz has been speculated (among others by
      <a href="/wiki/Jancis_Robinson" title="Jancis Robinson">
       Jancis Robinson
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jancis_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jancis-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) to be in fact a so-called "
      <a href="/wiki/Strine" title="Strine">
       strinization
      </a>
      " of Syrah's name via Scyras. However, while the names Shiraz and Hermitage gradually seem to have replaced Scyras in Australia from the mid-19th century, the spelling Shiraz has also been documented in British sources back to at least the 1830s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jbjourn81_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-jbjourn81-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gentmag1834_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gentmag1834-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crhist_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-crhist-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      So, while the name or spelling Shiraz may be an effect of the
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English language
      </a>
      on a French name, there is no evidence that it actually originated in Australia, although it was definitely the Australian usage and the Australian wines that made the use of this name popular.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_legends">
       Other legends
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other legends">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Syrah_leaf.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="649" data-file-width="676" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Syrah_leaf.JPG/220px-Syrah_leaf.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Syrah_leaf.JPG/330px-Syrah_leaf.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Syrah_leaf.JPG/440px-Syrah_leaf.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Syrah_leaf.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Syrah leaf
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Another legend of the grape variety's origin, based on the name Syrah, is that it was brought from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy" title="Syracuse, Italy">
       Syracuse
      </a>
      by the legions of Roman Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius_Probus" title="Marcus Aurelius Probus">
       Probus
      </a>
      sometime after AD 280. This legend also lacks documentary evidence and is inconsistent with ampelographic findings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Rh.C3.B4ne-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_fame">
       Rise to fame
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rise to fame">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The wines that made Syrah famous were those from Hermitage, the hill above the town
      <a href="/wiki/Tain-l%27Hermitage" title="Tain-l'Hermitage">
       Tain-l'Hermitage
      </a>
      in northern Rhne, where an
      <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">
       hermitage
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">
       chapel
      </a>
      ) was built on the top, and where de Strimberg is supposed to have settled as a
      <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">
       hermit
      </a>
      after his crusades. Hermitage wines have for centuries had a reputation for being powerful and excellent. While Hermitage was quite famous in the 18th and 19th centuries, and attracted interest from foreign
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oenophile" title="Oenophile">
       oenophiles
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux_wine" title="Bordeaux wine">
       Bordeaux
      </a>
      enthusiast
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">
       Thomas Jefferson
      </a>
      , it lost ground and foreign attention in the first half of the 20th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Hermitage_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Hermitage-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 18th and the first half of the 19th centuries, most Hermitage wine that left France did so as a blending component in Bordeaux wines. In an era when "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claret" title="Claret">
       clarets
      </a>
      " were less powerful than today, and before appellation rules, red wines from warmer regions would be used for improvement (or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adulteration" title="Adulteration">
       adulteration
      </a>
      , depending on the point of view) of Bordeaux wines. While Spanish and Algerian wines are also known to have been used for this purpose, top Bordeaux chteaux would use Hermitage to improve their wines, especially in weaker vintages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oz_pg_247_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-oz_pg_247-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Adulteraion_and_fraud_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Adulteraion_and_fraud-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_in_Australia">
       Arrival in Australia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arrival in Australia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1831, the Scotsman
      <a href="/wiki/James_Busby" title="James Busby">
       James Busby
      </a>
      , often called "the Father of Australian viticulture", made a trip back to Europe to collect
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuttings_(vine)" title="Cuttings (vine)">
       cuttings
      </a>
      from vines (primarily from France and
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      ) for introduction to Australia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Busby_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Busby-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of the varieties collected by him was Syrah, although Busby used the two spellings "Scyras" and "Ciras". The cuttings were planted in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sydney_Botanical_Gardens" title="Sydney Botanical Gardens">
       Sydney Botanical Gardens
      </a>
      , and in
      <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Region" title="Hunter Region">
       Hunter Region
      </a>
      , and in 1839 brought from Sydney to
      <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">
       South Australia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Syrah_WW_Austrlia_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Syrah_WW_Austrlia-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the 1860s, Syrah was established as an important variety in Australia.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">
       Modern history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Syrah continues to be the main grape of the northern Rhne and is associated with classic wines such as
      <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_AOC" title="Hermitage AOC">
       Hermitage
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cornas_AOC" title="Cornas AOC">
       Cornas
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-R%C3%B4tie_AOC" title="Cte-Rtie AOC">
       Cte-Rtie
      </a>
      . In the southern Rhne, it is used as a blending grape in such wines as
      <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teauneuf-du-Pape" title="Chteauneuf-du-Pape">
       Chteauneuf-du-Pape
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gigondas" title="Gigondas">
       Gigondas
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4tes_du_Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Ctes du Rhne">
       Ctes du Rhne
      </a>
      , where
      <a href="/wiki/Grenache" title="Grenache">
       Grenache
      </a>
      usually makes up the bulk of the blend. Although its best incarnations will age for decades, less-extracted styles may be enjoyed young for their lively red and blueberry characters and smooth tannin structure. Syrah has been widely used as a blending grape in the red wines of many countries due to its fleshy fruit mid-palate, balancing the weaknesses of other varieties and resulting in a "complete" wine.
     </p>
     <p>
      From the 1970s and even more from the 1990s, Syrah has enjoyed increased popularity, and plantings of the variety have expanded significantly in both old and new locations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the early 2000s, it broke into the top 10 of varieties planted worldwide for the first time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Vine_varieties_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Vine_varieties-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Syrah_wines">
       Syrah wines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Syrah wines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boushey_Syrah.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Boushey_Syrah.JPG/220px-Boushey_Syrah.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Boushey_Syrah.JPG/330px-Boushey_Syrah.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Boushey_Syrah.JPG/440px-Boushey_Syrah.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boushey_Syrah.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A Syrah from Washington State
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Smaller amounts of Syrah are also used in the production of other wine styles, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ros%C3%A9_wine" title="Ros wine">
       ros wine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fortified_wine" title="Fortified wine">
       fortified wine
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Port_wine" title="Port wine">
       Port wine
      </a>
      style, and sparkling red wine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While Australian
      <a href="/wiki/Sparkling_Shiraz" title="Sparkling Shiraz">
       sparkling Shiraz
      </a>
      traditionally have had some sweetness, a number of Australian winemakers also make a full-bodied sparkling dry Shiraz, that contains the complexity and sometimes earthy notes that are normally found in still wine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to their concentrated flavours and high
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_tannins" title="Grape tannins">
       tannin
      </a>
      content, many premium Syrah wines are at their best after some considerable bottle aging. In exceptional cases, this may be 15 years or longer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Syrah has one of the highest recommended
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine_serving_temperature" title="Wine serving temperature">
       wine serving temperatures
      </a>
      at 18C (65F).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taste_and_flavours">
       Taste and flavours
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Taste and flavours">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Wines made from Syrah are often powerfully flavoured and full-bodied. The variety produces wines with a wide range of flavor notes, depending on the climate and soils where it is grown, as well as other
      <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">
       viticultural
      </a>
      practices chosen. Aroma characters can range from violets to berries (usually dark as opposed to red), chocolate, espresso and black pepper. No one aroma can be called "typical" though blackberry and pepper are often noticed. With time in the bottle these "primary" notes are moderated and then supplemented with earthy or savory "tertiary" notes such as leather and truffle. "Secondary" flavor and aroma notes are those associated with several things, generally winemakers' practices (such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine_barrel" title="Wine barrel">
       oak barrel
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">
       yeast
      </a>
      treatment).
     </p>
     <p>
      C13-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norisoprenoid" title="Norisoprenoid">
       Norisoprenoids
      </a>
      such as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=7,8-dihydroionone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="7,8-dihydroionone (page does not exist)">
       7,8-dihydroionone
      </a>
      derivatives, such as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Megastigmane-3,9-diol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Megastigmane-3,9-diol (page does not exist)">
       megastigmane-3,9-diol
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=3-oxo-7,8-dihydro-%CE%B1-ionol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="3-oxo-7,8-dihydro--ionol (page does not exist)">
       3-oxo-7,8-dihydro--ionol
      </a>
      , are found in Shiraz leaves.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Syrah_or_Shiraz_on_labels">
       Syrah or Shiraz on labels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Syrah or Shiraz on labels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1936" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg/220px-Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg/330px-Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg/440px-Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Walla_walla_Syrah.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A New World wine labeled as
        <i>
         Syrah
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Syrah-dominated
      <a href="/wiki/Appellation_d%27origine_contr%C3%B4l%C3%A9e" title="Appellation d'origine contrle">
       appellations
      </a>
      (AOCs) of northern Rhne have, like most other French appellations and regions, no tradition of
      <a href="/wiki/Varietal" title="Varietal">
       varietal
      </a>
      labeling of their wines. Indeed, such practices are generally disallowed under AOC rules, and only the AOC name (such as Cote-Rotie, Crozes-Hermitage or Hermitage) appears on the label. Varietal labeling of Syrah/Shiraz wines is therefore a practice which has emerged in the New World, and primarily in Australia.
     </p>
     <p>
      To confuse matters, in northern Rhne, different clones of genuine Syrah are referred to as
      <i>
       Petite
       <b>
        Syrah
       </b>
      </i>
      (small Syrah) or
      <i>
       Gros Syrah
      </i>
      (large Syrah) depending on the size of their berries, with
      <i>
       Petite Syrah
      </i>
      being considered the superior version, giving wines higher in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenolic_compounds_in_wine" title="Phenolic compounds in wine">
       phenolics
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As a general rule, most Australian and South African wines are labeled Shiraz, and most European wines (from such regions where varietal labeling is practiced) are labeled Syrah. In other countries, practices vary and winemakers (or wine marketers) sometimes choose either Syrah or Shiraz to signify a stylistic difference in the wine they have made. "Syrah"-labelled wines are sometimes thought to be more similar to classic Northern Rhne reds; presumably more elegant, tannic, smoke-flavoured and restrained with respect to their fruit component. "Shiraz"-labelled wines, on the other hand, would then be more similar to archetypical Australian or other New World examples; presumably made from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riper_berries_(wine)" title="Riper berries (wine)">
       riper berries
      </a>
      , more fruit-driven, higher in alcohol, less obviously tannic, peppery rather than smokey, usually more easily approached when young, and possibly slightly sweetish in impression. It must however be realized that this rule of thumb is unevenly applied.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oz_pg_250_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oz_pg_250-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Syrah_in_different_countries">
       Syrah in different countries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Syrah in different countries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Syrah is a variety that during the last few decades has been imported for cultivation in several countries. It is primarily grown in warmer regions. Worldwide plantations of Syrah have increased considerably in the late 1990s and early 2000s, and both Syrah-labelled and Shiraz-labelled wines are on the increase.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It is grown in many
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_wine-producing_regions" title="List of wine-producing regions">
       wine producing regions
      </a>
      around the world, with concentrations in Australia, The Rhone Valley in France, and the US. It is often used as a blending grape in Spain and Italy as well. It is also planted in Portugal, which favor making
      <a href="/wiki/Varietal" title="Varietal">
       varietal
      </a>
      Syrah wine, and not only blending with other types.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">
       Italy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Italy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Italy, Syrah is grown in Tuscany (i.e. Cortona DOC), Lazio (Pontine Agro), and Apulia (Syrah Tarantino IGT). Also a DOC Sirah Piedmont exists. The region though which has made one of the best uses of Syrah, by blending it with Nero d'Avola grapes, is Sicily. The wines thus obtained are mellow with spicy accents and are in great demand on the international market.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="France">
       France
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: France">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="816" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg/220px-Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg/330px-Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg/440px-Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vignobles_sur_la_colline_dominant_Tain_l%27Hermitage.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Vineyards on the Hermitage hill are dominated by Syrah.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Syrah, as it is known in France, is grown throughout the Rhne valley. The wines that are made from it vary greatly, even over small changes in the vines locations. The differences in the soil quality as well as the changes in the slope of the terrain tend to produce different styles of wine. Ranging from the mineral and tannic nature of Hermitage, to fruity and perfumed in the case of Cte-Rtie.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oz_pg_250_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-oz_pg_250-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Syrah is also a key component to many blends. It may be used to add structure and color to
      <a href="/wiki/Grenache" title="Grenache">
       Grenache
      </a>
      in southern Rhne blends, including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4tes-du-Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Ctes-du-Rhne">
       Ctes-du-Rhne
      </a>
      and Chteauneuf-du-Pape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jancis_Robinson_2003_g_152_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jancis_Robinson_2003_g_152-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Syrah is also the only red grape used in the wines of the northern Rhne.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1968, there existed only 2,700 hectares (6,700 acres) of Syrah vineyards in France, primarily in the traditional appellations of northern Rhne, which at that time had not received much attention in the wine world for several decades, and the vineyards of which were not planted to full capacity. After the wines of northern Rhne were "rediscovered" by wine writers in the 1970s, plantings expanded considerably. This trend received an extra boost in the 1980s and 1990s, when influential wine writer
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_M._Parker,_Jr." title="Robert M. Parker, Jr.">
       Robert M. Parker, Jr.
      </a>
      started to award very high scores, up to the "perfect" score of 100 points, to wines of some Rhne producers. The popularity of Australian Shiraz on the export market may also have played a role. 1988, total French plantings stood at 27,000 hectares (67,000 acres), and the 1999 viticultural survey found 50,700 hectares (125,000 acres) of Syrah vineyards. France thus has the world's largest plantations of Syrah.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While previously unused parts of the northern Rhne vineyards have been planted with Syrah as part of the expansion, the major part of the new French Syrah plantations are located in southern Rhne (which covers a much larger vineyard area than the northern part) and
      <a href="/wiki/Languedoc-Roussillon" title="Languedoc-Roussillon">
       Languedoc-Roussillon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While southern Rhne produces relatively few wines where Syrah is in the majority, the proportion of Syrah in the blended wines of this region has been on the rise. Languedoc-Roussillon uses Syrah to produce both Southern Rhne-like blends with Grenache, Australian-style blends with Cabernet Sauvignon, and varietal Syrah.
     </p>
     <p>
      Syrah vines in France often suffer from a form of dieback characterised by the leaves turning red in late summer, deep cracks developing in the stem above the
      <a href="/wiki/Grafting" title="Grafting">
       graft
      </a>
      , and the premature death of the vine. The syndrome was first observed in 1990s in the
      <a href="/wiki/Gard" title="Gard">
       Gard
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9rault" title="Hrault">
       Hrault
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">
       departments
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_of_France" title="South of France">
       Southern of France
      </a>
      , but is now widespread. It is believed to be caused by mismatch between the
      <a href="/wiki/Rootstock" title="Rootstock">
       rootstock
      </a>
      and scion rather than an infection by a fungus or a virus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Switzerland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Syrah grape was introduced into Switzerland in 1926 and in 2009 was the 6th most common red wine grape in Switzerland (181 hectars).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wine_Grapes_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wine_Grapes-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mostly grown in
      <a href="/wiki/Valais" title="Valais">
       Valais
      </a>
      , along the upper Rhne valley above
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">
       Lake Geneva
      </a>
      , it produces "unexpectedly concentrated wine from mature vines".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wine_Grapes_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wine_Grapes-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">
       Australia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Australia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Syrah grape was introduced into Australia in 1832 by
      <a href="/wiki/James_Busby" title="James Busby">
       James Busby
      </a>
      , an immigrant who brought vine clippings from Europe with him, and it is almost invariably called Shiraz.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oz_pg_247_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-oz_pg_247-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Today it is Australia's most popular red grape, but has not always been in such favour; in the 1970s, white wine was so popular that growers were ripping out unprofitable Shiraz and Grenache vineyards, even those with very
      <a href="/wiki/Old_vine" title="Old vine">
       old vines
      </a>
      . In the
      <a href="/wiki/Barossa_Valley_(wine)" title="Barossa Valley (wine)">
       Barossa Valley
      </a>
      , the world's oldest continually producing commercial vineyard is believed to be the Shiraz vines at
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey_Flat" title="Turkey Flat">
       Turkey Flat
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanunda" title="Tanunda">
       Tanunda
      </a>
      that were originally planted in 1847.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miscellany_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Miscellany-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many factors, including the success of brands like Lindemans (part of
      <a href="/wiki/Treasury_Wine_Estates" title="Treasury Wine Estates">
       Treasury Wine Estates
      </a>
      ) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacob%27s_Creek_(wine)" title="Jacob's Creek (wine)">
       Jacob's Creek
      </a>
      in the UK, as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Rosemount_(wine)" title="Rosemount (wine)">
       Rosemount
      </a>
      in the US and UK, were responsible for a dramatic expansion of plantings during the 1980s and 1990s; a similar trajectory occurred in California. However, the biggest factor in this expansion during the 1990s was a federal government tax subsidy to those planting new vineyards.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 2005-2006 growing season, total Shiraz plantations in Australia stood at 41,115 hectares (101,600 acres), of which 39,087 hectares (96,590 acres) were old enough to be productive. These vines yielded a total of 422,430 tonnes of Shiraz grapes for wine production. This made Shiraz the most planted variety in Australia,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Australia the world's second largest Syrah/Shiraz grower, after France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Victorian regions include Heathcote, roughly 1.5 hours north of Melbourne. Cooler climate regions such as Western Australia's
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_River" title="Margaret River">
       Margaret River
      </a>
      produce Shiraz with marginally less alcohol content and often in a more traditional French style.
     </p>
     <p>
      A well-known example of the Shiraz grape in Australian viticulture is the
      <a href="/wiki/Penfolds" title="Penfolds">
       Penfolds
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Penfolds_Grange" title="Penfolds Grange">
       "Grange"
      </a>
      . This wine was created by winemaker
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Schubert" title="Max Schubert">
       Max Schubert
      </a>
      in 1951, and has a reputation of aging well. The Penfolds Grange is predominantly Shiraz, but often includes a small quantity of Cabernet Sauvignon. It is usually a multi-regional blend of quality South Australian Shiraz, with the Barossa Valley playing an important role, and matured in new American Oak. Other well-known Australian Shiraz wines include, the
      <a href="/wiki/Henschke" title="Henschke">
       Henschke
      </a>
      "Hill of Grace" and the Penfolds "RWT".
     </p>
     <p>
      Recently, Australian Shiraz producers have started to add up to 4%
      <a href="/wiki/Viognier" title="Viognier">
       Viognier
      </a>
      to their Shiraz to add apricot tones to the wine's nose and palate. With such a small percentage added, the producer wasn't obliged to declare the blend on the label. In the past 5 years however, it's becoming increasingly fashionable to label the wine Shiraz Viognier as Viognier gains consumer acceptance in the market place. The practise of blending
      <a href="/wiki/Viognier" title="Viognier">
       Viognier
      </a>
      with Syrah has actually been common for years in the Northern Rhne Valley region of Cote-Rotie.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shiraz is also the "S" in "GSM" (Grenache-Shiraz-Mourvdre), which is common Australian designation for a Chteneuf-du-Pape-like blend.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">
       South Africa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: South Africa">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/South_African_wine" title="South African wine">
       South African
      </a>
      plantations have expanded significantly, from 1% of the vineyard area in 1995 to 9.7% in 2007
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      making up a total area under cultivation of 9,856 hectares (24,350 acres). In South Africa, the variety is predominately known as Shiraz, but the designation Syrah is used for "Rhne-style" wines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Shiraz_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Shiraz-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some see this variety as the "great hope" for South African wines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platters_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Platters-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
       United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg/220px-Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg/330px-Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg/440px-Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Original_1986_Syrah_planting_at_Red_Willow.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The first planting of Syrah in Washington State was done at Red Willow Vineyards in 1986.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the United States, wine produced from the grape is normally called by its French name, Syrah. However, in cases where winemakers choose to follow a New World style, similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Penfolds_Grange" title="Penfolds Grange">
       Penfolds Grange
      </a>
      , they may choose to label their wines as Shiraz.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oz_pg_250_23-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-oz_pg_250-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Under American wine laws, either name may appear on the label. Syrah first appeared as a wine grape in California in the 1970s, where it was planted by a group of viticulturists who called themselves "Rhne rangers."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jancis_Robinson_2003_g_152_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jancis_Robinson_2003_g_152-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although most plantings of the grape are in California, there are increasing amounts of it being grown in Washington state. Syrah makes up a significant percentage of wine produced in several Washington AVA's such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Naches_Heights_AVA" title="Naches Heights AVA">
       Naches Heights AVA
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walla_Walla_AVA" title="Walla Walla AVA">
       Walla Walla AVA
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Washington_Syrah.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1936" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Washington_Syrah.jpg/220px-Washington_Syrah.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Washington_Syrah.jpg/330px-Washington_Syrah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Washington_Syrah.jpg/440px-Washington_Syrah.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Washington_Syrah.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A Washington Syrah from the Columbia Valley AVA.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      California Syrahs, much like those in France, vary a great deal based on the climate and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Terroir" title="Terroir">
        terroir
       </a>
      </i>
      that they inhabit. In exceptionally warm regions, such as parts of Napa, the wine is often blended with other Rhne varieties. Other appellations, primarily mountainous ones, tend to produce varietal-based wines that can stand on their own.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Syrah was introduced into Washington state in 1986 by
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Willow_Vineyard" title="Red Willow Vineyard">
       Red Willow Vineyard
      </a>
      through their partnership with
      <a href="/wiki/Woodinville,_Washington" title="Woodinville, Washington">
       Woodinville, Washington
      </a>
      based
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Winery" title="Columbia Winery">
       Columbia Winery
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Wine" title="Master of Wine">
       Master of Wine
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Lake_(wine)" title="David Lake (wine)">
       David Lake
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregutt_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gregutt-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Expanding at a significant rate, it is used to produce single varietial wines as well as being blended with grapes such as
      <a href="/wiki/Grenache" title="Grenache">
       Grenache
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinsault" title="Cinsault">
       Cinsault
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Viognier" title="Viognier">
       Viognier
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">
       Argentina
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Argentina">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Syrah plantations in
      <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
       Argentina
      </a>
      increased from less than 1,000 hectares (2,500 acres) in 1990 to 9,500 hectares (23,000 acres) in 2002.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Syrah has occasionally been used as a blending component with Argentina's signature dark-skinned grape
      <a href="/wiki/Malbec" title="Malbec">
       Malbec
      </a>
      to provide an "Argentinian take" on the Australian Cabernet-Shiraz blend.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">
       Chile
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Chile">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Around 2005, there were 2,500 hectares (6,200 acres) of Syrah in
      <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
       Chile
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCW_Syrah_7-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-OCW_Syrah-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Durif">
       Durif
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syrah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Durif">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Durif" title="Durif">
       Durif
      </a>
      , or Petite Sirah, is a descendant of Syrah and
      <a href="/wiki/Peloursin" title="Peloursin">
       Peloursin
      </a>
      . It was propagated by and named after Dr. Francois Durif. Durif is a cross breed between the lesser known Peloursin and Syrah. Regions such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rutherglen,_Victoria" title="Rutherglen, Victoria">
       Rutherglen
      </a>
      have achieved international regard for their Durif. This varietal is similar to Shiraz, but even more full bodied and tannic because of the smaller, thicker skinned grapes used. Petite Sirah is widely planted in California, where it is popular as a varietal (for example, by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bogle_Vineyards&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bogle Vineyards (page does not exist)">
       Bogle Vineyards
      </a>
      ), and especially blended with
      <a href="/wiki/Zinfandel" title="Zinfandel">
       Zinfandel
      </a>
      , which results in a rich, bold and fruity wine.
     </p>
