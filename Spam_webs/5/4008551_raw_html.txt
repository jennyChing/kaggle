     <p>
      <b>
       Upton Beall Sinclair, Jr.
      </b>
      (September 20, 1878  November 25, 1968) was an American author who wrote nearly 100 books and other works across a number of genres. Sinclair's work was well-known and popular in the first half of the twentieth century, and he won the
      <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Fiction" title="Pulitzer Prize for Fiction">
       Pulitzer Prize for Fiction
      </a>
      in 1943.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1906, Sinclair acquired particular fame for his classic
      <a href="/wiki/Muckraker" title="Muckraker">
       muckraking
      </a>
      novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">
        The Jungle
       </a>
      </i>
      , which exposed conditions in the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Meat_packing_industry" title="Meat packing industry">
       meat packing industry
      </a>
      , causing a public uproar that contributed in part to the passage a few months later of the 1906
      <a href="/wiki/Pure_Food_and_Drug_Act" title="Pure Food and Drug Act">
       Pure Food and Drug Act
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meat_Inspection_Act" title="Meat Inspection Act">
       Meat Inspection Act
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1919, he published
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Brass_Check" title="The Brass Check">
        The Brass Check
       </a>
      </i>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Muckraker" title="Muckraker">
       muckraking
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expos%C3%A9_(journalism)" title="Expos (journalism)">
       expos
      </a>
      of American journalism that publicized the issue of
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow_journalism" title="Yellow journalism">
       yellow journalism
      </a>
      and the limitations of the free press in the United States. Four years after publication of
      <i>
       The Brass Check
      </i>
      , the first code of ethics for journalists was created.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Time
      </i>
      magazine called him "a man with every gift except humor and silence."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timebelle_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-timebelle-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is remembered for writing the famous line: "It is difficult to get a man to understand something, when his salary depends upon his not understanding it."
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair was an outspoken socialist and ran unsuccessfully for Congress as a nominee from the
      <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Party of America">
       Socialist Party
      </a>
      . He was also the
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">
       Democratic Party
      </a>
      candidate for
      <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_California" title="Governor of California">
       Governor of California
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
      , but was defeated in the
      <a href="/wiki/California_gubernatorial_election,_1934" title="California gubernatorial election, 1934">
       1934 elections
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">
       Early life and education
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sinclair was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">
       Baltimore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">
       Maryland
      </a>
      to Upton Beall Sinclair and Priscilla Harden. His father was a liquor salesman whose
      <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">
       alcoholism
      </a>
      shadowed his son's childhood. Priscilla Harden Sinclair was a strict Episcopalian who disliked alcohol, tea, and coffee. As a child, Sinclair slept either on sofas or cross-ways on his parents' bed. When his father was out for the night, he would sleep alone in the bed with his mother.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sinclair did not get along with her when he became older because of her strict rules and refusal to allow him independence. Sinclair later told his son, David, that around Sinclair's 16th year, he decided not to have anything to do with his mother, staying away from her for 35 years because an argument would start if they met.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His mother's family was highly affluent: her parents were very prosperous in Baltimore, and her sister married a millionaire. Sinclair had wealthy maternal grandparents with whom he often stayed. This gave him insight into how both the rich and the poor lived during the late nineteenth century. Living in two social settings affected him and greatly influenced his books. Upton Beall Sinclair, Sr. was from a highly respected family in the South, but due to the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       Civil War
      </a>
      and disruptions of the labor system during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era" title="Reconstruction era">
       Reconstruction era
      </a>
      , as well as an extended agricultural depression, the family's wealth evaporated, and the family was financially ruined.
     </p>
     <p>
      As he was growing up, Upton's family moved frequently as his father was not successful in his career. He became close with Reverend William Wilmerding Moir. Moir specialized in sexual abstinence and taught his beliefs to Sinclair. He was taught to "avoid the subject of sex." Sinclair was to report to Moir monthly regarding his abstinence. Despite their close relationship, Sinclair identified as agnostic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sinclair, Jr. developed a love for reading at age five years. He read every book his mother owned for a deeper understanding of the world. He did not start school until he was ten years old. He was deficient in math and worked hard to catch up quickly because of his embarrassment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1888, the Sinclair family moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">
       Queens
      </a>
      , New York, where his father sold shoes. Sinclair, Jr. entered the
      <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_New_York" title="City College of New York">
       City College of New York
      </a>
      five days before his 14th birthday,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      on September 15, 1892.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He wrote jokes,
      <a href="/wiki/Dime_novel" title="Dime novel">
       dime novels
      </a>
      , and magazine articles in boys' weekly and
      <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">
       pulp magazines
      </a>
      to pay for his tuition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Cosmopolitan_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Cosmopolitan-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was able to move his parents to an apartment when he was seventeen years old with that income.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He graduated in June 1897 and studied for a time at
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His major was Law, but he was more interested in writing, and he learned several languages including Spanish, German and French. He paid the one-time enrollment fee to be able to learn a variety of different things. He would sign up for a class and then later drop it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He supported himself through college by writing boys' adventure stories and jokes. He also sold ideas to cartoonists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Using stenographers, he wrote up to 8,000 words of pulp fiction per day. His only complaint about his educational experience was that it failed to educate him about socialism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After leaving Columbia, he wrote four books in the next four years; they were commercially unsuccessful though critically well-received:
      <i>
       King Midas
      </i>
      (1901),
      <i>
       Prince Hagen
      </i>
      (1902),
      <i>
       The Journal of Arthur Stirling
      </i>
      (1903), and a Civil War novel titled
      <i>
       Manassas
      </i>
      (1904).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Upton Sinclair imagined himself a poet and dedicated his time to writing poetry initially.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>

     <p>
      In 1904, Sinclair spent seven weeks in disguise, working undercover in Chicago's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meatpacking" title="Meatpacking">
       meatpacking
      </a>
      plants to research his novel,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">
        The Jungle
       </a>
      </i>
      (1906), a political expos that addressed conditions in the plants as well as the lives of poor immigrants. When it was published two years later, it became a bestseller.
     </p>
     <p>
      With the income from
      <i>
       The Jungle
      </i>
      , Sinclair founded the utopians
      <a href="/wiki/Helicon_Home_Colony" title="Helicon Home Colony">
       Helicon Home Colony
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Englewood,_New_Jersey" title="Englewood, New Jersey">
       Englewood, New Jersey
      </a>
      . He ran as a Socialist candidate for Congress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The colony burned down under suspicious circumstances within a year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the spring of 1905, Sinclair issued a call for the formation of a new organization, a group to be called the
      <a href="/wiki/Intercollegiate_Socialist_Society" title="Intercollegiate Socialist Society">
       Intercollegiate Socialist Society
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler16_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Laidler16-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <br/>
      The Sinclairs moved to California in the 1920s and lived there for nearly four decades. During his years with his second wife, Mary Craig, Sinclair wrote or produced several films. Recruited by
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">
       Charlie Chaplin
      </a>
      , Sinclair and Mary Craig produced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_M._Eisenstein" title="Sergei M. Eisenstein">
       Eisenstein's
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%A1Qu%C3%A9_viva_M%C3%A9xico!" title="Qu viva Mxico!">
        Qu viva Mxico!
       </a>
      </i>
      in 193032.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Late in life Sinclair, with his third wife Mary Willis, moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Buckeye,_Arizona" title="Buckeye, Arizona">
       Buckeye, Arizona
      </a>
      . They returned East to
      <a href="/wiki/Bound_Brook,_New_Jersey" title="Bound Brook, New Jersey">
       Bound Brook, New Jersey
      </a>
      . Sinclair died there in a nursing home on November 25, 1968, a year after his wife.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is buried in
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_Creek_Cemetery" title="Rock Creek Cemetery">
       Rock Creek Cemetery
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
       Washington, D.C.
      </a>
      , next to Willis.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_interests">
       Other interests
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other interests">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Aside from his political and social writings, Sinclair took an interest in
      <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">
       occult
      </a>
      phenomena and experimented with
      <a href="/wiki/Telepathy" title="Telepathy">
       telepathy
      </a>
      . His book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mental_Radio" title="Mental Radio">
        Mental Radio
       </a>
      </i>
      (1930) included accounts of his wife Mary's telepathic experiences and ability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/William_McDougall_(psychologist)" title="William McDougall (psychologist)">
       William McDougall
      </a>
      read the book and wrote an introduction to it, which led him to establish the
      <a href="/wiki/Parapsychology" title="Parapsychology">
       parapsychology
      </a>
      department at
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">
       Duke University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">
       Political career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the 1920s, the Sinclairs moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Monrovia,_California" title="Monrovia, California">
       Monrovia, California
      </a>
      , near Los Angeles, where Sinclair founded the state's chapter of the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">
       American Civil Liberties Union
      </a>
      (ACLU). Wanting to pursue politics, he twice ran unsuccessfully for United States Congress on the
      <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_America" title="Socialist Party of America">
       Socialist
      </a>
      ticket: in 1920 for the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">
       House of Representatives
      </a>
      and in 1922 for the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">
       Senate
      </a>
      . During this period, Sinclair was also active in radical politics in Los Angeles. For instance, in 1923, to support the challenged
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_speech" title="Free speech">
       free speech
      </a>
      rights of
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">
       Industrial Workers of the World
      </a>
      , Sinclair spoke at a rally during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1923_san_pedro_maritime_strike" title="1923 san pedro maritime strike">
       San Pedro Maritime Strike
      </a>
      , in a neighborhood now known as Liberty Hill. He began to read from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights" title="Bill of Rights">
       Bill of Rights
      </a>
      and was promptly arrested, along with hundreds of others, by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LAPD" title="LAPD">
       LAPD
      </a>
      . The arresting officer proclaimed: "We'll have none of that Constitution stuff".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1934, Sinclair ran in the
      <a href="/wiki/California_gubernatorial_election,_1934" title="California gubernatorial election, 1934">
       California gubernatorial election
      </a>
      as a
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">
       Democrat
      </a>
      . Gaining 879,000 votes made this his most successful run for office, but incumbent Governor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_F._Merriam" title="Frank F. Merriam">
       Frank F. Merriam
      </a>
      defeated him by a sizable margin,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      gaining 1,138,000 votes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sinclair's platform, known as the
      <a href="/wiki/End_Poverty_in_California_movement" title="End Poverty in California movement">
       End Poverty in California movement
      </a>
      (EPIC), galvanized the support of the Democratic Party, and Sinclair gained its nomination.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair's plan to end poverty quickly became a controversial issue under the pressure of numerous migrants to California fleeing the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dust_bowl" title="Dust bowl">
       Dust bowl
      </a>
      . Conservatives considered his proposal an attempted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">
       communist
      </a>
      takeover of their state and quickly opposed him, using propaganda to portray Sinclair as a staunch communist. Sinclair had been a member of the Socialist Party from 1902 to 1934, when he became a Democrat, though always considering himself a Socialist in spirit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the same time, American and
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet
      </a>
      communists disassociated themselves from him, considering him a capitalist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Science fiction author
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
       Robert A. Heinlein
      </a>
      was deeply involved in Sinclair's campaign, although he attempted to move away from the stance later in his life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After his loss to Merriam, Sinclair abandoned EPIC and politics to return to writing. In 1935, he published
      <i>
       I, Candidate for Governor: And How I Got Licked
      </i>
      , in which he described the techniques employed by Merriam's supporters, including the then popular
      <a href="/wiki/Aimee_Semple_McPherson" title="Aimee Semple McPherson">
       Aimee Semple McPherson
      </a>
      , who vehemently opposed socialism and what she perceived as Sinclair's
      <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">
       modernism
      </a>
      . Sinclair's line from this book "It is difficult to get a man to understand something, when his salary depends upon his not understanding it" has become well known and was for example quoted by
      <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">
       Al Gore
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/An_Inconvenient_Truth" title="An Inconvenient Truth">
       An Inconvenient Truth
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Of his gubernatorial bid, Sinclair remarked in 1951:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       The American People will take Socialism, but they won't take the label. I certainly proved it in the case of EPIC. Running on the Socialist ticket I got 60,000 votes, and running on the slogan to 'End Poverty in California' I got 879,000. I think we simply have to recognize the fact that our enemies have succeeded in spreading the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Lie" title="Big Lie">
        Big Lie
       </a>
       . There is no use attacking it by a front attack, it is much better to out-flank them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         33
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In April of 1900, Sinclair went to Lake Massawippi in Quebec to work on a novel. He had a small cabin rented for three months and then he moved to a farmhouse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was here he and his future Wife, Meta Fuller, became close. She was three years younger than he and had aspirations of being more than a housewife. Sinclair gave her direction as to what to read and learn.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Meta had been a childhood friend and whose family was one of the
      <a href="/wiki/First_Families_of_Virginia" title="First Families of Virginia">
       First Families of Virginia
      </a>
      . Each had warned the other about themselves and would later bring that up in arguments. They married October 18, 1900.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They used abstinence as their main form of birth control. They became pregnant with a child shortly after they married and attempted to abort it multiples times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The child was born on December 1, 1901, and named David.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArthur2006_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArthur2006-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2013)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Meta and her family tried to get Sinclair to give up writing and get "a job that would support his family."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Around 1911, Meta left Sinclair for the poet
      <a href="/wiki/Harry_Kemp" title="Harry Kemp">
       Harry Kemp
      </a>
      , later known as the "Dunes Poet" of
      <a href="/wiki/Provincetown,_Massachusetts" title="Provincetown, Massachusetts">
       Provincetown
      </a>
      , Massachusetts.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1913, Sinclair married
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Craig_Sinclair" title="Mary Craig Sinclair">
       Mary Craig Kimbrough
      </a>
      (18831961), a woman from an elite
      <a href="/wiki/Greenwood,_Mississippi" title="Greenwood, Mississippi">
       Greenwood
      </a>
      , Mississippi, family. She had written articles and a book on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winnie_Davis" title="Winnie Davis">
       Winnie Davis
      </a>
      , the daughter of Confederate States of America President
      <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Davis" title="Jefferson Davis">
       Jefferson Davis
      </a>
      . He met her when she attended a lecture by him about
      <i>
       The Jungle
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArthur2006132.E2.80.9333_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArthur2006132.E2.80.9333-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the 1920s, the Sinclair couple moved to California. They were married until her death in 1961.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair married again, to Mary Elizabeth Willis (18821967).
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair was opposed to sex outside of marriage and he viewed marital relations as necessary only for procreation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArthur200696.E2.80.9397_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArthur200696.E2.80.9397-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArthur200646.E2.80.9347_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArthur200646.E2.80.9347-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite his beliefs, he had an adulterous affair with Anna Noyes during his marriage to Meta. He wrote a novel about the affair called
      <i>
       Love's Progress
      </i>
      , a sequel to
      <i>
       Love's Pilgrimage
      </i>
      . It was never published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArthur2006109_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArthur2006109-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArthur2006111.E2.80.9312_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArthur2006111.E2.80.9312-44">

      <i>
       Mammonart
      </i>
      , he suggested that
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">
       Writing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Sinclair devoted his writing career to documenting and criticizing the social and economic conditions of the early twentieth century in both fiction and non-fiction. He exposed his view of the injustices of capitalism and the overwhelming effects of poverty among the working class. He also edited collections of fiction and non-fiction.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Jungle">
       <i>
        The Jungle
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Jungle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      His novel based on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meatpacking_industry_in_Chicago" title="Meatpacking industry in Chicago">
       meatpacking industry in Chicago
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">
        The Jungle
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      was first published in serial form in the socialist newspaper
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Appeal_to_Reason_(newspaper)" title="Appeal to Reason (newspaper)">
        Appeal to Reason
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      from February 25, 1905 to November 4, 1905. It was published as a book by
      <a href="/wiki/Doubleday_(publisher)" title="Doubleday (publisher)">
       Doubleday
      </a>
      in 1906.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair had spent about six months investigating the Chicago meatpacking industry for
      <i>
       Appeal to Reason
      </i>
      , work which inspired his novel. He intended to "set forth the breaking of human hearts by a system which exploits the labor of men and women for profit".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The novel featured Jurgis Rudkus, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithuanian_people" title="Lithuanian people">
       Lithuanian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant">
       immigrant
      </a>
      who works in a meat factory in Chicago, his teenage wife Ona Lukoszaite, and their extended family. Sinclair portrays their mistreatment by Rudkus' employers and the wealthier elements of society. His descriptions of the unsanitary and inhumane conditions that workers suffered served to shock and galvanize readers.
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_London" title="Jack London">
       Jack London
      </a>
      called Sinclair's book "the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">
        Uncle Tom's Cabin
       </a>
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Wage_slavery" title="Wage slavery">
       wage slavery
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Domestic and foreign purchases of American meat fell by half.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair wrote in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_Magazine" title="Cosmopolitan Magazine">
        Cosmopolitan Magazine
       </a>
      </i>
      in October 1906 about
      <i>
       The Jungle
      </i>
      : "I aimed at the public's heart, and by accident I hit it in the stomach."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timebelle_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-timebelle-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The novel brought public lobbying for Congressional legislation and government regulation of the industry, including passage of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meat_Inspection_Act" title="Meat Inspection Act">
       Meat Inspection Act
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Pure_Food_and_Drug_Act" title="Pure Food and Drug Act">
       Pure Food and Drug Act
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the time, President
      <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">
       Theodore Roosevelt
      </a>
      characterized Sinclair as a "crackpot",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      writing to
      <a href="/wiki/William_Allen_White" title="William Allen White">
       William Allen White
      </a>
      , "I have an utter contempt for him. He is hysterical, unbalanced, and untruthful. Three-fourths of the things he said were absolute falsehoods. For some of the remainder there was only a basis of truth."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After reading
      <i>
       The Jungle,
      </i>
      Roosevelt agreed with some of Sinclair's conclusions but was opposed to legislation that he considered "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">
       socialist
      </a>
      ." He said, "Radical action must be taken to do away with the efforts of arrogant and selfish greed on the part of the capitalist."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Brass_Check">
       <i>
        The Brass Check
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Brass Check">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Brass_Check" title="The Brass Check">
        The Brass Check
       </a>
      </i>
      (1919), Sinclair made a systematic and incriminating critique of the severe limitations of the 
      <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">
       free press
      </a>
       in the United States. Among the topics covered is the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow_journalism" title="Yellow journalism">
       yellow journalism
      </a>
      techniques created by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">
       William Randolph Hearst
      </a>
      . Sinclair called
      <i>
       The Brass Check
      </i>
      "the most important and most dangerous book I have ever written."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lanny_Budd_series">
       Lanny Budd series
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lanny Budd series">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Between 1940 and 1953, Sinclair wrote a series of 11 novels featuring a central character named Lanny Budd. The son of an American arms manufacturer, Budd is portrayed as holding in the confidence of world leaders, and not simply witnessing events but often propelling them. As a sophisticated socialite, who mingles easily with people from all cultures and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic" title="Socioeconomic">
       socioeconomic
      </a>
      classes, Budd has been characterized as the antithesis of the stereotyped "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ugly_American_(epithet)" title="Ugly American (epithet)">
       Ugly American
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair placed Budd within the important political events in the United States and Europe in the first half of the twentieth century. (There was an actual company named the
      <a href="/wiki/Budd_Company" title="Budd Company">
       Budd Company
      </a>
      which manufactured arms during World War II, founded by
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_G._Budd" title="Edward G. Budd">
       Edward G. Budd
      </a>
      in 1912.)
     </p>
     <p>
      Today out of print and nearly forgotten, the novels were bestsellers upon publication and were published in translation, appearing in twenty-one countries. The third book in the series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_Teeth_(novel)" title="Dragon's Teeth (novel)">
        Dragon's Teeth
       </a>
      </i>
      (1942), won the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_the_Novel" title="Pulitzer Prize for the Novel">
       Pulitzer Prize for the Novel
      </a>
      in 1943.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_works">
       Other works
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Upton_Sinclair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other works">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Sinclair was keenly interested in health and nutrition. He experimented with various diets, and with fasting. He wrote about this in his book,
      <i>
       The Fasting Cure
      </i>
      (1911), another bestseller.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He believed that periodic fasting was important for health, saying, "I had taken several fasts of ten or twelve days' duration, with the result of a complete making over of my health".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sinclair favored a raw food diet of predominantly vegetables and nuts. For long periods of time, he was a complete vegetarian, but he also experimented with eating meat. His attitude to these matters was fully explained in the chapter, The Use of Meat, in the above-mentioned book.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
