     <p>
      <b>
       Robert Budd Dwyer
      </b>
      (November 21, 1939  January 22, 1987) was an American politician in the Commonwealth of
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">
       Pennsylvania
      </a>
      . He served from 1971 to 1981 as a Republican member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_Senate" title="Pennsylvania State Senate">
       Pennsylvania State Senate
      </a>
      representing the state's
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Senate,_District_50" title="Pennsylvania Senate, District 50">
       50th district
      </a>
      . He served as the 30th
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasurer_of_Pennsylvania" title="Treasurer of Pennsylvania">
       Treasurer of Pennsylvania
      </a>
      from January 20, 1981 to January 22, 1987. On that day, Dwyer called a news conference in the Pennsylvania state capital of
      <a href="/wiki/Harrisburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Harrisburg, Pennsylvania">
       Harrisburg
      </a>
      where he killed himself in front of the gathered reporters with a .357 caliber revolver.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.1987_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes.1987-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dwyer's suicide was broadcast later that day to a wide television audience across the state of Pennsylvania.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 1980s, Pennsylvania discovered its state workers had overpaid federal taxes due to errors in state withholding. Many accounting firms competed for a multimillion-dollar contract to determine compensation to each employee. In 1986, Dwyer was convicted of receiving a bribe from a California firm trying to gain the contract. Throughout his trial and after his conviction, he maintained that he was innocent of the charge and that he had been
      <a href="/wiki/Frameup" title="Frameup">
       framed
      </a>
      . Dwyer was scheduled to be sentenced on those charges on January 23, 1987, the day after his suicide. The prosecution's primary witness, whose testimony was largely used to obtain Dwyer's conviction, later admitted in an interview shown in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Honest_Man:_The_Life_of_R._Budd_Dwyer" title="Honest Man: The Life of R. Budd Dwyer">
        Honest Man: The Life of R. Budd Dwyer
       </a>
      </i>
      that he had lied under oath about Dwyer taking the bribe in order to receive a reduced sentence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel.online_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-spiegel.online-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._Budd_Dwyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dwyer graduated from
      <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_College" title="Allegheny College">
       Allegheny College
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Meadville,_Pennsylvania" title="Meadville, Pennsylvania">
       Meadville, Pennsylvania
      </a>
      , where he was a member of the Beta Chi chapter of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theta_Chi_Fraternity" title="Theta Chi Fraternity">
       Theta Chi Fraternity
      </a>
      . After earning a
      <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">
       master's degree
      </a>
      in education, he taught social studies and coached
      <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">
       football
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Springs,_Pennsylvania" title="Cambridge Springs, Pennsylvania">
       Cambridge Springs High School
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">
       Republican
      </a>
      , Dwyer became active in politics. He was elected to the
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_House_of_Representatives" title="Pennsylvania House of Representatives">
       Pennsylvania House of Representatives
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_House_of_Representatives,_District_6" title="Pennsylvania House of Representatives, District 6">
       6th
      </a>
      district (although seats were apportioned by county prior to 1969) from 1965 to 1970. He also served as a member of the Pennsylvania Senate from the 50th district from 1971 to 1981. After his tenure as a state senator, Dwyer was elected state treasurer, a position he held from 1981 until his death in January 1987.
     </p>

       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._Budd_Dwyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bribery investigation and conviction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the early 1980s, public employees of the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania overpaid millions of dollars in
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Insurance_Contributions_Act_tax" title="Federal Insurance Contributions Act tax">
       Federal Insurance Contributions Act
      </a>
      (FICA) taxes. As a result, the state solicited bids from accounting firms to determine refunds for its employees. The contract was eventually awarded to Computer Technology Associates (CTA), a
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      -based firm, owned by John Torquato, Jr, a native of Harrisburg, Pennsylvania. Later Pennsylvania Governor
      <a href="/wiki/Dick_Thornburgh" title="Dick Thornburgh">
       Dick Thornburgh
      </a>
      received an anonymous memo detailing allegations of bribery that took place during the bidding process for the $4.6 million contract.
     </p>
     <p>
      An investigation was undertaken by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney" title="United States Attorney">
       United States Attorney
      </a>
      . Dwyer was charged with agreeing to receive
      <a href="/wiki/Kickback_(bribery)" title="Kickback (bribery)">
       kickbacks
      </a>
      worth $300,000 in return for using his office to steer the contract toward CTA. The US Attorney also indicted Torquato, Torquato's attorney William Smith, Smith's wife, and
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Asher" title="Bob Asher">
       Bob Asher
      </a>
      , the former Republican Party Chairman for the State of Pennsylvania. In return for lighter sentences, Torquato and the Smiths pled guilty and testified on behalf of the Federal government against Dwyer and Asher. In a 2010 documentary about the case, Smith asserted that he did, in fact, offer the money to Dwyer.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dwyer denied any wrongdoing. Federal prosecutors
      <a href="/wiki/Plea_bargain" title="Plea bargain">
       offered
      </a>
      him a single charge of
      <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">
       bribe receiving
      </a>
      (which would have meant up to a maximum of five years imprisonment), resignation from his office as Treasurer of Pennsylvania and full cooperation with the government's investigation but he refused. Instead Dwyer went to full trial. However his defense was curtailed by the prosecution because the case was limited to only those who had been charged. The names of the
      <a href="/wiki/Unindicted_co-conspirator" title="Unindicted co-conspirator">
       unindicted co-conspirators
      </a>
      who were linked in the bribery scandal but were not on trial were withheld. These unnamed individuals were believed to have been staff members of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dauphin_County" title="Dauphin County">
       Dauphin County
      </a>
      Republican Party.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On December 18, 1986, Dwyer was convicted of having accepted a bribe. Even so he continued to profess his innocence, as did others close to him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famouspictures_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-famouspictures-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dwyer wrote to President
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
       Ronald Reagan
      </a>
      seeking a presidential pardon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dwyermovie_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dwyermovie-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite his conviction, Dwyer was allowed under Pennsylvania law to continue serving as state treasurer until his sentencing by U.S. District Court Judge
      <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Muir_(judge)" title="Malcolm Muir (judge)">
       Malcolm Muir
      </a>
      on January 23, 1987. Dwyer, on being found guilty, faced a sentence of up to 55 years imprisonment and a $300,000 fine.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Asher" title="Bob Asher">
       Bob Asher
      </a>
      , his co-defendant, was sentenced to one year in jail. He later returned to politics and served as a Republican national committeeman for Pennsylvania.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famouspictures_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-famouspictures-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_suicide">
       Public suicide
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._Budd_Dwyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public suicide">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On January 22, 1987, the day before his sentencing, Dwyer called a press conference.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Harrisburg
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriot-News" title="Patriot-News">
        Patriot-News
       </a>
      </i>
      reporter Kenn Marshall described the consensus among reporters that they were there to see Dwyer announce his resignation from office. "My mission was to stay there until he said those words, then call in a new top for our story."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PennLiveNewFilm_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PennLiveNewFilm-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Appearing agitated and nervous, Dwyer professed his innocence and began reading from prepared text described as a "rambling polemic about the criminal justice system":
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PennLiveNewFilm_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-PennLiveNewFilm-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also spoke out against the death penalty and expressed regret for voting in favor of it while he was in the Pennsylvania assembly. Upon reaching the final page of this text, he paused, "...and I'm on the last page now, and I don't have enough to pass out, but Duke, I'll leave this here, and you can make copies for the people; there's a few extra copies here right now."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FinalWords_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FinalWords-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Continuing,
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       <p>
        I thank the Good Lord for giving me 47 years of exciting challenges, stimulating experiences, many happy occasions, and, most of all, the finest wife and children any man could ever desire.
       </p>
       <p>
        Now my life has changed for no apparent reason. People who call and write are exasperated and feel helpless. They know I'm innocent and want to help. But in this nation, the world's greatest democracy, there is nothing they can do to prevent me from being punished for a crime they know I did not commit. Some who have called have said that I am a modern-day
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_(Biblical_figure)" title="Job (Biblical figure)">
         Job
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Judge Muir is also noted for his medieval sentences. I face a maximum sentence of 55 years in prison and a $300,000 fine for being innocent. Judge Muir has already told the press that he, quote, 'felt invigorated' when we were found guilty, and that he plans to imprison me as a deterrent to other public officials. But it wouldn't be a deterrent because every public official who knows me knows that I am innocent; it wouldn't be a legitimate punishment because I've done nothing wrong. Since I'm a victim of political persecution, my prison would simply be an American
        <a href="/wiki/Gulag" title="Gulag">
         gulag
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        I ask those that believe in me to continue to extend friendship and prayer to my family, to work untiringly for the creation of a true justice system here in the United States, and to press on with the efforts to vindicate me, so that my family and their future families are not tainted by this injustice that has been perpetrated on me.
       </p>
       <p>
        We were confident that right and truth would prevail, and I would be acquitted and we would devote the rest of our lives working to create a justice system here in the United States. The guilty verdict has strengthened that resolve. But as we've discussed our plans to expose the warts of our legal system, people have said, 'Why bother, no one cares.' 'You'll look foolish.' '
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">
          60 Minutes
         </a>
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20/20_(US_television_show)" title="20/20 (US television show)">
          20/20
         </a>
        </i>
        , the
        <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">
         ACLU
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Jack_Anderson_(columnist)" title="Jack Anderson (columnist)">
         Jack Anderson
        </a>
        , and others have been publicizing cases like yours for years and it doesn't bother anyone.'
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      At this point, Dwyer stopped reading his prepared text, which as yet had not said whether he intended to resign from office or not. The part he did not read follows:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       I've repeatedly said that I'm not going to resign as State Treasurer. After many hours of thought and meditation I've made a decision that should not be an example to anyone because it is unique to my situation. Last May I told you that after the trial, I would give you the story of the decade. To those of you who are shallow, the events of this morning will be that story. But to those of you with depth and concern the real story will be what I hope and pray results from this morning--in the coming months and years, the development of a true Justice System here in the United States. I am going to die in office in an effort to ...see if the shame[-ful] facts, spread out in all their shame, will not burn through our civic shamelessness and set fire to American pride. Please tell my story on every radio and television station and in every newspaper and magazine in the U.S. Please leave immediately if you have a weak stomach or mind since I don't want to cause physical or mental distress. Joanne, Rob, DeeDee - I love you! Thank you for making my life so happy. Good bye to you all on the count of 3. Please make sure that the sacrifice of my life is not in vain.
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Having stopped reading, he called to three of his staffers, giving each an envelope. One envelope contained a suicide note addressed to his wife. The second contained an
      <a href="/wiki/Organ_donation" title="Organ donation">
       organ donor
      </a>
      card and other related materials. The third envelope contained a letter addressed to then-Pennsylvania Governor
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_P._Casey" title="Robert P. Casey">
       Bob Casey
      </a>
      , who had taken office just two days earlier. Freelance photographer Gary Miller, one of the reporters in attendance, describes the scene to this point, "It was just kind of a long-winded, sad event."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PennLiveNewFilm_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-PennLiveNewFilm-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dwyer then produced a manila envelope with a blued
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson_Model_27" title="Smith &amp; Wesson Model 27">
       Smith &amp; Wesson Model 27
      </a>
      revolver in it. As he pulled the gun out of the envelope, he said to the gathered crowd, "Please, please leave the room if this will...if this will affect you." Attendees pleaded with Dwyer to put the gun down, while some ran to get help. Others tried to approach him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsday.1988_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newsday.1988-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dwyer advised everyone not to come near him, saying, "Don't, don't, don't, this will hurt someone."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1-23_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AP1-23-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As those around him continued to try to persuade him to reconsider, Dwyer turned the gun toward himself, opened his mouth, inserted the gun, and pulled the trigger.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grossman_p._108_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grossman_p._108-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The bullet exited out of the top of his head, leaving a bloody stream, and Dwyer collapsed to the floor behind the podium as more blood cascaded through his nose. Witnesses screamed and cursed as five news cameras recorded the events. Although Dwyer died instantly from the gunshot, he was not pronounced dead at the scene until 11:31a.m., EST.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP1-23_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AP1-23-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dwyer was buried in Blooming Valley Cemetery in
      <a href="/wiki/Blooming_Valley,_Pennsylvania" title="Blooming Valley, Pennsylvania">
       Blooming Valley, Pennsylvania
      </a>
      , near his hometown of Meadville.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">
       Aftermath
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=R._Budd_Dwyer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A number of television stations throughout Pennsylvania broadcast taped footage of Dwyer's suicide to a midday audience. Philadelphia station
      <a href="/wiki/WPVI-TV" title="WPVI-TV">
       WPVI
      </a>
      (Channel 6) showed Dwyer pulling the trigger and falling backwards, but did not show the bullet path.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over the next several hours, news editors had to decide how much of the graphic footage to air.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many stations, including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WCAU-TV" title="WCAU-TV">
       WCAU
      </a>
      and Pennsylvania's
      <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Broadcasting" title="Westinghouse Broadcasting">
       Group W
      </a>
      stations
      <a href="/wiki/KYW-TV" title="KYW-TV">
       KYW
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/KDKA-TV" title="KDKA-TV">
       KDKA
      </a>
      , froze the action just prior to the gunfire. However, the latter two allowed the audio of the shooting to continue under the frozen image. Group W's news cameraman William L. "Bill" Martin and reporter David Sollenberger had a camera set up at the conference. They chose to air the audio with a freeze frame of the gun in Dwyer's mouth. Only a handful aired the unedited press conference.
      <a href="/wiki/WPVI-TV" title="WPVI-TV">
       WPVI
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">
       Philadelphia
      </a>
      re-broadcast the suicide footage in full on their 5p.m. and 6p.m.
      <a href="/wiki/Action_News" title="Action News">
       Action News
      </a>
      broadcasts without a warning to viewers. That station's broadcast is a source for copies circulating on the Internet.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WPXI-TV" title="WPXI-TV">
       WPXI
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">
       Pittsburgh
      </a>
      is reported by the
      <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">
       Associated Press
      </a>
      to have broadcast the footage uncensored on an early newscast. In explaining the decision to air, WPXI operations manager By Williams said, "It's an important event [about] an important man." Williams avoided airing the footage in the evening newscasts, explaining, "Everyone knows by then that he did it. There are children out of school."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APNYTimes_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-APNYTimes-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, in central Pennsylvania, many children were home from school
      <i>
       at the hour
      </i>
      of Dwyer's suicide due to a snowstorm, and Harrisburg TV station
      <a href="/wiki/WHTM-TV" title="WHTM-TV">
       WHTM-TV
      </a>
      opted to broadcast uncut video of the suicide not once, but twice that day, defending the decision despite hundreds of viewer complaints afterward due to the important nature of the story. Nationally, none of the major broadcast news operations telecast the actual suicide, with NBC showing the news conference but freezing it before Dwyer put the gun in his mouth, and ABC and CBS showing no footage at all.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A copy of the unedited video is available on the Internet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LIveLeaks_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-LIveLeaks-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many older students reacted to the event by creating
      <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">
       black comedy
      </a>
      jokes similar to those that circulated after the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger" title="Space Shuttle Challenger">
        Challenger
       </a>
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster">
       disaster
      </a>
      . A study of the incidence of the jokes showed that they were told only in areas where stations showed uncensored footage of the press conference.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement">

      At least one reporter present at Dwyer's suicide suffered from being a witness.
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Romeo_(reporter)" title="Tony Romeo (reporter)">

     <p>
      Since Dwyer died in office, his widow Joanne was able to collect full survivor benefits, totaling over $1.28 million. A spokesman for Dwyer suggested that he may have committed suicide to preserve the state-provided pension for his family, whose finances had been ruined by legal defense costs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">

        </span>
        23
        <span>

     </p>
     <p>
      One year after her husband's suicide, Joanne Dwyer moved from their home in Hershey to the Tempe, Arizona area with her son Robert and daughter Dyan. She never married again. Joanne Dwyer remained in Tempe until her own death on Sunday, July 12, 2009, at the age of 70. Her body was laid to rest next to that of her husband in Blooming Valley Cemetery.

        </span>

        <span>

      </sup>
      The year it was released, the song reached No. 10 on Billboard's Modern Rock Tracks chart.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-billboard-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "
      <a href="/wiki/The_World_Is_Yours_(Faith_No_More_song)" title="The World Is Yours (Faith No More song)">
       The World is Yours
      </a>
      " is an outtake song from the 1992 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Angel_Dust_(Faith_No_More_album)" title="Angel Dust (Faith No More album)">
        Angel Dust
       </a>
      </i>
      by the band
      <a href="/wiki/Faith_No_More" title="Faith No More">
       Faith No More
      </a>
      . Some of its lyrics refer to Dwyer's last words and one of the various audio samples that it contains is from the recording of his suicide. Included on the 2003 album
      <i>
       Breathing is Irrelevant
      </i>
      by Ion Dissonance is a song titled "The Bud Dwyer Effect." The misspelling was apparently purposeful. The song reflects on Dwyer's suicide in the context of the vocalist's own suicidal thoughts. In 2004, Necro, an underground rapper based in Brooklyn, NY, released the album "Pre-fix for Death" featuring the song "You did it" which contains the audio of Budd Dwyer's suicide at the end of the track. In 2013, the deathcore band
      <i>
       Fit For an Autopsy
      </i>
      released a song entitled "Thank You, Budd Dwyer" to pay tribute and to further tell the story of his wrongful sentence. Pennsylvanian alternative metal band
      <a href="/wiki/CKY_(band)" title="CKY (band)">
       CKY
      </a>
      (Camp Kill Yourself) featured an artwork of Dwyer's suicide on their debut album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Volume_1_(CKY_album)" title="Volume 1 (CKY album)">
        Volume 1
       </a>
      </i>
      until their record label forced them to change it due to complaints of offensiveness. The Austrian black metal band Ellende featured audio from the news conference during an interlude on the song "Weltennacht" from the 2014 EP of the same title. The audio included the audience's tension from the time the gun was drawn, the fatal gunshot, and the screams of the aftermath which crossfade into black metal screams.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Traces_of_Death" title="Traces of Death">

      <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Happiness_is_a_Warm_Gun" title="Happiness is a Warm Gun">
       Happiness is a Warm Gun
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
