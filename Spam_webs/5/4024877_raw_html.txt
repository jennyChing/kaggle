           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad" id="tablet-ad-47494">
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           WASHINGTON  --  The Ebola outbreak in West Africa will be on the agenda when clinicians and researchers gather here for the
           <a href="http://www.icaac.org/index.php/meeting/icaac-2014" target="_blank">
            2014 Interscience Conference on Antimicrobial Agents and Chemotherapy (ICAAC).
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           "It's not because it's really new (but) the way it's spreading is new," says
           <a href="http://www.seattlechildrens.org/medical-staff/Craig-E-Rubens/" target="_blank">
            Craig Rubens, MD, PhD
           </a>
           , of Seattle Children's Hospital, the meeting's program chair.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "We're having to think about a broader scale of exposure than we're used to," Rubens told
           <em>
            MedPage Today
           </em>
           . Until this outbreak, which has now
           <a href="http://www.who.int/csr/don/2014_08_28_ebola/en/" target="_blank">
            claimed more than 1,550 lives in four countries
           </a>
           , Ebola flare-ups have been relatively small and isolated, he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           But the current epidemic has both rural and urban transmission across national borders  --  the first time that has happened.
          </p>
          <p>
           And also, for the first time, clinicians in the developed world have to be aware of the possibility that travellers from the affected countries  --  mainly Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone  --
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/HospitalBasedMedicine/Intensivists/47078" target="_blank">
            might import Ebola
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Several sessions on aspects of the Ebola crisis "will be of strong clinical interest," Rubens said.
          </p>
          <p>
           While the meeting will have some data ripped from the headlines, old standbys will also draw attention, he said, including the perennial issue of drug-resistant organisms.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "That's always a highlight of ICAAC," Rubens said, "what's happening with resistance, who's responding with new drugs, and how are we diagnosing these bugs more rapidly."
          </p>
          <p>
           The issue is not just restricted to bacteria, although
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/InfectiousDisease/GeneralInfectiousDisease/41661" target="_blank">
            they get most of the press
           </a>
           , he said. But drug resistance in parasites  --
           <a href="http://www.who.int/malaria/publications/atoz/update-artemisinin-resistance-jan2014/en/" target="_blank">
            artemisinin resistance
           </a>
           in malaria, for instance  --  and in fungal pathogens will also be in the line-up.
          </p>
          <p>
           This year, the program committee is also rearranging its categories, with two new abstract areas, he said, including sessions on experimental therapeutics and some on vaccines and immunization science.
          </p>
          <p>
           The experimental therapeutics area will include talks on new single- and multi-drug approaches to treatment, many of them being tested in animals, Rubens said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Such studies have been "more and more common" in recent years, he said. Now, instead of being shoehorned into other categories, they'll be broken out.
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The recent outbreaks of pertussis in the U.S. and elsewhere are a "good example" of why more emphasis is needed on vaccines and immunization, Rubens said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The outbreaks have "prompted a revisit as to how we can deal with vaccine-preventable diseases and the immunization science around that," he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The global health category  --  while not new  --  is starting to change focus, away from travel-related illness and toward neglected diseases that primarily affect indigenous populations, such as leishmaniasis.
          </p>
          <p>
           That's driven by the growing number of clinicians from the developing world attending ICAAC, he said, but also by the increasing availability of new drugs developed for neglected diseases.
          </p>
          <p>
           As with most scientific meetings, ICAAC also gives prominent scientists a platform for keynote talks.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           This year they include an overview of drug resistance, highlighting some of the recently approved drugs aimed at multidrug resistant bacteria, as well as the history and future of beta-lactamases.
          </p>
          <p>
           Two prominent researchers  --
           <a href="http://www.ladydavis.ca/en/markwainberg" target="_blank">
            Mark Wainberg, PhD
           </a>
           , of McGill University and
           <a href="http://id.ucsd.edu/faculty/benson.shtml" target="_blank">
            Constance Benson, MD,
           </a>
           of the University of California San Diego  --  will give their perspectives on the HIV/AIDS pandemic.
          </p>
