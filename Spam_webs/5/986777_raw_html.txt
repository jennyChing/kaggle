     <p>
      <b>
       Leadbeater's possum
      </b>
      (
      <i>
       Gymnobelideus leadbeateri
      </i>
      ) is an critically endangered
      <a href="/wiki/Possum" title="Possum">
       possum
      </a>
      restricted to small pockets of remaining
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_growth" title="Old growth">
       old growth
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_regnans" title="Eucalyptus regnans">
       mountain ash
      </a>
      forests in the
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Highlands_(Victoria)" title="Central Highlands (Victoria)">
       Central Highlands
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" title="Victoria, Australia">
       Victoria
      </a>
      (Australia) north-east of Melbourne.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is
      <a href="/wiki/Primitive_(phylogenetics)" title="Primitive (phylogenetics)">
       primitive
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">
       relict
      </a>
      , and non-gliding, and, as the only species in the
      <a href="/wiki/Petauridae" title="Petauridae">
       petaurid
      </a>
      genus
      <i>
       Gymnobelideus
      </i>
      , represents an ancestral form. Formerly, Leadbeater's possums were moderately common within the very small areas they inhabited; their requirement for year-round food supplies and tree-holes to take refuge in during the day restricts them to mixed-age wet
      <a href="/wiki/Sclerophyll" title="Sclerophyll">
       sclerophyll
      </a>
      forest with a dense mid-story of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">
        Acacia
       </a>
      </i>
      . The species was named after John Leadbeater, the then taxidermist at the
      <a href="/wiki/Museum_Victoria" title="Museum Victoria">
       Museum Victoria
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also go by the common name of
      <b>
       fairy possum
      </b>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 2 March 1971,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the State of Victoria made the Leadbeater's possum its
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Australian_mammal_emblems" title="List of Australian mammal emblems">
       faunal emblem
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2013_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2013-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadbeater%27s_possum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Leadbeater's possum is thought to have evolved about 20 million years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was not discovered until 1867 and was originally known only through five specimens, the last one collected in 1909.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friends_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Friends-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From that time on, the fear that it might be extinct gradually grew into near-certainty after the swamps and wetlands in Australia around
      <a href="/wiki/Bass_River_(Victoria)" title="Bass River (Victoria)">
       Bass River
      </a>
      in south-west
      <a href="/wiki/Gippsland" title="Gippsland">
       Gippsland
      </a>
      were drained for farming in the early 1900s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weekes_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-weekes-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By the time of the 1939
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(1939)" title="Black Friday (1939)">
       Black Friday
      </a>
      fires, the species was thought to have been extinct.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2013_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2013-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Then, on 3 April 1961, a member of the species was rediscovered by naturalist Eric Wilkinson in the forests near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambarville" title="Cambarville">
       Cambarville
      </a>
      , and the first specimen in more than 50 years was captured later in the month.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1961, a colony was discovered near
      <a href="/wiki/Marysville,_Victoria" title="Marysville, Victoria">
       Marysville
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Extensive searches since then have found the existing population in the highlands. However, the availability of suitable habitat is critical: forest must be neither too old nor too young, with conservation efforts for Leadbeaters possum involving protection of remaining old-growth stands, and maintenance of younger stands that are allowed to attain hollow-bearing age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macfarlane_MA.2C_Smith_J.2C_Lowe_K_1998_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Macfarlane_MA.2C_Smith_J.2C_Lowe_K_1998-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The combination of 40-year-old regrowth (for food) and large dead trees left still standing after the fires (for shelter and nesting) allowed the Leadbeater's possum population to expand to an estimated peak of about 7500 in the early 1980s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leadbeater.27s_Possum_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leadbeater.27s_Possum-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From its peak in the 1980s, the Leadbeater's possum population was expected to further decline rapidly, by as much as 90%,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      due to a habitat bottleneck. The population has dropped sharply since 1996.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weekes_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-weekes-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Particularly, the February 2009
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Saturday_bushfires" title="Black Saturday bushfires">
       Black Saturday bushfires
      </a>
      destroyed 43% of Leadbeater's possums habitat in the Central Highlands, halving the wild population to 1,500.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2013_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian_2013-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A study in 2014 concluded there is a 92% chance the Leadbeater's ecosystem in the Victoria central highlands will collapse within 50 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Habits">
       Habits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadbeater%27s_possum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Leadbeater's possums are rarely seen as they are nocturnal, fast-moving, and occupy the upper story of some of the tallest forest trees in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They have an average body length of 33cm with the tail included.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helmetedhoneyeater.org.au_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-helmetedhoneyeater.org.au-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They live in small family colonies of up to 12 individuals,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friends_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Friends-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      including one
      <a href="/wiki/Monogamy" title="Monogamy">
       monogamous
      </a>
      breeding pair. Mating occurs only once a year, with a maximum of two joeys being born in each pair.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helmetedhoneyeater.org.au_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-helmetedhoneyeater.org.au-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      All members sleep together in a nest made out of shredded bark in a tree hollow, anywhere from 6 to 30 metres above ground level and roughly in the centre of a territory of 3 hectares, which they defend actively. The society of Leadbeater's possums is
      <a href="/wiki/Matriarchy" title="Matriarchy">
       matriarchal
      </a>
      : each group is dominated by only one female Leadbeater's possum that is more active in expelling outsiders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friends_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Friends-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other juvenile females are weaned off before they reach sexual maturity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ADW-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, female Leadbeater's possums are more aggressive in nature, often engaging frequent fightings with other females including their own daughters. Due to the constant attacks, young females are forced to leave much earlier than their male brothers, which results in the extremely high male to female ratio 3:1.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADW_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ADW-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Solitary Leadbeater's possums have difficulty surviving: when young males disperse at about 15 months of age, they tend to either join another colony as a supernumerary member, or gather together into bachelor groups while they await an opportunity to find a mate.
     </p>
     <p>
      At dusk, Leadbeater's possums emerge from the nest and spread out to forage in the sub-canopy, often making substantial leaps from tree to tree (they require continuous understory to travel). Their diet is omnivorous: feeding on a range of wattle saps and exudates,
      <a href="/wiki/Lerp_(biology)" title="Lerp (biology)">
       lerps
      </a>
      , and a high proportion of
      <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">
       arthropods
      </a>
      which they find under the loose bark of eucalypts, including spiders, crickets, termites and beetles. Plant exudates make up 80% of their energy intake, but the protein provided by the arthropods is essential for successful breeding.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leadbeater.27s_Possum_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Leadbeater.27s_Possum-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Births are usually timed for the beginning of winter (May and June) or late spring (October and November). Most litters are of one or two young, which stay in the pouch for 80 to 90 days, and first emerge from the nest following this. Young, newly independent Leadbeater's possums are very vulnerable to
      <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">
       owls
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Leadbeater's possums are presently (2013) found in three habitat types: Lowland Swamp Gum, of Yellingbo Nature Conservation Reserve; Montane Ash Forest, wet
      <a href="/wiki/Sclerophyll" title="Sclerophyll">
       sclerophyll
      </a>
      forest dominated by
      <a href="/wiki/Mountain_ash" title="Mountain ash">
       Mountain ash
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shining_gum" title="Shining gum">
       Shining gum
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_Ash" title="Alpine Ash">
       Alpine Ash
      </a>
      with a dense mid-story of Acacia species; Sub-Alpine woodlands of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt_Baw_Baw" title="Mt Baw Baw">
       Mt Baw Baw
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mountain_(Victoria)" title="Lake Mountain (Victoria)">
       Lake Mountain
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mt_Bullfight&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mt Bullfight (page does not exist)">
       Mt Bullfight
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">
       Threats
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadbeater%27s_possum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Threats">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

         </a>
        </div>
        George, a taxidermied male Leadbeater's possum (Gymnobelideus leadbeateri), that Friends of Leadbeater's Possum uses for its educational work concerning this threatened species.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Leadbeater's possums and their forest habitat have been the subject of the largest longitudinal study of any species in the worldconducted by Professor
      <a href="/wiki/David_Lindenmayer" title="David Lindenmayer">
       David Lindenmayer
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">
       Australian National University
      </a>
      and his research assistants since 1983. Hundreds of peer reviewed scientific papers, journal articles and books have resulted from the years of data collection by the ANU team. Their findings show that the availability of suitable habitat is critical: forest must be neither too old nor too young, with conservation efforts for Leadbeaters possum involving protection of remaining old-growth stands, and maintenance of younger stands that are allowed to attain hollow-bearing age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macfarlane_MA.2C_Smith_J.2C_Lowe_K_1998_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Macfarlane_MA.2C_Smith_J.2C_Lowe_K_1998-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Clearfell logging and salvage logging (after bushfires) have been proven by the researchers to have been the greatest threat to their conservation in the wild over the past 30 years.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_loss">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

      The entire Central Highlands population distribution is confined to a 70 by 80 kilometre area. With 43% of its known Central Highlands habitat

        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Black_Saturday_bushfires" title="Black Saturday bushfires">

      <a href="/wiki/Toolangi,_Victoria" title="Toolangi, Victoria">

      </a>

      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healesville" title="Healesville">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theaustralian_2009_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-theaustralian_2009-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">

      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Burke" title="Tony Burke">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critically_Endangered" title="Critically Endangered">
       Critically Endangered
      </a>
      .

       <a href="#cite_note-20">

        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The only remaining population outside the Central Highlands is located at Yellingbo Natue Conservation Reserve. Zoos Victoria's Threatened Species Biologist, Dr. Dan Harley, has estimtaed this population to be fewer than 50.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Logging">
       Logging
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As the species is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">
       endangered
      </a>
      and occupies a restricted range,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">
       logging
      </a>
      continues to pose a critical threat to Leadbeater's possum. The logging in 1993 of "much of the possum's habitat, known as zone one" a five hectare reserve east of
      <a href="/wiki/Powelltown,_Victoria" title="Powelltown, Victoria">
       Powelltown
      </a>
      , followed a "mapping error."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Author Peter Preuss stated that the possum's population faltered in 1997 with current habitat (limited to a 50-square-kilometre area) under threat from logging. He emphasised the need to relaunch a breeding program.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite a joint Federal and State government plan to save it, since the 1980s, the Leadbeater's possum population halved to around 2000
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      even before the Black Saturday fires. Many more were killed early in 2007 when Government Backed Enterprise company,
      <a href="/wiki/VicForests" title="VicForests">
       VicForests
      </a>
      bulldozed large firebreaks through Leadbeater's monitoring stations following the Christmas fires - firebreaks and clear-felling also prevent breeding with nearby colonies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weekes_10-2">

      </sup>

       </a>

The group immediately appealed the decision by the presiding judge Justice Osborne, and the Supreme Court accepted there was a sound basis for an appeal to the original determination. The Supreme Court Appeal was heard on 24 June 2013 before three judges and MyEnvironment was represented in court by
      <a href="/wiki/Julian_Burnside" title="Julian Burnside">
       Julian Burnside
      </a>
      QC. The appeal was lost.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">
       Legislation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadbeater%27s_possum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legislation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On 27 June 2013 the
      <a href="/wiki/Denis_Napthine" title="Denis Napthine">
       Napthine
      </a>
      State government passed legislative changes to allow VicForests access to Victoria's forests for the next 25 years and to be self monitoring (this follows the success of other recent cases preventing logging of remaining possum habitat). According to
      <a href="/wiki/The_Wilderness_Society_(Australia)" title="The Wilderness Society (Australia)">
       The Wilderness Society
      </a>
      , "the Victorian government ... [is] virtually signing the death warrant of the remaining 500 or so Leadbeater's possums."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These changes to the
      <i>
       Sustainable Forests (Timber) Act 2004
      </i>
      will have implications not only for the Leadbeater's possum but to the biodiversity, carbon storage and water catchments of the forests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">
       Conservation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadbeater%27s_possum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On the 22nd April 2015, Greg Hunt, the Minister for the Environment announced that the Leadbeater's Possum would be listed as a 'critically endangered' species under the EPBC Act.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Of its
      <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_regnans" title="Eucalyptus regnans">
       Ash forest
      </a>
      habitat about 30% is protected, while the rest is allocated to logging. The habitat of a small isolated, genetically distinct, population is protected within the
      <a href="/wiki/Yellingbo_Nature_Conservation_Reserve" title="Yellingbo Nature Conservation Reserve">
       Yellingbo Nature Conservation Reserve
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-iucn-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2013 it was proposed to create the "
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Forest_National_Park" title="Great Forest National Park">
       Great Forest National Park
      </a>
      " to protect the mountain ash forest habitat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The park would protect the area between
      <a href="/wiki/Kinglake_National_Park" title="Kinglake National Park">
       Kinglake
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Baw_Baw_National_Park" title="Baw Baw National Park">
       Baw Baw
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eildon_National_Park" title="Lake Eildon National Park">
       Eildon
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_parks" title="National parks">
       national parks
      </a>
      , which is also important for Melbourne's drinking water and as carbon sink.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 2004, the Friends of Leadbeater's Possum community group has been active in raising the animal's profile and lobbying for its conservation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Through a joint community/government program, "Project Possum" has installed approximately 200 plastic nest boxes in the wild. Many of these nest boxes where paid for via a community fundraising campaign. The nest boxes are primarily used to assist with ongoing population monitoring and supplement the declining forest habitat. Project Possum has targeting two forest types: Montane Ash Forest (i.e. Mt Ritchie, Dowey Spur, Ben Cairn) and Sub-alpine Woodland (i.e. Mt Baw Baw, Lake Mountain &amp; Mount Bullfight, ). The nest boxes are routinely checked for habitation every one or two years. Nest boxes located in the Sup-alpine Woodland tend to have a high uptake, while the ones located in Montane Ash Forest have very limited uptake. An additional 50 nest boxes are due for installation in 2015-16.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Captive_breeding">
       Captive breeding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leadbeater%27s_possum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Captive breeding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Des_Hackett" title="Des Hackett">
       Des Hackett
      </a>
      is credited as the first person to successfully breed the Leadbeater's possum in captivity. In May 2006, the last Australian specimen at the time, held at
      <a href="/wiki/Healesville_Sanctuary" title="Healesville Sanctuary">
       Healesville Sanctuary
      </a>
      , died. In January 2010, Kasia, at the time the last captive Leadbeater's possum worldwide, died at
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Zoo" title="Toronto Zoo">
       Toronto Zoo
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The predation by a feral cat of the post 2009 bushfire few remaining
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mountain_(Victoria)" title="Lake Mountain (Victoria)">
       Lake Mountain
      </a>
      Leadbeater's possums in early 2012, led to three remaining individuals being taken into captivity for their own protection. One animal has since died.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are no plans to release the remaining two animals despite a further two colonies of Leadbeater's possums recently having been located at Lake Mountain in remnant gully vegetation. These two Lake Mountain animals are now on public display in the Nocturnal House as ambassadors for the species. Healesville Sanctuary's captive breeding program for Leadbeater's possums re-commenced in May 2012 and now comprises 6 individuals from the genetically distinct
      <a href="/wiki/Yellingbo_Nature_Conservation_Reserve" title="Yellingbo Nature Conservation Reserve">
       Yellingbo
      </a>
      population. They are housed as pairs in large enclosures off display, but are yet to breed (May 2015).
     </p>
