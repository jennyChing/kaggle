   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Standard Average European
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Standard Average European
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       SAE
      </b>
      ) is a concept introduced by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_Whorf" title="Benjamin Whorf">
       Benjamin Whorf
      </a>
      (1939) "The Relation of Habitual Thought and Behavior to Language"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to group the modern
      <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
       Indo-European
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Europe" title="Languages of Europe">
       languages of Europe
      </a>
      . Whorf argued that these
      <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
       languages
      </a>
      were characterized by a number of
      <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
       grammatical
      </a>
      similarities, which made them different from many of the world's other languages. His point was to argue that the disproportionate degree of knowledge of SAE languages biased
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguist" title="Linguist">
       linguists
      </a>
      towards considering grammatical forms to be highly natural or even universal, when in fact they were peculiar to the SAE
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_group" title="Language group">
       language group
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In Whorf's most famous example, he contrasted what he called the SAE tense system which contrasts past, present and future tenses with that of
      <a href="/wiki/Hopi_language" title="Hopi language">
       Hopi
      </a>
      , which Whorf analyzed as being based on a distinction not of
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
       tense
      </a>
      , but on distinguishing things that have in fact occurred (a
      <a href="/wiki/Realis_mood" title="Realis mood">
       realis mood
      </a>
      encompassing SAE past and present) as opposed to things that have as yet not occurred, but which may or may not occur in the future (
      <a href="/wiki/Irrealis_mood" title="Irrealis mood">
       irrealis mood
      </a>
      ). The accuracy of Whorf's analysis of Hopi tense has later been
      <a href="/wiki/Hopi_time_controversy" title="Hopi time controversy">
       a point of controversy
      </a>
      in linguistics.
     </p>
     <p>
      Whorf likely considered
      <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
       Romance
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_languages" title="West Germanic languages">
       West Germanic
      </a>
      to form the core of the SAE, i.e. the
      <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">
       literary languages
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      which have seen substantial cultural influence from
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">
       medieval period
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">
       North Germanic
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Balto-Slavic_languages" title="Balto-Slavic languages">
       Balto-Slavic languages
      </a>
      tend to be more peripheral members.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gode" title="Alexander Gode">
       Alexander Gode
      </a>
      , who was instrumental in the development of
      <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">
       Interlingua
      </a>
      , characterized this language as "Standard Average European".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Romance,
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
       Germanic
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
       Slavic
      </a>
      control languages of Interlingua are reflective of the language groups most often included in the SAE
      <i>
       Sprachbund
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Average_European_as_a_Sprachbund">
       Standard Average European as a
       <i>
        Sprachbund
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_Average_European&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Standard Average European as a Sprachbund">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Haspelmath" title="Martin Haspelmath">
       Martin Haspelmath
      </a>
      (2001), the SAE languages form a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">
        Sprachbund
       </a>
      </i>
      characterized by the following features, sometimes called "euroversals" by analogy with
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_universal" title="Linguistic universal">
       linguistic universals
      </a>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       definite and indefinite
       <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
        articles
       </a>
       (e.g. English
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       postnominal
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">
        relative clauses
       </a>
       with inflected, resumptive
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_pronoun" title="Relative pronoun">
        relative pronouns
       </a>
       (e.g. English
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        whose
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periphrastic" title="Periphrastic">
        periphrastic
       </a>
       perfect formed with 'have' plus a passive participle (e.g. English
       <i>
        I have said
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       a preponderance of generalizing
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/predicate" title="wikt:predicate">
        predicates
       </a>
       to encode
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Experiencer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Experiencer (page does not exist)">
        experiencers
       </a>
       , i.e. experiencers appear as surface subjects in
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative case
       </a>
       , (e.g. English
       <i>
        I like music
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        Music pleases me,
       </i>
       though compare Spanish
       <i>
        Me gusta la msica,
       </i>
       which is of the form "Music pleases me");
      </li>
      <li>
       a passive construction formed with a passive participle plus an intransitive copula-like verb (e.g. English
       <i>
        I am known
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       a prominence of
       <a href="/wiki/Anticausative_verb" title="Anticausative verb">
        anticausative verbs
       </a>
       in inchoative-causative pairs (e.g. in the pair
       <i>
        The snow melts
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        The flame melts the ice
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_verb" title="Intransitive verb">
        intransitive verb
       </a>
       is derived from the transitive);
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
        dative
       </a>
       external possessors (e.g. German
       <i>
        Die Mutter wusch
        <b>
         dem Kind
        </b>
        die Haare
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        The mother washed the child's hair
       </i>
       (lit.
       <i>
        The mother washed the hair to the child
       </i>
       , Portuguese
       <i>
        Ela lavou-
        <b>
         lhe
        </b>
        o cabelo
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        She washed his hair
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       verbal negation with a negative indefinite (e.g. English
       <i>
        <b>
         Nobody
        </b>
        listened
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       particle comparatives in comparisons of inequality (e.g. English
       <i>
        bigger
        <b>
         than
        </b>
        an elephant
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       equative constructions based on adverbial-relative clause structures (e.g. French
       <i>
        grand
        <b>
         comme
        </b>
        un lephant
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       subject person affixes as strict
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       markers, i.e. the verb is inflected for person and number of the subject, but subject pronouns may not be dropped even when this would be unambiguous (only in some languages, such as German, French and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoken_Finnish" title="Spoken Finnish">
        Spoken Finnish
       </a>
       , e.g.
       <b>
        m
       </b>
       oo
       <b>
        n
       </b>
       , "I am" and
       <b>
        s
       </b>
       oo
       <b>
        t
       </b>
       , "you are"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       ); this feature is called
       <a href="/wiki/Null-subject_language" title="Null-subject language">
        null subject
       </a>
       
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        pro-drop
       </a>
       is sometimes mentioned in this context, but is technically a term for a more general phenomenon;
      </li>
      <li>
       differentiation between intensifiers and
       <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">
        reflexive pronouns
       </a>
       (e.g. German intensifier
       <i>
        selbst
       </i>
       vs. reflexive
       <i>
        sich
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Besides these features, which are uncommon outside Europe and thus useful for defining the SAE area, Haspelmath (2001) lists further features characteristic of European languages (but also found elsewhere):
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       verb-initial order in yes/no questions;
      </li>
      <li>
       comparative inflection of adjectives (e.g. English
       <i>
        bigger
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       conjunction
       <i>
        A, B and C
       </i>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       syncretism of
       <a href="/wiki/Comitative_case" title="Comitative case">
        comitative
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">
        instrumental
       </a>
       cases (e.g. English
       <i>
        <b>
         with
        </b>
        my friends
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        <b>
         with
        </b>
        a knife
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suppletivism" title="Suppletivism">
        suppletivism
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        second
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        two
       </i>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       no distinction between alienable (e.g. legal property) and
       <a href="/wiki/Inalienable_possession" title="Inalienable possession">
        inalienable
       </a>
       (e.g. body part) possession;
      </li>
      <li>
       no distinction between
       <a href="/wiki/Clusivity" title="Clusivity">
        inclusive and exclusive
       </a>
       first-person plural pronouns ("we and you" vs."we and not you");
      </li>
      <li>
       no productive usage of
       <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">
        reduplication
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topic_(linguistics)" title="Topic (linguistics)">
        topic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Focus_(linguistics)" title="Focus (linguistics)">
        focus
       </a>
       expressed by intonation and word order;
      </li>
      <li>
       word order
       <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" title="Subjectverbobject">
        subjectverbobject
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       only one
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerund
       </a>
       , preference for finite subordinate clauses;
      </li>
      <li>
       specific "neither-nor" construction;
      </li>
      <li>
       phrasal adverbs (e.g. English
       <i>
        already
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        still
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        not yet
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       tendency towards replacement of
       <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">
        past tense
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">
        perfect
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      There is also a broad agreement in the following parameters (not listed in Haspelmath 2001):
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       absence of phonemic opposition
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">
        uvular
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       phonemic voicing oppositions (/p/ vs. /b/ etc.);
      </li>
      <li>
       initial consonant clusters of the type "stop+sonorant" allowed;
      </li>
      <li>
       only
       <a href="/wiki/Pulmonic_consonant" title="Pulmonic consonant">
        pulmonic consonants
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       at least three degrees of vowel height (minimum inventory
       <i>
        i e a o u
       </i>
       );
      </li>
      <li>
       lack of
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        lateral
       </a>
       fricatives and affricates;
      </li>
      <li>
       predominantly suffixing morphology;
      </li>
      <li>
       moderately synthetic
       <a href="/wiki/Fusional_language" title="Fusional language">
        fusional
       </a>
       morphological typology;
      </li>
      <li>
       nominativeaccusative
       <a href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic_alignment" title="Morphosyntactic alignment">
        morphosyntactic alignment
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Sprachbund
      </i>
      defined this way consists of the following languages:
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       ;
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Not all the languages listed above show all the listed features, so membership in SAE can be described as gradient. Based on nine of the above-mentioned common features, Haspelmath regards French and German as forming the core of the Sprachbund, while the Romance, Balkan, Nordic and Slavic languages as well as Hungarian (roughly in order of divergence from the core) form more peripheral groups. With the exception of Hungarian, all languages identified by Haspelmath as SAE are
      <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
       Indo-European languages
      </a>
      . However, not all Indo-European languages are SAE languages: the
      <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">
       Celtic
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armenian_languages" title="Armenian languages">
       Armenian
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">
       Indo-Iranian languages
      </a>
      remain outside the SAE
      <i>
       Sprachbund
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haspelmath_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-haspelmath-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Standard Average European
      <i>
       Sprachbund
      </i>
      is most likely the result of ongoing
      <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
       language contact
      </a>
      in the time of the
      <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">
       Migration Period
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haspelmath_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-haspelmath-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and later, continuing during the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
       Renaissance
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Inheritance of the SAE features from
      <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
       Proto-Indo-European
      </a>
      can be ruled out because Proto-Indo-European, as currently reconstructed, lacked most of the SAE features.
     </p>
     <p>
      <br/>
     </p>
