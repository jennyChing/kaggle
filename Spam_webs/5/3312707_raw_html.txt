             <div>
              <p>
               <em>
                This article discusses compellingand very nearly conclusiveevidence of Ebola fraud in the United States. The evidence we now have indicates, by force of deductive logic, that it cannot possibly be true that Dr. Kent Brantly and Nancy Writebol were both incompatible with Thomas Eric Duncan for plasma transfusion purposes.
               </em>
              </p>
              <p>
               That is, what follows shows that the U.S Government/MSMs plasma transfusion stories pertaining to Brantly and Writebol are necessarily false, since if they were true, either Brantly or Writebol (but not both) would have been compatible with Duncan for purposes of plasma transfusionbut we are told that neither was.
              </p>
              <p>
               Click
               <a href="http://www.myfoxcarolinas.com/story/26758136/ebola-survivor-nancy-writebol-offered-blood-to-thomas-duncan" target="_blank">
                here
               </a>
               to see that we are told that Writebol was not compatible with Duncan for plasma transfusion purposesor with
               <a href="http://www.nbcnews.com/storyline/ebola-virus-outbreak/american-ebola-survivor-nancy-writebol-dont-let-fear-take-hold-n224861" target="_blank">
                any of the other
               </a>
               U.S. Ebola patients either (at least including Sacra and Mukpo and probably Pham as well) either. Click
               <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Health/blood-transfusions-ebola-survivor-dr-kent-brantly-patients/story?id=26182136" target="_blank">
                here
               </a>
               to see that we are told that Brantly was not compatible with Duncan for plasma transfusion purposes either.
              </p>
              <p>
               Please note with respect to the subsequent analysis that donor/recipient compatibility for purposes of plasma transfusion is not the same as donor/recipient compatibility
               <a href="http://mytransfusion.com.au/node/blood-groups-and-compatibility" target="_blank">
                from the standpoint of whole blood compatibility
               </a>
               . Thus, as ABC News notes, for example: Though blood type O is considered the universal donor for whole blood, type AB is the universal donor for plasma, according to the Red Cross.
              </p>
              <p>
               Here is a table specifying blood type donor/recipient compatibility for the purpose of plasma transfusion, which is the sort of transfusion Brantly and Writebol (having
               <a href="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-2808175/New-York-Ebola-patient-Dr-Craig-Spencer-receives-blood-transfusion-survivor-Nancy-Writebol.html" target="_blank">
                supposedly donated
               </a>
               to the NYC doctor Craig Spencer) are said to have been involved in:
              </p>

                <tr>
                 <td>
                  O
                 </td>
                 <td style="text-align: center;">
                  O, AB, A, B
                 </td>
                </tr>
               </tbody>
              </table>
              <p>
               <a href="http://mytransfusion.com.au/node/blood-groups-and-compatibility" target="_blank">
                http://mytransfusion.com.au/node/blood-groups-and-compatibility
               </a>
              </p>
              <p data-ow-chain="orphan">
               Now consider that Brantly is said to have matched Sacra, Mukpo, and Pham but not Duncan.
              </p>
             </div>
            </div>
            <div>
             <div>
              <p data-ow-chain="widow">
               Writebol is said to have matched none of the four. As the chart shows, Writebol cannot have been AB, because AB is a universal donor for plasma purposes and so if she were AB, she would have matched everyone.
              </p>
              <p>
               But, Writebol cannot be O either, and that this is so follows from her own words, which appear in an October 2 interview she did with
               <a href="http://news.sciencemag.org/africa/2014/10/ebola-survivor-ii-nancy-writebol-we-just-dont-even-have-clue-what-happened" target="_blank">
                Science Insider
               </a>
               :
              </p>
              <p>
               Q: Did you have a blood transfusion?
              </p>
              <p>
               N.W.:I did. I had blood transfusions in Liberia and Emory. Neither was convalescent serum, though. There wasnt a match.
              </p>
              <p>
               As the above chart shows, however, if Writebols blood type for plasma transfusion purposes were O, she would have matched any plasma donor blood type. Therefore, she is not an O.
              </p>
              <p>
               And in case you are hesitating due to the supposition that convalescent serum and plasma refer to different things in the present context, consider that a plasma-type blood transfusion is exactly what Sacra, Mukpo, and Pham
               <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Health/blood-transfusions-ebola-survivor-dr-kent-brantly-patients/story?id=26182136" target="_blank">
                were said (over and over again) to have received
               </a>
               from Brantly,
               <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/news/morning-mix/wp/2014/10/14/the-decades-old-treatment-that-may-save-a-young-dallas-nurse-infected-with-ebola/" target="_blank">
                and that
               </a>
               , via the Washington Post:
              </p>
              <blockquote>
               <p>
                Injecting the blood of a patient such as Brantly, who has recovered from Ebola and developed certain antibodies, is a decades-old but promisingmethod of treatment that, academics and health officials agree, could beone of the best means to fight Ebola. Called a convalescent serum, it might alsosave Pham, an alum of Texas Christian University.
               </p>
               <p>
                Convalescent serum is high on our list of potential therapies and has been used in other outbreaks, a WHO spokesman
                <a href="http://news.sciencemag.org/africa/2014/08/who-ponders-treating-ebola-infected-people-blood-survivors" target="_blank">
                 told
                </a>
                Science magazine in August. There is a long history of its use, so lots of experience of what needs to be done, what norms and standards need to be met.
               </p>
              </blockquote>
              <p>
               Just in case you still need more convincing on this point, this language from another
               <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/news/to-your-health/wp/2014/10/08/ebola-survivor-kent-brantly-donates-blood-to-help-treat-nbc-cameraman-ashoka-mukpo/" target="_blank">
                Washington Post article
               </a>
               will very likely remove any residual doubt:
              </p>
              <blockquote>
               <p>
                The Nebraska hospital treating Ebola patient Ashoka Mukpo said that he is scheduled on Wednesday to receive the same convalescent serum from the same donor  Ebola survivor Kent Brantly  as American doctor Rick Sacra received several weeks ago.
               </p>
               <p>
                Mukpo, a freelance cameraman who was diagnosed with Ebola while working for the network in Liberia, will receive some of Brantlys blood as part of his treatment against the virus at the Nebraska Medical Center.
               </p>
               <p data-ow-chain="orphan">
                In September, Brantly, an American doctor,
                <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/news/to-your-health/wp/2014/09/11/ebola-survivor-kent-brantly-donates-blood-to-infected-american-being-treated-in-nebraska/" target="_blank">
                 donated some of his plasma
                </a>
                to Sacra, who was also treated at the Nebraska Medical Center.
               </p>
              </blockquote>
             </div>
            </div>
            <div>
             <p>
              So Writebol is neither AB nor O. Therefore, she is either A or B.
              <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Health/dr-kent-brantly-donate-blood-thomas-eric-duncan/story?id=26226388" target="_blank">
               Brantly, we are told
              </a>
              , is A. Writebols type cannot also be A, since Brantly, we are told, miraculously matched Sacra, Mukpo and Pham while Writebol, we are told, did not. We are left with B for Writebol. But Duncans family
              <a href="http://abcnews.go.com/Health/dr-kent-brantly-donate-blood-thomas-eric-duncan/story?id=26226388" target="_blank">
               has said that Duncan was also B
              </a>
              and so Writebol should have matched Duncan, but we are told she did not.
             </p>
             <p>
              The foregoing provides compelling reasons for concluding that the Government/MSM Axis plasma transfusion narratives are fraudulent. Obviously, this raises questions as whether the entirety of the U.S. Ebola narrative as presented by Government and the MSM is fraudulent.
             </p>
             <p>
              As noted at the outset, we now have another plasma transfusion narrative in regard to Writebol and the New York City doctor Craig Spencer. Might this plasma transfusion narrative be fraudulent as well, and might, therefore, the NYC Ebola case as well as the other United States cases amount to nothing more than mass-mediated fraud?
             </p>
