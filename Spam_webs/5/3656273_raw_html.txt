                <p align="left">
                 <br/>
                 Vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 has the largest and most complex chemical structure of all the
                 <a href="../glossary#vitamin">
                  vitamins
                 </a>
                 . It is unique among vitamins in that it contains a metal
                 <a href="../glossary#ion">
                  ion
                 </a>
                 , cobalt. For this reason cobalamin is the term used to refer to compounds having vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 activity. Methylcobalamin and 5-deoxyadenosylcobalamin are the forms of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 used in the human body
                 <a href="#references">
                  (1)
                 </a>
                 . The form of cobalamin used in most nutritional supplements and fortified foods, cyanocobalamin, is readily converted to 5-deoxyadenosylcobalamin and methylcobalamin in the body. In mammals, cobalamin is a
                 <a href="../glossary#cofactor">
                  cofactor
                 </a>
                 for only two
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzymes
                 </a>
                 , methionine synthase and L-methylmalonyl-coenzyme A mutase
                 <a href="#reference2">
                  (2)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>

                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="methionine-synthase-cofactor" name="methionine-synthase-cofactor">
                  </a>
                  Cofactor for methionine synthase
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 Methylcobalamin is required for the function of the folate-dependent
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzyme
                 </a>
                 , methionine synthase. This enzyme is required for the
                 <a href="../glossary#synthesis">
                  synthesis
                 </a>
                 of the
                 <a href="../glossary#amino-acid">
                  amino acid
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#methionine">
                  methionine
                 </a>
                 , from
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 . Methionine in turn is required for the synthesis of S-adenosylmethionine, a methyl group donor used in many biological
                 <a href="../glossary#methylation">
                  methylation
                 </a>
                 reactions, including the methylation of a number of sites within
                 <a href="../glossary#DNA">
                  DNA
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#RNA">
                  RNA
                 </a>
                 , and
                 <a href="../glossary#protein">
                  proteins
                 </a>
                 <a href="#reference3">
                  (3)
                 </a>
                 . Aberrant methylation of DNA and proteins, which causes alterations in
                 <a href="../glossary#chromatin">
                  chromatin
                 </a>
                 structure and
                 <a href="../glossary#gene-expression">
                  gene expression
                 </a>
                 , are a common feature of
                 <a href="../glossary#cancer">
                  cancer
                 </a>
                 cells. Inadequate function of methionine synthase can lead to an accumulation of homocysteine, which has been associated with increased risk of
                 <a href="../glossary#cardiovascular-disease">
                  cardiovascular disease
                 </a>
                 (
                 <strong>
                  Figure 1
                 </strong>
                 ).
                </p>

                <h3 align="center" style="text-align: left;">
                 <a id="deficiency" name="deficiency">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Deficiency
                 </strong>
                </h3>
                <p align="left">
                 In healthy adults, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency is uncommon, mainly because total body stores can exceed 2,500 mcg, daily turnover is slow, and dietary intake of only 2.4 mcg/day is sufficient to maintain adequate vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status (see
                 <a href="#RDA">
                  RDA
                 </a>
                 )
                 <a href="#reference4">
                  (4)
                 </a>
                 . In elderly individuals, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency is more common mainly because of impaired intestinal absorption that can result in marginal to severe vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in this population.
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <a id="deficiency-causes" name="deficiency-causes">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Causes of vitamin B
                  <sub>
                   12
                  </sub>
                  deficiency
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 Intestinal
                 <a href="../glossary#malabsorption-syndrome">
                  malabsorption
                 </a>
                 , rather than inadequate dietary intake, can explain most cases of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency
                 <a href="#reference5">
                  (5)
                 </a>
                 . Absorption of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 from food requires normal function of the stomach,
                 <a href="../glossary#pancreas">
                  pancreas
                 </a>
                 , and
                 <a href="../glossary#small-intestine">
                  small intestine
                 </a>
                 . Stomach acid and
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzymes
                 </a>
                 free vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 from food, allowing it to bind to R-protein (also known as transcobalamin-1 or haptocorrin), found in saliva and
                 <a href="../glossary#gastric">
                  gastric
                 </a>
                 fluids. In the
                 <a href="../glossary#alkaline">
                  alkaline
                 </a>
                 environment of the small intestine, R-proteins are degraded by pancreatic enzymes, freeing vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 to bind to intrinsic factor (IF), a protein secreted by specialized cells in the stomach.
                 <a href="../glossary#receptor">
                  Receptors
                 </a>
                 on the surface of the ileum (final part of the small intestine) take up the IF-B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 complex only in the presence of calcium, which is supplied by the pancreas
                 <a href="#reference5">
                  (5)
                 </a>
                 . Vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 can also be absorbed by passive diffusion, but this process is very inefficientonly about 1% absorption of the vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 dose is absorbed passively
                 <a href="#reference2">
                  (2)
                 </a>
                 . The prevalent causes of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency are (1) an
                 <a href="../glossary#autoimmune-disease">
                  autoimmune
                 </a>
                 condition known as
                 <a href="../glossary#pernicious-anemia">
                  pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 , and (2) a disorder called food-bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 malabsorption. Both conditions have been associated with a chronic
                 <a href="../glossary#inflammation">
                  inflammatory
                 </a>
                 disease of the stomach known as
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophic-gastritis">
                  atrophic gastritis
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <a id="atrophic-gastritis" name="atrophic-gastritis">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Atrophic gastritis
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophic-gastritis">
                  Atrophic gastritis
                 </a>
                 is thought to affect 10%-30% of people over 60 years of age
                 <a href="#reference6">
                  (6)
                 </a>
                 . The condition is frequently associated with the presence of autoantibodies directed towards stomach cells (see
                 <a href="#pernicious-anemia">
                  Pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 ) and/or infection by the
                 <a href="../glossary#bacteria">
                  bacteria
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <em>
                  Helicobacter pylori
                 </em>
                 (
                 <em>
                  H. pylori
                 </em>
                 )
                 <a href="#reference7">
                  (7)
                 </a>
                 .
                 <em>
                  H. pylori
                 </em>
                 infection induces chronic
                 <a href="../glossary#inflammation">
                  inflammation
                 </a>
                 of the stomach, which may progress to
                 <a href="../glossary#peptic-ulcer-disease">
                  peptic ulcer disease
                 </a>
                 , atrophic gastritis, and/or
                 <a href="../glossary#gastric">
                  gastric
                 </a>
                 <a href="../glossary#cancer">
                  cancer
                 </a>
                 in some individuals. Diminished gastric function in individuals with atrophic gastritis can result in bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine and cause food-bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 malabsorption. Vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 levels in
                 <a href="../glossary#serum">
                  serum
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#plasma">
                  plasma
                 </a>
                 , and gastric fluids are significantly decreased in individuals with
                 <em>
                  H. pylori
                 </em>
                 infection, and eradication of the bacteria has been shown to significantly improve vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 serum concentrations
                 <a href="#reference8">
                  (8)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <a id="pernicious-anemia" name="pernicious-anemia">
                 </a>
                 <em>
                  <strong>
                   Pernicious anemia
                  </strong>
                 </em>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 <a href="../glossary#pernicious-anemia">
                  Pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 has been estimated to be present in approximately 2% of individuals over 60 years of age
                 <a href="#reference9">
                  (9)
                 </a>
                 . Although
                 <a href="../glossary#anemia">
                  anemia
                 </a>
                 is often a symptom, the condition is actually the end stage of an
                 <a href="../glossary#autoimmune-disease">
                  autoimmune
                 </a>
                 <a href="../glossary#inflammation">
                  inflammation
                 </a>
                 of the stomach known as autoimmune
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophic-gastritis">
                  atrophic gastritis
                 </a>
                 , resulting in destruction of stomach cells by one's own
                 <a href="../glossary#antibody">
                  antibodies
                 </a>
                 (autoantibodies). Progressive destruction of the cells that line the stomach causes decreased secretion of acid and
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzymes
                 </a>
                 required to release food-bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 . Antibodies to intrinsic factor (IF) bind to IF preventing formation of the IF-B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 complex, further inhibiting vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 absorption. About 20% of the relatives of pernicious anemia patients also have the condition, suggesting a genetic predisposition. It is also thought that
                 <em>
                  H. pylori
                 </em>
                 infection could be involved in initiating the autoimmune response in a subset of individuals
                 <a href="#reference10">
                  (10)
                 </a>
                 . Further, co-occurrence of autoimmune atrophic gastritis with other autoimmune conditions, especially autoimmune thyroiditis and type 1
                 <a href="../glossary#diabetes-mellitus">
                  diabetes mellitus
                 </a>
                 , has been reported
                 <a href="#reference11">
                  (11, 12)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p align="left">
                 Treatment of pernicious anemia generally requires injections of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 to bypass intestinal absorption. High-dose oral supplementation is another treatment option, because consuming 1,000 mcg (1 mg)/day of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 orally should result in the absorption of about 10 mcg/day (1% of dose) by passive diffusion. In fact, high-dose oral therapy is considered to be as effective as intramuscular injection
                 <a href="#reference4">
                  (4)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <em>
                  <strong>
                   Food-bound vitamin B
                   <sub>
                    12
                   </sub>
                   malabsorption
                  </strong>
                 </em>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 Food-bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#malabsorption-syndrome">
                  malabsorption
                 </a>
                 is defined as an impaired ability to absorb food- or
                 <a href="../glossary#protein">
                  protein
                 </a>
                 -bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 ; individuals with this condition can fully absorb the free form
                 <a href="#reference13">
                  (13)
                 </a>
                 . While the condition is the major cause of poor vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status in the elderly population, it is usually associated with
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophic-gastritis">
                  atrophic gastritis
                 </a>
                 , a chronic
                 <a href="../glossary#inflammation">
                  inflammation
                 </a>
                 of the lining of the stomach that ultimately results in the loss of glands in the stomach (
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophy">
                  atrophy
                 </a>
                 ) and decreased stomach acid production (see
                 <a href="#atrophic-gastritis">
                  Atrophic gastritis
                 </a>
                 ). Because stomach acid is required for the release of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 from the proteins in food, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 absorption is diminished. Decreased stomach acid production also provides an environment conducive to the overgrowth of
                 <a href="../glossary#anaerobic">
                  anaerobic
                 </a>
                 <a href="../glossary#bacteria">
                  bacteria
                 </a>
                 in the stomach, which further interferes with vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 absorption
                 <a href="#reference3">
                  (3)
                 </a>
                 . Because vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in
                 <a href="../glossary#supplement">
                  supplements
                 </a>
                 is not bound to protein, and because intrinsic factor (IF) is still available, the absorption of supplemental vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 is not reduced as it is in
                 <a href="../glossary#pernicious-anemia">
                  pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 . Thus, individuals with food-bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 malabsorption do not have an increased requirement for vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 ; they simply need it in the crystalline form found in
                 <a href="../glossary#fortification">
                  fortified
                 </a>
                 foods and dietary supplements.
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <a id="other-deficiency-causes" name="other-deficiency-causes">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Other causes of vitamin B
                   <sub>
                    12
                   </sub>
                   deficiency
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 Other causes of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency include surgical resection of the stomach or portions of the
                 <a href="../glossary#small-intestine">
                  small intestine
                 </a>
                 where
                 <a href="../glossary#receptor">
                  receptors
                 </a>
                 for the IF-B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 complex are located. Conditions affecting the small intestine, such as
                 <a href="../glossary#malabsorption-syndrome">
                  malabsorption syndromes
                 </a>
                 (
                 <a href="../glossary#celiac-disease">
                  celiac disease
                 </a>
                 and tropical sprue), may also result in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency. Because the
                 <a href="../glossary#pancreas">
                  pancreas
                 </a>
                 provides critical
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzymes
                 </a>
                 , as well as calcium required for vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 absorption, pancreatic insufficiency may contribute to vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency. Since vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 is found only in foods of animal origin, a strict vegetarian (vegan) diet has resulted in cases of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency. Moreover, alcoholics may experience reduced intestinal absorption of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="#reference2">
                  (2)
                 </a>
                 , and individuals with
                 <a href="../glossary#AIDS">
                  acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS)
                 </a>
                 appear to be at increased risk of deficiency, possibly related to a failure of the IF-B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 receptor to take up the IF-B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 complex
                 <a href="#reference3">
                  (3)
                 </a>
                 . Further, long-term use of acid-reducing drugs has also been implicated in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency (see
                 <a href="#drug-interactions">
                  Drug interactions
                 </a>
                 ).
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Inherited disorders of vitamin B
                   <sub>
                    12
                   </sub>
                   absorption
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 Rare cases of inborn errors of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#metabolism">
                  metabolism
                 </a>
                 have been reported in the literature (reviewed in
                 <a href="#reference5">
                  5
                 </a>
                 ). Imerslund-Grsbeck syndrome is an inherited vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#malabsorption-syndrome">
                  malabsorption syndrome
                 </a>
                 that causes
                 <a href="../glossary#megaloblastic-anemia">
                  megaloblastic anemia
                 </a>
                 and
                 <a href="../glossary#neurologic">
                  neurologic
                 </a>
                 disorders of variable severity in affected subjects. Similar clinical symptoms are found in individuals with hereditary IF deficiency (also called congenital pernicious anemia) in whom the lack of IF results in the defective absorption of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 . Additionally,
                 <a href="../glossary#mutation">
                  mutations
                 </a>
                 affecting vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 transport in the body have been identified
                 <a href="#reference14">
                  (14)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="deficiency-symptoms" name="deficiency-symptoms">
                  </a>
                  Symptoms of vitamin B
                  <sub>
                   12
                  </sub>
                  deficiency
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 Vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency results in impairment of the activities of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 -requiring
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzymes
                 </a>
                 . Impaired activity of methionine synthase results in elevated
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 levels, while impaired activity of L-methylmalonyl-CoA mutase results in increased levels of a
                 <a href="../glossary#metabolite">
                  metabolite
                 </a>
                 of methylmalonyl-CoA called methylmalonic acid (MMA). While individuals with mild vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency may not experience symptoms, blood levels of homocysteine and/or MMA may be elevated
                 <a href="#reference15">
                  (15)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Megaloblastic anemia
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 Diminished activity of methionine synthase in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency inhibits the regeneration of tetrahydrofolate (THF) and traps
                 <a href="../vitamins/folate">
                  folate
                 </a>
                 in a form that is not usable by the body (
                 <strong>
                  Figure 3
                 </strong>
                 ), resulting in symptoms of folate deficiency even in the presence of adequate folate levels. Thus, in both folate and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiencies, folate is unavailable to participate in
                 <a href="../glossary#DNA">
                  DNA
                 </a>
                 <a href="../glossary#synthesis">
                  synthesis
                 </a>
                 . This impairment of DNA synthesis affects the rapidly dividing cells of the bone marrow earlier than other cells, resulting in the production of large, immature,
                 <a href="../glossary#hemoglobin">
                  hemoglobin
                 </a>
                 -poor red blood cells. The resulting
                 <a href="../glossary#anemia">
                  anemia
                 </a>
                 is known as
                 <a href="../glossary#megaloblastic-anemia">
                  megaloblastic anemia
                 </a>
                 and is the symptom for which the disease,
                 <a href="../glossary#pernicious-anemia">
                  pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 , was named
                 <a href="#reference3">
                  (3)
                 </a>
                 . Supplementation with folic acid will provide enough usable folate to restore normal red blood cell formation. However, if vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency is the cause, it will persist despite the resolution of the anemia. Thus, megaloblastic anemia should not be treated with folic acid until the underlying cause has been determined
                 <a href="#reference16">
                  (16)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p>
                 <a id="figure-3" name="figure-3">
                 </a>
                </p>

                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Neurologic symptoms
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 The
                 <a href="../glossary#neurologic">
                  neurologic
                 </a>
                 symptoms of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency include numbness and tingling of the hands and, more commonly, the feet; difficulty walking; memory loss; disorientation; and
                 <a href="../glossary#dementia">
                  dementia
                 </a>
                 with or without mood changes. Although the progression of neurologic complications is generally gradual, such symptoms may not be reversed with treatment of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency, especially if they have been present for a long time. Neurologic complications are not always associated with
                 <a href="../glossary#megaloblastic-anemia">
                  megaloblastic anemia
                 </a>
                 and are the only clinical symptom of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in about 25% of cases
                 <a href="#reference17">
                  (17)
                 </a>
                 . Although vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency is known to damage the
                 <a href="../glossary#myelin">
                  myelin
                 </a>
                 sheath covering cranial, spinal, and peripheral nerves, the biochemical processes leading to neurological damage in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency are not yet fully understood
                 <a href="#reference18">
                  (18)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Gastrointestinal symptoms
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 Tongue soreness, appetite loss, and constipation have also been associated with vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency. The origins of these symptoms are unclear, but they may be related to the stomach
                 <a href="../glossary#inflammation">
                  inflammation
                 </a>
                 underlying some cases of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency and to the progressive destruction of the lining of the stomach
                 <a href="#reference17">
                  (17)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>

                <h3 align="center" style="text-align: left;">
                 <a id="disease-prevention" name="disease-prevention">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Disease Prevention
                 </strong>
                </h3>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="cardiovascular-disease-prevention" name="cardiovascular-disease-prevention">
                  </a>
                  Cardiovascular disease
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 As mentioned above, chronic
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophic-gastritis">
                  atrophic gastritis
                 </a>
                 and infection by
                 <em>
                  H. pylori
                 </em>
                 can cause deficiency in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 secondary to
                 <a href="../glossary#malabsorption-syndrome">
                  malabsorption
                 </a>
                 disorders (see
                 <a href="#deficiency-causes">
                  Causes of vitamin B
                  <sub>
                   12
                  </sub>
                  deficiency
                 </a>
                 ). However, the occurrence of
                 <em>
                  H. pylori
                 </em>
                 infection and chronic atrophic gastritis did not modify the five-year incidence of
                 <a href="../glossary#cardiovascular">
                  cardiovascular
                 </a>
                 accidents (
                 <a href="../glossary#stroke">
                  stroke
                 </a>
                 and
                 <a href="../glossary#myocardial-infarction">
                  heart attack
                 </a>
                 ) or mortality in a large cohort study of nearly 10,000 men and women over 50 years old
                 <a href="#reference19">
                  (19)
                 </a>
                 . Yet, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status was not assessed in this study, despite the high prevalence of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in older individuals.
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Homocysteine and cardiovascular disease
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 <a href="../glossary#epidemiological-study">
                  Epidemiological studies
                 </a>
                 indicate that even moderately elevated levels of
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 in the blood raise the risk of
                 <a href="../glossary#cardiovascular-disease">
                  cardiovascular disease
                 </a>
                 (CVD)
                 <a href="#reference20">
                  (20)
                 </a>
                 , though the mechanism by which homocysteine may increase the CVD risk remains the subject of a great deal of research
                 <a href="#reference21">
                  (21)
                 </a>
                 . The amount of homocysteine in the blood is regulated by at least three vitamins:
                 <a href="../vitamins/folate">
                  folate
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../vitamins/vitamin-B6">
                  vitamin B
                  <sub>
                   6
                  </sub>
                 </a>
                 , and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 (see
                 <strong>
                  <a href="#figure-1">
                   Figure 1
                  </a>
                 </strong>
                 above). An early analysis of the results of 12
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-controlled-trial">
                  randomized controlled trials
                 </a>
                 showed that folic acid supplementation (0.5-5 mg/day) had the greatest lowering effect on blood homocysteine levels (25% decrease); co-supplementation with folic acid and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 (500 mcg/day) provided an additional 7% reduction (32% decrease) in blood homocysteine concentrations
                 <a href="#reference22">
                  (22)
                 </a>
                 . The results of a sequential supplementation trial in 53 men and women indicated that after folic acid supplementation, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 became the major determinant of
                 <a href="../glossary#plasma">
                  plasma
                 </a>
                 homocysteine levels
                 <a href="#reference23">
                  (23)
                 </a>
                 . It is thought that the elevation of homocysteine levels might be partly due to vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in individuals over 60 years of age. Two studies found blood methylmalonic acid (MMA) levels to be elevated in more than 60% of elderly individuals with elevated homocysteine levels. In the absence of impaired kidney function, an elevated MMA level in conjunction with elevated homocysteine suggests either a vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency or a combined vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 and folate deficiency
                 <a href="#reference24">
                  (24)
                 </a>
                 . Thus, it appears important to evaluate vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status, as well as kidney function, in older individuals with elevated homocysteine levels prior to initiating homocysteine-lowering therapy. For more information regarding homocysteine and CVD, see the article on
                 <a href="folate#homocysteine">
                  Folate
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Intervention studies
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 Although increased intake of folic acid and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 is effective in decreasing
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 levels, the combined intervention of these B vitamins did not lower risk for
                 <a href="../glossary#cardiovascular-disease">
                  CVD
                 </a>
                 . Indeed, several
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-design">
                  randomized
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#placebo">
                  placebo
                 </a>
                 -controlled trials have been conducted to determine whether homocysteine-lowering through folic acid, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  6
                 </sub>
                 supplementation reduces the incidence of CVD. A recent
                 <a href="../glossary#meta-analysis">
                  meta-analysis
                 </a>
                 of data from 11 trials, including nearly 45,000 participants at risk of CVD, showed that B-vitamin supplementation had no significant effect on risk of
                 <a href="../glossary#myocardial-infarction">
                  myocardial infarction
                 </a>
                 (heart attack) or
                 <a href="../glossary#stroke">
                  stroke
                 </a>
                 , nor did it modify the risk of all-cause mortality
                 <a href="#reference25">
                  (25)
                 </a>
                 . Other meta-analyses that included patients with chronic kidney disease have confirmed the lack of effect of homocysteine-lowering on risk of myocardial infarction and death. However, stroke risk was significantly reduced by 7%-12% with the B-vitamin supplementation
                 <a href="#reference26">
                  (26, 27)
                 </a>
                 . Another meta-analysis of 12
                 <a href="../glossary#clinical-trial">
                  clinical trials
                 </a>
                 measuring flow-mediated
                 <a href="../glossary#vasodilation">
                  vasodilation
                 </a>
                 (FMD; a surrogate marker of vascular health) in response to homocysteine reduction revealed that B-vitamin supplementation was accompanied by an improved FMD in short-term &lt;8 weeks) but not in long-term studies conducted in subjects with preexisting vascular diseases
                 <a href="#reference28">
                  (28)
                 </a>
                 . Yet, some of the studies included in these meta-analyses did not use vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , and folate administration on its own has shown a protective role on vascular function and stroke risk
                 <a href="#reference29">
                  (29)
                 </a>
                 . Besides, the high prevalence of
                 <a href="../glossary#malabsorption-syndrome">
                  malabsorption disorders
                 </a>
                 and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in elderly individuals might warrant the use of higher doses of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 than those used in these trials
                 <a href="#reference30">
                  (30)
                 </a>
                 ; in cases of malabsorption, only high-dose oral therapy or intramuscular injections can overcome vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency
                 <a href="#reference4">
                  (4)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="cancer-prevention" name="cancer-prevention">
                  </a>
                  Cancer
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 <a href="../vitamins/folate">
                  Folate
                 </a>
                 is required for
                 <a href="../glossary#synthesis">
                  synthesis
                 </a>
                 of
                 <a href="../glossary#DNA">
                  DNA
                 </a>
                 , and there is evidence that decreased availability of folate results in strands of DNA that are more susceptible to damage. Deficiency of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 traps folate in a form that is unusable by the body for DNA synthesis. Both vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 and folate deficiencies result in a diminished capacity for
                 <a href="../glossary#methylation">
                  methylation
                 </a>
                 reactions (see
                 <strong>
                  <a href="#figure-3">
                   Figure 3
                  </a>
                 </strong>
                 above). Thus, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency may lead to an elevated rate of DNA damage and altered methylation of DNA, both of which are important risk factors for
                 <a href="../glossary#cancer">
                  cancer
                 </a>
                 . A series of studies in young adults and older men indicated that increased levels of
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 and decreased levels of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in the blood were associated with a
                 <a href="../glossary#biomarker">
                  biomarker
                 </a>
                 of
                 <a href="../glossary#chromosome">
                  chromosome
                 </a>
                 breakage in white blood cells (reviewed in
                 <a href="#reference31">
                  31
                 </a>
                 ). In a
                 <a href="../glossary#double-blind">
                  double-blind
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#placebo">
                  placebo
                 </a>
                 -controlled study, the same biomarker of chromosome breakage was minimized in young adults who were supplemented with 700 mcg of folic acid and 7 mcg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 daily in cereal for two months
                 <a href="#reference32">
                  (32)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   <a id="breast-cancer-prevention" name="breast-cancer-prevention">
                   </a>
                   Breast cancer
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 A
                 <a href="../glossary#case-control-study">
                  case-control study
                 </a>
                 compared prediagnostic levels of
                 <a href="../glossary#serum">
                  serum
                 </a>
                 <a href="../vitamins/folate">
                  folate
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../vitamins/vitamin-B6">
                  vitamin B
                  <sub>
                   6
                  </sub>
                 </a>
                 , and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in 195 women later diagnosed with breast
                 <a href="../glossary#cancer">
                  cancer
                 </a>
                 and 195 age-matched, cancer-free women. Among postmenopausal women, the association between blood levels of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 and breast cancer suggested a
                 <a href="../glossary#threshold">
                  threshold
                 </a>
                 effect. The risk of breast cancer was more than doubled in women with
                 <a href="../glossary#serum">
                  serum
                 </a>
                 vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 levels in the lowest
                 <a href="../glossary#quintile">
                  quintile
                 </a>
                 compared to women in the four highest quintiles
                 <a href="#reference33">
                  (33)
                 </a>
                 . However, the
                 <a href="../glossary#meta-analysis">
                  meta-analysis
                 </a>
                 of this study with three additional case-control studies found no protection associated with high compared to low vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 serum levels
                 <a href="#reference34">
                  (34)
                 </a>
                 . A case-control study in Mexican women (475 cases and 1,391 controls) reported that breast cancer risk for women in the highest quartile of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 intake (7.3-7.7 mcg/day) was 68% lower than those in the lowest quartile (2.6 mcg/day). Stratification of the data revealed that the inverse association between dietary vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 intake and breast cancer risk was stronger in postmenopausal women compared to premenopausal women, though both associations were statistically significant. Moreover, among postmenopausal women, the apparent protection conferred by folate was only observed in women with the highest vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 quartiles of intake
                 <a href="#reference35">
                  (35)
                 </a>
                 . However, more recent case-control and
                 <a href="../glossary#prospective-cohort-study">
                  prospective cohort studies
                 </a>
                 have reported weak to no risk reduction with vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 intakes in different populations, including Hispanic, African American and European American women
                 <a href="#reference36">
                  (36, 37)
                 </a>
                 . A meta-analysis of seven case-control and seven prospective cohort studies concluded that the risk of breast cancer was not modified by high versus low vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 intakes
                 <a href="#reference34">
                  (34)
                 </a>
                 . There was no joint association between folate and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 intakes and breast cancer risk. Presently, there is little evidence to suggest a relationship between vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#status">
                  status
                 </a>
                 and breast cancer. In addition, results from
                 <a href="../glossary#observational-study">
                  observational studies
                 </a>
                 are not consistently in support of an association between high dietary folate intakes and reduced risk for breast cancer (see the article on
                 <a href="folate#breast-cancer-prevention">
                  Folate
                 </a>
                 ). There is a need to evaluate the effect of folate and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 supplementation in well-controlled,
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-design">
                  randomized
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#clinical-trial">
                  clinical trials
                 </a>
                 , while considering various factors that modify breast cancer risk, such as menopausal status, ethnicity, and alcohol intake.
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="neural-tube-defects-prevention" name="neural-tube-defects-prevention">
                  </a>
                  Neural tube defects
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 <a href="../glossary#neural-tube-defect">
                  Neural tube defects
                 </a>
                 (NTD) may result in
                 <a href="../glossary#anencephaly">
                  anencephaly
                 </a>
                 or
                 <a href="../glossary#spina-bifida">
                  spina bifida
                 </a>
                 , which are mostly fatal
                 <a href="../glossary#congenital-malformation">
                  congenital malformations
                 </a>
                 of the
                 <a href="../glossary#central-nervous-system">
                  central nervous system
                 </a>
                 . The defects arise from failure of embryonic neural tube to close, which occurs between the 21
                 <sup>
                  st
                 </sup>
                 and 28
                 <sup>
                  th
                 </sup>
                 days after conception, a time when many women are unaware of their pregnancy
                 <a href="#reference38">
                  (38)
                 </a>
                 .
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-controlled-trial">
                  Randomized controlled trials
                 </a>
                 have demonstrated 60% to 100% reductions in NTD cases when women consumed folic acid supplements in addition to a varied diet during the month before and the month after conception. Increasing evidence indicates that the
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 -lowering effect of folic acid plays a critical role in reducing the risk of NTD
                 <a href="#reference39">
                  (39)
                 </a>
                 . Homocysteine may accumulate in the blood when there is inadequate folate and/or vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 for effective functioning of the methionine synthase
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzyme
                 </a>
                 . Decreased vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 levels and elevated homocysteine concentrations have been found in the blood and amniotic fluid of pregnant women at high risk of NTD
                 <a href="#reference40">
                  (40)
                 </a>
                 . The recent
                 <a href="../glossary#meta-analysis">
                  meta-analysis
                 </a>
                 of 12
                 <a href="../glossary#case-control-study">
                  case-control studies
                 </a>
                 , including 567 mothers with current or prior NTD-affected pregnancy and 1,566 unaffected mothers, showed that low maternal vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status was associated with an increased risk of NTD
                 <a href="#reference41">
                  (41)
                 </a>
                 . Yet, whether vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 supplementation may be beneficial in the prevention of NTD has not been evaluated
                 <a href="#reference42">
                  (42)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="cognitive-decline-dementia-alzheimer-disease-prevention" name="cognitive-decline-dementia-alzheimer-disease-prevention">
                  </a>
                  Cognitive decline, dementia, and Alzheimer's disease
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 The occurrence of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency prevails in the elderly population and has been frequently associated with
                 <a href="../glossary#alzheimer-disease">
                  Alzheimer's disease
                 </a>
                 (reviewed in
                 <a href="#reference43">
                  43
                 </a>
                 ). One study found lower vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 levels in the
                 <a href="../glossary#cerebrospinal-fluid">
                  cerebrospinal fluid
                 </a>
                 of patients with Alzheimer's disease than in patients with other types of
                 <a href="../glossary#dementia">
                  dementia
                 </a>
                 , though blood levels of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 did not differ
                 <a href="#reference44">
                  (44)
                 </a>
                 . The reason for the association of low vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status with Alzheimer's disease is not clear. Vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency, like
                 <a href="../vitamins/folate">
                  folate
                 </a>
                 deficiency, may lead to decreased
                 <a href="../glossary#synthesis">
                  synthesis
                 </a>
                 of
                 <a href="../glossary#methionine">
                  methionine
                 </a>
                 and S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), thereby adversely affecting
                 <a href="../glossary#methylation">
                  methylation
                 </a>
                 reactions. Methylation reactions are essential for the
                 <a href="../glossary#metabolism">
                  metabolism
                 </a>
                 of components of the
                 <a href="../glossary#myelin">
                  myelin
                 </a>
                 sheath of nerve cells as well as for synthesis of
                 <a href="../glossary#neurotransmitter">
                  neurotransmitters
                 </a>
                 <a href="#reference18">
                  (18)
                 </a>
                 . Other metabolic implications of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency include the accumulation of homocysteine and methylmalonic acid, which might contribute to the neuropathologic features of dementia
                 <a href="#reference43">
                  (43)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Observational studies
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 A large majority of
                 <a href="../glossary#cross-sectional-study">
                  cross-sectional
                 </a>
                 and
                 <a href="../glossary#prospective-cohort-study">
                  prospective cohort studies
                 </a>
                 have associated elevated
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 concentrations with measures of poor
                 <a href="../glossary#cognitive">
                  cognitive
                 </a>
                 scores and increased risk of
                 <a href="../glossary#dementia">
                  dementia
                 </a>
                 , including
                 <a href="../glossary#alzheimer-disease">
                  Alzheimer's disease
                 </a>
                 (reviewed in
                 <a href="#reference45">
                  45
                 </a>
                 ). A
                 <a href="../glossary#case-control-study">
                  case-control study
                 </a>
                 of 164 patients with dementia of Alzheimer's type included 76 cases in which the diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease was confirmed by examination of brain cells after death. Compared to 108 control subjects without evidence of dementia, subjects with dementia of Alzheimer's type and confirmed Alzheimer's disease had higher blood homocysteine levels and lower blood levels of folate and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 . Measures of general nutritional status indicated that the association of increased homocysteine levels and diminished vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status with Alzheimer's disease was not due to dementia-related malnutrition
                 <a href="#reference46">
                  (46)
                 </a>
                 . In a sample of 1,092 men and women without dementia followed for an average of 10 years, those with higher
                 <a href="../glossary#plasma">
                  plasma
                 </a>
                 homocysteine levels at baseline had a significantly higher risk of developing Alzheimer's disease and other types of dementia. Specifically, those with plasma homocysteine levels greater than 14 micromol/L had nearly double the risk of developing Alzheimer's disease
                 <a href="#reference47">
                  (47)
                 </a>
                 . A study in 650 elderly men and women reported that the risk of elevated plasma homocysteine levels was significantly higher in those with lower cognitive function scores
                 <a href="#reference48">
                  (48)
                 </a>
                 . A prospective study in 816 elderly men and women reported that those with
                 <a href="../glossary#hyperhomocysteinemia">
                  hyperhomocysteinemia
                 </a>
                 (homocysteine levels &gt;15 micromol/L) had a significantly higher risk of developing Alzheimer's disease or dementia. Although raised homocysteine levels might be partly due to a poor vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status, the latter was not related to risk of Alzheimer's disease or dementia in this study
                 <a href="#reference49">
                  (49)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p align="left">
                 A recent
                 <a href="../glossary#systematic-review">
                  systematic review
                 </a>
                 of 35 prospective cohort studies assessing the association between vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status and cognitive deterioration in older individuals with or without dementia at baseline did not support a relationship between vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 serum concentrations and cognitive decline, dementia, or Alzheimer's disease
                 <a href="#reference50">
                  (50)
                 </a>
                 . Nevertheless, studies utilizing more sensitive
                 <a href="../glossary#biomarker">
                  biomarkers
                 </a>
                 of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#status">
                  status
                 </a>
                 , including measures of holo-transcobalamin (holo-TC; a vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 carrier) and methylmalonic acid, showed more consistent results and a trend toward associations between poor vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status and faster cognitive decline and risk of Alzheimer's disease
                 <a href="#reference51">
                  (51-55)
                 </a>
                 . Besides, it cannot be excluded that the co-occurrence of potential confounders like elevated homocysteine level and poor folate status might mitigate the true contribution of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status to cognitive functioning
                 <a href="#reference45">
                  (45)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h5 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <em>
                   Intervention studies
                  </em>
                 </strong>
                </h5>
                <p align="left">
                 High-dose B-vitamin supplementation has been proven effective for treating
                 <a href="../glossary#hyperhomocysteinemia">
                  hyperhomocysteinemia
                 </a>
                 in elderly individuals with or without
                 <a href="../glossary#cognitive">
                  cognitive
                 </a>
                 impairment. However,
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 -lowering trials have produced equivocal results regarding the prevention of cognitive deterioration in this population. A
                 <a href="../glossary#systematic-review">
                  systematic review
                 </a>
                 and
                 <a href="../glossary#meta-analysis">
                  meta-analysis
                 </a>
                 of 18
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-design">
                  randomized
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#placebo">
                  placebo
                 </a>
                 -controlled trials examining the effect of B-vitamin supplementation did not find that the decrease in homocysteine level prevented or delayed cognitive decline among older subjects
                 <a href="#reference56">
                  (56)
                 </a>
                 . A more recent randomized,
                 <a href="../glossary#double-blind">
                  double-blind
                 </a>
                 , placebo-controlled clinical trial in 900 older individuals at high risk of cognitive impairment found that daily supplementation of 400 mcg of folic acid and 100 mcg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 for two years significantly improved measures of immediate and delayed memory and slowed the rise in
                 <a href="../glossary#plasma">
                  plasma
                 </a>
                 homocysteine concentrations
                 <a href="#reference57">
                  (57)
                 </a>
                 . However, supplemented subjects had no reduction in homocysteine concentrations compared to baseline, nor did they perform better in processing speed tests compared to placebo. Another two-year, randomized, placebo-controlled study in elderly adults reported that a daily regimen of 800 mcg of folic acid, 500 mcg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , and 20 mg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  6
                 </sub>
                 significantly reduced the rate of brain
                 <a href="../glossary#atrophy">
                  atrophy
                 </a>
                 compared to placebo treatment (0.5% vs. 3.7%). Interestingly, a greater benefit was seen in those with high compared to low homocysteine concentrations at baseline, suggesting the importance of lowering homocysteine levels in prevention of brain atrophy and cognitive decline
                 <a href="#reference58">
                  (58, 59)
                 </a>
                 . The authors attributed the changes in homocysteine levels primarily to vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="#reference59">
                  (59)
                 </a>
                 . Finally, the most recent randomized, double blind, placebo-controlled trial in over 2,500 individuals who suffered a
                 <a href="../glossary#stroke">
                  stroke
                 </a>
                 showed that the normalization of homocysteine concentrations by B-vitamin supplementation (2 mg of folic acid, 500 mcg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , and 25 mg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  6
                 </sub>
                 ) did not improve cognitive performance or decrease incidence of cognitive decline compared to placebo
                 <a href="#reference60">
                  (60)
                 </a>
                 . Currently, there is a need for larger trials to evaluate the effect of B-vitamin supplementation on long-term outcomes, such as the incidence of
                 <a href="../glossary#alzheimer-disease">
                  Alzheimer's disease
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <a id="depression-prevention" name="depression-prevention">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Depression
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 <a href="../glossary#observational-study">
                  Observational studies
                 </a>
                 have found as many as 30% of patients hospitalized for depression are deficient in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="#reference61">
                  (61)
                 </a>
                 . A
                 <a href="../glossary#cross-sectional-study">
                  cross-sectional study
                 </a>
                 of 700 community-living, physically disabled women over the age of 65 found that vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 -deficient women were twice as likely to be severely depressed as non-deficient women
                 <a href="#reference62">
                  (62)
                 </a>
                 . A population-based study in 3,884 elderly men and women with depressive disorders found that those with vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency were almost 70% more likely to experience depression than those with normal vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#status">
                  status
                 </a>
                 <a href="#reference63">
                  (63)
                 </a>
                 . The reasons for the relationship between vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency and depression are not clear but may involve a shortage in S-adenosylmethionine (SAM). SAM is a methyl group donor for numerous
                 <a href="../glossary#methylation">
                  methylation
                 </a>
                 reactions in the brain, including those involved in the
                 <a href="../glossary#metabolism">
                  metabolism
                 </a>
                 of
                 <a href="../glossary#neurotransmitter">
                  neurotransmitters
                 </a>
                 whose deficiency has been related to depression
                 <a href="#reference64">
                  (64)
                 </a>
                 . Severe vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in a mouse model showed dramatic alterations in the level of
                 <a href="../glossary#DNA">
                  DNA
                 </a>
                 methylation in the brain, which might lead to
                 <a href="../glossary#neurologic">
                  neurologic
                 </a>
                 impairments
                 <a href="#reference65">
                  (65)
                 </a>
                 . This hypothesis is supported by several studies that have shown supplementation with SAM improves depressive symptoms
                 <a href="#reference66">
                  (66-69)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p align="left">
                 Increased
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 level is another nonspecific
                 <a href="../glossary#biomarker">
                  biomarker
                 </a>
                 of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency that has been linked to depressive symptoms in the elderly
                 <a href="#reference70">
                  (70)
                 </a>
                 . However, in a recent cross-sectional study conducted in 1,677 older individuals, higher vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="../glossary#plasma">
                  plasma
                 </a>
                 levels, but not changes in homocysteine concentrations, were correlated with a lower prevalence of depressive symptoms
                 <a href="#reference71">
                  (71)
                 </a>
                 . Few studies have examined the relationship of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 status, homocysteine levels, and the development of depression over time. In a
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-design">
                  randomized
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#placebo">
                  placebo
                 </a>
                 -controlled,
                 <a href="../glossary#intervention-trial">
                  intervention study
                 </a>
                 with over 900 older participants experiencing psychological distress, daily supplementation with folic acid (400 mcg) and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 (100 mcg) for two years did not reduce the occurrence of symptoms of depression despite significantly improving blood folate, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , and homocysteine levels compared to placebo
                 <a href="#reference72">
                  (72)
                 </a>
                 . However, in a long-term randomized,
                 <a href="../glossary#double-blind">
                  double-blind
                 </a>
                 , placebo-controlled study among sufferers of cerebrovascular accidents at high risk of depression, daily supplementation with 2 mg of folic acid, 25 mg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  6
                 </sub>
                 , and 500 mcg vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 significantly lowered the risk of major depressive episodes during a seven-year follow-up period compared to placebo
                 <a href="#reference73">
                  (73)
                 </a>
                 . Although it cannot yet be determined whether vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency plays a causal role in depression, it may be beneficial to screen for vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in older individuals as part of a medical evaluation for depression.
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <a id="osteoporosis-prevention" name="osteoporosis-prevention">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Osteoporosis
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 High
                 <a href="../glossary#homocysteine">
                  homocysteine
                 </a>
                 levels may affect
                 <a href="../glossary#bone-remodeling">
                  bone remodeling
                 </a>
                 by increasing bone
                 <a href="../glossary#resorption">
                  resorption
                 </a>
                 (breakdown), decreasing bone formation, and reducing bone blood flow. Another proposed mechanism involves the binding of homocysteine to the
                 <a href="../glossary#collagenous-matrix">
                  collagenous matrix
                 </a>
                 of bone, which may modify
                 <a href="../glossary#collagen">
                  collagen
                 </a>
                 properties and reduce bone strength (reviewed in
                 <a href="#reference74">
                  74
                 </a>
                 ). Alterations of bone biomechanical properties can contribute to
                 <a href="../glossary#osteoporosis">
                  osteoporosis
                 </a>
                 and increase the risk of
                 <a href="../glossary#fracture">
                  fractures
                 </a>
                 in the elderly. Since vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 is a determinant of homocysteine metabolism, it was suggested that the risk of osteoporotic fractures in older subjects might be enhanced by vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency. A
                 <a href="../glossary#meta-analysis">
                  meta-analysis
                 </a>
                 of four
                 <a href="../glossary#observational-study">
                  observational studies
                 </a>
                 , following a total of 7,475 older individuals for 3 to 16 years, found a weak association between an elevation in vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 of 50 picomoles/L in blood and a reduction in fracture risk
                 <a href="#reference75">
                  (75)
                 </a>
                 . A
                 <a href="../glossary#randomized-design">
                  randomized
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="../glossary#placebo">
                  placebo
                 </a>
                 -controlled trial in 559 elderly individuals with low
                 <a href="../glossary#serum">
                  serum
                 </a>
                 levels of folate and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 and at increased risk of fracture evaluated the combined supplementation of very high doses of folic acid (5 mg/day) and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 (1.5 mg/day). The two-year study found that the supplementation improved B-vitamin status, decreased homocysteine concentrations, and reduced risk of total fractures compared to placebo
                 <a href="#reference76">
                  (76)
                 </a>
                 . However, a multicenter study in 5,485 subjects with
                 <a href="../glossary#cardiovascular-disease">
                  cardiovascular disease
                 </a>
                 or
                 <a href="../glossary#diabetes-mellitus">
                  diabetes mellitus
                 </a>
                 showed that daily supplementation with folic acid (2.5 mg), vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 (1 mg), and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  6
                 </sub>
                 (50 mg) lowered homocysteine concentrations but had no effect on fracture risk compared to placebo
                 <a href="#reference77">
                  (77)
                 </a>
                 . Another small, randomized,
                 <a href="../glossary#double-blind">
                  double-blind
                 </a>
                 trial in 93 individuals with low vitamin D status found no additional benefit of B-vitamin supplementation (50 mg/day of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  6
                 </sub>
                 , 0.5 mg/day of folic acid, and 0.5 mg/day of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 ) on markers of bone health over a one-year period beyond that associated with vitamin D and calcium supplementation. Yet, the short-scale of the study did not permit a conclusion on whether the lowering of homocysteine through B-vitamin supplementation could have long-term benefits on bone strength and fracture risk
                 <a href="#reference78">
                  (78)
                 </a>
                 . A large
                 <a href="../glossary#intervention-trial">
                  intervention study
                 </a>
                 conducted in older people with no preexisting conditions is under way to evaluate the effect of B-vitamin supplementation on markers of bone health and incidence of fracture; this trial might clarify whether B vitamins could have a protective effect on bone health in the elderly population
                 <a href="#reference79">
                  (79)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h3 align="center" style="text-align: left;">
                 <a id="sources" name="sources">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Sources
                 </strong>
                </h3>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <a id="food-sources" name="food-sources">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Food sources
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 Only
                 <a href="../glossary#bacteria">
                  bacteria
                 </a>
                 can
                 <a href="../glossary#synthesis">
                  synthesize
                 </a>
                 vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="#reference80">
                  (80)
                 </a>
                 . Vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 is present in animal products, such as meat, poultry, fish (including shellfish), and to a lesser extent dairy products and eggs
                 <a href="#references">
                  (1)
                 </a>
                 . Fresh pasteurized milk contains 0.9 mcg per cup and is an important source of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 for some vegetarians
                 <a href="#reference17">
                  (17)
                 </a>
                 . Those strict vegetarians who eat no animal products (vegans) need
                 <a href="../glossary#supplement">
                  supplemental
                 </a>
                 vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 to meet their requirements. Recent analyses revealed that some plant-source foods, such as certain fermented beans and vegetables and edible algae and mushrooms, contain substantial amounts of bioactive vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 <a href="#reference81">
                  (81)
                 </a>
                 . Together with B-vitamin
                 <a href="../glossary#fortification">
                  fortified
                 </a>
                 food and supplements, these foods may contribute, though modestly, to prevent vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency in individuals consuming vegetarian diets. Also, individuals over the age of 50 should obtain their vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in supplements or fortified foods (e.g., fortified cereals) because of the increased likelihood of food-bound vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 malabsorption with increasing age.
                </p>
                <p align="left">
                 Most people do not have a problem obtaining the
                 <a href="../glossary#RDA">
                  RDA
                 </a>
                 of 2.4 mcg/day of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in food. According to a US national survey, the average dietary intake of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>

                 <sub>

                 </sub>

                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="supplements" name="supplements">
                  </a>
                  Supplements
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 Cyanocobalamin is the principal form of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 used in oral
                 <a href="../glossary#supplement">
                  supplements
                 </a>
                 , but methylcobalamin is also available as a supplement. Cyanocobalamin is available by prescription in an injectable form and as a nasal gel for the treatment of
                 <a href="../glossary#pernicious-anemia">
                  pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 . Over-the-counter preparations containing cyanocobalamin include multivitamins, vitamin B-complex supplements, and single-nutrient, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 supplements
                 <a href="#reference83">
                  (83)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h3 align="center" style="text-align: left;">
                 <a id="safety" name="safety">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Safety
                 </strong>
                </h3>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <a id="toxicity" name="toxicity">
                 </a>
                 <strong>
                  Toxicity
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 No toxic or adverse effects have been associated with large intakes of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 from food or supplements in healthy people. Doses as high as 2 mg (2,000 mcg) daily by mouth or 1 mg monthly by intramuscular (IM) injection have been used to treat
                 <a href="../glossary#pernicious-anemia">
                  pernicious anemia
                 </a>
                 without significant side effects
                 <a href="#reference84">
                  (84)
                 </a>
                 . When high doses of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 are given orally, only a small percentage can be absorbed, which may explain the low toxicity
                 <a href="#reference4">
                  (4)
                 </a>
                 . Because of the low toxicity of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , no tolerable upper intake level (
                 <a href="../glossary#UL">
                  UL
                 </a>
                 ) has been set by the US Food and Nutrition Board
                 <a href="#reference17">
                  (17)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="drug-interactions" name="drug-interactions">
                  </a>
                  Drug interactions
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 A number of drugs reduce the absorption of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 . Proton-pump inhibitors (e.g., omeprazole and lansoprazole), used for therapy of
                 <a href="../glossary#Zollinger-Ellison-syndrome">
                  Zollinger-Ellison syndrome
                 </a>
                 and
                 <a href="../glossary#gastroesophageal-reflux-disease">
                  gastroesophageal reflux disease
                 </a>
                 (GERD), markedly decrease stomach acid secretion required for the release of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 from food but not from
                 <a href="../glossary#supplement">
                  supplements
                 </a>
                 . Long-term use of proton-pump inhibitors has been found to decrease blood vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 levels. However, vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency does not generally develop until after at least three years of continuous therapy
                 <a href="#reference85">
                  (85, 86)
                 </a>
                 . Another class of gastric acid inhibitors known as Histamine
                 <sub>
                  2
                 </sub>
                 (H
                 <sub>
                  2
                 </sub>
                 )-receptor antagonists (e.g., cimetidine, famotidine, and ranitidine), often used to treat
                 <a href="../glossary#peptic-ulcer-disease">
                  peptic ulcer disease
                 </a>
                 , has also been found to decrease the absorption of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 from food. It is not clear whether the long-term use of H
                 <sub>
                  2
                 </sub>
                 -receptor antagonists could cause overt vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency
                 <a href="#reference87">
                  (87, 88)
                 </a>
                 . Individuals taking drugs that inhibit
                 <a href="../glossary#gastric">
                  gastric
                 </a>
                 acid secretion should consider taking vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in the form of a supplement because gastric acid is not required for its absorption. Other drugs found to inhibit vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 absorption from food include cholestyramine (a
                 <a href="../glossary#bile-acids">
                  bile acid
                 </a>
                 -binding resin used in the treatment of high
                 <a href="../glossary#cholesterol">
                  cholesterol
                 </a>
                 ), chloramphenicol and neomycin (antibiotics), and colchicine (medicine for
                 <a href="../glossary#gout">
                  gout
                 </a>
                 treatment). Metformin, a medication for individuals with type 2
                 <a href="../glossary#diabetes-mellitus">
                  diabetes
                 </a>
                 , was found to decrease vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 absorption by tying up free calcium required for absorption of the IF-B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 complex
                 <a href="#reference89">
                  (89)
                 </a>
                 . However, the clinical significance of this is unclear
                 <a href="#reference90">
                  (90)
                 </a>
                 . It is not known whether calcium supplementation can reverse vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 malabsorption; therefore, calcium supplementation is not currently prescribed for the prevention or treatment of metformin-induced vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency
                 <a href="#reference91">
                  (91)
                 </a>
                 . Previous reports that megadoses of vitamin C destroy vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 have not been supported
                 <a href="#reference92">
                  (92)
                 </a>
                 and may have been an artifact of the assay used to measure vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 levels
                 <a href="#reference17">
                  (17)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p align="left">
                 Nitrous oxide, a commonly used anesthetic, oxidizes and inactivates vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 , thus inhibiting both of the vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 -dependent
                 <a href="../glossary#enzyme">
                  enzymes
                 </a>
                 , and can produce many of the clinical features of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency, such as
                 <a href="../glossary#megaloblastic-anemia">
                  megaloblastic anemia
                 </a>
                 or
                 <a href="../glossary#neuropathy">
                  neuropathy
                 </a>
                 . Since nitrous oxide is commonly used for surgery in the elderly, some experts feel vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency should be ruled out prior to its use
                 <a href="#reference6">
                  (6
                 </a>
                 ,
                 <a href="#reference15">
                  15)
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p align="left">
                 Large doses of folic acid given to an individual with an undiagnosed vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency could correct megaloblastic anemia without correcting the underlying vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency, leaving the individual at risk of developing irreversible
                 <a href="../glossary#neurologic">
                  neurologic
                 </a>
                 damage
                 <a href="#reference17">
                  (17)
                 </a>
                 . For this reason, the Food and Nutrition Board of the US Institute of Medicine advises that all adults limit their intake of folic acid (supplements and
                 <a href="../glossary#fortification">
                  fortification
                 </a>
                 ) to 1,000 mcg (1 mg) daily.
                </p>
                <h3 align="center" style="text-align: left;">
                 <strong>
                  <a id="LPI-recommendation" name="LPI-recommendation">
                  </a>
                  Linus Pauling Institute Recommendation
                 </strong>
                </h3>
                <p align="left">
                 A varied diet should provide enough vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 to prevent deficiency in most individuals 50 years of age and younger. Strict vegetarians and women planning to become pregnant should take a multivitamin
                 <a href="../glossary#supplement">
                  supplement
                 </a>
                 daily or eat
                 <a href="../glossary#fortification">
                  fortified
                 </a>
                 cereal, which would ensure a daily intake of 6 to 30 mcg of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 in a form that is easily absorbed. Higher doses of vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 supplements are recommended for patients taking medications that interfere with its absorption (see
                 <a href="#drug-interactions">
                  Drug interactions
                 </a>
                 ).
                </p>
                <h4 align="left">
                 <strong>
                  Older adults (&gt;50 years)
                 </strong>
                </h4>
                <p align="left">
                 Because vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 malabsorption and vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 deficiency are more common in older adults, the Linus Pauling Institute recommends that adults older than 50 years take 100 to 400 mcg/day of supplemental vitamin B
                 <sub>
                  12
                 </sub>
                 .
                </p>
