     <h1 class="viewpoint__title h1-text">
      Memes are a myth
     </h1>
     <div class="viewpoint-card-expanded">
      <img alt="Thumb aeon" class="viewpoint__avatar viewpoint-card-expanded__avatar author__avatar author__avatar--tiny" src="https://omicron.aeon.co/avatars/daac3f9f-cb76-4fa8-bc62-f79b14fd8593/thumb_aeon.jpg"/>
      <p class="author viewpoint__author">
       <a href="/users/jonathan-goodman">
        <strong class="author__name">
         Jonathan Goodman
        </strong>
        <span class="author__role">
         Postgraduate Student, Birkbeck Colleg...
        </span>
        <span class="author__expertise">
         <i class="fa fa-star">
         </i>
         Philosophy
        </span>
       </a>
      </p>
      <div class="viewpoint__content viewpoint-card-expanded__content">
       <div class="viewpoint__content__inner viewpoint-card-expanded__content__inner">
        <p>
         Probably not. But everywhere you turn today, it seems youre bumping into memes. Since its introduction almost 40 years ago, the term has taken root in science, language, and popular culture. But the actual meaning of meme remains elusive.
        </p>
        <p>
         Richard Dawkins, the famed evolutionary biologist and, more recently, outspoken neo-Atheist, first introduced the term meme in his 1976 book,
         <em>
          The Selfish Gene
         </em>
         . Like many other natural scientists, Dawkins was interested in the possibility that culture evolves much like species do. But what were the units of selection? How or why is it that ideas seem to catch onmuch like religionin ways that dont promote an individuals genetic success?
        </p>
        <p>
         The memeshort for mimeme, which is Greek for that which is imitatedwas Dawkinss solution to the problem of how culture evolves. Memes are like genes in that they survive and propagate, but they do so by using people as vessels for propagation, much like Dawkins thinks selfish genes do. Memes are units of cultural evolutionthey are evolved so that we hold on to them, whether they are songs we like, architecture we find attractive, or beliefs we adopt. These memes, according to Dawkinss analogy, use us to transmit themselves just like genes do. They evolve, they propagate, and they spread from person to person in a way that makes culture what it is.
        </p>
        <p>
         But do memes have real, physical existence? Many researchers think they do. In
         <em>
          The Electric Meme
         </em>
         , biological anthropologist-by-training Robert Aunger describes how memes cause changes in our brains without recourse to DNA. Memes, he thinks, are real entities that effect changes in our neural pathways. And researchers have spent the past several decades trying to find memes in the physical world. In the new field of evolutionary neuroscience, people are spending a lot of time (and grant money) trying to find memes in the human brain. Many of these people think that we catch memes because our brains are structured to imitate; certain neurons, called mirror neurons fire both in an acting agents and in an observers brain, facilitate this process. If you tell me about an idea or an image or some other unit of culture that impresses me as it did you, some neuron will fire in my brain mirroring one in yours; I will have caught the meme!
        </p>
        <p>
         The philosopher Daniel Dennett claims in his book on evolution,
         <em>
          Darwins Dangerous Idea
         </em>
         , that Darwin would have hailed Dawkins for his discovery of the meme. Freelance writer and parapsychologist Susan Blackmore wrote perhaps the most influential book on memes,
         <em>
          The Meme Machine
         </em>
         , where she invented an entire theory of cultural evolution, purporting to explain everything from pop music to altruism. She claims that memes compete for our acceptance of them, and its not always the good memes that win. (Religion is typically every meme-theorists favorite example.) Memes form groups, called memeplexes, which take a strength-in-numbers approach to strong-arm other memes out of existence of the meme-pool (which parallels with the gene-pool). Every day cultural selection chooses memes that are better adapted to their environments and those memes continue to evolve, while other, less happy memes die out in the cold, exiled from our brains. In his review of her book, Matt Ridleyauthor of several successful popular science bookssays that the book draws attention to the fact that we human beings are not, as we fondly think, the authors of our own thoughts so much as the victims and receptacles of independent ideas. But we need to ask:where is the evidence for this meme-catching and cultural evolution?
        </p>
        <p>
         Physicists and cosmologists have been searching, with up until recently little success, for the Higgs-Boson particle for yearsotherwise known as the God-particlethe discovery of which would help to explain mass in subatomic particles. Talk of the God-particle derives from the understanding that there are certain processes in the physical world that we cannot explain with the scientific evidence we have today. The God-particle is the piece to a puzzle that makes other things fit together. And, according to the ideas proponents, so it is with memetics: since Darwin, evolutionists have sought to explain how culture evolved, and why we are not just automatons, spreading our genes throughout the world however we can. Memetics gives us the
         <em>
          illusion
         </em>
         of a solution, so now the memeticians are eager to prove their theory right, just as the physicists are anxious to find the God-particle. The only difference is that physics and cosmology are real sciences. Memetics is just another way of saying that culture evolves according to analogous laws to genetic natural selection. It sounds nice, and perhaps the meme is a useful analogybut it is
         <em>
          just
         </em>
         an analogy.
        </p>
        <p>
         We should ask Dawkins and his followers: who wins, the person who popularizes an idea, or the idea itself, cast successfully into a population? I submit that the answer is indeterminate, no matter what neuroscience purports to tell us. It is too easy to say that we just havent the technology to find memes yet. Dawkins famously objects to religion on the grounds that theists rely on blind faith. But just as we dont have the technology to prove that a deity exists, neither has it been shown that memes are real. So how is belief in the existence of memes different from believing in G-d, or Shiva, or Odin? Should we take the existence of memes on scientific faith, or countenance the parsimonious explanation that ideas are just ideas that we use for better or for worse?
        </p>
        <p>
         The story of memetics, if anything, is evidence that we need not try to explain everything in terms of evolution, or algorithms based in evolutionary theory. There is no reason to invent a language or a science, or insert some entirely fictitious evolutionary notion, every time we have a question that seems unanswerable. Let science be science, and myth be myth.
        </p>
