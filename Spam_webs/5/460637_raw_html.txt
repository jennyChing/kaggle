         <p>
          Californias low-end workers will all see bumps in their next paychecks as the first increase in the states minimum wage in six years takes effect.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          The increase, to $9 per hour from $8 per hour, officially becomes the state standard Tuesday. It will go to $10 per hour on Jan. 1, 2016.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          The law adopting the increase was initiated late in 2012 by Assemblyman Luis Alejo, a Salinas-area Democrat. It went through the legislative process, passing through committee and full Assembly and Senate votes almost completely along party lines before being signed into law by Gov. Jerry Brown in September.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          A second bill in California would raise the minimum wage to $11 per hour on Jan. 1, 2015, with an additional $1 to be added at the start of each of the next two years. The bill was ratified by the state Senate last month but has not been voted on by the Assembly. It is not known how the overlapping bills would be legally framed.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          It amounts to changing economic environments for workers and businesses. According to a recent report by Beacon Economics, a Los Angeles-based consulting firm, California saw a 2.8 percent increase in its gross state product in the first quarter of 2014, meaning the states economy is growing faster than the nations as a whole.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          However, the growth has been erratic, leaving business owners feeling vulnerable. And, growth is probably slower in the Inland area.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          Here are five things to keep in mind when the increase starts.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          <b>
           For workers
          </b>
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          It is not known how many Inland Southern California workers earn the minimum. However, according to the most recent Employment Development Department data, there are almost 350,000 people in Riverside and San Bernardino counties who work in the retail and accommodations industries and through temporary agencies.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          That is about 27 percent of the Inland workforce. And while not all are minimum wage workers, many are, and these are among the three fastest-growing job sectors in the Inland region in 2014.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          A minimum wage worker who puts in 40 hours a week makes about $16,650 per year, which would keep two people living together officially out of poverty. But the poverty threshold for a family of four is almost $24,000, according to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, which means that families supported by minimum wage jobs are not supported well.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          Opponents say minimum wage is intended as a starting point, mainly for teens, and not as a wage with which a family should be supported. However, a 2011 U.S. Census Bureau report contended that an estimated 900,000 people in California would benefit from an increase, and one-third of all workers who made less than $10 per hour were between the ages of 21 and 30.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          <b>
           For employers
          </b>
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          The ones who will feel the pinch most are probably independent retailers, who depend on through-the-door cash flow to survive. If their payrolls have to increase, the owners will probably look for other ways to make up the difference.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
         <!--googleon: all-->
         <p>
          That problem might be solved by raising prices. Many do not have the flexibility to cut payrolls; restaurants still need enough workers to cook, clean and wait on tables.
          <!--googleoff: all-->
         </p>
