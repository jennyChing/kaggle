      <h1>
       Growing sunflowers
       <br/>
       Easy, sun-loving annuals
      </h1>
      <p>
       Growing sunflowers attracts birds, bees and butterflies to
your garden. I used to plant these sunny bloomers at my country garden
for years, and it amused me each spring to find them in unexpected spots
where birds dropped seeds.
      </p>
      <div class="AdSenseBoxLeft">
       <script type="text/javascript">
        google_ad_client="ca-pub-5317526355726678";google_ad_slot="3214272087";google_ad_width=336;google_ad_height=280;
       </script>
       <script src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/show_ads.js" type="text/javascript">
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       The bird-planted volunteers always made me smile in
appreciation of nature's happy accident.
      </p>
      <h2>
       How to grow your own sunflowers
      </h2>
      <p class="ItemRight">
       <img alt="growing sunflowers" src="http://www.flower-gardening-made-easy.com/image-files/sunflower.jpg" style="width: 300px; height: 250px;"/>
       <span class="Caption">
        Loving
the sunshine
        <br/>
        Photo: Y.Cunnington
       </span>
      </p>
      Just like their name, sunflowers adore full sun and warm weather.
      <p>
       A North American native, this plant was a vital 
crop
for native peoples.
      </p>
      <p>
       Today, sunflowers are still grown for food, and not
just for birds:
their
seeds make a wholesome snack and are pressed into oil.
      </p>
      <p>
       Late spring is time to
       <a href="http://www.flower-gardening-made-easy.com/sunflowers-from-seed.html" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">
        sow
sunflower seeds
       </a>
       indoors for a head start. You can also direct
sow them into the garden once all danger of frost is passed.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Growing sunflowers: pollenless cutting types
      </h2>
      <p>
       In recent years, sunflowers have come into their own as
cutting
flowers. There are lots of new cultivars in many shapes - from standard
ray flowers to pompoms or fuzzy disks.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sizes range from dwarf to giant and as for color, that too now
goes
well beyond the standard sunny yellow into white, lemon, bicolors, and
even dusky burgundy.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Unique cultivars to grow
      </h2>
      <p>
       If you're interested in growing sunflowers, there are lots of
new
cultivars to try, many of them pollenless, which makes them perfect for
       <a href="http://www.flower-gardening-made-easy.com/bouquets-from-the-garden.html" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">
        cutting
       </a>
       and bringing into the house. Here are some of my favorites:
      </p>
      <p class="ItemRight">
       <img alt="growing sunflowers" src="http://www.flower-gardening-made-easy.com/image-files/sunflowerringfire.jpg" style="width: 150px; height: 158px;"/>
       <span class="Caption">
        Sunflower
Ring of Fire
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Ring of Fire:
       </strong>
       An AAS
(All-America-Selections)
winner with striking 5-inch wide pollenless flowers; bi-coloured petals
in dark red around a chocolate brown center taper off to golden yellow
tips. Grows 4 to 5 feet tall; late-blooming.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Claret:
       </strong>
       An excellent cutting
sunflower that keeps
its intense velvet burgundy color in the summer sun. Main stem grows 6
feet tall; produces side-shoots with lots of blooms.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Eversun:
       </strong>
       A deep yellow, early-blooming
pollenless type
with big 10- to 12-inch ray flowers around a dark brown disk; flowers
produced on one main stem.
      </p>
      <p class="ItemRight">
       <img alt="growing sunflowers" src="http://www.flower-gardening-made-easy.com/image-files/sunflower-teddybear.jpg" style="width: 150px; height: 159px;"/>
       <span class="Caption">
        Sunflower
Teddy Bear
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Teddy Bear:
       </strong>
       I'm generally not a
fan of overbred
flowers, but I've fallen in love with the sheer zaniness of this one.
Has soft, fluffy, pompom heads that make handsome cut flowers; 2 to 3
feet tall with lots of flowers per plant. A perfect plant for a
children's garden (and anyone who is young at heart), as well as
containers.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Giant Sungold:
       </strong>
       At 5 feet in
height with lots of flower-bearing branches, this is a taller version
of 'Teddy Bear'.
      </p>
