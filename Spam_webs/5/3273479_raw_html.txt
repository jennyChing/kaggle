     <p>
      <b>
       John Hope Franklin
      </b>
      (January 2, 1915 March 25, 2009) was an American historian of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      and former president of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">
       Phi Beta Kappa
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_Historians" title="Organization of American Historians">
       Organization of American Historians
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Historical_Association" title="American Historical Association">
       American Historical Association
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_Historical_Association" title="Southern Historical Association">
       Southern Historical Association
      </a>
      . Franklin is best known for his work
      <i>
       From Slavery to Freedom
      </i>
      , first published in 1947, and continually updated. More than three million copies have been sold. In 1995, he was awarded the
      <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">
       Presidential Medal of Freedom
      </a>
      , the nation's highest civilian honor.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">
       Early life and education
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Hope_Franklin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Franklin was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Rentiesville,_Oklahoma" title="Rentiesville, Oklahoma">
       Rentiesville, Oklahoma
      </a>
      in 1915 to attorney Buck (Charles) Colbert Franklin (1879-1957) and his wife Mollie (Parker) Franklin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-obit-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was named after
      <a href="/wiki/John_Hope_(educator)" title="John Hope (educator)">
       John Hope
      </a>
      , a prominent educator who was the first African-American president of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta_University" title="Atlanta University">
       Atlanta University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Franklin's father Buck Colbert Franklin was a civil rights lawyer, aka "Amazing Buck Franklin." He was of African-American and Choctaw ancestry and born in the
      <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw_Nation" title="Chickasaw Nation">
       Chickasaw Nation
      </a>
      in western
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">
       Indian Territory
      </a>
      (formerly Pickens County). He was the seventh of ten children born to David and Milley Franklin. David was a former slave, who became a Chickasaw
      <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">
       Freedman
      </a>
      when emancipated after the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      . Milley was born free before the war and was of one-fourth Choctaw and three-fourths African-American ancestry. Buck Franklin became a lawyer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Buck Franklin is best known for defending African-American survivors of the 1921
      <a href="/wiki/Tulsa_race_riot" title="Tulsa race riot">
       Tulsa race riot
      </a>
      , in which whites had attacked many blacks and buildings, and burned and destroyed the Greenwood District. This was known at the time as the "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwood,_Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Greenwood, Tulsa, Oklahoma">
       Black Wall Street
      </a>
      ", and was the wealthiest Black community in the United States, a center of black commerce and culture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Franklin and his colleagues also became experts at oil law, representing "blacks and Native Americans in Oklahoma against white lawyers representing oil barons."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-smith-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His career demonstrated a strong professional black life in the West, at a time when such accomplishments would have been more difficult to achieve in the Deep South.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-smith-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      John Hope Franklin graduated from
      <a href="/wiki/Booker_T._Washington_High_School_(Tulsa,_Oklahoma)" title="Booker T. Washington High School (Tulsa, Oklahoma)">
       Booker T. Washington High School
      </a>
      (then segregated) in
      <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">
       Tulsa, Oklahoma
      </a>
      . He graduated in 1935 from
      <a href="/wiki/Fisk_University" title="Fisk University">
       Fisk University
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historically_black_university" title="Historically black university">
       historically black university
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">
       Nashville, Tennessee
      </a>
      , then earned a master's in 1936 and a
      <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">
       doctorate
      </a>
      in history in 1941 from
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
       Harvard University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOHC-JHF_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOHC-JHF-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Hope_Franklin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "My challenge," Franklin said, "was to weave into the fabric of American history enough of the presence of blacks so that the story of the United States could be told adequately and fairly."
     </p>
     <p>
      In his autobiography, Franklin has described a series of formative incidents in which he confronted
      <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">
       racism
      </a>
      while seeking to volunteer his services at the beginning of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
       Second World War
      </a>
      . He responded to the Navy's search for qualified clerical workers, but after he presented his extensive qualifications, the Navy recruiter told him that he was the wrong color for the position. He was similarly unsuccessful in finding a position with a War Department historical project. When he went to have a blood test, as required for the
      <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">
       draft
      </a>
      , the doctor initially refused to allow him into his office. Afterward, Franklin took steps to avoid the draft, on the basis that the country did not respect him or have an interest in his well-being, because of his color.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autobio_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autobio-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 1950s, Franklin served on the
      <a href="/wiki/NAACP_Legal_Defense_and_Educational_Fund" title="NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund">
       NAACP Legal Defense Fund
      </a>
      team led by
      <a href="/wiki/Thurgood_Marshall" title="Thurgood Marshall">
       Thurgood Marshall
      </a>
      , and helped develop the sociological case for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_v._Board_of_Education" title="Brown v. Board of Education">
        Brown v. Board of Education
       </a>
      </i>
      . This case, challenging
      <i>
       de jure
      </i>
      segregated education in the South, was taken to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">
       United States Supreme Court
      </a>
      . It ruled in 1954 that the legal
      <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation" title="Racial segregation">
       segregation
      </a>
      of black and white children in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" title="Public school (government funded)">
       public schools
      </a>
      was unconstitutional, leading to integration of schools.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colleague_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-colleague-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Professor_and_researcher">
       Professor and researcher
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Hope_Franklin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Professor and researcher">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Franklin's teaching career
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dukebio_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dukebio-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      began at Fisk University. During WWII, he taught at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Augustine%27s_College_(North_Carolina)" title="St. Augustine's College (North Carolina)">
       St. Augustine's College
      </a>
      from 1939 to 1943 and the North Carolina College for Negroes, currently
      <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Central_University" title="North Carolina Central University">
       North Carolina Central University
      </a>
      from 1943 to 1947.
     </p>
     <p>
      From 1947 to 1956, he taught at
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">
       Howard University
      </a>
      . In 1956, Franklin was selected to chair the history department at
      <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_College" title="Brooklyn College">
       Brooklyn College
      </a>
      , the first person of color to head a major history department. Franklin served there until 1964, when he was recruited by the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">
       University of Chicago
      </a>
      . He spent 1962 as a visiting professor at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
       University of Cambridge
      </a>
      , holding the
      <a href="/wiki/Pitt_Professor_of_American_History_and_Institutions" title="Pitt Professor of American History and Institutions">
       Professorship of American History and Institutions
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/David_Levering_Lewis" title="David Levering Lewis">
       David Levering Lewis
      </a>
      , who has twice won the
      <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">
       Pulitzer Prize
      </a>
      for history, said that while he was deciding to become a historian, he learned that Franklin, his mentor, had been named departmental chairman at Brooklyn College.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "Now that certainly is a distinction. It had never happened before that a person of color had chaired a major history department. That meant a lot to me. If I had doubt about (the) viability of a career in history, that example certainly helped put to rest such concerns."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findarticles.com_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-findarticles.com-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      In researching his prize-winning biography of
      <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois" title="W. E. B. Du Bois">
       W. E. B. Du Bois
      </a>
      , Lewis said he became aware of Franklin's
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "courage during that period in the 1950s when Du Bois became an un-person, when many progressives were tarred and feathered with the brush of subversion. John Hope Franklin was a rock; he was loyal to his friends. In the case of W. E. B. Du Bois, Franklin spoke out in his defense, not (about) Du Bois's
       <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
        communism
       </a>
       , but of the right of an intellectual to express ideas that were not popular. I find that admirable. It was a high risk to take and we may be heading again into a period when the free concourse of ideas in the academy will have a price put upon it. In the final years of an active teaching career, I will have John Hope Franklin's example of high scholarship, great courage and civic activism."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findarticles.com_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-findarticles.com-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>

       University of Chicago

      <a href="/wiki/John_Matthews_Manly" title="John Matthews Manly">
       John Matthews Manly
      </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_Equality_in_America">
       <i>
        Racial Equality in America
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Hope_Franklin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Racial Equality in America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Racial Equality in America
      </i>
      is the published lecture series that Franklin presented in 1976 for the Jefferson Lecture sponsored by the National Endowment for Humanities. The book is composed of three lectures, given in three different cities, in which Franklin chronicled the history of race in the United States from revolutionary times to 1976. These lectures explore the differences between some of the beliefs related to race with the reality documented in various historical and government texts, as well as data gathered from census, property, and literary sources. The first lecture is titled The Dream Deferred and discusses the period from the Revolution to 1820. The second lecture is titled The Old Order Changeth Not and discusses the rest of the 19th century. The third lecture is titled Equality Indivisible and discusses the 20th century.
     </p>
