     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Shape of Punk to Come: A Chimerical Bombination in 12 Bursts
       </b>
      </i>
      , often shortened to
      <i>
       <b>
        The Shape of Punk to Come
       </b>
      </i>
      , is the third album by
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Swedish
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">
       hardcore punk
      </a>
      band
      <a href="/wiki/Refused" title="Refused">
       Refused
      </a>
      , released in October 1998 through
      <a href="/wiki/Burning_Heart_Records" title="Burning Heart Records">
       Burning Heart Records
      </a>
      . The album's title is a reference to a track on
      <a href="/wiki/The_Nation_of_Ulysses" title="The Nation of Ulysses">
       The Nation of Ulysses
      </a>
      ' second album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Plays_Pretty_for_Baby" title="Plays Pretty for Baby">
        Plays Pretty for Baby
       </a>
      </i>
      , which itself was a reference and shares its title with
      <a href="/wiki/Ornette_Coleman" title="Ornette Coleman">
       Ornette Coleman
      </a>
      's 1959
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde_jazz" title="Avant-garde jazz">
       avant-garde jazz
      </a>
      album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Shape_of_Jazz_to_Come" title="The Shape of Jazz to Come">
        The Shape of Jazz to Come
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PitchforkReview_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-PitchforkReview-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The song title "Worms of the Senses / Faculties of the Skull" is an allusion to a line from
      <a href="/wiki/Allen_Ginsberg" title="Allen Ginsberg">
       Allen Ginsberg
      </a>
      's long poem "
      <a href="/wiki/Howl" title="Howl">
       Howl
      </a>
      ". Additionally, the song "Refused Are Fuckin' Dead" is a reference to the
      <a href="/wiki/Born_Against" title="Born Against">
       Born Against
      </a>
      song "Born Against Are Fuckin' Dead".
     </p>
     <p>
      Although Refused broke up only months after the album's release,
      <i>
       The Shape of Punk to Come
      </i>
      has since found an audience for the band and largely contributed to their posthumous fame, as well as inspiring many later artists in a wide range of genres.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exclaim_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Exclaim-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blabbermouth_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blabbermouth-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">
        Kerrang!
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine listed
      <i>
       The Shape of Punk to Come
      </i>
      at #13 on their
      <i>
       50 Most Influential Albums of All Time
      </i>
      list in 2003.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Shape_of_Punk_to_Come&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      This album marked a sharp and conscious departure from Refused's earlier work. The philosophy of the album, expounded in the ample liner notes and encapsulated in the song "New Noise", was that punk and hardcore music could not be anti-establishment by continuing to package revolutionary lyrics in sounds which had been increasingly co-opted into the mainstream. The sound of the record challenged existing punk sensibilities; it was "punk" at a fundamental level, but thanks to bold and experimental combinations of
      <a href="/wiki/Post-hardcore" title="Post-hardcore">
       post-hardcore
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">
       post-punk
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">
       techno
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
       jazz
      </a>
      sounds, it was worlds apart from
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">
       pop punk
      </a>
      bands such as
      <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">
       Green Day
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Blink-182" title="Blink-182">
       Blink-182
      </a>
      , and even more traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">
       punk rock
      </a>
      bands such as
      <a href="/wiki/Bad_Religion" title="Bad Religion">
       Bad Religion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pennywise_(band)" title="Pennywise (band)">
       Pennywise
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The album also includes "political interludes" between some songs. The use of more technological sounds or
      <a href="/wiki/Drum_and_bass" title="Drum and bass">
       drum and bass
      </a>
      music, particularly on
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_Noise_Theology_E.P." title="The New Noise Theology E.P.">
        The New Noise Theology E.P.
       </a>
      </i>
      which followed the album, is a tactic that various members of Refused have credited to the influence of Philadelphia punk band
      <a href="/wiki/Ink_%26_Dagger" title="Ink &amp; Dagger">
       Ink &amp; Dagger
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="2004_Reissue">
       2004 Reissue
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Shape_of_Punk_to_Come&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2004 Reissue">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2004, a
      <a href="/wiki/DVD-Audio" title="DVD-Audio">
       DVD-Audio
      </a>
      version of the album was released, remixed in 5.1-channel
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surround_Sound" title="Surround Sound">
       Surround Sound
      </a>
      . Many of the songs were compositionally altered, some significantly. "Bruitist Pome #5," for example, was thoroughly reworked, while a seven-minute version of "Refused Are Fuckin Dead" jarringly transitions into a new second half, which incorporates elements of the Bombe Je Remix of the song. Other songs received new intros or outros.
     </p>
