                      <div class="views-row views-row-1 views-row-odd views-row-first views-row-last">

                    <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
                     <div class="field-items">
                      <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                       <p class="P2">
                        <strong>
                         Carnivorous plants
                        </strong>
                        have been populating children's cartoons you've been secretly watching since forever. But what is the truth about them? Do they eat humans? Do they hunt in prides? Do they poop? Truth is carnivorous plants
                        <em>
                         mostly
                        </em>
                        feed on insects, be it crawling, foraging or flying. I said mostly because some plants have been reported to capture rats, frogs, a bird here and there(although these animals were most likely already dying or sick). But there's no danger for you whatsoever. Not because you are at home, reading this, but in general for humans.
                       </p>
                       <p class="P3">
                        Carnivorous plants, being the tough beasts they are, tend to grow on a nutrient-low ground such as bogs or rocky areas. Therefore they have to get their nutrients from their pray  the tiny innocent cute nutrient rich insects(just kidding, no one thinks insects are cute). Over 600 species of these plants have been spotted but when someone says carnivorous plant you probably picture The
                        <em>
                         Venus's-flytrap.
                        </em>
                       </p>
                       <p class="P3">
                       </p>
                       <p>
                        <strong>
                         I'm a carnivorous plant and I want to snack. How do I do this?
                        </strong>
                       </p>
                       <p class="P4">
                        As we all know plants are not fast-runners. Nor can they reach and grab their pray. So what do they do? Easy. Typical for their passive aggressive lifestyle they just
                        <em>
                         attract
                        </em>
                        pray. This includes:
                       </p>
                       <ul>
                        <li>
                         <p class="P5">
                          <strong>
                           Pitfall based
                          </strong>
                          <strong>
                          </strong>
                           the nothing suspecting insects falls into a leaf filled with digestive enzymes. Yeah, literally falling in its stomach.
                         </p>
                        </li>
                        <li>
                         <p class="P7">
                          <strong>
                           Snap surprise
                          </strong>
                           That is what the
                          <em>
                           Venuss-flytrap
                          </em>
                          does. It snaps shut its mouth and traps food inside. No touching, kids!
                         </p>
                        </li>
                        <li>
                         <p class="P8">
                          <strong>
                           Flypaper based
                          </strong>
                           As you can figure it out yourself that's where the plant has a sticky glue substance so insects can't escape. So hospitable!
                         </p>
                        </li>
                        <li>
                         <p class="P8">
                          <strong>
                           Bladder vaccuum
                          </strong>
                           Some of the insects reading this article probably thought they could safely hide in water. Guess what  some plants use the so called bladder to suck bypassing aquatic animals and insects! They empty their cavity, and when the victim touches the trigger hair it opens which creates the sucking power equal totwenty times the acceleration pressure astronauts experience when taking off(
                          <em>
                           g-force
                          </em>
                          ). Go, escape that.
                         </p>
                        </li>
                        <li>
                         <p class="P8">
                          <strong>
                           Lobster-pot mechanism
                          </strong>
                          <strong>
                          </strong>
                           This creatively named technique is where the plant uses inward-pointing hairs to navigate its pray towards the digestive enzymes. Sounds cool. And gross.
                         </p>
                        </li>
                       </ul>
                       <p class="P9">
                        The
                        <em>
                         Venuss-flytrap
                        </em>
                        is not the only moving plantfrom the carnivorous but it's the most famous one. Contrary to the popular belief it doesn't have muscles. Nor does it lift. When the sensitive hairs register contact with the pray they trigger water being transferred from the inside to the outside cells of the plant, which makes the inside go limp. And that's how the snap occurs.
                        <a href="http://wizzley.com/5-weird-plants-you-don-t-want-in-your-garden">
                         Hostile plants
                        </a>
                        , yet they definitely like to have a closure with their pray. How sappy.
                       </p>
                       <p class="P9">
                       </p>
                       <p class="P9">
                        Some have even developed hunting techniques.
                        <em>
                         The pitcher plant
                        </em>
                        for example Is a very sly little thingie. Its insides, where the fooled bugs fall trapped, is usually very slippery when wet. But the plant turns off the wetness for a couple of hours during the day. "B-but why?". Because that's part of its malicious plan, that's why! So you know ants, right? They usually send some scout-ants to scan the area for goods. When the ant has the information needed, it goes back and tells the other ants where the treasure is and only then do they send more ants to collect it. So the evil pitcher plant stays dry the whole day, playing it cool and welcome and all. Scout ant goes there, finds the sweet nectar that the upper leaf of the plant has produced, thinks to itself "that's it, finally i will get some respect from the queen" and rushes back to share the good news. Soon after the plant turns on its "slipperiness" and when the collectors come they are trapped inside!
                        <a href="http://www.gardeners-sutton.co.uk/">
                         Pro gardeners
                        </a>
                        say they are awesome bug catchers for your home. After allno pesticides, all natural!
                       </p>
                       <p>
                        <strong>
                         Some inside facts
                        </strong>
                       </p>
                       <p class="P6">
                        So what happens inside those cruel monsters' stomachs? Most of the plants create their own digestive enzymes, some rely on bacteria to produce it. Some use both. Ugly truth is some plants even use the help of
                        <em>
                         other insects
                        </em>
                        to digest. The
                        <em>
                         carnivorous
                        </em>
                        <em>
                         sundew
                        </em>
                        for example lets
                        <em>
                         assassin bugs
                        </em>
                        crawl inside it and eat whatever insects have been trapped. Then they poop(duh) and the plant uses the feces to absorb the nutrients. Sorry about your dinner.
                       </p>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                    </div>
