   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Dermatophagia
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Dermatophagia
      </b>
      is a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Impulse_control_disorder" title="Impulse control disorder">
       impulse control disorder
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Obsessive%E2%80%93compulsive_disorder" title="Obsessivecompulsive disorder">
       obsessivecompulsive disorder
      </a>
      wherein sufferers compulsively bite their own skin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrondia_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wrondia-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncbi_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ncbi-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-produkte_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-produkte-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sufferers typically bite the skin around the nails, leading to bleeding and discoloration over time. Some may consume the flesh during an episode.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrondia_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wrondia-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43things_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-43things-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The behavior associated with skin-biting is not exclusively the compulsory response to anxious/obsessive cognition; it is impulsive, and while the skin-biting may present with anxiety or obsessive thoughts, the presence of anxiety or obsessions is not diagnostically required to precede the behavior (while such anxiety/obsessions are diagnostically required with OCD).
     </p>
     <p>
      However, contemporary research suggests a link between impulse control disorders and obsessivecompulsive disorders,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and this may be addressed in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/DSM-5" title="DSM-5">
        DSM-5
       </a>
      </i>
      , due for publication in May 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style_(dates_and_numbers)#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style (dates and numbers)">
         <span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (November 2013)">
          needs update
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Further information on differentiation between OCD, other anxiety disorders, and dermatophagia and other impulse-control disorders can be found in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_and_Statistical_Manual_of_Mental_Disorders#DSM-IV-TR_.282000.29" title="Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders">
        DSM-IV TR
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DSM-IV_TR_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DSM-IV_TR-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">
       Behavior
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dermatophagia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Behavior">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dermatophagia sufferers chew their skin out of compulsion, and can do so on a variety of places on their body.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-produkte_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-produkte-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sufferers typically chew the skin surrounding their fingernails and joints. They also chew on the inside of their mouth, cheeks, and/or lips, causing blisters in and outside of the mouth. If the behavior is left unchecked for an extended period,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calluses" title="Calluses">
       calluses
      </a>
      may start to develop where most of the biting is done.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-produkte_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-produkte-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Skin chewing can be bolstered by times of apprehension and other unpleasant events.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-produkte_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-produkte-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Blister" title="Blister">
       Blisters
      </a>
      in particular can cause a feeling of desire to pull or bite off the affected skin (since the skin is dead, thus easily pulled off), which could be detrimental, causing
      <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">
       infection
      </a>
      . Another disorder, known as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dermatillomania" title="Dermatillomania">
       dermatillomania
      </a>
      , the act of picking at one's skin, can sometimes accompany dermatophagia. People who suffer from dermatophagia can also be prone to infection as when they bite their fingers so frequently, they make themselves vulnerable to bacteria seeping in and causing infection. Dermatophagia can be considered a "sister" disorder to
      <a href="/wiki/Trichophagia" title="Trichophagia">
       trichophagia
      </a>
      , which involves compulsively biting and eating one's hair.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FingerFreak_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FingerFreak-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
