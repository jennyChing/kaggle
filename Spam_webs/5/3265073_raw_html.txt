                    <u>
                     //SAMUEL BECKETT//
                    </u>
                    <br clear="all"/>
                    <br/>
                    <br/>
                    <br/>
                    <p>
                    </p>
                    <ul>
                     <li>
                      <b>
                       Known as:
                      </b>
                      Irish Novelist and Playwright
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <b>
                       Born:
                      </b>
                      13/04/1906, Foxrock, County Dublin, Ireland
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <b>
                       Birthname:
                      </b>
                      Samuel Barclay Beckett
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <b>
                       Date of death:
                      </b>
                      22/12/1989, Paris, France (emphysema)
                     </li>
                     <li>
                      <b>
                       Buried:
                      </b>
                      Montparnasse Cemetery, Paris, France
                     </li>
                    </ul>
                    <br clear="all"/>
                    <p>
                     <a name="bigt">
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                    </p>
                    <li>
                     <a href="http://www.leninimports.com/samuel_beckett_big_text.html#bigt">
                      Larger Text
                     </a>
                     <p>
                      <b>
                       S a m u e l B e c k e t t
                      </b>
                      &gt; &gt;
                      <br/>
                      &gt;
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      <br/>
                     </p>
                     <center>
                      <i>
                       "Have you not done tormenting me with your
                       <br/>
                       accursed time! It's abominable! When! When! One day, is
                       <br/>
                       that not enough for you, one day he went dumb, one day
                       <br/>
                       I went blind, one day we'll go deaf, one day we were
                       <br/>
                       born, one day we shall die, the same day, the same
                       <br/>
                       second, is that not enough for you?."
                      </i>
                      <p>
                       ~
                       <b>
                        Samuel Beckett
                       </b>
                       ,
                       <b>
                        <i>
                         Waiting for Godot
                        </i>
                       </b>
                       <br clear="all"/>
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       <br/>
                       <img align="center" alt="beckett" border="0" height="127" hspace="0" src="http://www.leninimports.com/samuel_beckett_gallery_2_med.jpg" vspace="0" width="102"/>
                       <br clear="all"/>
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       <br/>
                       <br/>
                       <span class="st_sharethis_hcount" displaytext="ShareThis">
                       </span>
                       <br clear="all"/>
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       <br/>
                      </p>
                     </center>
                     <font color="#FFFFFF" face="verdana" size="2">
                      <ul>
                       <b>
                        I admired
                        <b>
                         Samuel Beckett
                        </b>
                        since I found out how much he loved
                        <b>
                         Buster Keaton
                        </b>
                        . Thus I came to
                        <b>
                         Beckett
                        </b>
                        via the 20-minute short
                        <b>
                         <i>
                          Film
                         </i>
                        </b>
                        (1965) which
                        <b>
                         Beckett
                        </b>
                        wrote and which
                        <b>
                         Keaton
                        </b>
                        , then 70, starred. Directed by
                        <b>
                         Beckett
                        </b>
                        's  frequent stage collaborator
                        <b>
                         Alan Schneider
                        </b>
                        , it was a departure for the writer as he usually wrote for the stage. But it is an outstanding film and
                        <b>
                         Keaton
                        </b>
                        draws from his tragic past for the part of
                        <i>
                         The Man
                        </i>
                        . The silent comic is a man from another time, another place ... a man as old as time, his face withered by too many years, his hands gnarled by age.
                        <b>
                         Keaton is
                         <i>
                          The Man
                         </i>
                        </b>
                        . Though he later admitted to not entirely understanding what
                        <b>
                         Beckett
                        </b>
                        was trying to get at, it didn't matter for he had a face that entirely understood every word for him.
                       </b>
                      </ul>
                      <p>
                       <font color="#FFFFFF" face="verdana" size="1">
                        <br/>
                        On a superficial level, or my level, no film resonates more for me than
                        <b>
                         <i>
                          Film
                         </i>
                        </b>
                        . It is the film for the shy guy.
                        <b>
                         Keaton
                        </b>
                        can't make eye contact with anyone! Am I the only person out there who knows exactly how he feels? Aren't there days when you don't want to look at anyone? Your eyes move anywhere than settle on another pair of eyes. Everyone, it seems, wants your eyes: the woman on the high street with her questionnaire; the post office clerk, the friend, the mother, the lover. Yet, you don't want anyone to see your face, see your ugliness, judge your ugliness, make comment to another on your ugliness. You want to be free of everything and everyone and your face to be the face you want it to be. Oh time, will you not let me be?
                        <p>
                         Is it running away or coming home, I wonder?
                        </p>
                        <p>
                         <b>
                          Beckett
                         </b>
                         said the movie was "about a man trying to escape from perception of all kinds-- from all perceivers --even divine perceivers." Escape. Escaping. I understand that. I also understand that the writer of
                         <b>
                          <i>
                           Film
                          </i>
                         </b>
                         had faced a loneliness that I know too. But there the similarity ends for
                         <b>
                          Beckett
                         </b>
                         could articulate that loneliness like no other and I'm no other.
                        </p>
                        <p>
                         
                         <i>
                          ~ Paul Page  for Lenin
                         </i>
                        </p>
                        <p>
                         -------------
                        </p>
                        <p>
                        </p>
                        <li>
                         <b>
                          Beckett
                         </b>
                         had the most incredible head of any writer you'll ever see. Long as long can be, with piercing eyes and aquiline nose, it could almost have been carved from stone. Once seen, never forgotten. It's no wonder that as a young man he had women swarming around him like bees to honey.
                         <p>
                         </p>
                        </li>
                        <li>
                         Astonishingly, the Irish
                         <b>
                          Beckett
                         </b>
                         wrote much of his best work originally in  French. I've always wondered what language the great man originally thought in?
                         <p>
                          
                          <i>
                           ~ Paul Page  for Lenin
                          </i>
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          -------------
                          <br clear="all"/>
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          <br/>
                          <img align="left" alt="beckett" border="0" height="119" hspace="0" src="http://www.leninimports.com/samuel_beckett_gallery_1_med.jpg" vspace="0" width="88"/>
                          <br clear="all"/>
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          <br/>
                          <b>
                           Samuel Beckett
                          </b>
                          was born in Foxrock, Dublin. He would later insist that he was born on Good Friday,13 April 1906, although his birth certficate puts the date a month later. The
                          <b>
                           Becketts
                          </b>
                          were of French Huguenot descent and, after a distinguished career at
                          <b>
                           Trinity College
                          </b>
                          , Dublin, he was to spend much of his life in France. His cricketing prowess earned him a mention in
                          <b>
                           Wisden
                          </b>
                          (the only
                          <b>
                           Nobel Prize
                          </b>
                          winner there), while he topped his year in modern languages. In 1928, he was appointed to an exchange lectureship in Paris, where he met and helped
                          <b>
                           James Joyce
                          </b>
                          before returning to
                          <b>
                           TCD
                          </b>
                          in 1930.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          A critical study of
                          <b>
                           Marcel Proust
                          </b>
                          (1931) pointed to an academic career, but
                          <b>
                           Beckett
                          </b>
                          chose to become a full-time writer. He travelled widely, living rather precariously, before settling in Montparnasse in Paris in 1937. His comic novel
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Murphy
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          was published in 1938. He also met
                          <b>
                           Suzanne Dumesnil
                          </b>
                          , when she helped him to hospital after a street stabbing; they were to marry in 1961.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          <b>
                           Beckett
                          </b>
                          was in Dublin at the outbreak of
                          <b>
                           World War II
                          </b>
                          , but 'preferred France at war to Ireland at peace'. He worked for the
                          <b>
                           French Resistance
                          </b>
                          , narrowly escaped the Gestapo, then moved to unoccupied France, where he wrote his novel
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Watt
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          .
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          In 1947, he returned to Paris, where within two years he wrote his trilogy of novels.
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Molloy
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          ,
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Malone Dies
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          and
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            The Unnameable
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          , and the 
play
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Waiting for Godot
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          .
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          By now, he was writing in French, then translating into English.
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Godot
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          had its first production in 1953, and its success made the reclusive
                          <b>
                           Beckett
                          </b>
                          an international figure. In this innovative tragi-comedy, the tramps
                          <i>
                           Vladimir
                          </i>
                          and
                          <i>
                           Estragon
                          </i>
                          await someone they have never met and who may not exist. 'If I knew who
                          <i>
                           Godot
                          </i>
                          was,' said
                          <b>
                           Beckett
                          </b>
                          , 'I would have said so in the play'.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          Other bleakly comic plays followed. 'Nothing is funnier than unhappiness, ' says
                          <i>
                           Nell
                          </i>
                          in
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            Endgame
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          , speaking from a dustbin.
                          <b>
                           <i>
                            In Happy Days
                           </i>
                          </b>
                          , the heroine is buried in sand.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          <b>
                           Beckett
                          </b>
                          was awarded the
                          <b>
                           Nobel Prize for Literature
                          </b>
                          in 1969, but shunned the presentation ceremony. He died in Paris on 22 December 1989, a few months after his wife.
                         </p>
                         <p>
                          The two were interred together in the
                          <b>
                           Cimetire du Montparnasse
                          </b>
                          in Paris, and share a simple marble gravestone.
                         </p>
