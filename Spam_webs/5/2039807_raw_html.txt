      <h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">
       DIY lab equipment, courtesy of 3D printing
      </h1>
      <h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">
       Open-source hardware and software could allow in-house design and fabrication
      </h2>

     <section id="article-guts">
      <div class="article-content clearfix" itemprop="articleBody">
       <figure class="intro-image image center full-width" style="width:640px">
        <img height="480" src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/2009-12-30-023824_preview_featured-640x480.jpg" width="640"/>
        <figcaption class="caption">
         <div class="caption-credit">

           Thingiverse
          </a>
         </div>
        </figcaption>
       </figure>
       <!-- cache miss 4117:single/related:59bff7dabd4579eb1e2f8446a65b4151 -->
       <!-- empty -->
       <p>
        A lot of scientific software is freeware or free/open source software (FOSS). That's appropriate: just as the process of science should be open to enhance reproducibility, its tools should be as transparent as possible. Researchers often share their data and algorithms, and publish the output from their simulations on open-access databases such as Cornell's
        <a href="http://arXiV.org">
         arXiv
        </a>
        . Research hardwareincluding computer hardwareis another matter entirely. Even small, common pieces of laboratory equipment can be costly, and may not be hackable without (at minimum) violating warranties or terms of use.
       </p>
       <p>
        Imagine a world where lab workers can create their own custom equipment in-house, using either their own designs or ones they've downloaded. A glimpse of that world appears in today's issue of
        <em>
         Science
        </em>
        , provided by 3D printing, the relatively low-cost fabrication technique where ceramics, polymers, and other materials are deposited in layers to build up a three-dimensional shape.
       </p>
       <p>
        With the prices of 3D printers dropping, laboratories at companies and universities have begun using them to build up research equipment. Even better, the printers themselves are often open sourcemeaning their designs are available and modifiable by end-usersand controlled by FOSS programs. Students in teaching-focused institutions can be involved in the process as well, providing hands-on instruction in design principles.
       </p>
       <p>
        The initial outlay for a 3D printer is still significant, but when compared to the cost of purchasing a whole lab's worth of equipment, it might be a cost-effective alternative for small schools or colleges. The
        <a href="http://www.reprapcentral.com/">
         RepRap printer
        </a>
        is an open-source project capable of fabricating some of its own components. Designs can be up or downloaded from repositories like the Thingiverse (I certainly wasted a few fun minutes browsing some of the projects on that site).
       </p>
       <p>
        A commercial centrifuge for biology labs may cost hundreds or thousands of dollars, but today's issue of
        <em>
         Science
        </em>
        describes a printed alternative. The
        <a href="http://www.thingiverse.com/thing:1483">
         DremelFuge
        </a>
        is simply a power tool attachment that holds centrifuge tubes. It costs only a little more than the Dremel drill that powers it, once the 3D printer is paid for.
       </p>
       <p>
        Of course, this won't solve every problem; I can't see inexpensive 3D printers making mass spectrometers in the near future. However, with continuing improvements in technology and lowering costs, we could see more lab equipment being made in-house with open-source hardwareboth in the equipment itself and in the machinery used in its fabrication.
       </p>
       <p>

       </p>

            , they are going to flip the fuck out. Weapons, easy. Art, easy. Half the little things you go to Home Depot for, easy. Toys, easy. Some parts will need to be made elsewhere for the foreseeable future, like CPUs, but the circuity, mechanisms, and cases can be 3D printed. You can bet that someone will be working on "drug machines" that take common ingredients and process them. That's just the stuff we can think of now, which is like trying to predict what we can do on the internet now in 1990.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            It's possible entire countries economies will collapse as mass manufacture is rendered much less important. Most of the generic cheap crap we get made in China, print it on demand. Hundreds of business that make simple things will go bankrupt. The police/government will go nuts when people can print guns, etc. The disruption the internet has caused so far is nothing compared to what it will cause in the next 10-20 years.
           </div>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Whoa there man...Calm down now let's not be too hasty on predicting the end of the world from a bunch of sorry ass people who want these printers for their own selfish gain! I'm sure there's going to be some stipulation over what you can and cannot print as it is with everything else in life that's not meant to be replicated.
          </div>
