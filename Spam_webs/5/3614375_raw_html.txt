             <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5210314156592309154" onerror='this.style.display="none"' src="http://bp1.blogger.com/_Qpk-NqSqEa4/SE7BHrffo6I/AAAAAAAADII/KWA7f4QDxpk/s400/lady1.jpg" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer;"/>
            </a>
            <br/>
            <span style="color: rgb(255, 102, 102);font-size:100%;">
             <span style="font-family:verdana;">
              For some reason Ladybugs popped into my mind today and i thought I'd do a post about them.
             </span>
             <span style="font-family:verdana;">
              Above, are some absolutely beautiful pictures of Ladybugs.
              <br/>
             </span>
             <span style="font-family:verdana;">
              And below is some info on them:
              <br/>
              <br/>
             </span>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             <b>
              Coccinellidae
             </b>
             is a
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_%28biology%29" title="Family (biology)">
              family
             </a>
             of
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">
              beetles
             </a>
             , known variously as
             <b>
              ladybirds
             </b>
             (
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
              British English
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">
              Australian English
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_African_English" title="South African English">
              South African English
             </a>
             ),
             <b>
              ladybugs
             </b>
             (
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">
              North American English
             </a>
             ) or
             <b>
              lady beetles
             </b>
             (preferred by some scientists). The family name comes from its
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_genus" title="Type genus">
              type genus
             </a>
             ,
             <i>
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinella" title="Coccinella">
               Coccinella
              </a>
             </i>
             . Coccinellids are found worldwide, with over 5,000
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species" title="Species">
              species
             </a>
             described
             <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-0">
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinellidae#cite_note-0" title="">
               [1]
              </a>
             </sup>
             , more than 450 native to
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
              North America
             </a>
             alone. Coccinellids are small
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">
              insects
             </a>
             , ranging from
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_E-3_m" title="1 E-3 m">
              1 mm to 10 mm
             </a>
             (0.04 to 0.4 inches), and are commonly yellow, orange, or scarlet with small black spots on their
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elytra" title="Elytra">
              wing covers
             </a>
             , with black legs, head and
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antenna_%28biology%29" title="Antenna (biology)">
              antennae
             </a>
             . A very large number of species are mostly or entirely black, gray, or brown and may be difficult for non-
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist">
              entomologists
             </a>
             to recognize as coccinellids (and, conversely, there are many small beetles that are easily mistaken as such, like
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tortoise_beetle" title="Tortoise beetle">
              tortoise beetles
             </a>
             ).
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Ladybirds are generally considered useful insects as many species feed on
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">
              aphids
             </a>
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">
              scale insects
             </a>
             , which are pests in gardens, agricultural fields, orchards, and similar places. The
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mall_of_America" title="Mall of America">
              Mall of America
             </a>
             , for instance, releases thousands of ladybugs into its indoor park as a natural means of pest control for its gardens
             <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinellidae#cite_note-1" title="">
               [2]
              </a>
             </sup>
             . Some people consider seeing them or having them land on one's body to be a sign of good
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luck" title="Luck">
              luck
             </a>
             to come, and that killing them presages bad luck. A few species are pests in North America and Europe. or
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Coccinellids are typically
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">
              predators
             </a>
             of
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">
              Hemiptera
             </a>
             such as aphids and scale insects, though members of the subfamily
             <a class="new" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Epilachninae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Epilachninae (page does not exist)">
              Epilachninae
             </a>
             are
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">
              herbivores
             </a>
             , and can be very destructive
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agricultural_pest" title="Agricultural pest">
              agricultural pests
             </a>
             (e.g., the
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexican_bean_beetle" title="Mexican bean beetle">
              Mexican bean beetle
             </a>
             ). They are also known to eat certain plants and crops when no other food is present, making them a possible pest to farmers and gardeners. While they are often used as
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_control" title="Biological control">
              biological control
             </a>
             agents, introduced species of ladybirds (such as
             <i>
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonia_axyridis" title="Harmonia axyridis">
               Harmonia axyridis
              </a>
             </i>
             or
             <i>
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinella_septempunctata" title="Coccinella septempunctata">
               Coccinella septempunctata
              </a>
             </i>
             in
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
              North America
             </a>
             ) can outcompete and displace native coccinellids, and become pests in their own right.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Coccinellids are often brightly colored to ward away potential predators. This defense works because most predators associate bright colors (especially orange and black or yellow and black) with poison and other unpleasant properties. This phenomenon is called
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aposematism" title="Aposematism">
              aposematism
             </a>
             . In fact, most coccinellids are indeed poisonous to smaller predators, such as lizards and small birds; however, a human would have to eat several hundred coccinellids before feeling any effects.
             <sup class="noprint Template-Fact">
              <span style="white-space: nowrap;" title="This claim needs references to reliable sources since September 2007">
               [
               <i>
                <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
                 citation needed
                </a>
               </i>
               ]
              </span>
             </sup>
             Adult coccinellids are able to
             <a class="new" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Reflex-bleed&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reflex-bleed (page does not exist)">
              reflex-bleed
             </a>
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">
              hemolymph
             </a>
             from their leg joints, releasing their oily yellow toxin with a strong repellent smell. This becomes quite obvious when one handles a coccinellid roughly.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Most coccinellids mate in spring or summer and the female lays a cluster of eggs (numbering from a few to a few hundred, depending on species) as near as possible to an aphid colony. In most species these eggs hatch into a
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">
              larval state
             </a>
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pupae" title="Pupae">
              pupal
             </a>
             stage before becoming an adult coccinellid. The entire
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">
              life cycle
             </a>
             of the Coccinellid is only 47 weeks. Most ladybird species are
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Univoltine" title="Univoltine">
              univoltine
             </a>
             , producing only one generation a year, although some are
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivoltine" title="Bivoltine">
              bivoltine
             </a>
             . within a week. This state lasts 1015 days, and they then go into a
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Coccinellids lay extra infertile eggs with the fertile eggs. These appear to provide a backup food source for the larvae when they hatch. The ratio of infertile to fertile eggs increases with scarcity of food at the time of egg laying.
             <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinellidae#cite_note-2" title="">
               [3]
              </a>
             </sup>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Some species are
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migratory" title="Migratory">
              migratory
             </a>
             and form large
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swarm" title="Swarm">
              aggregations
             </a>
             during the migratory period. They also form large aggregations when they go into
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">
              hibernation
             </a>
             in Winter.
             <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinellidae#cite_note-3" title="">
               [4]
              </a>
             </sup>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccinellidae#cite_note-3" title="">
              Most coccinellids are beneficial to gardeners in general, as they feed on
             </a>
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">
              aphids
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">
              scale insects
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mealybug" title="Mealybug">
              mealybugs
             </a>
             , and
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mite" title="Mite">
              mites
             </a>
             throughout the year. As in many insects, ladybirds in temperate regions enter
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diapause" title="Diapause">
              diapause
             </a>
             during the winter, so they often are among the first insects to appear in the spring. Some species (e.g.,
             <i>
              <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippodamia_convergens" title="Hippodamia convergens">
               Hippodamia convergens
              </a>
             </i>
             ) gather into groups and move to higher land, such as a mountain, to enter diapause. Predatory ladybirds are usually found on plants where aphids or scale insects are, and they lay their eggs near their prey, to increase the likelihood the larvae will find the prey easily. Ladybirds are cosmopolitan in distribution, as are their prey.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="font-family: verdana; color: rgb(255, 102, 102);">
            <span style="font-size:85%;">
             Although native species of coccinellids are typically considered benign, in North America the Asian lady beetle
             <i>
              (
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonia_axyridis" title="Harmonia axyridis">
               Harmonia axyridis
              </a>
              ),
             </i>
             introduced in the twentieth century to control
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphids" title="Aphids">
              aphids
             </a>
             on agricultural crops, has become a serious household
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pest_%28organism%29" title="Pest (organism)">
              pest
             </a>
             in some regions owing to its habit of overwintering in structures. It is similarly acquiring a pest reputation in Europe, where it is called the "Harlequin Ladybird" (see main article "
             <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_lady_beetle" title="Asian lady beetle">
              Asian lady beetle
             </a>
             " for discussion). Some ladybirds eat flies such as greenflies and smaller insects.
            </span>
           </p>
