                Helicopter to Enhance Future Mars Trips
               </h2>
               <!-- Item category -->
               <div class="itemCategory">

               <!-- Item introtext -->
               <div class="itemIntroText">
                <p style="line-height: 11.85pt;">
                 <span style="font-size: 9pt; font-family: Tahoma, sans-serif;">
                  The Mars Rover has succeeded in providing a great deal of data as well as incredible imagery -- all of which has helped scientists broaden and deepen their understanding of the Red Planet.Now, word comes that a new concept vehicle, planned for future missions, might help to increase that knowledge exponentially.
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p style="line-height: 11.85pt;">
                 <span style="font-size: 9pt; font-family: Tahoma, sans-serif;">
                  After all, a rover can cover only so much ground. Which is why NASA has introduced the Mars Helicopter, a low-flying scout craft that could cover three times the distance that the rovers manage to log every Martian day.
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p style="line-height: 11.85pt;">
                 <span style="font-size: 9pt; font-family: Tahoma, sans-serif;">
                  <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/jpl/helicopter-could-be-scout-for-mars-rovers/#.VMv07mjF-So">
                   According to NASA
                  </a>
                  , plans call for the helicopter to fly ahead of the rover every day, looking for different points of interest that could assist engineers back on Earth in planning the rover's subsequent routes.
                 </span>
                </p>
               </div>
               <!-- Item fulltext -->
               <div class="itemFullText">
                <p style="line-height: 11.85pt;">
                 <span style="font-size: 9pt; font-family: Tahoma, sans-serif;">
                  Another use for the chopper: Scientists could use the images it gathers to look for features on the planet that the rover might then study in greater detail. And the flying craft would also be tasked with finding the prime spots where the rover would be assigned to collect rocks and samples. These could be stored in a cache of some sort, which could then be picked up by a rover on a future mission.
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p>
                 <span style="line-height: 11.85pt; font-size: 9pt; font-family: Tahoma, sans-serif;">
                  The Mars Helicopter, with a body that looks like a tissue box, would weigh 2.2 pounds and measure 3.6 feet from the tip of one blade to the other. The proof-of-concept technology demonstration has been tested at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory. The JPL's chief engineer says,
                 </span>
                 <span style="font-family: Tahoma, sans-serif;">
                  <span style="font-size: 12px; line-height: 15.8000001907349px;">
                   "Because this thing is going to take off every day and land every day, we want to make sure we have a bulletproof landing system, and landing is the riskiest part of any mission."
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </p>
