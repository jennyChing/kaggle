         <p>
          <a href="http://www.bigbrandsystem.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Font-book.jpg">
           <img alt="company fonts can be hard to choose when there are so many typefaces out there" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2697" height="370" src="http://www.bigbrandsystem.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Font-book.jpg" title="Font Book" width="520"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           I have confessed to font addiction before
          </strong>
          , so thats nothing new. When you have over 10,000 fonts on your computer, you know your problem runs deep.
         </p>
         <p>
          With that size collection, what can you do to keep them organized? And honestly, how can you really keep track of and remember all those different letter forms?
         </p>
         <p>
          I was contacted by Lisa Wood of
          <a href="http://www.sproutnewmedia.com/">
           <strong>
            Sprout New Media
           </strong>
          </a>
          this week. Lisa had two questions, and by her questions alone, I knew she was a fellow addict!
          <span id="more-2696">
          </span>
         </p>
         <h3>
          How Do You Keep Track of All Your Fonts?
         </h3>
         <p>
          Lisa wondered if I had some kind of visual reference for fonts, and what system I used to organize them. Back in the B.C. (before children) days, I went through my collection and printed a sample page of the medium weight of every typeface style I had (see photo above).
         </p>
         <p>
          I had to purchase a 3-inch binder to hold them all. After I punched holes in every page, I separated the styles into categories: serif; sans serif; display; script; etc.
         </p>
         <p>
          When I was looking for a particular kind of typeface, I simply flipped through the pages. Each page showed the type large, and also had a sample paragraph in several different text sizes. You could see what the typeface would look like both in headlines and body copy.
         </p>
         <h3>
          A Paper Notebook: How Quaint!
         </h3>
         <p>
          Using a three-ring binder to classify your typefaces seems so old fashioned now. The tool I recommended to Lisa was a piece of software called
          <a href="http://www.fontexplorerx.com/pro/" target="_blank">
           <strong>
            FontExplorer X Pro,
           </strong>
          </a>
          by Linotype. Lisa is on a Mac, and I am, too.
         </p>
         <p>
          [EDIT: I have since installed
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.extensis.com/en/products/suitcasefusion3/overview.jsp" target="_blank">
            Suitcase Fusion 3
           </a>
          </strong>
          on one of my machines, and its a great piece of software, too.]
         </p>
         <p>
          I cant make first-hand recommendations for Windows users, but I have heard good things about a piece of software called
          <strong>
           MainType
          </strong>
          .
          <em>
           (Windows users, what software do you use to organize your typefaces? Tell me in the comments!)
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           There are many advantage to using software to organize your typefaces:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Software allows to to tag typefaces with descriptors like serif, display, script, or even playful, or corporate. You can use any descriptors that will help you find and choose the fonts you have.
          </li>
          <li>
           Font management software gives you the ability to turn fonts on and off, which makes a shorter font list in your software programs, and conserves computing power for other things.
          </li>
          <li>
           You can see a preview of the font youd like to use before you enable it (see below).
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.bigbrandsystem.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/FontExplorer.jpg">
           <img alt="company fonts can be a little easier to manage with a font organization tool" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2698" height="189" src="http://www.bigbrandsystem.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/FontExplorer.jpg" title="FontExplorer" width="520"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <h3>
          Where Can I Find More?
         </h3>
         <p>
          This is the question that told me the most. Lisa wants
          <em>
           more
          </em>
          font goodness, and I understand.
          <strong>
           Theres no such thing as too many typefaces.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Back on
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.bigbrandsystem.com/design-101-7-typographic-resources-and-1-type-joke/">
            this post about typography
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://www.TheBookDesigner.com/">
            Joel Friedlander
           </a>
          </strong>
          was generous enough to mention
          <a href="http://www.fontsquirrel.com/">
           <strong>
            FontSquirrel
           </strong>
          </a>
          in his comment. FontSquirrel has become my go-to resource for new typefaces.
         </p>
         <p>
          Other great resources are
          <a href="http://www.fonts.com/">
           <strong>
            Fonts.com,
           </strong>
          </a>
          <a href="http://new.myfonts.com/">
           <strong>
            MyFonts.com
           </strong>
          </a>
          and of course
          <a href="http://www.linotype.com/" target="_blank">
           <strong>
            Linotype.com,
           </strong>
          </a>
          which makes the FontExplorer software mentioned above.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           FontSquirrel
          </strong>
          features an extensive collection of no-cost fonts. The quality varies, but if you just need to inject some quick personality into a project, they may have just the font for you.
         </p>
         <h3>
          How Do You Juggle Fonts?
         </h3>
         <p>
          Id like to know how you juggle your fonts. Do you use software? Paper?
          <strong>
           Let me know in the comments,
          </strong>
          and please share any type questions you may have. I know its a confusing subject for many people, so fire away and Ill work to dispel your confusion.
         </p>

             I admit I organize my fonts the old fashioned way: paper.
            </p>
            <p>
             I created a standard paragraph and headine, then in Word I just keep copying and pasting it using each font I have. Then I print that document on 3-hole paper and put it in a binder.
            </p>
            <p>
             You only have to do it once for all your existing fonts, then update it when you get a new font.
            </p>
            <p>
             I really love to see the font in print in a book when Im choosing fonts. Now I can just pull my 3-ring binder off the shelf, and flip through it until something catches my eye.
            </p>
            <p>
             I also like it because I can pull out two pages, put them side by side, and compare two fonts to see if they really are all that much different from one another. Ive deleted a LOT of fonts from my computer when I discovered that 2 or 3 of them were so similar as to be redundant!
