     <p>
      <b>
       Ernst Frederick Werner Alexanderson
      </b>
      (January 25, 1878  May 14, 1975) was a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish-American" title="Swedish-American">
       Swedish-American
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_engineer" title="Electrical engineer">
       electrical engineer
      </a>
      , who was a pioneer in
      <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
       radio
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
       television
      </a>
      development. He invented the
      <a href="/wiki/Alexanderson_alternator" title="Alexanderson alternator">
       Alexanderson alternator
      </a>
      , an early
      <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">
       radio transmitter
      </a>
      used between 1906 and the 1930s for
      <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">
       longwave
      </a>
      long distance radio transmission.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Alexanderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Alexanderson was born at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uppsala,_Sweden" title="Uppsala, Sweden">
       Uppsala, Sweden
      </a>
      , and educated at the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_Technology" title="Royal Institute of Technology">
       Royal Institute of Technology
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">
       Stockholm
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Technische_Hochschule" title="Technische Hochschule">
       Technische Hochschule
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technical_University" title="Technical University">
       Technical University
      </a>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
       Berlin
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      . He emigrated to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S." title="U.S.">
       U.S.
      </a>
      in 1902 and spent much of his life working for the
      <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">
       General Electric
      </a>
      company.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_work">
       Engineering work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Alexanderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Engineering work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Alexanderson designed the
      <a href="/wiki/Alexanderson_alternator" title="Alexanderson alternator">
       Alexanderson alternator
      </a>
      , an early
      <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">
       longwave
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">
       radio transmitter
      </a>
      , one of the first devices which could transmit modulated audio (sound) over radio waves. He had been employed at
      <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">
       General Electric
      </a>
      for only a short time when GE received an order from Canadian-born professor and researcher
      <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Fessenden" title="Reginald Fessenden">
       Reginald Fessenden
      </a>
      , then working for the US Weather Bureau, for a specialized alternator with much higher frequency than others in existence at that time, for use as a radio transmitter. Fessenden had been working on the problem of transmitting sound by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">
       radio waves
      </a>
      , and had concluded that a new type of radio transmitter was needed, a
      <a href="/wiki/Continuous_wave" title="Continuous wave">
       continuous wave
      </a>
      transmitter. Designing a machine that would rotate fast enough to produce radio waves proved a formidable challenge. Alexanderson's family were convinced the huge spinning rotors would fly apart and kill him, and he set up a sandbagged bunker from which to test them. In the summer of 1906 Mr. Alexanderson's first effort, a 50
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KHz" title="KHz">
       kHz
      </a>
      alternator, was installed in Fessenden's radio station in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brant_Rock,_Massachusetts" title="Brant Rock, Massachusetts">
       Brant Rock, Massachusetts
      </a>
      . By fall its output had been improved to 500 watts and 75kHz. On Christmas Eve, 1906, Fessenden made an experimental broadcast of Christmas music, including him playing the violin, that was heard by Navy ships and shore stations down the East Coast as far as Arlington. This is considered the first AM radio entertainment broadcast.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alexanderson and G.E. continued improving his machine, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Alexanderson_alternator" title="Alexanderson alternator">
       Alexanderson alternator
      </a>
      became widely used in high power
      <a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">
       very low frequency
      </a>
      commercial and Naval wireless stations to transmit radiotelegraphy traffic at intercontinental distances, until by the 1930s it was replaced by
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">
       vacuum tube
      </a>
      transmitters. The only surviving transmitter in a working state is at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLF_transmitter_Grimeton" title="VLF transmitter Grimeton">
       Grimeton radio station
      </a>
      outside
      <a href="/wiki/Varberg" title="Varberg">
       Varberg
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      . It is a prime example of pre-electronic radio technology and was added to
      <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
       UNESCO
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_heritage" title="World heritage">
       World heritage
      </a>
      list in 2004.
     </p>
     <p>
      Alexanderson also created the
      <a href="/wiki/Amplidyne" title="Amplidyne">
       amplidyne
      </a>
      , a direct current amplifier.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mr. Alexanderson was also instrumental in the development of television. The first
      <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
       television
      </a>
      broadcast in the United States was to his
      <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_Realty_Plot" title="General Electric Realty Plot">
       GE Plot
      </a>
      home at 1132 Adams Rd, Schenectady, NY, in 1927. Over his lifetime, Mr. Alexanderson received 345 US patents, the last filed in 1968 at age 89. The inventor and engineer remained active to an advanced age, working as a consultant to GE and
      <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">
       RCA
      </a>
      in the 1950s. He died in 1975 and was buried at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vale_Cemetery" title="Vale Cemetery">
       Vale Cemetery
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schenectady" title="Schenectady">
       Schenectady
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">
       New York
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Alexanderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1983, he was inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Inventors_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Inventors Hall of Fame">
       National Inventors Hall of Fame
      </a>
      and in 2002 the
      <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Hall_of_Fame" title="Consumer Electronics Hall of Fame">
       Consumer Electronics Hall of Fame
      </a>
      .
     </p>
