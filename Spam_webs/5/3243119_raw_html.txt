         <em>
          I occasionally write productivity posts, or what my friend charmingly calls best practices in self-care. Mostly these are relatively small-scale save five minutes posts (as indeed, most writing on the topic is). Today Im writing something different. This post is part history (its about me) and part acknowledgement that change happens in levels. There are a lot of things that you can do to make sure that you juggle more plates without dropping them,but you have to have to do things one at a time.
         </em>
         <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File%3ALightmatter_paperwork.jpg" title="By By Aaron Logan (from http://www.lightmatter.net/gallery/albums.php) [CC BY 2.5 (http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/2.5)], via Wikimedia Commons">
          <img alt="Lightmatter paperwork" class="aligncenter" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/63/Lightmatter_paperwork.jpg" width="512"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         I used to be very disorganised,now Im organised.
        </p>
        <p>
         I still
         <em>
          feel
         </em>
         disorganised. On any given day I screw up as many things as I used to: the difference is that my bandwidth hasincreased. Im screwing things up, but Im getting more things right, remembering more birthdays, being less of a hassle to work with.
        </p>
        <p>
         The way I got the hang of my life was to recognise that I was a slave to my email and to make use of that fact.
        </p>
        <p>
         I was a Computer Scientist, so almost all of my life (academic, work, and social) came though my email  I checked it obsessively to see if
         <em>
          anything
         </em>
         had happened.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Permanent To-do List
        </h1>
        <p>
         In early 2008 I discovered that Gmail had an archive button (Yes, Computer Scientist I know). When something was done, I could make it vanish into the search place. Overnight, this changed how I dealt with my correspondence. Things in my inbox were things that I still needed reply to. I could get rid of the junk, reply to the rest, and generally exert some control over my life (to this day, I confused as how I managed to complete a degree and a PhD without having even got a this much of a handle on my organisation).
        </p>
        <p>
         A few months later, it occurred to me that I could send myself emails: if there was something I had to do (Buy milk is the classic) then I could send myself an email. That email would sit in my inbox until I did the thing and then I could archive it. Suddenly I have a permanent to-do list that Im checking obsessively. Moreover, because I was obsessively checking my email anyway so I could never have that experience of writing out a to-do list and then ignoring it or losing it, or forgetting what was on it. If something went on the to-do list then I saw it eight times a day until I did it.
        </p>
        <p>
         This was a transformation. I stopped forgetting things:if there was something I committed to, then I did it. Paperwork got done in time, bills were paid. In particular,
         <em>
          literal
         </em>
         nice things started happening. Before, I might think Hey, it would be really nice if you sent X one of those sweets she likes but Id be dealing with something else when the opportunity arose, now Id have the thought, send myself an email, and X would actually get the sweets. I didnt become a better person, but it sure looked like I did to everyone.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Level 2: Calendar
        </h2>
        <p>
         Three months later I started using Google Calendar to send me tasks, both forward tasks Submissions are now open so upload your paper and reoccurring ones Tidy house. They end up in the inbox, so they get done. Im now making all of my dentist appointments, avoiding deadline stresses because Ive done the work, and saving lots of money on train and plane tickets by buying them in advance. Im also been reminded when Im due to give blood so now I really am becoming a better person.
        </p>
        <p>
         Something that I didnt notice at the time but I realised after reading
         <a href="http://www.amazon.co.uk/gp/product/0142000280/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1634&amp;creative=19450&amp;creativeASIN=0142000280&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=joeredd-21&amp;linkId=LRJADE3DZD5TL6NU">
          Getting Things Done
         </a>
         was that Id taken my brain out of the process. At any time of the day I used to be thinking a half dozen things like must pay that bill, must talk to Steve, must buy milk  as soon as I had a
         <em>
          trusted
         </em>
         place to put these thoughts that I knew Id check, these thoughts all disappeared and with them much of a base level of stress that I didnt realise I was carrying around.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Level 3: Gamification
        </h1>
        <p>
         The masterstroke was yet to come:
         <span class="st">
          <em>
           Gamification
          </em>
         </span>
         . I started to chart the size of my inbox/to-do list. When you do that, getting though your tasks becomes a game. It started basic, but Id poke it every few months and, as it refined, so did how quickly I got into a flow state. You can see a selection of the images that evolved into
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/portfolio/stress-graphs/" title="Stress Graphs">
          my stress chart
         </a>
         below.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.30.01.png">
          <img alt="Screen Shot 2014-12-16 at 11.28.16" class="aligncenter wp-image-4641" height="118" src="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.28.16-300x241.png" width="145"/>
          <img alt="Screen Shot 2014-12-16 at 11.28.48" class="aligncenter size-thumbnail wp-image-4642" height="150" src="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.28.48-150x150.png" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.29.08.png">
          <img alt="Screen Shot 2014-12-16 at 11.29.08" class="aligncenter wp-image-4640" height="177" src="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.29.08-300x245.png" width="215"/>
         </a>
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.28.48.png">
          <img alt="Screen Shot 2014-12-16 at 11.30.01" class="aligncenter wp-image-4639" height="219" src="http://joereddington.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/Screen-Shot-2014-12-16-at-11.30.01-150x150.png" width="219"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <h2>
         Winning rather than working
        </h2>
        <p>
         Getting gamification right means that you are processing things faster, dealing with things, getting more free time, making more of an impact, mostly because you are looking at how you are scoring on a chart a few times a day Its embarrassing to admit, but it worked wonders.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Summary
        </h1>
        <p>
         There are two things that I want you to take away from this.
        </p>
        <p>
         The first one is that if you are a slave to your email, maybe thats okay. Maybe you can make use of it. If I hadnt have been a slave to my email I wouldnt have been able to free up nearly enough of my time to do the first prototype
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/portfolio/project-toomanycooks/" title="Project TooManyCooks">
          TooManyCooks
         </a>
         book (there are now 13!) I wouldnt have been able to commit to building the
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/aac-and-the-domesday-dataset/" title="AAC and the Domesday Dataset">
          Domesday Dataset
         </a>
         , which I think has made more of a difference than anything Ive ever done in academia.
        </p>
        <p>
         The second thing is that this process happened in layers. I got the hang of emails, then of open tasks, then of calendar events. There was NO way I could have put all this together in one leap. It had to be stage by stage. Sometimes I tried something new and it didnt work, thats okay, because I was still at a level above where I had been.
        </p>
        <p>
         As a last thing, Im a big fan of
         <a href="http://joereddington.com/673/2014/01/17/recommended-viewing-randy-pausch/" title="Recommended Viewing: Randy Pausch">
          Randy Pausch
         </a>
         , and this is the thing I respectively disagree with. From his wonderful
         <a href="http://www.cs.virginia.edu/~robins/Randy/TMenglishTranscript.pdf">
          time-management talk
         </a>
         :
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          How many people here  Im going to take it for granted that everybody here has an email inbox.  How many people here have more than 20 items in their email inbox?  Oooh! Im in the right room. Your inbox is not your To Do list. My wife has learned that I need to get my inbox clear. Sometimes this means just filing things away and putting something on my To Do list. Remember, the To Do list is sorted by importance but does anybody here have an email program where you can press this Sort By Importance button?
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         I have to say that maybe some of us need to do quite a bit of work before we can be at his level
         <img alt=":)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://joereddington.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/icon_smile.gif.pagespeed.ce.sphHKcO2zc.gif"/>
        </p>
       </span>
