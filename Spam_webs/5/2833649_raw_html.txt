pathjs.getPageFormatIds = function(pageId){

			//if this is an array, add all members to formatIdArr
			if (pathjs.isArray(myAdMeta[slot])){
				formatIdArr.concat(myAdMeta[slot]);
			//otherwise just add single value
			} else {
				formatIdArr.push(myAdMeta[slot]);
			}
		}
	}
	//make the array a CSV string
	return formatIdArr.join();

       <h1 class="entry-title">
        Why I dont trust the gods (at least not if Im alone with one)
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2014-06-25T19:09:59+00:00">
         June 25, 2014
        </span>
        by
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/author/allergicpagan/" rel="author" title="John Halstead">
           John Halstead
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="post-comments">
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2014/06/25/why-i-dont-trust-the-gods/#comments">
          <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="5713 http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/?p=5713">
           37 Comments
          </span>
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         I am a
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Dreaming-the-Myth/Blogger/Listings/john-halstead.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Jungian Neo-Pagan
         </a>
         , which means that, theologically speaking,
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://humanisticpaganism.com/2011/09/18/the-archetypes-are-gods-re-godding-the-archetypes-by-john-h-halstead/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          I fall somewhere between atheist Pagans and devotional polytheists regarding the existence of the gods
         </a>
         . By placing my beliefs in the middle here I do not mean to privilege my beliefs, only to make the point that I both agree and disagree with both groups about different things. One thing I agree with
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/sermonsfromthemound/2013/07/seeking-the-mystery-1-e-book-sale-giveaway-ch-1-excerpt/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          devotional polytheists
         </a>
         about is that the gods should be taken seriously. One thing I disagree with many of them about is that they can necessarily be trusted.*
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Taking the gods seriously
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         I worry sometimes that we
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://neo-paganism.org/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Neo-Pagans
         </a>
         dont take our own gods seriously enough. While I may disagree with
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/sermonsfromthemound/2013/07/seeking-the-mystery-1-e-book-sale-giveaway-ch-1-excerpt/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          devotional polytheists
         </a>
         about the metaphysical nature of the gods (whether they arereal, independent, sentient beingsor
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://humanisticpaganaism.com/2011/09/18/the-archetypes-are-gods-re-godding-the-archetypes-by-john-h-halstead/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          real, independent semi-conscious archetypes
         </a>
         ),one thing I admire about them is the seriousness (the
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://paganhumanist.com/2011/02/15/what-is-piety/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          piety
         </a>
         if you will) with which they approach the gods.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.amazon.com/The-Triumph-Moon-History-Witchcraft/dp/0192854496/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1403730394&amp;sr=8-1&amp;keywords=triumph+of+the+moon" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Ronald Hutton
         </a>
         has noted that one of the distinguishing features of Neo-Pagan witchcraft is the consecration of play. As the Charge of the Goddess affirms, mirth and reverence both play an important role in Neo-Paganism. This is an admirable corrective to the dreary seriousness of much of Christian liturgy. But there is a time and place for all things, and
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2012/02/08/if-i-cant-dance-i-dont-want-your-religion/">
          sometimes it seems that our revelry trivializes our religion
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         More to the point, sometimes we Neo-Pagans trivialize our gods. We do this with a lot of our art  consider how, in Pagan art on the Internet, the goddesses look like Barbie dolls or comic book superheroines. Barbara Ardingers
         <em>
          <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.amazon.com/Finding-New-Goddesses-Reclaiming-Playfulness/dp/1550225243/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1390333149&amp;sr=8-1&amp;keywords=finding+new+goddesses" rel="external nofollow" title="">
           Finding New Goddesses: Reclaiming Playfulness in Our Spiritual Lives
          </a>
         </em>
         is another good example of the trivialization of the gods. While Ardingers book was perhaps intended as a parody, there is more than a kernel of truthfulness in her characterization of Neo-Pagans worshiping
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://feminismandreligion.com/2014/01/05/who-are-the-pagans-by-barbara-ardinger/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          found gods like Spendifera (
          <em>
           spen-DIF-er-uh
          </em>
          ) Goddess of the Mall
         </a>
         . And then there isthe eclectic Neo-Pagan practice of using gods like tools, which has been called plug and play godsand the god faucet. These trivializations of the encounter with the divinehave encouraged a kind of backlash in our community, helping in part to fuel the growth of
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/sermonsfromthemound/2013/07/seeking-the-mystery-1-e-book-sale-giveaway-ch-1-excerpt/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          devotional polytheism
         </a>
          which I see as a much needed balance to the excesses of an eclectic and playful Neo-Paganism. The desire to take the gods seriously is, I think, much of what fueled one side of the Superhero debate in the Pagan blogosphere last yearand the more recent discussionwhich was sparked by Morpheus Ravennas post on how ritual might be different if we took the gods more seriously.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Are the gods worthy of worship?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         But as much as I am concerned about the trivialization of the gods, as a
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Dreaming-the-Myth/Blogger/Listings/john-halstead.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Jungian Neo-Pagan
         </a>
         *, I am also concerned when it is assumed that the gods are benign. Uncritical trust can be a form of disrespect too.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.etymonline.com/index.php?allowed_in_frame=0&amp;search=worship&amp;searchmode=none" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          The word worship derives from the Old English word for worthy.
         </a>
         I wonder then, why have I rarely seen the question asked whether the Pagan gods are worthy of worship. To put it another way, why should we assume the gods can be trusted? When I have asked the question of devotional polytheists in the past, it is often heard as a nonsensical. I have been met with responses ranging fromBecause I have faith they can be trustedtoBecause they can rip your arms off and beat you with them. The first echoes of monotheism for me, and the second is a non-sequitur (as well as counterfactual).
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/johnbeckett/2013/07/a-commitment-to-the-gods.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          John Beckett has written about the gods as paragons of virtue.
         </a>
         But are they not also paragons of vice? I concur with John Opsopaus that
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="https://web.eecs.utk.edu/~mclennan/BA/JO-MoG.txt" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          The Gods Are Not Moral Ideals
         </a>
         . The polytheistic gods, as I understand them, are not necessarily good and they are not omni-benevolent. If the myths are to be believed on any level, the gods are just as flawed as human beings  they just have more power. Why bow down to power, if it is not paired with virtue?
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_5715" style="width: 260px">
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/allergicpagan/files/2013/11/250px-Dionysus.png" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class=" wp-image-5715 " height="186" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/allergicpagan/files/2013/11/250px-Dionysus.png" title="250px-Dionysus" width="250"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          This guy bothers me.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_5717" style="width: 310px">
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/allergicpagan/files/2013/11/young-fit-bacchus-thumbnail1.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class=" wp-image-5717" height="180" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/allergicpagan/files/2013/11/young-fit-bacchus-thumbnail1-300x180.jpg" title="young-fit-bacchus-thumbnail" width="300"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          This guy bothers me more.
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         One answer perhaps is that the gods should be worshiped because
         <em>
          they are
         </em>
          just as nature can be worshiped because
         <em>
          it is
         </em>
         . Worship (or reverence, if you prefer) is the natural human response to nature.
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2013/01/19/i-worship-the-blind-god/">
          Nature is not moral or virtuous, and I have no reservations about worshiping it.
         </a>
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2013/01/19/i-worship-the-blind-god/">
          But worshiping something and trusting it are two different things.
         </a>
         I dont ever assume that nature is benign or trustworthy. Why should we assume the gods are trustworthy? Worshiping them, I understand. But trusting them? I suspect that for some devotional polytheists, this trust is built through experience, creating relationships with the gods (what I think Hellenics call
         <em>
          kharis
         </em>
         ). But, in my experiences, as a
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Dreaming-the-Myth/Blogger/Listings/john-halstead.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Jungian Neo-Pagan
         </a>
         *, the gods are anything but trustworthy.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The role of capricious gods in a consent culture
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Galina Krasskova writes that Dionysus and her own god, Odin, can be cruel, brutal, savage, even sadistic, and can even violate consent.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/sermonsfromthemound/2014/04/consent-culture-101-basic-practices-and-teaching-games/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Consent
         </a>
         is a core value for most Pagans, I think. Do we have space in our pantheons for gods who violate consent? Many of us worship nature even though nature does not respect consent. Why should the gods be any different? The Cambridge ritualist, Gilbert Murray, wrote the following about Euripides Dionysus:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          There are in the world things not of reason, but both below and above it; causes of emotion, which we cannot express, which we tend to worship, which we feel, perhaps, to be the precious elements in life. These things are Gods or forms of God: not fabulous immortal men, but Things which Are, things utterly non-human and non-moral, which bring man bliss or tear his life to shreds without a break in their own serenity.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Its easy to see how gods like
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Pagan-Paths-Blogs/the-real-shadow-gods-of-paganism-part-2.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Dionysus and Odin
         </a>
         fit this description, but I think it potentially applies to all the gods. Take Aphrodite, for example, the so-called goddess of love, perhaps one of the most trivialized pagan goddesses in mainstream culture.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.theoi.com/Olympios/AphroditeWrath.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          But in the myths, she was was responsible for the doom of dozens of mortals and gods who crossed her.
         </a>
         One thing that I take away from the myths is that the gods are just as likely to bring doom as they are to bring a boon.
        </p>
        <p>
         Christine Downing, author of
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.amazon.com/Gods-Our-Midst-Mythological-Masculine/dp/0824512421/ref=sr_1_5?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1403721549&amp;sr=8-5&amp;keywords=Gods+in+our+Midst" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <em>
           Gods in our Midst
          </em>
         </a>
         and
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.amazon.com/The-Goddess-Mythological-Images-Feminine/dp/0595467741/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1403721598&amp;sr=8-1&amp;keywords=The+Goddess%3A+Mythological+Images+of+the+Feminine" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <em>
           The Goddess: Mythological Images of the Feminine
          </em>
         </a>
         , describes the gods as the immortal, permanent, ineluctable aspects of the world. I think that definition could apply to the gods of devotional polytheists, as well as
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://neo-paganism.org/beliefs-of-neo-pagans/neo-pagan-gods/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Neo-Pagan gods
         </a>
         . Whether we call them gods, archetypes, forces of nature, immensities, or things which are, it seems to me that they should be respected  but not trusted. Respected, not the way Christians respect their benevolent father-god, but more like the way the ancient henotheistic Israelites respected Yahweh, who could be cruel and capricious. (Remember the story of Uzzah, who was struck down when he touched the ark of the covenant to steady it when the oxen carrying it stumbled in
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://biblehub.com/2_samuel/6-7.htm" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          2 Samuel 6
         </a>
         ?) Yahweh was like a terrible force of nature that could kill indiscriminately (as well as a
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.womensordination.org/resources/female-images-of-god-in-the-bible/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          nurturing mother
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <p>
         This is not to say that the gods should always be feared. I can revel in a thunderstorm, and still respect it. I agree with John Beckett, when he writes that
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/johnbeckett/2013/09/trusting-the-gods.html#ixzz35fOwNdli" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          we can trust the gods to be who and what they are
         </a>
          not to answer all our prayers, not to have our best interest in mind, but
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/johnbeckett/2013/09/trusting-the-gods.html#ixzz35fPamTrV" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          we can trust the gods to be the gods.
         </a>
         I agree, but I dont think thats saying very much. I trust a thunderstorm to be a thunderstorm or the ocean to be the ocean, which is to say, I dont trust either much at all. But I still respect them and reverence their beauty and power.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          How serious am I?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Isnt this all just hyperbole or metaphor, John?, you might ask. If a person like me believes the gods are natural forces or psychological archetypes, why does it matter if I respect them? If the gods dont grant us divine boons, what is the point of piety? I would argue that respect is still the appropriate attitude for natural forces and psychological archetypes If I dont respect the lightning storm or the ocean, they can kill. Attitudes determine actions. And an impious attitude toward nature can get me killed. If I treat the ocean like it is only the benevolent sheltering womb of the Goddess, then I overlook its dangers, the hidden undertow, the lurking predators. The ocean may not intend to kill me, but I would still be just as dead.
        </p>
        <p>
         And what about archetypes?
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Pagan-Paths-Blogs/the-return-of-the-gods.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Carl Jung called the archetypes gods
         </a>
         and compared the psyche to an Olympus full of deities who want to be propitiated, served, feared and worshipped. How can the archetypes be dangerous? They are dangerous if I assume they are benevolent. Consider if I treat Aphrodite as merely a saccharine goddess of love, if I confuse
         <em>
          Aphrodite Pandemos
         </em>
         (lust) for
         <em>
          Aphrodite Ourania
         </em>
         (universal love). Would that not wreak havoc on my personal life? Or what if I treat the intoxicating power of Dionysus  in the form of drugs, alcohol, or sex  as an unequivocal good? Or take the Morrigan, the goddess of sovereignty, who seems to be drawing many followers to her in recent years. Often this worship seems to take take the form of an expression of
         <em>
          personal
         </em>
         sovereignty. But a one-sided worship of personal sovereignty can undermine the connections that create community, something we Pagans need to be especially aware of, I think. (Remember that
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.timelessmyths.com/celtic/celtworld.html#Marriage" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          sovereignty in Celtic myth
         </a>
         meant, not independence, but a special kind
         <em>
          relationship
         </em>
         with ones community and the land.)
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Only pantheon is worthy of worship
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         And the gods can also be dangerous when they are not balanced with each other. What happens if Dionysus ecstasy is not balanced by the tempering influence of Apollos cool rationality? Or if Odins berzerker rage is not mitigated by the grounding influence of Frigga? Will not these archetypal power tear my life to shreds without a break in their own serenity, as Murray writes?
        </p>
        <p>
         This is one thing that Paganism has taught me about my former faith, Christianity: The problem with Yahweh wasnt so much that he was a bad god; it was that
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2012/08/03/these-are-my-gods/">
          he thought he was the only god
         </a>
         . Protestantism of the kind that I practiced (Mormonism, specifically) had done away with the Trinity, the saints, and the Theotokos, not to mention the spirits of nature  leaving only the jealous God who will have no others beside him. (
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2014/05/19/finding-the-pagan-goddess-in-a-mormon-temple/">
          Sure, Mormons believe in a Heavenly Mother, but she has no name, cannot be prayed to, and is only mentioned in whispers.
         </a>
         ) Paganism, especially
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Dreaming-the-Myth/Blogger/Listings/john-halstead.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Jungian Neo-Paganism
         </a>
         , taught me that
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Pagan-Paths-Blogs/dreaming-the-myth-forward.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          it is better to be whole than to be good
         </a>
          if to be good means to be one-sided.
        </p>
        <p>
         In this sense, it is only the gods
         <em>
          in their plurality
         </em>
         that are worthy of worship, not any individual god. To put it another way, it is only the pantheon, not the gods, that can be trusted. Christine Downing writes, At the very heart of polytheism lies the conviction that only the totality of the gods and goddesses constitutes the divine world.  There are many myths that reveal how fatal it is for us humans to overlook even one, to fail to give each his or her due honor. For us to disregard any one of the gods, she goes on is to curtail the richness of the world and the fullness of the human.
        </p>
        <p>
         Of course, I cant worship all the gods all the time. I cant even worship all the gods of one pantheon all the time. But I can be aware, as my attention is drawn to one god in particular for a while, that any deity exists in a sacred context  a pantheon  that includes other balancing powers. I can remember this in deed this by honoring different gods during different seasons or different times of the day. I can do this by honoring in my rituals those ADF druids call
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="https://www.adf.org/articles/cosmology/outsiders.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          the Outsiders
         </a>
         and what the Greeks called
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknown_God" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <em>
           Agnostos Theos
          </em>
          , the unknown god
         </a>
         . I can do this by celebrating the fact that
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2014/06/17/darkness-leaping-out-of-light-the-summer-solstice-from-a-different-angle/">
          every god has his or her shadow
         </a>
         
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/EasyBlog/paganism-s-real-shadow-gods-part-1.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          even the most seemingly benign
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Pagan priesthood and laity
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Of course, there will always be those who are called to be priests and priestesses (or even godspouses), devoted exclusively to one god or another. But perhaps such individuals should be approached with the same reservation and caution with which we approach the gods. Emerson is believed to have said,
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          The gods we worship write their names on our faces, be sure of that.  Therefore it behooves us to be careful what we are worshiping, for what we are worshiping we are becoming.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Odin devotee, Galina Krasskova, seems to suggest as much when she writes: 
         <span style="color: #000000;">
          Falling into Gods like Odin, and like Dionysos  carries with it the potential for finding those elements of the Gods nature in ourselves, for becoming in however small or large a way, transformed by Them, by the contact, transformed into becoming a bit more like Them in outlook. What does this mean when your god is a savage god?
         </span>
         I imagine this can be a good thing, just as there is such a thing as righteous anger  but it is not an unmitigated good. An exclusive worship of one deity can be pathological. Consider this example, from a certain priest of Dionysus:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          In the spirit of Savonarola and Charles Manson
         </p>
         <p>
          I think the cult of celebrity is destroying Paganism
          <br/>
          so Im going to transform myself into a Pagan celebrity.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats right. Im gonna get my nasty clown c*ck all up in that sh*t
          <br/>
          so that the whole institution ends looking gross and absurd
          <br/>
          and people will have no choice but to flee internet communities
          <br/>
          and return to the solace of their shrines, just them and their gods
          <br/>
          and never another thought given to what authorities think.
         </p>
         <p>
          To kill what you hate, you must become it.
         </p>
         <p>
          It aint gonna be easy. Im going to have to stir up a lot of drama.
         </p>
         <p>
          Innocent people will get burned.
         </p>
         <p>
          But they brought it on themselves. You make yourself a sheep,
          <br/>
          youre pretty much asking to get fleeced. 
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         Fortunately,
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/johnbeckett/2014/06/priesthood-a-modern-pagan-view.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          not all Pagan priests identify so completely with the object of their devotion
         </a>
         , and not all deities are as destructive as Dionysus. But, as a
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/Dreaming-the-Myth/Blogger/Listings/john-halstead.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Jungian Neo-Pagan
         </a>
         *,I think the danger is always there in focusing exclusively on one god or goddess. It seems like there is a fashion in Paganism recently for ever increasing levels of intimacy with individual gods (witness the proliferation of godspouses) which seems odd in a community which is defined primarily by its polytheism. Priests and priestesses are an essential part of our community, but its good, I think, that most of us are not priests (at least not in the traditional sense of the term). A large pagan laity is a good thing, in my mind. A community of true priests, like a community of shamans, would be no community at all  it would be a madhouse.
        </p>
        <p>
         As for me, I am content that no Pagan god has singled me out.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://lectiodivinapaganus.wordpress.com/2014/06/18/my-easy-god-is-gone-by-james-kavanaugh/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          I know what that kind of divine scrutiny is like
         </a>
         . I know what it is like to be
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://witchesandpagans.com/EasyBlog/polytheistic-experience-and-jung-s-experience-of-the-archetypes.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          possessed
         </a>
         by a god (Yahweh). I experienced it more like a disease than a blessing.
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2012/08/03/these-are-my-gods/">
          And I have learned that the only remedy for it is to open my eyes to discover a world full of gods
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         [*Note: I do not mean to imply by anything I write here that the experiences of devotional polytheists and Neo-Pagans are necessarily comparable.
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/allergicpagan/2014/02/08/these-arent-the-gods-youre-looking-for/">
          Oftentimes they are not comparable
         </a>
         , as I have learned from discussions with several devotional polytheists. Sometimes they are not entirely incomparable, as I have learned from Joshua Tenpenny's frank writing about his communication with the gods
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://servinggods.wordpress.com/2014/03/02/communicate-with-the-divinities/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          here
         </a>
         and
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://servinggods.wordpress.com/2014/03/04/inspiration/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          here
         </a>
         . Nor do I mean to imply that all Neo-Pagans experience the gods the same way, or that my experience is everyone else's.]
        </p>

            <p>
             I do struggle with the idea of gods and consent.  For me, its like a contract.  I agree that they can meddle about in my life, but they understand that meddling in ways that are harmful to me will result in me refusing to fulfill any promises Ive made them and refusing to serve them further.  There are certain gods I give a wide berth to (for example, Odin) because they tend to be more high-handed and less concerned with what their followers want.  The gods I work with tend to be more of the kind that poke you to go a direction, and poke you again, and then poke you harder, until you either go the direction or you say No, I refuse that path.  When I was first being pulled to become a priestess, I was getting pretty much all dark goddess and grief counseling all the time, and I finally said, OK, gods, if this is all my path as a priestess is gonna be, Im not gonna do it, because this is killing me.  The pull stopped abruptly, until a couple of years later when I had the opportunity to perform a wedding and some other more positive aspects of ritual, and then I said, OK, gods, this is a better balance.  This path, Ill walk.  I have to have the good.  You cannot only give me the hard things, because Ill become useless to you as I burn out and fail.  They seemed to understand I will do what is hard, but I need experiences that feed me as well.

I find that in certain parts of pagan community, some are inclined to want those benefits, but still refuse to earn them with service.  Thats the god faucet mentality you mention:  the gods should hear my words and do my will on my terms, but I should not be expected to hear their words and do their will on their terms.           <div class="dsq-comment-body" id="dsq-comment-body-4038">
           <div class="dsq-comment-message" id="dsq-comment-message-4038">
            <p>
             Reading this, frankly, I dont understand what you mean by trust. Trust them
             <i>
              to do what
             </i>
             ? I trust my friends, certainly, but I dont trust them to be nice or kind all the time, because people arent. I dont trust them to put my best interests before their own, because that would be unreasonable. Does that make them unworthy of my trust and friendship?
            </p>
            <p>
             The gods are individuals, and their natures and personalities are what they are, and we should give our trust and faith based on what they are, not what we think they ought to be. I trust the gods I worship to do what they say they will do, to carry out their duties, to be true to their natures. Mortals simply declaring that the gods ought to be or do this or that  to be omnibenevolent, to answer every prayer, to do whatever  doesnt make that actually their duty or nature.
            </p>
            <p>
             I trust that the things Hekate asks me to do have some purpose, and are not just whims, even if I dont understand them, because I understand her nature to be such that she makes deliberate choices, and I trust that she will accept my choices, and accept it when I make the choice not to do as she asks, because she has always done so. I trust Dionysos to
             <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://ariadne.dememe.info/i-shall-set-free-my-hair-and-wear-a-fawn-skin" rel="nofollow external" title="">
              grant me freedom through his ecstasy
             </a>
             , because that is his nature and his function. I trust all the gods I worship to do their things.
            </p>
            <p>
             I also dont understand how theres no functional difference to a Jungian between extreme devotion and henotheism. Henotheism, by definition, means worshiping only one god while acknowledging the existence of others. The people you mention not only worship multiple gods, but worship multiple gods with what you seem to be calling extreme devotion. How is there not a functional difference?
            </p>
            <p>
             And if you think that Apollo is a god of cool rationalism, then your understanding of him is both extreme modern and extremely facile. He is a god of philosophy and science, yes, but he is also a god of art and creativity, things which are not often coolly rational. He is a god of plague as well as healing. There are ecstatic rites of Apollo. The Oracle at Delphi was an ecstatic oracle, relying on trance and mind-altering fumes to contact the god. This supposed Apollonian/Dionysian split is an entirely modern notion, and again, has nothing to do with the nature of the god. Dionysos, meanwhile, is not only a god of excess. Abstinence, too, is a part of his worship, because yes, if we do not take time to live in the world, take time in a mundane consciousness, ecstasy rapidly loses meaning. It does us no good if it is not set aside as separate and different.
            </p>
            <p>
             For all you talk of taking the gods seriously, your understanding of them seems shallow and poorly considered, and to be based in what you think they ought to be rather than what they are. I dont mean that you think of them as archetypes, but that you do not seem to consider the full dimension of what those archetypes are, but to rely on only one facet of them, even as you mention multiple epithets for some of them.
            </p>
