     <p>
      <b>
       Undines
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       ondines
      </b>
      are a category of
      <a href="/wiki/Elemental" title="Elemental">
       elemental
      </a>
      beings associated with water, first named in the
      <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">
       alchemical
      </a>
      writings of
      <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">
       Paracelsus
      </a>
      . Similar creatures are found in classical literature, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">
       Ovid
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">
        Metamorphoses
       </a>
      </i>
      . Later writers developed the undine into a water nymph in its own right, and it continues to live in modern literature and art through such adaptations as
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">
       Hans Christian Andersen
      </a>
      's "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Mermaid" title="The Little Mermaid">
       The Little Mermaid
      </a>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      Undines are almost invariably depicted as being female, and are usually found in forest pools and waterfalls. The group contains many species, including
      <a href="/wiki/Nereid" title="Nereid">
       nereides
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Limnad" title="Limnad">
       limoniades
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Naiad" title="Naiad">
       naiades
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mermaid" title="Mermaid">
       mermaids
      </a>
      . Although resembling humans in form they lack a human soul, so to achieve immortality they must acquire one by marrying a human. Such a union is not without risk for the man, because if he is unfaithful he is fated to die.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Undine
      </i>
      is a term that appears in the
      <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">
       alchemical
      </a>
      writings of
      <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">
       Paracelsus
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESilver200038_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESilver200038-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a Renaissance
      <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">
       alchemist
      </a>
      and physician. It is derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       unda
      </i>
      , meaning "wave", and first appears in Paracelsus' book
      <i>
       Liber de Nymphis, sylphis, pygmaeis et salamandris et de caeteris spiritibus
      </i>
      , published posthumously in 1658.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OED-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Ondine
      </i>
      is an alternative spelling,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeletinski.C4.AD1998472_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeletinski.C4.AD1998472-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has become a female given name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERifkin2011258_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERifkin2011258-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Elementals">
       Elementals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elementals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Paracelsus believed that each of the four
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">
       classical elements
      </a>
      
      <a href="/wiki/Earth_(classical_element)" title="Earth (classical element)">
       earth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Water_(classical_element)" title="Water (classical element)">
       water
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">
       air
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_(classical_element)" title="Fire (classical element)">
       fire
      </a>
       is inhabited by different categories of
      <a href="/wiki/Elemental" title="Elemental">
       elemental spirits
      </a>
      , liminal creatures that share our world:
      <a href="/wiki/Gnome" title="Gnome">
       gnomes
      </a>
      , undines,
      <a href="/wiki/Sylph" title="Sylph">
       sylphs
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salamander_(legendary_creature)" title="Salamander (legendary creature)">
       salamanders
      </a>
      respectively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlban200347-48_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlban200347-48-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He describes these elementals as the "invisible, spiritual counterparts of visible Nature... many resembling human beings in shape, and inhabiting worlds of their own, unknown to man because his undeveloped senses were incapable of functioning beyond the limitations of the grosser elements."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHall1928105_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1928105-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_common_attributes">
       Description and common attributes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description and common attributes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Undines are almost invariably depicted as being female, which is consistent with the ancient idea that water is a female element.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHall1928107_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1928107-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They are usually found in forest pools and waterfalls,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBane2013333_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBane2013333-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and their beautiful singing voices
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodworthPope_Morris18272_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodworthPope_Morris18272-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      are sometimes heard over the sound of water. The group contains many species, including
      <a href="/wiki/Oread" title="Oread">
       oreades
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nereid" title="Nereid">
       nereides
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Limnad" title="Limnad">
       limoniades
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Naiad" title="Naiad">
       naiades
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mermaid" title="Mermaid">
       mermaids
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Potamides_(mythology)" title="Potamides (mythology)">
       potamides
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHall1928107_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1928107-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      What undines lack, compared to humans, is a soul. Marriage with a human shortens their lives on Earth, but earns them an immortal human soul.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fass_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fass-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The offspring of a union between an undine and a man are human with a soul, but also with some kind of aquatic characteristic, called a watermark. Moses Binswanger, the protagonist in Hansjrg Schneider's
      <i>
       Das Wasserzeichen
      </i>
      (1997), has a cleft in his throat, for instance, which must be periodically submerged in water to prevent it from becoming painful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneider_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Schneider-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       ancient Greek
      </a>
      philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">
       Empedocles
      </a>
      (c.490  c.430 BC) was the first to propose that the four classical elements were sufficient to explain everything present in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacauley201072_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacauley201072-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The philosophy of nature spirits was also familiar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">
       ancient Egyptians
      </a>
      and Greeks, and certainly to Paracelsus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHall1928106_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1928106-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Celtic languages scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jenner" title="Henry Jenner">
       Henry Jenner
      </a>
      has argued that the elementals grew out of the folklore that preceded them:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       The subdivisions and elaborations [of nature spirits] ... by Paracelsus, the Rosicrucians, and the modern theosophists are no doubt amplifications of that popular belief in the existence of a race, neither divine nor human, but very like to human beings, who existed on a "plane" different from that of humans, though occupying the same space which ... resembles the theory of these mystics in its main outlines, and was probably what suggested it to them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESilver200040_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESilver200040-15">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         15
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      David Gallagher argues that, although they had Paracelsus as a source, 19th and 20th-century German authors found inspiration for their many versions of undine in classical literature, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">
       Ovid
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">
        Metamorphoses
       </a>
      </i>
      , especially given the transformation of many of their undines into springs: Hyrie (book VII) and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egeria_(mythology)" title="Egeria (mythology)">
       Egeria
      </a>
      (book XV) are two such characters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGallagher2009345_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallagher2009345-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature.2C_drama.2C_ballet_and_music">
       In literature, drama, ballet and music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In literature, drama, ballet and music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Later writers embellished Paracelsus' undine classification by developing it into a water nymph in its own right. The romance
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Undine_(novella)" title="Undine (novella)">
        Undine
       </a>
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_de_la_Motte_Fouqu%C3%A9" title="Friedrich de la Motte Fouqu">
       Friedrich de la Motte Fouqu
      </a>
      , published in 1811, is based on a passage in Paracelsus'
      <i>
       Liber de Nymphis
      </i>
      in which he relates how an undine can acquire an immortal soul by marrying a human,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESax1998129_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESax1998129-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although it likely also borrows from the 17th-century Rosicrucian novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Comte_de_Gabalis" title="Comte de Gabalis">
        Comte de Gabalis
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeber_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Seeber-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Ondine
      </i>
      was the title of one of the poems in
      <a href="/wiki/Aloysius_Bertrand" title="Aloysius Bertrand">
       Aloysius Bertrand
      </a>
      's collection
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gaspard_de_la_Nuit_(book)" title="Gaspard de la Nuit (book)">
        Gaspard de la Nuit
       </a>
      </i>
      of 1842. This poem inspired the first movement of
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">
       Maurice Ravel
      </a>
      's 1908 piano suite
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gaspard_de_la_nuit" title="Gaspard de la nuit">
        Gaspard de la nuit
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The myth of the undine was one of the inspirations behind the French play
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pell%C3%A9as_and_M%C3%A9lisande" title="Pellas and Mlisande">
        Pellas et Mlisande
       </a>
      </i>
      written by
      <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Maeterlinck" title="Maurice Maeterlinck">
       Maurice Maeterlinck
      </a>
      , which was performed only once, on 17 May 1893. The composer
      <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">
       Claude Debussy
      </a>
      was in the audience, and he used the play's text as the
      <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">
       libretto
      </a>
      for his opera of the same name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJensen2014181_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJensen2014181-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 1939 play
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ondine_(play)" title="Ondine (play)">
        Ondine
       </a>
      </i>
      by French dramatist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Giradoux" title="Jean Giradoux">
       Jean Giradoux
      </a>
      is also based upon Fouqu's novella,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGallagher2009352_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallagher2009352-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as is a ballet by composer
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Werner_Henze" title="Hans Werner Henze">
       Hans Werner Henze
      </a>
      and choreographer
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Ashton" title="Frederick Ashton">
       Frederick Ashton
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lillyman_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lillyman-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Margot_Fonteyn" title="Margot Fonteyn">
       Margot Fonteyn
      </a>
      as Undine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holschuh_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Holschuh-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Austrian author
      <a href="/wiki/Ingeborg_Bachmann" title="Ingeborg Bachmann">
       Ingeborg Bachmann
      </a>
      , a friend of Henze's who collaborated with him frequently, attended the premiere of the ballet in London, and published her short story "Undine geht" in the collection
      <i>
       Das dreiigste Jahr
      </i>
      (1961),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baackmann_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Baackmann-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which Undine "is neither a human nor a water spirit, but an idea".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holschuh_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Holschuh-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Fouqu's
      <i>
       Undine
      </i>
      also exerted an influence on
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">
       Hans Christian Andersen
      </a>
      's "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Mermaid" title="The Little Mermaid">
       The Little Mermaid
      </a>
      " (1837),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEH.C3.B8yrup2008372_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEH.C3.B8yrup2008372-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holbek_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Holbek-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/H.D." title="H.D.">
       H.D.
      </a>
      plays on this identification in her autobiographical novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/HERmione" title="HERmione">
        HERmione
       </a>
      </i>
      (1927).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEH.D.1981120_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEH.D.1981120-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFriedman2008114_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFriedman2008114-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Burton Pollin notes the popularity of the tale in the English-speaking world: translations in English appeared in 1818 and 1830, and a "superior version" was published by American churchman Thomas Tracy in 1839 and reprinted in 1824, 1840, 1844, and 1845; he estimates that by 1966 almost a hundred English versions had been printed, including adaptations for children.
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">
       Edgar Allan Poe
      </a>
      was profoundly influenced by Fouqu's tale, according to Pollin, which may have come about through Poe's broad reading of
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">
       Walter Scott
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">
       Samuel Taylor Coleridge
      </a>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollin_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pollin-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scott had derived the character of the White Lady of Avenel (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Monastery" title="The Monastery">
        The Monastery
       </a>
      </i>
      , 1820) from
      <i>
       Undine
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boatright_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Boatright-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a passage by Coleridge on
      <i>
       Undine
      </i>
      was reprinted in Tracy's 1839 edition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollin_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pollin-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ondine.27s_curse">
       Ondine's curse
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Undine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ondine's curse">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Congenital central hypoventilation, a rare medical condition in which sufferers lack
      <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">
       autonomic
      </a>
      control of their breathing and are hence at risk of suffocation while sleeping, is also known as
      <a href="/wiki/Ondine%27s_curse" title="Ondine's curse">
       Ondine's curse
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobinson201028_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobinson201028-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ondine, the eponymous heroine of Giradoux's play, tells her future husband Hans, whom she has just met, that "I shall be the shoes of your feet... I shall be the breath of your lungs".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeiss1964334_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeiss1964334-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ondine makes a pact with her uncle, the King of the Ondines, that if Hans ever deceives her he will die. After their honeymoon Hans is reunited with his first love, the Princess Bertha, and Ondine leaves him, only to be captured by a fisherman six months later. On meeting Ondine again on the day of his wedding to Bertha, Hans tells her that "all the things my body once did by itself, it does now only by special order... A single moment of inattention and I forget to breathe".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeiss1964364_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeiss1964364-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hans and Ondine kiss, and he dies.
     </p>
