        <header class="entry-header">
         <h1 class="entry-title ">
          Mark Lanegan  Imitations
          <span class="comments-link">
           <a href="#tc-comment-title" title="1 Comment(s) on Mark Lanegan  Imitations">
            <span class="tc-comment-bubble default-bubble">
             1
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          This entry was posted in
          <a class="btn btn-mini btn-tag" href="http://www.thisisnotascene.com/category/music/albums/" title="View all posts in Album Reviews">
           Album Reviews
          </a>
          <a class="btn btn-mini btn-tag" href="http://www.thisisnotascene.com/category/music/" title="View all posts in Music">

          </a>

          <a href="http://www.thisisnotascene.com/2013/09/06/" rel="bookmark" title="2:00 pm">
           <time class="entry-date updated" datetime="2013-09-06T14:00:29+00:00">
            September 6, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="by-author">
           by
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.thisisnotascene.com/author/ball/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Chris Ball">
             Chris Ball
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <hr class="featurette-divider __before_content"/>
        </header>
        <section class="entry-content ">
         <p>
          <a class="grouped_elements" href="http://www.thisisnotascene.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/laneganart.jpg" rel="tc-fancybox-group43395">
           <img alt="Mark Lanegan - Imitations" class="alignright size-full wp-image-43402" height="240" src="http://www.thisisnotascene.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/laneganart.jpg" width="240"/>
          </a>
          The self-deprecatingly titled Imitations is
          <strong>
           Mark Lanegan
          </strong>
          s tribute to the songwriters and singers who have inspired him over his quite considerable career. As one of the go-to guys in alt rock for songwriters who want a great voice to interpret their lyrics, its no surprise that
          <em>
           Lanegan
          </em>
          is open to and fascinated by the business of crafting a classic song. I have to admit to being unfamiliar with many of the originals covered here and this is obviously a very personal record, as demonstrated by his re-workings of two
          <strong>
           Andy Williams
          </strong>
          numbers, a family favourite when he was growing up.
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course
          <em>
           Lanegan
          </em>
          could sing the phone book and make it captivating but the gentle and restrained backing here often means the songs lack the crackling electricity of his usually dark skies. Imitations reminds me of
          <strong>
           The Czars
          </strong>
          covers album Sorry I Made You Cry, where you miss the personality or voice of the singer. On Sorry I Made You Cryyou miss the wit and spite of
          <em>
           John Grant
          </em>
          and Imitations lacks the hardbitten junkie soul of
          <em>
           Lanegan
          </em>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Its not all disappointing, though; opener Flatlands  originally by
          <strong>
           Chelsea Wolfe
          </strong>
           has a lovely circular twanging guitar riff and traces of the menace you expect from the dark prince of grunge. You Only Live Twice (originally by
          <strong>
           Nancy Sinatra
          </strong>
          ) both suprises and delights. A Bond tune Im not overly sick of, this is a scaled down acoustic version that removes the showbiz and stands up as a great little tune with a haunting melody.
         </p>
         <p>
          The folky acoustic vibe doesnt work so well on
          <strong>
           Bobby Darin
          </strong>
          s standard Mack The Knife, as it ambles along politely and I miss the staccato attack of the
          <strong>
           Frank Sinatra
          </strong>
          version.
          <em>
           Sinatra
          </em>
          treats the melody with something bordering on contempt and that is not something
          <em>
           Lanegan
          </em>
          seems prepared to do here, where everything is too reverent.
         </p>
         <p>
          Tracks like
          <strong>
           Neil Sedaka
          </strong>
          s Solitaire and
          <strong>
           John Cale
          </strong>
          s Im Not the Loving Kind are overly respectful and I cant help thinking that
          <strong>
           Tom Waits
          </strong>
          would have made a much better fist of interpreting and personalising these songs. This album proves that a terrific singer and some classic songs do necessarily make a classic album.
         </p>
