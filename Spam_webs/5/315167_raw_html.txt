          </li>
          <li class="author-desc ">
           <h2>
            <span>
             by
            </span>
            <a href="mailto:n.walsh@medpagetoday.com">
             Nancy  Walsh
            </a>
            <br/>
            <i>
             Senior Staff Writer, MedPage Today
            </i>
           </h2>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div class="body-content">
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad" id="tablet-ad-46883">
           </div>
           <div class="action-points">
            <div class="ap-heading">
             <h4>
              Action Points
             </h4>
            </div>
            <div class="ap-content">
             <ul>
              <li>
               Note that a large series of patients with cryopyrin-associated periodic syndrome has demonstrated that common variants in the responsible gene,
               <em>
                NLRP3
               </em>
               , tend to be associated with milder phenotypes.
              </li>
              <li>
               A small cross-sectional study has demonstrated an association between fibromyalgia and poorer cognitive performance on certain neuropsychological measures.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div class="drawer">
              <span class="icon-arrow" href="">
              </span>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           Highlights of the rheumatology literature this week included a consensus statement on unmet needs in psoriatic arthritis, cognitive impairment in fibromyalgia, and clinical phenotypes of the cryopyrin-associated periodic syndrome.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Unmet Needs in Psoriatic Arthritis
           </strong>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           An international group of experts in psoriatic arthritis has identified multiple unmet needs in the diagnosis and treatment of this condition, detailed in
           <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/acr.22404/abstract" target="_blank">
            a new consensus statement published online in
           </a>
           <em>
            <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/acr.22404/abstract" target="_blank">
             Arthritis Care &amp; Research
            </a>
            .
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           A major concern is that psoriatic arthritis is underdiagnosed, with one estimate suggesting that half of patients diagnosed as having psoriasis actually have psoriatic arthritis.
          </p>
          <p>
           The associated diagnostic delays result in disease progression, disability, and diminished quality of life, according to
           <a href="http://medhealth.leeds.ac.uk/profile/700/655/philip_helliwell" target="_blank">
            Philip Helliwell, MD, PhD
           </a>
           , of the University of Leeds in England, and colleagues.
          </p>
          <p>
           There also have been no well validated screening tools for psoriatic arthritis, and clinicians have had to rely on an alphabet soup of questionnaires that have varying sensitivities and specificities, including PEST (Psoriasis Epidemiology Screening Tool), ToPAS (Toronto Psoriatic Arthritis Screening), and PASE (Psoriatic Arthritis Screening and Evaluation).
          </p>
          <p>
           An additional barrier to screening and diagnosis is the lack of awareness and understanding about the condition among primary care physicians and dermatologists, who may not accurately differentiate this inflammatory arthritis from osteoarthritis. Criteria for rheumatology referral have been lacking.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The establishment of multidisciplinary clinics such as those at the Cleveland Clinic and Harvard could help address this, the members of the consensus group agreed.
          </p>
          <p>
           Important gaps in treatment also exist. While it's been firmly established in rheumatoid arthritis that early, tight control leads to better long-term outcomes, only a few studies have considered this in psoriatic arthritis.
          </p>
          <p>
           Treatment algorithms developed thus far have been inconsistent. In severe disease, for example, the American Academy of Dermatology recommends a combination of a conventional disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drug (DMARD) and a tumor necrosis factor (TNF) inhibitor or other biologic, while the European League Against Rheumatism recommends methotrexate, then a different DMARD or a TNF inhibitor, and then a combination of the two or a switch to a different biologic.
          </p>
          <p>
           In addition, definitions for low disease activity and remission have been vague or nonexistent; defining treatment success should include perspectives of clinicians, patients, and regulators, the consensus panel advised.
          </p>
          <p>
           Patient adherence also is inadequate, and increased education is needed so patients understand the benefits and risks and the importance of preventing long-term joint damage.
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           A further concern is that the burden of disease among patients with psoriatic arthritis has been underappreciated. Many report that they experience physical and emotional impairments  --  more so than in patients with psoriasis alone or rheumatoid arthritis.
          </p>
          <p>
           These patients also have high rates of comorbidities, including hypertension, hepatitis, irritable bowel syndrome, and neuropathy.
          </p>
          <p>
           And the economic burden is high, with direct costs of medications and hospitalizations and indirect costs associated with decreased productivity and job loss.
          </p>
          <p>
           Addressing these inadequacies through the development of better tools, definitions, and algorithms, as well as improved patient and physician education, should result in better disease control, function, and quality of life for patients, the consensus report concluded.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Cognition in Fibromyalgia
           </strong>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The "fibro-fog" described by many patients with fibromyalgia turns out to be a measurable constellation of cognitive impairments,
           <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/acr.22403/abstract" target="_blank">
            Italian researchers reported online in
            <em>
             Arthritis Care &amp; Research
            </em>
            .
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Compared with healthy controls, patients with fibromyalgia performed significantly worse on tests of memory, with scores of 9.9 compared with 11.7 (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.033) on the Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test, and had slower reaction times on the 1-Back test of working memory (891 ms versus 722 ms,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.0001)), according to Valentina Tesio, PhD, and colleagues from the University of Turin.
          </p>
          <p>
           They also had significantly lower scores on the Digit Span Backward test, which measures the "updating" component of executive function (3.8 versus 4.4,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.031).
          </p>
          <p>
           "Although the presence of cognitive impairment has recently been added to the diagnostic criteria of the American College of Rheumatology, cognitive dysfunction remains one of the least assessed and treated fibromyalgia domains in general clinical practice, also because of the expertise and time required for neuropsychological tests," Tesio and colleagues wrote.
          </p>
          <p>
           One possible way to overcome this barrier could be through patient self-reports, but that approach might not be sufficiently sensitive to differentiate cognitive problems from depression, which also is very common in fibromyalgia.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Therefore, the researchers had two goals: to evaluate specific cognitive impairments on standard neuropsychological tests, and to see whether the results correlated with patient self-perception of various aspects of cognition.
          </p>
          <p>
           They enrolled 30 women with fibromyalgia diagnosed according to the American College of Rheumatology criteria and 30 matched healthy controls.
          </p>
          <p>
           Patient self-perception of cognitive impairment was assessed using the FACT-Cog 2, a test for evaluating cognitive impairment after cancer chemotherapy.
          </p>
          <p>
           Significant correlations were seen between the verbal fluency component of FACT-Cog 2 and three of the neuropsychological tests: the Digit Span Backward test (
           <em>
            r
           </em>
           = 0.396,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.03), the Trail Making Test B of attention shifting between numbers and letters (
           <em>
            r
           </em>
           =-0.361,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.049), and the difference between the Trail Making Test A of numbers only and the Trail Making Test B (
           <em>
            r
           </em>
           =-0.3.96,
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.03).
          </p>
          <p>
           "Our results indicate a good correlation between the verbal fluency subscale of the FACT-Cog and executive functions and working memory tests, thus suggesting that FACT-Cog can be used as an effective screening tool for objective cognitive deficits," the researchers wrote.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           "The use of a self-report questionnaire in clinical practice would provide a first and easy screen for the presence of cognitive impairment in fibromyalgia patients and, in most cases, avoid the need for a time-consuming full neuropsychological test battery," they concluded.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Clinical Variants in CAPS
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The largest published series of patients with the inherited group of autoinflammatory conditions known as cryopyrin-associated periodic syndrome (CAPS), caused by mutations in the
           <em>
            NLRP3
           </em>
           gene, suggested that the severest phenotypes were associated with rare mutations and symptom onset before 6 months.
          </p>
          <p>
           Compared with patients who had the seven most common mutations in that gene, the 20% of patients with rare variants had significantly more severe neurologic manifestations (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.008) and musculoskeletal problems (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.01), and more often had early-onset disease (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.003), according to
           <a href="http://www.orpha.net/consor/cgi-bin/Directory_Professionals.php?lng=EN&amp;data_id=20318&amp;MISSING%20CONTENT=Dr-Benedicte-NEVEN&amp;title=Dr-Benedicte-NEVEN&amp;search=Directory_Professionals_Simple" target="_blank">
            Benedicte Neven, MD
           </a>
           , of Hopital Necker-Enfants Malades in Paris, and colleagues.
          </p>
          <p>
           And in turn, early onset  --  before the age of 6 months  --  predicted subsequent hearing loss (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           =0.01) and severe neurologic manifestations such as seizures and hydrocephalus (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.001), t
           <a href="http://ard.bmj.com/content/early/2014/07/18/annrheumdis-2013-204991.abstract" target="_blank">
            he researchers reported online in
            <em>
             Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases.
            </em>
           </a>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The CAPS conditions consist of mild familial cold autoinflammatory syndrome, Muckle-Wells syndrome, and severe neonatal-onset multisystem inflammatory disease. Mutations in the
           <em>
            NLRP3
           </em>
           gene, which encodes cryopyrin, result in inflammasome activation, excessive production of the proinflammatory cytokine interleukin-1-beta, and a wide range of chronic inflammatory manifestations including fever, rashes, neurologic impairments, and musculoskeletal complications.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Since the discovery of the gene responsible for CAPS and the understanding of the syndrome's common molecular basis, some degree of genotype/phenotype correlation has been reported in several studies (despite clear phenotypic variability)," wrote Neven and colleagues.
          </p>
          <p>
           To further examine these correlations, the researchers conducted an analysis of 136 patients in the Eurofever Registry, which has been enrolling patients since late 2009.
          </p>
          <p>
           They identified 31 different germline missense mutations at exon 3 of
           <em>
            NLRP3
           </em>
           , including two that had not previously been found. Seven of the variants were responsible for almost 80% of cases.
          </p>
          <p>
           The most common mutation was R260W, which was associated with symptoms triggered with exposure to cold, onset after age 6 months, and a family history of CAPS. This mutation was present in 26%.
          </p>
          <p>
           Also common was T348M, which was associated with early onset, chronic symptoms, and hearing impairments.
          </p>
          <p>
           In the category of rare mutations, found in no more than two individuals, a sporadic pattern of disease was significantly more common than a chronic course (
           <em>
            P
           </em>
           &lt;0.001). Carriers of the rare variants also had more neurosensory hearing loss and severe ophthalmologic conditions such as glaucoma and atrophy of the optic nerve.
          </p>
          <p>
           More than half of the patients had chronic symptoms, often almost every day. Rashes were present in 97%, musculoskeletal problems such as arthralgias, joint contractures, and bone erosions were reported in 86%, and fever in 84%.
          </p>
          <p>
           Neurologic symptoms were seen in 40%, ocular involvement in 71%, and neurosensory hearing loss in 42%.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Our results give new insights into CAPS  --  insights that might be of value in routine clinical practice," they noted.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Even though prediction of the disease course remains challenging, we suggest that early onset, sporadic cases are at risk of severe neurological complications and neurosensory hearing loss, especially when patients are carrying rare germline
           <em>
            NLRP3
           </em>
           variants," they observed.
          </p>
          <p>
           The study was limited by its retrospective nature.
          </p>
