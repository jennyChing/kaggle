        <p>
         <strong>
          Matthew Lombardi was certain
         </strong>
         he'd be playing hockey in Switzerland this season. His family even left their dog there when they came back home to Canada for the summer. At the age of 31, he'd scored 50 points (goals plus assists) in only 46 games during his first season with the Swiss league's Genve-Servette HC. His wife and two daughters were happy in the French-speaking, lakeside city of Geneva. Lombardi signed a contract to return to the club. There was no reason to bring the dog overseas. They were only going to be in Canada for six weeks before he had to be back for training camp at the beginning of August. "My mindset was that I was going back," he says. "We were happy. My family was happy. That was it."
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-breaker-container">
         <div class="ad-gpt-breaker ad-article-breaker-mobile">
          <div class="ad-article-breaker-text">

        <p>
         Now, three months later, Lombardi and his family are living in Rye, New York, a waterfront town in Westchester County, less than an hour's drive from Manhattan and Lombardi's new place of business, Madison Square Garden. In less than a week, he'll take the ice as a member of the New York Rangers, the NHL's defending Eastern Conference champion who surprised him with a contract offer in July, just before the deadline to opt out of his deal with Genve-Servette passed. The last three months have been a whirlwind for Lombardi and his family, but no one knows better than he does how precious an opportunity to play in the NHL is, how fragile it is, and how quickly it can be taken away. A life of contentment in Switzerland can wait; an opportunity to hoist the Stanley Cup cannot. Lombardi weighed the pros and cons with his family, but in the end the decision was clear. "I wanted to jump on it," he says of the opportunity.
        </p>

        <p>
         <strong>
          Matthew Lombardi is fast.
         </strong>
         The 5'11" center's game is all about speed, all about darting around the ice, leading breaks, killing penalties, swooping in out of nowhere to set up or put away a goal. He's not a big hitter; his game is one of finesse.
        </p>
        <p>
         After the Montreal native was drafted 90th overall by the Calgary Flames in 2002, he quickly became an impact player on the team's run to the 2004 Stanley Cup Finals. But in Game 4 of the season's ultimate series, he suffered a brutal concussion after taking an elbow to the head. The next season's NHL lockout gave him additional time to recover and he came back stronger than ever, tallying 26 points in 55 games. He'd then go on to enjoy the prime of his career from 2006-2010, averaging 45 points over the course of four seasons, the last with the Phoenix Coyotes. Before the 2010-11 season, he signed with the Nashville Predators, but this new phase of his career was over before it could start: he suffered another brutal concussion in the preseason, and after trying to return for two games, ended up missing the entire remainder of the season. For the next two years he struggled to regain his confidence, hampered by additional injuries. He wasn't the same player. Just as his recovery from a devastating blow to the head jumpstarted the best four years of his career, so had one ended them. It looked like his career as a productive NHL player could be over.
        </p>
        <div class="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         <div class="gpt-tag" id="ad-gpt-sharethrough">
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The looming specter
         </strong>
         of a career-ending head injury is a reality of life in the NHL. The scariest thing about them is just how little we know about their nature. A concussion may seem minor compared to a torn ACL, a broken leg, or other injuries with which we are more familiar and for which a definite rehabilitation regimen can be prescribed. With concussions, there's no way to tell how long it will take to recover.
        </p>
        <div style="border-left-style:double;border-left-width:thick;float:right;margin-left:10px;margin-bottom:5px;">
         <h1 style="font-size:24px;">
          <em>
           "You just try to wake up in the morning and hope to feel better, hope that it will be gone and you can get back to what you do. Being in that unknown, you question if it's ever going to go away so that you're healthy enough to live."
          </em>
         </h1>
        </div>
        <p>
         "The concussion is tough in the sense that you have no idea," says Lombardi. "After a month, two months passes and you don't feel any better than from day one. If you do, it's not much better. You just don't see the end. In my experience it was such a slow process. Getting over it, you don't even really notice it. You just try to wake up in the morning and hope to feel better, hope that it will be gone and you can get back to what you do. Being in that unknown, you question if it's ever going to go away so that you're healthy enough to live."
        </p>
        <p>
         Just as uncertain as the short-term recovery process are a concussion's long-term effects. Over 4,500 former football players have already filed lawsuits against the NFL, and in July a judge
         <a href="http://espn.go.com/nfl/story/_/id/11188140/us-district-judge-anita-brody-gives-preliminary-ok-nfl-concussion-settlement" target="_blank">
          granted approval to a deal
         </a>
         that would compensate thousands of them. In 2013, 10 former hockey players
         <a href="http://grantland.com/features/the-nhl-concussion-lawsuit/" target="_blank">
          became the first to pursue litigation against the NHL
         </a>
         for the head trauma they suffered while in the league, claiming that league officials should have been more conscious of lasting trauma, and in April
         <a href="http://espn.go.com/nhl/story/_/id/10763942/ex-nhl-players-including-brad-maxwell-jack-carlson-sue-league-concussions" target="_blank">
          an additional group of former players joined them
         </a>
         . Less than two weeks ago, in September, two minor league hockey players
         <a href="http://www.si.com/nhl/2014/09/19/minor-leaguers-file-concussion-lawsuit-against-nhl" target="_blank">
          filed a suit
         </a>
         against the league despite never actually playing in the NHL.
        </p>
        <p>
         It's only in recent years, though, that the long-term effects of in-game head injuries have been considered seriously. "My first one was back in 2004," Lombardi says. "Back then, at that time it wasn't the same. You see the way it's looked at now and how much more educated everyone is. The teams and doctors, how they treat it. Everyone knows how serious it is now. We just don't know that much about the long-term effects."
        </p>
        <p>
         But Lombardi is a hockey player and short- and long-term health risks of playing such a violent sport are not lost on him. For all of the hockey's fluid grace, even the most routine hits are vicious, and barring a dramatic rule change revolutionary new helmet design, the possibility of a traumatic head injury are real. "When you play a contact sports it's something that can happen," says Lombardi. "That's part of it. My last one wasn't a dirty hit or anything. It was just bad luck. I got tripped up and slid head-first into the boards."
        </p>

        <p>
         <strong>
          After playing only 28 games
         </strong>
         in the 2012-13 season for the Coyotes and the Anaheim Ducks, then undergoing shoulder surgery in the offseason, Lombardi decided he could no longer cut in in the NHL. He signed with Genve-Servette because he felt his French-Canadian wife and French-speaking children would feel at home in the French region of Switzerland. He arrived after the team's training camp had ended and slowly began the process of working his way back into playing shape, both physically and mentally.
        </p>
        <p>
         Although jetting overseas is common for players who can no longer keep up with the fast pace and high level of competition in the NHL  Lombardi notes how many familiar faces he ended up playing against in his year in Switzerland  actually returning to the NHL after a foreign stint is rare, and Lombardi practically laughed when I asked him how often it happens. Players go overseas because they can no longer cut it athletically, and once lost, athleticism is not something that can be regained. Once the skills start to go, it's a slippery slope.
        </p>
        <p>
         But Lombardi had not lost his athleticism so much as he had lost his confidence following the concussion that caused him to miss an entire season and the subsequent injuries he would suffer. All he needed was somewhere to get his mind right; he needed to mentally  convalesce just as much as he needed to recover from any physical ailments. "I just wanted to go over there and play well and enjoy myself and get that good feeling again," he says. "When I first went there, [returning to the NHL] was a thought, but as the season went on I was more focused on playing there and then ended up signing an extension to stay."
        </p>
        <p>
         Which is when, early in July, his agent told him the Rangers had expressed interest, giving Lombardi a second chance at hoisting the Cup  an opportunity he missed out on in 2004, when he was concussed in Game 4. After all he's been through, donning an NHL jersey has never meant so much. "I get to play hockey for a living, and now I'm back [in the NHL]," he says. "It's another adventure. We've seen a lot of different things over the years. It doesn't last forever. It's a short career. I'm definitely enjoying it while it lasts."
        </p>
