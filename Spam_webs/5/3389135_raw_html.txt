      <h2>
       Alexandra Watkins: Creating Awesome, Trustworthy Brand Names That Stick
      </h2>
      <p class="small">
       Posted by
       <a href="http://www.ims.gs/author/kimberly/" rel="author" title="Posts by Kimberly B.">
        Kimberly B.
       </a>
       on
          Tuesday, August 12th, 2014
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Whats in a name?
      </p>
      <p>
       Branding expert
       <a href="http://eatmywords.com/about/boastful-bios/alexandra/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://eatmywords.com/about/boastful-bios/alexandra/', 'Alexandra Watkins');" target="_blank" title="Eat My Words">
        Alexandra Watkins
       </a>
       knows a thing or two about the importance of choosing the right name for your company. As the brains behind
       <a href="http://eatmywords.com/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://eatmywords.com/', 'Eat My Words');" target="_blank" title="Eat My Words">
        Eat My Words
       </a>
       , a successful company that specializes in creating brand names and taglines, Watkins is a recognized thought-leader in brand identity. How has she cultivated such a high level of respect in this highly competitive field? By thinking  and acting 
       <a href="http://www.ims.gs/blog/taking-risks-failing-better/" target="_blank" title="Taking risks and failing better">
        outside the box
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <p>
       [Thought leaders] have original thoughts; theyre not afraid to express them even if theyre contrary to popular opinion. They dont just blindly follow trends.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Why brand names matter
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       Watkins says that thought-leaders also recognize that their way is not the only way, and are good at sharing other peoples ideas  not just their own. In her vibrant book,
       <em>
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Hello-My-Name-Awesome-Create/dp/1626561869/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1396549231&amp;sr=1-1&amp;keywords=hello+my+name+is+awesome" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.amazon.com/Hello-My-Name-Awesome-Create/dp/1626561869/ref=sr_1_1?s=books&amp;ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1396549231&amp;sr=1-1&amp;keywords=hello+my+name+is+awesome', 'Hello My Name is Awesome');" target="_blank" title="Hello, My Name is Awesome">
         Hello My Name is Awesome
        </a>
        : How to Create Brand Names That Stick,
       </em>
       she explains that choosing a good name is paramount to long-term success:
      </p>
      <p>
       If the product name is really good, people will buy the product based on that name. Or, theyll at least have an affinity for your brand before they even buy it or even know what it is.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div style="text-align: center;">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" height="281" src="//player.vimeo.com/video/45460207" width="500">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
       <span id="more-992">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, Watkins branding company came up with the name Retriever for a dog GPS. It makes you smile, right? Equally important is choosing a name that will scale with your company  not one that youll outgrow within a few years, or even decades. Creating a strong brand identity is not a science; its a deeply creative process. Which begs the question, what does Watkins believe makes a good brand name?
      </p>
      <p>
       My philosophy is a name should make you smile instead of scratch your head. A good name is one that you like as soon as you hear it, before you have any idea what its referring to. People like names that they get because it makes them feel smart. You want to be approachable and friendly. They dont want to feel stupid.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div style="text-align: center;">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/C0cyTtdMe-k" width="560">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        The smile and scratch test
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       To help others, Watkins created a test for brainstorming brand names. If you want to know whether your name is making people smile or scratch their head, take
       <a href="http://eatmywords.com/tips/is-your-name-lame/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://eatmywords.com/tips/is-your-name-lame/', 'Watkins test');" target="_blank">
        Watkins test
       </a>
       .This involves running through her acronyms for smile and scratch to see if your name meets the tried-and-tested criteria:
      </p>
      <p>
       Does your name suggest a positive brand experience? Do your customers understand it? Does it have legs? Is your name visually evocative? Does it resonate with your customers on an emotional level?
      </p>
      <p>
       Watkins emphasizes the significance of visual imagery:
      </p>
      <p>
       People remember imagery much more easily than they remember words or random letters, so if people can picture your name in their head when they hear it, it stands a better chance of being a memorable name.
      </p>
      <p>
       On the other hand, here are some red flags to watch out for.Is your name difficult to spell? Is it similar to your competition? Does it have an annoying, hidden meaning? Is it lazy? Is it hard to pronounce, or challenging to understand? If your name contains any of these negative aspects, it might be time to consider re-branding.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div style="text-align: center;">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/sd-Jy4t8vHc" width="560">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Watkins offers that while theres no prescription for creating the perfect brand name, she frequently looks to the Internet for help, often searching on iTunes for song titles to fuel inspiration. And forget brainstorming sessions in sterile offices  Watkins warns theyre a surefire way to kill any creativity in the room. Instead, she encourages people to hold brainstorm meetings in colorful, exciting environments conducive to generating creative ideas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Does your companys name hold up in front of Watkins scratch and smile test?
      </p>
