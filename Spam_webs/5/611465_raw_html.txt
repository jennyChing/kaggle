       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The Power of the Press: Artists Celebrate Ebony and Jet at The Studio Museum in Harlem
       </h1>
       <div class="sharethispin">
        <span class="st_facebook_large" displaytext="Facebook">
        </span>
        <span class="st_twitter_large" displaytext="Tweet">
        </span>
        <span class="st_pinterest_large" displaytext="Pinterest">
        </span>
        <span class="st_sharethis_large" displaytext="ShareThis">
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-meta">
        By
        <span class="author-meta-name">
         <a href="http://www.artnews.com/author/alexandermahany/" rel="author" title="Posts by Alexander Mahany">
          Alexander Mahany
         </a>
        </span>
        Posted
        <span class="date-meta">
         <b>
          11/11/14 9:30 am
         </b>
        </span>
       </div>
       <!-- .entry-meta -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="share-this">
         <span class="st_facebook_hcount" displaytext="Facebook">
         </span>
         <span class="st_twitter_hcount" displaytext="Tweet" st_via="ARTnewsmag">
         </span>
         <span class="st_pinterest_hcount" displaytext="Pinterest">
         </span>
         <span class="st_sharethis_hcount" displaytext="ShareThis">
         </span>
        </div>
        <div class="main-content">
         <p>
          In November, 1942, John H. Johnson founded the
          <a href="http://www.johnsonpublishing.com/" target="_blank">
           Johnson Publishing Company
          </a>
          and began publishing
          <em>
           Negro Digest
          </em>
          . Modeled after
          <em>
           Readers Digest
          </em>
          , the weekly publication was the first to focus solely on African-American history, literature, arts, and culture. People thought he was a fool, Linda Johnson Rice, chairman of the Johnson Publishing Company and John H. Johnsons daughter, said. They didnt realize there was this completely untapped market.
         </p>
         Six months later, circulation for
         <em>
          Negro Digest
         </em>
         had risen to 50,000. Three years later, in November of 1945, the first issue of
         <a href="http://www.ebony.com/" target="_blank">
          <em>
           Ebony
          </em>
         </a>
         was publishedand sold out its initial run of 25,000 copies in less than a month.
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40847" style="width: 610px">
          <img alt="Hank Willis Thomas, Jet People, 2010, gouache acrylic on canvas. HANK WILLIS THOMAS/COURTESY THE ARTIST AND JACK SHAINMAN GALLERY, NEW YORK" class="size-full wp-image-40847" height="402" src="http://1vze7o2h8a2b2tyahl3i0t68.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Speaking-of-People_HW-Thomas_Jet-People.jpg" width="600"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Hank Willis Thomas,
           <em>
            Jet People
           </em>
           , 2010, gouache acrylic on canvas.
          </p>
          <small>
           HANK WILLIS THOMAS/COURTESY THE ARTIST AND JACK SHAINMAN GALLERY, NEW YORK
          </small>
         </div>
         <p>
          They were the first magazines of their kind, Rice explained. The magazines were born out of a need that my parents saw, that there were no magazines that really spoke to black people.
          <em>
           Ebony
          </em>
          wrote about architects and artists, the share cropper who sent his nine kids to college, real African Americans at a time when everyone else only covered them as entertainers and athletes.
         </p>
         Over 70 years after Johnson began his publishing company, the Studio Museum in Harlem is debuting a new group show of contemporary art inspired by the pages of
         <em>
          Ebony
         </em>
         and its sister magazine
         <a href="http://www.jetmag.com/" target="_blank">
          <em>
           Jet
          </em>
         </a>
         . Opening November 13,
         <a href="http://www.studiomuseum.org/exhibition/speaking-people-emebonyem-emjetem-and-contemporary-art" target="_blank">
          Speaking of People: Ebony, Jet and Contemporary Art
         </a>
         celebrates the legacy of Johnsons two most popular magazines and their continued cultural relevance.
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_40852" style="width: 310px">
          <img alt="Lorna Simpson, Riunite &amp; Ice Collage #2, 2014, collage and ink on paper. COURTESY THE ARTIST AND SALON 94, NEW YORK" class="size-full wp-image-40852" height="365" src="http://1vze7o2h8a2b2tyahl3i0t68.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Speaking-of-People_Simpson_Riunite-Ice-300.jpg" width="300"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Lorna Simpson,
           <em>
            Riunite &amp; Ice Collage #2
           </em>
           , 2014, collage and ink on paper.
          </p>
          <small>
           COURTESY THE ARTIST AND SALON 94, NEW YORK
          </small>
         </div>
         <p>
          Its incredibly exciting to see how so many artists have been so widely influenced by
          <em>
           Ebony
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Jet
          </em>
          , said Lauren Hayes, curator of the exhibition. The idea is that there is no one black experience in America, but all of these artists have been directly affected by both magazines.
         </p>
         Including such established art world figures as
         <a href="http://www.lsimpsonstudio.com/" target="_blank">
          Lorna Simpson
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://www.hankwillisthomas.com/" target="_blank">
          Hank Willis Thomas
         </a>
         (who also contributes an artist essay in the exhibitions catalogue),
         <a href="http://www.jackshainman.com/artists/kerryjames-marshall/" target="_blank">
          Kerry James Marshall
         </a>
         , and
         <a href="http://www.luhringaugustine.com/artists/glenn-ligon/" target="_blank">
          Glenn Ligon
         </a>
         , the exhibition primarily features work within the past decade, and reflects a contemporary point of view rather than a historical one. This is truly a contemporary show, said Haynes. Even though the artists are looking back at that post-WWII moment, when the magazines were starting, there is a very real, living feeling as well, connected to making art today with materials that arent of this physical moment.With over thirty works by a multi-generational, interdisciplinary group of sixteen artists, Speaking of People includes everything from photography and painting to sculpture and sound works. Haynes explained that, Theres no one way to look at these magazines. Theres a variety of ways to think about them.
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_40848" style="width: 610px">
          <img alt="Mickalene Thomas, Portrait of Lili in Color, 2008, color photograph in custom-built wood frame.  COURTESY THE ARTIST" class="size-full wp-image-40848" height="489" src="http://1vze7o2h8a2b2tyahl3i0t68.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Speaking-of-People_M-Thomas_Portrait-of-Lili.jpg" width="600"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Mickalene Thomas,
           <em>
            Portrait of Lili in Color
           </em>
           , 2008, color photograph in custom-built wood frame.
          </p>
          <small>
           COURTESY THE ARTIST
          </small>
         </div>
         <p>
          Contemporary artists like
          <a href="http://www.gagosian.com/artists/ellen-gallagher" target="_blank">
           Ellen Gallagher
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://www.jacktiltongallery.com/artists/anderson/" target="_blank">
           Noel Anderson
          </a>
          , and
          <a href="http://www.afronova.com/Godfried-Donkor.html" target="_blank">
           Godfried Donkor
          </a>
          plumb imagery from the magazine as a visual archive, the documentation of a shared cultural history, mixing photos and editorial spreads with more traditional artist materials like ink, oil, and watercolor.
         </p>
         As you look through the pages of
         <em>
          Ebony
         </em>
         and
         <em>
          Jet
         </em>
         , Rice said, you can see there are instances when it is riotous and aggressive or beautiful and celebratory. It is very much a timeline of our history, from 1942 to today. We consider ourselves the curators of the African American experience, past, present, and future.
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_40851" style="width: 310px">
          <img alt="Godfried Donkor, Ebony Jo'burg edition, 2014, collage on paper. COURTESY THE ARTIST" class="size-full wp-image-40851" height="424" src="http://1vze7o2h8a2b2tyahl3i0t68.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Speaking-of-People_Donkor_Ebony-Joburg-300.jpg" width="300"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Godfried Donkor,
           <em>
            Ebony Joburg edition
           </em>
           , 2014, collage on paper.
          </p>
          <small>
           COURTESY THE ARTIST
          </small>
         </div>
         <p>
          Other artists more glancingly reference the magazines, inspired by their symbolic value as iconic publications with an institutional history. Leslie Hewitts 2012
          <em>
           Untitled (Where Paths Meet, Turn Away, Then Align Again)
          </em>
          is a sheet metal sculpture with a powder coat so dark it renders the work a void, impenetrable and black. Rather than directly referring to
          <em>
           Ebony
          </em>
          or
          <em>
           Jet
          </em>
          , Haynes explained that the work plays off the conceptual idea of creating a physical space that is wholly black  like how
          <em>
           Ebony
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           Jet
          </em>
          created a space for discussion in the black community.
         </p>
         In June of this year
         <em>
          Jet
         </em>
         ceased its print publication. Playing against the backdrop of more and more digital landscape, theres a certain vindication in seeing so many artist use physical copies of the magazine in their work.It will be interesting to talk about how in the age of the digital, these magazines contributed physical images that resonated and inspired a generation of artists working today, Haynes said. Do we want to lose that? I dont think so.
        </div>
       </div>
