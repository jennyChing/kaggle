       <h3>
        Scorsese isa director that knows the importance of music in film
       </h3>
       <p>
        Martin Scorseses films are that of legend;
        <em>
         Goodfellas, The Departed,
        </em>
        and
        <em>
         Wolf of Wall Street
        </em>
        just to name a few. His films have it all, great acting, compelling stories,
        <a href="http://nofilmschool.com/2014/08/influential-editing-techniques-martin-scorsese-thelma-schoonmaker" target="_blank">
         eye-catchingcinematography
        </a>
        . But one overlooked aspect of Scorseses greatness as a filmmaker lies in his ability to score a film.
       </p>
       <p>
        In an
        <a href="http://www.industrycentral.net/director_interviews/MS01.HTM" target="_blank">
         interview with the BBC
        </a>
        , Scorsese goes on to say how the right song can really make an impact on a viewers movie-going experience.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Ive just always loved music. I cant read music, I dont play it, but its just a wonderful thing to me. It can carry an entire film, you know? It was a psychological level of importance, in terms of assisting the image in order to re-enforce a point across to the viewer. It can also help a film to cut better. Music is important on so many levels.
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        I can remember a number ofdistinct times where Scorsese used incredible songs to start his movies. His use of these songs set the tone for the entire movie.In the opening scene of
        <em>
         The Departed
        </em>
        the song Gimmie Shelter by The Rolling Stones gradually began to play over Frank Costellos (Jack Nicholson) monologue about not wanting to be a product of {his} own environment. Thesong perfectly accompanies the progression of the Costellos rise to power.
       </p>
       <h4>
        <i>
         The Departed
        </i>
        , Gimme Shelter by The Rolling Stones
       </h4>
       <p>
        <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/kIsyHKsjftY" width="560">
        </iframe>
       </p>
       <p>
        The Rolling Stones are by far and large one of Scorseses favorite bands to use in his films but his musical selections extend pretty far. Blues, Soul, Jazz, and Rock are his bread and butter. Artists like Artetha Franklin, Otis Redding, and BB King have been featured in
        <em>
         Casino and Goodfellas.
        </em>
        Where Scorseses music selections skills show range was in the 2014 Oscar-Nominated
        <em>
         Wolf of Wall Street.
        </em>
        I dont know if it was Scorsese decision to include Black Skinhead in the films trailer but it was a genius move. That whole film was filled with gems that paired with the
        <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7tX40xe2cnw" target="_blank">
         cocaine-filled debauchery
        </a>
        nicely.
       </p>
       <h4>
        <em>
         Wolf of Wall Street
        </em>
        [Trailer], Black Skinhead by Kanye West
       </h4>
       <p>
        <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/UmhmHEQigXs" width="560">
        </iframe>
       </p>
       <p>
        While he might not be able to read music (Fun Fact:
        <a href="http://www.herfitzpr.com/hpr.com/Rick_Rubin_Bio.html" target="_blank">
         Legedary Producer Rick Rubin cant read music either
        </a>
        ) Scorsese has a knack for picking incredible soundtracks. Picking a song that will define a moment in time is a skill that some directors dont have. Scorsese understands and knows the right level of detail can take a scene from good to classic.
       </p>
       <p>
        This playlist is composed from the following Scorsese films:
        <em>
         Raging Bull, Taxi Driver,The Color of Money, Casino, The Aviator, Gangs of New York, The Departed,
        </em>
        and
        <em>
         The Wolf of Wall Street.
        </em>
        Enjoy and put your favorite Scorsese moment in the comments.
       </p>
