                   <div class="grid-block">
                    <div class="grid-box width100" style="margin-top: 15px;">
                     <h1 class="title" style="padding-top: 0px; margin: 0px; margin-bottom: 5px">
                      Leveraging Healthcare Analytics to Reduce Heart Failure Readmission Rates
                     </h1>
                     <p>
                      <i>
                       Written by
                       <a data-uk-modal="" href="#author-36">
                        Kathleen Merkley
                       </a>
                       . Posted in
                       <a href="/knowledge-center/search-results?type=category&amp;target=improving-clinical-quality">
                        Quality and Process Improvement
                       </a>
                      </i>
                     </p>
                    </div>
                    <div class="grid-box width100">
                     <div class="grid-block">
                      <div class="grid-box width100">
                       <div>
                        <div class="add-to-folder" data-permalink="https://www.healthcatalyst.com/reduce-heart-failure-readmissions-with-healthcare-analytics/" data-title="Leveraging Healthcare Analytics to Reduce Heart Failure Readmission Rates" id="article_1928">
                         <div class="add-to-folder-img">
                         </div>
                         <div class="add-to-folder-text" data-add-text="Add To My Folder" data-added-text="Added">
                          Add To My Folder
                         </div>
                        </div>
                       </div>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </header>
                  <div class="content clearfix">
                   <p>
                    One Friday night I was working as a charge nurse in a large, inner-city emergency department when an ambulance arrived with a patient suffering from heart failure (HF). Events like this are typical, but the patient was not  she was my favorite aunt who had suffered an acute myocardial infarction (AMI) two weeks earlier. On that particular day, she started to develop shortness of breath, and by the time my uncle called 911, she was in serious respiratory distress from pulmonary edema, a condition caused by heart failure.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    I have cared for hundreds of patients with heart failure over my career, but this was different  this was someone I knew and loved. The experience made me contemplate: what could been done to prevent heart patients from going through this kind of suffering?
                   </p>
                   <h2>
                    <b>
                     Heart Failure Readmissions: One of the Most Common Sources of Readmissions
                    </b>
                   </h2>
                   <p>
                    Heart failure, including what was formerly referred to as congestive heart failure,* is an extremely serious problem in the United States for many reasons. First, there are a high number of patients who are suffering and dying from this disease. Second, the financial burden to treat heart disease patients is becoming an alarming public health issue. The following facts highlight the costs associated with these patient populations:
                   </p>
                   <ul>
                    <li>
                     Heart failure accounts for $38 billion of healthcare spending annually.
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     According to a
                     <a href="http://cms.hhs.gov/Research-Statistics-Data-and-Systems/Research/HealthCareFinancingReview/Downloads/08Springpg1.pdf" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'download', 'http://cms.hhs.gov/Research-Statistics-Data-and-Systems/Research/HealthCareFinancingReview/Downloads/08Springpg1.pdf');">
                      2008 Health Care Financing Review
                     </a>
                     , heart failure accounts for 43 percent of Medicare spending even though this patient population only makes up 14 percent of Medicare beneficiaries.
                    </li>
                   </ul>
                   <p>
                    The situation becomes even more costly because many of these patients will be readmitted to the hospital soon after being seen for their heart condition. These facts show the startling
                    <a href="https://www.healthcatalyst.com/how-survive-CMS-hospital-readmissions-penalties-increase" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article-int', 'https://www.healthcatalyst.com/how-survive-CMS-hospital-readmissions-penalties-increase', 'readmission');" title="How to Survive CMSs Most Recent 3% Hospital Readmissions Penalties Increase">
                     readmission
                    </a>
                    trends for heart failure patients:
                   </p>
                   <ul>
                    <li>
                     More than 25 percent of patients hospitalized for heart failure will be readmitted to the hospital within 30 days of discharge.
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     According to
                     <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22653415" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22653415', '2012 research');">
                      2012 research
                     </a>
                     , the top reason for readmission with the Medicare fee-for-service patient population is for patients suffering from heart failure.
                    </li>
                   </ul>
                   <p>
                    Because excessive
                    <a href="/success_stories/reduce-30-day-heart-failure-readmissions/">
                     readmissions
                    </a>
                    tend to indicate suboptimal care, government and commercial payers are focusing on 30-day readmission rates as a new quality measure for hospitals. The intent behind the measures is for the hospitals to provide better care by following evidence-based practice guidelines, which in turn, will reduce heart failure readmission rates. Some proposed methods include:
                   </p>
                   <ul>
                    <li>
                     Patient education at discharge
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Appropriate medications prescribed
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Medication reconciliation
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Timely access to care after discharge
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Hand-off communications between primary care providers and acute care facilities
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Rapid distribution of hospital documentation to primary care providers
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Home health interventions
                    </li>
                    <li>
                     Follow-up phone calls
                    </li>
                   </ul>
                   <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_11335" style="width: 640px">
                    <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/heart-failure-readmissions.png">
                     <img alt="heart failure readmissions" class=" wp-image-11335" height="498" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/heart-failure-readmissions.png" width="630"/>
                    </a>
                    <p class="wp-caption-text">
                     <em>
                      A heart failure readmission dashboard helps clinicians track the care they provide.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                   </div>
                   <h3>
                    <strong>
                     4 Ways to Leverage Healthcare Analytics to Reduce Heart Failure Readmission Rates
                    </strong>
                   </h3>
                   <p>
                    Now that hospitals have to comply with this new quality measure or face financial penalties, how should they address heart failure readmission rates? There is a solution:
                    <a href="/advanced-analytics-survive-healthcare-reform">
                     advanced analytics
                    </a>
                    .
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    Sophisticated analytics are able to comb through terabytes of clinical data to reveal opportunities to
                    <a href="/5-Deming-Principles-For-Healthcare-Process-Improvement">
                     improve quality
                    </a>
                    and efficiency. Whats more: analytics provide a way for hospitals to leverage their data to analyze and better manage specific
                    <a href="/population-health/">
                     patient populations
                    </a>
                    . Here are four ways hospitals can use their data to improve heart failure readmission goals:
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <span style="font-size: large;">
                     <strong>
                      1.
                     </strong>
                    </span>
                    <strong>
                     Understand your current readmission rates for your heart patients.
                    </strong>
                    Why? Because you cant improve what you dont measure. It is important to establish readmission baselines, track performance metrics, and distribute information to everyone who is trying to
                    <a href="/healthcare-data-warehouse-hospital-readmissions-reduction">
                     reduce readmissions
                    </a>
                    . This is the first step towards quality improvement. Health systems operating at level 5 or above on the
                    <a href="/healthcare-analytics-adoption-model/">
                     Healthcare Analytics Adoption Model
                    </a>
                    will be able to achieve this goal.
                   </p>
                   <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7828" style="width: 727px">
                    <a href="/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/Analytics-Adoption-Model-Comparison.png">
                     <img alt="Analytics Adoption Model Comparison" class=" wp-image-7828 " height="448" src="/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/Analytics-Adoption-Model-Comparison-1024x640.png" width="717"/>
                    </a>
                    <p class="wp-caption-text">
                     <em>
                      Building the right analytics foundation provides a way for hospitals to wisely leverage their data, but most important helps them improve care.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                   </div>
                   <p>
                    <span style="font-size: large;">
                     <strong>
                      2.
                     </strong>
                    </span>
                    <strong>
                     Establish 30- and 90-day readmission baseline measures for heart failure patients
                    </strong>
                    . Realize, however, that if youre looking old data, its difficult to engage clinicians in
                    <a href="/clinical-quality-improvement-in-healthcare">
                     clinical improvement
                    </a>
                    initiatives. Adopting an
                    <a href="/late-binding-data-warehouse/">
                     enterprise data warehouse
                    </a>
                    (EDW) as described below could help ensure the data is current.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <span style="font-size: large;">
                     <strong>
                      3.
                     </strong>
                    </span>
                    <strong>
                     Be aware of
                     <i>
                      balance measures
                     </i>
                     (
                    </strong>
                    changes designed to improve one part of the system without causing new problems in another part of the system). For heart patient readmissions, there are three types of balance measures:
                    <a href="/improve-patient-satisfaction-5-lessons-from-disney">
                     patient satisfaction
                    </a>
                    rates, emergency department (ED) visits, and observation stays. It is important to hold the gains with these measures while also improving readmission rates.
                   </p>
                   <p>
                    <span style="font-size: large;">
                     <strong>
                      4.
                     </strong>
                    </span>
                    <strong>
                     Use an enterprise healthcare data warehouse
                    </strong>
                    (EDW) to integrate
                    <a href="/white-paper/surviving-value-based-purchasing-html">
                     clinical
                    </a>
                    , financial, and patient satisfaction data. An enterprise healthcare data warehouse identifies all patients with a primary diagnosis of heart failure and then stratifies the populations as either high- or low-risk for readmission. Using this data, multi-disciplinary teams examine the root cause of readmissions to implement evidence-based, best-practice intervention plans for HF patients. The teams implement these interventions and track their impact on readmission rates and the balance measures.
