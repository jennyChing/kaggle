          <div class="ndn_embed" data-config-distributor-id="90299" id="ndn-video-player-1">

          <p>
           LANSING, MI  President Barack Obama should be in Washington D.C. working with the new Congress instead of giving himself a high-five over the so-called auto bailout, according to Michigan Republican Party Chairman Bobby Schostak.
          </p>
          <p>
           Obama is scheduled to
           <a href="http://www.mlive.com/news/detroit/index.ssf/2015/01/president_barack_obama_to_spea.html">
            speak atFords Michigan Assembly Plant in Wayne
           </a>
           on Wednesday afternoon as part of a three-day tour that will reportedly include additional stops in Phoenix and Tennessee.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Michigan event, according to the White House, will highlight the workers in the resurgent American automotive and manufacturing sector following an auto rescue estimated to have saved over one million jobs.
          </p>
          <p>
           But Schostak, speaking with MLive Monday morning, called the presidents tour another opportunity hes taking to run all over the country and high five himself. To come into Michigan and talk about his rescue of the auto workers is frankly disingenuous, and I believe inappropriate and inaccurate.
          </p>
          <p>
           The cars are selling because of great workers on the line and the engineering, theyre not selling because of some rescue he claims to take credit for.
          </p>
          <p>
           Former Republican President George W. Bush approved short-term bridge loans for the auto industry in 2008 shortly before Obama, a Democrat, took office during the midst of a national recession.
          </p>
          <p>
           But Obama, after convening an auto task force to study the industry, oversaw a larger rescue package that provided roughly $80 billion in taxpayer money to General Motors and Chrysler as they went through restructuring in bankruptcy.
          </p>
          <p>
           The federal government ended up losing about $9.26 billion on the deal, according to the U.S. Treasury Department, but the president and others have defended the expense as a worthwhile investment for an industry whose collapse could have had a much larger effect on the economy.
          </p>
          <p>
           New auto sales
           <a href="http://www.mlive.com/auto/index.ssf/2015/01/us_new_auto_sales_at_highest_l.html">
            topped the 16.5 million
           </a>
           marklast year, according to recent data, the highest rate since 2006.
          </p>
          <p>
           Josh Pugh, a spokesperson for the Michigan Democratic Party, responded to Schostak's comments about the central role thatautoworkers played in the industry's recovery with faint praise.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its great to finally hear Republicans giving Michigan's autoworkers the credit they deserve for helping rebuild our economy. America's auto industry was at the brink, and now, America's auto companies are leading the national recovery," Pugh said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The facts are clear: the auto industry is stronger than ever thanks to the hard work of our autoworkers, and the dedicated leadership of Michigan's Democrats in Congress. Michiganders deserve better than Republicans who still say they would've thrown us under the bus by opposing the auto rescue.
          </p>
          <p>
           Schostak acknowledged that the bailout provided the opportunity for refinancing in what was a difficult economic climate, but he noted that Ford was able to refinance on its own without government assistance and there were "other options."
          </p>
          <p>
           He also pointed out that workers at the Michigan Assembly Plant where Obama is scheduled to speak are on temporary hiatus right now because the fuel-efficient vehicles they produce are not selling well, a development that some analysts have attributed to low gas prices.
          </p>
          <p>
           Obama,
           <a href="http://www.detroitnews.com/story/news/politics/2015/01/06/exclusive-interview-obama-says-cheap-gas-last/21370731/">
            speaking with The Detroit News
           </a>
           ahead of his visit, warned that gas prices are unlikely to remain as low as they are now. But Schostak said the idled production shows that cars that Americans want are not the cars that he wants us to have, that he wants us to promote.
          </p>
          <p>
           Schostak, who recently announced he will not seek a third term as Michigan GOP Chairman, conceded that Obama is right on at least one front: Detroit Lions football fans have good reason to aggravated after the team lost a playoff game that featured a controversial non-penalty call.
          </p>
          <p>
           Yeah, we are angry, Schostak said. We got robbed. Its so frustrating. What a great record we had. They put together a great team, the Ford family did, and we were knocking on the door.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Jonathan Oosting is a Capitol reporter for MLive Media Group.
