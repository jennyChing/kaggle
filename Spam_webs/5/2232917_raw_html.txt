            <hgroup>
             <h1>
              Syphilis is a Red Flag for Potential HIV Acquisition
             </h1>
            </hgroup>
           </header>
           <div class="byline">
            By Kara Nyberg, PhD
            <br/>
            Reviewed by Mark Wainberg, PhD, Director, McGill AIDS Centre, Montreal, Canada
           </div>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <aside class="sidebar">
            <div class="media infographic">
             <img alt="image" src="http://clf1.medpagetoday.com/assets/images/cuso/Treponema-pallidum-300x225.jpg"/>
            </div>
            <section class="actionpoints">
             <header>
              <h2>
               Take Note
              </h2>
             </header>
             <div class="content">
              <ul>
               <li>
                Data from the prospective iPrEx trial show that the risk of acquiring HIV increases 2.6-fold in the presence of incident syphilis among MSM.
               </li>
               <li>
                Syphilis did not attenuate the protective benefit of HIV PrEP.
               </li>
               <li>
                The findings argue for prompt syphilis treatment and administration of HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis to prevent HIV transmission within this high-risk population.
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
            </section>
           </aside>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Men who have sex with men (MSM) who are HIV-seronegative but seropositive for syphilis may be prime candidates for HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP). A new analysis of data from the Pre-exposure Prophylaxis Initiative (iPrEx) trial shows that MSM have a 2.6-fold higher risk of acquiring HIV infection when infected with syphilis than when free of this highly contagious sexually transmitted disease (STD).
            <sup>
             1
             <sup>
             </sup>
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            We hope that these data will trigger clinicians to offer PrEP to men and transgender women who have sex with men who are newly diagnosed with syphilis unless otherwise contraindicated, lead investigator Marc M. Solomon, MD, MPH, of the University of CaliforniaSan Francisco, told
            <i>
             MedPage Today.
            </i>
           </p>
           <p>
            Substantial epidemiologic data support ties between syphilis and HIV. The infectious organisms causing these diseases affect similar patient groups, so coinfection with syphilis and HIV is common. Observational studies show that individuals infected with STDs, including syphilis, are at least 2 to 5 times more likely than uninfected individuals to acquire HIV if exposed to the virus through sexual contact. In addition, HIV-infected individuals who are coinfected with an STD are more likely to transmit HIV to others through sexual contact than HIV-infected individuals without STDs.
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            The link between syphilis and HIV may be the risky sexual behaviors that increase the danger of acquiring STDs, including HIV. Alternatively, genital ulceration and inflammation caused by syphilis may produce chinks in the mucosal barrier that promote acquisition of HIV infection.
           </p>
           <p>
            Although prior observational studies, many of which were retrospective analyses, established an association between syphilis infection and HIV acquisition, Dr. Solomon noted that the iPrEx study offered a unique opportunity to evaluate this relationship in a closed, prospective cohort of men and transgender women who have sex with men with almost complete follow-up and in the context of PrEP use.
           </p>
           <p>
            iPrEx sought to determine the safety and efficacy of once-daily oral PrEP consisting of two nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors for HIV prevention among HIV-seronegative MSM. The 2,499 individuals included in the trial were tested for syphilis and other STDs at screening and every 24 weeks thereafter, or on an as-needed basis following possible exposure or the emergence of suspicious symptoms.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            At screening, 13.3% of the study population tested positive for syphilis.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            During the trial, syphilis occurred at an incidence of 7.3 cases per 100 person-years.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            Factors that independently predicted for syphilis infection during the study included the presence of antibodies for herpes simplex virus-2 (HSV-2) at screening or follow-up, confirmed syphilis at screening, receptive anal intercourse without the protection of a condom, and insertive anal intercourse without the protection of a condom.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            The key findings centered on the effect of syphilis infection on HIV acquisition. Whereas the incidence of HIV infection reached 2.8 cases per 100 person-years in individuals with no syphilis, this rate jumped to 8.0 cases per 100 person-years in persons with incident syphilis (hazard ratio 2.6; 95% confidence interval, 1.6-4.4;
            <i>
             P
            </i>
            1
           </p>
           <p>
            We were able to detect an important increase in the hazard of acquiring HIV if infected with syphilis even when adjusted for potential confounders, Dr. Solomon pointed out. Although not entirely unexpected given the results of past retrospective studies, our finding adds a degree of scientific rigor to the current literature owing to the prospective study design.
           </p>
           <p>
            Importantly, syphilis did not appear to decrease PrEP efficacya potential concern that could result from things such as mucosal barrier compromise or poor PrEP adherence.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            Dr. Solomon feels that the collective findings point to a clear clinical course of action. Reducing the rate of syphilis and other sexually transmitted infections may be one approach to decreasing the incidence of HIV, he says. Toward this end, Dr. Solomon suggests that physicians should not only seek to diagnosis and treat syphilis as quickly as possible in infected individuals, but they should also strongly consider offering PrEP to these patients at the time of syphilis diagnosis.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention currently recommends prioritizing PrEP for MSM at high risk for HIV acquisition.
            <sup>
             3
            </sup>
            Given the strong independent effect of incident syphilis on HIV acquisition among MSM, these individuals arguably meet this high-risk criterion.
           </p>
