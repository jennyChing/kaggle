     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Rhinoviruses
       </b>
      </i>
      (from the Greek (gen.) "nose") are the most common viral infectious agents in humans and are the predominant cause of the
      <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">
       common cold
      </a>
      . Rhinovirus infection proliferates in temperatures between 3335C (9195F), the temperatures found in the nose. Rhinoviruses consist of three species in the genus
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Enterovirus" title="Enterovirus">
        Enterovirus
       </a>
      </i>
      of the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picornaviridae" title="Picornaviridae">
        Picornaviridae
       </a>
      </i>
      family of
      <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">
       viruses
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      There are 99 recognized types of human rhinoviruses that differ according to their surface proteins. They are
      <a href="/wiki/Lytic_cycle" title="Lytic cycle">
       lytic
      </a>
      in nature and are among the smallest viruses, with diameters of about 30 nanometers. By comparison, other viruses such as
      <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">
       smallpox
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vaccinia" title="Vaccinia">
       vaccinia
      </a>
      are around 10 times larger at about 300 nanometers.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission_and_epidemiology">
       Transmission and epidemiology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transmission and epidemiology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">
       Common cold
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are two modes of transmission: via aerosols of respiratory droplets and from contaminated surfaces, including direct person-to-person contact.
     </p>
     <p>
      Human rhinoviruses occur worldwide and are the primary cause of
      <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">
       common colds
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Symptom" title="Symptom">
       Symptoms
      </a>
      include
      <a href="/wiki/Pharyngitis" title="Pharyngitis">
       sore throat
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rhinitis" title="Rhinitis">
       runny nose
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nasal_congestion" title="Nasal congestion">
       nasal congestion
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sneeze" title="Sneeze">
       sneezing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cough" title="Cough">
       cough
      </a>
      ; sometimes accompanied by
      <a href="/wiki/Myalgia" title="Myalgia">
       muscle aches
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">
       fatigue
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">
       malaise
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">
       headache
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Muscle_weakness" title="Muscle weakness">
       muscle weakness
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_(symptom)" title="Anorexia (symptom)">
       loss of appetite
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">
       Fever
      </a>
      and extreme
      <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">
       exhaustion
      </a>
      are more usual in
      <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">
       influenza
      </a>
      . Children may have six to twelve colds a year. In the United States, the incidence of colds is higher in the autumn and winter, with most infections occurring between September to April. The seasonality may be due to the start of the school year
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs a source. The school year in the Southern Hemisphere starts in January, yet colds are still predominant in autumn &amp; winter (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and to people spending more time indoors (thus in proximity with each other)
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="needs proof that people spend more time in proximity to each other, e.g. use public transport more, go to work more etc (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , thereby increasing the chance of transmission of the virus. Lower ambient, especially outdoor, temperatures may also be factor
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lessler_2009_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lessler_2009-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      given that rhinoviruses preferentially replicate at 32C (89F) as opposed to 37C (98F) - see following section.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogenesis">
       Pathogenesis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pathogenesis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The primary route of entry for human rhinoviruses is the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">
       respiratory tract
      </a>
      (mouth and nose). Afterward, the virus binds to
      <a href="/wiki/ICAM-1" title="ICAM-1">
       ICAM-1
      </a>
      (Inter-Cellular Adhesion Molecule 1) also known as CD54 (Cluster of Differentiation 54)
      <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">
       receptors
      </a>
      on respiratory epithelial cells. As the virus replicates and spreads, infected cells release distress signals known as
      <a href="/wiki/Chemokine" title="Chemokine">
       chemokines
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">
       cytokines
      </a>
      (which in turn activate inflammatory mediators). Cell lysis occurs at the upper respiratory epithelium.
     </p>
     <p>
      Infection occurs rapidly, with the virus adhering to surface receptors within 15 minutes of entering the respiratory tract. High risk individuals includes children and the elderly. Just over 50% of individuals will experience symptoms within 2 days of infection. Only about 5% of cases will have an
      <a href="/wiki/Incubation_period" title="Incubation period">
       incubation period
      </a>
      of less than 20 hours, and, at the other extreme, it is expected that 5% of cases would have an incubation period of greater than four and a half days.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lessler_2009_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lessler_2009-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Human rhinoviruses preferentially grow at 32C (89F) as opposed to 37C (98F), hence infect the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_respiratory_tract" title="Upper respiratory tract">
       upper respiratory tract
      </a>
      where respiratory airflow is in continual contact to the (colder) extrasomatic environment.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">
       Taxonomy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Rhinovirus was formerly a genus from the family
      <i>
       Picornaviridae
      </i>
      . The 39th Executive Committee (EC39) of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_on_Taxonomy_of_Viruses" title="International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses">
       International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses
      </a>
      (ICTV) met in Canada during June 2007 with new taxonomic proposals. In April 2008, the International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses voted and ratified the following changes:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       2005.264V.04 To
       <b>
        remove the following species
       </b>
       from the existing genus
       <i>
        Rhinovirus
       </i>
       in the family
       <i>
        Picornaviridae
       </i>
       :
       <ul>
        <li>
         Human rhinovirus A
        </li>
        <li>
         Human rhinovirus B
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       2005.265V.04 To
       <b>
        assign the following species
       </b>
       to the genus
       <i>
        Enterovirus
       </i>
       in the family
       <i>
        Picornaviridae
       </i>
       :
       <ul>
        <li>
         Human rhinovirus A
        </li>
        <li>
         Human rhinovirus B
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       2005.266V.04 To
       <b>
        remove the existing genus
       </b>
       <i>
        Rhinovirus
       </i>
       from the family
       <i>
        Picornaviridae
       </i>
       . Note: The genus
       <i>
        Rhinovirus
       </i>
       hereby disappears.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In July 2009, the ICTV voted and ratified a proposal to add a third species,
      <i>
       Human rhinovirus C
      </i>
      to the genus
      <i>
       Enterovirus
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       2008.084V.A.HRV-C-Sp 2008.084V To
       <b>
        create a new species
       </b>
       named Human rhinovirus C in the genus
       <i>
        Enterovirus
       </i>
       , family
       <i>
        Picornaviridae
       </i>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      There have been a total of 215 taxonomic proposals, which have been approved and ratified since the 8th ICTV Report of 2005.
     </p>
     <p>
      Rhinovirus C, unlike the A and B species, may be able to cause severe infections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fuji2011_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fuji2011-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">
       Structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Rhinoviruses have single-stranded positive sense
      <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">
       RNA
      </a>
      genomes of between 7.2 and 8.5
      <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">
       kb
      </a>
      in length. At the 5' end of the
      <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">
       genome
      </a>
      is a virus-encoded protein, and like mammalian mRNA, there is a 3'
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poly-A_tail" title="Poly-A tail">
       poly-A tail
      </a>
      . Structural proteins are encoded in the 5' region of the genome and non structural at the 3' end. This is the same for all
      <a href="/wiki/Picornavirus" title="Picornavirus">
       picornaviruses
      </a>
      . The viral particles themselves are not
      <a href="/wiki/Viral_envelope" title="Viral envelope">
       enveloped
      </a>
      and are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icosahedral" title="Icosahedral">
       icosahedral
      </a>
      in structure.
     </p>
     <p>
      The viral proteins are transcribed as a single, long polypeptide, which is cleaved into the structural and nonstructural viral proteins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Human rhinoviruses are composed of a
      <a href="/wiki/Capsid" title="Capsid">
       capsid
      </a>
      , that contains four viral
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">
       proteins
      </a>
      VP1, VP2, VP3 and VP4.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossmann_1985_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Rossmann_1985-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_1986_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Smith_1986-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      VP1, VP2, and VP3 form the major part of the protein capsid. The much smaller VP4 protein has a more extended structure, and lies at the interface between the capsid and the RNA genome. There are 60 copies of each of these proteins assembled as an
      <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">
       icosahedron
      </a>
      . Antibodies are a major defense against infection with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epitopes" title="Epitopes">
       epitopes
      </a>
      lying on the exterior regions of VP1-VP3.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Novel_antiviral_drugs">
       Novel antiviral drugs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Novel antiviral drugs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Interferon" title="Interferon">
       Interferon
      </a>
      -alpha used intranasally was shown to be effective against
      <i>
       Human rhinovirus
      </i>
      infections. However, volunteers treated with this drug experienced some side effects, such as nasal bleeding, and began developing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistance_to_antiviral_drugs" title="Resistance to antiviral drugs">
       resistance
      </a>
      to the drug. Subsequently, research into the treatment was abandoned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farr_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Farr-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pleconaril" title="Pleconaril">
       Pleconaril
      </a>
      is an orally
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioavailable" title="Bioavailable">
       bioavailable
      </a>
      antiviral drug being developed for the treatment of infections caused by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picornaviruses" title="Picornaviruses">
       picornaviruses
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pevear_1999_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pevear_1999-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This drug acts by binding to a hydrophobic pocket in VP1, and stabilizes the protein capsid to such an extent that the virus cannot release its RNA genome into the target cell. When tested in volunteers, during the clinical trials, this drug caused a significant decrease in
      <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">
       mucus
      </a>
      secretions and illness-associated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symptoms" title="Symptoms">
       symptoms
      </a>
      . Pleconaril is not currently available for treatment of Human rhinoviral infections, as its efficacy in treating these infections is under further evaluation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fleisher_2003_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Fleisher_2003-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are potentially other substances such as
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Iota-Carrageenan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Iota-Carrageenan (page does not exist)">
       Iota-Carrageenan
      </a>
      that may lead to the creation of drugs to combat the Human rhinovirus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Asthma: Human rhinoviruses have been recently associated with the majority of asthma exacerbations for which current therapy is inadequate. Intercellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-1) has a central role in airway inflammation in asthma, and it is the receptor for 90% of Human rhinoviruses. Human rhinovirus infection of airway epithelium induces ICAM-1.
      <a href="/wiki/Desloratadine" title="Desloratadine">
       Desloratadine
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Loratadine" title="Loratadine">
       loratadine
      </a>
      are compounds belonging to the new class of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H1-receptor_blocker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="H1-receptor blocker (page does not exist)">
       H1-receptor blockers
      </a>
      . Anti-inflammatory properties of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihistamine" title="Antihistamine">
       antihistamines
      </a>
      have been recently documented, although the underlying molecular mechanisms are not completely defined. These effects are unlikely to be mediated by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H1-receptor" title="H1-receptor">
       H1-receptor
      </a>
      antagonism and suggest a novel mechanism of action that may be important for the therapeutic control of virus-induced asthma exacerbations.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vaccine">
       Vaccine
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vaccine">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are no
      <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">
       vaccines
      </a>
      against these viruses as there is little-to-no cross-protection between
      <a href="/wiki/Serotype" title="Serotype">
       serotypes
      </a>
      . At least 99 serotypes of Human rhinoviruses affecting humans have been
      <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">
       sequenced
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, a study of the VP4 protein has shown it to be highly conserved among many serotypes of Human rhinovirus,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      opening up the potential for a future pan-serotype Human rhinovirus vaccine.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">
       Prevention
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhinovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prevention">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Human rhinovirus is extremely contagious during the cold months of each year. The virus can live up to 3 hours outside human contact. Once contracted, a person is most contagious within the first 3 days. It is important to wash hands thoroughly with a reliable antimicrobial agent on a regular basis.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Avoid touching the mouth or nose, as those are the most common portals of entry for the virus.
     </p>
