      <h1>
       Brewers reap fresh Maine hop crop
      </h1>
      <div class="story-excerpt">
       <p>
        Thanks to Maine hop growers, this years hop harvest will be the largest in nearly 100 years, providing Maine craft brewers with ingredients for creating fresh hopped beers.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="date">
       Written by:
       <span>
        Carla Jean Lauter -
        <span>
         contributor
        </span>
        -
        <a href="mailto:carlajean@gmail.com" target="_blank">
         carlajean@gmail.com
        </a>
       </span>
      </div>
      <div class="post-sidebar-desktop">
      </div>
      <div class="p402_premium">
       <a href="http://mainetoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/DSCN4137_edit.jpg">
        <img alt="DSCN4137_edit" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-86163" height="547" src="http://mainetoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/DSCN4137_edit-1024x547.jpg" width="1024"/>
       </a>
       <p>
        Hops  one of the four foundational ingredients in beer  are important to creating a beers overall flavor, aroma and bitterness.The female
        <i style="color: #252525">
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humulus_lupulus" style="color: #0b0080" title="Humulus lupulus">
          Humulus lupulus
         </a>
        </i>
        plants create hop cones or flowers that contain the chemicals that provide its pungent flavoring. Depending on the brewerys size, brewers can use just a few ounces of any of the over eighty varieties of hops, or can utilize pounds at a time.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://mainetoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/DSCN4132_edited.jpg">
         <img alt="DSCN4132_edited" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-86167" height="300" src="http://mainetoday.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/DSCN4132_edited-225x300.jpg" width="225"/>
        </a>
        In Maine, and most of the Northeast, it has been much easier for commercial brewers to obtain hops that have been grown in Europe or the Pacific Northwest, where long-standing traditions of hop growing have thrived, and large quantities are produced annually.
       </p>
       <p>
        In the Pacific Northwest, for example, there are hundreds of beers produced each fallmade with freshly harvested hops  those that have been harvested and put directly into the beer within a few days of being picked. This can only be accomplished if the hop farms are in close proximity to their brewers  long distance transportation of fresh hops isnt possible. The preservationof hops usually involves drying and pelletizing the hops (making them into small, compact pelletsresembling hamster food in appearance). Fortunately, importing thesepellets are what allow brewers across the country to create strongly-flavored hoppy beersyear-round. The taste of fresh hops is significantly different than those of the pellets and their guaranteed freshness (hard to go stale if theres less than 24 hours from bine to kettle) and strong character makes them desirable to brewers.
       </p>
       <p>
        While there have been a handful of very small-scale (backyard-sized) hop farms in Maine  including some on brewery grounds like the yard at
        <strong>
         Gneiss Brewing
        </strong>
         Maine is beginning to see the rise of a more substantial hop crop that might make more fresh hop beers possible in the future.
        <a href="http://thehopyard.com/">
         <strong>
          The Hop Yard
         </strong>
         ,
        </a>
        a Maine hop farm with a locations in Gorham and Fort Fairfield, is contributing to that future with their crop  and is anticipated to be the largest Maine hop harvest in almost 100 years. This year, the mature hop plants are expected to yield approximately 2,000 lbs of wet hops.Overall, 80% of the yards plants are still a year from maturity, meaning that a significant increase in this harvest will be seen next year if all goes to plan.
       </p>
       <p>
        These hop growing efforts have not been without challenges. Recent heavy rainfall caused the poles that hold up the hops in the Gorham yard to sag significantly, and the growers are constantly battling attacks from fungus and bugs. The labor to set up the polesand trellises for the yard and the time it takes to train every single one of the bines to climb the twine used to support themis not insignificant.In addition, each bine has to be cut down manually when harvesting- making for an intense effort. This year, growers at The Hop Yard willbe testing amechanical harvester, which runsthe vines around a conveyor belt that slaps the cones off the plants usingrubber protuberances  hopefully saving hours of hand-picking.
       </p>
       <p>
        The crop from both farms will be sent toover a dozen commercial breweries in Maine  including
        <strong>
         Allagash Brewing Company
        </strong>
        ,
        <strong>
         Sebago Brewing Company
        </strong>
        ,
        <strong>
         Austin Street Brewery, Rising Tide Brewing, Bunker Brewing
        </strong>
        and more.
       </p>
       <p>
        Without historical baselines measuring the hops characteristics from year to year under different conditions, the harvest presents abit of an unknown. Theseparticular hops are newto brewers, andcome with a bit of uncertainty  but also with a sense of excitement and adventure. The plants environment-based character, or terroir, is virtually unknown, and the resulting fresh hop beers will be a true taste ofMaine.
       </p>
       <p>
        I dont know about, you, but Im thirsty already.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Where to taste MaineFresh Hop beers:
       </h2>
       <h4>
        InFinitiHoptoberfest  UPDATED
       </h4>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Where: Infiniti Fermentation &amp; Distillation, 250 Commercial Street, Portland
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Updated
         </strong>
         : Date changed to October 18
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        Fourteen Maine breweries have committed to this all-fresh-hop tap takeover. This is the best place to get a taste of what Maine brewers can do with local hops. Infiniti will be pouring a harvest rye beer, and theres rumors that Allagash will be brewing two beers  once with Maine-grown Cascade hops and the other with West-Coast Cascade for comparison.
       </p>
