     <p>
      The
      <b>
       fovea centralis
      </b>
      (the term
      <i>
       fovea
      </i>
      comes from the Latin, meaning
      <i>
       pit
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       pitfall
      </i>
      ) is a small, central pit composed of closely packed
      <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">
       cones
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">
       eye
      </a>
      . It is located in the center of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macula_lutea" title="Macula lutea">
       macula lutea
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">
       retina
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebvisionRetina_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WebvisionRetina-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOVSnomen_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IOVSnomen-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The fovea is responsible for sharp central
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">
       vision
      </a>
      (also called
      <a href="/wiki/Foveal" title="Foveal">
       foveal
      </a>
      vision), which is necessary in humans for activities where visual detail is of primary importance, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_(activity)" title="Reading (activity)">
       reading
      </a>
      and driving. The fovea is surrounded by the
      <i>
       parafovea
      </i>
      belt, and the
      <i>
       perifovea
      </i>
      outer region.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOVSnomen_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-IOVSnomen-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The parafovea is the intermediate belt, where the
      <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">
       ganglion cell
      </a>
      layer is composed of more than five rows of cells, as well as the highest density of cones; the perifovea is the outermost region where the ganglion cell layer contains two to four rows of cells, and is where visual acuity is below the optimum. The perifovea contains an even more diminished density of cones, having 12 per 100 micrometres versus 50 per 100 micrometres in the most central fovea. This, in turn, is surrounded by a larger
      <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_vision" title="Peripheral vision">
       peripheral
      </a>
      area that delivers highly compressed information of low resolution following the pattern of compression in
      <a href="/wiki/Foveated_imaging" title="Foveated imaging">
       foveated imaging
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Approximately half of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">
       nerve
      </a>
      fibers in the
      <a href="/wiki/Optic_nerve" title="Optic nerve">
       optic nerve
      </a>
      carry information from the fovea, while the remaining half carry information from the rest of the retina. The
      <a href="/wiki/Parafovea" title="Parafovea">
       parafovea
      </a>
      extends to a radius of 1.25mm from the central fovea, and the perifovea is found at a 2.75mm radius from the fovea centralis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">
       Structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fovea_centralis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AcuityHumanEye.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="547" data-file-width="724" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/AcuityHumanEye.svg/270px-AcuityHumanEye.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/AcuityHumanEye.svg/405px-AcuityHumanEye.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/AcuityHumanEye.svg/540px-AcuityHumanEye.svg.png 2x" width="270"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AcuityHumanEye.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The diagram shows the relative acuity of the left human eye (horizontal section) in degrees from the fovea.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          4
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fovea (or
      <i>
       Fovea centralis
      </i>
      ) is the depression in the inner retinal surface, about 1.5mm wide, the photoreceptor layer of which is entirely cones and which is specialized for maximum visual acuity. Fovea also grossly corresponds to the retinal avascular zone (which means without any blood vessels). This allows the light to be sensed without any dispersion or loss. This anatomy is responsible for the depression in the center of the fovea. The foveal pit is surrounded by the foveal rim that contains the neurons displaced from the pit. This is the thickest part of the retina.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vaughan_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vaughan-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The fovea is located in a small avascular zone and receives most of its
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      from the vessels in the
      <a href="/wiki/Choroid" title="Choroid">
       choroid
      </a>
      , which is across the
      <a href="/wiki/Retinal_pigment_epithelium" title="Retinal pigment epithelium">
       retinal pigment epithelium
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bruch%27s_membrane" title="Bruch's membrane">
       Bruch's membrane
      </a>
      . The high spatial density of cones along with the absence of blood vessels at the fovea accounts for the high visual acuity capability at the fovea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The center of the fovea is the foveola  about 0.2mm in diameter  or central pit where only cone photoreceptors are present and there are virtually no
      <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">
       rods
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebvisionRetina_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WebvisionRetina-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The central fovea consists of very compact cones, thinner and more rod-like in appearance than cones elsewhere. These cones are very densely packed (in a
      <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">
       hexagonal
      </a>
      pattern). Starting at the outskirts of the fovea, however, rods gradually appear, and the absolute density of cone receptors progressively decreases.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Size">
       Size
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fovea_centralis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Size">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Fovea size is relatively small compared to the rest of retina, but the fovea is the only area of the retina where 20/20 vision is attainable, and very important for seeing fine detail and color.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Function">
       Function
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fovea_centralis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Function">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ConeMosaics.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="600" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/ConeMosaics.jpg/300px-ConeMosaics.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/ConeMosaics.jpg/450px-ConeMosaics.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/ConeMosaics.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ConeMosaics.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Illustration of the distribution of cone cells in the fovea of an individual with normal color vision (left), and a color blind (protanopic) retina. Note that the center of the fovea holds very few blue-sensitive cones.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">
       primate
      </a>
      fovea (including humans) the ratios of
      <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">
       ganglion cells
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">
       photoreceptors
      </a>
      is about 2.5; almost every ganglion cell receives data from a single cone, and each cone feeds onto between 1 and 3 ganglion cells.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, the acuity of foveal vision is limited only by the density of the cone mosaic, and the fovea is the area of the eye with the highest sensitivity to fine details.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cones in the central fovea express pigments that are sensitive to green and red light. These cones are the 'midget' pathways that also underpin high acuity functions of the fovea.
     </p>
     <p>
      The fovea is employed for accurate vision in the direction where it is pointed. It comprises less than 1% of retinal size but takes up over 50% of the
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">
       visual cortex
      </a>
      in the brain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fovea sees only the central two degrees of the visual field, (approximately twice the width of your thumbnail at arm's length).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      If an object is large and thus covers a large angle, the eyes must constantly shift their gaze to subsequently bring different portions of the image into the fovea (as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eye_movements_in_reading" title="Eye movements in reading">
       in reading
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg/250px-Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg/375px-Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg/500px-Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_photoreceptor_distribution.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Distribution of rods and cones along a line passing through the fovea and the
        <a href="/wiki/Blind_spot_(vision)" title="Blind spot (vision)">
         blind spot
        </a>
        of a human eye
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          13
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Since the fovea does not have rods, it is not sensitive to dim lights. Hence, in order to observe dim stars,
      <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
       astronomers
      </a>
      use
      <a href="/wiki/Averted_vision" title="Averted vision">
       averted vision
      </a>
      , looking out of the side of their eyes where the density of rods is greater, and hence dim objects are more easily visible.
     </p>
     <p>
      The fovea has a high concentration of the yellow
      <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">
       carotenoid
      </a>
      pigments
      <a href="/wiki/Lutein" title="Lutein">
       lutein
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Zeaxanthin" title="Zeaxanthin">
       zeaxanthin
      </a>
      . They are concentrated in the
      <i>
       Henle fiber layer
      </i>
      (photoreceptor axons that go radially outward from the fovea) and to a lesser extent in the cones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krinsky2003_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Krinsky2003-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landrum2001_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Landrum2001-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They are believed to play a protective role against the effects of high intensities of blue light which can damage the sensitive cones. The pigments also enhance the acuity of the fovea by reducing the sensitivity of the fovea to short wavelengths and counteracting the effect of
      <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">
       chromatic aberration
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beatty1999_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Beatty1999-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is also accompanied by a lower density of blue cones at the center of the fovea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curcio1991_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Curcio1991-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The maximum density of blue cones occurs in a ring about the fovea. Consequently, the maximum acuity for blue light is lower than that of other colours and occurs approximately 1 off center.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curcio1991_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Curcio1991-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Angular_size_of_foveal_cones">
       Angular size of foveal cones
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fovea_centralis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Angular size of foveal cones">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Retina_Display" title="Retina Display">
       Retina Display
      </a>
      , a trademarked term describing a pixel density matching that of the human eye.
     </div>
     <p>
      On average, each square millimeter (mm) of the fovea contains approximately 147,000 cone cells,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or 383 cones per millimeter. The average
      <a href="/wiki/Focal_length" title="Focal length">
       focal length
      </a>
      of the eye, i.e. the distance between the lens and fovea, is 17.1 mm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From these values, one can calculate that the average angular size of each cone cell is approximately 31.46
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_seconds" title="Arc seconds">
       arc seconds
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The following is a table of
      <a href="/wiki/Pixel_density" title="Pixel density">
       pixel densities
      </a>
      required at various distances so that there is one pixel per 31.5 arc seconds:
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable">
      <tr>
       <th>
        Example object
       </th>
       <th>
        Distance from eye assumed
       </th>
       <th>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pixels_per_inch" title="Pixels per inch">
         PPI
        </a>
        (PPCM) to match
        <br/>
        avg. foveal cone density
        <br/>
        (20/10.5 vision)
       </th>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Phone or tablet
       </td>
       <td>
        10 inches (25.4cm)
       </td>
       <td>
        655.6 (258.1)
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        Laptop screen
       </td>
       <td>
        2 feet (61cm)
       </td>
       <td>
        273.2 (107.6)
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        42" (1.07m) 16:9 HDTV,
        <a href="/wiki/Optimum_HDTV_viewing_distance" title="Optimum HDTV viewing distance">
         30 view
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        5.69 feet (1.73m)
       </td>
       <td>
        96.0 (37.8)
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Peak cone density varies highly between individuals, such that peak values below 100,000 cones/mm
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      and above 324,000 cones/mm
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      are not uncommon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Assuming average focal lengths, this suggests that individuals with both high cone densities and perfect optics may resolve pixels with an angular size of 21.2 arc seconds, requiring PPI values at least 1.5 times those shown above in order for images not to appear pixelated.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is worth noting that individuals with
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_acuity" title="Visual acuity">
       20/20
      </a>
      (6/6 m) vision, defined as the ability to discern a 5x5 pixel letter that has an angular size of 5 arc minutes, cannot see pixels smaller than 60 arc seconds. In order to resolve a pixel the size of 31.5 and 21.2 arc seconds, an individual would need 20/10.5 (6/3.1 m) and 20/7.1 (6/2.1 m) vision, respectively. To find the PPI values discernible at 20/20, simply divide the values in the above table by the visual acuity ratio (e.g. 96 PPI / (20/10.5 vision) = 50.4 PPI for 20/20 vision).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Entoptic_effects_in_the_Fovea">
       Entoptic effects in the Fovea
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fovea_centralis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Entoptic effects in the Fovea">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The presence of the pigment in the radially arranged axons of the Henle fiber layer causes it to be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dichroic" title="Dichroic">
       dichroic
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birefringent" title="Birefringent">
       birefringent
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanNasdale2009_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VanNasdale2009-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to blue light. This effect is visible through the
      <a href="/wiki/Haidinger%27s_brush" title="Haidinger's brush">
       Haidinger's brush
      </a>
      when the fovea is pointed to a polarized light source.
     </p>
     <p>
      The combined effects of the macular pigment and the distribution of short wavelength cones results in the fovea having a lower sensitivity to blue light (blue light scotoma). Though this is not visible under normal circumstances due to "filling in" of information by the brain, under certain patterns of blue light illumination, a dark spot is visible at the point of focus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnussen2001_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Magnussen2001-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Also, if mixture of red and blue light is viewed (by viewing white light through a dichroic filter), the point of foveal focus will have a central red spot surrounded by a few red fringes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnussen2001_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Magnussen2001-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is called the
      <b>
       Maxwell's spot
      </b>
      after
      <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">
       James Clerk Maxwell
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who discovered it.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_animals">
       In other animals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fovea_centralis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In other animals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The fovea is also a pit in the surface of the retinas of many types of fish, reptiles, and birds. Among mammals, it is found only in
      <a href="/wiki/Simian" title="Simian">
       simian
      </a>
      primates. The retinal fovea takes slightly different forms in different types of animals. For example, in primates, cone photoreceptors line the base of the foveal pit, the cells that elsewhere in the retina form more superficial layers having been displaced away from the foveal region during late
      <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">
       fetal
      </a>
      and early
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postnatal" title="Postnatal">
       postnatal
      </a>
      life. Other foveae may show only a reduced thickness in the inner cell layers, rather than an almost complete absence.
     </p>
