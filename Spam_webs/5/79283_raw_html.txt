       <div class="pg-rail-tall__body" itemprop="articleBody">
        <section class="zn zn-body-text zn-body zn--idx-0 zn-has-multiple-containers zn-has-29-containers" data-containers="29" data-eq-pts="xsmall: 0, medium: 460, large: 780, full16x9: 1100" data-vr-zone="zone-1-0" data-zn-id="body-text" id="body-text">
         <div class="l-container">
          <div class="el__leafmedia el__leafmedia--storyhighlights el__leafmedia--">
           <div class="el__storyhighlights_wrapper">
            <div class="el__storyhighlights">
             <h3 class="el__headline">
              Story highlights
             </h3>
             <ul class="el__storyhighlights__list">
              <li class="el__storyhighlights__item el__storyhighlights--normal">
               "We're in the midst of a bionic revolution," says author Ramez Naam
              </li>
              <li class="el__storyhighlights__item el__storyhighlights--normal">
               Thought-controlled bionic legs and arms are already reality
              </li>
              <li class="el__storyhighlights__item el__storyhighlights--normal">
               Brain implants allowed monkeys to control robotic arms from a distance of 7,000 miles
              </li>
              <li class="el__storyhighlights__item el__storyhighlights--normal">
               These advances raise moral question over who will have access to this technology
              </li>
             </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="ad ad--epic" data-ad-text="show">
             <div class="ad-ad_ns_btf_01" id="ad_ns_btf_01">
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           We're in the midst of a bionic revolution, yet most of us don't know it.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Around
           <a href="http://www.nidcd.nih.gov/health/hearing/pages/coch.aspx" target="_blank">
            220,000
           </a>
           people worldwide already walk around with
           <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2012/12/28/health/cochlear-implants">
            cochlear implants
           </a>
           -- devices worn around the ear that turn sound waves into electrical impulses shunted directly into the auditory nerve.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Tens of thousands of people have been implanted with deep brain stimulators, devices that send an electrode tunneling several inches in the brain. Deep brain stimulators are used to control Parkinson's disease, though lately they've also been tested -- with encouraging results -- in use
           <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2012/04/14/health/battery-powered-brain">
            against severe depression
           </a>
           and obsessive compulsive disorder.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           The most obvious bionics are those that replace limbs. Olympian "Blade Runner" Oscar Pistorius, now awaiting trial for the alleged murder of his girlfriend, made a splash with his Cheetah carbon fiber prostheses. Yet those are a relatively simple technology -- a curved piece of slightly springy, super-strong material. In the digital age, we're seeing more sophisticated limbs.
          </p>
          <div class="el__image--standard js__image--standard">
           <img alt="Ramez Naam" class="media__image media__image--responsive" data-demand-load="not-loaded" data-eq-pts="mini: 0,  xsmall: 221,  small: 308,  medium: 461,  large:  781" data-src-full16x9="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-horizontal-large-gallery.jpg" data-src-large="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-horizontal-large-gallery.jpg" data-src-medium="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-horizontal-large-gallery.jpg" data-src-mini="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-hp-video.jpg" data-src-mini1x1="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-topics.jpg" data-src-small="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-story-top.jpg" data-src-xsmall="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-story-body.jpg" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhEAAJAJEAAAAAAP///////wAAACH5BAEAAAIALAAAAAAQAAkAAAIKlI+py+0Po5yUFQA7"/>
           <div class="img__preloader">
           </div>
           <noscript>
            <img alt="Ramez Naam" class="media__image" src="http://i2.cdn.turner.com/cnnnext/dam/assets/130422162806-ramez-naam-story-top.jpg"/>
           </noscript>
           <div class="media__caption el__gallery_image-title">
            Ramez Naam
           </div>
          </div>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           <a href="http://cnn.com/specials/tech/bionic-body/index.html">
            Explore the bionic body
           </a>
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Consider the
           <a href="http://cnn.com/2012/11/04/health/illinois-bionic-leg">
            thought-controlled bionic leg
           </a>
           that Zac Vawter used to climb all 103 floors of Chicago's Willis Tower. Or the
           <a href="http://thechart.blogs.cnn.com/2011/02/17/bionic-arm-gives-hope-for-amputees/">
            nerve-controlled bionic hand
           </a>
           that Iraq war veteran Glen Lehman had attached after the loss of his original hand.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Or the even more sophisticated
           <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2013/02/01/tech/bionic-hand-ilimb-prosthetic">
            i-limb Ultra
           </a>

          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           The 7,000-mile-away prosthetic arm makes an important point: These new prostheses aren't just going to restore missing human abilities. They're going to enhance our abilities, giving us powers we never had before, and augmenting other capabilities we have. While the current generation of prostheses is still primitive, we can already see this taking shape when a monkey moves a robotic arm on the other side of the planet just by thinking about it.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Other research is pointing to enhancements to memory and decision making.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           The hippocampus is a small, seahorse-shaped part of the brain that's essential in forming new memories. If it's damaged -- by an injury to the head, for example -- people start having difficulty forming new long-term memories. In the most extreme cases, this can lead to the complete inability to form new long-term memories, as in the film Memento. Working to find a way to repair this sort of brain damage, researchers in 2011 created a "hippocampus chip" that can replace damaged brain tissue. When they implanted it in rats with a damaged hippocampus, they found that not only could their chip repair damaged memory -- it could
           <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/17/science/17memory.html?_r=1&amp;" target="_blank">
            improve the rats' ability to learn new things
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Nor is memory the end of it. Another study, in 2012, demonstrated that we can boost intelligence -- at least one sort -- in monkeys. Scientists at Wake Forest University implanted specialized brain chips in a set of monkeys and trained those monkeys to perform a picture-matching game. When the implant was activated, it raised their scores by an average of 10 points on a 100-point scale. The implant
           <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2012/09/14/health/research/brain-implant-improves-thinking-in-monkeys.html" target="_blank">
            makes monkeys smarter
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           <strong>
            From disabled to super-capable
           </strong>
          </p>
          <div class="el__image--standard js__image--standard">
          </div>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Both of those technologies for boosting memory and intelligence are in very early stages, in small animal studies only, and years (or possibly decades) away from wide use in humans. Still, they make us wonder -- what happens when it's possible to improve on the human body and mind?
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           <a href="http://edition.cnn.com/2013/04/24/world/europe/alternative-limb-project/index.html">
            Read this: Snake arms and crystal legs: Amazing artificial limbs
           </a>
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           The debate has started already, of course. Oscar Pistorius had to fight hard for inclusion in the Olympics. Many objected that his carbon fiber prostheses gave him a competitive advantage. He was able -- with the help of doctors and biomedical engineers -- to make a compelling case that his Cheetah blades didn't give him any advantage on the field. But how long will that be true? How long until we have prostheses (not to mention drugs and genetic therapies) that make athletes better in their sports?
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           But the issue is much, much wider than professional sports. We may care passionately about the integrity of the Olympics or professional cycling or so on, but they only directly affect a very small number of us. In other areas of life -- in the workforce in particular -- enhancement technology might affect all of us.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           When it's possible to make humans smarter, sharper, and faster, how will that affect us? Will the effect be mostly positive, boosting our productivity and the rate of human innovation? Or will it be just another pressure to compete at work? Who will be able to afford these technologies? Will anyone be able to have their body, and more importantly, their brain upgraded? Or will only the rich have access to these enhancements?
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           We have a little while to consider these questions, but we ought to start. The technology will sneak its way into our lives, starting with people with disabilities, the injured, and the ill. It'll improve their lives in ways that are unquestionably good. And then, one day, we'll wake up and realize that we're doing more than restoring lost function. We're enhancing it.
          </p>
          <p class="zn-body__paragraph">
           Superhuman technology is on the horizon. Time to start thinking about what that means for us.
          </p>
