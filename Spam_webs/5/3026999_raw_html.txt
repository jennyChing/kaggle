        <p>
         Street artist Richard Wolter makes a squid on the side at Richmond's Holliava wine bar, in Swan Street. Some places, such as churches, are off limits, he says.
         <cite>
          <i>
           Photo: Craig Sillitoe
          </i>
         </cite>
        </p>
       </div>
       <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
       <p>
        STREET artist Richard Wolter would never get a tattoo. The 27-year-old Englishman said he could not bear the idea of an image permanently etched on his body, because perspective changes over time.
       </p>
       <p>
        Over the past 20 years, street art has evolved to a mainstream art form, but debate continues over how it is perceived.
       </p>
       <p>
        Luke Cornish's stencilled portrait of Father Bob Maguire was a finalist in this year's Archibald Prize, and in 2008, a work by the UK graffiti artist Banksy sold for $1.87 million.
       </p>
       <p>
        At the same time, vandalism, including graffiti, costs Melbourne train operators $11 million a year to clean up. A Metro spokesman said a mural took on average three hours to clean, which caused delays, as Metro's franchise agreement with the state government requires trains be taken out of service within 24 hours of an incident being reported.
       </p>
       <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
        <small>
         Advertisement
        </small>
        <script>
         titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
        </script>
       </div>
       <p>
        ''As soon as we take it down it goes straight back up again,'' the spokesman said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Today Wolter, who is based in Brunswick, paints for private collectors and public projects, including in Europe, Peru, South Africa, Kashmir and Hong Kong.
       </p>
       <p>
        A private collector in Melbourne recently paid $7000 for a 4.3-metre canvas depicting a city laneway, and last week Wolter completed a mural of a squid at Holliava wine bar, in Swan Street in Richmond. But it was not always that way. He cut his teeth as a teen armed with a spray can on the Paris Metro.
       </p>
       <p>
        He said anyone who had gone on to do amazing things had at first spent some time painting trains. That is a problem for Sergeant Duncan Browne, of the police's transit safety division.
       </p>
       <p>
        "People often say, 'if they just did that really nice stuff I see on walls, I'd actually like it','' Sergeant Browne said. ''Well, you can't have one form of graffiti without the other.''
       </p>
       <p>
        The City of Melbourne's graffiti management plan distinguishes unwanted graffiti from street art done with the consent of property owners. There are 23 registered sites around the city, such as Hosier Lane, where street art is allowed.
       </p>
       <p>
        Sergeant Browne, whose own passion is photography, does not have the luxury of debating what constitutes art.
       </p>
       <p>
        ''I'm looking at it from a forensic point of view, not an aesthetic point of view," he said. "Art has got permission, vandalism no permission."
       </p>
       <p>
        Sergeant Browne has studied the city's graffiti culture since 1996, sometimes working undercover.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The levels of skill that I see helps me determine who is involved,'' Sergeant Browne said. ''Their ability to put a piece on a train, the structure of it, the colours used, how they got in, the time they did it in, will give us some idea as to who they are."
       </p>
       <p>
        According to Metro, more authorised officers could now respond quicker to reported break-ins, and there was better surveillance at sidings across the network.
       </p>
       <p>
        Wolter said while some people were content to remain ''pure vandals'', there were some sites, such as churches, that remained off limits to all but the ''heavy anarchists''.
       </p>
