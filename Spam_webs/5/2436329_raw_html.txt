          <p>
           Neurologists and virologists still have no explanation for nearly two dozen cases of limb paralysis in California seen from August 2012 through early 2014, a CDC report said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Described as "acute flaccid paralysis (AFP) with anterior myelitis"  --  reflecting inflammation of spinal cord gray matter, including anterior horn cell bodies  --  the paralysis lasted at least 2 months in all 13 patients with that much follow-up. One adult patient died and five were sent to rehab facilities on ventilators.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           With clinical specimens available from 19 of the cases, CDC and California health experts looked at numerous potential infection causes but found no common link. The report,
           <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/mm63e1003a2.htm?s_cid=mm63e1003a2_w" target="_blank">
            published online in
            <em>
             Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report
            </em>
           </a>
           , listed the following agents for which tests were run: "enteroviruses (including poliovirus), arboviruses, herpes viruses (HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV, and EBV), parechoviruses, adenoviruses, rabies, influenza A and B, human metapneumovirus, respiratory syncytial virus, parainfluenza 1-4,
           <em>
            Mycoplasma pneumoniae
           </em>
           , rickettsial pathogens, and free-living amoebas."
          </p>
          <p>
           Results were mostly negative. No evidence of poliovirus was found. Samples from two patients were serologically reactive for mycoplasma (but negative on PCR testing) and one patient showed molecular evidence of a rhinovirus. And enterovirus D68 DNA was found in respiratory specimens from two patients.
          </p>
          <p>
           The latter finding has gained significance in the wake of recent D68 outbreaks in Colorado and elsewhere, in which
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/Neurology/GeneralNeurology/47882" target="_blank">
            some pediatric patients also showed neurological symptoms including paralysis
           </a>
           . This virus usually causes only respiratory symptoms, although these can be severe.
          </p>
          <p>
           Overall, the California investigation failed to turn up firm explanations for any of the cases. "The etiology of AFP with anterior myelitis in the cases described in this report remains undetermined," according to the authors, led by Patrick Ayscue, DVM, of the CDC's Epidemic Intelligence Service.
          </p>
          <p>
           They noted that the investigation had been hindered by late, improper, or absent specimen collection in many cases and lack of clinical follow-up.
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Ayscue and colleagues recommended that physicians seeing potential AFP cases should collect clinical samples early, following CDC and World Health Organization guidelines and include stool and serum specimens and throat swabs.
          </p>
          <p>
           Following these procedures will be vital in catching cases of imported polio, the authors stressed.
          </p>
