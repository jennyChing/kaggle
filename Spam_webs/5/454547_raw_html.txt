          <p>
           The morning that I returned to work after the birth of my son, I rode the train with the distinct feeling that I was missing something. I had my phone, my wallet, the lunch that would be one of the first eaten in its entirety in many weeks. I mentally checked that I had showered, did a quick once over my legs to recall if I had shaved them, and then realized: it was my arms. They were empty.
          </p>
          <p>
           With a borderline-colicky baby, I had done a lot of holding, rocking, and bouncing my son over the previous ten weeks. My glutes were stronger than theyd ever been during this post-partum period, ironically enough, due to hours of squatting motions while praying for my son to
           <i>
            pleasejustsleeppleasegod
           </i>
           . It felt strange to not have his warm, tiny body pressed close to mine.
          </p>
          <p>
           And thus the return to my job was bittersweet. Those first days saw me swing from gratitude to excitement to exhaustion to guilt and back to exhaustion. I was ready to return to the career I loved. I missed my patients, my co-workers, my occasional Starbucks on the walk to the office. I had longed for someone to talk back when I spoke to them. But I spent my days  long ones  without the tiny being that had been physically a part of me for almost a year. It was liberating and excruciating.
          </p>
          <p>
           Six months later, I dont regret my decision to go back to my job. I knew that my career was a part of my identity and that I would miss it too much to leave it. That doesnt mean that there arent days that I dont long to keep my baby by my side or beat myself up over the fact that he hasnt spent a week without a daycare cold.
          </p>
          <p>
           The decision to work outside the home comes with sacrifices  lots of them. I rise early, oh so early, even when my son sleeps in. I spend too much money on extra under-eye concealer. I deal with the mortification of forgetting names of people Ive known for years in important meetings. I spend every evening rushing home to quickly feed and bathe my son and put him down for sleep in a mad dash before hes totally melted down, missing him even as Im holding him. And worst of all, I wave goodbye to him each day as his caretakers get down on their hands and knees and get the joyful belly laughs that I wish were reserved for me.
          </p>
          <p>
           My nights involve spending the couple hours I have in another mad dash to wash bottles, launder diapers, fold clothes, pack lunches, clean up dinner I made, put away toys, and maybe watch an episode of
           <i>
            New Girl
           </i>
           while hopping back online to check the 50 emails Ive gotten since I ducked out early and wondering if this is really
           <i>
            leaning in
           </i>
           ?
          </p>
          <p>
           So I take offense when some of the other women I know call themselves Full Time Moms. I mean, I get it. I dont think the term stay at home mom conveys the significance of what mothers who take care of their children during the day do.
          </p>
          <p>
           Maybe nothing does. These parents days may look slightly different than mine, but they dont look easier. I have the utmost respect for that decision, and there are so many reasons many parents make it. For some, it may not even feel like a choice, as when its whats culturally expected or more financial viable.
          </p>
          <p>
           The problem is that terms do not just define the thing to which they refer, but they imply something about the things to which they do not refer. Calling the woman who cares for her child(ren) during the day a Full Time Mom makes me what? A Part Time Mom? Because I assure you, nothing about my life
           <i>
            feels
           </i>
           part-time.
          </p>
          <p>
           I already gave you my sob story above about my daily routine. And many women and men who parent and work outside the home have even more challenging and chaotic schedules than me. Take for example, the parents with a child with special needs or a non-traditional shift or the absence of a partner. If they work in a paid job, do they cease being their childs full-time parent? Of course not. And neither do I.
          </p>
          <p>
           I can say unequivocally that I do not take my mom hat off when I step into my office. As many of my friends similarly report, my mind is now always divided between the task at hand and my son. In fact, I think in many ways he makes me a stronger and smarter employee. I work more efficiently. I have a different perspective on the lives of others with whom I work. Hes not a distraction from my work as much as part of me as a worker. I am his mother every minute of my day.
          </p>
          <p>
           I get email updates from his daycare and laugh out loud at the silly photos they send. I text my husband to remind him  its picture day tomorrow. I plan his feedings. I make a grocery list. I blink heavy from the four times he woke up the night before. And in the midst of my work, I remind myself my son benefits from having his mother make the best choices
           <i>
            she
           </i>
           can for
           <i>
            her
           </i>
           family
           <i>
            at this point
           </i>
           . I am parenting constantly.
          </p>
          <p>
           I cant pretend to know the best terms to define the role of parents in various situations. But what I know is that we need to rethink these labels. Whether were home or at an outside job or working in the basement or skipping around on the moon, balancing parenthood and the other aspects of self is a full-time job for anyone.
          </p>
