             <br/>
             <p>
              This is a great resource for those of us who are runners. Being a former Nike employee and having trained and run many races, I can say that buying the right running shoes for your feet and running style is the most important thing to do if you want to be a happy runner and stay injury-free.  You could buy the most expensive and trendy running shoe out there (try not to fall for all the marketing speak)  but it could be entirely wrong for your footstrike, usual running surface, distance and speed.  Been there, done that and ended up with nasty shin splints and plantar fasciitis that took a month+ to heal (and missed the race to boot). Gave away those $150 running shoes and went back to a new pair of my Saucony standbys at 1/2 the price.
             </p>
             <p>
              THE best advice Ive been given and follow religiously when buying new shoes (unless you are replacing a tried-and-true pair with the same shoe) is that whatever shoe you choose, it should be immediately comfortable the minute you lace up and do your trial run around the store. And, always buy at least 1/2 to one size up even if it feels weird when you try them on (youll save yourself a lot of blisters and black toenails by doing this).  By the way, never buy a new brand/model/style of running shoe unless you can run in them for several minutes before buying. Even trying on at the end of the day when your feet could be a tad swollen isnt enough.  Most reputable running shoe stores have treadmills in place so you can test out the shoes. Even before that, many will use those treadmills  hooked up to cameras and footstrike analytics software  to help determine best shoe for you.
             </p>
             <p>
              Happy running!
