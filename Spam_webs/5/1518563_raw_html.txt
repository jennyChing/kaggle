        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Is Apple positioning itself for a new wave of data?
        </h1>
        <div class="post-info">
         <span class="date published time" title="2013-09-20T08:22:12+00:00">
          September 20, 2013
         </span>
         by
         <span class="author vcard">
          <span class="fn">
           <a href="http://insidetechtalk.com/author/gil-press/" rel="author">
            Gil Press
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
        </div>
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p>
          Apples September 10th announcement of the new iPhones was greeted by negative commentary and a drop in the price of its stock. Dylan Tweney, executive editor at VentureBeat spoke for many when he asked if this is the beginning of a long decline. After a couple of days, however, some commentators got over the colors and the plastic and declared that there were indeed a few new things to chew on: A 64-bit chip and a motion coprocessor.
         </p>
         <p>
          The 64-bit chip allows applications to handle large chunks of data more efficiently than 32-bit. And for applications that require the analysis of movement, the coprocessor can determine if a person is stationary, walking, or driving. No one has done this before and it required a complete re-write of the operating system. But is this just marketing, allowing Apple to project an aura of progress and differentiate itself, at least for a while, from Android-based competitors? Is this only about opening up new opportunities for developers of video games and health and fitness applications?
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_6147" style="width: 510px">
          <a href="http://www75-98-168-16.a2hosted.com/itt/2015/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/shutterstock_107851007.jpg">
           <img alt="pio3 / Shutterstock.com" class="size-full wp-image-6147" height="333" src="http://www75-98-168-16.a2hosted.com/itt/2015/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/shutterstock_107851007.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           <a href="http://www.shutterstock.com/gallery-438058p1.html?cr=00&amp;pl=edit-00">
            pio3
           </a>
           <a href="http://www.shutterstock.com/?cr=00&amp;pl=edit-00">
            <br/>
           </a>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Maybe. Its possible that Apple is settling into a long drawn-out battle, concerned mostly with protecting its shrinking smartphone market share and its margins. Its possible that it will continue to do more of the samepull into the smartphone the functions of specialized devices (e.g., cameras, GPS, eBook readers) and, at the same time, introduce new specialized devices such as a watch.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its also possible that thinking about the iPhone (or the Galaxy or Moto X, for that matter) as a smartphone is so 2007. Its possible that rather than just continuing on automatic pilot on a well-trodden path, Apple is positioning itself for a new wave of data, applications, and analysis, the likes of which we have never seen before.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Three Data Waves
         </h3>
         <p>
          The worlds data has been created, used and stored in three distinct repositories: Enterprise, consumer, and machine-to-machine. The first wave of digital data started in the enterpriseproduced by people working for enterprises, housed in their databases and electronic files, and analyzed to further the objectives of these enterprises. Then came consumer data. It lives in personal devices and the storehouses of enterprises that collect data from individuals using their online services and is analyzed by both enterprises and (rarely) individuals. The new data wave is produced and transmitted by and among networked sensors and is captured and analyzed by the owners of the sensors.
         </p>
         <p>
          The enterprise data pool was created with the proliferation of enterprise computer networks and gave rise to or amplified the presence of IT vendors such as IBM, HP, EMC, Cisco, and Oracle. The consumer data pool has been driven by the advent of the World Wide Web and gave rise to new IT vendors such as Google, Facebook, Netflix, and Amazon. Now we have more and more data created by the Internet of Things and GE and other industrial giants want to be the new IT vendors.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Convergence now
         </h3>
         <p>
          These three data pools are now converging into one data tsunami. In the early 1990s, it was fashionable to talk about digital convergencethe merging of the computer, consumer electronics, and media industries. When Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, his new product strategy explicitly followed this vision. Ten years later, on the day Jobs introduced the iPhone, the company removed the word computer from its name.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its a mistake, however, to think about the new Apple as a consumer electronics company. Jobs has created a company that did not fit anymore into any specific industry pigeonhole. It did not move from one industry to another; rather, it placed itself at the center of the shift from analog to digital.
         </p>
         <p>
          The waves of digitization (and the resulting digital data) sweeping all these industries, the shift from analog to digital, was Steve Jobs guiding light and he rode each wave successfully. In 2002, digital storage surpassed non-digital storage for the first time, according to
          <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/graphic/2011/02/11/GR2011021100614.html" target="_blank" title="Martin Hilbert and Priscila Lopez">
           Martin Hilbert and Priscila Lopez
          </a>
          . By 2007, 94% of storage capacity in the world was digital, a complete reversal from 1986, when 99.2% of all storage capacity was analog.
         </p>
         <p>
          Jobs is gone but digitization continues its relentless march. The most recent wave is all about things  cars, homes, electrical grids, even
          <a href="http://infostory.wordpress.com/2011/07/18/tipping-point-for-the-internet-of-things-and-connected-cows/" target="_blank">
           cows
          </a>
           and all industries. By 2030, according to HP, there will be 1 trillion sensors in the world, one sensor every 10 square feet. The global industrial infrastructure, which accounts for 46% of the worlds economy, according to GE, is being digitized, transforming all industries and all types of businesses and organizations.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its digital convergence on a global scale, the convergence of enterprise, consumer, and machine data. Its the Internet of stationary and moving things. Its a plethora of new applications for tapping into all data from all sources in real-time. Apples investment in next-generation technologies may not be just a marketing gimmick. It may position it to become the platform of choice for capturing, processing, and analyzing converged data.
         </p>
