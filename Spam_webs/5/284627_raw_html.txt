     <p>
      A
      <b>
       name day
      </b>
      is a tradition in many countries in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
       Latin America
      </a>
      that consists of celebrating a day of the year that is associated with one's
      <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">
       given name
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The custom originated with the Christian
      <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">
       calendar of saints
      </a>
      : believers named after a
      <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">
       saint
      </a>
      would celebrate that saint's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">
       feast day
      </a>
      . In many countries, however, there is no longer any explicit connection to
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rus_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rus-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gr_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gr-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It remains more popular in the Catholic and Orthodox parts of Europe, as Protestant churches generally do not
      <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">
       venerate
      </a>
      saints.
     </p>
     <p>
      The concept of name days is similar to the more common
      <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">
       birthday
      </a>
      , except that the association of a name to a day is entirely arbitrary. In the Eastern Orthodox Tradition, the name day corresponds to the day of a saint's death.
     </p>

     <p>
      The celebration of name days has been a tradition in Catholic and Orthodox countries since the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      , and has also continued in some measure in those countries, such as England and the Scandinavian countries, whose
      <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">
       Protestant
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Established_church" title="Established church">
       established church
      </a>
      retains certain Catholic traditions. The name days originate in the list of holidays celebrated in commemoration of saints and
      <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">
       martyrs
      </a>
      of the church. For example, the name
      <i>
       Karl
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Carl
      </i>
      is celebrated in Sweden on January 28, the anniversary of the death of
      <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">
       Charlemagne
      </a>
      (Charles
      <i>
       magnus
      </i>
      , i.e., 'the great'). The church promoted celebration of name days (or rather saints'
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">
       feast days
      </a>
      ) over
      <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">
       birthdays
      </a>
      , as the latter was seen as a pagan tradition.
     </p>
     <p>
      Where name days occur an official list is held containing the current assignations of names to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Days" title="Days">
       days
      </a>
      . There are different lists for
      <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
       Finnish
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
       Swedish
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sami_language" title="Sami language">
       Sami
      </a>
      , and other countries that celebrate namedays, though some names are celebrated on the same day in many countries. From the 18th century and onwards the list of name days has been modified in
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
       Finland
      </a>
      , but not in other countries.
     </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgaria">
       Bulgaria
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bulgaria">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Bulgaria" title="Name days in Bulgaria">
       Name days in Bulgaria
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Name days ( ) in Bulgaria are almost always associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
       Bulgarian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">
       Eastern Orthodox
      </a>
      celebrations. Some names can be celebrated on more than one day and some have even started following foreign traditions
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      (like Valentina being celebrated on the
      <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
       Catholic
      </a>
      St. Valentine's Day).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      St. George's day (, celebrated on 6 May) and St. Joan's day (, celebrated on 7 January) are two of the most popular name days in Bulgaria.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another example of a name day connected with Christianity is
      <i>
       Tsvetnitsa
      </i>
      (,
      <a href="/wiki/Palm_Sunday" title="Palm Sunday">
       Palm Sunday
      </a>
      ). On this day people with names derived from flowers, trees, herbs, etc., celebrate. Name days are frequently connected with some year or season features like
      <i>
       Dimitrovden
      </i>
      (Dimitar's day, 26 Oct.) being the beginning of winter and
      <i>
       Gergyovden
      </i>
      (George's day, 6 May) being the end of it according to traditional folklore.
     </p>
     <p>
      Name days in Bulgaria are important and widely celebrated. Children celebrate their name days by bringing sweets and chocolates to school. By an ancient Bulgarian tradition, everybody is welcome on name days; there is no need to invite guests. Presents are given.
     </p>
     <p>
      Common well-wishes include "May you hear your name from grandchildren and great-grandchildren!" (       !), "May you hear your name only in good things!" (       !) and "May your name be healthy and well!" (      !).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Croatia">
       Croatia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Croatia" title="Name days in Croatia">
       Name days in Croatia
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">
       Croatia
      </a>
      , name day (
      <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">
       Croatian
      </a>
      : imendan) is a day corresponding to a date in the Catholic calendar when the respective
      <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">
       saint's day
      </a>
      is celebrated. Even though celebration of the name day is less usual than celebrating birthday, the name day is more often congratulated by broader number of acquaintances. This is due to the fact that the date of birth is seldom known and the person's name is known to many.
     </p>
     <p>
      The names that are celebrated on the certain saint's day are all the names that correspond to the respective name and all the derivative names. For example, if there are different versions of the same name in different languages (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">
       John
      </a>
      ), i.e. different versions in
      <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
       Slavic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
       Romance
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
       Germanic
      </a>
      or other language groups, all the respective names are celebrated.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">
       Czech Republic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_the_Czech_Republic" title="Name days in the Czech Republic">
       Name days in the Czech Republic
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the Czech Republic, each day of the year corresponds to a personal name (or several names). People celebrate their name day (
      <i>
       svtek
      </i>
      or more formally
      <i>
       jmeniny
      </i>
      ) on the date corresponding to their own given name.
     </p>
     <p>
      Name days are commonly of less importance than birthdays to Czech people. However, name day celebrations can be, and often are, held together with friends or co-workers of the same name and in this way it can grow in size and importance.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the past, by law, parents were not allowed to choose just any name for a child. This has changed, although it is still common to choose the name from the name day "calendar" and any highly unusual name has to be approved by a special office. The original list was the Roman Catholic calendar of saints, but changes have been made to reflect the present-day usage of names.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">
       Finland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Finland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.nordicnames.de/wiki/Finnish_Name_Days" rel="nofollow">
       Finnish_Name_Days
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      Finns celebrate their name days (Finnish
      <i>
       nimipiv
      </i>
      , Swedish
      <i>
       namnsdag
      </i>
      ) according to their
      <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">
       given name
      </a>
      on the date given by the calendar published by the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">
       University of Helsinki
      </a>
      Almanac Office (
      <i>
       Almanakkatoimisto
      </i>
      ). Every day except
      <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">
       New Year's Day
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" title="Christmas Day">
       Christmas Day
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/February_29" title="February 29">
       February 29
      </a>
      is a name day. For each day there are names in both Finnish and Swedish; the names are frequently, but not always,
      <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
       cognates
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Women are slightly underrepresented in the calendar: approximately 45 percent of name days celebrate only women while some 49 per cent are name days of men. The rest are those of names that may be given to either sex, such as Rauni (July 15), or have both a man's and a woman's name, such as Oliver and Olivia (May 29).
     </p>
     <p>
      Many traditional beliefs attach to various name days, especially involving the weather and the appropriate times to perform seasonal agricultural tasks such as planting some particular crop. For example there is a saying that "Jaakko (James) casts a cold rock into the water", meaning that on Jaakko's day, the 25th of July, the waters start getting colder, which is not far from true on average
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . The seven days from July 18 to 24th, being all women's name days, are known as the women's week. It is popularly believed to be an especially rainy week, and this is to some extent supported by statistics, as late July and early August are the rainiest time of the year in Finland
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Almanac Office reviews the lists at intervals of 510 years, adding new names as they gain popularity and striking others that have faded into disuse. The university owns the copyright to the lists of names and their corresponding dates.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Orthodox_Church" title="Finnish Orthodox Church">
       Finnish Orthodox Church
      </a>
      has its own calendar of name days, corresponding to the feasts of Orthodox
      <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">
       saints
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Greece">
       Greece
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Greece">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Greece" title="Name days in Greece">
       Name days in Greece
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
       Greece
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">
       Cyprus
      </a>
      , a name day (
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      :  ,
      <i>
       onomastik eort
      </i>
      , or ,
      <i>
       yiort
      </i>
      , "feast") is celebrated in a similar way to a
      <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">
       birthday
      </a>
      except of expected differences (e.g. no
      <a href="/wiki/Birthday_cake" title="Birthday cake">
       birthday cake
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gr_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gr-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is a strong Greek tradition since antiquity for newborn children to be
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_name" title="Greek name">
       named
      </a>
      after one of their grandparents. This results in a continuation of names in the family line.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">
       Greek Orthodox Church
      </a>
      , every day of the year is dedicated to the memory of at least one (usually more than one)
      <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">
       saint
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">
       martyr
      </a>
      . If someone is named after a saint, then there is a big celebration on his or her name day. In Greece and Cyprus many names derive from long pagan tradition (Greek antiquity), and there may not be a Christian saint by the same name. In such a case the person is said "not to have" a name day, or they may choose to celebrate on
      <a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Day" title="All Saints' Day">
       All Saints' Day
      </a>
      . The vast majority of name days are on the
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Greece" title="Name days in Greece">
       same date every year
      </a>
      ; the few exceptions are names directly or indirectly associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
       Easter
      </a>
      , and are floating. This facilitates social interaction, as all
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek language
      </a>
      calendars include detailed name day lists. Some name days coincide with major Christian feasts, for example people whose names are
      <i>
       Chrstos
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Christine
      </i>
      have their name day on
      <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">
       Christmas
      </a>
      , people named after
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Basil" title="St. Basil">
       St. Basil
      </a>
      have their name day on New Year's Day,
      <i>
       Anastsios
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Anastasa
      </i>
      on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" title="Easter Sunday">
       Easter Sunday
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Mara
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Mrios
      </i>
      on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dormition_of_the_Theotokos" title="Dormition of the Theotokos">
       the Dormition
      </a>
      or on
      <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_Mary" title="Presentation of Mary">
       the Presentation
      </a>
      , etc.
     </p>
     <p>
      The traditional format of a name day celebration is an open house: once a family or person may choose to celebrate with invited guests (at home, at a restaurant, a bar or a club) if at all (e.g. following a recent bereavement) all well-wishers may be welcomed. Children celebrate their birthdays and name days equally festively; as the person grows up the emphasis may shift decisively. Entertainment provided by the celebrating host may include a meal, drinks, desserts, music,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partying" title="Partying">
       partying
      </a>
      , etc. instead of the guests fussing over the celebrant. Gifts are expected from the guests although they may be something as trivial as a
      <a href="/wiki/Greeting_card" title="Greeting card">
       greeting card
      </a>
      or a few flowers. Optionally,
      <a href="/wiki/Allowance_(money)#Allowances_for_children" title="Allowance (money)">
       pocket money
      </a>
      may be given by an adult relative or a
      <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">
       godparent
      </a>
      to a celebrant child or teenager instead of a gift. In cases where birthdays and name days are close to each other the celebrations are best merged. It is also common to shift a name day celebration to a more convenient day, e.g. the following Friday or weekend.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">
       Hungary
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hungary">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Hungary" title="Name days in Hungary">

      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Name days (in
      <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
       Hungarian
      </a>
      : nvnap) in
      <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">
       Hungary
      </a>
      are very popular, often as much as a person's actual birthdate. A woman is typically given
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowers" title="Flowers">
       flowers
      </a>
      on her name day by acquaintances, including in the workplace, and the price of flowers often rises around the dates of popular names because of demand. A bottle of alcohol is a common gift for men on their name day. Children frequently bring sweets to school to celebrate their name days. Name days are more often celebrated than birthdays in workplaces, presumably because it is simpler to know the date since most calendars contain a list of name days. You can also find the name day on daily newspapers by the date and on Hungarian websites. Some highly popular names have several name days; in that case, the person chooses on which day he or she wishes to celebrate. The list of the name days is, as usual in name day celebrating cultures, based on the traditional Catholic saints' feasts, but the link of the secular name days calendar to the Catholic calendar is not maintained any more. For example, even religious Catholic people named Gergely (Gregory) after Pope Gregory the Great still celebrate their name days on 12 March, although the Church moved the feast of that saint to 3 September in 1969.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Latvia">
       Latvia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Latvia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">
       Latvia
      </a>
      , name days (in
      <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">
       Latvian
      </a>
      "vrda dienas") are settled on certain
      <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">
       dates
      </a>
      ; each day (except for February 29 in
      <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">
       leap year
      </a>
      ) is a name day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Usually
      <a href="/wiki/Latvians" title="Latvians">
       Latvian
      </a>
      calendars list up to four names each day  around 1,000 names a year. Recently an extended calendar with around 5,000 names was published, and there are also a few extended calendars found on the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
       Internet
      </a>
      listing names even on February 29. February 29 is a popular date to celebrate name days of people who do not have a name day; another such date is May 22. People who do not have name days in ordinary calendars can enjoy many variations when to celebrate  on February 29 or May 22 and, if they have their name in an extended calendar or in the church calendar, on the date listed there (so in leap year such a person can choose from 2 to 4 dates when to celebrate). The Latvian name days calendar is updated at one or two-year intervals; anyone can suggest a name for the calendar, usually by sending an application to the State Language Centre ("Valsts valodas centrs").
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celebration_(party)" title="Celebration (party)">
       Celebrations
      </a>
      are very much like birthday celebrations. It is popular to celebrate name days in one's workplace  usually the one that has a name day prepares snacks for well-wishers, and during the day colleagues arrive one after another with flowers, sweets and small presents to greet him. Sometimes, especially in smaller companies, a certain time is set for the main celebrations. It is normal to come to a name day celebration without an invitation. At school one is expected to arrive with candy for classmates and teachers. Celebrating name days at home is similar to celebrating a birthday, although it may vary depending on the period of time between one's birthday and name day; usually one will eat cake with household members and receive presents.
     </p>

       </i>
      </span>
      ) have enjoyed a celebratory emphasis greater than that of birthday celebrations in Poland (source?). However, birthday celebrations are increasingly popular and important, particularly among the young.
      <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl">
       <i>
        Imieniny
       </i>
      </span>
      involve the gathering and socializing of friends and family at the celebrant's home, as well as the giving of gifts and flowers at home and elsewhere, such as at the workplace. Local calendars often contain the names celebrated on a given day.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Macedonia">
       Macedonia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Macedonia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Name-days () in
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">
       Macedonia
      </a>
      are celebrated throughout the history of this country. It has some similarities with the other Balkan countries but there are some name-days unique for the country. The name-days are scheduled according to the Christian Orthodox church following the Julian calendar. Each month there are several name-days which are celebrated by the people with the same name. Some of the name-days that are more significant to the history and culture are non-working days for the whole country. At these days everyone's invited or would like to say     ("Whoever comes  is welcomed") at the topic language. There is no need for bringing presents but if you like you can bring some (usually wine or something symbolic). You salute the celebrant by saying "Let your name lasts forever" (    ) or "For years to come" (  ). The most celebrated name-days in the Republic of Macedonia are St. Stefan (January, 9th), Bogojavlenie (January, 19), St, Jovan (January, 20), Blagovec (April, 7), Gjurgjovden (May, 06), Ss. Cyril and Methodius (May, 24), St. Kostadin and Elena (June, 03), St. Petar (July, 12), St. Pavle (July, 13), St.Dimitar (November, 8th)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">
       Romania
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Romania">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Name days (onomastica) in Romania are associated with the Orthodox saints celebrations. Name days are almost as important as birthdays, and only those who have the name of that particular saint get celebrated on that day. Some of the most important celebration days are: January, 7  Sf. Ioan (St. John), April 23  Sf. Gheorghe ( St.George), June 29  Sf. Petru si Pavel (St. Peter and Paul), August 15 and September 8  Sf. Maria (St. Mary), November 8  Sf. Mihail si Gavril (St, Michael and Gabriel), November, 30  Sf. Andrei (St. Andrew).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">
       Russia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Russia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      , name days (
      <i>
        (imeniny)
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      ) are celebrated apart from birthdays. Some calendars contain name days, but usually one must buy a special name day calendar. Celebrations range from cards and flowers to full-blown celebrations similar to birthday parties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rus_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rus-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Such a celebration begins with attendance at the divine services marking that day (in the Russian tradition, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-Night_Vigil" title="All-Night Vigil">
       All-Night Vigil
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">
       Divine Liturgy
      </a>
      ), and usually with a festive party thereafter. Before the
      <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">
       October Revolution
      </a>
      , the name day was considered as important as, or more important than, the celebration of the birthday. The rationale for this importance is that one's baptism is the event by which they are "born anew" in Christ. In modern times, however, few people know the traditions associated with it.
     </p>
     <p>
      In royal tradition, gifts would be given, such as a diamond or a pearl.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the most popular references of a Russian name day is the entire first act of
      <a href="/wiki/Anton_Chekhov" title="Anton Chekhov">
       Anton Chekhov
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Three_Sisters_(play)" title="Three Sisters (play)">
        Three Sisters
       </a>
      </i>
      , where Irina is celebrating her name day.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another literary depiction of a formal Russian name day ceremony occurs in
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pushkin" title="Alexander Pushkin">
       Alexander Pushkin
      </a>
      's "Eugene Onegin" where Tatiana's name day is celebrated. Name days are also mentioned in
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">
       Leo Tolstoy
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/War_and_Peace" title="War and Peace">
       War and Peace
      </a>
      , such as Book I, chapter 7 where both mother and youngest daughter of the Rostov family are celebrating the same name day of Natalya.
     </p>
     <p>
      Note: although name day (""/"imeniny") celebration is not as popular as birthday celebration, the Russian word for a birthday (" "/"djen' rozhdenia") person is still ""/"imeninnik" (a person whose name day is being celebrated).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavia">
       Scandinavia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Scandinavia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">
       medieval
      </a>
      times name days were of little significance in the
      <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">
       Nordic countries
      </a>
      , except for the celebration of
      <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">
       patron saints
      </a>
      for various guilds. A more widespread celebration of name days began in the 17th century in
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      , at first in the royal court and among aristocracy, but successively also among the general population. The
      <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Sweden" title="Church of Sweden">
       Church of Sweden
      </a>
      promoted celebration of name days over birthdays, as the latter was seen as a pagan tradition. Although the name day tradition never gained similar popularity in
      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
       Norway
      </a>
      , it is occasionally celebrated.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Slovakia">
       Slovakia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Slovakia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Slovakia" title="Name days in Slovakia">
       Name days in Slovakia
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In Slovakia name days (
      <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
       Slovak
      </a>
      : meniny) are widely celebrated. Name days are more often celebrated than birthdays in workplaces, presumably because it is simpler to know the date since most calendars contain a list of name days. You can also find the name day in the header of daily newspapers. Celebrations in elementary schools are different from those within the family as the celebrant gives candies to his or her classmates. Within the family, birthday-like celebrations are often held with cakes, presents and toasts. Flowers are sometimes sold out for popular name days. In the past, by law, parents were not allowed to choose just any name for their child. This has changed, although it is still common to choose the name from the nameday list in the calendar. The original list was the Roman Catholic calendar of saints, but changes have been made to reflect the present-day usage of names.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Slovenia">
       Slovenia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Slovenia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Name days (called
      <i>
       god
      </i>
      , n., m., sing.) were widely celebrated, and preferred over
      <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">
       birthday
      </a>
      celebrations, until after
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      and the advent of
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       Communism
      </a>
      . In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural" title="Rural">
       rural
      </a>
      areas as well as among certain strata of town people the custom of celebrating name days lasted longer. Nowadays, while the tradition has not been completely obliterated, name days are celebrated mostly among older people.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spain_and_Latin_America">
       Spain and Latin America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Spain and Latin America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Until recently,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="The text preceding this tag needs specification. (March 2009)">
          specify
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      name days in Spain and Latin America (called
      <i>
       onomsticos
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       da de mi/su santo
      </i>
      ) were widely celebrated.
      <i>
       Onomsticos
      </i>
      are not limited to saints but also include the celebration days of the different representations of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" title="Virgin Mary">
       Virgin Mary
      </a>
      . For example, the name day of a woman named Carmen would be July 16, day of
      <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Mount_Carmel" title="Our Lady of Mount Carmel">
       Our Lady of Mount Carmel
      </a>
      . Currently,
      <i>
       onomsticos
      </i>
      are still remembered in more traditional families but are not generally celebrated with festive parties and presents as they were in the past. To celebrate name days, practicing Catholics typically attend mass and have some intimate family celebration.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sweden">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2811" data-file-width="3511" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg/220px-Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg/330px-Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg/440px-Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         A Day of Celebration
        </i>
        . A painting by Swedish artist
        <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Brate" title="Fanny Brate">
         Fanny Brate
        </a>
        depicting preparations for a name day celebration. Oil on canvas, 1902.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      From the 18th century onwards, names used by the royal family were introduced to the Swedish list of name days, followed by other common names. In 1901 a comprehensive modernization was made to make the list up to date with current names. The monopoly on almanacs, held by the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">
       Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences
      </a>
      , expired in 1972 and so did the official name day list. Competing name day lists began to emerge but the official list was still in general use until 1986 when consensus of a new list with three names on each day was reached. This list was revised in 1993 and reduced to two names on each day. However, widespread dissatisfaction with the list prompted the
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Academy" title="Swedish Academy">
       Swedish Academy
      </a>
      to compile a new two-name list which was finally accepted and brought into use in 2001. Although it does not have the official status of the 1901 or older lists, it is now universally used in Sweden.
     </p>
