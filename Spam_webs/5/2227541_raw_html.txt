     <p>
      A
      <b>
       smokejumper
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildland_firefighter" title="Wildland firefighter">
       wildland firefighter
      </a>
      who
      <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">
       parachutes
      </a>
      into a remote area to combat
      <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">
       wildfires
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Smokejumpers are most often deployed to fires that are extremely remote. The risks associated with this method of personnel deployment are mitigated by a training program that has developed over more than 70 years. Smokejumpers are capable of reaching a wildfire shortly after ignition when it is still relatively small and extinguishing the blaze before it becomes a problem to land managers and the public. When there is no significant fire activity, smokejumpers can also be used for outside fire suppression in work assignments such as forestry, disaster relief and emergency management.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Smokejumpers_worldwide">
       Smokejumpers worldwide
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smokejumper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Smokejumpers worldwide">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Smokejumpers are employed in large numbers by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">
       Russian Federation
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service" title="United States Forest Service">
       United States Forest Service
      </a>
      . Russia maintains more smokejumpers than any other nation in the world (several thousand) and claims the longest history of established smokejumping of any nation (reportedly established in 1936; smokejumping in the United States was established in 1939).
     </p>

        </a>
        , 1948
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Prior to the full establishment of smokejumping, experiments with parachute insertion of firefighters were conducted in 1934 in Utah and in the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      . Earlier aviation firefighting experiments had been conducted with air delivery of equipment and "water bombs." Although this first experiment was not pursued, another began in 1939 in Washington's Methow Valley, where professional parachutists jumped into a variety of timber and mountainous terrain, proving the feasibility of the idea. This also saw the first Forest Service employee jumper,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Lufkin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francis Lufkin (page does not exist)">
       Francis Lufkin
      </a>
      , who was originally hired as a climber to extract the professional parachutists from the trees. It is believed that he made this first jump on a dare from the parachutists.
     </p>
     <p>
      The following year, in 1940, permanent jump operations were established at
      <a href="/wiki/Winthrop,_Washington" title="Winthrop, Washington">
       Winthrop, Washington
      </a>
      and Ninemile Camp, an abandoned
      <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">
       Civilian Conservation Corps
      </a>
      camp (Camp Menard) located a mile north of the Forest Service's Ninemile Remount Depot (pack
      <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">
       mule
      </a>
      ) at
      <a href="/wiki/Huson,_Montana" title="Huson, Montana">
       Huson, Montana
      </a>
      , about thirty miles northwest of
      <a href="/wiki/Missoula,_Montana" title="Missoula, Montana">
       Missoula
      </a>
      . The first actual fire jumps in the history of smokejumping were made by Rufus Robinson and
      <a href="/wiki/Earl_Cooley_(smokejumper)" title="Earl Cooley (smokejumper)">
       Earl Cooley
      </a>
      at Rock Pillar near Marten Creek in the
      <a href="/wiki/Nez_Perce_National_Forest" title="Nez Perce National Forest">
       Nez Perce National Forest
      </a>
      on July 12, 1940, out of Ninemile, followed shortly by a two-man fire jump out of Winthrop. In subsequent years, the Ninemile Camp operation moved to Missoula, where it became the Missoula Smokejumper Base. The Winthrop operation remained at its original location, as North Cascades Smokejumper Base. The "birthplace" of smokejumping continues to be debated between these two bases, the argument having persisted at this time for approximately 70 years.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first smoke jumper training camp was located at the
      <a href="/wiki/Seeley_Lake,_Montana" title="Seeley Lake, Montana">
       Seeley Lake Ranger Station
      </a>
      , over sixty miles northeast of Missoula. Later the training relocated to Camp Menard in July 1943. Here, when not fighting fires, the men spent much time putting up hay to feed the hundreds of pack mules that carried supplies and equipment to guard stations and fire locations. In order to work fires, men organized into squads of eight to fifteen were stationed at six strategic points, also known as "spike camps": Seeley Lake, Big Prairie and Ninemile in Montana; Moose Creek and McCall in Idaho; and Redwood Ranger Station in southwestern Oregon at the edge of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_Junction" title="Cave Junction">
       Cave Junction
      </a>
      . The men worked from other spike camps as well, including some in Washington State.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_the_military">
       Relations with the military
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smokejumper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relations with the military">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After observing smokejumper training methods at Seeley Lake in June 1940, then-Major
      <a href="/wiki/William_C._Lee" title="William C. Lee">
       William C. Lee
      </a>
      of the U.S. Army went on to become a major general and establish the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/101st_Airborne_Division_(United_States)" title="101st Airborne Division (United States)">
       101st Airborne Division
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In May 1978, members of the Army National Guard's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19th_Special_Forces_Group_(United_States)" title="19th Special Forces Group (United States)">
       19th Special Forces Group
      </a>
      (Airborne) and other Western military units briefly began airborne training at the Missoula Smokejumper School. Although in years past the Army has conducted basic Airborne training at various locations, it has since been consolidated at
      <a href="/wiki/Fort_Benning" title="Fort Benning">
       Fort Benning
      </a>
      , Georgia.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_and_post-war">
       World War II and post-war
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smokejumper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II and post-war">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Approximately 240 workers from
      <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Public_Service" title="Civilian Public Service">
       Civilian Public Service
      </a>
      camps, mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">
       conscientious objector
      </a>
      draftees from historic
      <a href="/wiki/Peace_churches" title="Peace churches">
       peace churches
      </a>
      , worked as smokejumpers during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . An initial group of 15 men began training in parachute rigging in May 1943 at Seeley Lake, and a total of 33 completed jump training in the middle of June, followed by two weeks of intense training in fire ground control and first aid. Approximately 500 training jumps were made by the first 70 CPS smokejumpers in 1943, who went on to fight 31 fires that first season. In 1944 their number increased to 110, and in 1945 to 220, as more equipment became available from the War Department. 29 jumpers battled the remote Bell Lake fire in September 1944, among 70 fires suppressed that year, and 179 were fought in the Missoula Region alone by September 1945, with other jumpers assigned to McCall and Cave Junction. The last CPS smokejumper left the service in January 1946.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/555th_Parachute_Infantry_Battalion_(United_States)" title="555th Parachute Infantry Battalion (United States)">
       555th Parachute Infantry Battalion
      </a>
      gained fame as the only entirely black airborne unit in United States Army history. The 555th was purportedly not sent to combat because of segregation within the military during World War II; however, in May 1945, it was sent to the west coast of the United States to combat forest fires ignited by
      <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">
       Japanese
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_balloon" title="Fire balloon">
       balloons
      </a>
      carrying
      <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">
       incendiary devices
      </a>
      , an operation designated Operation Firefly. Although this potentially serious threat did not fully materialize, the 555th fought numerous other forest fires while there. Stationed at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pendleton_Field" title="Pendleton Field">
       Pendleton Field
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      , with a detachment in
      <a href="/wiki/Chico,_California" title="Chico, California">
       Chico, California
      </a>
      , 300 unit members participated in dangerous firefighting missions throughout the Pacific Northwest during the summer and fall of 1945, earning the nickname "Smoke Jumpers". The 555th made a total of 1200 jumps to 36 fires, 19 from Pendleton and 17 from Chico.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the interim the Smokejumper Project had become a permanent establishment of the USFS in 1944. In 1946 the Missoula Region had 164 smokejumpers, 84% of them military veterans, many of them also college students, who eventually constituted 40% of the smokejumper ranks in the post-war years. New bases were opened in
      <a href="/wiki/Grangeville,_Idaho" title="Grangeville, Idaho">
       Grangeville, Idaho
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/West_Yellowstone,_Montana" title="West Yellowstone, Montana">
       West Yellowstone, Montana
      </a>
      . Most smokejumpers of the era were not career professionals but seasonal employees lured by the prospect of earning as much as $1,000 over a summer.
     </p>
     <p>
      They were assigned jobs from a rotational list, for which there was keen competition. They tended to be well-behaved, self-motivated, and responsible, since breaches of discipline meant loss of one's spot on the rotation, and a violation of law resulted in dismissal from the project altogether. As a result, they were not expert firefighters, but were well-trained in the skills of preventing small fires from becoming large ones, and often worked minor fires in pairs. Squad-sized and larger crews were supervised by foremen who were career USFS firefighters and experts in all types of wildfires.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mann_Gulch_Fire">
       Mann Gulch Fire
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Smokejumper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mann Gulch Fire">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The largest disaster involving smokejumper deaths on the job was the
      <a href="/wiki/Mann_Gulch_fire" title="Mann Gulch fire">
       Mann Gulch fire
      </a>
      blowup of 1949 which occurred north of
      <a href="/wiki/Helena,_Montana" title="Helena, Montana">
       Helena, Montana
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Gates_of_the_Mountains_Wilderness" title="Gates of the Mountains Wilderness">
       Gates of the Mountains
      </a>
      area along the Missouri River. Thirteen firefighters died during the blowup, 12 of them jumpers. This disaster directly led to the establishment of modern safety standards used by all wildland firefighters. Noted author
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_MacLean" title="Norman MacLean">
       Norman MacLean
      </a>
      , described the incident in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Young_Men_and_Fire" title="Young Men and Fire">
        Young Men and Fire
       </a>
      </i>
      (1992).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_record">
       Safety record
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      Despite the seemingly dangerous nature of the job, fatalities from jumping are rare. The best known fatalities in the United States being those that occurred at the Mann Gulch Fire in 1949, and the
      <a href="/wiki/South_Canyon_Fire" title="South Canyon Fire">
       South Canyon Fire
      </a>
      in 1994.
     </p>
     <p>
      Statistically, smokejumping remains as safe as ground-based wildland firefighting as a whole
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . Although jump injuries do occur, they are not frequent, and smokejumper personnel take deliberate precautions before deciding whether to jump a particular fire. Multiple factors are analyzed, and then a decision is made as to whether it is safe to jump the fire or it is unsafe. Bases tend to look for highly motivated individuals who are in superior shape and have the ability to think independently and react to changing environments rapidly
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . Because of their fire experience and physical conditioning, most
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotshot_crew" title="Hotshot crew">
       Hotshots
      </a>
      make good smokejumpers. Smokejumping is not a crew-based firefighting tactic, and it takes time for firefighters who have been entrenched within the "crew mentality" to break free and think independently.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is argued that smokejumper operations are expensive to maintain and marginally effective; however, the comparable cost is that of many more helicopter deployments
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . The range of smokejumper aircraft is greater than a helicopter, the speed is greater than a helicopter, and the payload is greater also. In a realistic assessment, the two delivery systems bring different advantages and disadvantages, as both the primary vehicles and delivery method (fast-roping vs. parachute) are so different in capability. A typical smokejumper mantra is "Speed, Range, Payload." Advocates of smokejumping
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2013)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      believe that, due to their extreme initial-attack function, smokejumping is one of the most  if not the most  cost-effective wildland firefighting method employed in the U.S. today.
     </p>
