       <h2>
        WINE: Obscure grapes take a stand against the New World order
       </h2>
       <div class="meta">
        by
        <a href="http://www.bdlive.co.za/opinion/staffprofiles/2012/08/03/michael-fridjhon">
         Michael Fridjhon
        </a>
        , 2014-07-04 07:52:16.0
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
      <div class="author-kindle" style="display: none;">
       Michael Fridjhon
      </div>
      <div class="article-page-title-kindle" style="display: none;">
       WINE: Obscure grapes take a stand against the New World order
      </div>
      <div class="article-page-content-kindle" style="display: none;">
       <p>
        IN THE days before vinous globalisation  in other words, back in the 1950s  wine producers worked with the grapes indigenous to their regions. The choice of cultivars had been made by generations of growers who had worked out what performed best in that particular location.
       </p>
       <p>
        To the extent that there was any refinement to the strategy, it was primarily in the choice of clones. In the major French vineyard areas the principle of massal selection was applied, thus ensuring a continuation of the range and variety of clones that had been present in previous generations. True, more commercial growers looked at the vines that consistently yielded the higher volumes (either because they were heavier bearers, or because they produced larger berries) and made their selection on this basis. Either way, each region had the varieties and the clones which had progressed through a Darwinian elimination process involving countless generations.
       </p>
       <p>
        What changed was the evolution of the New World as a wine-producing zone. The Americans took the lead. They turned to the premium French appellations not only for varieties but also for wine-making techniques. First the major cultivars  cabernet sauvignon, chardonnay and pinot noir  and then the fruit management strategies became part of Californian wine culture.
       </p>
       <p>
        Where the French applied extended maceration ahead of, or following, the fermentation, the Americans experimented along the same lines. Where new barrels were part of deluxe wine making in France (in those days mainly in Bordeaux, for the top growths, and in Burgundy for grand cru chardonnay), the Californians drew on their experience. With more enthusiasm and money to spend, and unhampered by tradition, wine makers in the US (and then in Australia and South Africa) discovered the flashiest ways to vinify the "noble varieties". By the 1980s cabernet, chardonnay, merlot and sauvignon blanc had pretty much invaded the worlds temperate zones, replacing cultivars which had flourished for centuries and had been an integral part of the identity of ancient wine-producing areas.
       </p>
       <p>
        Cabernet and merlot usurped sangiovese in parts of Tuscany, chardonnay and merlot replaced chenin blanc and cinsaut in South Africa, cabernet began to take over from shiraz and grenache in Australia. In no time a terrible sameness began to characterise commercial wine making in both the Old and New World.
       </p>
       <p>
        Languedoc and Roussillon in the south of France became an industrial landscape of high-yielding, trellised vineyards, supplanting low-yielding grenache, carignan, aramon, piquepoul, bourboulenc and macabeu bushvines. It was only a matter of time before the anarchic creativity of fringe wine makers rebelled against the tyranny of supermarket taste and sought to rescue some of these less well-known (and often less approachable) varieties from vinous obscurity.
       </p>
       <p>
        Were seeing a little of this in South Africa with cinsaut, once the most prolific of our reds and now down to under 2% of the national vineyard. Other than Eben Sadies impossible-to-find bottling, there is a superb current release from Waterkloof and a highly regarded wine from Stellenrust  but not much else. Carignan is just hanging in. So is clairette blanche. The great success story has been chenin blanc, rescued from the same fate as cinsaut and now one of the stand-out features of the Cape wine scene.         <p>
         IN THE days before vinous globalisation  in other words, back in the 1950s  wine producers worked with the grapes indigenous to their regions. The choice of cultivars had been made by generations of growers who had worked out what performed best in that particular location.
        </p>
        <p>
         To the extent that there was any refinement to the strategy, it was primarily in the choice of clones. In the major French vineyard areas the principle of massal selection was applied, thus ensuring a continuation of the range and variety of clones that had been present in previous generations. True, more commercial growers looked at the vines that consistently yielded the higher volumes (either because they were heavier bearers, or because they produced larger berries) and made their selection on this basis. Either way, each region had the varieties and the clones which had progressed through a Darwinian elimination process involving countless generations.
        </p>
        <p>
         What changed was the evolution of the New World as a wine-producing zone. The Americans took the lead. They turned to the premium French appellations not only for varieties but also for wine-making techniques. First the major cultivars  cabernet sauvignon, chardonnay and pinot noir  and then the fruit management strategies became part of Californian wine culture.
        </p>
        <p>
         Where the French applied extended maceration ahead of, or following, the fermentation, the Americans experimented along the same lines. Where new barrels were part of deluxe wine making in France (in those days mainly in Bordeaux, for the top growths, and in Burgundy for grand cru chardonnay), the Californians drew on their experience. With more enthusiasm and money to spend, and unhampered by tradition, wine makers in the US (and then in Australia and South Africa) discovered the flashiest ways to vinify the "noble varieties". By the 1980s cabernet, chardonnay, merlot and sauvignon blanc had pretty much invaded the worlds temperate zones, replacing cultivars which had flourished for centuries and had been an integral part of the identity of ancient wine-producing areas.
        </p>
        <p>
         Cabernet and merlot usurped sangiovese in parts of Tuscany, chardonnay and merlot replaced chenin blanc and cinsaut in South Africa, cabernet began to take over from shiraz and grenache in Australia. In no time a terrible sameness began to characterise commercial wine making in both the Old and New World.
        </p>
        <p>
         Languedoc and Roussillon in the south of France became an industrial landscape of high-yielding, trellised vineyards, supplanting low-yielding grenache, carignan, aramon, piquepoul, bourboulenc and macabeu bushvines. It was only a matter of time before the anarchic creativity of fringe wine makers rebelled against the tyranny of supermarket taste and sought to rescue some of these less well-known (and often less approachable) varieties from vinous obscurity.
        </p>
        <p>
         Were seeing a little of this in South Africa with cinsaut, once the most prolific of our reds and now down to under 2% of the national vineyard. Other than Eben Sadies impossible-to-find bottling, there is a superb current release from Waterkloof and a highly regarded wine from Stellenrust  but not much else. Carignan is just hanging in. So is clairette blanche. The great success story has been chenin blanc, rescued from the same fate as cinsaut and now one of the stand-out features of the Cape wine scene. Were also seeing some experimental attempts with more off-the-beaten-track international cultivars  really good nebbiolo at Steenberg, Idiom and Morgenster, durif at Fairview, sangiovese at Anura, Terra del Capo and Idiom, verdelho at Feiteras and trincadeira at Sjinn  to name but a few.
        </p>
        <p>
         This coincides with a swing towards a more southern European wine feel  a function of the popularity of the Swartland appellation, the tidal wave of shiraz (which made Rhne-style blends a mandatory escape route) and the recognition that many of our vineyards cannot be described as "cool climate". Add to the Italian, Portuguese and Spanish varieties the new plantings of mourvedre, grenache (red and white), marsanne, roussanne and viognier, and the future has "the past" written all over it.
        </p>
