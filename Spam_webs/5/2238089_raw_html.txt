                     </div>
                     So if you've been on Facebook sometime in the last fifty years or so, you've probably run across this little turd of a meme. I've personally seen it forty or fifty times, including one day where it decided to show up on my wall something like eight or nine times as my less literary minded friends bounced it between them with the online equivalent of "Hur Hur."
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I hate this meme.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <u>
                       Hate it.
                      </u>
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I hate it so bad. If hate were pebbles, my hate would be....um....a great big boulder. I hate it with the white hot fury of a billion supernovas in a synchronous explosion up my left nostril.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I want to travel back in time and find the father of the person who made this on the night before their conception and kick daddy in the nuts
                     <b>
                      so
                     </b>
                     hard that this person simply ceases to have ever existed in the time space continuum. No fading Polaroid and transparent hand. Just one kick....gone.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Mostly I hate the way people don't just have a giggle and move on. I could
                     <i>
                      almost
                     </i>
                     spare daddy's scroat from my snap kick of protodoom if people just posted it with a "Snerk" and moved on.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     But no.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     They insist on acting like this meme contains the wisdom of ages, that teachers really are like this, and that it isn't just sour grapes by someone who failed their Steinbeck paper. "Oh my god, this is so true!" or "ZOMG YES!!!"
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       1-
                      </span>
                     </b>
                     <b>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       Being proud of your ignorance isn't cool.
                      </span>
                     </b>
                     I get it. Literary analysis is hard. I understand. There's a reason that you have to take half a dozen college courses before you really start to get it. Drawing connections and thinking critically is not easy. Understanding meaning beyond the literal words on the page is a skill. And discerning multiple layers of meaning from combinations of twenty-six letters and fourteen pieces of punctuation is ...well it's almost like it could be a
                     <b>
                      whole
                     </b>
                     discipline. Symbolism, allegory, metaphor...that is some next level shit. When we learn to read with these things in mind whole new meaning opens up.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     And if you don't want to have that skill, that's fine. You don't have to. You can wonder why people who read a lot
                     <i>
                      really
                     </i>
                     like Toni Morrison and Margret Atwood. It's cool. But don't turn around and ignore the people who
                     <b>
                      do
                     </b>
                     have that skill, or pretend they are making stuff up. That's like saying evolution doesn't exist because
                     <u>
                      you
                     </u>
                     don't understand the evidence for it. Maybe instead of making stupid memes, you should be asking yourself what your English teacher knows that you don't.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-srrVTDpU7uE/UU4DiPBvK5I/AAAAAAAADVg/fgGP_EvoLFo/s1600/Willful.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="150" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-srrVTDpU7uE/UU4DiPBvK5I/AAAAAAAADVg/fgGP_EvoLFo/s200/Willful.jpg" width="200"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     Being ignorant is fine. No one can know everything--especially at the age where one is usually facing down English teachers. Staying ignorant is....less fine, but a choice. Maintaining willful ignorance and mocking the people who do not is a particular kind of idiotic. It's a little bit like looking at the pictures in a picture book when you can't read, deciding you know the story, and telling the literate person who is reading the text that they're just making shit up. No they're not. They have a skill you lack. And if you "learned to read" you'd be able to see it too.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       2- Art is considered.
                      </span>
                     </b>
                     <b style="font-size: x-large;">
                     </b>
                     Any book you're reading in an English class is most likely some of the best literature out there. It's predominantly dead white guys, and other voices are often just as good or better, but it's still pretty good writing. And like any art, literature is done with great care, consideration, and thought. It wasn't slapped out in a single draft on a Saturday night. Stephen King or Jim Butcher might do three or four drafts, but an author in the canon has probably done ten or more drafts at least, refining their language to fit their themes with each draft. I absolutelyguarantee that the writer thought carefully about practically every word. There is almost no chance anything in the entire work is not a conscious decision.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     And if you weren't embracing your ignorance, you would know the clues to look for and how to tell the difference between something that really is a coincidence and something the author is drawing attention to.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Of course that might mean admitting that there's something you don't know...
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <span style="font-size: large;">
                      <b>
                       3- Your English teacher isn't operating in a vacuum.
                      </b>
                     </span>
                     Your English teacher has things they need to cover and a limited amount of time. They have established lesson plans based on learning objectives that came from a curriculum generated by lots of other English teachers. They can't really just wax philosophically and go off on tangents forever (unless they suck), and they don't really have time to regale you with their every pet theory aboutminutiae or teach you something as complex as their Masters thesis. They're going to focus on what is generally agreed upon by wide bodies of scholars and literary analysis.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Your English teacher isn't just spitballing. Chances are they probably took the time to isolate the
                     <i>
                      most
                     </i>
                     important aspects of the work you're reading, choosing only what works well with the lesson they're trying to teach. Also, they probably picked out a few of the glaring examples--the best examples for the lesson at hand--not everything that could possibly fit.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     So you're getting the
                     <b>
                      most
                     </b>
                     obvious examples of the
                     <b>
                      most
                     </b>
                     elementary lessons. And if you weren't being a complete literalist dillhole, you might understand that. It's like someone trying to teach you algebra and you claiming that they're just "making up" their solutions to the variable.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     But by all means don't learn that skill. Just make up a meme. Reading comprehension isn't, like, an important skill or anything.
                     <br/>
                     <span style="font-size: large;">
                      <b>
                       <br/>
                      </b>
                     </span>
                     <b>
                      <span style="font-size: large;">
                       4- Books do not spontaneously generate.
                      </span>
                     </b>
                     <b style="font-size: x-large;">
                     </b>
                     Authors don't live on the moon, sending their books down by shuttle to be interpreted in a (wait for it....) vacuum. They have lives. They walk around on Terra Firma. They eat at restaurants. They have friends. And sometimes they even talk about their writing. Even the most antisocial writers have often done interviews, panels, and articles about their own work.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; text-align: right;">
                      <tbody>
                       <tr>
                        <td style="text-align: center;">
                         <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-ml-GwypmDlQ/UU4YE_Fl-UI/AAAAAAAADVo/1TzaVtHUh94/s1600/books.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;">
                          <img border="0" height="282" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-ml-GwypmDlQ/UU4YE_Fl-UI/AAAAAAAADVo/1TzaVtHUh94/s320/books.jpg" width="320"/>
                         </a>
                        </td>
                       </tr>
                       <tr>
                        <td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">
                         I just turned around and there they were!
                        </td>
                       </tr>
                      </tbody>
                     </table>
                     One of the ways we know what the author meant is BECAUSE THEY FUCKING
                     <b>
                      TELL
                     </b>
                     US!!
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     And even if we don't know
                     <b>
                      exactly
                     </b>
                     what the author meant about one particular symbol, we can extrapolate from what we do know about them. Raymond Carver may never have specifically discussed the fading light in "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love," but he did talk about how he tends to use light meaningfully in his stories, and we can apply that to his later pieces.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     We also know from talking to authors how carefully they choose their words when they write. Shakespeare may not have done a series of interviews for the BBC, but from what we do know, he pondered over every word, so we can assume any given word has been debated andconsciouslychosen.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <span style="font-size: large;">
                      <b>
                       5- Authorial intent hasn't mattered as much as reader response since about the sixties.
                      </b>
                     </span>
                     For over five decades, we've moved away from what the author
                     <i>
                      meant
                     </i>
                     as the authority of analysis. The author is one person. How a work resonates in a culture is much more interesting. In post-structuralism there is some incorporation of a work's context and that includes biographical analysis, but the interpretation of a work is as much (arguably more) in the hands of the reader than of any right or wrong interpretation. Anyone who has gone back to a favorite book years later and read a
                     <b>
                      COMPLETELY
                     </b>
                     different book is familiar with this.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     We are much more interested in how words arereceived rather than what was intended by them. One of the reasons "I didn't mean it like
                     <b>
                      that
                     </b>
                     " is a fairly unacceptable counter-argument for someone using "gay" or "bitch" as a pejorative. No one cares what they
                     <b>
                      meant.
                     </b>
                     We care about how those words resonate culturally. The same is true of art and literature. James Cameron didn't mean to make a recruiting poster movie for postcolonial theory, but he did. When we can more deeply understand art and how it resonates in the world around us, we open doors to communication.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Thus literary analysis can be something of aRorschachinkblot test for a group or culture (or a sub-culture if you are looking at it through intentionalist lenses like Critical Race Theory or Feminist Theory). An author may not have meant anything, but the fact that we see it is still worth exploring. Literary analysis is about critical thinking, not an absolute, objective cypher of authorial intent.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Of course to know what all that means, you might have to question your ignorance instead of making meme's that presume every English teacher in the world is just randomly making stuff up.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I know it hurts, but give it a try.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>

                             I think this is the first blog entry where I significantly disagree with the post's position. I don't disagree that some people subscribe to this meme due to ignorance, but I submit that there are other reasons that have nothing to do with ignorance.
                             <br/>
                             <br/>
                             For example, I know that sometimes the author meant that the curtains were fucking blue because I was the author and I meant that the curtains were fucking blue. That never seemed to stopped English teachers from trying to tell the class otherwise when they'd read homework examples to the class. As you point out, sometimes we perceive things differently from what the author intended and these perceptions have importance. If an English teacher perceives some deeper meaning than simply blue curtains, that has validity, but it doesn't change what I meant.
                             <br/>
                             <br/>
                             Other times, there's a good case to be made for multiple interpretations that preclude each other. For example, you might call out Steinbeck's use of A names and C names to denote good and evil in East of Eden or you might say he used them to denote ability to choose (C) and inability to choose (A).
                             <br/>

                             I have um, issues, with high school English teachers (college was far better), so I've got some baggage here. While college professors did return my appreciation for literature, high school and junior high school English teachers had me for much longer and did damage that I've allowed to last even to now.
                             <br/>
                             <br/>
                             I wonder if the people who pass the meme around in a celebration of ignorance (though I have my swayable doubts about that being most people's motivation) do so in reaction to their own poor experiences with English teachers in their high school and junior high school years.

                                   Good questions!
                                   <br/>
                                   <br/>
                                   1-It's tough to answer exactly what makes a piece of literature enter the canon.  It has to be literary--which is to say of a much higher quality than most other writing, but I also think things GET canonized when they are representative of movements or resonate particularly for the culture in which they were written.  Most of what we read in school are classics of one stripe or another.  They might be dry and boring (especially to a teenager) but they quality is very, very high.  What makes literature GOOD has to do with how the various elements work in tandem with the artistic vision of the work and the technical skill.  Consider how LeGuin uses sentence structures that reflect the mood of the moment or how Steinbeck's settings are symbolic representations of his conflicts.  You don't get so much of that in something like Harry Potter--which is mostly just a story.
                                   <br/>
                                   <br/>
                                   However, I think you're on to something when you point out that what emotionally inspires a bunch of M.A.'s who are setting curriculum and have studied literature for years is probably not the same thing that inspires kids in high school.  (Then again, it seems like the job of kids in high school is to be unemotional and unimpressed by anything adults like.)
                                   <br/>
                                   <br/>
                                   Mostly this literature is picked because it provides fecund soil for critical thinking while developing reading and writing skills.  You could do a book report of a Robert Asprin novel, but you aren't likely to find that the internal conflict of the main character is reflected symbolically in the setting.
                                   <br/>
                                   <br/>
                                   2- Reader Response is a literary analysis theory that is based on groups of people like culture or sub-cultures.  It's more of a cultural analysis tool and less of an "everyone's opinion is right" sort of sentiment.  It would be like if I used "gay" as a pejorative at a convention and then said "I don't mean it like THAT" if called on it.  Someone trying to explain it to me is probably not approaching me as an individual "reader," but rather they are saying "In this culture, this is what you are communicating."  This is why some stories really don't date well.  We know they were written before civil rights or the ERA, or something, but except for their historical value, we don't receive them well.
                                   <br/>
                                   <br/>
                                   In theory, no one's opinion IS better than any other, but thinking critically about literature is a skill, and people who are trained are going to be able to go deeper and make more astute and meaningful observations.  Chris Farley might say "That was awesome" about Prometheus but Chris Brecheen can write a huge article.  And Chris Englishdoctor can tie the thematic elements into philosophical and intellectual movements and point out how it is very much like the neo-sporamian movement of the late thirties (I'm making this up).  The opinion isn't BETTER.  But one person clearly understands the work more than the other.
                                   <br/>
                                   <br/>
                                   What is the value? Well, you know I'm the first person to admit that the ivory tower goes way too high, so pragmatically some of it breaks down.  It's critical thinking.  It teaches us to draw connections.  It gives us the tools to THINK about art, which is important when we think about what matters to us.  And for a high school student it's training in reading comprehension and writing skills.
