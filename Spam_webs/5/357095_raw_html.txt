       <p>
        <strong>
         Do you find yourself saying thanks a lot on social media? Ever thought
         <em>
          WHY
         </em>
         you do that?
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         In this article I am going to tell you to stop thanking people. Have I gone mad? Ill tell you why
        </strong>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <p>
        Ive been blogging for over two years now and its encouraging to see a lot of people sharing my articles. Some of these people share straight from my blog using one of the social networking share buttons, or from my RSS news feed in
        <a href="http://feedly.com/" title="Feedly">
         Feedly
        </a>
        or perhaps on the blogging network,
        <a href="http://triberr.com/" title="Triberr">
         Triberr
        </a>
        . I am extremely grateful to these people and I do try and respond to as many of the people who share my articles to say thank you.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          But why?
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Why do I say thank you on Twitter? A strange question, you ask?
       </p>
       <h3>
        No Thanks!
       </h3>
       <p>
        <a href="/assets/thanks-tweets.jpg" rel="lightbox[3219]" title="Stop Saying Thank You on Social Media &amp; Say Something Meaningful!">
         <img alt="Thanks Tweets" class="alignright size-medium-large wp-image-3224 img-polaroid" height="550" src="http://cdn.iag.me/assets/thanks-tweets-400x550.jpg" style="margin: 0px 0px 10px 20px;" width="400"/>
        </a>
        Being encouraging is part of my nature and I value politeness very highly. I always say please and thank you in real life, so surely its the same on social media?
       </p>
       <p>
        Well, kind of
       </p>
       <p>
        The thing is, sometimes a
        <em>
         thank you
        </em>
        can lose its value in the noise and sheer volume of posts.
       </p>
       <p>
        Do you find yourself replying, sending a direct message or commenting with the following?
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Thanks for following me. Looking forward to reading your tweets!
         </strong>
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Thanks for sharing :-)
         </strong>
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Thanks for the awesome RTs
         </strong>
        </li>
       </ol>
       <p>
        All of the above examples are very polite and encouraging, but what do they actually mean? Have you thought about the meaning of your thank you messages or are you just doing what every one else is doing?
       </p>
       <p>
        Dont get me wrong, I am doing exactly the same- but recently I was made to think about whether all this thanking was doing any good. I havemarketing consultant,
        <a href="http://www.businessesgrow.com/us/" title="Mark Schaefer">
         Mark Schaefer
        </a>
        to thank for that. Thanks, Mark (oops!)
       </p>
       <p>
        It was one of Marks blog posts entitled 
        <a href="http://www.businessesgrow.com/2013/08/15/why-i-stopped-saying-thank-you-on-the-social-web/" title="Why I stopped thanking people on the social web">
         Why I Stopped Thanking People on the Social web
        </a>
         that made me stop and think. Do read it- it will give you an idea of what I am referring to.
       </p>
       <p>
        In the article, Mark says that the tipping point came when someone tweeted him:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <em>
           You are too damn polite Stop thanking people, will you!
          </em>
         </strong>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Ouch.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Mark then went on to say
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          I knew he had a point. I had reached the thank you tipping point. So I stopped.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          This saddened me. I hate it that the more popular you become on the social web, the less engaging you can be. Isnt that ironic? The very characteristic people appreciate is doomed over time. Authentic social media engagement is not scalable.
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        I think Mark makes an interesting point. Social media is about creating meaningful content or building meaningful relationships- and that includes
        <em>
         replies
        </em>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Its also about knowing who is in your
        <em>
         core community
        </em>
        and knowing why people are sharing your content in the first place.
       </p>
       <hr style="clear: both;"/>
       <h3>
        Translating Thanks
       </h3>
       <div style="float: right; margin: 0px 0px 10px 20px;">
        <a href="/assets/dictionaries.jpg" rel="lightbox[3219]" title="Stop Saying Thank You on Social Media &amp; Say Something Meaningful!">
         <img alt="dictionaries" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-3226 img-polaroid" height="199" src="http://cdn.iag.me/assets/dictionaries-300x199.jpg" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <br/>
        <small>
         Photo Credit:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/93416311@N00/4954528861/">
          Tim Green aka atoach
         </a>
         via
         <a href="http://compfight.com">
          Compfight
         </a>
         <a href="http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/2.0/">
          cc
         </a>
        </small>
       </div>
       <p>
        So, coming back to the above example thank you messages, lets do a bit of translating. Could it be that the above messages could mean the following?.
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Thanks for for following me. Looking forward to reading your tweets!
         </strong>
         <br/>
         TRANSLATION:
         <em>
          This is an automated message because I am too busy to send you ahand-craftedone. I might be interested in reading what you say although to be honest Iprobably dont have that much time to read any of them. I was hoping to reach 10,000 followers, and Im almost there. Basically I have no idea why I am tweeting you to say thank you for following me, but it seems like the polite thing to do.
         </em>
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Thanks for sharing :-)
         </strong>
         <br/>
         TRANSLATION:
         <em>
          I am genuinely thankful that you shared my article, however I dont have the time to properly engage with you, but I wanted to say thanks because thats the right thing to do- my mummy/mommy &amp; daddy taught me to be polite.
         </em>
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Thanks for the awesome RTs
         </strong>
         <br/>
         TRANSLATION:
         <em>
          Thanks for retweeting some of my tweets to your followers. I havent thought about it, but come to think about it, some of your followers may have seen my tweets in their timelines. Thats quite good really and it gives me a bit of exposure. Please do keep on doing it! I dont really talk to you much on Twitter, but I do like the fact that youre doing my marketing for me.
         </em>
        </li>
       </ol>
       <p>
        OK, please note that I had my tongue firmly in my cheek with the above, but I hope you got my point!
       </p>
       <hr style="clear: both;"/>
       <h3>
        Taking up the Opportunity
       </h3>
       <p>
        <img alt="Opportunity Center" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-3225 img-polaroid" height="300" src="http://cdn.iag.me/assets/opportunity.jpg" style="margin: 0px 20px 10px 0px;" width="400"/>
        Part of the issue that Mark Schaefer was referring to, was the lack of value of a thanks for sharing type of reply. Its not quite meaningless, but once you start sending out dozens of these a day, they are going to lose their value.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         <strong>
          How about doing something a little different?
         </strong>
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Instead of just thanking them, why not introduce them to someone else they might find interesting? How about reading one of their articles and giving some feedback? You wont necessarily be able to do that with all your replies, but you could create an opportunity and build upon the relationship.
       </p>
       <div style="clear: both;">
        <small>
         Photo Credit:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/20224904@N00/2340982008/">
          MikeLove
         </a>
         via
         <a href="http://compfight.com">
          Compfight
         </a>
         <a href="http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/2.0/">
          cc
         </a>
        </small>
       </div>
       <hr style="clear: both;"/>
       <h3>
        Be Encouraging!
       </h3>
       <div style="float: right; margin: 0px 0px 10px 20px;">
        <a href="/assets/friendship.jpg" rel="lightbox[3219]" title="Stop Saying Thank You on Social Media &amp; Say Something Meaningful!">
         <img alt="friendship" class="alignright size-medium-large wp-image-3228 img-polaroid" height="266" src="http://cdn.iag.me/assets/friendship-400x266.jpg" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <br/>
        <small>
         Photo Credit:
         <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/23094783@N03/4408159729/">
          eflon
         </a>
         via
         <a href="http://compfight.com">
          Compfight
         </a>
         <a href="http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/2.0/">
          cc
         </a>
        </small>
       </div>
       <p>
        Weve missed something though and it is a biggy
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Encouragement.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Most of us like to be encouraged. If we dont get any feedback it can be a bit depressing.
        <strong>
         <em>
          Words of Affirmation
         </em>
        </strong>
        is one of the
        <a href="http://www.5lovelanguages.com/" title="5 Love Languages">
         5 Love Languages
        </a>
        (read
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/The-Heart-5-Love-Languages/dp/1881273806/?tag=selectperform-20">
         the book
        </a>
        if you havent already). Each of us accept and give love and attention in different ways, but one that works well on the social web is
        <em>
         Words of Affirmation
        </em>
         encouragement.
       </p>
       <p>
        I love it when someone leaves a comment on my articles, but the truth is that most readers of blogs dont.
       </p>
       <p>
        Talk to most bloggers out there, and theyll tell you the same- they wish they had more comments. The same goes for Twitter, LinkedIn and Facebook. Its great when someone replies, mentions us or leaves a comment.
       </p>
       <p>
        However which would you prefer- 20 thank you for your article one line comments or 5 longer comments where they share their thoughts? We need to be encouraging but also add value to the conversation. Thats how it is in real life, so why should it be any different online?
       </p>
       <hr style="clear: both;"/>
       <h3>
        Thanks Replacement Ideas List
       </h3>
       <p>
        So what should we do instead? Im not saying we should never just say thank you, but in most cases you should always be looking to make your engagement more meaningful.
       </p>
       <p>
        So here is my thank you Replacement List.
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Ask them what they think
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Ask them what they thought of one aspect of the article. If your article was a list of tools or services, ask them what their favourite was. They may not have read the article, but your tweet may quickly remedy that!
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Let then know what you think
         </strong>
         <br/>
         If they have a website or blog, have a visit and read an article. Reply to let them know youve visited with some thoughts.
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Introduce them to someone new
         </strong>
         <br/>
         If youve not engaged with them before, check their profile. If relevant, you could introduce them to someone else in your core community they may find interesting. Being in touch with your community is important.
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Share a relevant article
         </strong>
         <br/>
         If relevant, share a similar article that you have written
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Ask them to subscribe
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Recommend they subscribe to your blog or email newsletter. Use Twitter Lead Generation Cards
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Be funny
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Tell a joke or share something funny- make their day!
        </li>
        <li>
         <strong>
          Encourage them
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Encourage them by sharing with them something you like or admire about them or a quality you see in them.
        </li>
       </ol>
       <hr style="clear: both;"/>
       <h3>
        Using Commun.it to help you
       </h3>
       <p>
        <em>
         Thats all very well
        </em>
        , you say,
        <em>
         but I dont have enough time!.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        I understand- I have the same issue. However thats why spending time thanking everyone without a strategy isnt going to help you!
       </p>
       <p>
        Firstly, make your own thank you replacement list and save it somewhere where you can easily access it. In order to make things easier, we are going to use a tool called
        <a class="thirstylink" href="/go/communit/" onclick="ga('send', 'event', 'Affliate Link', 'Commun.it', '/go/communit/')" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="Commun.it">
         Commun.it
        </a>
        * whichis a Twitter Relationship Management Tool. You can quickly respond to people who have shared your content. Commun.it segments people into influencers, supporters and engaged members. For more information see my
        <a href="/socialmedia/how-to-build-and-manage-your-twitter-community-with-commun-it/" title="Complete Guide on Using Commun.it">
         Complete Guide on Using Commun.it
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: center;">
        <img alt="communit" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3220 img-polaroid" height="287" src="http://cdn.iag.me/assets/communit1-540x287.jpg" width="540"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        With your
        <em>
         thank you replacement
        </em>
        list to hand, you can quickly go through your pending replies and mentions and actually engage much more meaningfully with the core people in your community. You may discover people who you havent engaged with before- people who you didnt realise were in your community. This is the power of Commun.it.
       </p>

        <em>
         <strong>

        and
        <em>
         <strong>
          high value members
         </strong>
        </em>

        <em>

        </li>

         (potential people to follow relating to certain keywords)
        </li>
       </ul>
