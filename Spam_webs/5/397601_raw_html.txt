     <p>
      The time had finally arrived!
     </p>
     <p>
      After spending many years toiling in grade school, taking tests and playing sports, trying to be taken seriously (and failing), struggling to survive with only the legal minimum of cool, I was finally at the pinnacle of my achievements! I was in my dorm, on my own, out of the house, readying for some serious education. There was about two weeks of socials and meetings scheduled for new arrivals before my first class, and I spent my time walking through the grand halls of the institutions of knowledge; and I could think of only one thing:
      <em>
       Tony Hawk!
      </em>
     </p>
     <p>
      You see, my roommate had a PlayStation. I wasn't much of a console gamer as a child, having only owned two versions of Nintendo, and a Sega Genesis, at sparing intervals of life. I did some computer gaming, too, but not excessively so (mostly Flight Simulator and Sim City). It wasn't really my thing, I was more of an outdoorsy, sporty kind of person. But this PlayStation came coupled with Tony Hawk 3, and for reasons I cannot explain with mere words, this game was the most awesome thing ever!
     </p>
     <p>
      In theory this game and console belonged to my roommate (for legal reasons, I won't mention him by name and will refer to him only as Ahuged Ruglover). But he was a hardcore partier, a drug dealer and a pothead, although for the sake of his future political ambitions I will pretend that he was just a skilled florist. Due to his flower-loving ways, he was basically never in the room. I think I saw him in human form only ten times my entire freshman year. Due to his extended absences, I spent an outrageous amount of time enjoying his personal possessions as if they were my own, especially the PlayStation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Initially, my love of Tony was casual; I played for a bit in the evening to unwind and nothing more. It just a small bit of fun. But then, in classical form, the itch of obsession began to appear, like a pimple, or a stroke. I started trying to top my previous night's score, which was initially quite easy - until I was suddenly good enough that I found myself unable to get much better. After a few nights in a row of roughly the same score, I started staying up later and later, trying harder and harder, drinking ever more Mountain Dew and coffee, desperate to become the best Tony Hawk player of all time. Soon I was focused on only one map (the airport), and was drawing my own maps and planning my strategy in exceptional detail. I bought strategy guides and started making phone calls (don't ask). By the end of my first week in the dormitory, I was no longer sleeping by any objective definition.
     </p>
     <p>
      I mean, I would technically "sleep" in the sense that I would pass out with the controller in my hand for about fifteen minutes just after the sun started to appear before waking up to eat breakfast, drink a gallon of coffee, and then resume playing. I'm not so sure it would, legally and medically, be considered genuine sleep (and I dreamt about the game anyway). I think my roommate made one appearance during this stretch, in between parties and flower shows. I vaguely remember him asking for "a turn," but I bit him and he was stoned out of his gourd on tulips so he ran away yelling something about vampires.
     </p>
     <p>
      The only thing that rescued me from the depths of pure and crippling obsession was the arrival of my first class. Despite the allure of the game gnawing at the back of my skull like a ravenous wolf, I was eventually pulled away by the educational reasons that I was, technically speaking, at the school for (I was not majoring in Tony Hawk-ology). I still played on occasion, and a few weekends were lost in a fog of grinding and spinning, but I resumed my normal sleep schedule and stopped injecting Mountain Dew directly into my shoulder.
     </p>
     <p>
      Up next: learnin'! Maybe...
     </p>
