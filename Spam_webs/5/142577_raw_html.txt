       <h2>
        How to Choose a Power Amplifier
       </h2>
       <p>
        What do you need in a power amplifier? The right amount of wattage, and the right features, thats what marks the right power amp for your live PA system. Sweetwaters Power Amplifier Buying Guide contains the information that you need to choose the perfect power amp for your needs. Since theres so much to consider when purchasing a power amp, dont hesitate to call and speak to one of our knowledgable Sales Engineers at
        <strong>
         (800) 222-4700
        </strong>
        .
       </p>

        <li>
         <a href="#matching">
          Matching Amps to Speakers

         </a>

       </ul>
       <p>
        <a name="matching">
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <h2>
        Matching Amps to Speakers
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.sweetwater.com/insync/media/2013/06/power_amp_speaker.jpg">
         <img alt="power_amp_speaker" class="alignright size-full wp-image-30323" height="100" src="http://www.sweetwater.com/insync/media/2013/06/power_amp_speaker.jpg" width="46"/>
        </a>
        When youre matching a power amp to a PA speaker setup, a good rule of thumb is to pick an amplifier that can deliver power equal to twice the speakers program rating. This means a speaker with a nominal impedance of 8 ohms and a program power rating of 350 watts will require an amplifier that can produce 700 watts into an 8-ohm load. For a stereo pair of speakers, the amplifier should be rated at 700 watts per channel into 8 ohms. A quality professional loudspeaker can handle transient peaks above its power rating if they occur.
       </p>
       <p>
        Using an amp with some extra headroom will help assure that only clean, undistorted power gets to your speakers. Headroom is the difference between the normal operating level of an amplifier, and the maximum level that the amp can pass without distorting. Music has wide variations in dynamic range; without enough headroom, youll find your gear clipping (distorting) far too frequently! Some professional amplifiers are designed so they have additional headroom. These amps can cleanly reproduce transient peaks that exceed their rated power. In this case select a model with an output power rating equal to the program power rating of the speaker. Consult the amplifier manufacturer or owners manual to learn more.
       </p>
       <p>
        In some applications, such as critical listening in a studio environment, it is important to maintain peak transient capability. For these applications, use an amplifier that can deliver 6db (or four times as much) more power than the speakers program power rating.
       </p>
       <p>
        If budget restraints or legacy equipment force you to use an amplifier with less power, extreme care should be taken to see that the amplifier is not driven into clipping. It may surprise you to learn that low power can result in damage to your speaker or system.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a name="impedance">
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <h2>
        Impedance: Its All in the Ohms
       </h2>
       <p>
        Ohms are measures of resistance. Audio amplifiers are commonly designed to work with 4, 8 or 16 ohms of resistance, and optimum system performance can be obtained if the total ohm load of the loudspeakers is exactly correct for the amplifier. If the total loudspeaker impedance is too high, the power delivered to the loudspeakers will be reduced. If the total loudspeaker impedance is too low, the power delivered to the loudspeakers will be higher, which can overload your speakers and damage the amplifier. You can connect any amount of speakers to one amplifier provided that they are correctly wired and dont collectively fall below the specified output impedance of the amp.
       </p>
       <p>
        Multiple loudspeakers can be connected together. Dual speaker connections whether on an amplifier, a mixer/amplifier, or a speaker enclosure are all wired in parallel. The following general rule will help you match the impedance of PA speakers to power amplifiers for optimized performance (avoiding overloads and other issues). Dont worry, its an easy one to use and remember.
       </p>
       <p>
        Parallel:
        <br/>
        To keep life as simple as possible, most people put enclosures of the same impedance in a parallel circuit. If you do this its all just a matter of dividing that impedance by the number of speakers.
       </p>
       <p>
        Take the ohm rating for the speakers and divide by the number of speakers.
       </p>
       <p>
        If you have four speakers that are rated at 16 ohms, you would take 16/4 to get the overall rating of 4 ohms. (Similarly, two 8-ohm speakers in parallel = 8/2 = 4 ohms.)
       </p>
       <p>
        The following is a quick reference listing of some commonly used parallel loads: (Avoid the ones that go lower than output impedance rating of your power amp.)
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Two 16-ohm speakers = 8 ohm
        </li>
        <li>
         Two 8-ohm speakers = 4 ohm
        </li>
        <li>
         Two 4-ohm speakers = 2 ohm
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Three 16-ohm speakers = 5.33 ohm
        </li>
        <li>
         Three 8-ohm speakers = 2.67 ohm
        </li>
        <li>
         Three 4-ohm speakers = 1.3 ohm
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Four 16-ohm speakers = 4 ohm
        </li>
        <li>
         Four 8-ohm speakers = 2 ohm
        </li>
        <li>
         Four 4-ohm speakers = 1 ohm
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        <a name="class_d">
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <h2>
        Class D Amplifiers  Smaller, Cooler, Lighter
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.sweetwater.com/insync/media/2013/06/power_amp_classd.jpg">
         <img alt="power_amp_classd" class="alignright size-full wp-image-30326" height="42" src="http://www.sweetwater.com/insync/media/2013/06/power_amp_classd.jpg" width="150"/>
        </a>
        An amplifiers Class (A, AB, C, D, etc..) is determined by its circuit design. One of the most efficient and common classes in power amplifiers is Class D. Class D is a switching-amplifier design, sometimes mistakenly described as digital, that offers great sound quality in compact designs. These amps offer just as much power as traditional power amplifiers but are lighter, smaller, and run much cooler. The days of lugging big, heavy power amps are almost gone, thanks to Class D amplifiers.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a name="bridged">
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <h2>
        Operating an Amplifier in Bridged Mode
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.sweetwater.com/insync/media/2013/06/power_amp_bridged.jpg">
         <img alt="Bridged outputs" class="alignright size-full wp-image-30322" height="100" src="http://www.sweetwater.com/insync/media/2013/06/power_amp_bridged.jpg" width="91"/>
        </a>
        A method of configuring a two-channel amplifier so that the two channels can be ganged or bridged to be used together on one load. The purpose of this is to take a two-channel amp and create a larger single channel amp that can deliver more power. The result is more power to the speaker than would be possible from either channel alone. For example, a 100-watts-per-channel amp may output a single channel of 300 watts after bridging. Most modern amps have a special switch to enable mono bridge operation.
       </p>
       <p>
        Typically, amplifiers operating in bridged mode can only do so with speakers that have twice the impedance of the minimum rating load on the amp. For example, an amp rated at 4 ohms running in normal mode will generally require 8 ohms in bridged mode.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a name="limiter">
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <h2>
        What Does a Limiter Do?
       </h2>
       <p>
        Limiters are used for a variety of applications. A few: maximizing signal levels while preventing distortion, preventing overload in a signal chain, setting a maximum volume level to protect users of in-ear monitors, protecting speakers and amplifiers from clipping, and so on. Any time you want to establish a maximum gain setting and prevent signals from passing it, a limiter is your tool of choice!
       </p>
       <p>
        <a name="look_for">
        </a>
       </p>
       <h2>
        What to Look For
       </h2>
       <p>
        How do I choose the right amplifier power for my speaker system? When it comes to choosing a power amplifier there are a two important factors to consider.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Power
        </strong>
        <br/>
        Generally you should pick an amplifier that can deliver power equal to twice the speakers program/continuous power rating. This means that a speaker with a nominal impedance of 8 ohms and a program rating of 350 watts will require an amplifier that can produce 700 watts into an 8 ohm load. For a stereo pair of speakers, the amplifier should be rated at 700 watts per channel into 8 ohms.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Headroom
        </strong>
        <br/>
        Using an amp with some extra headroom will help assure that only clean, undistorted signal gets to your speakers. Headroom is the difference between the normal operating level of an amplifier, and the maximum level that the amp can pass without distorting. Music has wide variations in dynamic range; without enough headroom, youll find that your gear will clip and distort.
       </p>
       <p>
        Of course, with all of the options available, the most logical thing to do is call a Sweetwater Sales Engineer at
        <strong>
         (800) 222-4700
        </strong>
        to help you determine which power amplifier is best suited to your needs.
       </p>
