     <section id="article-section-1">
      <p>
       In this, our first truly social World Cup, certain images have branded themselves on the nation's consciousness with a viral indelibility: the
       <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/early-lead/wp/2014/06/04/world-cup-logo-is-a-great-big-ol-facepalm/">
        facepalming trophy
       </a>
       , the unfortunately pointed teeth of Luis Suarez, andmost unpredictablythe glorious, airborne dreadlocks of U.S. midfielder Kyle Beckerman as he belts across the pitch like a 21st-century Merovingian warrior. Whatever the USMNT does on Tuesday, Beckerman will return home from Brazil with legions of new fans, not entirely thanks to his skills on the field (the second search result for his name on Google right now after "kyle beckerman hair" is "kyle beckerman wife").
      </p>
      <p>
       This is all despite the fact that the word that most frequently seems to accompany Beckermans name in social-media mentions is "patchouli." The 32-year-old's hairwhich he first started growing into dreads as a teenageris arguably more talked about than his fearsome speed (he can reach upwards of 15 mph during a game), his successful passing rate (88.6 percent), and even his temerity when it comes to tackling (here's a nice gif of him
       <a href="http://giant.gfycat.com/GorgeousGiganticArrowcrab.gif">
        elbowing Portugal's Raul Meireles in the face
       </a>
       ). And he isn't alone. Cristiano Ronaldo, for example, sported three separate, unique hairstyles for his three games this tournament, while Brazil's Neymar recently summoned a stylist to Brazil's training camp so that he could have his bangs bleached before facing off against Mexico.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:68.2456140351%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/06/RTR3VXW5/15e67126f.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        Cristiano Ronaldo (Reuters)
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       With a tournament that brings a new generation of strutting, entitled peacocks every four years, its easy to get carried away on a wave of follicular curiosity/mirth, and to forget that the likes of Neymar and Bosnias
       <a href="http://sportsport.ba/ino_fudbal/zbog-zutih-kartona-ermin-bicakcic-ne-igra-protiv-bayerna/129215">
        Ermin Bicakcic
       </a>
       (the higher the hair, the closer to goal?) are merely following in the footsteps of the genuinely bizarre hairstyles that dribbled before them. I love people who are just discovering that footballers have stupid haircuts,
       <a href="https://twitter.com/mattspencedc/status/480854426052472833">
        tweeted
       </a>
       <em>
        The
       </em>
       <em>
        London Times
       </em>
       Matt Spence during the USA/Portugal game. In reality, it was ever thus, or at least it has been ever since 70s legend Kevin Keegan discovered the bubble perm.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:72.4561403509%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/06/AP729642381153/cdc359301.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        Kevin Keegan (AP)
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       In a sport that depends entirely on teamwork (which is why Ann Coulter isn't a fan), hair has always enabled soccer players to establish their own unique identities as individuals, often with ridiculous results. For the UEFA European cup in 1996, troubled England midfielder Paul Gascoigne memorably
       <a href="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/multimedia/archive/00266/1996_266367c.jpg">
        bleached the top half of his head
       </a>
       , leaving the bottom a mousy brown (it was emulated to varying wonky degrees of success by fans). Around the same time, Colombian midfielder Carlos Valderrama was a household name not so much for his mad skills as for a mop of extravagant orange ringlets that presumably made Richard Simmons jealous.
      </p>
      <aside class="pullquote instapaper_ignore">
       <span>
        "Players in other sports brand themselves with piercings and tattoos, but soccer is all about feet and heads."
       </span>
      </aside>
      <p>
       For many players, their hairstyle is the only feature that can be altered to give them uniqueness and character, says Dr. Vivian Diller, a psychologist who specializes in issues that relate to physical appearances. Theyre all wearing the same uniform, so its an opportunity to express themselves, and when theyre center stage, unusual styles can grab the attention of millions. Players in other sports take opportunities to brand themselves with piercings and tattoos, but soccer is all about feet and heads, since thats all that can touch the ball.
      </p>
      <p>
       In other words, Ronaldos constantly changing do is a canny attempt to get us to pay attention to him (as if we didnt already). Neymar, whose hair has run the gamut from mohawk to quiff to spiky mullet, is obviously just learning from the master.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:77.8947368421%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/06/RTR3VXUD/aa4c89c84.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        Neymar (Reuters)
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Going deeper into the psychology of hair in soccer is a fraught but fascinating enterprise. Consider Beckerman, whose appropriation of Rastafarian culture (his
       <a href="http://kylebeckerman.wordpress.com/">
        blog
       </a>
       has a picture of him in front of the colors of the Jamaican flag) could seem like a rejection of American values if it weren't for his gleeful, downright patriotic fouling on behalf of the United States. Ditto Neymar, whose blond locks carry a certain extra weight in Brazil, where pale skin and light hair are typically associated with wealth (the expression
       <em>
        o dinheiro embraquence
       </em>
       literally translates as
       <em>
        money whitens
       </em>
       ). Is Neymars hair aspirational any more than Beckermans hair is countercultural? (The latter also has a giant tattoo of a Native American chief on his right arm, so its possible hes just confused.)
      </p>
      <aside class="callout">
       <hr/>
       <h4>
        More on the World Cup
       </h4>
       <div>
        <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/06/the-sibling-dynamics-of-world-cup-teams/373361/">
         <img alt="" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/mt/2014/06/photo1-1/lead.jpg?n7qmrf"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/06/the-sibling-dynamics-of-world-cup-teams/373361/">
          The (Actual) Bros of the USMNT
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/06/welcome-to-the-emotional-torture-of-soccer-america/373224/">
          Welcome to the Exquisite Torture of Soccer, America
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/06/the-world-cup-is-here-its-videogames-never-left/373143/">
          The Best Cure for World Cup Heartbreak: Video Games
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2014/06/kiefer-sutherland-righteous-patriotic-chills/373562/">
          If Jack Bauer Told the Truth About America's World Cup Chances
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <hr/>
      </aside>
      <p>
       Few teams have such a broad divergence of styles as the U.S.s does. If hair is a creative outpost for soccer players with lots of money and far too much free time, Clint Dempseys crew cut signals that his burgeoning side gig as a rapper keeps him perfectly satisfied. Graham Zusis man bun, by contrast, combines the practical with the inspiredits long enough for him to tie it away from his face while on the pitch, but it also trails behind him like a magical unicorn mane when he sets it free. And though Tim Howard seems to have compensated for being trichologically challenged this tournament by growing a beard (a look we could label upside-down head if we were being unkind), it isnt outside the realms of possibility to conclude that the reason Michael Bradleys getting such a bad rap this month is because he has no hair at all.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a world where hair has long been associated with everything from religion to sexuality to mysticism to strength, perhaps the most interesting thing about the prevalence of eccentric hairstyles in soccer is how openly the players defy heteronormative standards of masculinity. Ever since a youthful David Beckham frosted his tips and stepped out wearing a sarongnext to a Spice Girl, no lesssoccer players have been liberated to experiment with hairdos that defy boundaries and social norms (and yes, sometimes gravity). It may not be much, but
       <span>
        at an event that doesn't have any openly gay soccer players
       </span>
       , Neymars golden locks, no matter how absurd, are something to cherish.
      </p>
     </section>

        <h3 class="hed">
         Why the Iran Deal Makes Obama's Critics So Angry
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/peter-beinart/" title="Peter Beinart">
          Peter Beinart
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         The nuclear agreement highlights the limits of American powersomething the presidents opponents wont accept.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Mankind faces a crossroads,
          <a href="http://www.skeptic.ca/Woody_Allen_Speech_to_Graduates.htm">
           declared
          </a>
          Woody Allen. One path leads to despair and utter hopelessness. The other, to total extinction. Let us pray we have the wisdom to choose correctly.
         </p>
         <p>
          The point is simple: In life, what matters most isnt how a decision compares to your ideal outcome. Its how it compares to
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/alternatives-obama-iran-nuclear-deal-israel/389751/">
           the alternative at hand
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The same is true for the Iran deal,
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-33518524">
           announced Tuesday
          </a>
          <span>
           between Iran and six world powers
          </span>
          . As Congress begins debating the agreement, its opponents have three real alternatives. The first is to kill the deal, and the
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/the-iran-nuclear-deal-by-the-numbers/389592/">
           interim agreement
          </a>
          that preceded it, and do nothing else, which means few restraints on Irans nuclear program. The second is war. But top American and Israeli officials have warned that military action against Iranian nuclear facilities could ignite a catastrophic regional conflict and would be ineffective, if not counterproductive, in delaying Irans path to the bomb. Meir Dagan, who oversaw the Iran file as head of Israels external spy agency, the Mossad, from 2002 to 2011,
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/04/world/middleeast/04mossad.html">
           has said
          </a>
          an attack would mean regional war, and in that case you would have given Iran the best possible reason to continue the nuclear program. Michael Hayden, who ran the CIA under George W. Bush from 2006 to 2009,
          <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2012/01/19/bushs-cia-director-we-determined-attacking-iran-was-a-bad-idea/">
           has warned
          </a>
          that an attack would guarantee that which we are trying to prevent: an Iran that will spare nothing to build a nuclear weapon.
         </p>

        <h3 class="hed">
         The Iran Deal Upends U.S. Politics
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/david-frum/" title="David Frum">
          David Frum
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Five ways Tuesdays announcement reshapes the terms of the domestic political debate
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          The Iran nuclear deal upends the security of the Middle East region. Old alliances have been shoved aside. Massive new resources have been put at the disposal of the Iranian regime. The security implications of the agreement will take time to be felt. The U.S. political consequences will be immediate.
         </p>
         <p>
          Here are five:
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The Rand Paul Candidacy for the Republican Nomination Is Over
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          The Iran deal presents Republicans with a sharp binary choice. Line up with President Obama in support of a treaty achieved mostly by American concessions that puts Iran on the path to an internationally accepted nuclear weapon sometime in the 2020s? A treaty that bypasses Congress role and that is opposed by every U.S. ally in the region, including but not limited to Israel? No cash prize for predicting how the Republican primary electorate will align.
         </p>
        </div>

         <img class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/mt/2015/07/RTR2DHZS/hero_wide_640.jpg?GE2DGNRYG44TGOBVFYYA===="/>
         <figcaption class="credit">
          Morteza Nikoubazl / Reuters

        <h3 class="hed">
         The Single Most Important Question to Ask About the Iran Deal
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/jeffrey-goldberg/" title="Jeffrey Goldberg">
          Jeffrey Goldberg
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         <span>
          Making sense of a morally dubious agreement that might be a practical necessity
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          <em>
           Look, 20 years from now, Im still going to be around, God willing. If Iran has a nuclear weapon, its my name on this. 
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/05/obama-interview-iran-isis-israel/393782/">
            President Barack Obama, May 21, 2015
           </a>
          </em>
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <p>
          The theocratic regime that rules Irana regime that is a committed and proficient sponsor of terrorism, according to John Kerrys State Department
          <span>
           
          </span>
          will be more powerful tomorrow than it is today, thanks to the
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/07/15/world/middleeast/iran-nuclear-deal-is-reached-after-long-negotiations.html?hp&amp;action=click&amp;pgtype=Homepage&amp;module=span-ab-top-region&amp;region=top-news&amp;WT.nav=top-news">
           agreement it has just negotiated
          </a>
          with the Obama administration, Americas European allies, and two U.S. adversaries as well.
         </p>
         <p>
          This sad conclusion is unavoidable. The lifting of crippling sanctions, which will come about as part of the nuclear deal struck in Vienna, means that at least
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/05/obama-interview-iran-isis-israel/393782/">
           $150 billion
          </a>
          , a sum Barack Obama first invoked in May, will soon enough flow to Tehran. With this very large pot of money, the regime will be able to fund both domestic works and foreign adventures in Syria, Yemen, Lebanon, Iraq, and elsewhere.
         </p>

A three-member panel of judges ruled that the Obama administration has come up with a sufficient accommodation for religious organizations like the Little Sisters: If they object to providing insurance coverage to employees who want to buy birth control, organizations can sign a two-page form stating that objection. Thats itfrom there, the administration will arrange for a third-party provider to make sure the employee can get coverage. But the Little Sisters, along with schools

        </h3>

         <a href="/author/adam-chandler/" title="Adam Chandler">
          Adam Chandler
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Years of talks have produced an agreement over the Islamic Republics nuclear program. A look at some of the details and reactions
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          <small>
           <em>
            Updated at 1:31 p.m.
           </em>
          </small>
         </p>
         <p>
          On Tuesday, following nearly two and a half years of negotiations, Iran and six world powers struck a nuclear agreement. Under the accord, 
          <span>
           sanctions imposed by the United States, European Union and
          </span>
          <span>
           United Nations
          </span>
          <span>
          </span>
          <span>
           will be lifted in return for
          </span>
          <span>
           Iran
          </span>
          <span>
          </span>
          <span>
           agreeing long-term curbs on a nuclear program,
           <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/07/14/us-iran-nuclear-idUSKCN0PM0CE20150714">
            Reuters reported
           </a>
           .
          </span>
          While details of the agreement are still being parsed, this outcome is consistent with the
          <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/world/2015/apr/02/iran-nuclear-deal-negotiators-announce-framework-agreement">
           basic parameters of a framework agreement
          </a>
          signed between Iran and the six world powers in April in Switzerland.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Background
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Although that framework only sketched out the contours of a deal, it was both embraced and derided around the world. My colleague Jeffrey Goldberg called it
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/on-iran-the-least-worst-option/389598/">
           President Obamas least-worst option,
          </a>
          in that it would restrict Irans nuclear program over the course of a decade, but also formalize[d] Irans status as a potential nuclear-threshold state by allowing it to maintain a vast nuclear infrastructure. Western allies of the United States described the deal as a historic breakthrough, while Russia praised it for
          <a href="http://www.aljazeera.com/news/middleeast/2015/04/world-leaders-react-iran-nuclear-deal-150403020548163.html">
           reflecting President Vladimir Putins suggestions
          </a>
          . Israel was among the most vocal in its opposition to the framework,
          <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2015/04/05/politics/netanyahu-iran-deal/">
           calling it a a very bad deal,
          </a>
          while Saudi Arabia and the Gulf Cooperation Council states were less vocal, but also displeased. Others expressed pessimism that the deal could last.
          <span>
           In the United States, many Republicans blasted the framework, while some Democrats were nervous enough about it to vote with their GOP colleagues on a
           <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/04/07/us-iran-nuclear-congress-idUSKBN0MY2A020150407">
            measure that would require Congress to approve
           </a>
           any final agreement.
          </span>

        <div class="excerpt">
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            LINCOLN HEIGHTS, OhioAfrican Americans started coming to Cincinnati more than a century ago, fleeing the violence and economic constraints of the South for jobs and homes.
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            But redlining and other restrictive zoning laws prohibited black families from buying homes in many of the citys neighborhoods.            </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            The land had no paved roads and no streetlights. Few homes had running water and there was no police or fire protection. Carl Westmoreland, who grew up in this village in the 1940s, remembers watching black men rush over a hill toward a burning home with a small fire cart theyd bought. They didnt save it in time, but the neighborhood banded together and rebuilt the house together. He refers to the community at the time as Americas Soweto for the primitive living conditions there.
