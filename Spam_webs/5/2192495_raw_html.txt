        <p>
         <!-- Swellfire-creation = 2014-12-01 19:32:12 -->
         <br/>
         Along the Way
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/images/Little_Colorado_Log.jpg">
          <img border="2" height="460" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Little_Colorado_Log_-_thumb.jpg" width="342"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Little Colorado Log, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <b>
          Little Colorado River
         </b>
         joins the Colorado at Mile 61. Its colour is the most exquisite shade of crystalline turquoise Ive ever seen. We spent several hours here swimming, playing and of course doing photography.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whether due to the remembrance of a joyful morning playing and shooting by the river, or the inherent qualities of the photograph itself, this image summarizes for me the sense of isolation and tranquility that this voyage provided.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 28~70mm f/2.8L lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/travertine-falls/">
          <img border="2" height="460" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Travetine_Falls_-_thumb.jpg" width="389"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Travertine Falls, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         On the last day of the trip, at Mile 230, one encounters
         <b>
          Travertine Falls
         </b>
         . We were fortunate to drift by just as a waning moon positioned itself above the cliff.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 100~400mm f/5.6L IS lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/moonlight---sc.shtml">
          <img border="2" height="319" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Moonlight_Saddle_Canyon_-_thumb.jpg" width="460"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Moonlight at Saddle Canyon, May 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         This ones fun.
        </p>
        <p>
         The trip began with a full moon and each night we were woken up with what at first appeared to be a bright spotlight in our faces. (We were sleeping without tents.)
        </p>
        <p>
         On our second night, camped at
         <b>
          Saddle Canyon,
         </b>
         Steve Kossack and I got up at 2am and did a series of time exposures. This one was for 4 minutes @ f/4.0. It was the longest of the series but still slightly underexposed. Proper exposure would have likely been about 6-8 minutes.
        </p>
        <p>
         This was my first experience doing long time exposures with the new Provia 100F and I was very pleased to see that reciprocity characteristics appeared good and colour shift was minor.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 24mm f/3.5L T/S lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/star-trails/">
          <img border="2" height="304" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Star_Trails_-_thumb.jpg" width="460"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Star Trails, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         By the sixth night of the trip the moon wasnt going to rise until about 2am, so we set up to do star trails. This is a 4 hour exposure, at f/4.0. I terminated the exposure just as the moon was starting to rise and so it provided some illumination on the distant cliffs.
        </p>
        <p>
         In a print, or looking at the original transparency, you can see that the light from the rising moon that is being cast on the central cliff is warm, due to passing through the thickness of a great deal of the earths atmosphere, just as is sunlight at dawn.
        </p>
        <p>
         Also, without the limitations of a web display one can also see the rafts moored by the edge of the river in the lower left hand corner. This part of the picture was illuminated solely by moonlight.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 24mm f/3.5L T/S lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/cactus-cliff/" name="Cactus Cliff">
          <img border="2" height="306" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Cactus_Cliff_-_thumb.jpg" width="423"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Cactus Cliff, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         Just one of many shots taken that week of rock and plants details. You really need to see a high resolution print to enjoy the forms and textures of this image. The web is good for some photographs, but simply cant do justice to ones, like this, where detail defines the image.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 28~70mm f/2.8L lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/redwall-canyon/" name="Redwall Cavern">
          <img border="2" height="190" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Redwall_Cavern_-_thumb.jpg" width="460"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Redwall Cavern, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          Redwall Canyon
         </b>
         , at mile 33, is a huge amphitheater carved out of the canyon wall by massive floods of the river over countless millennium. Many hundreds of people could fit in this cave at one time.
        </p>
        <p>
         I took few shots with the XPan on this trip, but here using the 30mm lens I was able to exploit its wide field of view. Its worth viewing the enlarged 10" version so you can see peoples silhouettes in the lower left hand corner to add a sense of scale.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Hasselblad XPan and 30mm lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/sidelight-tree/" name="Sidelight Tree">
          <img border="2" height="318" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Sidelight_Tree_-_thumb.jpg" width="460"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Sidelight Tree, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         As we drifted and motored down the Colorado, in addition to the grand vistas there were countless opportunities to shoot detail along the riverbank. Frankly, much of this is mundane, but is a valued personal record of what I saw.
        </p>
        <p>
         A few images stand out though. This one, of a tree sidelight by a shaft of sunlight streaming down a narrow side-canyon, was a common sight.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 100~400mm f/5.6L IS lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/clouds-and-cliffs/" name="Clouds and Cliffs">
          <img border="2" height="423" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Clouds_and_Cliffs_-_thumb.jpg" width="303"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Clouds and Cliffs, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         I have no recollection of where this was shot, or even of having taken it. Just one of the hundreds of passing images as the boat drifted down the river, day after day. I almost passed it by when reviewing my film, but theres something about the juxtaposition of the cliffs and clouds that I find haunting.
        </p>
        <p>
         I also find myself staring at a print of this photograph for longer than many others from this trip. There must be
         <i>
          something
         </i>
         there.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 100~400mm f/5.6L IS lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="/yellow-tuft/" name="Yellow Tufts">
          <img border="2" height="254" src="/wp-content/uploads/2010/09/Yellow_Tuft_-_thumb.jpg" width="360"/>
         </a>
         <i>
          Yellow Tufts, Grand Canyon May, 2000
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         Again, a detail taken from the raft as we floated past the riverbank.
        </p>
        <p>
         What made this trip so interesting was that the scenery came to us, rather than the other way round. Sitting on the raft looking though a stabilized 100~400mm lens provided a wealth of ever-changing subject matter. A few shots, such as most of the ones seen on this page, stand out in some way.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          Photographed with a Canon EOS-1V and 100~400mm f/5.6L IS lens on Fuji Provia 100F
         </i>
        </p>
