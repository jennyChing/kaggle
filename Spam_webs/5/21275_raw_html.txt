     <section id="article-section-1">
      <p>
       If theres anything Americans agree on, its that racial discrimination is very bad and should not be permitted.
      </p>
      <p>
       What they dont agree on is what racial discrimination means. Popular definitions range all the way from KKK-style terror to affirmative action to insensitive language in college classes. Fair enough. The meaning of discrimination in ordinary parlance is and should be open to debate.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its more of a problem, however, when people dont understand what discrimination means in constitutional law. To forget that is to forget the nations history and some hard-won victories.
      </p>
      <aside class="callout">
       <hr/>
       <h4>
        Related Story
       </h4>
       <div>
        <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2014/10/on-race-and-voter-id-john-roberts-wants-it-both-ways/381868/">
         <img alt="" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/mt/2014/10/AP050914023648/lead.jpg?ndyfi3"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p>
        <a data-omni-click="r'.',r' ',d,r'.',r' ',l.pathname" href="http://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2014/10/on-race-and-voter-id-john-roberts-wants-it-both-ways/381868/">
         On Race and Voter ID, John Roberts Wants It Both Ways
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
      </aside>
      <p>
       I make these reflections because of the response Ive received to a
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2014/10/on-race-and-voter-id-john-roberts-wants-it-both-ways/381868/">
        column
       </a>
       last week suggesting that Chief Justice John Roberts, who has made his abhorrence of racial discrimination abundantly clear, might have been more alert to its presence in the record of the Texas voter-ID law,
       <a href="http://legiscan.com/TX/text/SB14/id/294690">
        SB 14
       </a>
       . The Court
       <a href="http://www.supremecourt.gov/o">
        just allowed the Texas law to go into effect
       </a>
       for the 2014 general election, even though a lower-court judge had found as a fact that it constitutes intentional discrimination against minority voters. (We dont know for sure how Roberts voted, but we do know that Justices Ruth Bader Ginsburg, Sonia Sotomayor, and Elena Kagan would have blocked the law, meaning the vote was either 6-3 or 5-4.)
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of correspondents have explained the paradox this way: Roberts and many others object to laws and policies (like affirmative action) that actually use racial terms in their language. A law that doesnt do this is neutral and thus poses no problem.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_politics&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_politics&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       This distinction is foreign to the Constitution. What the Equal Protection Clause as interpreted by the courts actually targets is intentional discrimination. Constitutionally, it doesnt matter whether lawmakers write that purpose into the language or not. As anyone who has studied American history knows, its possible to further white supremacy without admitting anything publicly; thats how Southern states kept the ballot all white for a century despite the 15th Amendments prohibition of race discrimination in voting. Jim Crow election laws didnt say only whites could vote; instead, they set up a maze of restrictionsgrandfather clauses, poll taxes, literacy testsdesigned to winnow out non-whites and, to a lesser extent, poor people of any race.
      </p>
      <p>
       A law that says, whites can vote, non-whites not so much, for example, is invalid on its face. But what if it says, persons convicted of a crime of moral turpitude may not vote, when that rule disenfranchises blacks far more often than whites? In a 1985 case called
       <a href="https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/471/222/case.html">
        <em>
         Hunter v. Underwood
        </em>
       </a>
       <em>
        ,
       </em>
       the Supreme Court found that, neutral in language or not, the moral turpitude clause was invalid under the 15th Amendment. The historical evidence, wrote then-Justice William H. Rehnquist, demonstrates conclusively that [the turpitude provision] was enacted with the intent of disenfranchising blacks. The sponsors of the measure had admitted in 1901 that they wanted, as one of them said, to establish white supremacy in this State." The sponsors were all dead by 1985, but the passage of time didnt matter, nor did the fact that it also disenfranchised some poor white voters.
      </p>
      <p>
       The intent, not the language, is what matters under the case law. And its not necessary that those creating or enforcing the law actually hate minorities; all that must be shown is that they intended to treat minorities less favorably than the majority.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thats what discrimination is. It neednt speak its name; it neednt wear a robe or burn a cross; it may speak in a soothing voice. Discriminatory lawmakers can have more than one motive. But if they intentionally target race, it is discrimination.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxright-wrapper" data-pos="boxright">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxright1">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_politics&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxright1&amp;sz=300x250%7C300x600&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=12" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_politics&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxright1&amp;sz=300x250%7C300x600&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=12"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-3">
      <p>
       How do we determine discriminatory intent? Legislators, even those with bad intentions, tend to be more discreet today than they were in segregation-era Alabama. But the Supreme Court has developed ways of smoking out discriminatory intent. If you want a thorough introduction to how this is done, read Judge Nelva Gonzales Ramoss 147-page opinion in the Texas-ID case,
       <a href="http://moritzlaw.osu.edu/electionlaw/litigation/documents/VeaseyOpinion100914.pdf">
        <em>
         Veasey v. Perry
        </em>
       </a>
       <em>
        .
       </em>
       Ramos follows Supreme Court precedent carefully as she analyzes the history of SB 14. In a 1977 case called
       <a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/429/252">
        <em>
         Village of Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Development Corporation
        </em>
       </a>
       <em>
        ,
       </em>
       the Court instructed lower courts to ask certain questions when neutral-seeming laws are challenged. Do they affect minorities more than whites? Some civil-rights statutes forbid such a disparate impact, but under the Constitution impact by itself is never enough to prove discrimination. More evidence is needed. What is the history of discrimination in the city or state that enacted the law, and what was going on when the law was passed? Was the law enacted by unusual, hasty, or secretive procedures? What did the lawmakers themselves have to say about the law during the process?
      </p>
      <p>
       Ramos held
       <a href="http://www.brennancenter.org/blog/texas-photo-id-trial-updates">
        a nine-day trial
       </a>
       , including testimony by members of the legislature and former state election officials. She applied the law carefully, and found that (1) the law would disfranchise minorities much more heavily than whites; (2) Texas has a long and continuing history of denying the vote to African Americans and Latinos, and the 2011 legislative session was pervaded with a divisive and anti-Hispanic atmosphere; (3) the Republican majority used aggressive parliamentary maneuvers to force SB 14 through without full debate; and (4) the bills own sponsor admitted publicly that it would disfranchise a disproportionate share of minorities. By the Supreme Courts own precedents, these facts seem more than enough to trigger the
       <em>
        Hunter v. Underwood
       </em>
       rule.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of my correspondents read the Ramos opinion and werent convinced. Understand, however, that neither the Fifth Circuit nor the Supreme Court had the option of saying, Whats the big deal anyway? Ramoss conclusions are what lawyers call
       <a href="http://definitions.uslegal.com/a/adjudicative-fact/">
        adjudicative facts.
       </a>
       Having heard the testimony and read the exhibits (see here for a hint of the
       <a href="http://moritzlaw.osu.edu/electionlaw/litigation/VeaseyV.Perry.php">
        volume of the evidence
       </a>
       ), she issued findings of fact that SB 14 was enacted with racist intent.
      </p>
      <aside class="pullquote instapaper_ignore">
       The intent, not the language, is what matters under the case law. All that must be shown is that its creators intended to treat minorities less favorably.
      </aside>
      <p>
       That language has legal effect.
       <a href="http://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/frcp/rule_52">
        Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 52(a)(6)
       </a>
       is quite clear: Findings of fact, whether based on oral or other evidence, must not be set aside unless clearly erroneous, and the reviewing court must give due regard to the trial court's opportunity to judge the witnesses credibility. The appeals court and the Supreme Court didnt set aside Ramoss findings, nor did they find clear error in her opinion. They havent had a chance to consider them.
      </p>
      <p>
       Her findings are, for the time being, the facts of this case, in her court and all others. Thus, the Supreme Courts majority tacitly chose to allow Texas to violate the Constitution in its election procedures, on the apparent grounds that it would inconvenience the state to follow the law. This principle should concern us all.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector2">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_politics&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_politics&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-4">
      <p>
       One final note: Some commentators gloss over measures like SB 14 on the grounds that they are only discriminating by party. Texas itself has made this argument. Latinos and African Americans tend to be Democrats; Republican legislators dont hate minorities, they hate Democrats. Hey, presto! Discrimination disappears!
      </p>
      <p>
       The short answer to this is,
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_McAuliffe">
        to quote General Anthony McAuliffe
       </a>
       , nuts. The more complete answer,
       <a href="http://harvardlawreview.org/2014/01/race-or-party-how-courts-should-think-about-republican-efforts-to-make-it-harder-to-vote-in-north-carolina-and-elsewhere/">
        offered by Rick Hasen
       </a>
       , the gold standard of election-law commentators, is that [w]hen a legislature passes an election-administration law ... discriminating against a partys voters or otherwise burdening voters, that fact should not be a defense to charges of race discrimination.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tell it! What kind of court would regard Im only trying to rig the election for my party as an excuse rather than a confession?
      </p>
     </section>

        <h3 class="hed">
         3 Truths About Trump
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/james-fallows/" title="James Fallows">
          James Fallows
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         The passions evoked by Donald Trump deserve notice. His candidacy does not.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          The first two of these points about Donald Trump are obvious but nonetheless worth repeating, during the froth of current Trump-driven excitement. The third might have escaped your notice.
         </p>
         <p>
          1) Donald Trump
          <strong>
           will not
          </strong>
          be the 45th president of the United States. Nor the 46th, nor any other number you might name. The chance of his winning nomination and election is exactly zero.
         </p>
         <p>
          Why? Forget the majorities of his own partys voters who
          <a href="http://www.desmoinesregister.com/story/news/elections/presidential/caucus/2015/05/30/iowa-poll-donald-trump-never-support/28231261/">
           say in some surveys
          </a>
          that they would never vote for him. Instead, consider this aspect of his background:
         </p>
         <p>
          All former incumbents had been through some sort of public service before running for the presidency. The great majority of them had been elected to something: The House, the Senate, a mayorship, a governorship, the state legislature, the vice-presidency, something and usually many things. Of the few exceptions, all had held important non-elective public positions. William Howard Taft and Herbert Hoover had been Cabinet secretaries before running for the White House; Zachary Taylor, Ulysses Grant, and of course Dwight Eisenhower had been famous military commanders.
         </p>
