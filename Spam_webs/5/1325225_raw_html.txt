     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Colour Blind
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2009 film depicting racism.
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 2009 shooting was completed with
      <a href="/wiki/Wil_Johnson" title="Wil Johnson">
       Wil Johnson
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      star of
      <a href="/wiki/Waking_the_Dead_(TV_series)" title="Waking the Dead (TV series)">
       Waking the Dead
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cavanah" title="Robert Cavanah">
       Robert Cavanah
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Lara_Croft_Tomb_Raider:_The_Cradle_of_Life" title="Lara Croft Tomb Raider: The Cradle of Life">
       Tomb Raider
      </a>
      /
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0318649" rel="nofollow">
       Sahara
      </a>
      ) on a short film entitled Colour Blind to bring attention to a UK audience of the dangers of seeing racism everywhere.
     </p>
     <p>
      Colour Blind had its official press screening at the
      <a href="/wiki/Odeon_Leicester_Square" title="Odeon Leicester Square">
       Odeon Leicester Square
      </a>
      on Monday 23 March 2009
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">

      </sup>
      and its UK Premiere at the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Urban_Film_Festival" title="British Urban Film Festival">
       British Urban Film Festival
      </a>
      on Saturday 3 October 2009 at Oxford House Cinema
      <a href="/wiki/Bethnal_Green" title="Bethnal Green">
       Bethnal Green
      </a>
      London.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_Blind_(2009_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The premise of the film is based on the statement that "White people often never see skin colour, but Black people always do".
     </p>
     <p>
      Through a chance encounter, two men meet through a mutual friend, Peter, who is arriving late for their meeting.
     </p>
     <p>
      John (played by Robert Cavanah) is a white British liberal who would have a Black friend if he knew any and Dan (played by Wil Johnson) is a Black Briton who understands he lives in a White Society and needs to engage with it to succeed.
     </p>
     <p>
      After being introduced to both characters the film unfolds through the eyes of John and we see how cultural references and shared experiences impact on how he sees Dan's skin colour.
     </p>
     <p>
      Through a mundane conversation about work, girlfriends and friendships John gradually watches Dan's skin colour go from
      <a href="/wiki/Idi_Amin" title="Idi Amin">
       Idi Amin
      </a>
      Black to almost white, as he discovers that they have a lot in common.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the end of the film John, doesn't even think about Dan's skin colour.
     </p>
     <p>
      In stark contrast, when the audience is shown the world through Dan's eyes, John is shown in Golliwog make-up, Blackface and frizzy wig speaking in what the makers of the film refer to as Wigger (White Nigger as in the type of speech
      <a href="/wiki/Sacha_Baron_Cohen" title="Sacha Baron Cohen">
       Sacha Baron Cohen
      </a>
      's Character
      <a href="/wiki/Ali_G" title="Ali G">
       Ali G
      </a>
      uses).
     </p>
     <p>
      Representing that Dan sees white men as constantly parodying his race and eternally racist.
     </p>
     <p>
      The objective of the film was to prompt an informed debate about race. The makers felt that any mention of race in the current political climate is festooned with political correctness and therefore unhelpful in dealing with the real issues that are impacting on the UK today.
     </p>
     <p>
      The implication that Black people were as racist, if not more so, than White people in Britain was designed to be the spark for serious debate around the UK.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_Blind_(2009_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Written by Amanda Baker (who has written and directed with a number of stand-up comedians including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reginald_D_Hunter" title="Reginald D Hunter">
       Reginald D Hunter
      </a>
      ) the film is clearly designed to get audiences thinking about skin colour and placing themselves in each other's shoes.
     </p>
     <p>
      The producer, Paul Atherton, made the decision to have the white lead in (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_face" title="Black face">
       blackface
      </a>
      make-up) to make the point in stark contrast to his contemporaries who preferred to avoid the controversy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ironically during pre-production of the film on 3 February 2009
      <a href="/wiki/Carol_Thatcher" title="Carol Thatcher">
       Carol Thatcher
      </a>
      (daughter of former Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">
       Margaret Thatcher
      </a>
      ) during the
      <a href="/wiki/2009_Australian_Open" title="2009 Australian Open">
       2009 Australian Open
      </a>
      , in a conversation in the show's
      <a href="/wiki/Green_room" title="Green room">
       green room
      </a>
      , referred to a black
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      tennis player, reportedly
      <a href="/wiki/Jo-Wilfried_Tsonga" title="Jo-Wilfried Tsonga">
       Jo-Wilfried Tsonga
      </a>
      , as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golliwogg" title="Golliwogg">
       golliwog
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
        The Times
       </a>
      </i>
      , Thatcher called Tsonga "half-golliwog" and "the golliwog
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_ethnic_slurs#F" title="List of ethnic slurs">
       Frog
      </a>
      " the subsequent debates surrounding the issue prompted Producer
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Atherton" title="Paul Atherton">
       Paul Atherton
      </a>
      to change the make-up in Colour Blind from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_face" title="Black face">
       Black Face
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Golliwog" title="Golliwog">
       Golliwog
      </a>
      to further underline and show how current and important the point the film is making.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Filming_Locations">
       Filming Locations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_Blind_(2009_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Filming Locations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The film was shot entirely on location in London.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_Information">
       Technical Information
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colour_Blind_(2009_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technical Information">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The film was shot by Dutchman Dirk Van Der Velden on the Sony HDW-750P High Definition HDCAM Camera. Lighting was done by Colin Clarke, a veteran of many documentary and drama shoots around the globe. The lighting equipment used was a 3KW dimmable "Novalight" (now no longer in production), as well as a 1KW ARRILITE Redhead with a Chimera Softbox as well as three Dedolights and the practicals on set.
     </p>
     <p>
      Colour Blind was edited at PabloPost at
      <a href="/wiki/Pinewood_Studios" title="Pinewood Studios">
       Pinewood Studios
      </a>
      . Editor, Ralston Humble, developed an entirely new way to produce the special effect of Dan's changing skin colour by using
      <a href="/wiki/Rotoscoping" title="Rotoscoping">
       Rotoscoping
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Color_grading" title="Color grading">
       Color grading
      </a>
      and layering them on to the original film to get the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ft1e0bKtb54" rel="nofollow">
       final result
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
