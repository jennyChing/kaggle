     <p>
      <b>
       Military animals
      </b>
      are
      <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">
       domesticated
      </a>
      animals that are used in
      <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">
       warfare
      </a>
      and other
      <a href="/wiki/Combat" title="Combat">
       combat
      </a>
      related activities. As
      <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">
       working animals
      </a>
      , military animals serve a variety of functions.
      <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">
       Dogs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">
       pigs
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxen" title="Oxen">
       oxen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">
       camels
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">
       horses
      </a>
      and other animals are sometimes used for transportation and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_detection" title="Bomb detection">
       bomb detection
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">
       Elephants
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigeon" title="Pigeon">
       pigeons
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">
       rats
      </a>
      are also used during wartime, while
      <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">
       dolphins
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">
       sea lions
      </a>
      are in active use.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="For_transport_and_hauling">
       For transport and hauling
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>

     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">
        horse
       </a>
       was the most widely used animal throughout the recorded
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_warfare" title="History of warfare">
        history of warfare
       </a>
       . Early mounts could pull a
       <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">
        chariot
       </a>
       or carry lightly armored
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skirmish" title="Skirmish">
        skirmishing
       </a>
       forces. With the appearance of heavier mounts and the invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Stirrup" title="Stirrup">
        stirrup
       </a>
       , the horse-mounted
       <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">
        cavalry
       </a>
       became the most prestigious
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combat_arm" title="Combat arm">
        combat arm
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       for several centuries. A knight's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warhorse" title="Warhorse">
        warhorse
       </a>
       was trained to bite and kick.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse_archer" title="Horse archer">
        The combination
       </a>
       of the horse-mounted warrior armed with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_(weapon)" title="Bow (weapon)">
        bow
       </a>
       made the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steppe_people" title="Steppe people">
        steppe people
       </a>
       's armies the most powerful military force in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_history" title="Asian history">
        Asian history
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       With the appearance of modern
       <a href="/wiki/Ranged_weapon" title="Ranged weapon">
        ranged weapons
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">
        motor vehicles
       </a>
       , horse use for military purposes fell into decline. However, horses and mules are still used extensively by various armies today for transport in difficult terrain.
      </li>
      <li>
       While
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elephants" title="Elephants">
        elephants
       </a>
       are not considered
       <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">
        domesticable
       </a>
       , they can be trained to serve as mounts, or for moving heavy loads.
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       hymns record their use for military purposes as early as 1,100
       <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">
        B.C.
       </a>
       A group of elephants was notably employed by
       <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">
        Hannibal
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Punic_War" title="Second Punic War">
        Second Punic War
       </a>
       . They were employed as recently as
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       by both the
       <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">
        Allies
       </a>
       . Elephants could perform the work of machines in locations where vehicles could not penetrate, so they found use in the
       <a href="/wiki/Burma_Campaign" title="Burma Campaign">
        Burma Campaign
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">
        Camels
       </a>
       have typically seen use as mounts in arid regions (
       <a href="/wiki/Camel_cavalry" title="Camel cavalry">
        Camel cavalry
       </a>
       ). They are better able to traverse sandy
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserts" title="Deserts">
        deserts
       </a>
       than horses, and require far less water. Camels were employed in both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_wars" title="World wars">
        world wars
       </a>
       . Camels are used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army" title="Indian Army">
        Indian Army
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Border_Security_Force" title="Border Security Force">
        Border Security Force
       </a>
       for patrolling in the desert regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">
        Rajasthan
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">
        Mules
       </a>
       were used by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army" title="U.S. Army">
        U.S. Army
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       to carry supplies and equipment over difficult terrain.
       <a href="/wiki/Pack_animal" title="Pack animal">
        Pack animals
       </a>
       that are innately patient, cautious, and hardy, mules could carry heavy loads of supplies where
       <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">
        Jeeps
       </a>
       and even
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pack_horse" title="Pack horse">
        pack horses
       </a>
       could not travel. Mules were used in
       <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
        North Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">
        Burma
       </a>
       , and in
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       . They are also used for transporting supplies in
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
        mountainous
       </a>
       regions.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxen" title="Oxen">
        Oxen
       </a>
       have been used widely in war as beasts of burden, especially to transport heavy or siege
       <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">
        artillery
       </a>
       through heavy terrain.
      </li>
      <li>
       Both
       <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
        Sweden
       </a>
       and, later, the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       , attempted to utilize
       <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">
        moose
       </a>
       as deep-snow cavalry. Moose were discovered to be unsuitable for warfare, as they easily contracted livestock diseases, were difficult to feed, and fled the battleground. The Soviets later trained moose not to be gun-shy, but were unable to make use of their cavalry because of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet-Finnish_War" title="Soviet-Finnish War">
        Soviet-Finnish War
       </a>
       and World War II.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">
       Dogs
      </a>
      were used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       ancient Greeks
      </a>
      for war purposes, and they were undoubtedly used much earlier in history. During their conquest of
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
       Latin America
      </a>
      , Spanish
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquistadors" title="Conquistadors">
       conquistadors
      </a>
      used
      <a href="/wiki/English_Mastiff" title="English Mastiff">
       Mastiffs
      </a>
      to kill warriors in the
      <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
       Caribbean
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
       Peru
      </a>
      . Mastiffs, as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Dane" title="Great Dane">
       Great Danes
      </a>
      , were used in England during the Middle Ages, where their large size was used to scare horses to throw off their riders or to pounce on knights on horseback, disabling them until their master delivered the final blow. More recently, canines with explosives strapped to their backs saw use during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Army" title="Soviet Army">
       Soviet Army
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-tank_dog" title="Anti-tank dog">
       anti-tank weapons
      </a>
      . In all armies, they were used for detecting mines. They were trained to spot trip wires, as well as mines and other booby traps. They were also employed for sentry duty, and to spot snipers or hidden enemy forces. Some dogs also saw use as messengers.
     </p>

     <ul>
      <li>
       It is unsubstantiated that
       <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">
        rhinoceros
       </a>
       were used for war functions. By analyzing
       <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Drer">
        Albrecht Drer
       </a>
       's famous 1515
       <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCrer%27s_Rhinoceros" title="Drer's Rhinoceros">
        woodcut
       </a>
       , it is possible that the liberties taken with the rhino's design were in fact designs for a suit of armour created for the rhinoceros's fight in Portugal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">

         </span>

      </li>

       <a href="/wiki/War_elephant" title="War elephant">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Diadochi" title="Diadochi">
        Diadochi
       </a>
       kingdoms and the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="As_living_bombs">
       As living bombs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_animal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As living bombs">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Anti-tank_dog" title="Anti-tank dog">
        Anti-tank dogs
       </a>
        a Soviet, World War II weapon that had mixed success.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Pigeon" title="Project Pigeon">
        Project Pigeon
       </a>
        a proposed U.S. World War II weapon that used pigeons to guide bombs.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Bat_bomb" title="Bat bomb">
        Bat bomb
       </a>
       , a U.S. project that used
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_free-tailed_bat" title="Mexican free-tailed bat">
        Mexican free-tailed bats
       </a>
       to carry small incendiary bombs.
      </li>
      <li>
       In 1267, the sheriff of Essex was accused of plotting to release flying cockerels carrying bombs over London.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       According to Pr. Shi Bo, in "Trente-six Stratagmes Chinois" (in French,
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/2911858069">
        ISBN 2-911858-06-9
       </a>
       ), monkeys were used in the beginning of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Song_Dynasty" title="Southern Song Dynasty">
        Southern Song Dynasty
       </a>
       , in a battle between rebels of the Yanzhou (Yasuo) province and the Chinese Imperial Army, led by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zhao_Yu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zhao Yu (page does not exist)">
        Zhao Yu
       </a>
       . The monkeys were used as live
       <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">
        incendiary devices
       </a>
       . The animals were clothed with straw, dipped in oil and set on fire. They were set loose into the enemy's camp, thereby setting the tents on fire, and driving the whole camp into chaos.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Animal-borne_bomb_attacks" title="Animal-borne bomb attacks">
        Animal-borne bombs
       </a>
       have been used by modern terrorists and insurgents in the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       , who have affixed explosives to animals, sometimes left wandering alone,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">

         </span>
         10
         <span>

         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       and other times ridden by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suicide_bombers" title="Suicide bombers">
        suicide bombers
       </a>
       , in modern insurgent attacks in the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       .

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploding_rat" title="Exploding rat">
        Exploding rat
       </a>
        dead
       <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">
        rats
       </a>
       were prepared for use by the British
       <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">
        Special Operations Executive
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       against
       <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       . Rat carcasses were filled with
       <a href="/wiki/Plastic_explosive" title="Plastic explosive">
        plastic explosives
       </a>
       , to be left in locations such as factories where, it was hoped, the
       <a href="/wiki/Fireman_(steam_engine)" title="Fireman (steam engine)">
        stoker
       </a>
       tending a boiler would likely dispose of the unpleasant discovery by shoveling it into the furnace, causing it to explode.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rats contained only a small amount of explosive; however, a puncture of a high-pressure boiler could trigger a devastating
       <a href="/wiki/Boiler_explosion" title="Boiler explosion">
        boiler explosion
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Animal carcasses have been used to camouflage roadside
       <a href="/wiki/Improvised_explosive_device" title="Improvised explosive device">

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraqi_insurgency_(Iraq_War)" title="Iraqi insurgency (Iraq War)">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_communications">
       In communications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_animal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In communications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/War_pigeon" title="War pigeon">
       War pigeon
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Homing_pigeon" title="Homing pigeon">
       Homing pigeons
      </a>
      have seen use since medieval times for carrying messages. They were still employed for a similar purpose during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       World War I
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . In World War II, experiments were also performed in the use of the pigeon for guiding missiles, known as
      <a href="/wiki/Project_Pigeon" title="Project Pigeon">
       Project Pigeon
      </a>
      . The pigeon was placed inside so that they could see out through a window. They were trained to peck at controls to the left or right, depending on the location of a target shape.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="For_espionage">
       For espionage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Military_animal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: For espionage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the years before the First World War
      <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_photography" title="Pigeon photography">
       pigeon photography
      </a>
      was introduced to military intelligence gathering. Although employed during major battles like at Verdun and Somme, the method was not particularly successful. Various attempts in this direction were made during the Second World War as well. A CIA pigeon camera dating from the 1970s is displayed in the
      <a href="/wiki/CIA_Museum" title="CIA Museum">
       CIA Museum
      </a>
      ; details of CIA missions using this camera are still classified.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_Kitty" title="Acoustic Kitty">
       Acoustic Kitty
      </a>
      was a CIA project to use surgically modified cats to spy on the Kremlin and Soviet embassies in the 1960s. Despite expenditure of around $10 million, the project failed to produce practical results and was cancelled in 1967. Documents about the project were declassified in 2001.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">
        The Independent
       </a>
      </i>
      ran a story that the "Pentagon develops brain implants to turn sharks into military spies".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2007 Iranian authorities captured 14 squirrels, which were allegedly carrying spying equipment. The story was widely dismissed in the West as "nuts".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A number of spying scares in the Middle East involved birds. According to Israeli ornithologist Yossi Leshem, Sudanese authorities detained an
      <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_vulture" title="Egyptian vulture">
       Egyptian vulture
      </a>
      in the late 1970s, and a
      <a href="/wiki/Great_white_pelican" title="Great white pelican">
       white pelican
      </a>
      in the early 1980s, both carrying Israeli equipment used for
      <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration_tracking" title="Animal migration tracking">
       animal migration tracking
      </a>
      . A more mediatized event was the 2011 capture by a Saudi farmer of a
      <a href="/wiki/Griffon_vulture" title="Griffon vulture">
       griffon vulture
      </a>
      , which was eventually released by the Saudi authorities after they determined that the Israeli equipment it carried was used for scientific purposes. This was followed by international mockery and criticism of the Arab media outlets which uncritically had reported on the bird's alleged role in espionage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.sciencemag.org_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-news.sciencemag.org-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2012, a dead
      <a href="/wiki/European_bee-eater" title="European bee-eater">
       European bee-eater
      </a>
      tagged with an Israeli
      <a href="/wiki/Bird_ringing" title="Bird ringing">
       leg band
      </a>
      was found by villagers near the south-eastern Turkish city of
      <a href="/wiki/Gaziantep" title="Gaziantep">
       Gaziantep
      </a>
      . The villagers worried that the bird may have carried a micro-chip from Israeli intelligence to spy on the area. Turkish authorities examined the corpse of the bee-eater and assured villagers that it is common to equip migratory birds with rings in order to track their movements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>

      </span>

     <p>
      Beginning during the
      <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
       Cold War
      </a>
      , research has been done into the uses of many species of
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">
       marine mammals
      </a>
      for military purposes. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy_Marine_Mammal_Program" title="U.S. Navy Marine Mammal Program">
       U.S. Navy Marine Mammal Program
      </a>
      uses
      <a href="/wiki/Military_dolphin" title="Military dolphin">
       military dolphins
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">
       sea lions
      </a>
      for underwater sentry duty, mine clearance, and object recovery.

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">
       Cats
      </a>
      were used in the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
       Royal Navy
      </a>
      to control
      <a href="/wiki/Vermin" title="Vermin">
       vermin
      </a>
      on board ships. Able seacat
      <a href="/wiki/Simon_(cat)" title="Simon (cat)">
       Simon
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/HMS_Amethyst_(F116)" title="HMS Amethyst (F116)">
       HMS
       <i>
        Amethyst
       </i>
      </a>
      received the
      <a href="/wiki/Dickin_Medal" title="Dickin Medal">
       Dickin Medal
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      (19361939),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalist_faction" title="Nationalist faction">
       Nationalist
      </a>
      pilots attached fragile supplies to live
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkeys" title="Turkeys">
       turkeys
      </a>
      , which descended flapping their wings, thus serving as parachutes which could also be eaten by the defenders of the monastery of Santa Maria de la Cabeza.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">
       Chickens
      </a>
      were used during the Gulf Wars to detect poisonous gases in an operation called Kuwaiti Field Chicken (KFC); the designation of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marines" title="US Marines">
       US Marines
      </a>
      for chickens used in this role was Poultry Chemical Confirmation Devices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The plan was put on hold after 41 of 43 chicken used for such purposes died within a week of arrival in
      <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">
       Kuwait
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Gulf_War" title="First Gulf War">
       First Gulf War
      </a>
      , the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Weekly_World_News" title="Weekly World News">
        Weekly World News
       </a>
      </i>
      published a fictional account how such a chicken was awarded a medal after saving a French general's life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-News1991_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-News1991-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Furthermore, use of military chickens was proposed in the British
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Peacock" title="Blue Peacock">
       Blue Peacock
      </a>
      project. The scheme involved burying nuclear bombs in the ground for later detonation should occupied (West) Germany be overrun by
      <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">
       Warsaw Pact
      </a>
      forces. The primitive electronic devices of the 1950s were unreliable in frozen ground, and the chickens were considered as a source of biogenic heat. This story has often been reported as an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_Fool%27s" title="April Fool's">
       April Fool's
      </a>
      joke, but when it was declassified and proven to be a true story on 1 April 2004 (April Fool's Day), the head of education and interpretation at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_National_Archives" title="UK National Archives">
       UK National Archives
      </a>
      said, "It does seem like an April Fool but it most certainly is not. The Civil Service does not do jokes."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_examples">
       Notable examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <ul>
      <li>
       Many famous generals had renowned mounts, including
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       's legendary horse with "toes" described by
       <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">
        Suetonius
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         29
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">
        Duke of Wellington
       </a>
       's famed charger Copenhagen,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         30
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" title="Napoleon I of France">
        Napoleon Bonaparte
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Marengo_(horse)" title="Marengo (horse)">
        Marengo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander the Great
       </a>
       's horse
       <a href="/wiki/Bucephalus" title="Bucephalus">
        Bucephalus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee" title="Robert E. Lee">
        Robert E. Lee
       </a>
       's horse
       <a href="/wiki/Traveller_(horse)" title="Traveller (horse)">
        Traveller
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The movie
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wanted_(2008_film)" title="Wanted (2008 film)">
         Wanted
        </a>
       </i>
       features rats strapped with explosive devices, used in a similar manner to the bats of
       <a href="/wiki/Bat_bomb" title="Bat bomb">
        Project X-ray
       </a>
       during World War II.
      </li>
      <li>
       The 2010 British film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Four_Lions" title="Four Lions">
         Four Lions
        </a>
       </i>
       has one of the main characters attaching home made bombs to crows, in an attempt to commit a
       <a href="/wiki/Jihad" title="Jihad">
        Jihad
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EA" title="EA">
        EA
       </a>
       game
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer" title="Command &amp; Conquer">
         Command &amp; Conquer
        </a>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer:_Red_Alert_2" title="Command &amp; Conquer: Red Alert 2">
         Red Alert 2
        </a>
       </i>
       features the
       <a href="/wiki/Allies" title="Allies">
        Allies
       </a>
       using dolphins, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviets" title="Soviets">
        Soviets
       </a>
       using giant squid, and both sides using dogs.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wojtek_(soldier_bear)" title="Wojtek (soldier bear)">
        Wojtek, a Syrian brown bear cub
       </a>
       , served with the Polish II Corps, and famously fought at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monte_Cassino" title="Battle of Monte Cassino">
        Battle of Monte Cassino
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
