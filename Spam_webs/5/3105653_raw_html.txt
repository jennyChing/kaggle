            <h3 data-textannotation-id="6c98e06de6d18c21aa592d496567f478">
             Find Your Way Around Android
            </h3>
            <p data-textannotation-id="b0accc026c3aa92483c11b11dd69a1ae">
             As a former iPhone user, you'll likely find that the biggest adjustment to using Android is navigating your way through the operating system. In iOS, pretty much everything happens with the home button. In Android, you don't have a physical home button but rather a series of three or four touch buttons at the bottom of your screen. They generally go in this order:
            </p>
            <p class="has-media media-470 has-image" data-textannotation-id="64527691bb233228c7fabf1f663c76a0">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--rEzUjDmT--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_320/18g37titzj5hvjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18g37titzj5hvjpg" data-format="jpg" height="50" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--rEzUjDmT--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_320/18g37titzj5hvjpg.jpg" width="320"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li data-textannotation-id="43733e56de735e2e3deceb02fb791b09">
              <b>
               Back
              </b>
              : A curvy arrow pointing left
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="b78d284c25cdfb3bdb687449b2e66bbc">
              <b>
               Home
              </b>
              : The outline of a house
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="418ce0feb709b0cff55709e5677924e7">
              <b>
               Multitasking Drawer
              </b>
              : One rectangle on top of the other
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="02a611228a341e50f96e69d3bfb5edca">
              <b>
               Settings
              </b>
              : Vertical ellipsis (not all devices display this button at all times, or in the bottom row)
             </li>
            </ul>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="a93e8e9dae57fda21e25fb82cd5250bd">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--tK-fvcHH--/18g37wx5umr3vjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18g37wx5umr3vjpg" data-format="jpg" height="94" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--tK-fvcHH--/18g37wx5umr3vjpg.jpg" width="140"/>
              </span>

               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="4c884c935a61efba505d900b44ff217f">
             The
             <b>
              back button
             </b>
             has long been a source of confusion for everyone because its functionality is far from consistent. In most cases, it'll just take you to the last thing you did in an app. If there is no last thing, it may take you back to your home screen. In general, you can expect this functionality but don't be surprised if it does something else. This is just an unfortunate user experience issue in Android that has yet to be fixed.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="0104ced73ccf61a66faf131d7fedac35">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--_YaieroH--/18g37wx5p0g0kjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18g37wx5p0g0kjpg" data-format="jpg" height="94" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--_YaieroH--/18g37wx5p0g0kjpg.jpg" width="129"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="53f54329c664abd4f9467eab0fee1eb4">
             The
             <b>
              home button
             </b>
             simply takes you back to your home screen.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="ec9ebaaf5ac59db0c9073542397d1beb">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--eEGWIVzS--/18g37wz4otyk7jpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18g37wz4otyk7jpg" data-format="jpg" height="94" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--eEGWIVzS--/18g37wz4otyk7jpg.jpg" width="130"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="54cbfdad385980bb3551c7be43091b30">
             The
             <b>
              multitasking drawer
             </b>
             shows you your active apps so you can quickly switch between them without the need to go back to your home screen or open your app drawer. It works similarly to iOS, though it provides a vertical list (instead of a horizontal one) and provides a preview of the open app. Instead of tapping and holding to close apps, you just swipe them away.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p class="has-media media-300 has-image" data-textannotation-id="e09a6f3fbfb8474279532bb909fff996">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">

               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--s9HUW4L2--/18g3828jwpsivjpg.jpg" data-chomp-id="18g3828jwpsivjpg" data-format="jpg" height="93" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--s9HUW4L2--/18g3828jwpsivjpg.jpg" width="99"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>

            <p data-textannotation-id="f8b79166634edd42bf1cbbf6c9bc52dd">
             The
             <b>
              settings button
             </b>
             is sometimes a soft button like the others mentioned in this section, but on certain devices it'll just appear contextually in apps. Just know that if you see a vertical ellipsis (three dots stacked on top of each other), that's where you'll access an app's settings. To get to your phone's system settings, just open the Settings app in your app drawer, on your home screen (if you keep it there), or via the notification drawer (explained later, in the Notifications section).
            </p>

             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="910b51997f74785a3f7224b77f755426">
             Like iOS, Android can have many home screens. On many phones, you'll have five. Some phones may have more or less. Rather than left to right, your primary home screen (or page, if you prefer to think of it that way) starts in the middle with additional ones to the left and right. On Android, you can display apps and make folders just like you did on iOS by tapping, holding, and dragging an app onto another app. Android doesn't force you to place anything on your home screen, however, so you have to do a little more work to make it look the way you want. While this might seem more complicated and tedious, organizing your Android home screen offers several distinct advantages. First, you don't have to display any apps you don't want and only the ones you do. Second, you can add widgets that provide information and functionality. Third, you can download custom launchers that let your home screen do even more for you, such as customize its appearance. We'll discuss all three in this section.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <h4 class="modfont" data-textannotation-id="0b3380469b2568e1dd201a0e81951a4e">
             <b>
              How to Add and Organize Apps and Widgets
             </b>
            </h4>
            <p class="has-media media-640 has-image" data-textannotation-id="2e844a29aa86058cab9b4164dbbf501f">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--Fq__jxeJ--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18g36pih9wgfbpng.png" data-chomp-id="18g36pih9wgfbpng" data-format="png" height="1060" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--Fq__jxeJ--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18g36pih9wgfbpng.png" width="636"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="552e08b8be4c11e56aaf9a3d81807f80">
             Unlike iOS, Android doesn't show all your apps on the home screen by default. Instead, you can add your most important ones to your home screen and find the rest inside the app drawer. The app drawer is a little icon (in your home screen's dock by default) that you tap to view your entire collection of apps. Once you're inside your app drawer, tapping and holding any app will take you back to your home screen so you can create a shortcut. Just place it wherever you want and you're all done.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="b069df9e2ecec0ca089c13797ec9365f">
             Alternatively, there is a second way to add shortcuts to your home screen. While on the home screen, you can tap any empty space and hold for a moment. This will bring up a menu asking you want you want to add. Just choose the app you want and it'll appear on the home screen. Tap and hold to move it around.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="2b9c2017daf60991743a3a5069fe5a74">
             If you want to remove an app shortcut (which will
             <em>
              not
             </em>
             uninstall the app), just drag it to the letter X at the top of the screen and let go.
            </p>
            <associate>
            </associate>
            <p data-textannotation-id="1d8e0347300207e4b0abcf917ff448df">
             Widgets work in the exact same ways. If you tap an empty space on your home screen, choose the Widgets option to add a widget instead. You can also add widgets from your app drawer by scrolling past all your apps and into the widget section. Tapping and holding will allow you to place them on your home screen. In the latest version of Android, you can also change their size by dragging along the edges.
            </p>

            <p data-textannotation-id="ed502f3f04a62f60e0609c4648a15535">
             In iOS, Apple forces you to use their apps as the defaults. In Android, you don't have to. If you prefer a different navigation app for your driving needs or an alternative mail client, you can make the switch without much of a hassle.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="eb7d241ef422af765c484d730b506dd2">
             All you have to do to change a default app is to download a new one and open it. Next time an app is required for a specific function, such as opening an image, you'll be asked which app you want to use for the job. Simply selecting the new one and tapping the "Always" button to let Android know you want it to be the default for the indefinite future will make the change.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="7f27235bef3964b4c9d826c1527f6c7a">
             If you ever want to undo this change, you can clear the defaults pretty easily. All you have to do is open your app drawer, find the current default app you want to clear, and tap and hold down on its icon like you're going to add a shortcut to your home screen. Instead of adding that shortcut, however, drag the app to the top of the screen where you'll see the text "App Info." Let go and a screen will appear with a bunch of settings for that app. You can do all sorts of things like force close it and clear its cache, but under the Defaults section you'll find a "Clear Defaults" button. Tap it and you're all set.

            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="28f43add881adbba73315974948f2841">
             Android offers a number of additional features in its notification drawer that you won't find in iOS. You still drag down from the top of the screen to bring it up, but dismissing notifications is a little easier. You can swipe any individual ones from left to right to get rid of them, or you can dismiss them all by tapping an icon up top that looks like three horizontal bars. Android's notification drawer also has a handy settings menu that you can view by tapping the little human icon in the upper-righthand corner. This makes it easy to check battery status, toggle airplane mode, change screen brightness, and much more.

              LockerPro
             </a>
             . It not only provides a similar experience to iOS, but offers quick app launching shortcuts and (in my opinion) a better design.

             Overall, you'll like how notifications work on Android. You can add any lacking functionality you liked about iOS through third-party apps and get a few bonus' the operating system adds by default.

This was not the case with Maps, and so you'll be very glad to make the switch to Google's phenomenal built-in navigation.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="bd6ecca8f56e6e6ba7dc366ba3b424fa">

If you want turn-by-turn navigation, you just tap the navigation button after getting directions and the Maps app will launch the Navigation app to handle your request. Again, this feels a little unnecessary and you'll find you're tapping through more menus and buttons than you would on iOSstrangely, even with Google's official Maps app for iOSbut it's a small price to pay for great navigation that's fully-integrated with the operating system.

               <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.danez.headphonecontroller&amp;feature=search_result#?t=W251bGwsMSwyLDEsImNvbS5kYW5lei5oZWFkcGhvbmVjb250cm9sbGVyIl0." target="_blank">
                Headphone Remote Control
               </a>
              </b>
              : iOS does a fantastic job of using the remote built into many wired headsets to easily control your phone. Android does not. Although not perfect, Headphone Control will add some of that functionality back.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="4283e29742a0dc26a21094f5558b8f29">
              <b>
               <a href="https://www.lookout.com/features/missing-device" target="_blank">
                Lookout
               </a>
              </b>
              : If you want Find My iPhone but for your Android, you'll want to check out this app. It pretty much does the same thing. (Thanks for the tip, Russ!)
             </li>
            </ul>

            <h3 data-textannotation-id="da6dbf0ff8d174f455fb76b8fb488815">
             Discover the Other Benefits of Android
            </h3>
            <p class="has-media media-640 has-image" data-textannotation-id="d575655d197d2f511a199f30a88a7ced">
             <span class="lightBoxWrapper">
              <span class="img-border">
               <img alt="Jumping Ship from iPhone to Android: A Switcher's Guide" data-asset-url="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--vbC1TUHE--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18g36k54c1boppng.png" data-chomp-id="18g36k54c1boppng" data-format="png" height="1060" src="http://i.kinja-img.com/gawker-media/image/upload/s--vbC1TUHE--/c_fit,fl_progressive,q_80,w_636/18g36k54c1boppng.png" width="636"/>
              </span>
              <span class="magnifier lightBox hide">
               <span class="icon icon-white icon-search-plus">
               </span>
              </span>
             </span>
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="53fff6b3a7724b3ca656b1d5980371d1">
             Android can do a lot of awesome stuff that your iPhone couldn't. You'll find it fun to discover these new features as you explore everything the operating system has to offer. Here are a few things you'll want to check out:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li data-textannotation-id="01b27ac7c0a11bc6474a3a61542eafec">
              <b>
               <a href="http://www.google.com/landing/now/" target="_blank">
                Google Now
               </a>
              </b>
              is, perhaps, one of the greatest additions to Android. If you want to research something or ask a question, you just search Google Now and it'll provide you with that information on a concise and attractive summary card. You can search via text or with your voiceit's up to you. Most Android phones will have a Google Search bar on the home screen by default, but you can bring up Google Now in your app drawer or by swiping up from the bottom of your home screen. For more on Google Now,
              <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5950162/how-to-turn-your-phone-into-a-mind+reading-personal-assistant" x-inset="1">
               read our guide
              </a>
              .
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="f1191a585fc9b627de07a97621986e4f">
              You can
              <b>
               <a href="https://play.google.com/store" target="_blank">
                install apps remotely through the Google Play Store
               </a>
              </b>
              even when you're away (or don't want to deal with) your phone. Just visit the store, search for the app you want, and click the buy/install button. You'll be asked which device to send the app to. Just select your phone and it'll be there in minutes.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="cb7aef39bb4e3f641c33bb71d3302fa0">
              <b>
               Smarter keyboards
              </b>
              are an option on Android. In fact, in Android 4.2 you can
              <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5955861/android-42-adds-gesture+based-typing-multiple-users-actionable-notifications-and-more" x-inset="1">
               use gesture-based typing
              </a>
              . Of course, you don't have to stick with the default. Android is flexible, and you can
              <a href="http://lifehacker.com/5922522/five-best-android-keyboards" x-inset="1">
               choose your favorite
              </a>
              .
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="9ee0f3b041d4b670ffee393871e6adeb">
              <b>
               <a href="http://www.google.com/wallet/" target="_blank">
                Google Wallet
               </a>
              </b>
              makes paying with your
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_field_communication" target="_blank">
               NFC
              </a>
              -enabled Android phone possible. You give it a credit card number and you'll instantly be able to pay by tapping your phone onto a platform at any participating store (in the real world).
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="e7f357f91df0b970933a8d85f5372bf0">
              <b>
               Lock screen widgets
              </b>
              add a little extra info to your lock screen. Just swipe from left to right and you'll find an empty box. Tap and hold that box to add a widget that'll be available on your lock screen when you need it.
             </li>
             <li data-textannotation-id="b1fb96fcb0d2d3363714bb896f60ab0d">
              <b>
               Explore the File System
              </b>
              is possible in Android. iOS doesn't give you direct access, so this is the sort of thing you might miss if you don't look for it. In Android, you can download files from web browsers and store things where you want them just like you would on a computer. Your phone likely comes with a file explorer app, but
              <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=pl.solidexplorer&amp;feature=search_result#?t=W251bGwsMSwxLDEsInBsLnNvbGlkZXhwbG9yZXIiXQ.." target="_blank">
               Solid Explorer
              </a>
              is my favorite alternative.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p data-textannotation-id="2e639d7ec8bea237343c032405d1ddda">
             This is just the beginning. As you explore Android, you'll likely find tons of new features that you love as well as apps and methods that help you through the transition.
            </p>
