   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lord of the Sabbath
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christ_and_the_Apostles_-_Tiffany_Glass_%26_Decorating_Company,_c._1890.JPG">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christ_and_the_Apostles_-_Tiffany_Glass_%26_Decorating_Company,_c._1890.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>

        <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_glass" title="Tiffany glass">

        </a>
        stained glass, 1890.

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Lord of the Sabbath
      </b>
      is an expression describing
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      which appears in all three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_Gospel" title="Synoptic Gospel">
       Synoptic Gospels
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Matthew&amp;verse=12:1-8&amp;src=NIV">
       Matthew 12:1-8
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Mark&amp;verse=2:23-28&amp;src=NIV">
       Mark 2:23-28
      </a>
      and
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Luke&amp;verse=6:1-5&amp;src=NIV">
       Luke 6:1-5
      </a>
      . These sections each relate an encounter between Jesus, his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostles" title="Apostles">
       Apostles
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Pharisees" title="Pharisees">
       Pharisees
      </a>
      , the first of the four "
      <a href="/wiki/Sabbath" title="Sabbath">
       Sabbath
      </a>
      controversies".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">
       Gospel of Mark
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       One Sabbath, Jesus was going through the grainfields, and as his disciples walked along, they began to pick some heads of grain. The Pharisees said to him, "Look, why are they doing what is unlawful on the Sabbath?" He answered, "Have you never read what David did when he and his companions were hungry and in need? In the days of Abiathar the high priest, he entered the house of God and ate the consecrated bread, which is lawful only for priests to eat. And he also gave some to his companions." Then he said to them, "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath. So the Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Some versions of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luke%27s_Gospel" title="Luke's Gospel">
       Luke's Gospel
      </a>
      provide a specific date for the incident - the second Sabbath after the first (likely to mean the Sabbaths counted from the
      <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">
       Feast of First Fruits
      </a>
      in accordance with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leviticus" title="Leviticus">
       Leviticus
      </a>
      23).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matthew%27s_Gospel" title="Matthew's Gospel">
       Matthew's Gospel
      </a>
      provides an additional example to justify working on the Sabbath:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       [According] to the law, on the Sabbath the priests in the
       <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">
        temple
       </a>
       profane the Sabbath, and are blameless
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      and there are two statements regarding Jesus' view of his role: he is Lord [even] of the Sabbath and also he is 'one greater than the Temple'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are different interpretations of the reference to the
      <a href="/wiki/Son_of_man_(Christianity)" title="Son of man (Christianity)">
       Son of man
      </a>
      statement in
      <a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/bibleversefinder/?book=Matthew&amp;verse=12:1-8&amp;src=NIV">
       Matthew 12:1-8
      </a>
      that "the Son of man is Lord of the Sabbath". It may mean that Jesus is claiming to be the Lord or that his Apostles are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_liberty" title="Christian liberty">
       entitled to do as they wish
      </a>
      on the Sabbath.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
