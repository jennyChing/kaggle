     <header>
      <h2 class="entry-title title" itemprop="name">
       Tech Candidates: Top bugbears with recruiters
      </h2>
      <p class="byline author vcard article-info">
       by
       <span class="author-info" itemprop="author" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
        <strong itemprop="name">
         Ben Robinson
        </strong>
       </span>
       on
       <time content="2014-05-08" datetime="2014-05-08" itemprop="datePublished">
        08 May 2014
       </time>
      </p>
     </header>
     <div class="entry-content" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="blog-img">
       <img alt="" class="responsive-img" src="https://technicallycompatible.blob.core.windows.net/websitemedia/1001/bugbears.jpg"/>
      </div>
      <p>
       Do you know the origin of the term bugbear? (Id be impressed if you did). In fact, bugbear can be traced back to the 16
       <sup>
        th
       </sup>
       century, appearing in English folklore as 
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bugbear" target="_blank">
        a creepy bear that lurked in the woods to scare children
       </a>
       , akin to the bogeyman.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now, Im not suggesting that the moral of this article is to compare recruiters with strange woodland bears, but there are certainly some recruiter behaviours that can be considered candidate pet peeves. By identifying them and implementing changes to processes accordingly, recruiters (both from specialist agencies and in-house HR staff) can aim to facilitate a better and more constructive relationship between themselves and the wider job market.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Poor communication:
      </h2>
      <p>
       Perhaps one of the biggest annoyances candidates face when hunting for jobs is poor (or a complete lack of) communication from recruitment representatives. This can occur at various different points in the recruitment funnel; from not receiving a simple acknowledgement to an application, to a lack of any constructive feedback at all should it be rejected. That first point in particular, gets on candidates nerves  when CVs and applications seemingly disappear into the recruitment abyss, never to be seen again. To be fair, some job adverts will specifically state that not all applications will receive a reply, simply due to the sheer volume expected  but are they being lazy, or just pragmatic?
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        'Am I the only getting annoying calls from IT Recruiters that promise you the best job of your life - then when you submit your resume you NEVER hear back from them?'
       </p>
       <cite>
        <a class="roll-link" href="https://www.linkedin.com/groups/Am-I-only-getting-annoying-48650.S.96918545">
         <span data-title="Wayne Stewart">
          Wayne Stewart
         </span>
        </a>
        , LinkedIn User
       </cite>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       If I may venture into the realm of opinion for a moment, I dont believe this reasoning to be as valid as it used to be. New products and services in the
       <a href="http://searchfinancialapplications.techtarget.com/news/2240210860/Candidate-relationship-management-tech-fills-recruitment-black-hole" target="_blank">
        CRM arena
       </a>
       (thats Candidate Relationship Management, rather than the more traditional acronym), have made it easier for recruiters to manage and communicate with candidates effectively, whether they are successful for not. If recruiters (be it agency based or HR representatives) are in a position where they are regularly receiving a high volume of applicants for positions, there is little excuse not to utilise such services, or introduce appropriate in-house processes.
      </p>
      <p>
       The danger here lies is damaging your employer and company brand. Inadequate recruitment communication is often a particularly short-sighted way of looking upon things. Have you ever applied for a job and not heard anything back? How did it make you feel about the company, both as a potential employee and a potential customer? Have you ever discussed a company with a friend and been told  Oh I applied for a job there and they didnt even bother getting back to me!
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        'Our applicant tracking system, Jobvite, sends an auto reply to acknowledge a candidate's application but upon resume review the recruiter will send a personalized note to the candidate either letting them know we want to move forward to the next step or thanking them but gently letting them know the role isn't a fit. Rejections are never fun but we have gotten so many replies back from candidates who are simply amazed that we took the time to get back to them.'
       </p>
       <cite>
        <a class="roll-link" href="http://www.zappos.com/">
         <span data-title="Christa Foley">
          Christa Foley
         </span>
        </a>
        , Recruiting Manager - Zappos.com
       </cite>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Negative word-of-mouth from a poor recruitment experience can seem like small fry, but if it happens enough, it could inadvertently come back to bite.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Unclear job adverts and descriptions:
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are various aspects of job adverts that can get on a candidates nerves. On the most simplistic level, some job adverts posted by third party recruiters do not specify who the company are and what wage is available (Competitive salaries anyone!?). Recruitment consultants will often not disclose the name of their client for fear that youll simply cut out the middle man and approach them yourself, or a rival agency will attempt to poach the opportunity. These are probably valid fears - as long as such highly relevant information is disclosed soon after application.
      </p>
      <p>
       Less forgiving however is intentionally vague job postings. For tech industries in particular, job descriptions and desired skills are often very particular, with highly specialist knowledge differentiating between roles. This means that, seemingly similar IT roles may in fact need quite a specialist employee. It is counter-intuitive therefore, for job postings to be vague and generic, lacking that required level of extra detail. This doesnt stop the practise though  some recruiters will intentionally post job adverts with the intention of throwing the net as wide as possible, thus increasing their applicant pool and eventually, their chance of gaining commission. In reality though, this simply leads to candidates making the time and effort to apply for roles, only to find some way down the road that they werent even suitable in the first place  very frustrating!
      </p>
      <p>
       Roger Emmens writes on one such example for
       <a href="http://www.techrepublic.com/blog/career-management/10-ways-in-which-recruiters-annoy-candidates/3273/#." target="_blank">
        TechRepublic
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        I just spent a couple of hours on two applications where the job posting sounded like a perfect fit for my background, which is services, products, and software. When I got to speak to the agent it became clear in both cases that hardware development experience was essential. Nowhere did the job description mention this. These applications were thus a complete waste of my time.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Now lets address it while were on the subject; the internet is awash with examples of people complaining about tech recruiters  so Ill try to avoid indulging in such activity. Theres no hiding from it, recruiters have a bad name, but is it just a few bad apples ruining it for the rest? Many dont get the credit or respect that they deserve as trusted partners in the resourcing process. Rather than preaching to the choir, its probably fair to suggest good recruiters will be fully aware of what they need to do to build a positive reputation (and avoid building a bad one). Candidates should rightly be careful about whom they select to represent their interests, but they shouldnt be afraid of engaging in the services of a recruitment agency for the sake of it. Perhaps take a read of
       <a href="http://www.frazerjohn.com/blog/recruitment-agencies-theyre-not-all-bad/" target="_blank">
        this article
       </a>
       via Frazer John (admittedly a recruitment agency)  there are some good practical tips in there to candidates find good and reliable agencies.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Inappropriate recruitment mechanisms:
      </h2>
      <p>
       In our February whitepaper regarding recruitment testing, we touched upon why some candidates felt that it could be unfair.As much as 40% suggested that, in some way, a computerised test they had been asked to undertake for a position wasnt fair, for a number of reasons:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Relevance to the role  Testing can be annoying if it isnt actually directly relevant to the role that is being applied for.
       </li>
       <li>
        Ease of use  some companies use dated online testing platforms with unintuitive and ugly user-interfaces.
       </li>
       <li>
        Whether testing is used on a standalone basis or as part of a wider assessment  Candidates can be frustrated if testing results are used independently as a reason for rejection. Better practise would be to use such results alongside accompanying recruitment mechanisms, to build a wider-picture of the candidate.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Recruiters should put themselves in a candidates shoes and make sure that all of the mechanisms used in a recruitment process are fair and not likely to cause unnecessary effort or frustration for candidates.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Got any comments or anecdotes that are relevant to our post? We'd love to hear from you via
        <a href="https://twitter.com/TechCompatible" target="_blank">
         Twitter
        </a>
        or
        <a href="https://www.facebook.com/TechnicallyCompatible" target="_blank">
         Facebook
        </a>
        !
       </strong>
      </p>
