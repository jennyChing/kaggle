     <p>
      <b>
       Jinn
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       jann
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       djinn
      </b>
      (singular:
      <b>
       jinn
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       djinni
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       genie
      </b>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
       Arabic
      </a>
      :
      <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
       
      </span>
      
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        al-jinn
       </span>
      </i>
      , singular
      <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        al-jinn
       </span>
      </i>
      ) are
      <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">
       supernatural
      </a>
      creatures in
      <a href="/wiki/Islamic_mythology" title="Islamic mythology">
       Islamic mythology
      </a>
      as well as pre-Islamic
      <a href="/wiki/Arabian_mythology" title="Arabian mythology">
       Arabian mythology
      </a>
      . They are mentioned frequently in the
      <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">
       Quran
      </a>
      (the 72nd
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura" title="Sura">
       sura
      </a>
      is titled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Jinn" title="Al-Jinn">
        Srat al-Jinn
       </a>
      </i>
      ) and other Islamic texts and inhabit an unseen world called
      <a href="/wiki/Djinnestan" title="Djinnestan">
       Djinnestan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Multiverse_(religion)#Islam" title="Multiverse (religion)">
       another universe
      </a>
      beyond the known universe. The Quran says that the
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      are made of a smokeless and "scorching fire",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but are also physical in nature, being able to interact in a tactile manner with people and objects and likewise be acted upon. The
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      , humans, and
      <a href="/wiki/Islamic_view_of_angels" title="Islamic view of angels">
       angels
      </a>
      make up the three known
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sapience" title="Sapience">
       sapient
      </a>
      creations of
      <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">
       God
      </a>
      . Like human beings, the
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      can be good, evil, or neutrally benevolent and hence have
      <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">
       free will
      </a>
      like humans and unlike angels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Devil_(Islam)" title="Devil (Islam)">
       shaytan
      </a>
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      are the analogue of
      <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">
       demons
      </a>
      in Christian tradition, but the
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      are not angels and the Quran draws a clear distinction between the two creations. The Quran states in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura" title="Sura">
       surat
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Kahf" title="Al-Kahf">
        Al-Kahf
       </a>
       (The Cave)
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ayah" title="Ayah">
       Ayah
      </a>
      50,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that
      <a href="/wiki/Devil_(Islam)" title="Devil (Islam)">
       Iblis
      </a>
      (Azazel) is one of the
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      .

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_definitions">
       Etymology and definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Jinn
      </i>
      is a noun of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collective_number" title="Collective number">
       collective number
      </a>
      in the Persian language literally meaning "hidden from sight", and it derives from the
      <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">
       Arabic root
      </a>
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        j-n-n
       </span>
      </i>
      (pronounced: jann/ junn  / ) meaning "to hide" or "be hidden". Other words derived from this root are
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        majnn
       </span>
      </i>
      'mad' (literally, 'one whose intellect is hidden'),
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        junn
       </span>
      </i>
      'madness', and
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        jann
       </span>
      </i>
      'embryo, fetus' ('hidden inside the womb').
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       genie
      </i>
      in English is derived from Latin
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Genius_(mythology)" title="Genius (mythology)">
        genius
       </a>
      </i>
      , meaning a sort of tutelary or guardian spirit thought to be assigned to each person at birth. English borrowed the French descendant of this word,
      <i>
       gnie
      </i>
      ; its earliest written attestation in English, in 1655, is a plural spelled "genyes". The French translators of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Book_of_One_Thousand_and_One_Nights" title="The Book of One Thousand and One Nights">
        The Book of One Thousand and One Nights
       </a>
      </i>
      used
      <i>
       gnie
      </i>
      as a translation of
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      because it was similar to the Arabic word in sound and in meaning. This use was also adopted in English and has since become dominant.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
       Arabic
      </a>
      , the word
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        jinn
       </span>
      </i>
      is in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collective_number" title="Collective number">
       collective number
      </a>
      , translated in English as plural (e.g., "several genies");
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        jinn
       </span>
      </i>
      is in the
      <a href="/wiki/Singulative_number" title="Singulative number">
       singulative number
      </a>
      , used to refer to one individual, which is translated by the singular in English (e.g., "one genie"). Therefore, the word
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
       English
      </a>
      writing is treated as a plural.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_pre-Islamic_era">
       In the pre-Islamic era
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In the pre-Islamic era">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Inscriptions found in Northwestern Arabia seem to indicate the worship of jinn, or at least their tributary status, hundreds of years before Islam. For instance, an inscription from Beth Fasi'el near
      <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">
       Palmyra
      </a>
      pays tribute to the "
      <i>
       jinnaye
      </i>
      ", the "good and rewarding gods".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Islam">
       In Islam
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="606" data-file-width="389" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Idris_the_prophet.jpg/220px-Idris_the_prophet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Idris_the_prophet.jpg/330px-Idris_the_prophet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e2/Idris_the_prophet.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Idris_the_prophet.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophet_Idris" title="Prophet Idris">
         Prophet Idris
        </a>
        visits Heaven (Paradise) and Hell,
        <a href="/wiki/Persian_miniature" title="Persian miniature">
         Persian miniature

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Islamic_theology" title="Islamic theology">
       Islamic theology
      </a>
      jinn are said to be creatures with
      <a href="/wiki/Predestination_in_Islam" title="Predestination in Islam">
       free will
      </a>
      , made from smokeless fire by
      <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">
       Allah
      </a>
      as humans were made of clay, among other things.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">
       Quran
      </a>
      , jinn have free will, and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Devil_(Islam)" title="Devil (Islam)">
        <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
         Ibls
        </span>
       </a>
      </i>
      abused this freedom in front of Allah by refusing to bow to
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_in_Islam" title="Adam in Islam">
       Adam
      </a>
      when Allah ordered angels and jinn to do so. For disobeying Allah, Ibls was expelled from Paradise and called "
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Devil_(Islam)" title="Devil (Islam)">
        <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
         Shaytn
        </span>
       </a>
      </i>
      " (Satan). Jinn are frequently mentioned in the
      <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">
       Quran
      </a>
      :
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura" title="Sura">
       Surah
      </a>
      72 (named
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Jinn" title="Al-Jinn">
        Srat al-Jinn
       </a>
      </i>
      ) is named after the jinn, and has a passage about them. Another surah (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Nas" title="Al-Nas">
        Srat al-Ns
       </a>
      </i>
      ) mentions jinn in the last verse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Quran also mentions that
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_in_Islam" title="Muhammad in Islam">
       Muhammad
      </a>
      was sent as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophets_of_Islam" title="Prophets of Islam">
       prophet
      </a>
      to both "humanity and the jinn", and that prophets and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_(Islam)" title="Apostle (Islam)">
       messengers
      </a>
      were sent to both communities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      They are usually invisible to humans, but humans do appear clearly to jinn, as they can possess them. Jinn have the power to travel large distances at extreme speeds and are thought to live in remote areas, mountains, seas, trees, and the air, in their own communities. Like humans, jinn will also be judged on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_of_Judgment" title="Day of Judgment">
       Day of Judgment
      </a>
      and will be sent to
      <a href="/wiki/Jannah" title="Jannah">
       Paradise
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Jahannam" title="Jahannam">
       Hell
      </a>
      according to their deeds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classifications_and_characteristics">
       Classifications and characteristics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classifications and characteristics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="910" data-file-width="606" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg/220px-Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg/330px-Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg/440px-Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kitab_al-Bulhan_---_devils_talking.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The black king of the djinns, Al-Malik al-Aswad, in the late 14th century
        <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Wonders" title="Book of Wonders">
         Book of Wonders
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The social organization of the jinn community resembles that of humans; e.g., they have kings, courts of law, weddings, and mourning rituals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One common belief in Muslim lore lists five distinct orders of
      <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">
       demons
      </a>
       the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Marid" title="Marid">
        Marid
       </a>
      </i>
      (the strongest type), the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ifrit" title="Ifrit">
        Ifrit
       </a>
      </i>
      , the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaitan" title="Shaitan">
        Shaitan
       </a>
      </i>
      , the
      <i>
       Jinn
      </i>
      , and the
      <i>
       Jann
      </i>
      (the weakest type). A few traditions (hadith), divide jinn into three classes: those who have wings and fly in the air, those who resemble snakes and dogs, and those who travel about ceaselessly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      described them as creatures of different forms; some resembling vultures and snakes, others tall men in white garb.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They may even appear as dragons,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onagers" title="Onagers">
       onagers
      </a>
      , or a number of other animals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition to their animal forms, the jinn occasionally assume human form to mislead and destroy their human victims.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Certain
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadiths" title="Hadiths">
       hadiths
      </a>
      have also claimed that the jinn may subsist on bones, which will grow flesh again as soon as they touch them, and that their animals may live on dung, which will revert to grain or grass for the use of the jinn flocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Taymiyyah" title="Ibn Taymiyyah">
       Ibn Taymiyyah
      </a>
      believed the jinn were generally "ignorant, untruthful, oppressive and treacherous",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taym_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-taym-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      thus representing the very strict interpretations adhered by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salafi" title="Salafi">
       Salafi
      </a>
      schools of thought. Ibn Taymiyyah believes that the jinn account for much of the "magic" perceived by humans, cooperating with magicians to lift items in the air unseen, delivering hidden truths to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortune_teller" title="Fortune teller">
       fortune tellers
      </a>
      , and mimicking the voices of deceased humans during
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seance" title="Seance">
       seances
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taym_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-taym-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Srat
      <a href="/wiki/Ar-Rahman" title="Ar-Rahman">
       al-Ramn
      </a>
      , verse 33, God reminds jinn as well as mankind that they would possess the ability to pass beyond the furthest reaches of space only by His authority, followed by the question: "Then which of the favors of your Lord will you deny?" In Srat
      <a href="/wiki/Al-Jinn" title="Al-Jinn">
       Al-Jinn
      </a>
      , verses 810, Allah narrates concerning the jinn how they touched or "sought the limits" of the sky and found it full of stern guards and shooting stars, as a warning to man. It goes on further to say how the jinn used to take stations in the skies to listen to divine decrees passed down through the ranks of the angels (Sura al Jinn verse 9),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but those who attempt to listen now (during and after the revelation of the Quran) shall find fiery sentinels awaiting them. The Quran forbids their association with God, and advises men not to worship jinns instead of Him, Quran Says " And they imagine kinship between him and the jinn, whereas the jinn know well that they will be brought before (Him)", Quran Surah 37, Verse 158.
     </p>
     <p>
      Seven kings of the Jinn are traditionally associated with days of the week.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lebling2010_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lebling2010-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Sunday: Al-Mudhib (Abu 'Abdallah Sa'id)
      </li>
      <li>
       Monday: Murrah al-Abyad Abu al-Harith (Abu al-Nur)
      </li>
      <li>
       Tuesday: Abu Mihriz (or Abu Ya'qub) Al-Ahmar
      </li>
      <li>
       Wednesday: Barqan Abu al-'Adja'yb
      </li>
      <li>
       Thursday: Shamhurish (al-Tayyar)
      </li>
      <li>
       Friday: Abu Hasan Zoba'ah (al-Abyad)
      </li>
      <li>
       Saturday: Abu Nuh Maimun
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Qar.C4.ABn">
       Qarn
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Qarn">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A related belief is that every person is assigned one's own special jinni, also called a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Qareen" title="Qareen">
        qarn
       </a>
      </i>
      , and if the
      <i>
       qarin
      </i>
      is evil it could whisper to people's souls and tell them to submit to evil desires.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The notion of a
      <i>
       qarin
      </i>
      is not universally accepted among all Muslims, but it is generally accepted that
      <a href="/wiki/Devil_(Islam)" title="Devil (Islam)">
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        Shayn
       </span>
      </a>
      whispers in human minds, and he is assigned to each human being.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a hadith recorded by
      <a href="/wiki/Muslim_ibn_al-Hajjaj" title="Muslim ibn al-Hajjaj">
       Muslim ibn al-Hajjaj
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Sahabah" title="Sahabah">
       companion
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdullah,_son_of_Masud" title="Abdullah, son of Masud">
       Abdullah, son of Masud
      </a>
      reported: 'The Prophet
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
       Muhammad
      </a>
      said: 'There is not one of you who does not have a jinn appointed to be his constant companion (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Qareen" title="Qareen">
        qarn
       </a>
      </i>
      ).' They said, 'And you too, O Messenger of Allah?' He said, 'Me too, but Allah has helped me and he has submitted, so that he only helps me to do good.'
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solomon_and_the_Jinn">
       Solomon and the Jinn
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solomon and the Jinn">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Solomon_in_Islam" title="Solomon in Islam">
       Solomon in Islam
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to traditions, the jinn stood behind the learned humans in Solomon's court, who in turn, sat behind the prophets. The jinn remained in the service of Solomon, who had placed them in bondage, and had ordered them to perform a number of tasks.
     </p>
     <p>
      "And before Solomon were marshalled his hosts,- of jinn and men and birds, and they were all kept in order and ranks." (Quran 27:17)
     </p>
     <p>
      The Quran relates that Solomon died while he was leaning on his staff. As he remained upright, propped on his staff, the jinn thought he was still alive and supervising them, so they continued to work. They realized the truth only when Allah sent a creature to crawl out of the ground and gnaw at Solomon's staff until his body collapsed. The Quran then comments that if they had known the unseen, they would not have stayed in the humiliating torment of being enslaved.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Then, when We decreed (Solomon's) death, nothing showed them his death except a little worm of the earth, which kept (slowly) gnawing away at his staff: so when he fell down, the jinn saw plainly that if they had known the unseen, they would not have tarried in the humiliating penalty (of their task)." (Quran 34:14)

     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Bible">
       In the Bible
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In the Bible">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The word or concept of jinn as such does not occur in the original Hebrew text of the Bible, but the Arabic word
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        jinn
       </span>
      </i>
      is often used in several old Arabic translations. In Isaiah 6, the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Seraph" title="Seraph">
        seraphim
       </a>
      </i>
      (lit. "burning/fiery ones") appear to
      <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">
       Isaiah
      </a>
      , with their six wings being used to cover, or
      <i>
       hide
      </i>
      , their body, face and feet.
     </p>
     <p>
      In several verses in those
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_translations_by_language" title="Bible translations by language">
       Urdu
      </a>
      translations, the words
      <i>
       jinn
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
       
      </span>
      ),
      <i>
       jann
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        al-jnn
       </span>
      </i>
      ), majnoon (
      <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        Majnn
       </span>
      </i>
      ), and
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        Ibls
       </span>
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
       
      </span>
      ) are mentioned as translations of "familiar spirit" or  (J
      <i>
       ob
      </i>
      ) for
      <i>
       jann
      </i>
      and "the devil" or
      <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
       
      </span>
      (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Daemon_(classical_mythology)" title="Daemon (classical mythology)">
        daimnion
       </a>
      </i>
      ) for
      <i>
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="ar ALA transliteration">
        Ibls
       </span>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Several passages from the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
       New Testament
      </a>
      refer to Jesus casting out evil spirits (or demons) from those that were demon-possessed. According to Islamic tradition, these evil spirits are strikingly similar to the jinn creatures mentioned in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">
       Qur'an
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">
       Hadith
      </a>
      literature. Among the similarities of these creatures is their ability to take possession of human beings.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornelius_Van_Allen_Van_Dyck" title="Cornelius Van Allen Van Dyck">
       Van Dyck
      </a>
      's Arabic translation of the Bible, these words are mentioned in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leviticus" title="Leviticus">
       Leviticus
      </a>
      19:31, Lev 20:6,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Samuel" title="1 Samuel">
       1 Samuel
      </a>
      28:3, 1 Sa 28:9, 1 Sa 28:7,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Chronicles" title="1 Chronicles">
       1 Chronicles
      </a>
      10:13,
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">
       Gospel of Matthew
      </a>
      4:1, Mat 12:22,
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">
       Gospel of Luke
      </a>
      4:5, Luk 8:12,
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">
       Gospel of John
      </a>
      8:44 and other verses
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      as well. Also, in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocrapha" title="Apocrapha">
       apocryphal
      </a>
      book
      <a href="/wiki/Testament_of_Solomon" title="Testament of Solomon">
       Testament of Solomon
      </a>
      , Solomon describes particular
      <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">
       demons
      </a>
      whom he enslaved to help build the
      <a href="/wiki/Solomon%27s_Temple" title="Solomon's Temple">
       temple
      </a>
      , the questions he put to them about their deeds and how they could be thwarted, and their answers, which provide a kind of self-help manual against demonic activity.
     </p>
