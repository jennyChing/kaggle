     <p>
      <b>
       Photo manipulation
      </b>
      is a process to transform a photograph into a desired image. There are currentlty many different software applications to choose from ranging from professional applications to basic imaging software for the casual user.
     </p>

     <p>
      Photo manipulation is as old as photography itself, and its history can be seen as part of the history of image manipulation in general, which can be found already in Ancient Egypt. So Kanawati
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      gives examples where images have been removed to penalize persons.
     </p>
     <p>
      Before computers, photo manipulation was achieved by retouching with ink,
      <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">
       paint
      </a>
      , double-exposure, piecing photos or negatives together in the
      <a href="/wiki/Darkroom" title="Darkroom">
       darkroom
      </a>
      , or scratching
      <a href="/wiki/Polaroid_Corporation" title="Polaroid Corporation">
       Polaroids
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Airbrush" title="Airbrush">
       Airbrushes
      </a>
      were also used, whence the term "
      <a href="/wiki/Airbrush#Photo_retouching" title="Airbrush">
       airbrushing
      </a>
      " for manipulation. Darkroom manipulations are sometimes regarded as traditional art rather than job related skill. In the early days of photography, the use of technology was not as advanced and efficient as it is now. Results are similar to digital manipulation but they are harder to create.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Photo_manipulation_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Photo_manipulation-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An early example of tampering was in the early 1860s, when a photo of
      <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">
       Abraham Lincoln
      </a>
      was altered using the body from a portrait of
      <a href="/wiki/John_C._Calhoun" title="John C. Calhoun">
       John C. Calhoun
      </a>
      and the head of Lincoln from a famous seated portrait by
      <a href="/wiki/Mathew_Brady" title="Mathew Brady">
       Mathew Brady
      </a>
       the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_five-dollar_bill#The_redesigned_.245_bill" title="United States five-dollar bill">
       same portrait
      </a>
      which was the basis for the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_five-dollar_bill#Small_size_note_history" title="United States five-dollar bill">
       original
      </a>
      Lincoln
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_five-dollar_bill" title="United States five-dollar bill">
       Five-dollar bill
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 1980s saw the advent of digital retouching with

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_and_ethical_issues">
       Political and ethical issues
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political and ethical issues">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Contrary to the idea of a photo having inherent
      <a href="/wiki/Verisimilitude" title="Verisimilitude">
       verisimilitude
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement">
         <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2012)">
          dubious
         </span>
        </a>
        <span class="metadata">
         
         <a href="/wiki/Talk:Photo_manipulation#Dubious" title="Talk:Photo manipulation">
          discuss
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Photo manipulation has been regularly used to deceive or persuade viewers, or for improved storytelling and self-expression. Often even subtle and discreet changes can have profound impacts on how we interpret or judge a photograph
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      which is why learning when manipulation has occurred is important. As early as the American Civil War, photographs were published as engravings based on more than one negative.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">
       Joseph Stalin
      </a>
      made use of photo retouching for propaganda purposes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On May 5, 1920 his predecessor
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">
       Vladimir Lenin
      </a>
      held a speech for Soviet troops that
      <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">
       Leon Trotsky
      </a>
      attended. Stalin had Trotsky retouched out of a photograph showing Trotsky in attendance.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In a well known case of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Damnatio_memoriae" title="Damnatio memoriae">
        damnatio memoriae
       </a>
      </i>
      image manipulation,
      <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">
       NKVD
      </a>
      leader
      <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Yezhov" title="Nikolai Yezhov">
       Nikolai Yezhov
      </a>
      (the "Vanishing Commissar"), after his execution in 1940, was removed from an official press photo where he was pictured with Stalin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (For more information, see
      <a href="/wiki/Censorship_of_images_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Censorship of images in the Soviet Union">
       Censorship of images in the Soviet Union
      </a>
      .) The pioneer among journalists distorting photographic images for news value was
      <a href="/wiki/Bernarr_Macfadden" title="Bernarr Macfadden">
       Bernarr Macfadden
      </a>
      : in the mid-1920s, his "
      <a href="/wiki/Composograph" title="Composograph">
       composograph
      </a>
      " process involved reenacting real news events with costumed
      <a href="/wiki/Body_double" title="Body double">
       body doubles
      </a>
      and then photographing the dramatized scenesthen pasting faces of the real news-personalities (gathered from unrelated photos) onto his staged images. In the 1930s, artist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Heartfield" title="John Heartfield">
       John Heartfield
      </a>
      used a type of photo manipulation known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Photomontage" title="Photomontage">
       photomontage
      </a>
      to critique Nazi propaganda.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some ethical theories have been applied to image manipulation. During a panel on the topic of ethics in image manipulation
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Aude Oliva theorized that categorical shifts are necessary in order for an edited image to be viewed as a manipulation. In
      <i>
       Image Act Theory
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Carson Reynolds extended
      <a href="/wiki/Speech_act" title="Speech act">
       speech act
      </a>
      theory by applying it to photo editing and image manipulations. In
      <i>
       How to Do Things with Pictures,
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      William Mitchell details the long history of photo manipulation and discusses it critically.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_journalism">
       Use in journalism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in journalism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Photojournalism#Ethical_and_legal_considerations" title="Photojournalism">
       Photojournalism  Ethical and legal considerations
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A notable case of a controversial photo manipulation was a 1982
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Magazine" title="National Geographic Magazine">
        National Geographic
       </a>
      </i>
      cover in which editors photographically moved two Egyptian pyramids closer together so that they would fit on a vertical cover. This case triggered a debate about the appropriateness of photo manipulation in journalism;
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      the argument against editing was that the magazine depicted something that did not exist, and presented it as fact. There were several cases since the
      <i>
       National Geographic
      </i>
      case of questionable photo manipulation, including editing a photo of
      <a href="/wiki/Cher" title="Cher">
       Cher
      </a>
      on the cover of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Redbook" title="Redbook">
        Redbook
       </a>
      </i>
      to change her smile and her dress. Another example occurred in early 2005, when
      <a href="/wiki/Martha_Stewart" title="Martha Stewart">
       Martha Stewart
      </a>
      's release from prison was featured on the cover of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">
        Newsweek
       </a>
      </i>
      ; her face was placed on a slimmer woman's body to suggest that she had lost weight while in prison.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another famous instance of controversy over photo manipulation, this time concerning race, arose in the summer of 1994. After
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O.J._Simpson" title="O.J. Simpson">
       O.J. Simpson
      </a>
      was arrested for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O.J._Simpson_murder_case" title="O.J. Simpson murder case">
       allegedly murdering his wife and her friend
      </a>
      , multiple publications carried his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mugshot" title="Mugshot">
       mugshot
      </a>
      . Notably,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      published an edition featuring an altered mugshot credited to
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Mahurin" title="Matt Mahurin">
       Matt Mahurin
      </a>
      , removing the photograph's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color_saturation" title="Color saturation">
       color saturation
      </a>
      (making, perhaps inadvertently, Simpson's skin darker),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Dodging_and_burning" title="Dodging and burning">
       burning
      </a>
      the corners, and reducing the size of the prisoner ID number. This appeared on newsstands right next to an unaltered picture by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">
        Newsweek
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A further noted example is the
      <a href="/wiki/Adnan_Hajj_photographs_controversy" title="Adnan Hajj photographs controversy">
       Adnan Hajj photographs controversy
      </a>
      (2006), when the photographer in question retouched war images using the clone tool to increase the size of a smoke plume and to duplicate flares.
     </p>
     <p>
      Photo manipulation alters the content of the images in a devious manner. It becomes difficult for the audience to differentiate between a manipulated image and reality. But Photoshop's popularity has proven to be divisive. While some laud it for its ability to allow subjects to look their best in a photograph, others see it as a vehicle for feeding our culture's desire for ultimate perfection. Manipulated images are created to deceive the audiences and form their understanding on how the media presents everything with perfection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Photo_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Photo-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore, with digital democratization
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
         <span title="You can help  (March 2012)">
          vague
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      increasing at a rapid rate it
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
         <span title="You can help  (March 2012)">
          vague
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      is creating problems. Since more people have access to technology it
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
         <span title="You can help  (March 2012)">
          vague
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      creates curiosity in the readers mind when they see an image published in newspapers or magazines. The reader begins to question the ethics of the publication which results in a debate. Photo images were considered as a reliable source and were known as a medium of communication to present the truth to the media.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Photo_manipulation_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Photo_manipulation-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a growing body of writings devoted to the ethical use of digital editing in
      <a href="/wiki/Photojournalism" title="Photojournalism">
       photojournalism
      </a>
      . In the United States, for example, the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Press_Photographers_Association" title="National Press Photographers Association">
       National Press Photographers Association
      </a>
      (NPPA) have set out a Code of Ethics promoting the accuracy of published images, advising that photographers "do not manipulate images [...] that can mislead viewers or misrepresent subjects."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Infringements of the Code are taken very seriously, especially regarding digital alteration of published photographs, as evidenced by a case in which
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulitzer_prize" title="Pulitzer prize">
       Pulitzer prize
      </a>
      -nominated photographer
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Detrich" title="Allan Detrich">
       Allan Detrich
      </a>
      resigned his post following the revelation that a number of his photographs had been manipulated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_glamour_photography">
       Use in glamour photography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use in glamour photography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The photo manipulation industry has often been accused of promoting or inciting a distorted and unrealistic image of self; most specifically in younger people. The world of
      <a href="/wiki/Glamour_photography" title="Glamour photography">
       glamour photography
      </a>
      is one specific industry which has been heavily involved with the use of photo manipulation (an obviously concerning element as many people look up to celebrities in search of embodying the 'ideal figure').
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photo_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-photo-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Manipulation of a photo to alter a models appearance can be used to change features such as skin complexion, hair color, body shape, and other features. Many of the alterations to skin involve removing blemishes through the use of the healing tool in Photoshop. Photo editors may also alter the color of hair to remove roots or add shine. Additionally, the models teeth and eyes may be made to look whiter than they are in reality. Make up and piercings can even be edited into pictures to look as though the model was wearing them when the photo was taken. Through photo editing, the appearance of a model may be drastically changed to mask imperfections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Support_for_photo_manipulation_in_media">

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Some editors of magazine companies do not view manipulating their cover models as an issue. In an interview with the editor of the French magazine
      <a href="/wiki/Marie_Claire" title="Marie Claire">
       Marie Claire
      </a>
      , she stated that their readers are not idiots and that they can tell when a model has been retouched. Also, some who support photo manipulation in the media state that the altered photographs are not the issue, but that it is the expectations that viewers have that they fail to meet, such as wanting to have the same body as a celebrity on the cover of their favorite magazine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Surveys_done_regarding_photo_manipulation">
       Surveys done regarding photo manipulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Surveys done regarding photo manipulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Surveys have been done to see how photo manipulation affects society and to see what society thinks of it. One survey was done by a fashion store in the United Kingdom,
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/New_Look" title="New Look">
       New Look
      </a>
      , and it showed that 90% of the individuals surveyed would prefer seeing a wider variety of body shapes in media. This would involve them wanting to see cover models that are not all thin, but some with more curves than others. The survey also talked about how readers view the use of photo manipulation. One statistic stated that 15% of the readers believed that the cover images are accurate depictions of the model in reality. Also, they found that 33% of women who were surveyed are aiming for a body that is impossible for them to attain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dove" title="Dove">
       Dove
      </a>
      also did a survey to see how photo manipulation affects the self-esteem of females. In doing this, they found that 80% of the woman surveyed felt insecure when seeing photos of celebrities in the media. Of the woman surveyed who had lower self-esteem, 71% of them do not believe that their appearance is pretty or stylish enough in comparison to cover models.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_cultural_implications">
       Social and cultural implications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Social and cultural implications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The growing popularity of image manipulation has raised concern as to whether it allows for unrealistic images to be portrayed to the public. In her article "
      <a href="/wiki/On_Photography" title="On Photography">
       On Photography
      </a>
      " (1977),
      <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">
       Susan Sontag
      </a>
      discusses the objectivity, or lack thereof, in photography, concluding that "photographs, which fiddle with the scale of the world, themselves get reduced, blown up, cropped, retouched, doctored and tricked out".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A practice widely used in the magazine industry, the use of photo manipulation on an already subjective photograph, creates a constructed reality for the individual and it can become difficult to differentiate fact from fiction. With the potential to alter body image, debate continues as to whether manipulated images, particularly those in magazines, contribute to self-esteem issues in both men and women.
     </p>
     <p>
      In today's world, photo manipulation has a positive impact by developing the creativity of one's mind or maybe a negative one by removing the art and beauty of capturing something so magnificent and natural or the way it should be. According to the Huffington Post, Photoshopping and airbrushing, many believe, are now an inherent part of the beauty industry, as are makeup, lighting and styling. In a way, these image alterations are selling actual people to the masses to affect responses, reactions, and emotions toward these cultural icons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_digital_photo_manipulation">
       Types of digital photo manipulation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Types of digital photo manipulation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In digital editing, photographs are usually taken with a
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera" title="Digital camera">
       digital camera
      </a>
      and input directly into a computer.
      <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">
       Transparencies
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Negative_(photography)" title="Negative (photography)">
       negatives
      </a>
      or printed photographs may also be
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digitized" title="Digitized">
       digitized
      </a>
      using a
      <a href="/wiki/Image_scanner" title="Image scanner">
       scanner
      </a>
      , or images may be obtained from
      <a href="/wiki/Stock_photography" title="Stock photography">
       stock photography
      </a>
      databases. With the advent of computers,
      <a href="/wiki/Graphics_tablet" title="Graphics tablet">
       graphics tablets
      </a>
      , and digital cameras, the term
      <i>
       image editing
      </i>
      encompasses everything that can be done to a photo, whether in a
      <a href="/wiki/Darkroom" title="Darkroom">
       darkroom
      </a>
      or on a computer. Photo manipulation is often much more explicit than subtle alterations to color balance or contrast and may involve overlaying a head onto a different body or changing a sign's text, for examples. Image editing software can be used to apply effects and warp an image until the desired result is achieved. The resulting image may have little or no resemblance to the photo (or photos in the case of
      <a href="/wiki/Compositing" title="Compositing">
       compositing
      </a>
      ) from which it originated. Today, photo manipulation is widely accepted as an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_form" title="Art form">
       art form
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      There are several subtypes of digital image-retouching:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Technical retouching
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Manipulation for photo restoration or enhancement (adjusting colors / contrast / white balance (i.e. gradational retouching), sharpness, noise, removing elements or visible flaws on skin or materials, ...)
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Creative retouching
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Used as an art form or for commercial use to create more sleek and interesting images for advertisements. Creative retouching could be manipulation for fashion, beauty or advertising photography such as pack-shots (which could also be considered inherently technical retouching in regards to package dimensions and wrap-around factors). One of the most prominent disciplines in creative retouching is image compositing whereby the digital artist uses multiple photos to create a single image. Today,
      <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">
       3D computer graphics
      </a>
      are used more and more to add extra elements or even locations and backgrounds. This kind of image composition is widely used when conventional photography would be technically too difficult or impossible to shoot on location or in studio.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Photoshopping">
       Photoshopping
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photo_manipulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Photoshopping">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Photoshopping
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">
       neologism
      </a>
      for the digital editing of photos.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term originates from
      <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop" title="Adobe Photoshop">
       Adobe Photoshop
      </a>
      , the image editor most commonly used by professionals for this purpose;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      however, any image-editing program could be used, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paint_Shop_Pro" title="Paint Shop Pro">
       Paint Shop Pro
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corel_Photopaint" title="Corel Photopaint">
       Corel Photopaint
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pixelmator" title="Pixelmator">
       Pixelmator
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paint.NET" title="Paint.NET">
       Paint.NET
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/GIMP" title="GIMP">
       GIMP
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Systems" title="Adobe Systems">
       Adobe Systems
      </a>
      , the publisher of Adobe Photoshop, discourages use of the term "photoshop" as a
      <a href="/wiki/Conversion_(word_formation)" title="Conversion (word formation)">
       verb
      </a>
      out of concern that it may become
      <a href="/wiki/Generic_trademark" title="Generic trademark">
       generic
      </a>
      , undermining the company's trademark.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite this,
      <i>
       photoshop
      </i>
      is widely used as a verb, both colloquially and academically, to refer to retouching,
      <a href="/wiki/Compositing" title="Compositing">
       compositing
      </a>
      (or splicing), and
      <a href="/wiki/Color_balance" title="Color balance">
       color balancing
      </a>
      carried out in the course of
      <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">
       graphic design
      </a>
      , commercial
      <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">
       publishing
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Image_editing" title="Image editing">
       image editing
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In popular culture, the term
      <i>
       photoshopping
      </i>
      is sometimes associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Photomontage" title="Photomontage">
       montages
      </a>
      in the form of visual jokes, such as those published on
      <a href="/wiki/Fark" title="Fark">
       Fark
      </a>
      and in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAD_Magazine" title="MAD Magazine">
       MAD Magazine
      </a>
      . Images may be propagated
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">
       memetically
      </a>
      via e-mail as humor or passed as actual news in a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">
       hoax
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      An example of the latter category is "
      <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_Shark" title="Helicopter Shark">
       Helicopter Shark
      </a>
      ," which was widely circulated as a so-called "
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">
        National Geographic
       </a>
      </i>
      Photo of the Year" and was later revealed to be a hoax.

       <a href="#cite_note-39">

     </p>
