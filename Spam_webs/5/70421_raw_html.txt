            <p>
             The comet landing was the iconic science story of 2014, capturing the imagination of a global audience. On 12 November, the little Philae probe detached from its mothership Rosetta, beginning a seven-hour descent to the surface of comet 67P.
            </p>
            <p>
             There was elation as the probe touched down, then drama as it
             <a class="story-body__link" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-30026398">
              bounced a kilometre up
             </a>
             , followed by dismay as Philae came to rest in a high-walled trap that prevented sunlight reaching its solar panels. But scientists made the most of their window of opportunity, gathering as much data as possible before the little probe's battery life ebbed away.
            </p>
            <p>
             Results started to emerge quickly, with two instrument teams declaring their discovery of carbon-based
             <a class="story-body__link" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-30097648">
              (organic) compounds on the comet
             </a>
             , and another concluding that objects like 67P were probably
             <a class="story-body__link" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-30414519">
              not the source of Earth's water
             </a>
             .
            </p>

             <a class="story-body__link-external" href="https://insights.spotify.com/br/2014/11/21/aerosmith-rosetta-comet/">
              showed a massive spike
             </a>

                &lt;/figcaption&gt;&lt;/figure&gt;&lt;p&gt;In March, scientists organised a news conference to trumpet "spectacular" evidence to support &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-26605974" class="story-body__link"&gt;the Big Bang theory for the origin of the Universe&lt;/a&gt;. The American team working on the BICEP2 experiment at the South Pole claimed to have discovered the signal left in the sky by the rapid expansion of space trillionths of a second after the cosmos came into being. &lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;This exponential growth spurt, known as cosmic inflation, left its signature as a distinctive twist in the oldest light detectable with telescopes, according to the team. But it wasn't long before the cracks started to appear, re-igniting the debate about science-by-press release. &lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Other groups countered that the team had underestimated the &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-29305985" class="story-body__link"&gt;confounding effects of dust&lt;/a&gt; in our galaxy, and by June the US scientists who made the claim revealed that they were &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-27935479" class="story-body__link"&gt;less confident of their result&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;&lt;h2 class="story-body__crosshead"&gt;Tractor beams and cosmic webs&lt;/h2&gt;&lt;figure class="media-landscape full-width has-caption"&gt;&lt;div class="js-delayed-image-load" alt="Harrison Ford, Carrie Fisher and Mark Hamill in Star Wars (1977)" data-src="http://ichef-1.bbci.co.uk/news/624/media/images/79932000/jpg/_79932651_79932650.jpg" data-width="624" data-height="351"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
                &lt;figcaption class="media-caption"&gt;
                    &lt;span class="media-caption__text"&gt;
                        Tractor beams have traditionally been the preserve of sci-fi films - such as Star Wars
                    &lt;/span&gt;
                &lt;/figcaption&gt;&lt;/figure&gt;&lt;p&gt;While the jury remains out on BICEP2, the year produced plenty of other physics and astronomy discoveries to marvel at. In January, astronomers outlined how they had used a natural "cosmic flashlight" to trace out the &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-25809967" class="story-body__link"&gt;hidden tendrils of dark matter&lt;/a&gt; that underlie the visible Universe. &lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;And one of the staples of science fiction - the tractor beam - came into its own this year. Scientists have been saying for several years that the devices, which can trap and manipulate objects and are featured in the Star Wars films, were theoretically possible. &lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Several lab-based versions appeared in 2014, with teams coming up with different ways of moving objects around, including the use of specific &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-28739368" class="story-body__link"&gt;wave patterns in water&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-scotland-tayside-central-27636019" class="story-body__link"&gt;ultrasound&lt;/a&gt;, and a &lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-29708358" class="story-body__link"&gt;doughnut-shaped laser&lt;/a&gt;. &lt;/p&gt;&lt;p&gt;Follow Paul &lt;a href="http://twitter.com/rincon_p" class="story-body__link-external"&gt;on Twitter&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;
