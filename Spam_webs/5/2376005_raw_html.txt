   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Finite potential well
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       finite potential well
      </b>
      (also known as the
      <b>
       finite square well
      </b>
      ) is a concept from
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">
       quantum mechanics
      </a>
      . It is an extension of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infinite_potential_well" title="Infinite potential well">
       infinite potential well
      </a>
      , in which a particle is confined to a box, but one which has finite
      <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">
       potential
      </a>
      walls. Unlike the infinite potential well, there is a
      <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">
       probability
      </a>
      associated with the particle being found outside the box. The quantum mechanical interpretation is unlike the classical interpretation, where if the total
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      of the particle is less than the potential energy barrier of the walls it cannot be found outside the box. In the quantum interpretation, there is a non-zero probability of the particle being outside the box even when the energy of the particle is less than the potential energy barrier of the walls (cf
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_tunnelling" title="Quantum tunnelling">
       quantum tunnelling
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Particle_in_a_1-dimensional_box">
       Particle in a 1-dimensional box
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finite_potential_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Particle in a 1-dimensional box">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      For the 1-dimensional case on the
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      -axis, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-independent_Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Time-independent Schrdinger equation">
       time-independent Schrdinger equation
      </a>
      can be written as:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="-\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \frac{d^2 \psi}{d x^2} + V(x) \psi = E \psi \quad (1)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/6/d/96d815b6c44baf5731932944b97cb949.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\hbar = \frac{h}{2 \pi}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/6/a/06a64fbc10d59c310c5a0107d9ab9268.png"/>
       ,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="h \," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/c/4/7c4073ca34bcc95361750a3f1fddc7a8.png"/>
       is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plancks_constant" title="Plancks constant">
        Planck's constant
       </a>
       ,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="m \," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/9/d/79dd9720ffa5bbe026e23afc9ab4df3c.png"/>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       of the particle,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi\," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/d/7bd3df974219dcf8a390bfa9b79565d1.png"/>
       is the (complex valued)
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction" title="Wavefunction">
        wavefunction
       </a>
       that we want to find,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="V\left(x\right)\," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/6/be68d1e44ec56846b9b3e268d205b40e.png"/>
       is a function describing the potential energy at each point
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       , and
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="E\," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/8/4b88f47f80273fd5788e1e20aa81c38a.png"/>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       , a real number, sometimes called eigenenergy.
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <br/>
      For the case of the particle in a 1-dimensional box of length
      <i>
       L
      </i>
      , the potential is zero inside the box, but rises abruptly to a value
      <img alt="V_o" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/3/613dc95ccddd5d8fdcc03b1bf5fc03fd.png"/>
      at
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      = -
      <i>
       L/2
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      =
      <i>
       L/2
      </i>
      . The wavefunction is considered to be made up of different wavefuctions at different ranges of
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      , depending on whether
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      is inside or outside of the box. Therefore the wavefunction is defined such that:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi = \begin{cases} \psi_1, &amp; \mbox{if }x&lt;-L/2\mbox{ (the region outside the box)} \\ \psi_2, &amp; \mbox{if }-L/2&lt;x&lt;L/2\mbox{ (the region inside the box)} \\ \psi_3 &amp; \mbox{if }x&gt;L/2\mbox{  (the region outside the box)} \end{cases}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/d/09d02b84b09a87988776cc762804304a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inside_the_box">
       Inside the box
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finite_potential_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inside the box">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For the region inside the box
      <i>
       V
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      ) = 0 and Equation 1 reduces to
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="-\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \frac{d^2 \psi_2}{d x^2} = E \psi_2 ." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/e/1/7e1c507868767f1f4adbfbe8f084c261.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Letting
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="k = \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar}," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/a/d/fade294e68996d50318aa82f146b3449.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      the equation becomes
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\frac{d^2 \psi_2}{d x^2} = -k^2 \psi_2 ." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/6/f/56f380202d4f659bd7f5f55279b86e46.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This is a well-studied
      <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">
       differential equation
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenvectors" title="Eigenvectors">
       eigenvalue
      </a>
      problem with a general solution of
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_2 = A \sin(kx) + B \cos(kx)\quad." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/b/d/3bdf8dd5528f527159a591b6e59c9ed6.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Hence,
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="E = \frac{k^2 \hbar^2}{2m} ." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/b/e/5be9297ca78e35374bad38622c043e2b.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Here,
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       B
      </i>
      can be any
      <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">
       complex numbers
      </a>
      , and
      <i>
       k
      </i>
      can be any real number.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_the_box">
       Outside the box
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finite_potential_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Outside the box">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For the region outside of the box, since the potential is constant,
      <i>
       V
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      ) =
      <img alt="V_o" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/3/613dc95ccddd5d8fdcc03b1bf5fc03fd.png"/>
      and Equation 1 becomes:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="-\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m} \frac{d^2 \psi_1}{d x^2} = ( E - V_o) \psi_1 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/a/0/ca076e15fb901ca4b5e1f56f77057291.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      There are two possible families of solutions, depending on whether
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      is less than
      <img alt="V_o" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/3/613dc95ccddd5d8fdcc03b1bf5fc03fd.png"/>
      (the particle is bound in the potential) or
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      is greater than
      <img alt="V_o" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/3/613dc95ccddd5d8fdcc03b1bf5fc03fd.png"/>
      (the particle is free).
     </p>
     <p>
      For a free particle,
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      &gt;
      <img alt="V_o" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/3/613dc95ccddd5d8fdcc03b1bf5fc03fd.png"/>
      , and letting
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="k'=\frac{\sqrt{2m(E - V_o)}}{\hbar}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/4/4/4440e6f63377517e7abe12bef0c7be7d.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      produces
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\frac{d^2 \psi_1}{d x^2} = -k'^2 \psi_1 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/8/2/f821f5452f80c168db5dedea3097a957.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      with the same solution form as the inside-well case:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_1 = C \sin(k' x) + D \cos(k' x)\quad" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/2/272b12b6b6a0986caf98971a44114078.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This analysis will focus on the bound state, where
      <img alt="V_o" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/1/3/613dc95ccddd5d8fdcc03b1bf5fc03fd.png"/>
      &gt;
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      . Letting
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\alpha = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V_o - E)}}{\hbar}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/e/a/3ea865db585ba131ae7f68de2bdce74e.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      produces
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\frac{d^2 \psi_1}{d x^2} = \alpha^2 \psi_1 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/9/4/1944fa86cc08f76aca7fafd34242532a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where the general solution is exponential:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_1 = Fe^{- \alpha x}+ Ge^{ \alpha x} \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/b/3/1b3345d95580fff9d7f5a68e40439c68.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Similarly, for the other region outside the box:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_3 = He^{- \alpha x}+ Ie^{ \alpha x} \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/c/1/fc17d2e6d33cfafc80aaa263c0532e81.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Now in order to find the specific solution for the problem at hand, we must specify the appropriate boundary conditions and find the values for
      <i>
       A
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       B
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       F
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       G
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       H
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       I
      </i>
      that satisfy those conditions.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Finding_wavefunctions_for_the_bound_state">
       Finding wavefunctions for the bound state
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finite_potential_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Finding wavefunctions for the bound state">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Solutions to the Schrdinger equation must be continuous, and continuously differentiable. These requirements are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_condition" title="Boundary condition">
       boundary conditions
      </a>
      on the differential equations previously derived.
     </p>
     <p>
      In this case, the finite potential well is symmetrical, so symmetry can be exploited to reduce the necessary calculations.
     </p>
     <p>
      Summarizing the previous section:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi = \begin{cases} \psi_1, &amp; \mbox{if }x&lt;-L/2\mbox{ (the region outside the box)} \\ \psi_2, &amp; \mbox{if }-L/2&lt;x&lt;L/2\mbox{ (the region inside the box)} \\ \psi_3 &amp; \mbox{if }x&gt;L/2\mbox{  (the region outside the box)} \end{cases}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/d/09d02b84b09a87988776cc762804304a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where we found
      <img alt="\psi_1, \psi_2 \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/c/c/8cc612fad88a6efcf75e2616800bc937.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="\psi_3 \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/e/6/9e628d575d50d6ed837a550abbd52dbd.png"/>
      to be:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_1 = Fe^{- \alpha x}+ Ge^{ \alpha x} \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/b/3/1b3345d95580fff9d7f5a68e40439c68.png"/>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_2 = A \sin(k x) + B \cos(k x)\quad" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/e/c/5ec66ef94dde775cdbe799f2dc828ff5.png"/>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\psi_3 = He^{- \alpha x}+ Ie^{ \alpha x} \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/c/1/fc17d2e6d33cfafc80aaa263c0532e81.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      We see that as
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      goes to
      <img alt="-\infty" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/a/beab416080922c84a90ba092f7734fe5.png"/>
      , the
      <img alt="F" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/0/0/800618943025315f869e4e1f09471012.png"/>
      term goes to infinity. Likewise, as
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      goes to
      <img alt="+\infty" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/8/c/28cfe0a2608499ff5984a938e0d16d64.png"/>
      , the
      <img alt="I" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/d/7/dd7536794b63bf90eccfd37f9b147d7f.png"/>
      term goes to infinity. As the wave function must have finite total integral, this means we must set
      <img alt="F=I=0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/1/1/51109163e5459f4514354e802d7df44b.png"/>
      , and we have:
     </p>
     <table cellpadding="4">
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="\psi_1 = Ge^{ \alpha x} \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/0/e/d0eb45f3ae8aa4b74bb305142f38a2cc.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
        and
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="\psi_3 = He^{- \alpha x} \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/2/b/f2bff019132e3396c795473a3d1b3eff.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Next, we know that the overall
      <img alt="\psi \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/1/4b1c491f938e0f03982ce86761904296.png"/>
      function must be continuous and differentiable. In other words the values of the functions and their derivatives must match up at the dividing points:
     </p>
     <table cellpadding="4">
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="\psi_1(-L/2) = \psi_2(-L/2) \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/0/de0bf06d44327946e7108b1e25695960.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="\psi_2(L/2) = \psi_3(L/2) \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/2/a/e2aa1cda527c5f548653d91548f298fa.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <img alt="\frac{d\psi_1}{dx}(-L/2) = \frac{d\psi_2}{dx}(-L/2) \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/a/67a45f1abf1285ea98f3f7c8fbcad5f4.png"/>
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
       <td>
        <img alt="\frac{d\psi_2}{dx}(L/2) = \frac{d\psi_3}{dx}(L/2) \,\!" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/0/4/f042cdf34ea4b0463e83b67412c851e6.png"/>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      These equations have two sorts of solutions, symmetric, for which
      <img alt="A=0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/5/7/357e564112c8be448ad371ce602b0658.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="G=H" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/d/c/6dc1dcc00c125a79c68e7e21ea1d6c2c.png"/>
      , and antisymmetric, for which
      <img alt="B=0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/8/ee829ea61bcc3fb3efccf2a3987528fc.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="G=-H" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/9/1/d9105b87e4c47958cd278e51958284c6.png"/>
      . For the symmetric case we get
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" He^{- \alpha L/2}  = B \cos(k L/2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/6/4/0648794bd57e263611c18331f65125d2.png"/>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" - \alpha He^{- \alpha L/2}  =  - k B \sin(k L/2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/1/921b28cf9b74581aea72f98483692a3a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      so taking the ratio gives
     </p>
     <div class="floatright">
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Finite-well-roots.gif" title="Roots of the equation for the quantized energy levels">
       <img alt="Roots of the equation for the quantized energy levels" data-file-height="373" data-file-width="360" height="373" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/Finite-well-roots.gif" width="360"/>
      </a>
     </div>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \alpha=k \tan(k L/2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/9/9/c99d7d3881e9b42d50cad68c13675cdd.png"/>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Similarly for the antisymmetric case we get
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \alpha=-k \cot(k L/2)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/6/f/d6f5659552a2b9c0ffce8315d92a8997.png"/>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Recall that both
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/c/e/8ce4b16b22b58894aa86c421e8759df3.png"/>
      depend on the energy. What we have found is that the continuity conditions
      <i>
       cannot
      </i>
      be satisfied for an arbitrary value of the energy. Only certain energy values, which are solutions to one or other of these two equations, are allowed. Hence we find, as always, the bound-state energies are quantized.
     </p>
     <p>
      The energy equations cannot be solved analytically. Graphical or numerical solutions are aided by rewriting them a little. If we introduce the dimensionless variables
      <img alt="u=\alpha L/2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/2/d/f2d0b55145691a4f3ae215e2184908eb.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="v=k L/2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/0/8/308ca4359795944261705e1f16ab56a8.png"/>
      , and note from the definitions of
      <img alt="\alpha" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/c/c/bccfc7022dfb945174d9bcebad2297bb.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/c/e/8ce4b16b22b58894aa86c421e8759df3.png"/>
      that
      <img alt="u^2=u_0^2-v^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/0/920510c6df6cc18d2343b0f9c18e3265.png"/>
      , where
      <img alt="u_0^2=m L^2 V_0/2 \hbar^2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/4/de4eeb3af9a67ba072b5f5e622a57a5c.png"/>
      , the master equations read
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\sqrt{u_0^2-v^2} = \begin{cases} v \tan v, &amp; \mbox{(symmetric case) }   \\ -v \cot v, &amp; \mbox{(antisymmetric case) } \end{cases}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/b/4/bb43cbe52b53dce8c459402c1620f532.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In the plot to the right, for
      <img alt="u_0^2=20" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/c/1/9c1d0c2ea558d2ec813e503d2dcc853d.png"/>
      , solutions exist where the blue semicircle intersects the purple or grey curves (
      <img alt="v \tan v" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/2/0925602e25350bb24c182c6b34395e15.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="-v \cot v" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/4/a/14aef0c6105f4524de67e6cf4de8237c.png"/>
      ). Each purple or grey curve represents a possible solution,
      <img alt="v_i" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/0/e/f0e66f55342ef85ba8be3415dd92d8e2.png"/>
      within the range
      <img alt="\frac{\pi}{2}(i-1) \leq v_i &lt; \frac{\pi}{2}i" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/6/a/c6a244e99d87d1d159de30b2fca807a6.png"/>
      . The total number of solutions,
      <img alt="N" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/9/8d9c307cb7f3c4a32822a51922d1ceaa.png"/>
      , (i.e., the number of purple/grey curves that are intersected by the blue circle) is therefore determined by dividing the radius of the blue circle,
      <img alt="u_0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/1/0/110a451bff14cf0501f2ebb7c418a581.png"/>
      , by the range of each solution
      <img alt="\pi/2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/f/8/1f8b57e932cf2d585ed71af904be8638.png"/>
      and then rounding up the result to the nearest integer.
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="N = \left\lceil\frac{2u_0}{\pi}\right\rceil" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/f/3/7f36a28eacbe89f0ac26aedda3892f62.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In this case there are exactly three solutions, since
      <img alt="N = \lceil 2\sqrt{20}/\pi\rceil = \lceil 2.85 \rceil = 3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/5/6/556a8807692534b06a7f83ec4a19b208.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="floatright">
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Finite-well-solutions.gif" title="Solutions of the finite square well">
       <img alt="Solutions of the finite square well" data-file-height="432" data-file-width="318" height="432" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Finite-well-solutions.gif" width="318"/>
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <img alt="v_1 =1.28, v_2=2.54" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/d/5/ed541f2b448a24f3683f777b0939480b.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="v_3=3.73" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/7/8/c78badbd9dd4936f942a9f91ae377f04.png"/>
      , with the corresponding energies
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="E_n={2\hbar^2 v_n^2\over m L^2}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/9/8/49811304bbe25295706d0aad50f1364a.png"/>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      If we want, we can go back and find the values of the constants
      <img alt="A, B, G, H" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/f/8/7f82214fada32292f3a2b755bf28615f.png"/>
      in the equations now (we also need to impose the normalisation condition). On the right we show the energy levels and wave functions in this case (where
      <img alt="x_0\equiv\hbar/\sqrt{2m V_0}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/a/e/1aebca7646ac58b5e1bef761273b3d15.png"/>
      ):
     </p>
     <p>
      We note that however small
      <img alt="u_0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/1/0/110a451bff14cf0501f2ebb7c418a581.png"/>
      is (however shallow or narrow the well), there is always at least one bound state.
     </p>
     <p>
      Two special cases are worth noting. As the height of the potential becomes large,
      <img alt="V_0\to\infty" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/1/8/1181dcb09ff701ee8d5d5889314260b3.png"/>
      , the radius of the semicircle gets larger and the roots get closer and closer to the values
      <img alt="v_n=n\pi/2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/c/bec406e2f3090bc944f0cdf26224187f.png"/>
      , and we recover the case of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infinite_square_well" title="Infinite square well">
       infinite square well
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The other case is that of a very narrow, deep well - specifically the case
      <img alt="V_0\to\infty" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/1/8/1181dcb09ff701ee8d5d5889314260b3.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="L\to 0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/f/9/2f97dba344b128d4865e489e865b9041.png"/>
      with
      <img alt="V_0 L" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/4/c24016c8803c8344f937046c350a82bf.png"/>
      fixed. As
      <img alt="u_0\propto V_0 L^2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/0/a/50aa25e48d125e2d25852c689a0079a9.png"/>
      it will tend to zero, and so there will only be one bound state. The approximate solution is then
      <img alt="v^2=u_0^2-u_0^4" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/f/92f34e5233934c39fd37ebc6d24b0157.png"/>
      , and the energy tends to
      <img alt="E=-m L^2 V_0^2/2\hbar^2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/f/5/6f51a701e37e5f4334099a2110445d6e.png"/>
      . But this is just the energy of the bound state of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_function_potential" title="Delta function potential">
       Delta function potential
      </a>
      of strength
      <img alt="V_0 L" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/4/c24016c8803c8344f937046c350a82bf.png"/>
      , as it should be.
     </p>
     <p>
      Note: The above derivation does not consider the possibility that the effective mass of the particle could be different inside the potential well and the region outside the well.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spherical_cavity">
       Spherical cavity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finite_potential_well&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spherical cavity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The results above can be used to show that, contrary to the one-dimensional case, there is not always a bound state in a spherical cavity.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ground state of a spherically symmetric potential will always have zero orbital angular momentum, and the reduced wave function
      <img alt="U(r)\equiv r \psi(r)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/8/2/4822d0d72c8cf5e240c6af873bdfb939.png"/>
      satisfies the equation
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" -\frac{\hbar^2}{2 m}{d^2 U\over d r^2}+V(r) U(r)=E U(r) " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/6/6/16636a9d6157734ae5dcf2483c89bf80.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This is identical to the one-dimensional equation, except for the boundary conditions. As before,
      <img alt="U(r)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/b/0/eb022140fb7c7161fe9a904457d44e65.png"/>
      and its first derivative must be continuous at the edge of the well
      <img alt="r=R" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/6/0/9606f1d16f8586d3788318c63c70ff4c.png"/>
      . However there is another condition, that
      <img alt="\psi(0)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/c/1/8c1a7342735d157d97b6c25873ff7c5a.png"/>
      must be finite, and that requires
      <img alt="U(0)=0" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/f/7/8f71b58651e4fd8298b89a9b86494352.png"/>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      By comparison with the solutions above, we can see that only the antisymmetric ones have nodes at the origin. Thus only the solutions to
      <img alt=" \alpha=-k \cot(k R)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/7/687e29a9b3f6bf7325e4be60aaf94eef.png"/>
      are allowed. These correspond to the intersection of the semicircle with the grey curves, and so if the cavity is too shallow or small, there will be no bound state.
     </p>
