     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Five Punishments
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        W Xng
       </span>
      </i>
      ) was the collective name for a series of physical penalties meted out by the legal system of pre-modern
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Dynasties" title="Chinese Dynasties">
       Dynastic China
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over time, the nature of the Five Punishments varied. Before the time of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">
       Western Han Dynasty
      </a>
      Emperor
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Wendi" title="Han Wendi">
       Han Wendi
      </a>
      (r. 180-157 BC) they involved tattooing, cutting off the nose, amputation of one or both feet, castration and death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sui_Dynasty" title="Sui Dynasty">
       Sui
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">
       Tang
      </a>
      Dynasties (581-907 CE) these were changed to penal servitude, banishment, death, or
      <a href="/wiki/Corporal_punishment" title="Corporal punishment">
       corporal punishment
      </a>
      in the form of whipping with bamboo strips or flogging with a stick. Although the Five Punishments were an important part of Dynastic China's penal system they were not the only methods of punishment used.
     </p>

     <p>
      The earliest users of the Five Punishments are believed by some to be the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sanmiao&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sanmiao (page does not exist)">
       Sanmiao
      </a>
      Clan (). Other sources claim they originated with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chi_You" title="Chi You">
       Chi You
      </a>
      , the legendary creator of metalwork and weapons and leader of the ancient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chi_You" title="Chi You">
       Nine Li
      </a>
      () ethnic group. During the subsequent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xia_Dynasty" title="Xia Dynasty">
       Xia Dynasty
      </a>
      (ca. 2070 BCEca. 1600 BCE),
      <a href="/wiki/Qi_of_Xia" title="Qi of Xia">
       Qi of Xia
      </a>
      , son of
      <a href="/wiki/Yu_the_Great" title="Yu the Great">
       Yu the Great
      </a>
      , the dynastys founder, adopted the Miaos punishments of amputation of one or both feet (
      <i>
       yu
      </i>
      ), cutting off of the nose (
      <i>
       y
      </i>
      ), chiseling (
      <i>
       zhu
      </i>
      ), tattooing the face or forehead (
      <i>
       qng
      </i>
      ) and other types of punishment. Tattooing, amputation of the nose or feet, removal of the reproductive organs and death became the main five forms of the punishment system during this period. From the Xia Dynasty onwards through the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shang_Dynasty" title="Shang Dynasty">
       Shang Dynasty
      </a>
      (1600-1046 BCE) and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhou_Dynasty" title="Zhou Dynasty">
       Zhou Dynasty
      </a>
      (1046-256 BCE). The Five Punishments for Slaves were abolished during the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wen_of_Han" title="Emperor Wen of Han">
       Emperor Wen of Han
      </a>
      following a petition from a female subject
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chunyu_Tiying&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chunyu Tiying (page does not exist)">
       Chunyu Tiying
      </a>
      (), and replaced by the Five Punishments for Serfs.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Five_Punishments_in_Ancient_China">
       The Five Punishments in Ancient China
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Punishments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Five Punishments in Ancient China">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Apart from the death penalty, the remaining four Punishments for Slaves were designed to bring about damage to their bodies that would mark them for life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      All ordinary citizens were subjected to these punishments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These punishments were for men. The number of crimes to which the punishment was applicable is listed next to each one.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       (), also known as
       <i>
        qng
       </i>
       (), where the offender would be tattooed on the face or forehead with indelible ink. (1,000 crimes)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Y
       </i>
       (), where the offender's nose was cut off. (1,000 crimes)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Yu
       </i>
       (), also known as
       <i>
        bn
       </i>
       (/) during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xia_Dynasty" title="Xia Dynasty">
        Xia Dynasty
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        zhnzh
       </i>
       () during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Dynasty" title="Qin Dynasty">
        Qin Dynasty
       </a>
       , involved amputation of the left or right foot or both. Other sources claim that this punishment involved removal of the kneecap, which is claimed to be the source of
       <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">
        Warring States period
       </a>
       military strategist
       <a href="/wiki/Sun_Bin" title="Sun Bin">
        Sun Bin
       </a>
       s name. (500 crimes)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Gng
       </i>
       (), also known as
       <i>
        ynxng
       </i>
       (),
       <i>
        fxng
       </i>
       ()
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <i>
        cnsh xng
       </i>
       (), where the male offenders reproductive organs were removed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         10
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Penis_removal" title="Penis removal">
        penis was removed
       </a>
       and testicles were cut off, and the offender was sentenced to work as a
       <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">
        eunuch
       </a>
       in the Imperial palace.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Gng
       </i>
       for men was applied to the same crime as
       <i>
        Gngxing
       </i>
       for women, namely adultery, "licentious" or "promiscuous" activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul_Rakita_Goldin_2002_76_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Paul_Rakita_Goldin_2002_76-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       (300 crimes)
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        D P
       </i>
       (), the death sentence. Methods of execution were quartering, or cutting the body into four pieces (
       <i>
        fn wi l
       </i>
       );
       <a href="/wiki/Death_by_boiling" title="Death by boiling">
        boiling alive
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        png
       </i>
       ); tearing off an offender's head and four limbs by attaching them to
       <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">
        chariots
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        chli
       </i>
       ); beheading (
       <i>
        xioshu
       </i>
       ); execution then abandonment of the offenders body in the local public market (
       <i>
        qsh
       </i>
       ); strangulation (
       <i>
        jio
       </i>
       ); and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slow_slicing" title="Slow slicing">
        slow slicing
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        lngch
       </i>
       ). Other methods of execution were also used. (200 crimes)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Five_Punishments_in_Imperial_China">
       The Five Punishments in Imperial China
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Punishments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Five Punishments in Imperial China">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Han" title="Western Han">
       Western Han
      </a>
      dynasty, tattooing and amputation were abolished as punishments and in subsequent dynasties, the five punishments underwent further modification. By the
      <a href="/wiki/Sui_dynasty" title="Sui dynasty">
       Sui dynasty
      </a>
      , the five punishments had attained the basic form they would have until the end of the imperial era. This is a brief survey of the five punishments during the
      <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">
       Qing dynasty
      </a>
      :

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Five_Punishments_for_female_offenders">
       The Five Punishments for female offenders
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Five_Punishments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Five Punishments for female offenders">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      These punishments were applied to women for the same crimes as committed by men.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Xngchng
       </i>
       (), where the offender was forced to grind grain.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Znxng
       </i>
       (), also known as (
       <i>
        znzh
       </i>
       ), squeezing of the fingers between sticks.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Zhngxng
       </i>
       (), beating with wooden staves.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Cs
       </i>
       (),
       <a href="/wiki/Forced_suicide" title="Forced suicide">
        forced suicide
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Gngxng
       </i>
       (), sequestration or confinement to a room. Punishment for licentiousness or
       <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">
        adultery
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        Gngxing
       </i>
       for women was applied for the same crimes as the
       <i>
        gng
       </i>
       punishment for men.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul_Rakita_Goldin_2002_76_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Paul_Rakita_Goldin_2002_76-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
