              <h2 class="heading ">
               What is Encaustic Paint?
              </h2>
             </div>
            </div>
            <figure class="content-img">
             <div class="sbs-img-frame">
              <div class="sbs-prev-next-top">

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p>
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/artglossarye/g/defencaustic.htm">
               Encaustic painting
              </a>
              uses a form of paint where wax is the main substance used as the
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/artglossaryb/g/defbinder.htm">
               binder
              </a>
              . The term "encaustic" sounds a bit intimidating and dangerous because the name tends to make us think of
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="http://saltaquarium.about.com/library/glossary/bldef_caustic.htm">
               caustic
              </a>
              and dangerous chemicals, but it's nothing like that.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              The term "encaustic" is derived from Greek, meaning simply "to burn in"
              <sup>
               1
              </sup>
              . The "recipe" for encaustic is simple:
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="4" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/artglossaryp/g/defpigment.htm">
               pigment
              </a>
              plus wax (typically a mixture of beeswax and damar resin).
             </p>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <div class="radlinks ads-half" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">
             </div>
             <p class="cb-split">
              You melt the wax, mix in the pigment, and you have encaustic paint.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Working with encaustic paint is quite different to using oil or acrylic paint because you have to heat the paint in order for it to be spreadable. You also need to fuse the paint to the support and existing layers of paint, again with heat.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              But first you need some paint. This step-by-step will show you how to make your own encaustic paint.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <b>
               You will need:
              </b>
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Paint pigment (in powder form) (
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="5" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.tkqlhce.com/click-2258946-11424551?url=http://www.dickblick.com/products/gamblin-artists-colors-dry-pigments/" rel="nofollow">
                Buy Direct
               </a>
               )
              </li>
              <li>
               Damar crystals (a type of resin) (
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="6" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.tkqlhce.com/click-2258946-11424551?url=http://www.dickblick.com/products/sennelier-damar-gum/" rel="nofollow">
                Buy Direct
               </a>
               )
              </li>
              <li>
               Beeswax (
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="7" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.tkqlhce.com/click-2258946-11424551?url=http://www.dickblick.com/products/gamblin-wax-pastilles/" rel="nofollow">
                Buy Direct: wax granules
               </a>
               or
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="8" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.tkqlhce.com/click-2258946-11424551?url=http://www.dickblick.com/products/lineco-bees-wax/" rel="nofollow">
                wax cake
               </a>
               )
              </li>
              <li>
               A pan to "cook" the ingredients
              </li>
              <li>
               A muffin tray (a silicone one makes it easier to get out the "paint muffins"
              </li>
              <li>
               A hot plate or heating element (to put the pan on)
              </li>
              <li>
               A metal or plastic spoon to stir the mix
              </li>
              <li>
               Something to measure off portions of the liquid wax (a washed out, small tincan for instance)
              </li>
              <li>
               Oven gloves to protect your hands
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Always work with encaustic paints in a well ventilated area, and don't overheat them. You just want the wax liquid, not on the boil!
             </p>
             <p class="cb-split">
              (See
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="9" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="externalLink" href="http://www.rfpaints.com/media/k2/attachments/RF_TechSheet_-_Venting_Your_Studio.pdf">
               Info sheet
              </a>
              on studio ventilation for encaustics from RF Paints.)
             </p>
             <p>
              So, let's learn a little more about the ingredients. First up,
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="10" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/encausticpainting/ss/encaustic-paints_2.htm">
               what is damar resin?
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              <sub>

                <i>
                 The Artist's Handbook
                </i>
               </a>
               , p427.
              </sub>

             <div class="content-img-caption">

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p>
              Damar resin is a natural resin from a tree. It seeps out of the tree from a cut, similar to how maple syrup is harvested from maple trees. It dries in large lumps or crystals. You melt these and mix them with beeswax for encaustic paints.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Damar resin is mixed with beeswax to harden it and raise its melting temperature. It also keeps the paint translucent and prevents blooming (whitening). It can also be polished to a glossy shine.
             </p>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <div class="radlinks ads-half" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">
             </div>
             <p class="cb-split">
              <br/>
              <br/>
              How much damar resin you mix with the beeswax is a matter of personal preference. Typically it's between four and eight measures of beeswax to one measure of damar resin, depending on how hard you want the final result to be.
             </p>

             <p>
              The process is simple: put your pot onto the heat, put in your beeswax, wait for it to melt, then put in the damar resin, and stir as this melts. Know the characteristics of your pigments, whether they're transparent and opaque, as this will also influence how much dry pigment you use. Don't use too much pigment because if there's not sufficient wax to "stick" it down, the paint will flake off.
             </p>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="3" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <div class="radlinks ads-half" data-character-limit="null" data-display-inline="true" data-display-label="false" data-num-links="5" id="radlinks3">
             </div>
             <p class="cb-split">
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Start with one or two spoonfuls of pigment. Remember you can always melt it down later again and add more pigment if you decide to.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Always be aware of
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/paintingforbeginners/a/art_safety.htm">
               art materials safety
              </a>
              when working with pigments, not least knowing whether a particular pigment is poisonous or not. Avoid breathing in the pigment and don't blow it off a surface if you spill some, but wipe it off with a damp cloth.
             </p>
             <p>
              Next,
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/encausticpainting/ss/encaustic-paints_6.htm">
               mixing pigment and wax...
              </a>
             </p>

             <p>
              Work carefully, as the wax is hot, obviously. Pour some of the beeswax/damar resin mix into the sections of the muffin tray. Use a small container to do this rather than trying to pour some from your pot. Bend a small tincan so it's got a bit of a spout, for instance.
