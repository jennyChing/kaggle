     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Dragunov sniper rifle
      </b>
      (formal
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru">
            1963 
      </span>
      <i>
       Snayperskaya Vintovka sistem'y Dragunova obraz'tsa 1963 goda
      </i>
      (SVD-63), officially "Sniper Rifle, System of Dragunov, Model of the Year 1963") is a
      <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_rifle" title="Semi-automatic rifle">
       semi-automatic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Snipers_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Snipers of the Soviet Union">
       sniper
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Designated_marksman_rifle" title="Designated marksman rifle">
       designated marksman rifle
      </a>
      chambered in
      <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9754mmR" title="7.6254mmR">
       7.6254mmR
      </a>
      and developed in the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet Union
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Dragunov was designed as a
      <a href="/wiki/Squad" title="Squad">
       squad
      </a>
      support weapon since, according to
      <a href="/wiki/Snipers_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Snipers of the Soviet Union">
       Soviet and Soviet-derived military doctrines
      </a>
      , the long-range engagement ability was lost to ordinary troops when
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submachine_guns" title="Submachine guns">
       submachine guns
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assault_rifles" title="Assault rifles">
       assault rifles
      </a>
      (which are optimized for close-range and medium-range, rapid-fire combat) were adopted. For that reason, it was originally named      1963  "Self-Loading Rifle, System of Dragunov, Model of the Year 1963."
     </p>
     <p>
      It was selected as the winner of a contest that included three competing designs: by
      <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Gavrilovich_Simonov" title="Sergei Gavrilovich Simonov">
       Sergei Simonov
      </a>
      , Aleksandr Konstantinov and
      <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Dragunov" title="Yevgeny Dragunov">
       Yevgeny Dragunov
      </a>
      . Extensive field testing of the rifles conducted in a wide range of environmental conditions resulted in Dragunovs proposal being accepted into service in 1963. An initial pre-production batch consisting of 200 rifles was assembled for evaluation purposes, and from 1964 serial production was carried out by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Izhmash" title="Izhmash">
       Izhmash
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Since then, the Dragunov has become the standard squad support weapon of several countries, including those of the former
      <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">
       Warsaw Pact
      </a>
      . Licensed production of the rifle was established in China (
      <i>
       Type 79
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Type 85
      </i>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
       Iran
      </a>
      (as a direct copy of the Chinese Type 79).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_details">
       Design details
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design details">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_mechanism">
       Operating mechanism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operating mechanism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Dragunov is a
      <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_firearm" title="Semi-automatic firearm">
       semi-automatic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gas-operated_reloading" title="Gas-operated reloading">
       gas-operated
      </a>
      rifle with a short-stroke gas-piston system. The barrel breech is locked through a
      <a href="/wiki/Rotating_bolt" title="Rotating bolt">
       rotating bolt
      </a>
      (left rotation) and uses three locking lugs to engage corresponding locking recesses in the barrel extension. The rifle has a manual, two-position gas regulator.
     </p>
     <p>
      After discharging the last cartridge from the magazine, the bolt carrier and bolt are held back on a bolt catch that is released by pulling the cocking handle to the rear. The rifle has a hammer-type striking mechanism and a manual lever safety selector. The firing pin is a "free-floating" type and, as a result, some soft-primered ammunition had the reputation of causing a "
      <a href="/wiki/Slamfire" title="Slamfire">
       slam fire
      </a>
      " event. Thus, military grade ammunition with primers confirmed to be properly seated is recommended for the Dragunov and its variants. This appears to have solved the "slam fire" issue. The rifle's
      <a href="/wiki/Receiver_(firearms)" title="Receiver (firearms)">
       receiver
      </a>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">
       machined
      </a>
      to provide additional accuracy and
      <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">
       torsional
      </a>
      strength. The Dragunov's receiver bears a number of similarities to the
      <a href="/wiki/AK-47" title="AK-47">
       AK
      </a>
      action, such as the large dust cover, iron sights and lever safety selector, but these similarities are primarily cosmetic in nature.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Barrel">
       Barrel
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Barrel">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">
       barrel
      </a>
      profile is relatively thin to save weight and is ended with a slotted
      <a href="/wiki/Flash_suppressor" title="Flash suppressor">
       flash suppressor
      </a>
      . The barrels bore is
      <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">
       chrome
      </a>
      -lined for increased corrosion resistance, and features 4 right-hand grooves. It is not
      <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">
       rifled
      </a>
      over its full length but partly over a length of 547mm (21.5in). In the 1960s, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twist_rate" title="Twist rate">
       twist rate
      </a>
      was 320mm (1:12.6 in). During the 1970s, the twist rate was tightened to 240mm (1:9.4 in), which reduced the accuracy of fire with sniper cartridges by 19%. This adaptation was done in order to facilitate the use of tracer and armor-piercing incendiary ammunition, since these bullet types required a shorter twist rate for adequate stabilization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ammunition_feeding">
       Ammunition feeding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ammunition feeding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The weapon is fed from a detachable curved
      <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(firearms)#Box" title="Magazine (firearms)">
       box magazine
      </a>
      with a 10-round capacity and the cartridges are double-stacked in a staggered zigzag pattern.
     </p>

      The Dragunov is issued with a quick-detachable

      <a href="/wiki/PSO-1" title="PSO-1">

      <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
       infrared
      </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stock">
       Stock
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stock">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SVD_Dragunov.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1209" data-file-width="3072" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/SVD_Dragunov.jpg/220px-SVD_Dragunov.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/SVD_Dragunov.jpg/330px-SVD_Dragunov.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/SVD_Dragunov.jpg/440px-SVD_Dragunov.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SVD_Dragunov.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        SVD rifle featuring a wooden handguard/gas tube cover and skeletonized stock used before the change to synthetic black furniture.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Dragunov has a vented, two-piece wooden handguard/gas tube cover and a skeletonized wooden thumbhole stock equipped with a detachable cheek rest; the latter is removed when using iron sights. Newer production models feature synthetic furniture made of a black
      <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">
       polymer
      </a>
       the handguard and gas tube cover are more or less identical in appearance, while the thumbhole stock is of a different shape.
     </p>
     <p>
      The barrel is semi
      <a href="/wiki/Free-floating_barrel" title="Free-floating barrel">
       free-floated
      </a>
      , since it is connected by a spring-loaded mechanism to the handguard/gas tube cover so the handguard can move with the barrel during firing.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ammunition">
       Ammunition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ammunition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For precision shooting, specifically designed
      <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">
       sniper
      </a>
      cartridges are used, developed by V. M. Sabelnikov, P. P. Sazonov and V. M. Dvorianinov. The proprietary 7N1 load has a
      <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
       steel
      </a>
      jacketed projectile with an air pocket, a steel core and a lead knocker in the base for maximum terminal effect. The 7N1 was replaced in 1999 by the 7N14 round. The 7N14 is a new load developed for the SVD. It consists of a 151 grain projectile that travels at the same 830m/s, but it has a sharp hardened steel core projectile. The rifle can also fire standard
      <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9754mmR" title="7.6254mmR">
       7.6254mmR
      </a>
      ammunition with either conventional, tracer or armor piercing incendiary rounds.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Russian military has established accuracy standards that the SVD and its corresponding sniper grade ammunition have to meet. Manufacturers must perform firing tests to check if the rifles and sniper grade ammunition fulfill these standards. To comply to the standards, the SVD rifle with 7N1 sniper cartridges may not produce more than 1.24
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
       MOA
      </a>
      extreme vertical spread with 240mm twist rate barrels and no more than 1.04 MOA extreme vertical spread with 320mm twist rate barrels. When using standard grade 57-N-323S cartridges, the accuracy of the SVD is reduced to 2.21 MOA extreme vertical spread. The extreme vertical spreads for the SVD are established by shooting 5-shot groups at 300 m range. The accuracy requirements demanded of the SVD with sniper grade ammunition are similar to the American
      <a href="/wiki/M24_Sniper_Weapon_System" title="M24 Sniper Weapon System">
       M24 Sniper Weapon System
      </a>
      with M118SB cartridges (1.18 MOA extreme vertical spread) and the
      <a href="/wiki/M110_Semi-Automatic_Sniper_System" title="M110 Semi-Automatic Sniper System">
       M110 Semi-Automatic Sniper System
      </a>
      with M118LR ammunition (1.27 MOA extreme vertical spread).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Accessories">
       Accessories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Accessories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A number of accessories are provided with the rifle, including a blade-type
      <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">
       bayonet
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/AK-47" title="AK-47">
       AKM
      </a>
      clipped point or the
      <a href="/wiki/AK-74" title="AK-74">
       AK-74
      </a>
      spear point bayonet), four spare magazines, a leather or nylon sling, magazine pouch, cleaning kit and an accessory/maintenance kit for the telescopic sight. Also included is a cold weather battery case with a "shirt clip", with a permanently attached cord [approximately 24" long] ending with another battery case cap that has an extension to press against the internal contact in lieu of the battery to complete the circuit. Placing the external battery case into the shooters' clothing close to the body keeps it from freezing; using the clip ensures it remains in place. The clamp-style bipod attaches to machined-out reliefs near the front of the receiver, it literally grabs the two cut out areas and securely mounts with a large round sized head on the clamp bolt able to tightly attach the bipod. The legs are individually adjustable [as opposed to fixed length found on many rifles and LMG's] and can be folded and stowed in a forward position negating the need to remove the bipod before placing the rifle into the canvas carrying case. Interestingly enough, the two legs are held close together with a "J" shaped clamp attached to one leg and swung over the other leg. Original Soviet/Russian SVD bipods fetch a very high price when they rarely appear on the market.
     </p>

        Pair of Dragunovs imported to the U.S. as Tigers. Top rifle has cheek pad, two 10-round magazines, and flash suppressor. Bottom rifle was marketed as a hunting "carbine". It has no cheek pad, two 5-round magazines, and no flash suppressor.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the early 1990s, a compact variant of the SVD designed for airborne infantry was introduced, known as the
      <b>
       SVDS
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :    , short for
      <i>
       Snayperskaya Vintovka Dragunova Skladnaya
      </i>
      , "Dragunov Sniper Rifle with folding stock"), which features a tubular metal stock that folds to the right side of the receiver (equipped with a synthetic shoulder pad and a fixed cheek riser) and a synthetic pistol grip. The barrel was also given a heavier profile, the receiver housing was strengthened, the gas cylinder block was improved and a ported, conical flash hider was adopted.
     </p>
     <p>
      The SVDS also comes in a night-capable variant designated
      <b>
       SVDSN
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1994, the Russian TsKIB SOO company (currently, a division of the
      <a href="/wiki/KBP_Instrument_Design_Bureau" title="KBP Instrument Design Bureau">
       KBP Instrument Design Bureau
      </a>
      ) developed the
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Dragunov_SVU" title="Dragunov SVU">
        SVU
       </a>
      </b>
      sniper rifle (short for
      <i>
       Snayperskaya Vintovka Ukorochennaya
      </i>
      , "Sniper Rifle, Shortened") offered to special units of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Ministry_of_Internal_Affairs" title="Russian Ministry of Internal Affairs">
       Russian Ministry of Internal Affairs
      </a>
      (MVD).
     </p>
     <p>
      The SVU, compared to the SVD, has a considerably shorter overall length because of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bullpup" title="Bullpup">
       bullpup
      </a>
      layout and shortened barrel that also received a triple-baffle
      <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_brake" title="Muzzle brake">
       muzzle brake
      </a>
      with an approx. 40% recoil reduction effectiveness. The rifle was equipped with folding iron sights (rear aperture sight in a rotating drum) and the PSO-1 telescopic sight.
     </p>
     <p>
      A variant of the SVU, designed with a selective-fire capability and using 20-round magazines, is called the
      <b>
       SVU-A
      </b>
      (A 
      <i>
       Avtomaticheskaya
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/SVDK" title="SVDK">
        SVDK
       </a>
      </b>
      is a Russian SVD variant chambered for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9.3x64mm_Brenneke" title="9.3x64mm Brenneke">
       9.364mm 7N33
      </a>
      cartridge. The SVDK is mechanically adapted to use dimensionally larger 9.3x64mm Brenneke cartridges.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1998,
      <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
       Poland
      </a>
      adopted a modernized variant of the SVD designated the
      <b>
       SWD-M
      </b>
      , which uses a heavy barrel,
      <a href="/wiki/Bipod" title="Bipod">
       bipod
      </a>
      (mounted to the forearm) and LD-6 (6x42) telescopic sight.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another variant of the SVD is the Iraqi
      <b>
       Al-Kadesih
      </b>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9754mmR" title="7.6254mmR">
       7.6254mmR
      </a>
      Al-Kadesih rifle is not to be confused with the Iraqi
      <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9739mm" title="7.6239mm">
       7.6239mm
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Tabuk_Sniper_Rifle" title="Tabuk Sniper Rifle">
       Tabuk sniper rifle
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Al-Kadesih while stylistically similar to the SVD is kind of a hybrid between the SVD and Romanian
      <a href="/wiki/PSL_(rifle)" title="PSL (rifle)">
       PSL
      </a>
      rifles and has some key differences with the SVD that prevent parts interchangeability with the SVD. The Al-Kadesih has a unique pressed-metal receiver which is longer than that of the SVD, although the overall length of the rifle is similar to that of the SVD. It is fitted for the Soviet-era PSO-1 optical sight. Further, the barrel is pinned, rather than screwed, to the receiver, although it is of the same length as that of the SVD. The fore-end has four longitudinal slots on each side instead of six short slots. Another readily visible distinguishing feature of the Al-Kadesih is that the magazine has an ornamental relief pattern showing a stylised palm tree.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_variants">
       Commercial variants
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial variants">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Dragunov also served as the basis for several hunting rifles. In 1962, the state armory in
      <a href="/wiki/Izhevsk" title="Izhevsk">
       Izhevsk
      </a>
      developed the
      <i>
       Medved
      </i>
      (Bear) rifle, initially chambered first in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9x53mm" title="9x53mm">
       9x53mm
      </a>
      cartridge and later in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7.62x51mm_NATO" title="7.62x51mm NATO">
       7.62x51mm NATO
      </a>
      round for export. In the early 1970s, Izhevsk introduced the
      <i>
       Tigr
      </i>
      (Tiger) hunting rifle with a fixed thumbhole stock without a cheekpiece. They were originally produced individually, but, since 1992, they have been made serially in batches. Today, they are available with shortened (520mm) and full length (620mm) barrel, different stocks (including SVDS style folding stock) and chambered in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7.62x54mmR" title="7.62x54mmR">
       7.62x54mmR
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/.308_Winchester" title=".308 Winchester">
       .308 Winchester
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/.30-06_Springfield" title=".30-06 Springfield">
       .30-06 Springfield
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9.3x64mm_Brenneke" title="9.3x64mm Brenneke">
       9.3x64mm Brenneke
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Deployment">
       Deployment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dragunov_sniper_rifle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deployment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Dragunov is an original rifle design for several reasons. First, it was not meant for highly trained and specialized sniper teams, but rather for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Designated_Marksman" title="Designated Marksman">
       designated marksmen
      </a>
      . After the introduction of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragunov_Sniper_Rifle" title="Dragunov Sniper Rifle">
       SVD
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Army" title="Soviet Army">
       Soviet Army
      </a>
      deployed designated marksmen at the basic
      <a href="/wiki/Mechanized_infantry" title="Mechanized infantry">
       motorized infantry
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Platoon#USSR_organization" title="Platoon">
       rifle platoon
      </a>
      level.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Those designated marksmen were often chosen from personnel who did well in terms of rifle marksmanship while members of
      <a href="/wiki/DOSAAF" title="DOSAAF">
       DOSAAF
      </a>
      . Such marksmen were estimated to have a 50% probability of hitting a standing, man-sized target at 800m (875yd), and an 80% probability of hitting a standing, man-sized target at 500m (547yd). For distances not exceeding 200m (219yd) the probability was estimated to be well above 90%. To attain this level of accuracy the sniper could not engage more than two such targets per minute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later in every
      <a href="/wiki/Platoon" title="Platoon">
       platoon
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">
       Warsaw Pact
      </a>
      troops, there was at least one Dragunov rifle marksman. In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" title="German Democratic Republic">
       German Democratic Republic
      </a>
      arsenals alone, there were almost 2,000 Dragunov rifles,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while in many Western armies there was not even a single sniper rifle except in special forces units (as an example, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Italian_Army" title="Italian Army">
       Italian Army
      </a>
      until the 1990s), but in Warsaw Pact troop formations, the Dragunov marksmen were widespread among the regular units. To fulfill this role, the rifle is relatively light for a sniper rifle, but well balanced, making it easier to use in a dynamic battle. It is also a semi-automatic rifle, a rare feature for accuracy-oriented rifles in the 1960s (except for customized ordnance, like
      <a href="/wiki/M1_Garand" title="M1 Garand">
       M1 Garands
      </a>
      ), to allow rapid fire and quicker engagement of multiple targets. As with all precision-oriented rifles, the user has to take care not to overheat the barrel and limit the use of rapid fire. In order to fire effective API ammunition, its accuracy potential was slightly downgraded by shortening the twist rate, another uncommon priority for a pure sniper rifle. It has a relatively light barrel profile; its precision is good, but not exceptional. Like an assault rifle, the rifle has mounts on the barrel to fix a bayonet. The standard AKM bayonet can even be used to cut electrified barbed wire. Lastly, the rifle was meant to be a relatively cheap mass-produced firearm.
     </p>
     <p>
      These features and unusual characteristics were driven by the tactical use doctrine of Dragunov armed marksman, which was: from (just behind) the first line targeting high-value targets of opportunity and providing special long-distance disrupting and
      <a href="/wiki/Suppressive_fire" title="Suppressive fire">
       suppressive fire
      </a>
      on the battlefield, even with sudden close encounters with enemy troops in mind. A relatively small number of marksmen could assist conventional troops by combating or harassing valuable targets and assets such as: key enemy personnel like
      <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">
       officers
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">
       non-commissioned officers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Radioman" title="Radioman">
       radio operators
      </a>
      , exposed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tank_commander" title="Tank commander">
       tank commanders
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Designated_marksmen" title="Designated marksmen">
       designated marksmen
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snipers" title="Snipers">
       snipers
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinegun" title="Machinegun">
       machinegun
      </a>
      teams,
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-tank_warfare" title="Anti-tank warfare">
       anti-tank warfare
      </a>
      teams, etc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
