     <p>
      <b>
       Fac 51 The Haienda
      </b>
      (better known simply as
      <b>
       The Haienda
      </b>
      ) was a
      <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">
       nightclub
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Music_venue" title="Music venue">
       music venue
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">
       Manchester
      </a>
      , England. It became most famous during the "
      <a href="/wiki/Madchester" title="Madchester">
       Madchester
      </a>
      " years of the late 1980s and early 1990s; during the 1990s it was labelled the most famous club in the world by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">
        Newsweek
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Haienda opened in 1982, and despite considerable and persistent financial troubles survived until 1997  during much of this time the club was mainly supported by record sales from
      <a href="/wiki/New_Order" title="New Order">
       New Order
      </a>
      . The Haienda is associated with the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Acid_house" title="Acid house">
       acid house
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">
       rave
      </a>
      music.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">
       Creation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ha%C3%A7ienda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Creation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda-300.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="302" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Hacienda-300.jpg/200px-Hacienda-300.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Hacienda-300.jpg/300px-Hacienda-300.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d7/Hacienda-300.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda-300.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Haienda's Exterior on the corner of Whitworth St. West and Albion St.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The former warehouse occupied by the club was at 11-13,
      <a href="/wiki/Whitworth_Street" title="Whitworth Street">
       Whitworth Street West
      </a>
      on the south side of the
      <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_Canal" title="Rochdale Canal">
       Rochdale Canal
      </a>
      : the frontage was curved and built of red brick. Before it was turned into a club, the Haienda was a yacht builder's shop and warehouse before becoming a
      <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">
       Bollywood
      </a>
      cinema in the 1970s, showing films to the local Asian community.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Originally conceived by
      <a href="/wiki/Rob_Gretton" title="Rob Gretton">
       Rob Gretton
      </a>
      , it was largely financed by the record label
      <a href="/wiki/Factory_Records" title="Factory Records">
       Factory Records
      </a>
      and the band
      <a href="/wiki/New_Order" title="New Order">
       New Order
      </a>
      along with label boss
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Wilson" title="Tony Wilson">
       Tony Wilson
      </a>
      . It was located on the corner of Whitworth Street West and Albion Street, close to
      <a href="/wiki/Castlefield" title="Castlefield">
       Castlefield
      </a>
      , in the centre of the city. FAC 51 was its official designation in the Factory catalogue. New Order and Tony Wilson were directors of the club.
     </p>
     <p>
      Designed by
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Kelly_(designer)" title="Ben Kelly (designer)">
       Ben Kelly
      </a>
      , upon recommendation by Factory graphic designer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Saville_(artist)" title="Peter Saville (artist)">
       Peter Saville
      </a>
      , upstairs consisted of a stage, dance area, bar, cloakroom, cafeteria area and balcony with a DJ booth. Downstairs was a cocktail bar called 'The Gay Traitor,' which referred to
      <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Blunt" title="Anthony Blunt">
       Anthony Blunt
      </a>
      , a British art historian who spied for the Soviet Union. The two other bars, 'The
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Philby" title="Kim Philby">
       Kim Philby
      </a>
      ' and '
      <a href="/wiki/Guy_Burgess" title="Guy Burgess">
       Hicks
      </a>
      ,' were named after Blunt's fellow spies. From 1995 onwards, the lower cellar areas of the venue were converted to create the 5th Man, a smaller music venue.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
       Name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ha%C3%A7ienda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The name comes from a slogan of the radical group
      <a href="/wiki/Situationist_International" title="Situationist International">
       Situationist International
      </a>
      : "The Hacienda Must Be Built", from
      <i>
       Formulary for a New Urbanism
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Chtcheglov" title="Ivan Chtcheglov">
       Ivan Chtcheglov
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCManc_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCManc-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Hacienda" title="Hacienda">
       hacienda
      </a>
      is a large homestead in a ranch or estate usually in places where Colonial Spanish culture has had architectural influence. Even though the
      <a href="/wiki/Cedilla" title="Cedilla">
       cedilla
      </a>
      is not used in
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
       Spanish
      </a>
      , the spelling "Haienda" was decided on for the club because the cedilla makes the "i" resemble "51," the club's catalogue number.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ha%C3%A7ienda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda-301.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="330" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/Hacienda-301.jpg/200px-Hacienda-301.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/Hacienda-301.jpg/300px-Hacienda-301.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c5/Hacienda-301.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda-301.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Haienda's exterior on Whitworth St. West
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Haienda was opened on 21 May 1982, when the comedian
      <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Manning" title="Bernard Manning">
       Bernard Manning
      </a>
      remarked to the audience, "I've played some shit-holes during my time, but this is really something."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ed_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ed-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His jokes did not go down well with the crowd and he returned his fee.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A wide portfolio of important 20th century musical acts appeared at the club. One of the earliest was the German
      <a href="/wiki/Electronic_body_music" title="Electronic body music">
       EBM
      </a>
      band
      <a href="/wiki/Liaisons_Dangereuses_(band)" title="Liaisons Dangereuses (band)">
       Liaisons Dangereuses
      </a>
      , which played there on 7 July 1982.
      <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">
       The Smiths
      </a>
      performed there three times in 1983. It served as a venue for
      <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">
       Madonna
      </a>
      on her first performance in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      , on 27 January 1984. She was invited to appear as part of a one-off, live television broadcast by
      <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
       Channel 4
      </a>
      music programme
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tube_(TV_series)" title="The Tube (TV series)">
        The Tube
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEN_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MEN-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Madonna performed "
      <a href="/wiki/Holiday_(Madonna_song)" title="Holiday (Madonna song)">
       Holiday
      </a>
      " whilst at The Haienda and the performance was described by
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Cook" title="Norman Cook">
       Norman Cook
      </a>
      (perhaps better known as Fatboy Slim) as one that "mesmerised the crowd".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At one time, the venue also included a hairdressing salon. As well as club nights there were regular concerts, including one in which
      <a href="/wiki/Einst%C3%BCrzende_Neubauten" title="Einstrzende Neubauten">
       Einstrzende Neubauten
      </a>
      drilled into the walls that surrounded the stage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCManc_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBCManc-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1986, it became one of the first British clubs to start playing
      <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">
       house music
      </a>
      , with DJs
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Pickering" title="Mike Pickering">
       Mike Pickering
      </a>
      (of
      <a href="/wiki/Quando_Quango" title="Quando Quango">
       Quando Quango
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/M_People" title="M People">
       M People
      </a>
      ) and Little Martin (later with
      <a class="external text" href="http://thisisgraemepark.com" rel="nofollow">
       Graeme Park
      </a>
      ) hosting the visionary "Nude" night on Fridays. This night quickly became legendary,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and helped to turn around the reputation and fortunes of the Haienda, which went from making a consistent loss to being full every night of the week by early 1987.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Acid_house_and_rave">
       Acid house and rave
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ha%C3%A7ienda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acid house and rave">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda06.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="404" data-file-width="613" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/Hacienda06.jpg/250px-Hacienda06.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/Hacienda06.jpg/375px-Hacienda06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/Hacienda06.jpg/500px-Hacienda06.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda06.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Haienda's interior before opening
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The growth of the '
      <a href="/wiki/Madchester" title="Madchester">
       Madchester
      </a>
      ' scene had little to do with the healthy
      <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">
       house music
      </a>
      scene in Manchester at the time but it was boosted by the success of the Haienda's pioneering
      <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">
       Ibiza
      </a>
      night, "Hot", an
      <a href="/wiki/Acid_house" title="Acid house">
       acid house
      </a>
      night hosted by Pickering and
      <a href="/wiki/Jon_DaSilva" title="Jon DaSilva">
       Jon DaSilva
      </a>
      in July 1988.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, drug use became a problem. On 14 July 1989, the UK's first
      <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">
       ecstasy
      </a>
      -related death occurred at the club; 16-year-old Clare Leighton collapsed and died after her boyfriend gave her an ecstasy tablet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The police clampdown that followed was opposed by
      <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_Council" title="Manchester City Council">
       Manchester City Council
      </a>
      , which argued that the club contributed to an "active use of the city centre core" in line with the government's policy of regenerating urban areas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ed_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ed-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The resulting problems caused the club to close for a short period in early 1991, before reopening with increased security later the same year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Haienda DJs made regular and guest appearances on radio and TV shows like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granada_TV" title="Granada TV">
       Granada TV
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Juice
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sunset_102" title="Sunset 102">
       Sunset 102
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">
       BBC Radio 1
      </a>
      . Between 1994 and 1997
      <i>
       Hacienda FM
      </i>
      was a weekly show on Manchester dance station
      <a href="/wiki/Kiss_102" title="Kiss 102">
       Kiss 102
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Security was frequently a problem, particularly in the club's latter years. There were several shootings inside and outside the club, and relations with the police and licensing authorities became troubled. When local magistrates and police visited the club in 1997, they witnessed a near-fatal assault on a man in the streets outside when 18-year-old Andrew Delahunty was hit over the head from behind with what looked like a metal bar before being pushed into the path of an on-coming car.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mixmag_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mixmag-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although security failures at the club were one of the contributing factors to the club eventually closing, the most likely cause was finances. The club simply did not make enough money from the sale of alcohol, and this was mainly because many patrons instead turned to drug use. As a result, the club rarely broke even as alcohol sales are the main source of income of nightclubs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ultimately, the club's long-term future was crippled and, with spiralling debts, the Haienda eventually closed definitively in the summer of 1997. Peter Hook stated in 2009 that the Haienda lost up to 18 million during its latter years of existence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ha%C3%A7ienda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haciendaapartments.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Haciendaapartments.jpg/200px-Haciendaapartments.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Haciendaapartments.jpg/300px-Haciendaapartments.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Haciendaapartments.jpg/400px-Haciendaapartments.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Haciendaapartments.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The rebuilt Haienda flats logo in 2007
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda_flats.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Hacienda_flats.jpg/200px-Hacienda_flats.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Hacienda_flats.jpg/300px-Hacienda_flats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Hacienda_flats.jpg/400px-Hacienda_flats.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hacienda_flats.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Haienda flats in 2007
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Haienda lost its entertainments licence in June 1997. The last night of the club was 28 June 1997, a club night called 'Freak' featuring DJs Elliot Eastwick and
      <a href="/wiki/Dave_Haslam" title="Dave Haslam">
       Dave Haslam
      </a>
      (the final live performance was by
      <a href="/wiki/Spiritualized" title="Spiritualized">
       Spiritualized
      </a>
      on 15 June 1997). The club remained open for a short period as an art gallery before finally going bankrupt and closing for good. After the Haienda officially closed, it was used as a venue for two free parties organised by the Manchester
      <a href="/wiki/Free_party" title="Free party">
       free party
      </a>
      scene. One of the parties ended in a police siege of the building while the party continued inside. These parties resulted in considerable damage and the application of graffiti to the Ben Kelly designed interior.
     </p>
     <p>
      Following a number of years standing empty, the Whitworth Street West site was purchased from the receivers by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crosby_Lend_Lease" title="Crosby Lend Lease">
       Crosby Homes
      </a>
      . They chose to demolish the nightclub, and reuse the site for the construction of domestic flats. The iconic name was kept for the new development, with the Haienda name licensed from
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hook" title="Peter Hook">
       Peter Hook
      </a>
      , who owns the name and trademark. The nightclub was demolished in 2002 - Crosby Homes had acquired the property some time before that and, on Saturday 25 November 2000, held a charity auction of the various fixtures and fittings from the nightclub. Clubgoers and enthusiasts from across the country attended to buy memorabilia ranging from the DJ booth box and radiators to emergency exit lights. The DJ booth was bought by Bobby Langley, ex Haienda DJ, and Head of Merchandise for Sony Music London for an undisclosed fee.
     </p>
     <p>
      Crosby Homes were widely criticised for using the Haienda brand name - and featuring the strapline Now the partys over...you can come home in the promotional material. Another controversial feature of the branding campaign was the appropriation of many of the themes which ran through the original building. One of these was the iconic yellow and black hazard stripe motif which was a powerful element in the clubs original design, featuring as it did on the clubs dominant supporting pillars and later in much of the clubs literature and flyers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Winterbottom" title="Michael Winterbottom">
       Michael Winterbottom
      </a>
      's 2002 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/24_Hour_Party_People" title="24 Hour Party People">
        24 Hour Party People
       </a>
      </i>
      starring
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Coogan" title="Steve Coogan">
       Steve Coogan
      </a>
      as Tony Wilson, tells the story of the Haienda. The movie was filmed in 2001, and required reconstructing the Haienda as a temporary set in a Manchester factory, which was then opened to ticket holders for a night, acting as a full-scale nightclub (except with free bar) as the film shooting took place.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Manchester exhibition centre
      <a href="/wiki/Urbis" title="Urbis">
       Urbis
      </a>
      hosted an exhibition celebrating the 25th anniversary of the club's opening, which ran from mid-July 2007 until mid-February 2008.
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hook" title="Peter Hook">
       Peter Hook
      </a>
      and many other of those originally involved contributed or loaned material.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchester_Museum_of_Science_and_Industry" title="Manchester Museum of Science and Industry">
       Manchester Museum of Science and Industry
      </a>
      now holds a variety of Haienda and Factory records artefacts, including the main loading bay doors from the club, and a wide array of posters, fliers and props.
      <a href="/wiki/Rob_Gretton" title="Rob Gretton">
       Rob Gretton
      </a>
      bequeathed his collection of Haienda memorabilia to the Museum.
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 2009, Peter Hook published his book on his time as co-owner of the Haienda,
      <i>
       How Not to Run a Club
      </i>
      .
     </p>
