   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Electromagnetic forming
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Electromagnetic forming
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       EM forming
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       magneforming
      </b>
      ) is a type of high velocity, cold
      <a href="/wiki/Category:Metal_forming" title="Category:Metal forming">
       forming
      </a>
      process for electrically conductive metals, most commonly
      <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
       copper
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
       aluminium
      </a>
      . The workpiece is reshaped by high intensity
      <a href="/wiki/Pulse_(signal_processing)" title="Pulse (signal processing)">
       pulsed
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
       magnetic fields
      </a>
      that induce a current in the workpiece and a corresponding repulsive magnetic field, rapidly repelling portions of the workpiece. The workpiece can be reshaped without any contact from a tool, although in some instances the piece may be pressed against a die or former. The technique is sometimes called
      <i>
       high velocity forming
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       electromagnetic pulse technology
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">
       Explanation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_forming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A special coil is placed near the metallic workpiece, replacing the pusher in traditional forming. When the system releases its intense magnetic pulse , the coil generates a magnetic field which in turn accelerates the workpiece to hyper speed and onto the die. The magnetic pulse (MP) and the extreme deformation speed transforms the metal into a visco-plastic state  increasing formability without affecting the native strength of the material
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.bmax.com/technology/magnetic-pulse-forming/" rel="nofollow">
       magnetic pulse forming illustration
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A rapidly changing magnetic field induces a circulating
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">
       electric current
      </a>
      within a nearby
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductor_(material)" title="Conductor (material)">
       conductor
      </a>
      through
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">
       electromagnetic induction
      </a>
      . The induced current creates a corresponding magnetic field around the conductor (see
      <a href="/wiki/Pinch_(plasma_physics)" title="Pinch (plasma physics)">
       Pinch (plasma physics)
      </a>
      ). Because of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lenz%27s_Law" title="Lenz's Law">
       Lenz's Law
      </a>
      , the magnetic fields created within the conductor and work coil strongly repel each other.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1468" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png/220px-Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png/330px-Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png/440px-Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Electromagnetic_Forming_01.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        When the switch is closed, electrical energy stored in the
        <i>
         capacitor bank
        </i>
        (left) is discharged through the
        <i>
         forming coil
        </i>
        (orange) producing a rapidly changing magnetic field which induces a current to flow in the metallic
        <i>
         workpiece
        </i>
        (pink). The current flowing the workpiece produces a corresponding opposite magnetic field which rapidly repels the workpiece from the forming coil, reshaping the workpiece - in this case, compressing the diameter of the cylindrical tube. The reciprocal forces acting against the forming coil are resisted by the '
        <i>
         supportive coil casing
        </i>
        (green).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In practice the metal workpiece to be fabricated is placed in proximity to a heavily constructed coil of wire (called the
      <i>
       work coil
      </i>
      ). A huge pulse of current is forced through the work coil by rapidly discharging a high voltage
      <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">
       capacitor
      </a>
      bank using an
      <a href="/wiki/Ignitron" title="Ignitron">
       ignitron
      </a>
      or a
      <a href="/wiki/Spark_gap" title="Spark gap">
       spark gap
      </a>
      as a
      <a href="/wiki/Switch" title="Switch">
       switch
      </a>
      . This creates a rapidly oscillating, ultrastrong
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">
       electromagnetic field
      </a>
      around the work coil.
     </p>
     <p>
      The high work coil current (typically tens or hundreds of thousands of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amperes" title="Amperes">
       amperes
      </a>
      ) creates ultrastrong magnetic forces that easily overcome the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yield_strength" title="Yield strength">
       yield strength
      </a>
      of the metal work piece, causing permanent deformation. The metal forming process occurs extremely quickly (typically tens of
      <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">
       microseconds
      </a>
      ) and, because of the large forces, portions of the workpiece undergo high
      <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">
       acceleration
      </a>
      reaching velocities of up to 300 m/s.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
       Applications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_forming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The forming process is most often used to shrink or expand cylindrical tubing, but it can also form sheet metal by repelling the work piece onto a shaped
      <a href="/wiki/Die_(manufacturing)" title="Die (manufacturing)">
       die
      </a>
      at a high
      <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
       velocity
      </a>
      . High-quality joints can be formed, either by electromagnetic pulse crimping with a mechanical interlock or by
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_pulse_welding" title="Magnetic pulse welding">
       electromagnetic pulse welding
      </a>
      with a true metallurgical weld. Since the forming operation involves high
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerate" title="Accelerate">
       acceleration
      </a>
      and deceleration, mass of the work piece plays a critical role during the forming process. The process works best with good
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">
       electrical conductors
      </a>
      such as copper or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">
       aluminum
      </a>
      , but it can be adapted to work with poorer conductors such as
      <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
       steel
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_Mechanical_Forming">
       Comparison with Mechanical Forming
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_forming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Comparison with Mechanical Forming">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Electromagnetic forming has a number of advantages and disadvantages compared to conventional mechanical forming techniques.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of the advantages are;
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Improved formability (the amount of stretch available without tearing)
      </li>
      <li>
       Wrinkling can be greatly suppressed
      </li>
      <li>
       Forming can be combined with joining and assembling with dissimilar components including glass, plastic, composites and other metals.
      </li>
      <li>
       Close tolerances are possible as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Springback" title="Springback">
        springback
       </a>
       can be significantly reduced.
      </li>
      <li>
       Single sided dies are sufficient which can reduce tooling costs
      </li>
      <li>
       Lubricants are reduced or are unnecessary, so forming can be used in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_room" title="Clean room">
        clean room
       </a>
       conditions
      </li>
      <li>
       Mechanical contact with the workpiece is not required, this avoids surface contamination and tooling marks. As a result, a surface finish can be applied to the workpiece before forming.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The principle disadvantages are;
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Non conductive materials cannot be formed directly, but can be formed using a conductive drive plate
      </li>
      <li>
       The high voltages and currents involved require careful safety considerations
      </li>
     </ul>
