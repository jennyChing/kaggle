          <h1 class="post_title">
           <a href="http://www.affordablehealthmag.com/small-business-motivation-first-evolu/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Small Business: Motivation first  Evolu">
            Small Business: Motivation first  Evolu
           </a>
          </h1>
          <ul class="post_meta">
           <li class="posted_by">
            <span>
             <a href="http://www.affordablehealthmag.com/author/HughMartin/" rel="author" title="Posts by Hugh Martin">
              Hugh Martin

          <div style="float:left;clear:both;margin-top:5px;margin-bottom:5px;padding-top:5px;padding-bottom:5px;margin-right:5px;">
           <!--featured-image:/img/mix/.jpg-->
          </div>
          <div class="insertedContent">
           <strong>
            Beauty business founders tell how finding the market gap and filling it has led to international recognition.
           </strong>
          </div>
          <div class="articleMedia ">
           <figure>
            <img alt="Kati Kasza founded the New Zealand botanical skincare brand Evolu with her husband, Paul, in 1997, when the 
natural beauty products category was still in its infancy. Photo / Mara Sommer" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/media.nzherald.co.nz/webcontent/image/jpg/20149/SCCZEN_210214SPLKASZA1_620x310.jpg?resize=620%2C310"/>
           </figure>
           <p>
            <figcaption class="caption">
             Kati Kasza founded the New Zealand botanical skincare brand Evolu with her husband, Paul, in 1997, when the
             <br/>
             natural beauty products category was still in its infancy. Photo / Mara Sommer
            </figcaption>
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Necessity is said to be the mother of invention, and its a phrase that seems to capture the motivations of many Ive heard from who have started small natural beauty products businesses.
          </p>
          <p>
           The founder of
           <a href="http://evolu.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Evolu
            </strong>
           </a>
           , Kati Kasza, is one who says a sense of frustration drove her to start her business when she found the usual skincare brands werent meeting her needs and she felt she was wasting her money.
          </p>
          <p>
           Another is Lucy Vincent Marr, who couldnt find products for her beauty and hair salons that satisfied her requirements for purity of ingredients, beautiful design and efficacy.
          </p>
          <p>
           This led her to set up the hair and bodycare range Sans [ceuticals].
          </p>
          <!--Ad Injection:random-->
          <div style="float:left;clear:both;margin-top:10px;margin-bottom:10px;padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-right:5px;">
           <div itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
            <a href="http://www4.affordablehealthmag.com" itemprop="url" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             <img alt="Best Healthcare Website" itemprop="contentURL" src="/img/mix/Best_Healthcare_website_154.jpg"/>
            </a>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           There is a proliferation of brands in this space, so strong personal motivation is certainly helpful for making a go of things in what can be a crowded market.
          </p>
          <p>
           The executive director of Natural Products New Zealand, Alison Quesnel, says the natural skincare sector is growing, and the local market is showing enormous interest in natural and organic products.
          </p>
          <p>
           Quesnel points to the growing LOHAS trend  it stands for lifestyles of health and sustainability  as a driver.
          </p>
          <p>
           Many of the companies interviewed this week are also exporting their products, tapping into strong international growth in this market.
          </p>
          <p>
           A recent global report by Kline and Company says that last year, the natural personal care market grew 10.6 per cent worldwide and sales in China were up 24 per cent.
          </p>
          <p>
           Many of the firms in this market say they are emphasising their New Zealandness when selling overseas, capitalising on the association with our 100% pure image.
          </p>
          <p>
           But the products still have to stack up on their own merits.
          </p>
          <p>
           Solid cosmetics startup Sorbet is talking to a chain of department stores in Taiwan about stocking the Canterbury firms products, and founder Brianne West says shes been told New Zealand-made products are in high demand.
          </p>
          <p>
           We just need to ensure New Zealand maintains that reputation, she says.
          </p>
          <p>
           Other trends Kline sees as driving growth in the sector include a greater consumer push for transparency in product labelling and new product offerings for different demographic groups.
          </p>
          <p>
           Alana Riley, of Nelson brand Oxygen, for example, has developed products for teens as part of her range and is taking a roadshow around New Zealand this year to teach personal presentation skills to teenagers.
          </p>
          <p>
           For me, its important were more than a company selling skincare, she said. Its about educating teens in how to care about themselves.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Authenticity, trust key to skincare brand
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Kati Kasza is the founder of New Zealand botanical skincare brand Evolu. Started in 1997, the Auckland-based firm has eight staff and markets in New Zealand, Asia and the US.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            How did you get into the natural beauty products business?
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Before Evolu existed, I was making Kottis Kreams. My passion for plants comes from my Hungarian parents, and Kotti is the Hungarian pronunciation of my name. Mum was a botanist and herbalist and Dad was one of New Zealands wine-making pioneers. I was driven to start making my own natural skincare out of frustration; none of the usual brands were satisfying my skins needs and I felt like I was wasting so much money.
          </p>
          <p>
           So I took to heart my mothers advice, which was youre better off making your own. This was the mid-1990s, and the natural beauty products category was still in its early days. Id just married my husband, Paul, and we saw an opportunity to evolve a money-making hobby into a full-time business together. Thats how we came up with the brands name.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            The company has been around for a number of years in a crowded market. What factors do you think help a company endure in this space?
           </strong>
           <br/>
           Perseverance, design and staying true to yourself. Competing in such a mature and saturated market has been just as tough as everybody warned us it would be. Weve learned to take the setbacks, learn from them and just keep going. Design has also been something weve focused on. Beauty is all about self-image and attraction, so we think theres no reason why natural shouldnt also be beautiful, even a bit sexy.
          </p>
          <p>
           Above all the most important motivation for us was to build a brand with real meaning, to have it stand for something. So much beauty marketing feels superficial. We wanted Evolu to be about authenticity and trust. We like what a business guru once said: culture eats strategy for breakfast. Weve come to really appreciate what that means. Were proud of Evolus reputation and its more valuable to us than having the perfect strategy.
          </p>

             <img alt="Best Healthcare Website" itemprop="contentURL" src="/img/mix/health_nutrition_vitamin_011.jpg"/>
            </a>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           <strong>
            What have been some of the other major challenges?
           </strong>
           <br/>
           For any new beauty brand, getting scale in distribution is probably the biggest challenge. From day one we embraced e-commerce as a way to reach customers, and we were one of the earliest in New Zealand to do so. Weve also had to become very creative and agile in developing products to keep pace with changes in the natural beauty category.
          </p>
          <p>
           Exporting has always been a challenge. In the early years we chased overseas markets before we were really ready, but it was invaluable experience. In recent years weve got real traction with international business and it has been being disciplined in our home market that has given us the credentials to achieve that.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            In terms of exporting, have you found New Zealands 100% pure image helpful?
           </strong>
           <br/>
           In our experience, the pure New Zealand brand has certainly created trust for New Zealand beauty brands in overseas markets. But that trust is only one of many drivers for customers choosing beauty products and New Zealand isnt the only country trying to leverage pure and natural in cosmetics. So we believe New Zealand-made helps to open doors and minds, but its not enough of a brand differentiator by itself. The focus is so much more on performance from natural products these days. Overseas buyers responses tend to be in the vein of great, youre from New Zealand  and what else have you got?
          </p>
