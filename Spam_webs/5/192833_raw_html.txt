        <article class="post-1257 reviews type-reviews status-publish format-standard hentry category-movie-reviews genre-drama director-david-fincher mpaa_rating-pg-13 actor-andrew-garfield actor-jesse-eisenberg actor-justin-timberlake actor-rooney-mara" id="post-1257">
         <div class="textcontent clearfix entry-content" itemprop="reviewBody">
          <p>
           The best thing about the social-networking site Facebook is that you can practically say anything you want as long as you add lol at the end. Failing to add this very important acronym results in comments such as these, which were literally copied from my own Facebook account (however, I corrected them for grammatical issues/spelling):
          </p>
          <p>
           Youre a complete dick; I can see why you cant get any fucking girls.
          </p>
          <p>
           I cant wait for the day that you commit suicide.
          </p>
          <p>
           I want to slap you.
          </p>
          <p>
           Interestingly enough, the actual rise of Facebook, to the phenomenon that it is today, was just as hostile as the aforementioned comments, as made evident by director David Fincher and screenwriter Aaron Sorkins latest film
           <strong>
            The Social Network
           </strong>
           . The film, based off of Ben Mezrichs novel
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Accidental_Billionaires" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            The Accidental Billionaires
           </a>
           , uses two different lawsuit depositions as a framing device and offers a multi-perspective approach to narration.  The manner in which it is told, was a smart move by Sorkin, as the script is near perfect.
          </p>
          <p>
           Initially set on the Harvard University campus,
           <strong>
            The Social Network
           </strong>
           follows Mark Zuckerberg, a neurotic, sarcastic, pompous asshole who doesnt have trouble telling his girlfriend (Rooney Mara) that she isnt fit to join Harvards final club (an undergraduate social club for Harvards elite, in which ecstasy and drunken sex are plentiful). The thing is you cant just go in with a fake ID or claim that you know someone to get in; no, you need to do something absolutely mind-blowing and get recognized before youre even considered to be one of the members.
          </p>
          <p>
           Zuckerbergs obsession in joining the final club leads him on a drunken tirade during which he publically insults his now ex-girlfriend on his blog before creating a website by the name of Facemash.com which pits all of the undergraduate females against each other in a competition to see who is sexier. The site is a huge success with the campuss horny young men and pissed-off women and soon the sites traffic becomes so high that Harvards network crashes.
          </p>
          <p>
           This event is Marks big-break and though he faces disciplinary trouble with the school council (he hacked the Harvard directory to get the pics and info on the contestants), he meets the Winklevoss twins (Armie Hammer and Josh Pence), a duo of text-book douchebags who want to hire Mark for a website that theyve been planning  a social networking site that promised to transfer the entire experience of college right onto your personal computer. This would turn out to be the inspiration for Zuckerbergs own website, TheFacebook.com, whose popularity spreads like wildfire.
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, Zuckerbergs stint pisses off a lot of people and he eventually gets sued by both the Winklevoss twins who claim intellectual theft and Eduardo Saverin (Andrew Garfield), Facebooks co-founder and Marks best and only friend. From this point on,
           <strong>
            The Social Network
           </strong>
           uses a combination of flashbacks and flashfowards in order to move the narrative along. This method usually dislocates a films pacing but Sorkin avoids any such problems.
          </p>
          <p>
           Jesse Eisenberg is an excellent Mark Zuckerberg simply because he was so absorbed with his character. Everything from Zuckerbergs sarcasm, cynicism, and rude demeanor is played so naturally by Eisenberg, who is a bit of a black sheep in Hollywood, and is usually referred to as a Michael Cera clone. In a way Zuckerberg, who is a very conflicted character, reminded me of Gordon Gekko from
           <a href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0094291/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
            Wall Street
           </a>
           fame in his callous and calculating persona. Aaron Sorkin does an excellent job at making Zuckerberg likeable enough, while still portraying his demons. I also thought that Eisenberg sounded like Zuckerberg and his fast-paced style of reciting lines really mixed well with the character.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its very surprisingly that the other standout performance is actually by Justin Timberlake, who plays Sean Parker, the cocaine-using and womanizing creator of Napster, who signs onto the Facebook project. Parker serves as a sharp contrast to Zuckerberg and serves as a bit of a devil on Marks shoulder. Zuckerberg, who is easily intoxicated with the life of the social elite, quickly befriends Parker, who though charismatic, never shakes that feeling of sleaziness  its almost as if you want to trust him but you wouldnt be surprised if he stabbed you in the back.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, I did find it sort of distracting that they cast Disney-star Brenda Song in a role where she drops Eduardos pants and gives him a blowjob.
          </p>
          <p>
           Finchers impressive direction also deserves recognition.
           <strong>
            The Social Network
           </strong>
           is his best film, no doubt about it. Everything from the way it is shot to the way it is edited makes absolute sense and nothing is misplaced or forgotten about. But as I mentioned before, Aaron Sorkin is the real winner here. His writing talents are impeccable and he masterfully mixes a healthy dose of mature humor with dramatic tension, great characterization, fantastic build-up, and an excellent climax  all of the staples of a fantastic drama.
          </p>
         </div>
