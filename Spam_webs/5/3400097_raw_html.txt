      <p>
       <em>
        This post is part of

      <p>
       <strong>
        Today we will learn a fascinating little feature in excel called goal seek.
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       But what good is a feature if we cant find a use for it? So we will build a simple retirement calculator using excel.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before plunging in to the complex retirement calculations, let us spend a bunch of words understanding what this goal seek is all about.
      </p>
      <h3>
       What is goal seek in excel?
      </h3>
      <p>
       <img align="right" alt="goal-seek-excel-help" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-2229" height="142" src="http://static.chandoo.org/wp/wp-content/uploads/2009/07/goal-seek-excel-help.png" title="goal-seek-excel-help" width="166"/>
       <strong>
        We can think of goal seek as opposite of formulas.
       </strong>
       Formulas tell you what is the output of a bunch of variables used in an equation (for eg. sumproduct is an equation involving + and *).
       <strong>
        Goal seek tells you what inputs you need to give in order to get certain output.
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, you can use goal seek to solve a linear equation or find the internal return rate (IRR) of an investment.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now that you understand goal seek, let us plan your retirement.
       <img alt=":)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://static.chandoo.org/wp/wp-includes/images/smilies/simple-smile.png" style="height: 1em; max-height: 1em;"/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Make a financial model to estimate your monthly savings to meet retirement goals.
      </h3>
      <p>
       <img align="right" alt="Retirement Planning Worksheet using Excel" class="ppic" src="http://static.chandoo.org/img/l/retirement-calculator-worksheet.png"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       (Note: the image shows commas according to Indian currency formatting.)
      </p>
      <p>
       In order to proceed, we would need some data, like,
       <br/>
       (1) What is your current age?
       <br/>
       (2) What is your expected retirement age?
       <br/>
       (3) How much do you think you will spend every month when you retire (of course in todays prices)
       <br/>
       (4) Your expectation of inflation (%)?
       <br/>
       (5) Your expected return (%) on investments?
      </p>
      <p>
       Once the data is available, we will need to calculate the following,
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        I have shown the worksheet on the right with some dummy data.
       </em>
       <br/>
       (6) The yearly expenses at the time of retirement:  (3) * (1+(4))^((2)-(1))*12
       <br/>
       (7) Corpus required to generate the above amount every year (and leave the principle behind): (6)/(5)
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        (
        <strong>
         If these calculations are overwhelming
        </strong>
        , download the excel retirement calculator workbook
        <a href="http://cid-b663e096d6c08c74.skydrive.live.com/self.aspx/Public/retirement-calculator-excel.xls" target="_blank">
         here
        </a>
       </em>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       We know how much corpus is needed.
      </p>
      <p>
       We can use
       <a href="http://chandoo.org/excel-formulas/fv.html">
        FV() formula
       </a>
       to determine the future value of a series of payments made periodically and compounded at a given interest rate.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        We know how much the FV() out come should be, but we dont know how much the input (monthly investment) should be.
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        <strong>
         This is where goal seek is going to help us.
        </strong>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       Let us assume the monthly investment amount will be in cell A5. Let us also assume, the interest rate is in cell A4, retirement age is in A3, current age is in A2.
       <br/>
       We will write the FV formula in cell A6 like this =
       <code>
        -FV(A4/12,(A3-A2)*12,A5)
       </code>
       <br/>
       (we have to negate FV since it uses weird accounting notations)
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the cell A5 is blank, the FV will show the value as 0.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now, we will use goal seek to find out how much cell A5 should have so that A6 will be calculated to the corpus amount required.
      </p>
      <p>
       Go to Data tab and click on What if analysis and select goal seek. (In excel 2003, it should be in tools menu)
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        See this screen cast to understand how the goal seek works:
       </strong>
       <br/>
       <img alt="Retirement Savings Estimation using Excel Goal Seek" class="ppic" src="http://static.chandoo.org/img/l/retirement-calculator-using-excel.gif"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       The goal seek window has 3 inputs. The cell you need to change. The cell you want to set and the value to set.
      </p>
      <p>
       Once you use the goal seek it will find the correct (or closest) value to meet the goal and displays it. If you press OK, the value will be placed in the cell (in our case, in A5)
      </p>
      <p>
       That is all.
      </p>
      <h3>
       Download the Retirement Calculator Excel Worksheet and play with it
      </h3>
      <p>

      </p>

           @Tom.. What I dont understand is that there is no parameter for how long that I expect to live. In my mind this is a parameter relevant for the calculation.
          </p>
          <p>
           Not really. No one knows how long they can live. So instead my calculations find the corpus required for generating annuity forever (technically as long as the person lives). This has 2 advantages  (1) No need to assume the life span (2) the principle is left behind for people inheriting it.
          </p>
          <p>
           Another option is to find corpus needed to generate annuity for say 20 years (or 40 years). In this case, we would need the life expectancy.
          </p>
          <p>
           PS: I have corrected the descriptions.
          </p>
          <p>
           @Mathias: Agree. Goal seek is good at finding solutions for linear and simpler equations. The more complicated it gets, one should either use solver or go for simulations. As you can guess, goal seek uses some kind of brute force to crack the formula.
          </p>
          <p>
           Solver on the other hand uses more sophisticated operations research principles like maxima, minima, derivatives, boundaries (most OR problems have solutions at the boundaries alone). But modeling a problem in Solver is much more difficult. Goal seek comes handy in those cases.
          </p>
          <p>
           Your post highlights some of the problems of this brute force approach.
          </p>
