     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">
       botany
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Dendrology" title="Dendrology">
       dendrology
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       rhizome
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
           r
          </span>
          <span title="/a/ long 'i' in 'tide'">
           a
          </span>
          <span title="'z' in 'Zion'">
           z
          </span>
          <span title="/o/ long 'o' in 'code'">
           o
          </span>
          <span title="'m' in 'my'">
           m
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      , from
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       AncientGreek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <i>
        rhzma
       </i>
      </span>
      "mass of roots",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      from
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <i>
        rhiz
       </i>
      </span>
      "cause to strike root")
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a modified
      <a href="/wiki/Subterranea_(geography)" title="Subterranea (geography)">
       subterranean
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">
       stem
      </a>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">
       plant
      </a>
      that is usually found underground, often sending out
      <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">
       roots
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shoot" title="Shoot">
       shoots
      </a>
      from its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Node_(botany)" title="Node (botany)">
       nodes
      </a>
      . Rhizomes are also called
      <b>
       creeping rootstalks
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       rootstocks
      </b>
      . Rhizomes develop from
      <a href="/wiki/Axillary_bud" title="Axillary bud">
       axillary buds
      </a>
      and are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diageotropic" title="Diageotropic">
       diageotropic
      </a>
      or grow perpendicular to the force of gravity. The rhizome also retains the ability to allow new shoots to grow upwards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jang1148_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jang1148-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      If a rhizome is separated into pieces, each piece may be able to give rise to a new plant. The plant uses the rhizome to store
      <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">
       starches
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">
       proteins
      </a>
      , and other nutrients. These nutrients become useful for the plant when new shoots must be formed or when the plant dies back for the winter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jang1148_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jang1148-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is a process known as
      <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">
       vegetative reproduction
      </a>
      and is used by farmers and gardeners to propagate certain plants. This also allows for lateral spread of grasses like
      <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">
       bamboo
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunch_grasses" title="Bunch grasses">
       bunch grasses
      </a>
      . Examples of plants that are propagated this way include
      <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">
       hops
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Asparagus" title="Asparagus">
       asparagus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">
       ginger
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Iris_(plant)" title="Iris (plant)">
       irises
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lily_of_the_Valley" title="Lily of the Valley">
       Lily of the Valley
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canna_(plant)" title="Canna (plant)">
       Cannas
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sympodial" title="Sympodial">
       sympodial
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchid" title="Orchid">
       orchids
      </a>
      . Some rhizomes which are used directly in cooking include ginger,
      <a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">
       turmeric
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Galangal" title="Galangal">
       galangal
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fingerroot" title="Fingerroot">
       fingerroot
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Stored rhizomes are subject to bacterial and fungal infections making them unsuitable for replanting and greatly diminishing stocks. However rhizomes can also be produced artificially from tissue cultures. The ability to easily grow rhizomes from tissue cultures leads to better stocks for replanting and greater yields.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The plant hormones
      <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">
       ethylene
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jasmonic_acid" title="Jasmonic acid">
       jasmonic acid
      </a>
      have been found to help induce and regulate the growth of rhizomes, specifically in
      <i>
       Rheum rabarbarum
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Rhubarb" title="Rhubarb">
       rhubarb
      </a>
      ). Ethylene that was applied externally was found to affect internal ethylene levels, allowing for easy manipulations of ethylene concentrations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Knowledge of how to use these hormones to induce rhizome growth could help farmers and biologists producing plants grown from rhizomes more easily cultivate and grow better plants.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poplars" title="Poplars">
       poplars
      </a>
      (species of
      <i>
       Populus
      </i>
      ) are an example of trees that propagate using a rhizome. The
      <a href="/wiki/Pando_(tree)" title="Pando (tree)">
       Pando
      </a>
      colony in Utah is a famous example, which has been living for about 80,000 years. The rhizome of a poplar colony is the key to its longevity: foragers, insects, fungus, and forest fires may destroy the above-ground portion of the tree, but the underground rhizome is somewhat protected against these threats.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">
       stolon
      </a>
      is similar to a rhizome, but, unlike a rhizome, which is the main stem of the plant, a stolon sprouts from an existing stem, has long internodes, and generates new shoots at the end, such as in the
      <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">
       strawberry
      </a>
      plant. In general, rhizomes have short internodes; they send out roots from the bottom of the nodes and new upward-growing shoots from the top of the nodes. A stem
      <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">
       tuber
      </a>
      is a thickened part of a rhizome or
      <a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">
       stolon
      </a>
      that has been enlarged for use as a
      <a href="/wiki/Storage_organ" title="Storage organ">
       storage organ
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In general, a tuber is high in
      <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">
       starch
      </a>
      , for example, the common
      <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">
       potato
      </a>
      , which is a modified stolon. The term tuber is often used imprecisely, and is sometimes applied to plants with rhizomes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some plants have rhizomes that grow above ground or that lie at the soil surface, including some
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Iris_(plant)" title="Iris (plant)">
        Iris
       </a>
      </i>
      species, and
      <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">
       ferns
      </a>
      , whose spreading stems are rhizomes. Plants with underground rhizomes include
      <a href="/wiki/Zingiberaceae" title="Zingiberaceae">
       gingers
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">
       bamboo
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venus_Flytrap" title="Venus Flytrap">
       Venus Flytrap
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Physalis_alkekengi" title="Physalis alkekengi">
       Chinese lantern
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_poison-oak" title="Western poison-oak">
       Western poison-oak
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">
       hops
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Alstroemeria" title="Alstroemeria">
       Alstroemeria
      </a>
      , and the weeds
      <a href="/wiki/Johnson_grass" title="Johnson grass">
       Johnson grass
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_grass" title="Bermuda grass">
       bermuda grass
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Cyperus_rotundus" title="Cyperus rotundus">
       purple nut sedge
      </a>
      . Rhizomes generally form a single layer, but in
      <a href="/wiki/Equisetum" title="Equisetum">
       Giant Horsetails
      </a>
      , can be multi-tiered.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many rhizomes have culinary value, and some, such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zhe%27ergen" title="Zhe'ergen">
        zhe'ergen
       </a>
      </i>
      , are commonly consumed raw.

          citation needed

        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
