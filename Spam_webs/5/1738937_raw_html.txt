     <p>
      A
      <b>
       kiss
      </b>
      is the touch or pressing of one's lips against another person or an object. Cultural connotations of kissing vary widely. Depending on the culture and context, a kiss can express sentiments of
      <a href="/wiki/Love" title="Love">
       love
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Passion_(emotion)" title="Passion (emotion)">
       passion
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Romance_(love)" title="Romance (love)">
       romance
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_attraction" title="Sexual attraction">
       sexual attraction
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_activity" title="Sexual activity">
       sexual activity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_arousal" title="Sexual arousal">
       sexual arousal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Affection" title="Affection">
       affection
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Respect" title="Respect">
       respect
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Greeting" title="Greeting">
       greeting
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Friendship" title="Friendship">
       friendship
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">
       peace
      </a>
      and good luck, among many others. In some situations a kiss is a ritual, formal or symbolic gesture indicating devotion, respect, or
      <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">
       sacrament
      </a>
      . The word came from
      <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
       Old English
      </a>
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:cyssan#Old_English" title="wikt:en:cyssan">
        cyssan
       </a>
      </i>
      (to kiss), in turn from
      <i>
       coss
      </i>
      (a kiss).
     </p>

     <p>
      Anthropologists are divided into two schools on the origins of kissing, one believing that it is instinctual and intuitive and the other that it evolved from what is known as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Premastication" title="Premastication">
        kiss feeding
       </a>
      </i>
      , a process used by mothers to feed their infants by passing chewed food to their babies' mouths.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-demirjian_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-demirjian-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Lombroso" title="Cesare Lombroso">
       Cesare Lombroso
      </a>
      , Italian criminologist, physician and founder of the Italian School of Positivist Criminology, supported this idea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lombroso_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lombroso-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The earliest reference to kissing-like behavior comes from the Vedas, Sanskrit scriptures that informed Hinduism, Buddhism and the Jain religion, around 3,500 years ago, according to Vaughn Bryant, an anthropologist at Texas A&amp;M University who specializes in the history of the kiss.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the later Classical period, affectionate mouth-to-mouth kissing was first described in the Hindu epic, the Mahabharata.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Academics who have studied it say kissing spread slowly to other parts of the world after Alexander the Great and his army conquered parts of Punjab in northern India in 326 B.C.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Vaughn Bryant, an anthropologist at Texas A&amp;M University who specializes in the history of the kiss, the origin of the word 'kiss' itself originated in ancient India, where busa or bosa were used to refer to kissing and from these early words, the Latin term for kiss basium and the Old English words ba and buss are derived. Meanwhile, the root of the English word we use today kiss stems from kus which was used in northern India.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">
       Sumer
      </a>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">

       <br/>

      </p>

       <br/>

      <i>
       osculum
      </i>

      <i>

      </i>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
       Types
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Kristoffer Nyrop identified a number of types of kisses, including kisses of love, affection, peace, respect and friendship. He notes, however, that the categories are somewhat contrived and overlapping, and some cultures have more kinds, including the French with twenty and the Germans with thirty.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Expression_of_affection_and_love">
       Expression of affection and love
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expression of affection and love">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kissing another person's lips has become a common expression of affection in many cultures worldwide. Yet in certain cultures, kissing was introduced only through European settlement, before which it was not a routine occurrence. Such cultures include certain indigenous peoples of Australia, the Tahitians, and many tribes in Africa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyer_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dyer-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Kissing on the lips is a physical expression of affection or love between two people in which the sensations of touch, taste, and smell are involved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brayer_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brayer-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to the psychologist Menachem Brayer, although many "mammals, birds, and insects exchange caresses" which appear to be kisses of affection, they are not kisses in the human sense. The psychologist William Cane notes that kissing in Western society is most often a romantic act and describes a few of its attributes:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       It's not hard to tell when two people are in love. Maybe they're trying to hide it from the world, still they cannot conceal their inner excitement. Men will give themselves away by a certain excited trembling in the muscles of the lower jaw upon seeing their beloved. Women will often turn pale immediately of seeing their lover and then get slightly red in the face as their sweetheart draws near. . . . This is the effect of physical closeness upon two people who are in love.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cane_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cane-19">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         19
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :9
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Romantic kissing in Western cultures is a fairly recent development and is rarely mentioned even in ancient Greek literature. In the Middle Ages it became a social gesture and was considered a sign of refinement of the upper classes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brayer_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brayer-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :150151
      </sup>
      Other cultures have different definitions and uses of kissing, notes Brayer. In
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      , for example, a similar expression of affection consists of rubbing one's nose against the cheek of another person. In other Eastern cultures kissing is not common. In South East Asian countries the "sniff kiss" is the most common form of affection and Western
      <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">
       mouth
      </a>
      to mouth kissing is often reserved for sexual foreplay. In some tribal cultures the "equivalent for our 'kiss me' is 'smell me.'"
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Surveys indicate that kissing is the second most common form of
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_intimacy" title="Physical intimacy">
       physical intimacy
      </a>
      among United States adolescents (after
      <a href="/wiki/Holding_hands" title="Holding hands">
       holding hands
      </a>
      ), and that about 85% of 15 to 16-year-old adolescents in the US have experienced it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In many cultures, it is considered a harmless custom for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teenagers" title="Teenagers">
       teenagers
      </a>
      to kiss on a
      <a href="/wiki/Dating" title="Dating">
       date
      </a>
      or to engage in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Party_games" title="Party games">
       kissing games
      </a>
      with friends. These games serve as icebreakers at parties and may be some participants' first exposure to sexuality. There are many such games, including
      <a href="/wiki/Truth_or_Dare%3F" title="Truth or Dare?">
       Truth or Dare?
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_Minutes_in_Heaven" title="Seven Minutes in Heaven">
       Seven Minutes in Heaven
      </a>
      (or the variation "Two Minutes in the Closet"),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin_the_Bottle" title="Spin the Bottle">
       Spin the Bottle
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Post_Office_(game)" title="Post Office (game)">
       Post Office
      </a>
      , and Wink.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_or_romantic_kiss">
       Sexual or romantic kiss
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sexual or romantic kiss">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kuss.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="421" data-file-width="600" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Kuss.jpg/220px-Kuss.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Kuss.jpg/330px-Kuss.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Kuss.jpg/440px-Kuss.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kuss.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A romantic kiss.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The kiss can be an important expression of love and
      <a href="/wiki/Eroticism" title="Eroticism">
       erotic
      </a>
      emotions. In his book
      <i>
       The Kiss and its History
      </i>
      , Kristoffer Nyrop describes the kiss of love as an "exultant message of the longing of love, love eternally young, the burning prayer of hot desire, which is born on the lovers' lips, and 'rises,' as Charles Fuster has said, 'up to the blue sky from the green plains,' like a tender, trembling thank-offering." Nyrop adds that the love kiss, "rich in promise, bestows an intoxicating feeling of infinite happiness, courage, and youth, and therefore surpasses all other earthly joys in sublimity."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :30
      </sup>
      He also compares it to achievements in life: "Thus even the highest work of art, yet, the loftiest reputation, is nothing in comparison with the passionate kiss of a woman one loves."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :31
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The power of a kiss is not minimized when he writes that "we all yearn for kisses and we all seek them; it is idle to struggle against this passion. No one can evade the omnipotence of the kiss ..." Kissing, he implies, can lead one to maturity: "It is through kisses that a knowledge of life and happiness first comes to us. Runeberg says that the angels rejoice over the first kiss exchanged by lovers," and can keep one feeling young: "It carries life with it; it even bestows the gift of eternal youth." The importance of the lover's kiss can also be significant, he notes: "In the case of lovers a kiss is everything; that is the reason why a man stakes his all for a kiss," and "man craves for it as his noblest reward."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :37
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As a result, kissing as an expression of love is contained in much of literature, old and new. Nyrop gives a vivid example in the classic love story of
      <a href="/wiki/Daphnis_and_Chloe" title="Daphnis and Chloe">
       Daphnis and Chloe
      </a>
      . As a reward "Chloe has bestowed a kiss on Daphnisan innocent young-maid's kiss, but it has on him the effect of an electrical shock":
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :47
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       Ye gods, what are my feelings. Her lips are softer than the rose's leaf, her mouth is sweet as honey, and her kiss inflicts on me more pain than a bee's sting. I have often kissed my kids, I have often kissed my lambs, but never have I known aught like this. My pulse is beating fast, my heart throbs, it is as if I were about to suffocate, yet, nevertheless, I want to have another kiss. Strange, never-suspected pain! Has Chloe, I wonder, drunk some poisonous draught ere she kissed me? How comes it that she herself has not died of it?
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Romantic kissing "requires more than simple proximity," notes Cane. It also needs "some degree of intimacy or privacy, ... which is why you'll see lovers stepping to the side of a busy street or sidewalk."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cane_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cane-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Psychologist
      <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Reich" title="Wilhelm Reich">
       Wilhelm Reich
      </a>
      "lashed out at society" for not giving young lovers enough privacy and making it difficult to be alone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cane_19-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cane-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Cane describes how many lovers manage to attain romantic privacy despite being in a public setting, as they "lock their minds together" and thereby create an invisible sense of "psychological privacy." He adds, "In this way they can kiss in public even in a crowded plaza and keep it romantic."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cane_19-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cane-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :10
      </sup>
      Nonetheless, when Cane asked people to describe the most romantic places they ever kissed, "their answers almost always referred to this ends-of-the-earth isolation, ... they mentioned an apple orchard, a beach, out in a field looking at the stars, or at a pond in a secluded area ..."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cane_19-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cane-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :10
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      A kiss can also be used to express feelings without an erotic element but can be nonetheless "far deeper and more lasting," writes Nyrop. He adds that such kisses can be expressive of love "in the widest and most comprehensive meaning of the word, bringing a message of loyal affection, gratitude, compassion, sympathy, intense joy, and profound sorrow."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">

      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The most common example is the "intense feeling which knits parents to their offspring," writes Nyrop, but adds that kisses of affection are not only common between parents and children, but also between other members of the same family, which can include those outside the immediate family circle, "everywhere where deep affection unites people."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :82
      </sup>
      The tradition is written of in the Bible, as when
      <a href="/wiki/Orpah" title="Orpah">
       Orpah
      </a>
      kissed her mother-in-law and when
      <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">
       Moses
      </a>
      went to meet his father-in-law, he "did obeisance, and kissed him; and they asked each other of their welfare; and they came into the tent" (Exodus 18:7); and when
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">
       Jacob
      </a>
      had wrestled with the Lord he met
      <a href="/wiki/Esau" title="Esau">
       Esau
      </a>
      , ran towards him, fell on his neck and kissed him. The family kiss was traditional with the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
       Romans
      </a>
      and kisses of affection are often mentioned by the early
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       Greeks
      </a>
      , as when
      <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">
       Odysseus
      </a>
      , on reaching his home, meets his faithful shepherds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :8283
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Affection can be a cause of kissing "in all ages in grave and solemn moments," notes Nyrop, "not only among those who love each other, but also as an expression of profound gratitude. When the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostle_Paul" title="Apostle Paul">
       Apostle Paul
      </a>
      took leave of the elders of the congregation at
      <a href="/wiki/Ephesus" title="Ephesus">
       Ephesus
      </a>
      , "they all wept sore, and fell on Paul's neck and kissed him" (Acts 20:37). Kisses can also be exchanged between total strangers, as when there is a profound sympathy with or the warmest interest in, another person.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :85
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_poetry" title="Folk poetry">
       Folk poetry
      </a>
      has been the source of affectionate kisses where they sometimes played an important part, as when they had the power to cast off spells or to break bonds of witchcraft and sorcery, often restoring a man to his original shape. Nyrop notes the poetical stories of the "redeeming power of the kiss are to be found in the literature of many countries, especially, for example, in the Old French Arthurian romances (Lancelot, Guiglain, Tirant le blanc) in which the princess is changed by evil arts into a dreadful dragon, and can only resume her human shape in the case of a knight being brave enough to kiss her." In the reverse situation, in the tale of "
      <a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast" title="Beauty and the Beast">
       Beauty and the Beast
      </a>
      ," a transformed prince then told the girl that he had been bewitched by a wicked fairy, and could not be recreated into a man unless a maid fell in love with him and kissed him, despite his ugliness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :9596
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A kiss of affection can also take place after death. In
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">
       Genesis
      </a>
      it is written that when
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">
       Jacob
      </a>
      was dead, "Joseph fell upon his father's face and wept upon him and kissed him." And it is told of
      <a href="/wiki/Abu_Bakr" title="Abu Bakr">
       Abu Bakr
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">
       Muhammad
      </a>
      's first disciple, father-in-law, and successor, that, when the prophet was dead, he went into the latter's tent, uncovered his face, and kissed him. Nyrop writes that "the kiss is the last tender proof of love bestowed on one we have loved, and was believed, in ancient times, to follow mankind to the nether world."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :97
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kiss_as_ritual">

     <p>
      Throughout history, a kiss has been a ritual, formal, symbolic or social gesture indicating devotion, respect or greeting. It appears as a ritual or symbol of religious devotion. For example, in the case of kissing a temple floor, or a religious book or icon. Besides devotion, a kiss has also indicated subordination or, nowadays, respect.
     </p>
     <p>
      In modern times the practice continues, as in the case of a bride and groom kissing at the conclusion of a wedding ceremony or national leaders kissing each other in greeting, and in many other situations.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
       Religion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Religion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A kiss in a religious context is common. In earlier periods of
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
       Islam
      </a>
      kissing became a ritual gesture, and is still treated as such in certain customs, as when "kissing... relics, or a bishop's ring."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brayer_18-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brayer-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">
       Judaism
      </a>
      , the kissing of prayer books such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">
       Torah
      </a>
      , along with kissing
      <a href="/wiki/Tallit" title="Tallit">
       prayer shawls
      </a>
      , is also common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Crawley notes that it was "very significant of the affectionate element in religion" to give so important a part to the kiss as part of its ritual. In the early Church the baptized were kissed by the celebrant after the ceremony, and its use was even extended as a salute to saints and religious heroes, with Crawley adding, "Thus
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_(Hebrew_Bible)" title="Joseph (Hebrew Bible)">
       Joseph
      </a>
      kissed
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">
       Jacob
      </a>
      , and his disciples kissed
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">
       Paul
      </a>
      . Joseph kissed his dead father, and the custom was retained in our civilization," as the farewell kiss on dead relatives, although certain sects prohibit this today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawley_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Crawley-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :126
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A distinctive element in the Christian
      <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">
       liturgy
      </a>
      was noted by
      <a href="/wiki/Justin_Martyr" title="Justin Martyr">
       Justin
      </a>
      in the 2nd century, now referred to as the "
      <a href="/wiki/Kiss_of_peace" title="Kiss of peace">
       kiss of peace
      </a>
      ," and once part of the rite in the primitive Mass.
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Cornwallis_Conybeare" title="Frederick Cornwallis Conybeare">
       Conybeare
      </a>
      has stated that this act originated within the ancient Hebrew
      <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">
       synagogue
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">
       Philo
      </a>
      , the ancient Jewish philosopher called it a "kiss of harmony," where, as Crawley explains, "the Word of God brings hostile things together in concord and the kiss of love."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawley_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Crawley-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :128
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Cyril_of_Jerusalem" title="Cyril of Jerusalem">
       Saint Cyril
      </a>
      also writes, "this kiss is the sign that our souls are united, and that we banish all remembrance of injury."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawley_22-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Crawley-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :128
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kiss_of_peace">
       Kiss of peace
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Kiss of peace">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Nyrop notes that the
      <a href="/wiki/Kiss_of_peace" title="Kiss of peace">
       kiss of peace
      </a>
      was used as an expression of deep, spiritual devotion in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Christian_Church" title="Early Christian Church">
       early Christian Church
      </a>
      . Christ said, for instance, "Peace be with you, my peace I give you," and the members of Christ's Church gave each other peace symbolically through a kiss.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Paul" title="St Paul">
       St Paul
      </a>
      repeatedly speaks of the "holy kiss," and, in his
      <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">
       Epistle
      </a>
      to the Romans, writes: "Salute one another with an holy kiss" and his first Epistle to the Thessalonians (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Thessalonians" title="1 Thessalonians">
       1 Thessalonians
      </a>
      5:26), he says: "Greet all the brethren with an holy kiss."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :101
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The kiss of peace was also used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secular" title="Secular">
       secular
      </a>
      festivities. During the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      , for example, Nyrop points out that it was the custom to "seal the reconciliation and pacification of enemies by a kiss." Even knights gave each other the kiss of peace before proceeding to the combat, and forgave one another all real or imaginary wrongs. The holy kiss was also found in the ritual of the Church on solemn occasions, such as baptism, marriage, confession, ordination, or obsequies. However, toward the end of the Middle Ages the kiss of peace disappears as the official token of reconciliation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :109
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kiss_of_respect">
       Kiss of respect
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kiss of respect">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_(5851205620)_(2)_(cropped).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1427" data-file-width="1816" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_%285851205620%29_%282%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_%285851205620%29_%282%29_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_%285851205620%29_%282%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_%285851205620%29_%282%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_%285851205620%29_%282%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_%285851205620%29_%282%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Re-enactment_at_the_Seashore_(5851205620)_(2)_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Man kissing the ground after a long sea voyage (as part of a reenactment of the first landing of English settlers in Virginia in 1607)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The kiss of respect is of ancient origin, notes Nyrop. He writes that "from the remotest times we find it applied to all that is holy, noble, and worshipfulto the gods, their statues, temples, and altars, as well as to kings and emperors; out of reverence, people even kissed the ground, and both sun and moon were greeted with kisses."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :114
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He notes some examples, as "when the prophet
      <a href="/wiki/Hosea" title="Hosea">
       Hosea
      </a>
      laments over the idolatry of the children of
      <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
       Israel
      </a>
      , he says that they make molten images of calves and kiss them." In classical times similar homage was often paid to the gods, and people were known to kiss the hands, knees, feet, and the mouths, of their idols.
      <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
       Cicero
      </a>
      writes that the lips and beard of the famous statue of
      <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">
       Hercules
      </a>
      at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrigentum" title="Agrigentum">
       Agrigentum
      </a>
      were worn away by the kisses of devotees.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :115
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      People kissed the Cross with the image of the Crucified, and such kissing of the Cross is always considered a holy act. In many countries it is required, on taking an oath, as the highest assertion that the witness would be speaking the truth. Nyrop notes that "as a last act of charity, the image of the Redeemer is handed to the dying or death-condemned to be kissed." Kissing the Cross brings blessing and happiness; people kiss the image of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">
       Our Lady
      </a>
      and the pictures and statues of saintsnot only their pictures, "but even their relics are kissed," notes Nyrop. "They make both soul and body whole." There are legends innumerable of sick people regaining their health by kissing relics, he points out.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :121
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The kiss of respect has also represented a mark of fealty, humility and reverence. Its use in ancient times was widespread, and Nyrop gives examples: "people threw themselves down on the ground before their rulers, kissed their footprints, literally 'licked the dust,' as it is termed."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :124
      </sup>
      "Nearly everywhere, wheresoever an inferior meets a superior, we observe the kiss of respect. The Roman slaves kissed the hands of their masters; pupils and soldiers those of their teachers and captains respectively."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :124
      </sup>
      People also kissed the earth for joy on returning to their native land after a lengthened absence, as when
      <a href="/wiki/Agamemnon" title="Agamemnon">
       Agamemnon
      </a>
      returned from the
      <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">
       Trojan War
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kiss_of_friendship">
       Kiss of friendship
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Kiss of friendship">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The kiss is also commonly used in American and European culture as a salutation between friends or acquaintances. The friendly kiss until recent times usually occurred only between ladies, but today it is also common between men and women, especially if there is a great difference in age. According to Nyrop, up until the 20th century, "it seldom or never takes place between men, with the exception, however, of royal personages," although he notes that in former times the "friendly kiss was very common with us between man and man as well as between persons of opposite sexes." In guilds, for example, it was customary for the members to greet each other "with hearty
      <a href="/wiki/Handshake" title="Handshake">
       handshakes
      </a>
      and smacking kisses," and, on the conclusion of a meal, people thanked and kissed both their hosts and hostesses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyrop_16-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-Nyrop-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :142
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">
       Cultural significance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Approximately ten percent of the world do not kiss for a variety of reasons, including that they find it dirty or for superstitious reasons. For example, in parts of Sudan they believe that the mouth is the portal to the soul, so they do not want to invite death or have their spirit taken.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-demirjian_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-demirjian-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Psychology professor Elaine Hatfield noted that "kissing was far from universal and even seen as improper by many societies."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-India_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-India-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite kissing being wide spread, in some parts of the world it still is taboo to kiss publicly and is often banned in films or in other media. On-screen lip-kissing was not a regular in
      <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">
       Bollywood
      </a>
      until the 1990s, although it has been present from the time of the inception of Bollywood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are also
      <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">
       taboos
      </a>
      about whom one can kiss in some
      <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">
       Muslim
      </a>
      societies governed by religious law. In
      <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
       Iran
      </a>
      , a man who kisses or touches a woman who is not his wife or relative can be punished.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vitello_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Vitello-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">
       East Asia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Donald Richie comments that in Japan, as in China, although kissing took place in erotic situations, in public "the kiss was invisible," and the "touching of the lips never became the culturally encoded action it has for so long been in Europe and America." The early Edison film,
      <i>
       The Widow Jones the May Irwin-John Rice Kiss
      </i>
      (1886) created a sensation when it was shown in Tokyo, and people crowded to view the enormity. Likewise, Rodin's sculpture
      <i>
       The Kiss
      </i>
      was not displayed in Japan until after the Pacific War.

      <a href="/wiki/Manchu_people" title="Manchu people">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Fellatio" title="Fellatio">
       fellatio
      </a>
      on their male babies, placing its penis in their mouths and stimulating it, since it was not considered a sexual act, while the Manchu regarded public kissing with revulsion, which was considered sexual.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>

        </span>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>

        </span>

        <span>

        </span>
       </a>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_practices">
       Contemporary practices
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Contemporary practices">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In modern
      <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">
       Western culture
      </a>
      , kissing on the lips is most commonly an expression of
      <a href="/wiki/Affection" title="Affection">
       affection
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zambianchronicle-kiss_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zambianchronicle-kiss-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When lips are pressed together for an extended period, usually accompanied with an
      <a href="/wiki/Hug" title="Hug">
       embrace
      </a>
      , it is an expression of romantic and sexual desire. The practice of kissing with an open mouth, to allow the other to suck their lips or move their
      <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
       tongue
      </a>
      into their mouth, is called
      <a href="/wiki/French_kiss" title="French kiss">
       French kissing
      </a>
      . "
      <a href="/wiki/Making_out" title="Making out">
       Making out
      </a>
      " is often an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adolescent" title="Adolescent">
       adolescent
      </a>
      's first experience of their
      <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">
       sexuality
      </a>
      and games which involve kissing, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin_the_Bottle" title="Spin the Bottle">
       Spin the Bottle
      </a>
      , facilitate the experience. People may kiss children on the forehead to comfort them or the cheek to show affection.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kissing_in_films">
       Kissing in films
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Kissing in films">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first romantic kiss on screen was in American silent films in 1896, beginning with the film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Kiss_(1896_film)" title="The Kiss (1896 film)">
        The Kiss
       </a>
      </i>
      . The kiss lasted 30 seconds and caused many to rail against decadence in the new medium of silent film. Writer
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Black" title="Louis Black">
       Louis Black
      </a>
      writes that "it was the United States that brought kissing out of the Dark Ages."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Texas-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, it met with severe disapproval by defenders of public morality, especially in New York. One critic proclaimed that "it is absolutely disgusting. Such things call for police interference."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Texas-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="937" data-file-width="1226" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG/240px-Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG/360px-Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG/480px-Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rock_Hudson_-_Julie_Andrews.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Rock_Hudson" title="Rock Hudson">
         Rock Hudson
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">
         Julie Andrews
        </a>
        kissing in film
        <i>
         Darling Lilli
        </i>
        (1968)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Young moviegoers began emulating romantic stars on the screen, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Colman" title="Ronald Colman">
       Ronald Colman
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Valentino" title="Rudolph Valentino">
       Rudolph Valentino
      </a>
      , the latter known for ending his passionate scenes with a kiss. Valentino also began his romantic scenes with women by kissing her hand, traveling up her arm, and then kissing her on the back of her neck. Female actress were often turned into stars based on their screen portrayals of passion. Actresses like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazimova" title="Nazimova">
       Nazimova
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pola_Negri" title="Pola Negri">
       Pola Negri
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vilma_B%C3%A1nky" title="Vilma Bnky">
       Vilma Bnky
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Greta_Garbo" title="Greta Garbo">
       Greta Garbo
      </a>
      , became screen idols as a result.
     </p>
     <p>
      Eventually the film industry was forced by law to follow the dictates of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Production_Code" title="Production Code">
       Production Code
      </a>
      established in 1934, overseen by
      <a href="/wiki/Will_H._Hays" title="Will H. Hays">
       Will Hays
      </a>
      and supported by the church. According to the new code, "Excessive and lustful kissing, lustful embraces, suggestive postures and gestures, are not to be shown."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_33-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Texas-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result, kissing scenes were shortened, with scenes cut away, leaving the imagination of the viewer to take over. Under the code, actors kissing had to keep their feet on the ground and had to be either standing or sitting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Citron_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Citron-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The heyday of romantic kissing on the screen took place in the early sound era, during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Hollywood" title="Golden Age of Hollywood">
       Golden Age of Hollywood
      </a>
      in the 1930s and 1940s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :watch
      </sup>
      Body language began to be used to supplement romantic scenes, especially with the eyes, a talent that added to Greta Garbo's fame. Author Lana Citron writes that "men were perceived as the kissers and women the receivers. Should the roles ever be reversed, women were regarded as vamps . . ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Citron_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Citron-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Citron,
      <a href="/wiki/Mae_West" title="Mae West">
       Mae West
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Anna_May_Wong" title="Anna May Wong">
       Anna May Wong
      </a>
      were the only Hollywood actresses never to have been kissed on screen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Citron_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Citron-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among the films rated for having the most romantic kisses are
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">
        Gone with the Wind
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/From_Here_to_Eternity" title="From Here to Eternity">
        From Here to Eternity
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Casablanca_(film)" title="Casablanca (film)">
        Casablanca
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/To_Have_and_Have_Not_(film)" title="To Have and Have Not (film)">
        To Have and Have Not
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Citron_34-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Citron-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sociologist Eva Illouz notes that surveys taken in 1935 showed that "love was the most important theme represented in movies. Similar surveys during the 1930s found the 95% of films had romance as one of their plot lines, what film critics called "the romantic formula."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Illouz_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Illouz-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In early Japanese films, kissing and sexual expression were controversial. In 1931, a director slipped a kissing scene past the censor (who was a friend), but when the film opened in a downtown Tokyo theater, the screening was stopped and the film confiscated. During the American Occupation of Japan, in 1946, an American censor required a film to include a kissing scene. One scholar says that the censor suggested "we believe that even Japanese do something like kissing when they love each other. Why don't you include that in your films?" Americans encouraged such scenes to force the Japanese to express publicly actions and feelings that had been considered strictly private, since ever since Pearl Harbor they had felt that Japanese were "sneaky": "if Japanese kissed in private, they should do it in public too."

       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>

     <p>
      Female friends and close acquaintances commonly offer

      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Cheek_kissing" title="Cheek kissing">
       kisses on the cheek
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Greeting" title="Greeting">

      </a>

      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/farewell" title="wikt:farewell">

         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To a lesser extent this is also common between male and female friends, but men usually greet each other with a handshake. In some countries a single kiss is the custom, while in others a kiss on each cheek is the norm. In the United States an
      <a href="/wiki/Air_kiss" title="Air kiss">
       air kiss
      </a>
      is becoming more common. This involves kissing in the air near the cheek, with the cheeks touching or not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UIO-kiss_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UIO-kiss-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After a first
      <a href="/wiki/Dating" title="Dating">
       date
      </a>
      , it is common for the couple to give each other a quick kiss on the cheek on parting, to indicate that a good time was had and perhaps to indicate an interest in another meeting.
     </p>
     <p>
      A symbolic kiss is frequent in Western cultures. A kiss can be "blown" to another by kissing the fingertips and then blowing the fingertips, pointing them in the direction of the recipient. This is used to convey affection, usually when parting or when the partners are physically distant but can view each other. Blown kisses are also used when a person wishes to convey affection to a large crowd or audience. The term
      <i>
       flying kiss
      </i>
      is used in India to describe a blown kiss. In written correspondence a kiss has been represented by the letter "X" since at least 1763.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     </p>
     <p>
      In some Western cultures it is considered
      <a href="/wiki/Luck" title="Luck">
       good luck
      </a>
      to kiss someone on
      <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">
       Christmas
      </a>
      or on
      <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">
       New Year's Eve
      </a>
      , especially beneath a sprig of
      <a href="/wiki/Mistletoe" title="Mistletoe">
       mistletoe
      </a>
      . Newlyweds usually kiss at the end of a
      <a href="/wiki/Wedding" title="Wedding">
       wedding
      </a>
      ceremony.
     </p>

     <h2>

     <p>
      Within the natural world of animals there are numerous analogies, notes Crawley, such as "the billing of birds, the cataglottism of pigeons and the antennal play of some insects." Even among higher animals such as the dog, cat and bear, similar behavior is noted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawley_22-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Crawley-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :114
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">
       Anthropologists
      </a>
      have not reached a conclusion as to whether kissing is learned or a behavior from instinct. It may be related to
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_grooming" title="Personal grooming">
       grooming
      </a>
      behavior also seen between other animals, or arising as a result of mothers
      <a href="/wiki/Premastication" title="Premastication">
       premasticating
      </a>
      food for their children. Non-human
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">
       primates
      </a>
      also exhibit kissing behavior.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">
       Dogs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">
       cats
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">
       birds
      </a>
      and other animals display
      <a href="/wiki/Licking" title="Licking">
       licking
      </a>
      , nuzzling, and
      <a href="/wiki/Social_grooming" title="Social grooming">
       grooming
      </a>
      behavior among themselves, but also towards humans or other species. This is sometimes interpreted by observers as a type of kissing.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kissing in humans is postulated to have evolved from the direct mouth-to-mouth regurgitation of food (kiss-feeding) from parent to offspring or male to female (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Courtship_feeding" title="Courtship feeding">
       courtship feeding
      </a>
      ) and has been observed in numerous mammals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EE_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EE-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The similarity in the methods between kiss-feeding and deep human kisses (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/French_kiss" title="French kiss">
       French kiss
      </a>
      ) are quite pronounced; in the former, the tongue is used to push food from the mouth of the mother to the child with the child receiving both the mother's food and tongue in sucking movements, and the latter is the same but forgoes the premasticated food. In fact, through observations across various species and cultures, it can be confirmed that the act of kissing and premastication has most likely evolved from the similar relationship-based feeding behaviours.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EE_55-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EE-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EEChapt_56-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EEChapt-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Kissing is a complex behavior that requires significant muscular
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_(physiology)" title="Coordination (physiology)">
       coordination
      </a>
      involving a total of 34facial muscles and 112postural muscles.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent-kiss_57-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-independent-kiss-57">
        <span>

        </span>
        57
        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>

       <a href="#cite_note-telegraph-kiss-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Orbicularis_oris_muscle" title="Orbicularis oris muscle">
       orbicularis oris muscle
      </a>
      , which is used to pucker the
      <a href="/wiki/Lip" title="Lip">
       lips
      </a>
      and informally known as the
      <i>
       kissing muscle
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        </span>

      </sup>
      In the case of the
      <a href="/wiki/French_kiss" title="French kiss">
       French kiss

      , the tongue is also an important component. Lips have many nerve endings which make them sensitive to touch and bite.

        <span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Health_benefits">
       Health benefits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Health benefits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Affection in general has
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(biological)" title="Stress (biological)">
       stress
      </a>
      -reducing effects. Kissing in particular has been studied in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controlled_experiment" title="Controlled experiment">
       controlled experiment
      </a>
      and it was found that increasing the frequency of kissing in marital and cohabiting relationships results in a reduction of perceived stress, an increase in relationship satisfaction, and a lowering of
      <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">
       cholesterol
      </a>
      levels.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Kissing can also cause the adrenal glands to secrete
      <a href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">
       epinephrine
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">
       norepinephrine
      </a>
      (adrenaline and noradrenaline) into the blood, thereby causing an adrenaline rush, which has a beneficial impact on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiovascular" title="Cardiovascular">
       cardiovascular
      </a>
      system because the heart pumps faster. In an experiment by Dr. Alexander DeWees, a passionate kiss generally burns up to 23 calories per minute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Disease_transmission">
       Disease transmission
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Disease transmission">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Kissing on the lips can result in the transmission of some diseases, including
      <a href="/wiki/Infectious_mononucleosis" title="Infectious mononucleosis">
       infectious mononucleosis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex" title="Herpes simplex">
       herpes simplex
      </a>
      when the infectious viruses are present in saliva. Research indicates that contraction of
      <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">
       HIV
      </a>
      via kissing is extremely unlikely, although there was a documented case in 1997 of an HIV infection by kissing. Both the woman and infected man had
      <a href="/wiki/Gingivitis" title="Gingivitis">
       gum disease
      </a>
      , so transmission was through the man's blood, not through saliva.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
