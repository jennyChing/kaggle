            <h2 datatype="" property="dc:title">
             Capturing Daylight On A Memorable October Dawn
            </h2>
           </header>
           <div class="content">
            <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
             <div class="field-items">
              <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
               <p>
                <em>
                 Following his article on
                 <a href="http://www.thedaylightproject.co.uk/content/8-tips-using-daylight-your-photography">
                  8 ways to make the most of daylight in your photography
                 </a>
                 ,Baxter Bradford shares the story behind one of his beautiful images Dawn on the Beaulieu River.
                </em>
               </p>
               <p>
                Id like to talk through the process and decisions I made to produce this picture from a memorable October dawn. The image as shown looks exactly like the original 5x4 inch Fuji Velvia transparency  this is a large format colour transparency film, and what you see was done using the camera, rather than created on a computer.
               </p>
               <p>
                I now use a digital camera, so have no axe to grind about digital photography! I just feel that choosing such an image is more helpful for examining the use of daylight.
               </p>
               <p>
                Everything looked serene upstream, where there were moored boats. As it was obvious that colour was going to develop in the sky, I concentrated on looking into the light from that direction. I opted for a landscape orientation not only to give more width but also, vitally, to exclude the less interesting part of the sky above the frame.
               </p>
               <p>
                Before working out how to control the contrast and light levels, I had to set the shot up.
               </p>
               <p>
                Strange as it might seem, the whole image is based on the oak leaf bottom left, on the diagonal wood! There needed to be space between the end of this spar and the edge of the frame to make it a bold compositional statement, and the leaf prevents it creating a triangle cutting off the corner of the image.
               </p>
               <p>
                On the right-hand side, it looked better having the bulge in the river away from the frame edge instead of tangential to it. Mud reflects light beautifully and easily accepts colour from borrowed sources  as you can see in this case, from the sky.
               </p>
               <p>
                I was using a fixed focal length lens, meaning that there was no zoom function on the lens, and I had to move the tripod myself in order to frame the image. To ensure the foreground posts were well separated from the slipway ahead of them in the final picture, I chose to adjust the tripod height to eye level.
               </p>
               <p>
                Having framed the shot, I had to work out how to control the contrast. The brightness range of the sky far exceeded the capability of the film to capture it. To control the levels of light to help achieve an image that is closer to how it appears in real life, I use what are called graduated neutral density (ND grad) filters. These are rectangular pieces of resin, one half is clear, the other looks grey.
               </p>
               <p>
                By placing the filter in a slotted holder attached to the front of the lens, the filter is slid down so that bright areas such as skies are darkened by the grey portion. Care has to be taken to position carefully to prevent white or dark bands. The filters come in different strengths and hardness of transition from grey to clear.
               </p>
               <p>
                To balance the sky and river with the oak leaf, my light meter showed I had to use what is called a 3 stop hard ND grad filter which permits only a fraction of the available light to pass through from the sky. The transition to the clear part of the filter (3 stop soft ND grad) happens at the top of the treeline and goes down through the image to even out the light fall-off, so the foreground posts are in the clear part of the filter. The combination of these two filters permitted just 1/64 of the light in the sky and 1/8 on the water to pass through, so that the picture appears natural.
               </p>
               <p>
                As you can see, enhancing daylight in photography neednt rely solely on digital manipulation  if you have the right tools and use the right techniques, you can achieve such images without the aid of computers!
               </p>
               <p>
                To learn more about effectively capturing daylight in your photos, have a read of
                <a href="http://www.thedaylightproject.co.uk/content/8-tips-using-daylight-your-photography">
                 Baxters 8 ways to make the most of natural light when taking photos outdoors
                </a>
                .
               </p>
