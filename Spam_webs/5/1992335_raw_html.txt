     <p>
      <b>
       Molokan
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      for "milk-drinkers") is a Slavonic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exonym" title="Exonym">
       exonym
      </a>
      for members of different
      <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_Christianity" title="Spiritual Christianity">
       Spiritual Christian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Sect" title="Sect">
       sects
      </a>
      that evolved from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Christian" title="Eastern Christian">
       Eastern Christians
      </a>
      in the lands "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Of_all_Rus" title="Of all Rus">
       of all Rus
      </a>
      '" whose traditions (especially regulated dairy consumption during Christian fasts) did not conform to those of the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">
       Russian Orthodox Church
      </a>
      . Since they are regarded as neither
      <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christianity" title="Orthodox Christianity">
       Orthodox
      </a>
      , nor
      <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">
       Catholic
      </a>
      nor
      <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">
       Protestant
      </a>
      , they tend to call themselves "
      <b>
       Spiritual Christians
      </b>
      ".
     </p>
     <p>
      Unlike the conformist Protestant "reformists" of Western Europe, Molokans rejected conformity. There are almost as many different ways among Molokans as there are Molokans some building chapels, keeping sacraments, revering saints and icons, while others (like Iconobortsi) not at all. In general, they are scripturists who rejected the institutionalized formalism of Orthodoxy and those with similar doctrines in favor of more Primitive Christianity placing great emphasis on spirituality and spiritual practice while physical observances (such as water baptism) have been permitted only as tangible signs and symbols of more important spiritual truths. They are comparable to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">
       Presbyterians
      </a>
      in that they have a council of dominant
      <a href="/wiki/Elder_(Christianity)" title="Elder (Christianity)">
       elders
      </a>
      who preserve an albeit controversial sort of
      <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">
       apostolic succession
      </a>
      . Though Molokans are somewhat similar to the European
      <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">
       Quakers
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mennonites" title="Mennonites">
       Mennonites
      </a>
      for their
      <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">
       pacifism
      </a>
      , communal organization, spiritual meetings, and sub-groupings their genesis is tied to that of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doukhobors" title="Doukhobors">
       Doukhobors
      </a>
      and Sabbatarians (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subbotniki" title="Subbotniki">
       Subbotniks
      </a>
      ) having evolved alongside Russia's similar Spiritual Christian movements of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Duhovnye_Kristyanye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Duhovnye Kristyanye (page does not exist)">
       Duhovnye Kristyanye
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ikonobortsy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ikonobortsy (page does not exist)">
       Ikonobortsy
      </a>
      (icon-wrestlers), migrating at around the same time.
     </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are approximately 200
      <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">
       fasting
      </a>
      days especially the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Fast" title="Great Fast">
       Great Fast
      </a>
      (Lent) when drinking milk was prohibited by Orthodox ecclesiastical authorities. Milk-drinking practice during these fasts was first sanctioned by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nestorian_Church" title="Nestorian Church">
       Nestorian Church
      </a>
      in the 11th century in order to accommodate the conversion of some 200,000
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerait" title="Kerait">
       Karait
      </a>
      -
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tatar" title="Tatar">
       Tatar
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastoralists" title="Pastoralists">
       pastoralists
      </a>
      , who lived on meat and milk, to Nestorian Christianity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The practice was adopted by unaffiliated Armenian Christians, also pastoralizing the Eurasian plains (particularly from the
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Moldau" title="Moldau">
       Moldau
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mordova" title="Mordova">
       Mordova
      </a>
      and the Kuban-Dagestan-Baku regions), out of whom the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulicians" title="Paulicians">
       Paulicians
      </a>
      and subsequent "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bogomils" title="Bogomils">
       Bogomils
      </a>
      " of
      <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">
       Thrace
      </a>
      , Macedonia, Bulgaria, Bosnia and Serbia had already developed. Turkic Molokan Pastors still use the term for an Armenian Catholic Priest (
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
       Russian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
       
      </span>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
       Ukrainian
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="uk" xml:lang="uk">
       
      </span>
      ). Arriving in
      <a href="/wiki/All-Russian_nation#Background" title="All-Russian nation">
       the Rus' lands
      </a>
      with the 13th century Tatar invasion
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Batu_Khan" title="Batu Khan">
       Batu
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ngke_Khan" title="Mngke Khan">
       Mngke
      </a>
      , Karait-Tatar practices had reached as far as Lithuania by the end of the 1300s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>

        <span>

        </span>

     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="15th_to_16th_Centuries">
       15th to 16th Centuries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 15th to 16th Centuries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Bible-based Hebraic tradition, promoted by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zacharias_de_Ghisolfi" title="Zacharias de Ghisolfi">
       Zacharias de Ghisolfi
      </a>
      , captivated the court of Ivan the Terrible's Grandfather
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_III" title="Ivan III">
       Ivan III
      </a>
      but was soon labelled heretical and its supporters suppressed as
      <a href="/wiki/Judaizers" title="Judaizers">
       Judaizers
      </a>
      by the Russian Orthodox Church. Nevertheless, a
      <a href="/wiki/Sola_scriptura" title="Sola scriptura">
       sola scriptura
      </a>
      tendency survived. After the schism of 1552 during the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Ivan_the_Terrible" title="Ivan the Terrible">
       Ivan the Terrible
      </a>
      (15471584),
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Matvei_Dalmatov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Matvei Dalmatov (page does not exist)">
       Matvei Simyonich Dalmatov (  )
      </a>
      , the first recorded martyr of Russia's Molokans, led local villagers in and around the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Tambov" title="Tambov">
       Tambov
      </a>
      into Molokan Evangelism. Dalmatov carried this sectarian belief into
      <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">
       Moscow
      </a>
      , where a group of
      <a href="/wiki/Mordvins" title="Mordvins">
       Mordvins
      </a>
      heard his message and embraced it. Dalmatov was later martyred by Orthodox priests in a monastery prison by
      <a href="/wiki/Breaking_wheel" title="Breaking wheel">
       wheeling
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="17th_to_18th_Centuries">
       17th to 18th Centuries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 17th to 18th Centuries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Molokans were ostracized from Russian society in the 17th century for their refusal to bear arms and for their refusal to assist in any form of military service. The first recorded use of the term "Molokan" appears in the 1670s, in reference to the people who differed concerning the 200 fasting days by drinking
      <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">
       milk
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       moloko
      </i>
      means "milk" in Russian). Molokans themselves did not completely reject the nameeven adding words like "drinking of the spiritual milk of
      <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
       God
      </a>
      " (according to I Peter 2:2, "Like newborn babies, crave pure spiritual milk, so that by it you may grow up in your salvation").
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 18th century, the Dukhobors who neglected the Bible by believing that God had placed the Word directly into their hearts split from the Molokans of Tambov's Semen Matveev Uklein (1733-1809) who held the Bible in the highest regard. Semen's Molokans from Tambov energetically proselytized along the Volga River and Russia's south-eastern frontier spreading the Molokan faith in Orenburg, Saratov and Astrakhan provinces organizing congregations until his death in 1809.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="19th_to_20th_Centuries">
       19th to 20th Centuries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 19th to 20th Centuries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From the intervention of Count
      <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Zubov" title="Nikolay Zubov">
       Nikolay Zubov
      </a>
      in 1795 Molokans () were tolerated under Tsarina Catherine but with strict rules imposed upon them intended to curb community growth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Those who ignored the restrictions were punished in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" title="Imperial Russia">
       Tsarist Russia
      </a>
      as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_heresy" title="Christian heresy">
       heretics
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Molokan evangelists and missionaries suffered imprisonment, banishment and other forms of punishment. Prohibited from winning converts
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the Molokans were forced into endogamy. The government's policy was to send the Molokans away from the center of the country into Caucasus (1833), especially
      <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">
       Armenia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">
       Azerbaijan
      </a>
      (1834),
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      (1830s), central
      <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
       Asia
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">
       Siberia
      </a>
      where many communities have survived into the present.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Molokans_vs_Jumpers">
       Molokans vs Jumpers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Molokans vs Jumpers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The sectarians in south Ukraine were influenced by neighboring German Protestants, particularly Heufers (Springers, Jumpers). A similar movement began among a number of Molokans exiled in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcaucasus" title="Transcaucasus">
       Transcaucasus
      </a>
      region in 1833. This created a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">
       schism
      </a>
      between regular Molokans termed
      <i>
       Constants
      </i>
      (Postoyaniye) and the newly evolved
      <i>
       Pryguni
      </i>
      (Jumping) or
      <i>
       Skakuni
      </i>
      (Leaping) also called
      <i>
       Shalaputy
      </i>
      sect. With what the
      <i>
       Jumpers
      </i>
      believed to be a manifestation of the Holy Spirit, this new smaller sect began a revival with intense zeal and reported miracles that purportedly rivaled those of Christ's Apostles.
     </p>
     <p>
      Condemnation from the constant Molokan sect led to betrayals and imprisonment for many of the Jumpers and Leapers, who called themselves "New Israelites" under their anointed leader
      <a href="/wiki/Maxim_Rudometkin" title="Maxim Rudometkin">
       Maxim Rudometkin
      </a>
      witnessed by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Efim_G._Klubnikin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Efim G. Klubnikin (page does not exist)">
       Efim G. Klubnikin
      </a>
      , a divinely inspired 12-year-old boy prophet, which prophesied about a "coming time that would be unbearable and that the time to leave Russia was now". He wrote that "soon the doors will close and leaving Russia would be impossible" which he later wrote in his memoirs in his elder years. During the early 20th century under his fellowship, about 2,000 Jumpers left for the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      and settled in the
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
       Los Angeles
      </a>
      area near the area of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boyle_Heights" title="Boyle Heights">
       Boyle Heights
      </a>
      , and some other parts of the West Coast and Canada. It is there that they influenced in practice and doctrine a later American Revival called "the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentecostal" title="Pentecostal">
       Pentecostal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Azusa_Street_Revival" title="Azusa Street Revival">
       Azusa Street Revival
      </a>
      " near the beginning of the 20th century. The founder of
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Full_Gospel_Business_Men%27s_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Full Gospel Business Men's Association (page does not exist)">
       The Full Gospel Business Men's Association
      </a>
      associates this
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentecostal" title="Pentecostal">
       Pentecostal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_revival" title="Christian revival">
       Revival
      </a>
      to the child prophet of the Jumpers. When they arrived in Los Angeles, they were befriended by local
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Settlement_house" title="Settlement house">
       settlement house
      </a>
      director
      <a href="/wiki/Dana_W._Bartlett" title="Dana W. Bartlett">
       Dana W. Bartlett
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Klubnikin's jumpers continued to be involved in cattle and groceries, as they probably had done in the area of Tambov prior to exile. Other Jumpers received a land grant from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_government" title="Mexican government">
       Mexican government
      </a>
      and settled in the
      <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe,_Baja_California" title="Guadalupe, Baja California">
       Guadalupe Valley
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">
       Baja California
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      At the end of the 19th century, there were about 500,000 Molokans within the Russia empire. Around 1825, there was a well-known colony of Molokans that had been exiled to the
      <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">
       Caucasus
      </a>
      (an area long within Russian hegemony), mainly to what is now
      <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">

Approximately 25,000 Molokans reside in the United States, of which only about 5,000 "ethnically" identify themselves as Molokans; most of whom reside in

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Molokans adhere to the Old Testament laws but in the spirit of the Christian
      <a href="/wiki/New_Covenant" title="New Covenant">
       New Covenant
      </a>
      . They perform morning and evening worship as symbols of the morning and evening offerings prescribed in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentateuch" title="Pentateuch">
       Pentateuch
      </a>
      . They do not eat pork, shellfish, or other "unclean" foods on holy days. They keep Sabbaths as well as the first Sunday of every Month as a Biblical
      <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Chodesh" title="Rosh Chodesh">
       Rosh Chodesh
      </a>
      and some refuse to serve on juries or file lawsuits against fellow church members. Church services led by a
      <a href="/wiki/Lector" title="Lector">
       Lector
      </a>
      are conducted predominantly in the local language, but still commonly
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Old_Slavonic" title="Old Slavonic">
       Old Slavonic
      </a>
      , men and women sit apart, and services are usually quite active. They have been using the liturgy of Trakai at least since the 1890s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Like Muslims, Molokans have clerics but no clerical hierachy, they must be pure of pork and alcohol for worship, they respect but do not worship saints, nor worship symbols like icons or crosses. Proselytism is encouraged, as children in Molokan families are encouraged not to marry outside of the faith.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_Days">
       Holy Days
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Holy Days">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Molokan year is a system of weeks based around the Julian Calendar the use of which by 1st century Hellenists and Romans in Palestine was never spoken against by Jesus when he lived there. Some Molokan congregations have adopted the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregorian_Calendar" title="Gregorian Calendar">
       international civil calendar
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Christmastide">
       <a href="/wiki/Christmastide" title="Christmastide">
        Christmastide
       </a>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Christmastide">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      25th of December to 5th January, a 12-day celebration marking the visitation of the Magi and completion of another year of Christ's sovereignty as one would celebrate a King's official birthday.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Epiphany">
       <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">
        Epiphany
       </a>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Julian Calendar 6th January (currently January 19th) is the celebration of Christ's Baptism before the last three days of fasting in the wilderness where he would be tempted by Satan.
     </p>

     <p>
      Also known as
      <a href="/wiki/Maslenitsa" title="Maslenitsa">
       Maslenitsa
      </a>
      . The week before Julian Lent when Molokans use up all their cheese which Molokans substitute as a symbolic firstfruit not to be consumed in Lent although they do milk.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">

     </p>

     <p>
      Also called emigration fast. A 30-day fast in commemoration of a fast the Apostles took on in preparation for leaving Jerusalem to to take the Gospel around the known world. A popular time for visiting the graves. Restrictions are tightened for the last 10 days but most solemnly for the last 3 days.
     </p>

     <p>
      Julian Calendar 10th July (currently July 23rd) is a Feast Day unique to Molokans. Molokans have a roast to mark the end of the Apostle's Fast period. Somewhat similar in spirit to Orthodox St. Peter's Day (Julian Calendar June 29th) with which it is normally conflated.
     </p>

     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_in_the_United_States">
       Naming in the United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Molokan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Naming in the United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Molokans are known for having different spellings of last names within the same immediate family.
     </p>
     <p>
      Molokans are also known for having "first names" that aren't their legal names. Many common "first names" are actually based on nicknames from childhood within the church that stuck. These are not legal names, and as such become names that make tracing family history very difficult. For example "Hazel Valov" became known as "Percy Valov", for being very "persistent".
     </p>
     <p>
      This naming process can help with family trees, but because so many relatives have similar names, and many nicknames aren't legal names, it can be complex.
     </p>
