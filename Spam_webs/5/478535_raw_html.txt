       <div class="left-content col-lg-8 col-md-8 col-sm-12 col-xs-12" id="fallback-sidebar">
        <p>
         <a href="http://factmag-images.s3.amazonaws.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/factmix-kingbritt-3.25.2012.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-69344" height="400" src="http://factmag-images.s3.amazonaws.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/03/factmix-kingbritt-3.25.2012.jpg" title="factmix-kingbritt-3.25.2012" width="400"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          FACT mix 322 is by
          <a href="http://www.kingbritt.com" target="_blank">
           King Britt
          </a>
          in his
          <a href="http://kingbritt.com/tag/fhloston-paradigm/" target="_blank">
           Fhloston Paradigm
          </a>
          guise.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The
         <em>
          King Britt Presents Fhloston Paradigm
         </em>
         EP is released on Hyperdub this week.  This project  which has evolved across three digital-only releases,
         <em>
          Charlie Sleeps
         </em>
         EP (2009),
         <em>
          Fiction Science
         </em>
         (2011) and
         <em>
          After The Rave
         </em>
         (2011)  is an outlet for Britt to explore his fascination with the  science fiction movies and TV series of his youth, creating imaginary  soundtracks on entirely analogue gear  mainly Roland DR110, Korg Mono/Poly,  Kork MS20, Roland JX3P, Moog Sonic 6 and SQ1 sequencer.
        </p>
        <p>
         This process forces me to think differently, Britt recently told FACT,  much like when I started out in the 80s, about arrangement and rhythm.  The sound is also all analogue which is what was used in all the  science fiction films. So naturally the music leans toward that vibe.
        </p>
        <p>
         I mean as kids, we all loved sci-fi.
         <em>
          Star Trek, Flash Gordon
         </em>
         ,
         <em>
          Ultraman
         </em>
         ,
         <em>
          Johnny Socko
         </em>
         and then
         <em>
          Star Wars c
         </em>
         hanged the game. So its in our  psyche, our DNA. When I play a Moog or any analog keyboard, I think  automatically of those movies. Its just in my genes!
        </p>
        <p>
         Philadelphia native Britt first troubled the world of electronic music in 1987, when he worked as singles/import buyer at his local Tower Records. While at Temple University he honed his DJ skills, taking up residencies at Phillys Silky City and Revival in 1990, and also began producing music with his friend Josh Wink, leading to the release of Tribal Confusion on
         <strong>
          Strictly Rhythm
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Britt toured with hip-hop collective
         <strong>
          Digable Planets
         </strong>
         , before co-founding  with Wink 
         <strong>
          Ovum Recordings
         </strong>
         in 94. He quit Ovum in 2001, but remains responsible for some of its most important signings and releases, including a hit collaboration with Ursula Rucker, Supernatural, and some classic deep house-leaning 12s under his
         <strong>
          Scuba
         </strong>
         alias. Adopting the name
         <strong>
          Sylk130
         </strong>
         , Britt created some of the most popular music of his career in the shape of neo-soul LP
         <em>
          When The Funk Hits The Fan
         </em>
         (1998), and was able to call upon such guests as Martin Fry, Alison Moyet, Grover Washington, Jr. and Kathy Sledge for the 2001 follow-up, Remembers Only.
         <br/>
         <strong>
          <br/>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <h3>
         <span style="color: #008080;">
          <strong>
           Sci-fi is in our  psyche, our DNA.
          </strong>
         </span>
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <br/>
         </strong>
         <br/>
         The spirit of collaboration persisted in
         <em>
          Adventures In Lo Fi
         </em>
         , which featured Madlib (as Quasimoto), De La Soul and more, and then Britt  always looking to reinvent himself while holding tightly to his roots  reworked a collection of a cappella voice and tambourine recordings made in the 1970s by Georgian preacher Sister Gertrude Morgan  the album,
         <em>
          King Britt Presents: Sister Gertrude Morgan
         </em>
         , was a great success, and sections of it have since been used in Michael Manns
         <em>
          Miami Vice
         </em>
         and HBOs
         <em>
          True Blood
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Recent years have showed no slowing of activity from the mind and studio of King Britt; quite the opposite. Hes reconnected with his love of techno with the
         <strong>
          Nova Dream Sequence
         </strong>
         and
         <strong>
          Dynamic
         </strong>
         projects, releasing through Compost and Carl Craigs Planet E, and teamed up with multimedia artist and singer Rucyl to develop the Sun Ra-influenced improvisational performance group
         <strong>
          Saturn Never Sleeps
         </strong>
         . Which brings us to Fhloston Paradigm.
        </p>
        <p>
         Kode9  who has for some time been working various Britt productions into his DJ sets  has really picked a winner with this 12. Its three gripping tracks cover techno of a truly epic sweep, Detroit-style beatdown and kosmische synth experimentation, and Britts intuitive command of rhythm mechanics, harmonic arrangement and solid gold hooks is in thrilling evidence throughout. He makes it all sound so simple, but the DNA of this music is incredibly complex.
        </p>
        <p>
         The kaleidoscopic FACT mix 322 helps a little to map out that DNA. As well as a generous helping of Fhloston Paradigms own productions  including the marvellous Chase from the Hyperdub EP  there are excerpts from the
         <em>
          Logans Run
         </em>
         and
         <em>
          Blade Runner
         </em>
         soundtracks, gravely romantic 80s balladry from David Sylvian and Eurhythmics (the show-stopping Take Me To Your Heart), Alice Coltrane-style astral jazz from Low Leaf, soaring techno from Galaxy 2 Galaxy (Underground Resistance), precious ambience from Ryuichi Sakamoto and Alva Noto, as well as plenty of unexpected surprises  like Tenkos bewitching, Fred Frith and Arto Lindsay-featuring Gradual Disappearance. Its all moulded together with an auteurs refined sense of narrative and atmosphere, and we suspect that this is going to be one of those FACT mixes  like King Midas Sounds or The Soft Moons  that becomes a permanent fixture in our lives.
