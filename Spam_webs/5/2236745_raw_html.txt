     <p>
      The moth
      <i>
       <b>
        Megalopyge opercularis
       </b>
      </i>
      has numerous common names, including
      <b>
       southern flannel moth
      </b>
      for its adult form, and
      <b>
       puss moth
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       puss caterpillar
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       tree asp
      </b>
      , and the
      <b>
       asp caterpillar
      </b>
      for its larval form.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalopyge_opercularis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       M. opercularis
      </i>
      is visually striking in both
      <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">
       larval
      </a>
      and adult forms.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Larva">
       Larva
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalopyge_opercularis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Larva">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The inch-long larva is generously coated in long, luxuriant hair-like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Setae" title="Setae">
       setae
      </a>
      , making it resemble a tiny
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_(cat)" title="Persian (cat)">
       Persian cat
      </a>
      , the characteristic that presumably gave it the name "puss." It is variable in color, from downy grayish-white to golden-brown to dark charcoal gray. It often has a streak of bright orange running longitudinally. The 'fur' on early-stage larvae is sometimes extremely curly, giving the larva a cottony, puffed-up look. The body tapers to a tail that extends well beyond the body, unlike its relative
      <i>
       M. crispata
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The middle
      <a href="/wiki/Instar" title="Instar">
       instar
      </a>
      has a more dishevelled, 'bad-hair-day' appearance, without a distinctive tail.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 'fur' of the larva contains
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venom_(poison)" title="Venom (poison)">
       venomous
      </a>
      spines that cause extremely painful reactions in human skin upon contact. The reactions are sometimes localized to the affected area but are often very severe, radiating up a limb and causing burning, swelling, nausea, headache, abdominal distress, rashes, blisters, and sometimes chest pain, numbness, or difficulty breathing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eagle_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eagle-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, it is not unusual to find sweating from the welts or hives at the site of the sting. Ironically, the resemblance of the larvae to soft, colorful cotton balls encourages people to pick them up and pet them.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adult">
       Adult
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalopyge_opercularis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Adult">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The adult moth is covered in long fur in colors ranging from dull orange to lemon yellow, with hairy legs and fuzzy black feet.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">
       Habitat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <p>
      <i>
       M. opercularis
      </i>
      can be found on
      <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">
       oaks
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Elm" title="Elm">
       elms
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">
       citrus
      </a>
      and other trees, and many garden plants such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">
       roses
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivy" title="Ivy">
       ivy
      </a>
      . It is distributed throughout the southern
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      , and parts of
      <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
       Central America
      </a>
      . The larva does not spin a real
      <a href="/wiki/Pupa#Cocoon" title="Pupa">
       cocoon
      </a>
      ; rather, it separates from its furry skin and uses it as a protective covering while it pupates.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers_and_treatment_of_stings">
       Dangers and treatment of stings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Megalopyge_opercularis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dangers and treatment of stings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Exposure to the caterpillar's fur-like spines will lead to an immediate skin irritation. The caterpillar is regarded as a dangerous insect because of its venomous spines. Medical advice may be sought in case of contact with one. It is best if the venom from the spines is treated within hours of first contact. For first aid, it is recommended that the spines (if present) be removed by using cellophane tape.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eagle_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eagle-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other remedies, which are reported to have varying degrees of success, include
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_pack" title="Ice pack">
       ice packs
      </a>
      , oral
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihistamine" title="Antihistamine">
       antihistamine
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baking_soda" title="Baking soda">
       baking soda
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocortisone" title="Hydrocortisone">
       hydrocortisone
      </a>
      cream, juice from the stems of
      <a href="/wiki/Comfrey" title="Comfrey">
       comfrey
      </a>
      plants, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calamine_lotion" title="Calamine lotion">
       calamine lotion
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eagle_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Eagle-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rubbing wounds, or using
      <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">
       alcohol
      </a>
      , spirits,
      <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
       ammonia
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">
       urine
      </a>
      may have strongly negative effects as these can encourage the release of additional venom.
