    <h1>
     <i>
      Laganosuchus
     </i>
    </h1>
    <img border="" src="../../images/species/l/laganosuchus-size.jpg"/>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Name:
     </b>
     <i>
      Laganosuchus
     </i>
     (Pancake crocodile).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Phonetic:
     </b>
     La-gan-oh-soo-kuss.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Named By:
     </b>
     Paul Sereno &amp; Hans Larsson - 2009.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Classification:
     </b>
     Chordata, Reptilia,
Archosauromorpha, Crurotarsi, Crocodylomorpha, Stomatosuchidae.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Species:
     </b>
     <i>
      L. thaumastos (type), L.
maghrebensis
     </i>
     .
     <br/>
     <b>
      Type:
     </b>
     Carnivore/Piscivore.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Size:
     </b>
     Estimated 6 meters long.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Known locations:
     </b>
     Niger, Echkar Formation. Morocco,
Kem Kem Beds.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Time period:
     </b>
     Cenomanian of the Cretaceous.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Fossil representation:
     </b>
     Two specimens.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     <i>
      Laganosuchus
     </i>
     acquired its name from its flat 'pancake like' jaws. These had small
but numerous teeth running across the edges.
     <i>
      Laganosuchus
     </i>
     is thought to
have used these specialised jaws as a fish trap, laying in the water
with its jaws open for extended periods until a fish swam in. Once this
happened, the jaws would snap together preventing escape.
     <i>
      Laganosuchus
     </i>
     is one of many strange
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/prehistoric-crocodiles.html">
      prehistoric
crocodiles
     </a>
     discovered in the African
deserts, but
lost much of the limelight due to the nearby discovery of the 'Boar
croc'
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/k/kaprosuchus.html">
      <i>
       Kaprosuchus
      </i>
     </a>
     .
    </p>
    <br/>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Further reading
     </b>
     <br/>
     - Cretaceous crocodyliforms from the Sahara. - ZooKeys 28 (2009):
1143. - P. C. Sereno &amp; H. C. E. Larsson - 2009.
    </p>
