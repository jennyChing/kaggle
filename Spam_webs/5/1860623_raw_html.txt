           <h3>
            Third annual Prague event is part of a global tradition
           </h3>
           <p>
            No Pants Subway Ride is an international event held every January, when thousands of people in various cities ride in the metro without pants, or trousers as they are called in the UK. Underwear, called pants in the UK, is still pretty much required.
           </p>
           <div style="margin: 5px; display: block; float: right;">
            <div class="moduletable">
             <div class="custom">
              <p>
               <script id="mNCC" language="javascript" type="text/javascript">

              </p>
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="moduletable">
             <div class="custom">
              <p>
               <script type="text/javascript">

             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            The Prague event tales place Sunday, Jan. 12, at 2 p.m. at the Wenceslas Square (V
            <span lang="cs-CZ">
             clavsk nmst
            </span>
            <span lang="en-US">
             ) station, with people meeting behind the statue of St Wenceslas, or under the tail as people call it. People, however, are free to start at any station.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span lang="en-US">
             Some 450 have
             <a href="https://www.facebook.com/events/602874483112290/" target="_blank">
              promised to attend the event via Facebook.
             </a>
             The event in Prague already took place in 2012 and '13.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            <span lang="en-US">
             Videos on YouTube of both previous events show very little in the way of any reaction from other passengers in the metro.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p lang="en-US">
            In 2013, I stumbled upon No Pants through a Facebook event [listing] and immediately wanted to go. It was organized by a group of enthusiastic Czech students, so the people attending were mostly Czechs, videographer Anna Kakabadze told the
            <em>
             Prague Post.
            </em>
           </p>
           <p lang="en-US">
            As I video blog on a regular basis, I tend to look for subjects to film, so anything that can get smiles, ideally where people act out of norm, such as a flash-mob is a wonderful subject. The reason it's probably held around this time is because in Arctic temperatures, it's more surprising to see people without pants riding the Metro. Last year around this time it was freezing, she added.
           </p>
           <p lang="en-US">
            Only around 20 people out of the over 100 confirmed people, attended due to or despite that. I'm hoping that this year's turnout is going to be much bigger and more international. The confirmations to the event sound promising; the more people show up, the merrier, she said.
           </p>
           <p lang="en-US">
            I was a bit disappointed with last year's reaction shots. If people expressed anything they did it quietly or it was hardly noticeable but it did get a few smiles, so it was worth the effort. I can't wait to see what will happen this year, she said.
           </p>
           <p>

           <p>
            <span lang="en-US">
             The first No Pants Subway Ride was in 2002 in New York City, and was organized by a group called Improv Everywere. At that event, eight people were issued summonses for disorderly conduct although the charges were later dismissed. In 2013, some 60 cities participated, according to Wikipedia.
            </span>
           </p>
