       <h1 class="entry-title">
        No One is Holding Your Face in the Twitter Stream
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2011-03-29T13:01:39+00:00">
         29 March 2011
        </span>
        by
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://geeksdreamgirl.com/author/e/" rel="author">
           e
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="post-comments">
         <a href="http://geeksdreamgirl.com/2011/03/29/no-one-is-holding-your-face-in-the-twitter-stream/#comments">
          14 Comments
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignright size-full wp-image-5751" height="223" src="http://geeksdreamgirl.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/08/twitterfollowme.jpg" style="margin-top: 5px; margin-bottom: 5px; margin-left: 10px; margin-right: 10px;" title="twitterfollowme" width="300"/>
         Ive been noticing a trend on Twitter in the past few months that baffles me.
        </p>
        <p>
         It involves people bitching and whining about things theyre reading on Twitter.
        </p>
        <p>
         It involves people demanding that other people change the way they use Twitter.
        </p>
        <p>
         It involves people asking that other people change the topics they care about and the things they care to share.
        </p>
        <p>
         And I think its a load of bull.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Youre Doing It Wrong
        </h2>
        <p>
         If your social networking experience isnt fun, youre doing it wrong.
        </p>
        <p>
         Mike Shea (geek famous for
         <a href="http://slyflourish.com" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/slyflourish.com');" target="_blank" title="Sly Flourish - Excellent DMing advice here!">
          SlyFlourish.com
         </a>
         ), wrote an excellent post about this phenomenon in his personal blog, entitled
         <a href="http://mikeshea.net/You_Are_Responsible.html" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/mikeshea.net/You_Are_Responsible.html');" target="_blank" title="You Are Responsible">
          You Are Responsible
         </a>
         . He summarizes the post like this:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <em>
           Ultimately, each of us is responsible for dealing with the situations in which we find ourselves. We have a lot more control over our lives than we think. Its easy to assign responsibility for our problems to the rest of the world. We hate how people write email. We hate how they tweet. We hate that our boss is a micromanager. We hate that we dont have a single evening to ourselves when we have a family of six. It seems like these are external forces but the responsibility for dealing with them is completely up to us. Its our problem, not theirs. Analyzing our problems ultimately comes down to a single question:
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           What are you going to do about it?
          </em>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         If youre not enjoying Twitter, then YOU are the one responsible for doing something about it. And whining and complaining thats not doing anything about it.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Unfollow, Already!
        </h2>
        <p>
         There was a certain person that I followed back in the early days of my Twitter account. I wont specify whether they were a dating-related person or a geek-related person to protect their privacy.
        </p>
        <p>
         At first, this person was pleasant to follow. But as the months wore on, I got tired of watching them argue with other people I followed. So I unfollowed them.
        </p>
        <p>
         A few days later, they popped up in my Mentions. Hey! they said. I was trying to DM you. You unfollowed me! Im so hurt! (Not exact words, but the sentiment.) Feeling conflict-avoidant, I mumbled something about accidentally unfollowing them while tidying up my stream. I re-followed. They DMed me instantly with some juicy gossip, which reinforced why I unfollowed in the first place.
        </p>
        <p>
         A week passed and I was again becoming more and more irritated about the arguments this person was instigating with other people I followed. So I unfollowed again. I was pestered again.
        </p>
        <p>
         Lather, rinse, repeat until FINALLY this person and I ended up in a fight because I was tired of watching them abuse another person I followed. This person was standing up for their own interests just fine and didnt *need* me to jump in, but I was so irritated that I blew up. The anger ruined my day. I unfollowed for the final time.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Moral of the Story:
          </strong>
          If I had stuck to my guns and told the person the first time that I could not follow them because of the way they treated other people I followed, I would have saved myself all that frustration. My social networking experience would have been more fun.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <h2>
         What Paul &amp; Storm Taught Me About Global Filter
        </h2>
        <p>
         I love
         <a href="http://www.paulandstorm.com/" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/www.paulandstorm.com/');" target="_blank" title="Paul &amp; Storm">
          Paul &amp; Storm
         </a>
         . For those who are like, Who? allow me to introduce you. Paul &amp; Storm are a geek music phenomenon. I was first introduced to them as the opening act for my other favorite geek musician,
         <a href="http://jonathancoulton.com" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/jonathancoulton.com');" target="_blank" title="Jonathan Coulton">
          Jonathan Coulton
         </a>
         . Theyre hard to describe, so Ill let their music speak for them.
         <a href="http://www.paulandstorm.com/songs/" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/www.paulandstorm.com/songs/');" target="_blank" title="Songs by Paul &amp; Storm">
          Click here
         </a>
         and listen to Opening Band, The Captains Wifes Lament, Frogger: The Frogger Musical, and Nun Fight. These are my favorites, but Im sure youll discover some of your own.
        </p>
        <p>
         Back to my soapbox!
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://twitter.com/paulandstorm" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/twitter.com/paulandstorm');" target="_blank">
          Paul &amp; Storm have a Twitter account
         </a>
         , which I follow because they are goddamn hilarious. However, they have a tendency to start hashtag memes on a near daily basis. Sometimes, I love the hashtag memes and ROFL in my cubicle as I read them. Other times, they irk me and I want nothing more than for them to GO AWAY.
        </p>
        <p>
         Do I bitch at @paulandstorm and say, DUDES! I AM SO TIRED OF YOUR HASHTAG NONSENSE! YOU SHOULD STOP TWEETING THAT STUFF.?
        </p>
        <p>
         No.
        </p>
        <p>
         I go into
         <a href="http://tweetdeck.com" onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/tweetdeck.com');" target="_blank" title="Tweetdeck">
          Tweetdecks
         </a>
         Global Filter, and I add the hashtag to the list. Suddenly, my stream is completely free of the meme and I can go on with my day, blissfully unawares.
        </p>
        <p>
         This method also works great if youre totally over hearing about a certain subject thats trending in your stream. Say. oh, dickwolves.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Moral of the Story:
          </strong>
          Unless youre the type of person who enjoys being riled up and annoyed, theres no reason to get that way. Use your global filters (and if you dont use a service that allows it, get one).Your social networkingexperience will be more fun.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <h2>
         Know When to Walk Away, Know When to Run
        </h2>
        <p>
         Sometimes youre having a Very Bad Day.
        </p>
        <p>
         Sometimes your normally awesome friends are having a Very Bad Day.
        </p>
        <p>
         If this happens,
         <strong>
          you can close your Twitter app
         </strong>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         No one is going to unfollow you for missing a day of tweeting. Youre not going to lose hundreds of dollars of income from your small business. In fact, your absence will mostly go unnoticed in the grand scheme of things.
        </p>
        <p>
         What
         <strong>
          will
         </strong>
         get noticed is if you blow up and throw a hissy fit because youre having a horrible day. Remember Thumpers mom from Bambi? 
         <em>
          If you dont have something nice to say, dont say nothing at all.
         </em>
          Grammatically incorrect, yes. But the sentiment is right on.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Moral of the Story:
          </strong>
          Stepping away from Twitter for a day is sometimes the best option for days when social media frustrates you. Yes, youll miss it, but in the end, youll have prevented yourself from anger, frustration, and possibly losing the respect of your followers. In the end, your social networkingexperience will be more fun.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <h2>
         Before You Comment
        </h2>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Im not saying that the best way to deal with offensive people is to ignore them.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         By all means, if something bothers you, you have the right to speak up and let the offending person know that they are being offensive. If theres someone being sexist/racist/____-ist in your stream, let them know that you are
         <em>
          unfollowing them
         </em>
         because of their ____-ist tweets.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          I am saying that you shouldnt keep obnoxious people in your stream.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         After youve let them know why youre unfollowing, drop the topic. Block the person.
        </p>
        <p>
         You know why? Because you are not going to be able to change their worldview in 140 characters. If more of their followers tweet them with the same unfollowing message, maybe then theyll think about it. But save yourself the arguing. Save yourself the headaches. Send your one tweet, block, and move on.
        </p>
        <h2>
         How Awesome is Your Stream?
        </h2>
        <p>
         Is your Twitter stream full of people who make you laugh?
        </p>
        <p>
         Is your Twitter stream full of people who share awesome stories?
        </p>
        <p>
         Is your Twitter stream full of the breaking news that is relevant to your interests?
        </p>
        <p>
         If it isnt, then youre doing it wrong. And you have the power to fix it. Your Twitter stream should be a float down the Lazy River, not a swirly in a middle school toilet bowl. Freshen up your stream today and well all benefit from a brighter, happier you.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
