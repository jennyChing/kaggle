     <div class="articleBody" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="The Derby wharf precinct, about 2 kilometres from town was a popular hangout spot for salties." src="/content/dam/images/2/w/0/q/e/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.2vz61.png/1382567879957.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        The Derby wharf precinct, about 2 kilometres from town was a popular hangout spot for salties.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Jeremy Rooke - crocstalker.com.au
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       Derby West Kimberley Shire president Paul White fondly remembers swimming near the Derby wharf as a child in an effort to ward off the heat. These days, he wouldn't dare.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since being made a protected species four decades ago, many locals in Western Australia's north have noticed more crocodiles making their way further into towns.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the evidence is anecdotal, residents in these areas say now is the time to consider crocodile numbers and safety implications.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Crocodile stalking around the pontoon at Wyndham Port." src="/content/dam/images/2/w/0/u/g/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.2vz61.png/1382581004437.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Crocodile stalking around the pontoon at Wyndham Port.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Jeremy Rooke - crocstalker.com.au
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       Despite annual monitoring of saltwater crocodile numbers being carried out in Cambridge Gulf region, in WA's north, since 1989, it is not known how many exist. The last wide-scale count of adult crocodiles was completed in 1986 which estimated there to be about 2500 saltwater crocodiles, not including hatchlings. This was restricted to the Kimberley region. Crocodiles are also also
       <a href="http://www.watoday.com.au/wa-news/saltwater-crocodile-stalks-port-hedland-20101231-19bm3.html">
        present in the Pilbara
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       Saltwater crocodiles became protected in WA in 1970 after unregulated hunting had caused a rapid decline in their numbers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mr White said while Derby was surrounded by marshland and tidal creeks - the perfect habitat for saltwater crocodiles - he said they never used to be so near to town.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Crocodiles along the Ord River at low tide." src="/content/dam/images/2/w/0/u/d/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.2vz61.png/1382567879957.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Crocodiles along the Ord River at low tide.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Jeremy Rooke - crocstalker.com.au
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       He said numbers had "definitely" increased since becoming protected.
      </p>
      <p>
       "That's a fact," Mr White said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There are many more sightings near the Wharf Precinct, many more than there used to be."
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="A crocodile feasts on some ill-fated cattle." src="/content/dam/images/2/w/0/u/8/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.2vz61.png/1382567879957.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        A crocodile feasts on some ill-fated cattle.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Jeremy Rooke - crocstalker.com.au
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       He said the wharf precinct, about 2 kilometres from town was a popular hangout spot for "salties".
      </p>
      <p>
       "Down near the jetty you'll usually find at least one big one," Mr White said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "As a child I did all my swimming at the wharf, now you always see them there."
      </p>
      <p>
       He said the bigger ones were more than three or four metres long.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mr White said he used to go out in his boat in Doctors Creek and would never see saltwater crocodiles but now he sees them in the creek just 10 kilometres from town "all the time".
      </p>
      <p>
       "They menace our crab pots, you pull them up and they are wrecked, but I've never had them try to get into my boat like some people," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I do believe numbers need to be looked at.
      </p>
      <p>
       "They are definitely increasing on a large scale.
      </p>
      <p>
       "If the larger crocs come in close to town in large numbers we definitely could have a problem."
      </p>
      <p>
       Shire of Broome president Graeme Campbell agreed that numbers had increased but said he did not think the issue was an overwhelming one just yet.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There's no doubt there's been an increase in sightings," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Obviously with the fact that crocs have not been able to be taken from the wild, there's been an increase in numbers.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There is a potential in the future that there could be an issue.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There's no recorded history of attacks in and around Broome but if a person's dog, or even a child, was taken it would have an effect in tourism and also a significant amount of locals who swim, fish and crab in the area.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I'm not suggesting culling, but if they [crocodiles] do come down here they need to be relocated immediately.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Action needs to be a bit quicker at the outset rather than waiting for continued reports of sightings."
      </p>
      <p>
       Shire of Wyndham East Kimberley president John Moulden said while numbers of saltwater crocodiles had increased slower near the Ord River than the areas bordering it, there was no doubt there were more of them these days.
      </p>
      <p>
       He said some of the crocodiles had to be removed from Lake Kununurra - which borders the town and is widely used by locals and tourists.
      </p>
      <p>
       "They walk across the highway from the diversion dam and then they grow quite big once they get in there," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "DPAW takes these things very seriously, they go and set traps and remove them."
      </p>
      <p>
       Mr Campbell said there had been a few recent reports of cattle being taken from stations.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I'm not personally concerned; it's the crocodile's habitat," he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I'd still be happy to launch a boat in the lower Ord but I wouldn't go swimming there."
      </p>
      <p>
       Jeremy Rooke, an amateur photographer who spends his weekends photographing saltwater crocodiles in and around Wyndham for his website crocstalker.com.au, said people in the area did need to be more vigilant when spending time near or on the water.
      </p>
      <p>
       He said he had seen crocodiles stalking cattle and warned that it was often very difficult to spot them.
      </p>
      <p>
       A spokeswoman for the Department of Parks and Wildlife said crocodiles are a natural part of the environment in northern Australia and are protected fauna.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There is no way to guarantee an area is free of crocodiles and therefore safe, especially in areas where there are no physical barriers that prevent re-colonisation after a crocodile is removed.
      </p>
      <p>
       "The key to maintaining public safety is by raising awareness so that people know how to behave safely and responsibly in crocodile habitats.
      </p>
      <p>
       "However, if there are particular areas of concern or individual animals causing problems, the Department of Parks and Wildlife will, on a case by case basis, attempt to remove or destroy the offending animal giving consideration to the location and the risk posed to public safety."
      </p>
      <p>
       The department does not have current figures for saltwater crocodiles in Western Australia.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to DPAW there have been two deaths as a result of crocodile attacks in WA since 1980.
      </p>
      <p>
       The DPAW spokeswoman said there were no plans to introduce schemes to reduce crocodile numbers in the state.
      </p>
      <p>
       If there was any sort of crocodile cull, it is unlikely that any meat would be able to be processed for human consumption.
      </p>
      <p>
       One West Australian meat wholesaler told Fairfax Media that crocodile meat for human consumption was sourced from farms in Queensland and the Northern Territory.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike kangaroo meat, crocodile meat is farmed more as a by-product of crocodile skins, which for consistancy needed to be of uniform age, shape and size, he said.
      </p>
      <p>
       Wild crocodiles were not always safe for human consumption as they eat a lot of rotten meat and digest food slowly, which poses a listeria risk.
      </p>
