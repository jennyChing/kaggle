     <p>
      A
      <b>
       talk box
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       talkbox
      </b>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/Effects_unit" title="Effects unit">
       effects unit
      </a>
      that allows musicians to modify the sound of a
      <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">
       musical instrument
      </a>
      . The effect can be used to shape the frequency content of the sound and to apply speech sounds (in the same way as singing) onto a musical instrument, typically a Synthesizer. Typically, a talk box directs sound from an instrument into the musician's mouth by means of plastic tube adjacent to their vocal microphone. The musician controls the modification of the instrument's sound by changing the shape of the mouth, "vocalizing" the instrument's output into a microphone.
     </p>

     <p>
      A talk box is usually an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_pedal" title="Effects pedal">
       effects pedal
      </a>
      that sits on the floor and contains a speaker attached with an airtight connection to a plastic tube; however, it can come in other forms, including homemade, usually crude, versions, and higher quality custom-made versions. The speaker is generally in the form of a
      <a href="/wiki/Compression_driver" title="Compression driver">
       compression driver
      </a>
      , the sound-generating part of a
      <a href="/wiki/Horn_loudspeaker" title="Horn loudspeaker">
       horn loudspeaker
      </a>
      with the horn replaced by the tube connection.
     </p>
     <p>
      The box has connectors for the connection to the speaker output of an
      <a href="/wiki/Instrument_amplifier" title="Instrument amplifier">
       instrument amplifier
      </a>
      and a connection to a normal instrument speaker. A foot-operated switch on the box directs the sound either to the talk box speaker or to the normal speaker. The switch is usually a push-on/push-off type. The other end of the tube is taped to the side of a microphone, extending enough to direct the reproduced sound in or near the performer's mouth.
     </p>
     <p>
      When activated, the sound from the amplifier is reproduced by the speaker in the talk box and directed through the tube into the performer's mouth. The shape of the mouth filters the
      <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">
       sound
      </a>
      , with the modified sound being picked up by the microphone. The shape of the mouth changes the
      <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">
       harmonic
      </a>
      content of the sound in the same way it affects the harmonic content generated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Vocal_folds" title="Vocal folds">
       vocal folds
      </a>
      when speaking.
     </p>
     <p>
      The performer can vary the shape of the mouth and position of the tongue, changing the sound of the instrument being reproduced by the talk box speaker. The performer can mouth words, with the resulting effect sounding as though the instrument is speaking. This "shaped" sound exits the performer's mouth, and when it enters a microphone, an instrument/voice hybrid is heard.
     </p>
     <p>
      The sound can be that of any musical instrument, but the effect is most commonly associated with the guitar. The rich harmonics of an
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">
       electric guitar
      </a>
      are shaped by the mouth, producing a sound very similar to voice, effectively allowing the guitar to appear to "speak".
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Singing_guitar">
       Singing guitar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Singing guitar">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Alvino_Rey" title="Alvino Rey">
       Alvino Rey
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1939,
      <a href="/wiki/Alvino_Rey" title="Alvino Rey">
       Alvino Rey
      </a>
      , amateur radio operator W6UK, used a carbon throat microphone wired in such a way as to modulate his electric steel guitar sound. The mic, originally developed for military pilot communications, was placed on the throat of Rey's wife Luise King (one of
      <a href="/wiki/The_King_Sisters" title="The King Sisters">
       The King Sisters
      </a>
      ), who stood behind a curtain and mouthed the words, along with the guitar lines. The novel-sounding combination was called "Singing Guitar", and employed on stage and in the movie
      <i>
       Jam Session
      </i>
      , as a "novelty" attraction, but was not developed further.
     </p>
     <p>
      Rey also created a somewhat similar "talking" effect by manipulating the tone controls of his Fender electric guitar, but the vocal effect was less pronounced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recforums.prosoundweb.com_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-recforums.prosoundweb.com-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sonovox">
       Sonovox
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sonovox">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Another early voice effect using the same principle of the throat as a filter was the
      <b>
       Sonovox
      </b>
      . Instead of a throat microphone modulating a guitar signal, it used small loudspeakers attached to the performer's throat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was used in films such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Letter_to_Three_Wives" title="A Letter to Three Wives">
        A Letter to Three Wives
       </a>
      </i>
      (1949),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Possessed_(1947_film)" title="Possessed (1947 film)">
        Possessed
       </a>
      </i>
      (1947),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Life_of_Walter_Mitty_(1947_film)" title="The Secret Life of Walter Mitty (1947 film)">
        The Secret Life of Walter Mitty
       </a>
      </i>
      (1947), the voice of Casey Junior the
      <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">
       train
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dumbo" title="Dumbo">
        Dumbo
       </a>
      </i>
      (1941) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Reluctant_Dragon_(film)" title="The Reluctant Dragon (film)">
        The Reluctant Dragon
       </a>
      </i>
      (1941), the instruments in
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rusty_in_Orchestraville&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rusty in Orchestraville (page does not exist)">
        Rusty in Orchestraville
       </a>
      </i>
      , the piano in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sparky%27s_Magic_Piano" title="Sparky's Magic Piano">
        Sparky's Magic Piano
       </a>
      </i>
      , and the airplane in
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Whizzer_The_Talking_Airplane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Whizzer The Talking Airplane (page does not exist)">
        Whizzer The Talking Airplane
       </a>
      </i>
      (1947). The Sonovox was also used in many radio station IDs produced by
      <a href="/wiki/PAMS" title="PAMS">
       PAMS
      </a>
      of Dallas and
      <a href="/wiki/JAM_Creative_Productions" title="JAM Creative Productions">
       JAM Creative Productions
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Ball" title="Lucille Ball">
       Lucille Ball
      </a>
      made one of her earliest film appearances during the 1930s in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Path%C3%A9_Newsreel" title="Path Newsreel">
       Path Newsreel
      </a>
      demonstrating the Sonovox.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Sonovox was commercialized by the Wright-Sonovox company, an affiliate of the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Free_%26_Peters&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Free &amp; Peters (page does not exist)">
       Free &amp; Peters
      </a>
      advertising agency.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Sonovox makes an even earlier appearance in the 1940 film
      <i>
       You'll Find Out
      </i>
      starring Kay Kyser and his orchestra, Bela Lugosi, Boris Karloff, and Peter Lorre. Lugosi uses the Sonovox to portray the voice of a dead person during a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seance" title="Seance">
       seance
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      British rock band
      <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">
       The Who
      </a>
      included a cut on their album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Who_Sell_Out" title="The Who Sell Out">
        The Who Sell Out
       </a>
      </i>
      , that consisted of the days of the week "spoken" by guitar chords using the Sonovox. These were in fact a standard set of Sonovox cuts sold by
      <a href="/wiki/PAMS" title="PAMS">
       PAMS
      </a>
      of Dallas. It also appears in use for a short time at the end of their
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Quadrophenia" title="Quadrophenia">
        Quadrophenia
       </a>
      </i>
      album in "The Rock" track.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Talking_steel_guitar">
       Talking steel guitar
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>

      </a>

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/1964_in_music" title="1964 in music">
       1964
      </a>
      album
      <i>
       Forever
      </i>

      <i>

      </i>

      <i>

      </i>

      <i>

      </i>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Drake's device consisted of an 8-inch paper cone speaker driver attached to a funnel from which a clear tube brought the sound to the performer's mouth. It was only loud enough to be useful in the recording studio.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Kustom_Electronics_Talk_Box_.28The_Bag.29">
       Kustom Electronics Talk Box (The Bag)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kustom Electronics Talk Box (The Bag)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Kustom Electronics device, "The Bag",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was the first mass market talk box and was housed in a decorative bag slung over the shoulder like a wine bottle. It used a 30-watt driver and was released to the mass music market in early 1969, two years before Bob Heil's Talk Box became widely available. The Bag is claimed to have been designed by Doug Forbes,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who states that exactly the same concept (speaker attached to a plastic tube and inserted into the mouth) had previously been patented as an artificial
      <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">
       larynx
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/Steppenwolf_(band)" title="Steppenwolf (band)">
       Steppenwolf
      </a>
      used the Kustom Electronics Talk Box (The Bag) in studio recordings and live performances beginning in 1969. On the album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Steppenwolf_Live" title="Steppenwolf Live">
        Steppenwolf Live
       </a>
      </i>

      <a href="/wiki/John_Kay_(musician)" title="John Kay (musician)">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">
       Charlotte, North Carolina
      </a>
      in June 1970 and at two shows in New Jersey (Wildwood and Cherry Hill) in 1971.
      <a href="/wiki/Steppenwolf_(band)" title="Steppenwolf (band)">
       Steppenwolf
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/The_Midnight_Special_(TV_series)" title="The Midnight Special (TV series)">
       The Midnight Special (TV series)
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Kirshner%27s_Rock_Concert" title="Don Kirshner's Rock Concert">
       Don Kirshner's Rock Concert

      <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Cochran" title="Bobby Cochran">

       John Kay

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heil_High_Powered_Talk_Box">
       Heil High Powered Talk Box
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talk_box&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heil High Powered Talk Box">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first high-powered Talk Box was developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Heil" title="Bob Heil">
       Bob Heil
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but there is clearly prior art in the form of the Kustom Electronics device, "The Bag", referenced above. Heil came up with the first
      <i>
       high-powered
      </i>
      Talk Box that could be reliable when used on high-level rock stages. His first Heil Talk Box was built for
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Walsh" title="Joe Walsh">
       Joe Walsh
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Barnstorm_(band)" title="Barnstorm (band)">
       Barnstorm
      </a>
      tour. Heil and Walsh both avid ham radio experimenters (K9EID and WB6ACU), along with Walsh's guitar tech "Krinkle" combined a 250-watt JBL driver and suitable hi-pass filter which was used for Walsh's single "
      <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Way_(song)" title="Rocky Mountain Way (song)">
       Rocky Mountain Way
      </a>
      ". Walsh gives credit to Bill West, an electrical engineer, Nashville steel guitarist and first husband of country music legend
      <a href="/wiki/Dottie_West" title="Dottie West">
       Dottie West
      </a>
      , for inventing the talk box for him in the May 2012 issue of
      <i>
       Guitar World
      </i>
      magazine.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pete_Townshend" title="Pete Townshend">
       Pete Townshend
      </a>
      , in his 2012 autobiography
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Who_I_Am_(book)" title="Who I Am (book)">
        Who I Am
       </a>
      </i>
      , claimed to have invented a version of the Talk Box during a Who tour of the USA in 1976. "I built a speaker in a small box, attached a tube and put the tube in my mouth, allowing me to speak music." Townshend did not specify if his version was for guitar or only for music.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1988, Heil sold the manufacturing rights to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunlop_Manufacturing,_Inc." title="Dunlop Manufacturing, Inc.">
       Dunlop Manufacturing, Inc.
      </a>
      , which currently builds the Heil Talk Box to the exact standards that Heil designed in 1973.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Tell_Me_Something_Good" title="Tell Me Something Good">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rufus_and_Chaka_Khan" title="Rufus and Chaka Khan">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">
       Stevie Wonder

      <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">
       <i>
        Billboard
       </i>
       Hot 100
      </a>

     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road" title="Abbey Road">
       Abbey Road
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Luxembourg_(English)" title="Radio Luxembourg (English)">
       Radio Luxembourg
      </a>
      in the later 1960s. Frampton acquired one as a Christmas present from Bob Heil in 1974. It was a hand-built Talk Box in a fiberglass box using a 100-watt high-powered driver. This was the Heil Talk Box used for the
      <i>
       Frampton Comes Alive
      </i>
      tour and album.

      <a href="/wiki/Nuno_Bettencourt" title="Nuno Bettencourt">
       Nuno Bettencourt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">
       Brian May
      </a>
      was questioned about whether the song "Delilah" was recorded using a talk box on
      <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">
       Queen
      </a>
      's 1991
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Innuendo_(album)" title="Innuendo (album)">
        Innuendo
       </a>
      </i>
      record. May answered: "Yes, I finally succumbed and used one ... I suppose theres no other way to make the meow sounds, meow, meow, meow."
      <a href="/wiki/Tom_Morello" title="Tom Morello">
       Tom Morello
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Rage_Against_the_Machine" title="Rage Against the Machine">
       Rage Against the Machine
      </a>
      used a talk box on their song "Wake Up" in 1992.
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hartman" title="Bob Hartman">
       Bob Hartman
      </a>
      , from
      <a href="/wiki/Petra_(band)" title="Petra (band)">
       Petra
      </a>
      , used the talk box during the 1993 song "Underneath the Blood", from their
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wake-Up_Call_(album)" title="Wake-Up Call (album)">
        Wake-Up Call
       </a>
      </i>
      album.
      <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">
       Metallica
      </a>
      used a talk box during the solo on "The House Jack Built", from the 1996 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Load_(album)" title="Load (album)">
        Load
       </a>
      </i>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Foo_Fighters" title="Foo Fighters">
       Foo Fighters
      </a>
      '
      <a href="/wiki/Dave_Grohl" title="Dave Grohl">
       Dave Grohl
      </a>
      used a talk box during their song "
      <a href="/wiki/Generator_(Foo_Fighters_song)" title="Generator (Foo Fighters song)">
       Generator
      </a>
      " off their 1999 release,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/There_Is_Nothing_Left_To_Lose" title="There Is Nothing Left To Lose">
        There Is Nothing Left To Lose
       </a>
      </i>
      ; his use of the device was partly inspired by Grohl's admiration of
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Frampton" title="Peter Frampton">
       Peter Frampton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Walsh" title="Joe Walsh">
       Joe Walsh
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Dream_Theater" title="Dream Theater">
       Dream Theater
      </a>
      guitarist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Petrucci" title="John Petrucci">
       John Petrucci
      </a>
      used the talk box for live performances of the song "Home", from the band's 1999 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_Pt._2:_Scenes_from_a_Memory" title="Metropolis Pt. 2: Scenes from a Memory">
        Metropolis Pt. 2: Scenes from a Memory
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Avenged_Sevenfold" title="Avenged Sevenfold">
       Avenged Sevenfold
      </a>
      vocalist
      <a href="/wiki/M._Shadows" title="M. Shadows">
       M. Shadows
      </a>
      used a talk box in their song "Lost" off their 2007 self-titled album,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Avenged_Sevenfold_(album)" title="Avenged Sevenfold (album)">
        Avenged Sevenfold
       </a>
      </i>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Godsmack" title="Godsmack">
       Godsmack
      </a>
      's cover of the Joe Walsh song "Rocky Mountain Way", frontman
      <a href="/wiki/Sully_Erna" title="Sully Erna">
       Sully Erna
      </a>
      used a talk box.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Chains" title="Alice in Chains">
       Alice in Chains
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_Jones_(musician)" title="Adam Jones (musician)">
       Adam Jones
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Tool_(band)" title="Tool (band)">
       Tool
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Slash_(musician)" title="Slash (musician)">
       Slash
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">
       Eagles
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chromeo" title="Chromeo">
       Chromeo
      </a>
      , plus dozens of other groups continue to keep the Heil Talk Box in their song sets.
     </p>
     <p>
      The talk box was used in
      <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">
       Elton John
      </a>
      's 1975 album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_of_the_Westies" title="Rock of the Westies">
        Rock of the Westies
       </a>
      </i>
      , on the song "Dan Dare (Pilot of the Future)", as played by
      <a href="/wiki/Davey_Johnstone" title="Davey Johnstone">
       Davey Johnstone
      </a>
      .
     </p>
