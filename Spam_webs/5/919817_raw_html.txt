     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-41989 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="41989" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="article-inline-half">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="Keyboard novel" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half/public/blogs/1726/2010/05/41989-87162.jpg?itok=G22dqxVm" title=""/>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            It's only an appendix to a richly practical and inspiring book, but it certainly got my attention.  I'm referring to Jeff VanderMeer's
            <a href="http://www.jeffvandermeer.com" target="_blank" title="Jeff Vandermeer site">
             <em>
              BookLife: Strategies and Survival Tips for the 21st-Century Writer
             </em>
            </a>
            . What does novelist and nonfiction author VanderMeer have to say in the appendix he named "How to Write a Novel in Two Months"?
           </p>
           <p>
            After laboring on my first novel for a couple of long years (not counting the year-long journal I kept a few years previously that I mined heavily, nor the notes gathered over years in my writer's notebook that I was now able to use, and certainly not counting the very long time to sell the book), I wondered if it would be possible to write a novel more efficiently.  The word
            <em>
             efficiently
            </em>
            initially makes me think, Uh-oh, how creative can efficiency be?  Yet there are no physical laws saying quick (or at least quicker) writing can't also be creative.
           </p>
           <p>
            VanderMeer divides the ideas in this enticing appendix into the two categories of
            <em>
             Support
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             Writing-Related
            </em>
            .  In this post I'll discuss the latter category.  Though VanderMeer's quickie novel, the one that led to this batch of tips, was one of a series of novels tied-in to
            <em>
             Predator
            </em>
            , the suggestions are actually much more widely applicable:
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             How to Write a Novel Fast
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             1. Make sure you know what kind of novel you're writing
            </strong>
            .  Some, such as "a multi-generational saga about a powerful
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/law-and-crime" title="Psychology Today looks at crime ">
             crime
            </a>
            family," can't be written in two months. Come up with  a sort of mission statement, a precise sentence that tells you which way you're headed.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             2. Keep your style relatively transparent
            </strong>
            , rather than heavily layered (i.e., literary).
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             3. Base at least some of your main characters on people you know
            </strong>
            and like, but haven't spent a lot of time with.  That is, someone you know a little, whose details you can fill in.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             4. Create tension by cutting
            </strong>
            scenes in half.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             5. Leave rough what should be rough
            </strong>
            anyway.  His example is setting a battle scene at night in a confusing old temple complex.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             6. Watch out for exotic settings
            </strong>
            .  Find a parallel in your own surroundings that you can get away with without having to do a lot of research.
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             BookLife
            </em>
            contains a lot more, and I've
            <a href="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/writers-mind/2010/05/how-to-live-a-writers-life" target="_blank" title="How to Live a Writer's Life post">
             written elsewhere
            </a>
            about some of that.
           </p>

                 Also, steal a tip from movie writers and TV writers (yes, I am one, and have more than a few published novels under my belt, too): Get into your story as late as you possibly can!  Get into your relevant scenes as late as possible.  No one wants to read about the hero circling the block endessly looking for a parking place unless it's crucial to the action (cf. RUN LOLA RUN).  Start the scene with him or her getting of the damn car!
                </p>
               </div>
