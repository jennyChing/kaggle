     <p>
      <b>
       "Rappin' Duke"
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/1983_in_music" title="1983 in music">
       1983
      </a>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hip-hop" title="Hip-hop">
       hip-hop
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Novelty_song" title="Novelty song">
       novelty song
      </a>
      by Shawn Brown performing as the Rappin' Duke. The concept of the song is that actor
      <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">
       John Wayne
      </a>
      (a.k.a. the Duke) is rhyming (Wayne had died in 1979).
     </p>
     <p>
      "Rappin' Duke"'s lyrics parody the hip hop bragging style popular at the time, wherein the rapper boasts of his/her seniority and superior
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhymes" title="Rhymes">
       rhymes
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flow_(rapping)" title="Flow (rapping)">
       flow
      </a>
      in his/her performance: "So ya think you're bad with your rap? / Well I'll tell ya, pilgrim, I started the crap. / When you were in diapers and wetting the sheets, / I was at the
      <a href="/wiki/Bonanza#Premise" title="Bonanza">
       Ponderosa
      </a>
      , rappin' to the beat". The lyrics also reference
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Beverly_Hillbillies" title="The Beverly Hillbillies">
        The Beverly Hillbillies
       </a>
      </i>
      TV theme song "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Jed_Clampett" title="The Ballad of Jed Clampett">
       The Ballad of Jed Clampett
      </a>
      ", with the words "
      <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">
       Santa Barbara
      </a>
      , that is / Swimmin' pools and movie stars", while also alluding to
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">
       Ronald Reagan
      </a>
      's presidency (the acting careers of Wayne and Reagan overlapped for many years). The song's constant refrain is "dah-ha, dah-ha", a parody of Wayne's distinctive laughter, sung to the music's slow beat.
     </p>
     <p>
      The song uses the hip hop vernacular to make numerous pop cultural references from contemporary hip hop musicians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Kurtis_Blow" title="Kurtis Blow">
       Kurtis Blow
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Run-DMC" title="Run-DMC">
       Run-DMC
      </a>
      , to parodying the opening of
      <a href="/wiki/Chaka_Khan" title="Chaka Khan">
       Chaka Khan
      </a>
      's 1984 hit "
      <a href="/wiki/I_Feel_for_You" title="I Feel for You">
       I Feel for You
      </a>
      ". "Rappin' Duke" was widely played in 1985, peaking at 73 on the 1985 Hot R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Songs
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      chart; and also charting on the Hot Dance Music/Maxi-Singles Sales for 1985. The song's success led to Brown opening in 1985 and 1986 for such artists as
      <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Brown" title="Bobby Brown">
       Bobby Brown
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">
       Stevie Wonder
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Brown signed with
      <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Boy_Records" title="Tommy Boy Records">
       Tommy Boy Records
      </a>
      after the album
      <i>
       Que Pasa?
      </i>
      ran its course on the charts.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">
       Music video
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rappin%27_Duke&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Music video">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The video opens with an African-American cowboy alternately walking and
      <a href="/wiki/Moonwalk_(dance)" title="Moonwalk (dance)">
       back-sliding
      </a>
      onscreen with a
      <a href="/wiki/Boombox" title="Boombox">
       boombox
      </a>
      propped on his shoulder, obscuring his face. He is replaced by various images from old cowboy movies: gun battles, rope tricks, and the like, as well as other assorted images vaguely connected with the song lyrics. Halfway through the song, the cowboy  his features always hidden  reappears poolside, together with a trio of swimsuit-clad women. He shows them a few tricks, including
      <a href="/wiki/Moonwalk_(dance)" title="Moonwalk (dance)">
       moonwalking
      </a>
      in his cowboy boots, and then ends up in the pool with the girls. The video fades out with the cowboy staggering into the sunset.
     </p>
