         <h3>
          <a href="/view/articles/butterflies-bees-and-birds-attracting-the-three-bs-551">
           Butterflies, Bees, and Birds--Attracting the Three "B's"
          </a>
         </h3>
         <div>
          [Gardening]
         </div>
         <span class="articleauthor">
          <a href="/JaymeKinsey">
           by Jayme Kinsey
          </a>
          published 2 years ago
          <div class="g-plusone" data-annotation="inline" data-width="300">
          </div>
          <!-- Place this tag after the last +1 button tag. -->
          <script type="text/javascript">
           (function() {

       <div class="article" id="R551">
        <!--INFOLINKS_ON-->
        <div>
         <p>
          <span style="margin-left: 10px; margin-right: 10px; float: right;">
           <img alt="" src="/images/gallery/107/480_1371454189_11bird.jpg" style="width: 480px; height: 320px;"/>
           <br/>
           <i>
            Credits
            <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Archilochus_colubris_NBII.jpg">
             wikimedia public domain image
            </a>
           </i>
          </span>
         </p>
         <div>
          If you grow a garden, you probably like seeing butterflies, bees and birds (the three "B's") as well. Although they are fun to watch, they are also important to your plants.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Hummingbirds, bees, and butterflies are all "
          <strong>
           pollinators
          </strong>
          ". They carry pollen from one plant to another. This is what helps flowers bloom and produce fruits, nuts, and vegetables.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Pollinators also help to transfer seeds from one place to another. This is why you may notice last years annuals and perennials sprouting in places far away from their original locations.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Pollinators are responsible for 150 food crops in the US
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           There are more than 250, 000 recognized flowering species --pollinators are responsible for reproduction in over 90% of these.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           The contribute to everything from the food we eat to the medicine we need
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Without pollinators, there would be little to no agriculture
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Pollinators contribute to a healthy ecosystem by ensuring that native flowers continue to grow and support wildlife
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Human intervention has caused a decline in the natural number of pollinators. This is a direct threat not only to your garden, but to your well-being as a human. More and more gardeners are choosing to include flowers to attract pollinators, and eschewing the use of harmful chemicals.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          You can help too! Include some friendly plants in your garden to attract these three B's.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Flowers to Attract Hummingbirds
         </h2>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Plenty of yards have hummingbird feeders to attract these tiny creatures. Whereas this is a great way to attract them, it may not be beneficial to the ecosystem. Hummingbirds will fill up on the nectar you provide, and ignore the flowers.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          It is okay to have nectar feeders, especially to attract the birds before your flowers bloom, but you may want to limit the number of feeders so that birds are encouraged to 'sip' at the blooms too.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Hummingbirds are attracted primarily to red, but will also come to purple and orange flowers too. They like tubular, sweet flowers. Some great hummingbird flowers include:
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Bee Balm
          </li>
          <li>
           Nasturtium
          </li>
          <li>
           Fuchsia
          </li>
          <li>
           Delphinium
          </li>
          <li>
           Lantana
          </li>
          <li>
           Rose of Sharon
          </li>
          <li>
           Butterfly Bush
          </li>
          <li>
           Hollyhock
          </li>
          <li>
           Daylily
          </li>
          <li>
           Hibiscus
          </li>
          <li>
           Columbine
          </li>
          <li>
           Oriental Poppies
          </li>
          <li>
           Coral Bells
          </li>
          <li>
           Peony
          </li>
          <li>
           Mimosa
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Attracting Bees to the Garden
         </h2>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          A common assumption many people make is that honeybees are the only "good" bees. All bees contribute to the health and reproduction of flowers. Honey bees are just the ones that produce an edible crop that humans enjoy. Therefore, don't be discouraged if the only bees you see in your flower bed are bumble bees. They are pollinators too.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Unlike hummingbirds, bees like pollen as well as nectar. Think of the nectar as the bees "carb" fix, and the pollen as their "protein shake". Bees use a lot of energy and need plenty of sustenance. To keep bees in your garden longer, stagger your planting so that flowers are available all season.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          You can also plan for flowers that bloom longer, as well as plants that bloom in different months. This will keep your garden looking full and pretty too! Here are some favorite bee plants. Always try to include a few native plants that may be specific attractants for local bees.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Purple Coneflower (echinacea)
          </li>
          <li>
           Butterfly weed
          </li>
          <li>
           Marigolds
          </li>
          <li>
           Daisies
          </li>
          <li>
           Black-eyed Susan
          </li>
          <li>
           Sunflower
          </li>
          <li>
           Sage
          </li>
          <li>
           Lavender
          </li>
          <li>
           Zinnia
          </li>
          <li>
           Rosemary
          </li>
          <li>
           Hyssop
          </li>
          <li>
           Evening Primrose,
          </li>
          <li>
           Rose
          </li>
          <li>
           Cosmos
          </li>
          <li>
           Marjoram
          </li>
          <li>
           Fruit and vegetable plants
          </li>
          <li>
           Bee Balm
          </li>
          <li>
           Clover
          </li>
          <li>
           Dandelion
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Attracting Butterflies to the Garden
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Considered by many to be the most attractive of the pollinators, butterflies are easy to please in the food department. Like hummingbirds, they depend primarily on nectar. Some butterflies also like the occasional sip of water. Butteflies aren't just attracted to flowers for the nectar. They also need plants to host their eggs. Most species will only lay eggs on one certain type of plant. Here are some butterfly plants;
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Queen Anne's Lace
          </li>
          <li>
           Clover
          </li>
          <li>
           Bee Balm
          </li>
          <li>
           Butterfly weed
          </li>
          <li>
           Hollyhock
          </li>
          <li>
           Zinnia
          </li>
          <li>
           Cosmos
          </li>
          <li>
           Dandelion
          </li>
          <li>
           Purple Coneflower
          </li>
          <li>
           Sunflower
          </li>
          <li>
           Daisy
          </li>
          <li>
           Milkweed
          </li>
          <li>
           Privet
          </li>
          <li>
           Forget-me-not
          </li>
          <li>
           Verbena
          </li>
          <li>
           Peas
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Other Ways To Support Pollinators
         </h2>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          It is nice to attract these creatures to your flower garden. But once they are there, it is only polite to extend your hospitality. Provide them with housing, water, and additional food supplements, and you will have pollinators in your yard all year.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Some things you can make or place in the garden to encourage the permanent presence of pollinators include:
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Bat houses
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Butterfly houses
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Butterfly feeder
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Butterfly puddler (also useful for bees)
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Hummingbird nest
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Bees nests (Some bees do not build nests or hives on their own.)
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <h2>
          More Pollinators For The Garden
         </h2>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          Bees, butterflies and birds are not the only pollinators. Unfortunately, some of the others are much less attractive. They can also cause damage to some plants. Here are some plant pollinators you might not have been aware of:
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Ants--yes, these tiny bugs are actually useful! Even though they have been known to munch on some plants, they play a beneficial role in the reproduction in others. They primarily pollinate short plants, such as ground covers.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Moths--Moths are just as helpful as butterflies when it comes to helping the garden. Too bad they also eat clothing, produce harmful caterpillars, and in the South, raid our pantries to destroy dry-goods. Still, moths have their role in the ecosystem. They are attracted to plants that bloom in the late afternoons and evenings.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Flies--They are annoying, but they are part of the lifecycle of humans, plants and animals. Flies are generally attracted to shallow flowers that are pale in color (light pink, white).
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div>
         </div>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Bats--You will probably never know if bats are drinking at your flowers or not. They prefer flowers that bloom in the evening and at night. They also prefer flowers that have very strong scents.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Wasps
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Beetles
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Other Birds
          </li>
         </ul>
         <h2>
          Make Friends With Weeds and Wildflowers
         </h2>
         <div>
          Most flowers that attract pollinators are wildflowers. For many, these are considered nuisance plants. even weeds have a place in the garden. They make excellent companion plants for vegetables and other plants. Some can be invasive however.
         </div>
         <div>
         </div>
         <div>
          If you don't want them in your garden, consider alloting a small area away from the main part of your garden for these plants. Once the pollinators find them, they will discover your other plants as well.
         </div>
