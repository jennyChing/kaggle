     <p>
      <b>
       Cabeus
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       lunar
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
       crater
      </a>
      that is located about 100km (62mi) from the
      <a href="/wiki/Lunar_south_pole" title="Lunar south pole">
       south pole
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grego2005_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-grego2005-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At this location the crater is seen obliquely from
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      , and it is almost perpetually in deep shadow due to lack of
      <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
       sunlight
      </a>
      . Hence, not much detail can be seen of this crater, even from orbit. Through a
      <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
       telescope
      </a>
      , this crater appears near the southern limb of the Moon, to the west of the crater
      <a href="/wiki/Malapert_(crater)" title="Malapert (crater)">
       Malapert
      </a>
      and to the south-southwest of
      <a href="/wiki/Newton_(lunar_crater)" title="Newton (lunar crater)">
       Newton
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clementine_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-clementine-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitaker1999_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-whitaker1999-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabeus_(crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cabeus_crater.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="724" data-file-width="724" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Cabeus_crater.jpg/220px-Cabeus_crater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Cabeus_crater.jpg/330px-Cabeus_crater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Cabeus_crater.jpg/440px-Cabeus_crater.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cabeus_crater.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Cabeus Crater.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The crater name
      <i>
       Cabeus
      </i>
      first appeared in the 1651 work
      <i>
       Almagestum Novum
      </i>
      by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Riccioli" title="Giovanni Riccioli">
       Giovanni Riccioli
      </a>
      , who named it after
      <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Cabeo" title="Niccol Cabeo">
       Niccol Cabeo
      </a>
      . However, the position of the Cabeus crater was in the location later assigned to
      <a href="/wiki/Newton_(lunar_crater)" title="Newton (lunar crater)">
       Newton crater
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The official name and location for this crater was adopted by the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
       IAU
      </a>
      Commission 17, as established in the 1935 work
      <i>
       Named Lunar Formations
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Adela_Blagg" title="Mary Adela Blagg">
       Mary A. Blagg
      </a>
      and Karl Mller.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-USGS-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This crater is a worn formation that has been eroded by subsequent impacts. The rim is eroded and uneven, with prominent ridges at the northern and southern ends. A small crater lies across the northeastern rim and there is 1011km crater on the interior floor near the west-southwestern rim. Near the center of the crater floor is a small ridge. The floor of the crater has an average depth of 4km and it is 60km across. The slope of the crater walls is 1015.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kozlova2010_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-kozlova2010-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Because of the crater's location near the lunar south pole, the main part of the crater is illuminated by the Sun during only 25% of each lunar day. The inner walls receive illumination for 30% of a lunar day, while part of the western end of the crater is in permanent shadow.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kozlova2010_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-kozlova2010-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Volatiles">
       Volatiles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabeus_(crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Volatiles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="715" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg/220px-LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg/330px-LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg/440px-LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The flash from the
        <a href="/wiki/LCROSS" title="LCROSS">
         LCROSS
        </a>
        Centaur impact.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The south polar region of the Moon was surveyed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
       Lunar Prospector
      </a>
      spacecraft and a hydrogen signature was detected. Potential sources for this hydrogen include water deposits from comet or meteorite impacts, the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
       solar wind
      </a>
      or out-gassing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science5420_1658_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-science5420_1658-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This crater is sufficiently large that the temperature within the shaded region is below 100K (173C). This would allow water ice to remain on or near the crater surface for billions of years without sublimating.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jgr106_e10_23_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jgr106_e10_23-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Aufnahme_Zielgebiet_Centaur_im_Cabeus_Krater_VIS.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Target area of the Centaur rocket at Cabeus crater.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
       National Aeronautics and Space Administration
      </a>
      launched the
      <a href="/wiki/LCROSS" title="LCROSS">
       LCROSS
      </a>
      , impactor spacecraft on 18 June 2009, to look for
      <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
       water
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Lunar_south_pole" title="Lunar south pole">
       Lunar south pole
      </a>
      . On 28 September 2009, Cabeus proper was selected as the impact target for the
      <a href="/wiki/LCROSS" title="LCROSS">
       LCROSS
      </a>
      mission, switching the target from satellite crater Cabeus A. The change was made after review of the latest data gathered by other lunar exploration craft, which indicated that Cabeus proper had a higher concentration of hydrogen than Cabeus A.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcrossB_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lcrossB-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At 11:31
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
       UTC
      </a>
      on 9 October 2009 the
      <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">
       Centaur
      </a>
      upper stage of its
      <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">
       Atlas V
      </a>
      carrier rocket impacted Cabeus, followed shortly thereafter by the impact of the LCROSS spacecraft itself. The impact of the Centaur was intended to throw up a plume of lunar surface material to be sampled by sensors carried on the LCROSS spacecraft as it traversed the plume. But the debris plumes were smaller than predicted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcross_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lcross-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Preliminary data from the LCROSS spectrometer measurements of the impact plume appeared to confirm the presence of water in the crater.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prn20091113_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-prn20091113-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Analysis of the plume observations supports the presence of water in the regolith. Absorption in the near-infrared can be attributed to ice and water vapor, while emissions in the ultraviolet indicate the presence of hydroxyl radicals, which also supports the likelihood of water. The estimated total amount of water vapor and ice in the plume is up to
      <span class="nowrap">
       155  12 kg
      </span>
      , or an estimated
      <span class="nowrap">
       5.6  2.9%
      </span>
      by mass. The spectral signatures of other volatiles were observed, matching the signatures of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      , light
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
       hydrocarbons
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
       sulfur
      </a>
      -bearing compounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science330_6003_463_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-science330_6003_463-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
