        By &lt;a class="userlink" href="/user/808647" onmouseover="djp.user.tt(this,event);" pg="5" ach="" achh=""&gt;Andrew John&lt;/a&gt;              &nbsp;

&lt;div class="abstract"&gt;Secularists in the UK are outraged after Pope Benedict XVI seemed to associate atheism with Nazism during his speech to Queen Elizabeth II yesterday.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;/div&gt;




      &lt;div class="img" id="artis-145658"&gt;&lt;div style="height:200px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
  

&lt;div class="article-column"&gt;

  &lt;div class="rs_skip"&gt;
      &lt;/div&gt;

  &lt;div class="body"&gt;&lt;a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-11332515"&gt;The BBC reports&lt;/a&gt; that humanists in the country have called the Popes remarks a terrible libel against nonbelievers.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

The Pope, speaking at Holyroodhouse in Edinburgh, talked first of a wartime Nazi tyranny that wished to eradicate God from society. Then he said the UK should guard itself against aggressive forms of secularism.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

However, the Catholic Church has played down the Popes words, saying the Pope knew rather well what the Nazi ideology is about.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

The British Humanist Association condemned the Popes remarks. In a statement, it said: The notion that it was the atheism of Nazis that led to their extremist and hateful views or that it somehow fuels intolerance in Britain today is a terrible libel against those who do not believe in God.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

The notion that it is nonreligious people in the UK today who want to force their views on others, coming from a man whose organisation exerts itself internationally to impose its narrow and exclusive form of morality and undermine the human rights of women, children, gay people and many others, is surreal.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

&lt;a href="http://www.vatican.va/holy_father/benedict_xvi/speeches/2010/september/documents/hf_ben-xvi_spe_20100916_incontro-autorita_en.html"&gt;The Popes speech&lt;/a&gt; contained the paragraph:
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

&lt;q&gt;Even in our own lifetime, we can recall how Britain and her leaders stood against a Nazi tyranny that wished to eradicate God from society and denied our common humanity to many, especially the Jews, who were thought unfit to live. I also recall the regimes attitude to Christian pastors and religious who spoke the truth in love, opposed the Nazis and paid for that opposition with their lives. As we reflect on the sobering lessons of the atheist extremism of the twentieth century, let us never forget how the exclusion of God, religion and virtue from public life leads ultimately to a truncated vision of man and of society and thus to a reductive vision of the person and his destiny.&lt;/q&gt;
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

And later in his speech he referred to aggressive secularism as he said:
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

&lt;q&gt;Today, the United Kingdom strives to be a modern and multicultural society. In this challenging enterprise, may it always maintain its respect for those traditional values and cultural expressions that more aggressive forms of secularism no longer value or even tolerate. Let it not obscure the Christian foundation that underpins its freedoms; and may that patrimony, which has always served the nation well, constantly inform the example your Government and people set before the two billion members of the Commonwealth and the great family of English-speaking nations throughout the world.&lt;/q&gt;
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

The four-day visit by Pope Benedict to the UK  which began yesterday  has never been without controversy. Yesterday, a senior cardinal was &lt;a href="http://www.digitaljournal.com/article/297648"&gt;called on to apologize&lt;/a&gt; after he likened England to a Third World Country.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

Earlier this week, 50 notables from the worlds of science and the arts  including Stephen Fry, Richard Dawkins and Philip Pullman  signed an open letter &lt;a href="http://www.digitaljournal.com/article/297570"&gt;condemning the state visit&lt;/a&gt;, which has put a burden on the British taxpayer.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

Meanwhile, nuns and monks &lt;a href="http://www.digitaljournal.com/article/297691"&gt;have been frisked&lt;/a&gt; and their robes and habits scrupulously checked by police in London today as Pope Benedict begins the second day of his state visit.
&lt;div style="padding-bottom:15px;"&gt;&lt;/div&gt;&lt;/div&gt;

&lt;/div&gt;
