       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Way Too Late Movie Review: Birdman. Is it science fiction?
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-byline">
         Posted by
         <span class="author vcard">
          <a class="url fn n" href="http://nonlocalscifi.com/author/nonlocal2731/" rel="author" title="Daniel J. Dombrowski">
           Daniel J. Dombrowski
          </a>
         </span>
         on
         <time class="published" datetime="2015-04-01T16:27:11-04:00" title="Wednesday, April 1st, 2015, 4:27 pm">
          April 1, 2015
         </time>
         in
         <span class="category">
          <a href="http://nonlocalscifi.com/category/reviews/" rel="tag">
           Reviews
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://nonlocalscifi.com/category/reviews/wtl-reviews/" rel="tag">
           WTL Reviews
          </a>
         </span>
         | 4 Views  |
         <a class="comments-link" href="http://nonlocalscifi.com/2015/04/way-too-late-movie-review-birdman-is-it-science-fiction/#respond" title="Comment on Way Too Late Movie Review: Birdman. Is it science fiction?">
          Leave a response
         </a>
        </div>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <em>
          Way Too Late Reviews are reviews that come well after the fact. These days, theres far too much out there for any one person to consume it as it happens. We catch up when we can  usually in the cheapest way possible. Looking for something to watch this weekend on demand or in Redbox? Maybe you want to go back even further and find a hidden gem on Netflix. Before you do, check out Way Too Late Reviews!
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Warning: The trailer below is the international version and contains quite a bit of profanity. Just a heads up:
        </p>
        <p>
         <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/uJfLoE6hanc" width="560">
         </iframe>
        </p>
        <p>
         I really dont know where to begin. First of all, I suppose it is debatable whether or not reviewing
         <em>
          Birdman or (The Unexpected Virtue of Ignorance)
         </em>
         at this point is way too late or slightly behind the curve or just in time. I suppose it all depends on who you ask. The film has been out for quite a while, making the festival rounds throughout the latter half of 2014 and eventually releasing somewhat widely on 854 screens across the country in November. In that sense, I am, in fact, way too late to be reviewing this movie.
        </p>
        <p>
         But for those who werent even really aware that this movie existed until the Academy Awards a few months back, for those who are now seeing
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         in rental stores and kiosks and wondering whether the film is worth their time, I believe I can be of some use.
        </p>
        <p>
         There is also, of course, the obvious question of why Im reviewing what is, in reality, a very artsy film on a science fiction website that usually espouses pop culture more than anything else To that I will answer that, whether it is the popular conception or not,
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is science fiction. Those who tell you that
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is not science fiction are also likely to tell you that Kurt Vonnegut was not a science fiction writer, or to at least downplay the importance of his genre underpinnings in the face of what he accomplished as a modern American literary luminary.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is a science fiction film. Dont let anymore tell you otherwise. It it, however, more than a little different. This aint no
         <em>
          Guardians of the Galaxy
         </em>
         . If that intrigues you, read on.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <strong>
          <em>
           Birdman
          </em>
          : The Meat vs. The Sauce
         </strong>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Several weeks ago, Thad Kanupp wrote a wonderful piece for this site called
         <a href="http://nonlocalscifi.com/2015/02/saucy-meat-aesthetics-define-science-fiction-genre/" title="Saucy Meat, or: How Aesthetics Define the Science Fiction Genre">
          Saucy Meat, or: How Aesthetics Define the Science Fiction Genre
         </a>
         . In it, he discussed how we are oftentimes handed books, television, and movies that are only science fiction on the surface. They are classic, archetypical stories, brushed with a layer of gooey genre goodness and set in space, in the future, on other planets, etc.
        </p>
        <p>
         Thads most classic example of this is the
         <em>
          Star Wars
         </em>
         franchise, a fantasy tale about wizards and knights doing battle to decide the fate of a kingdom that is, perhaps, the most recognizable science fiction franchise in the world despite the fact that it has mystical powers as a central plot element. Thads final conclusions are that it doesnt matter whether the science fiction is in the meat or just in the sauce. What matters is that it is present and that you enjoy it.
        </p>
        <p>
         Im trying to figure out how to explain this in the context of
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         without ruining the film for anyone who hasnt already seen it. Ill say it this way: There are science fiction elements in play throughout the movie. Some will argue that they are an illusion, that they are art and symbolism and nothing more. They may well be, but you can also make the argument that something more is going on in the film, something wonderful and science fiction-y.
        </p>
        <p>
         Director
         <span class="itemprop">
          Alejandro Gonzlez Irritu
         </span>
         never resolves this dispute, giving ammunition to both sides up until the final seconds of the film. The more you read about
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         , the more youll be convinced that
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is
         <strong>
          not
         </strong>
         science fiction, but that probably has more to do with the industry wanting to play up the artsy side of things more than the genre-weirdness. But trust me, when you watch it, youll know. It might be the silver screen equivalent to literary science fiction,
         <em>
          a la
         </em>
         Mr. Vonnegut, but it is science fiction nonetheless.
        </p>
        <p>
         And in the end it doesnt matter if the sci-fi elements of the plot are real or an illusion. If theyre an illusion, nothing more than sauce brushed on to lessen the harsh tones of reality, that doesnt make
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         any less of a sci-fi film. All it means is that the film shows us how science fiction can exist overlaid over our own perceptions of reality. And thats actually pretty cool to think about. Or scary. I suppose it might also be really, really scary.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Is
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         worth your time?
        </h2>
        <p>
         I know what many of you are thinking. Why spend time and money on a movie that is the darling of the Academy? Youve been tricked into this before. You rented
         <em>
          Black Swan
         </em>
         because you heard Mila Kunis and Natalie Portman hooked up, only to be creeped the hell out for a good week afterwards. You shelled out some of your hard-earned cash to see
         <em>
          Million Dollar Baby
         </em>
         because Clint Eastwood is a badass, only to find yourself in tears, throwing the remote at your TV after
         <em>
          that ending
         </em>
         . You wont be fooled again.
        </p>
        <p>
         Heres the thing, though.
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is so incredibly different from anything else youve ever seen that all arguments about whether or not you like good movies should go right out the window. First of all, the cinematography (Yes, yes, I know. You hate that word. But seriously, just stick with me.) is amazing. The movie was conceived as one, long, unbroken camera shot. Clearly, there are breaks and transitions at different points, but there are very few. Youll feel like youre riding along, watching the action as it happens
         <em>
          because you actually are
         </em>
         . Go out and find articles about how arduous it was to shoot this movie. The actors filmed scenes that went on for eons in Hollywood camera time, and if they screwed up, they had to go back and do it again. There was no editing aside from, Which version of this 25 minute scene should we use? If you cant appreciate that, if youd rather watch Iron Man and Hulk punch each other through buildings, then I dont know what to tell you.
        </p>
        <h2>
         You havent convinced me to rent
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         yet. Keep trying.
        </h2>
        <p>
         Other little details really jump out as well. Youve undoubtedly heard a lot about the cast (Michael Keaton, Emma Stone, Edward Norton, Zach Galifianakis, and Naomi Watts are the core talent), and they definitely shine. Theres also the soundtrack, which is comprised mainly of frenetic jazz drumming that captures the not-quite-sane mood of the film as a whole perfectly. Finally, at least for this article, theres the underlying theme of the conflict between legitimate theater and film.
        </p>
        <p>
         I havent gone much into the actual plot here because there is so much else to talk about, but in a nutshell,
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is about a washed-up superhero actor trying to revive his career by directing/producing/starring in a Broadway play that he adapted from a short story. The film is rife with comments that demean Hollywood actors and place them firmly below their stage-bound cousins. The irony of a highly-paid Hollywood actor delivering lines about how high-paid Hollywood actors are the worst is laid on thickly. The irony of Michael Keaton playing an actor trying to escape the shadow of his superhero origins is also pretty funny. (His character played Birdman. Keaton played Batman. Get it?) Luckily for Keaton, he was spared the fate of Riggin Thompson, his character, in real life.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Final Thoughts
        </h2>
        <p>
         <em>
          Birdman
         </em>
         is an Academy Award-winning film that has become far too prestigious to admit what it really is: a weird, existential take on science fiction. It isnt a film that you can watch while relaxing after a hard week with the goal of turning your brain off. It isnt a film that you can spring on unsuspecting friends and family for movie night and expect them to enjoy without at least a little preparation.
        </p>
        <p>
         But it is a film that you should watch.
        </p>
