           <p>
            The clock is ticking on XP, so we thought wed check in with a few SpiceHeads to see how its going. We talked to one who has completely migrated off of XP already, one who is in the process, and one who has XP machines that are going to stay that way. For security reasons, our participants are numbered rather than named.
           </p>
           <br/>
           <p>
            <b>
             <i>
              IT PRO #42 HAS ALREADY MIGRATED OFF XP. HE WORKS IN THE TELECOM INDUSTRY:
             </i>
            </b>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <b>
             When did you kick XP to the curb?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            I started the process of upgrading approximately 110 workstations to Windows 7 about two months after the notice was received for XP losing support.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Youre definitely ahead of the curve  what was the big hurry?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            I wanted the company to roll into it as soon as possible to mitigate the learning curve and be able to ask questions before it was too late.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Did you encounter any resistance to the migration, either from a budget standpoint or just from users who didnt want to move?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Of courseon both accounts. There was the initial budget meeting and my plea to the CEO/managers that the upgrade was needed. We also started early for budgeting reasons, as I was budgeted for 5-10 workstations per month until the task was completed. Of course I got the 10 done before the month was up and ordered more. Only being able to do 5-10 per month was going to cut it very close to the end-of-life deadline, so I did my due diligence to make sure our company made the deadline. Thankfully we have already completed the upgrade.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Was the migration harder or easier than you thought it would be?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            It was fairly easy. I created a base image with Clonezilla and Sysprepped the image. Since I didnt have a volume license, I made the image ask for the product key when the image was deployed. It was one of the only manual tasks that I had to do.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             What was the biggest challenge?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            The biggest challenge was getting the budget approved. The CEO and managers did not want to let that big of a price tag fly.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Did you upgrade the OS on existing devices or did you buy new devices  or a combination?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            A combination. Any device that was more than five years old was depreciated. Anything still new enough was just wiped clean and the Windows 7 image was pushed out.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             If you had it all to do over again, is there anything about the migration that youd do differently?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            I don't think I would do anything differently. Overall the migration went very smoothly. The users didn't even put up a big fight; most were excited for something new and shiny. A couple of users were reluctant to switch but they are on board now.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Is there anything you miss about XP?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Windows XP was one of the best and most stable OSes Microsoft has produced. A lot can be missed, but Windows 7 runs really smooth, and I really have no complaints about it.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Any words of advice for the IT pros out there who are still on XP?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            For the safety of your company's data, it would be best to get this done before the support ends. The last thing you want is to have security holes in your network.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Some say peanut butter is crucial to the migration process. Assuming that's the case, which is your preference  crunchy or smooth?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            There is no competition; hands down I am a smooth peanut butter kind of man. And apparently, there are some
            <a href="http://www.prevention.com/which-healthier-creamy-peanut-butter-vs-crunchy-peanut-butter#" rel="nofollow">
             health reasons
            </a>
            to choose one over the other that I never knew about.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <b>
             <i>
              IT PRO #867-5309 IS IN THE PROCESS OF MIGRATING OFF XP. HE WORKS IN THE HEALTHCARE INDUSTRY:
             </i>
            </b>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <b>
             When did you start the migration process?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            We upgrade pretty much as soon as a new product is out so we can start the testing. Then we deploy to a very small set us users who can deal with the pain of a new product. So we started when Vista, then 7, then 8, then 8.1, and finally 8.1u1 were released. We usually get pre-releases so this process starts very early on. With each of those releases, we did limited rollouts. We upgraded our doctors to Windows 7 about three years ago, but left the clinic staff on XP. We are now in the process of moving the staff to Windows 7 and the doctors to Windows 8.1u1 (depending on browser compatibility). This started about a month ago.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Hows it going so far? Harder or easier than you thought?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            So far everything is going well. We are in the Windows 7 portion of the rollout to staff. This is well documented for us as our doctors have been using Windows 7 for three years. We will start the Windows 8 portion next week.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Whats been the biggest challenge so far?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Getting new equipment and licensing purchased. Explaining to the doctors that they really did need to spend around $50k per year to be compliant with MS licensing was not an easy task. Also explaining that seven-year-old laptops and ten-year-old desktops really do need replacing was a challenge. Another hurdle was explaining that XP was not going to be HIPAA compliant after April 8. Their thoughts were, "If the hospitals, labs, insurance companies, etc. aren't upgrading, why should we?" There was considerable budget resistance from the doctors.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             What about the clinic staff? How are they feeling about it?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Users getting new hardware are very grateful. Most have Windows 7 or 8 at home so they are happy we are catching up. The docs are getting brand new Dell Latitude 14 7000 series Ultrabooks, so they will be happy too.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Will you make it off XP by the dreaded EOL on 4/8? If not, whats going to get in the way?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            We will not make it by 4/8/2014, but we should be done by the end of April. There are just two of us and we can't physically get it done that fast. Had we received the equipment and software a month earlier... Well, it didn't happen that way.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Do you feel like the risks are well understood in your organization?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            The IT department understands the risks. The rest of the company is just going along because IT said they need to.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Are you just upgrading the OS on existing devices or are you buying new devices  or a combination?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            We are upgrading three-year-old and newer equipment. We have also purchased 25 new desktops and 35 new laptops.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             What are you going to miss most about XP?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            I know where everything is. It's like moving out of a house you have lived in for 10+ years. It was home and it was comfortable. But the new house is bigger and it has a better home theater and an outside kitchen and there is a pool and the view is spectacular. It won't take long to get used to the new house, but we will always remember the good times from the old house.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Any words of advice for IT pros who are still on XP?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Try to get the budget to upgrade, but do not skirt MS licensing to get on the new OSes. It is just not worth the headache and fines.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Some say peanut butter is crucial to the migration process. Assuming that's the case, which is your preference  crunchy or smooth?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            This is an easy one. Crunchy on a sandwich, smooth on a spoon or between two Nilla wafers.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <b>
             <i>
              IT PRO #3.14 IS STILL RUNNING XP AND WILL BE FORA WHILE. HE WORKS IN MANUFACTURING:
             </i>
            </b>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <b>
             Like most of the rest of the world, youre still running XP. Whats keeping you from migrating?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            The computers that will remain in XP in our environment are used almost exclusively on the production floor of the factory in various capacities, be it machine control, product testing, etc. Among our other relics, we also have a half dozen Windows 9x machines, and a DOS machine. For one reason or another, it is not possible to upgrade these systems.
           </p>
           <p>
            We use several programs written in-house that are untested on anything newer than XP. Instead of devoting resources to shoe-horning those programs, new ones are being written and tested which should be more agile or "OS agnostic." Since we have ~150 such machines, most of which do not have the hardware needed to handle a newer OS, its a cost saving measure as well.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Whats the plan for handling the potential risks of running an EOL OS?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Most if not all of these computers are set to auto-login. Because of this, we have GPO's in place for these logins which prevent them from reaching the outside world. We also have an in-line web filter to further mitigate risk in the rare event one of these machines would need to have access, even temporarily.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Do you feel like the risks are well understood in your organization, or is it an uphill battle to make your case?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            The risks have been thoroughly discussed, and we feel our plan of action is the best balance of risk management and business continuity. When we explained the risks and the plan of action to our President, he agreed.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             How long do you think youll run XP?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            I would hope between hardware failures and OS upgrades where possible, we can be off in the next 36 months.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             What will you miss most about XP when its finally gone?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Familiarity and nostalgia. XP was kind of like a brother or a sister. It might have been a pain in the neck, but they were good to you and were pretty dependable.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Some folks in the industry are predicting an XPocalypse  presumably of Y2K proportions. Do you think April 8 will be a day like any other, or should I really be stocking up on canned foods and bottled water??
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            I wouldn't be too alarmist about it, but it is going to cause a lot of headaches for some, especially in the consumer space, who either refuse to upgrade because "This works just fine" or can't afford a new PC.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             What words of advice do you have for other IT pros who are in your shoes  who for one reason or another cant migrate before EOL?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Get a plan together to try and limit those computers exposure to the outside world and at least ballpark what it will take to get to something newer. And most importantly, talk to management about it, and get them on board for an upgrade, even if it's planned and spread out over a period of time.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Some say peanut butter is crucial to the migration process. Assuming that's the case, which is your preference  crunchy or smooth?
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            Crunchy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <i>
             Thanks for giving us a peek behind your IT curtains! What about the rest of you? What will you miss most about XP?
            </i>
           </p>
