         <h1 id="page-title">
          An Emerging High Schooler, Interest-led, Learner-Specific Homeschool Curriculum
         </h1>
        </header>
        <div class="region region-content">
         <div class="block block-system block-odd block-count-3 block-region-content" id="block-system-main">
          <div class="block-inner clearfix">
           <div class="block-content content no-title">
            <div class="block-content-inner clearfix">
             <article about="/interest-led-emerging-high-school-homeschool-curriculum" class="article article-type-article article-promoted odd article-with-picture article-full clearfix" id="article-8584" role="article" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">
              <div class="article-content">
               <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden view-mode-full">
                <div class="field-items">
                 <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                  <p>
                   Oh my word. That's a mouthful of a title.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I originally started this post as an overview of all three of our kids' homeschool curriculum. Yikes. If I had continued down that track it would never have seen the light of day. So I decided to limit this to Cline's current curriculum only.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   As it is, with my sometimes flighty writing habits, "oh look, something shiny", I had to discipline myself to finish writing this. I was distracted by oh so many nice writing ideas and you'll notice the blog was pretty quiet last week. This post is why. It's a tome.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I think a lot of FIMBY readers will find this interesting becauseClineis definitely out of the elementary years and we all wonder (at least I did), "what does homeschooling look like as kids get older?" Well, I'm going to share with you what it looks like in our home, for one of our children.
                  </p>
                  <p class="rtecenter" style="">
                   <img alt="junior high homeschooling" src="/sites/fimby.tougas.net/files/images/2012-09-21T10_01_15.jpg"/>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Just a quick note on that.
                   <strong>
                    This is what works for us, given our family culture, long term homeschool vision, Cline's unique interests and strengths.
                   </strong>
                   This isCline's curriculum, tailored for
                   <em>
                    her
                   </em>
                   needs within the context of
                   <em>
                    our
                   </em>
                   family life. Your middle schooler/emerging high schooler curriculum will look different.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   And just like everything we do, this curriculum is a
                   <em>
                    living work in progress
                   </em>
                   . The plan, the curriculum, the resources - they serve our needs. Not the other way around. We are continually tweaking and adapting.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Our two youngest are in the late elementary years and on a very similar track (with lots of room for individual interests) in terms of developmental growth.Clineis in a different stage all together. Even so, there is a lot of cross over and shared learning in our home. How can there not be? Learning, especially in a non-competitive homeschool environment is a collaborative, shared experience.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   There's a lot in this post. And reading posts like this on other blogs can scare me. I get overwhelmed thinking about all the things different families appear to do. So before you get that way reading mine, let me say this:
                   <strong>
                    This is an overview of all the pieces toCline's homeschool puzzle. And the puzzle gets put together slowly over time.
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   There are many elements to this curriculum, because it's a complete course of study. We don't do those elements every day or even every week. So don't panic. Our home is pretty laid back and a visitor might even ask, "when do you guys actually home 'school'?" Trust me, the pieces of the puzzle I discuss in this post are not as overwhelming as they look at first glance.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   All that to say there's a lot in this post. A lot of resources and links. I hope this will help you in your own homeschool journey. But please take it for what it is - our journey.
                  </p>
                  <h3>
                   Definitions
                  </h3>
                  <h4>
                   Curriculum
                  </h4>
                  <p>
                   I define curriculum as a course of study. See
                   <a href="/elementary-homeschool-curriculum-series">
                    this post
                   </a>
                   for more on that. For the purposes of this post I am talking about curriculum in broadest terms - the complete course of study for the term or entire year. I will not be going into too much specifics but instead giving an overview.
                  </p>
                  <h4>
                   Term
                  </h4>
                  <p>
                   I plan our homeschool routine around three terms: Fall, Winter and Spring/Summer. We are currently in the fall term. The beginning of the fall term is when I do the majority of my planning for all three terms but everything is subject to change.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   We'll start things this term that will carry over into the next and other items we'll drop all together when we realize they aren't a good fit for us. Other learning activities, like Math, will continue regardless of the term - the thing that might change is the method of teaching.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I have certain goals for each term but mostly the time frames are fluid and simply serve as a framework for when I re-evaluate our resources and progress.

                  <h3 id="science-inspiration">
                   Science
                  </h3>
                  <p>
                   I had thought I would write a whole post on this topic. That is not going to happen. I just don't have the time. So I'll attempt to condense it here.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   We don't teach elementary science. Not because we don't value science - hardly! Damien is a civil engineer and computer programmer. The focus of my Education degree was biology. Science plays a huge part in how we understand the world and make the decisions we do (i.e.: why we eat mostly plants).
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I just don't think you need to actively, with lesson plans and assignments,
                   <em>
                    teach
                   </em>
                   science principles to young children (unless you want to and they love it!). I think my young children would have loved more science instruction and parent-led experiments but I didn't have the time to do that.
                  </p>
                  <p class="rtecenter" style="">
                   <img alt="the story of science" src="/sites/fimby.tougas.net/files/images/2012-09-14T15_01_58.jpg"/>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Our
                   <a href="http://simplehomeschool.net/elementary-science/">
                    approach for the elementary years
                   </a>
                   was to lay a foundation for future science study with hands-ons exploration, child-led experimenting, lots of reading, and asking questions.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Now it's time to take that deeper, but how?
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I am not in favor of a textbook approach to science, unless our young adults are agreeable to that.
                   <strong>
                    I truly want to inspire our kids to want to study science.
                   </strong>
                   I'm figuring this out as I go.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   What we're doing this term (and probably into next) is exploring the history of science and foundational scientific principles. We're using
                   <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1588341607/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1588341607&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=fimby-20">
                    The Story of Science: Aristotle Leads the Way
                   </a>
                   and ifClinelikes this format we'll follow up with the other books in the series. (We only use the book you see pictured, not the teacher or student guide.)
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   We have discussions around these ideas and do follow up reading and instruction. I'd like to also bring in science biographies and stories. Finding science biographies that are both engaging and appropriate for young adults is a bit challenging. Recommends anyone?
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   We are using
                   <a href="http://www.khanacademy.org/">
                    Khan Academy
                   </a>
                   to help teach the scientific principles that arise from these studies. AndClineuses Khan independently to learn about whatever interests her.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Do you see where I'm going with this?
                   <strong>
                    Our goal is to inspire our young adults to study science.
                   </strong>
                   Because science is important for life and for future scholastic endeavors (i.e.: college). But
                   <em>
                    we want them to want
                   </em>
                   to study science. And then we pull in the teaching resources we need to teach the science they want learn.

                  <h3>
                   Technology Literacy
                  </h3>
                  <p>
                   This relates to science but is definitely distinct and on its own in our estimation. The way we see it - as workers in the knowledge and technology economy -
                   <strong>
                    understanding and using technology is the new literacy
                   </strong>
                   . We think being able to access and use technology will separate the haves from the have nots in the future, if this isn't true already. We live in an increasingly technologically complex world and we have to prepare our children for this reality.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Some people are technology generalists, simply because of how they are wired. Damien is like this. Other people use technology to support and develop their other intelligences. For example, our son plays around with a (challenging, though he doesn't know it) computer drawing program to expand his drawing skill and explore new artistic mediums.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This is a post about my emerging-high schooler so I'm not going to discuss technology for younger kids, except to say I believe early childhood should be rooted in nature and hands-on activities. However, I don't have a hard and fast philosophical stance against using technology in the earlier years, I just think you need to limit it.
                  </p>
                  <p class="rtecenter" style="">
                   <img alt="homeschool math" src="/sites/fimby.tougas.net/files/images/2012-10-04T08_48_28.jpg"/>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   We recently did a simple personality and intelligence test for each of our kids and not surprisingly this was included inCline's assessment:
                  </p>
                  <blockquote>
                   "Logical-mathematical learners likeClinequantify or conceptualize information in order to process it. These kids naturally excel in mathematics, chess, computer programming, reasoning capabilities, abstract patterns of recognition, scientific thinking and investigation, and the ability to perform complex calculations."
                  </blockquote>
                  <p>
                   Damien and I had already come to this conclusion on our observations and introduced computer programming one year ago intoCline's curriculum.Clineworked on two
                   <a href="/Celine-learns-to-program">
                    different
                   </a>
                   <a href="http://www.udacity.com/">
                    courses
                   </a>
                   until she lost interest and Damien's schedule got too busy to be as involved as she needed. Speaking of that, Damien is responsible for this part of our childrens' learning. This is well outside of my areas of expertise or even interest.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This term we have no specific programming lessons.Clinegot her own MacBook this fall and Damien has been teaching her networking and other stuff I have no clue about. All I know is that when I have a computer problem I now have two people in the house I can ask for help!
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Damien will be starting Lego Robotics with all the kids in the winter term.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    Our goal with technology literacy is that our children grow to be comfortable using technology (to not be afraid of change and new ideas) and know how to use specific technologies to their best advantage. This will be different for each of our children based on their unique intelligence and gifts.
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   (And that's my post on technology literacy wrapped up in this curriculum post.)

                  <h3>
                   Writing
                  </h3>
                  <p>
                   Writing has been one of the most challenging aspects ofCline's curriculum throughout her entire homeschooling "career".Clineis a naturally reticent person, and doesn't have a strong desire to express herself in writing and I believe in inspired and interest-led writing. So that puts
                   <strong>
                    the responsibility on me to inspire my daughter to writing vs. requiring her to sit down and learn grammar, spelling, etc
                   </strong>
                   . How do I teach these important skills? Through the inspired writing. How do I inspire my daughter to write? Well, that's what I'm trying to figure out!
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I haven't worried a whole lot about this in her younger years because she has always been a competent reader with a vast vocabulary and I know it's all "in there".Clinehas demonstrated she knows how a sentence should sound (grammar) and how words look (spelling).
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   She even has lovely handwriting from her early years handwriting practice. And now with a dedicated computer at her disposal, handwriting is totally optional. The knowledge and tools are all in place. I'm trying to piece it all together in a way that is naturally motivating forCline.
                  </p>
                  <p class="rtecenter" style="">
                   <img alt="brave writer planning" src="/sites/fimby.tougas.net/files/images/2012-03-23T12_04_44.jpg"/>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This part ofCline's curriculum is very personal and individualized toCline's personality and interests so I'm not going into great detail except to say I loosely follow the principles from the
                   <a href="http://www.bravewriter.com/">
                    The Brave Writer
                   </a>
                   resources, specifically
                   <a href="http://www.bravewriter.com/program/home-study-courses/the-writers-jungle/">
                    The Writer's Jungle
                   </a>
                   , as geared toward the
                   <em>
                    Transition to Ownership
                   </em>
                   phase of writing.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Another resource I use for inspiration is Patricia Zaballos'
                   <a href="http://patriciazaballos.com/writing-with-kids/">
                    writing with kids
                   </a>
                   .
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I am considering an on-line
                   <a href="http://www.bravewriter.com/program/online-classes/class-list/kidswrite-intermediate/">
                    Brave Writer course
                   </a>
                   in the winter term to help kick startCline's high school (gulp) writing instruction and experience.
                  </p>
                  <h3 class="rtecenter" style="">
                   <strong>
                    ~~~
                   </strong>
                  </h3>
                  <p>
                   I had hoped to include with this post:
                  </p>
                  <ol>
                   <li>
                    <a href="/winter-2013-schedule">
                     The schedule
                    </a>
                    - how we structure our days, months and long term planning to meet our goals.
                   </li>
                   <li>
                    Measuring progress, making evaluations and assessments.
                   </li>
                  </ol>
                  <p>
                   I think I need to write a book.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   We'll leave it at this for today.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I invite your comments, especially if you have resources and recommends for interest-led emerging high schooler resources! I am also available for
                   <a href="/homeschool-coaching">
                    coaching
                   </a>
                   around your specific homeschooling concerns.
                  </p>

                      <br/>
                      <em>
                       This is the natural progression of interest-led learning.
                      </em>
                      The childhood years are spent mostly in play and exploration growing a love of learning, this builds the base for more scholarly young adult years (that's the really short version - I direct you to
                      <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0967124646/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=fimby-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0967124646">

                      <br/>
                      There is still freedom but it's different. The freedom now is expressed this way - "who do you want to be and how do we support that?" She is free to grow on her own path, but doing so is hard work. When children are younger the freedom looks different. It looks honestly, like lots of free time (though there is a lot of learning going on).
                      <br/>
                      I am available to discuss this more in
                      <a href="http://fimby.tougas.net/homeschool-coaching">
                       coaching

                    <p>
                     Oh, I love these kinds of posts!!  So many new courses/books/ideas are brought to my attention.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     If the book club that you are organizing this winter is open to the public, I would love to have my daughter join.  She is 11 and is a voracious reader as well.  She choses, what she reads and when I find a book she might like, she looks at the book and then writes the title/author on her list of books she wants to read and when she finishes the current book she is reading goes to the library and check out a book on her list.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I have just started, in fact, we meet this Friday, a homeschool writing group.  It is initially just to get the ideas and words to flow from mind to paper.  Initially, we aren't going to set word limits, correct grammar etc.  The kids will be given a set time to write.  When the time is up,  they all read what they wrote.  After a few meetings we will have the critique each other.  Us moms won't be putting any pressure on them to write etc.  They will do that themselves.  No one is going to want to be the one that doesn't write anything  or write the least amount(or at least that is what we are hoping), so in a sense they will create their own pressure.
                     <br/>
                     For our first meeting we are going to have the kids pair up and work together on writing a short story together.  Each weeks we will have them do something else that involves writing, like writing a newspaper article, the next week, have them all write a story using the same 3 or 5 (you decide the number)  words in thier stories etc.  Hopefully, we can keep it varied and fun and at the same time find something everyone is interested in.  Maybe, we will have the all corrobrate and write a play at the last few meetings.  Just some ideas we have been throwing around.  Maybe something you can do, with some homeschool kids in your area.  Our group currently ranges from 5 years-13 years old.  There are so many different things we can do, we are excited to get started.
                    </p>

                   <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                    <p>
                     I didn't homeschool my child who is now 19 and a (public) high school graduate, mainly because as a single parent with full-time outside work, it seemed impossible. However, I vowed that I would not "give over" education entirely to the school system. I'm pleased to find that my system was not unlike yours - I listed values I wanted to pass on, and followed my child's interests, and gave extra support to certain subjects when they were most needed (such as friendship, volunteering, or life skills like cooking and money management). I also supported (through $ and driving and chaperoning) art and music activities, as well as doing them together at home. We were always quite keen on math and science through news stories and museum exhibits. So I find your methods are not just schooling...they're life building!
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Lately I read a terrific young adult science biography, Charles and Emma: The Darwins' Leap of Faith, by Deborah Heiligman, about Charles Darwin's marriage and the conflict in his home between science and religion. I found the book approached the issues with respect for all, as did Charles and Emma themselves!
                    </p>

                    <p>
                     This is wonderful! Here are a few books (with history/science undertones) I HIGHLY recommend (I have three children in public high school):
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Science
                     <br/>
                     Charles and Emma: The Darwins' Leap of Faith, by Deborah Heiligman
                     <br/>
                     State of Wonder by Ann Patchett
                     <br/>
                     Animals in Translation: Using the Mysteries of Autism to Decode Animal Behavior by Temple Grandin
                     <br/>
                     Like a Thorn (originally in french, this one is more psychology--and is fairly disturbing--it is about a girl with OCD) by Clara Videl
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     History/Social Studies
                     <br/>
                     The Poisonwood Bible (I'd read this yourself first--there are some depictions of faith which some may feel uncomfortable with. That being said, it is one of my middle daughter's favorite books and has sparked many interesting discussions in our house.) by Barbara Kingsolver
                     <br/>
                     Animal Dreams by Barbara Kingsolver (accompanied by discussion of Native American traditions)
                     <br/>
                     American Born Chinese by Gene Yang
                     <br/>
                     Persepolis by Marjane Satrapi
                     <br/>
                     The Lacuna by Barbara Kingsolver
                     <br/>
                     The Kite Runner (upsetting) by Khaled Hosseini
                     <br/>
                     A Lesson Before Dying by Earnest Gaines
                     <br/>
                     The Help by Kathryn Stockett
                     <br/>
                     Revolution by Jennifer Donnelly
                     <br/>
                     The Book Thief by Markus Zusak
                     <br/>
                     Middlesex by Jeffrey Eugenides (discussions of sex, genetics also in here)
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     also Drug Education (perhaps you're not worried about this with your circumstances?)
                     <br/>
                     Imperfect Birds by Anne Lamott (she also wrote an amazing book on writing: Bird by Bird...and on faith: Traveling Mercies)
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Also, a writing project that my older daughter loved (in eighth grade) was a research based writing project in which she chose books written by authors with "good" (well respect and enjoyable to her) writing in the genre she chose to write in (poetry, nonfiction, analytical...). She chose to write an opinion-based essay and loved her reading. My children have also enjoyed writing (simulation!) articles for local papers. Perhaps she'd even like blogging?
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Thank you for your insight! Lots of luck!
                    </p>
