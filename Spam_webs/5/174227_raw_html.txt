         <h2>
          Walgreens Sells Beer Yet Again!
         </h2>
         <div class="meta">
          By Blake Potolicchio | 
									June 9, 2010 |
									1 Comments |
         </div>
         <div class="addthis">
          <a class="addthis_button" href="http://addthis.com/bookmark.php?v=250&amp;username=beeruniverse">
           <img alt="Bookmark and Share" height="16" src="http://s7.addthis.com/static/btn/v2/lg-share-en.gif" style="border:0" width="125"/>
          </a>
          <script src="http://s7.addthis.com/js/250/addthis_widget.js#username=beeruniverse" type="text/javascript">
          </script>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="image left">
         <img src="http://www.beer-universe.com/images/articles/124/walgreens.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <p class="first">
         Reversing a 15 year-old ban on selling alcohol in their stores, Walgreens has recently announced they will once again offer beer and wine. According to the
         <a href="http://www.nacsonline.com/">
          National Association of Convenience Stores
         </a>
         , nearly
         <strong>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           80%
          </span>
         </strong>
         of convenience stores sell beer, accounting for nearly
         <strong>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           one-third
          </span>
         </strong>
         of all beer purchased in the United States or approximately
         <strong>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           2 billion gallons
          </span>
         </strong>
         of beer a year. Beer and wine ranked 5
         <sup>
          th
         </sup>
         among all in-store categories sold in 2008, representing a potential single digit sales increase to most bottom lines. So youre asking yourselves, whats the difference in Walgreens and my local grocery store selling beer?
         <strong>
          Answer:
         </strong>
         longer hours of operation for convenience stores and refrigerated space dedicated to the beverage as opposed to food helps pull in a higher margin of profit.
        </p>
        <p>
         The catch-22 for the Deerfield, Ill. retailer is if the offering of beer and wine, which hopefully will increase sales, will tarnish the neighborhood-friendly image that makes it a one-stop shop. As of now, Walgreens offers a limited beer and wine selection in roughly 3,100 stores out of the 7,500 they operate and plan to increase those offering to at least 5,000 stores by year-end 2010. It is a small step toward the historic 1990s Walgreens model that relied on full liquor stores to make up approximately 10% of total sales. Realistically, Walgreens understands that state legislation will inhibit its intent for nationwide rollout, but expects to work with states, such as Alabama and Georgia, in applying for licensing.
        </p>
        <p>
         Noting a fact sheet from the National Association of Convenience Stores, making proper space allocation in the cooler is essential. Because cold beer sells better, it is imperative to rely on highly distributed beer with little shelf-life. Although Walgreens has announced it plans to sell local beer and wines from smaller craft companies, Beer Universe anticipates little room for regional breweries offerings. Unfortunately for many of our craft beer fans, this bodes well for beers such as Bud and Miller and family. Locally distributed beers within a particular jurisdiction could see some traction if shelf turnover warrants sales for Walgreens.
        </p>
        <p>
         The addition of Walgreens to again sell beer and wine in most all of their stores further exemplifies the importance beer sales have on retailers bottom line. Hopefully, locally run brewers will approach their Walgreens management to lobby for space on their shelves.
        </p>
        <p>
         Your Choice. Your Beer. Drink Up.
         <br/>
         - Beer Universe
        </p>
