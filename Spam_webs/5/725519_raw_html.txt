      <div class=" hnews hentry item " id="blox-story-frame">
       <p class="story-times dtstamp">
        Posted:
        <span class="updated" title="2014-08-16T06:00:00-07:00">
         Saturday, August 16, 2014 6:00 am
        </span>
       </p>
       <p class="byline">
        <span class="bookmark hide">
         <a class="url entry-title" href="http://gazettes.com/lifestyle/craft-beer-corner-hanging-onto-summer-brews/article_5688cd56-2335-11e4-99e5-0019bb2963f4.html" rel="bookmark nofollow">
          CRAFT BEER CORNER: Hanging Onto Summer Brews
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          By Sean Smith
          <br/>
          Gazette Columnist
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="hide source-org vcard">
         <span class="org fn">
          Grunion Gazette
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
       <div class="entry-content" id="blox-story-text">
        <div class="container" id="paging_container">
         <div class="content">
          <span class="first-paragraph">
           <span class="paragraph-0">
            <p>
             Summer is winding down, vacations are coming to end, kids are returning to school, and summer beer is leaving the shelves.
            </p>
           </span>
          </span>
          <span class="paragraph-1">
           <p>
            Yes, I know that Southern California has hot spells that last well into October, and that there is nothing better than beating the heat with a cold, refreshing beer, but like holiday creep (Christmas displays in August), marketers and brewers trying to satisfy a national audience are quick to replace summer beers with fall flavors. Ive already seen the release of some pumpkin beers, dark ales and fresh hopped beers.
           </p>
          </span>
          <div class="p402_hide no-images no-quotes no-pagination view-standard">
           <div id="in-story">
            <div class="tncms-region-ads" id="tncms-region-ads-in-story">
             <script src="http://bloximages.chicago2.vip.townnews.com/gazettes.com/content/tncms/ads/in-story/in-story1.js?_dc=1435759280" type="text/javascript">
             </script>
             <script type="text/javascript">
              oTNCMS_Ad.setRelative();
oTNCMS_Ad.show(null, null, 'http://bloximages.chicago2.vip.townnews.com/gazettes.com');
             </script>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           If, like me, youre looking to extend your summer, it might be time to hit up your favorite retailer and squirrel away some before its gone. In the spirit of hoarding summer gold, I offer you my five favorite, still widely available, summer beers that can be found in Long Beach.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            21st Amendment (San Francisco), Hell or High Watermelon, American wheat:
           </strong>
           I dont normally like fruit in my beer, but for Hell or High Watermelon I make an exception. Unlike many fruit beers, the watermelon in this beer complements rather than overpowers it. At its base, Hell or High Watermelon is a light American wheat beer, golden in color, refreshing flavors, with a crisp wheat finish. The brewers load up the secondary fermenters with fresh watermelon, making it subtle but absolutely pleasant. Drinking this beer is like sitting at a summer picnic, beer in hand, while munching on a fresh slice of watermelon. Nothing really says summer more.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Avery (Colorado), Karma, Belgian pale ale:
           </strong>
           I learned the pleasures of Belgian beers while traveling through Belgium and France. Karma brings those memories of spending warm sunny days people watching on cafe patios with a light Belgian pale ale in hand. A farmhouse-style pale ale, Karma is fruity and estery on the nose, medium bodied with a bit of Belgian funk on the finish. This a perfect beer for enjoying outside with grilled meats or nice bowl of moules frites.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Deschutes (Oregon), Twilight, American pale ale:
           </strong>
           A crisp clean American pale ale that wont overwhelm the palate or overburden with alcohol, but packs enough hop flavor, aroma and bitterness to keep things interesting. At 5% abv, this is a great session beer that goes well with summer picnics or a BBQ.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Dogfish Head (Delaware), Festina Peche, Berliner Weisse:
           </strong>
           The most interesting and perhaps challenging beer on my list. Festina Peche is sour, fermented with lactic bacterias that to many tastes of beer gone bad. But for those of us who love sour beers, the flavors are reminiscent of a very dry cider or even Champagne. Festina Peche adds to that flavor profile fresh peaches, giving the beer a decidedly fruity note and aroma, making for a wonderfully light and refreshing experience. This is a perfect aperitif to pair nicely with summer fruit and cheese.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Uinta Brewing (Utah), Sumr, blonde ale:
           </strong>
           Another light (both in body and alcohol) and refreshing citrusy ale. This is the beer to share with those who normally drink one of the mass-market beers. Light enough to make them happy, but delicious enough to satisfy finicky beer snobs. In other words a perfect party beer to serve in mixed company. Sumr is also an easy beer to pair with food. It works with all your summer favorites, from grilled meats and fish to summer salads and spicy dishes.
          </p>
          <p>
           So, even though the calendar and those wily marketers say that summer is nearly over, there is no reason we cant enjoy whats left of it. I urge you all to get out there and pick up the last bits of summer while they last.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <em>
             Sean Smith is an award winning homebrewer, historian and writer. You can find more of his writing
             <a href="http://atJustAnotherBeerBlog.com">
              atJustAnotherBeerBlog.com
             </a>
             .
            </em>
           </strong>
          </p>
