                           <p>
                            <a class="colorbox colorbox-insert-image" href="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/1552-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-1.jpg" rel="gallery-all" title="">
                             <img alt="" class="image-large-728" src="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/styles/large_728/public/1552-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-1.jpg" title=""/>
                            </a>
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            We love the simple clean lines of these microwavable neck and lap/back heating pads. After under a minute in the microwave, you'll get up to an hour of safe, warm heat without the dangers inherent in electric heating pads: burns, fires and electrical shock. Electric heating pad cautions actually read:
                            <em>
                             Never use in a situation where you could fall asleep
                            </em>
                            . Hmmm... often falling asleep is my goal. These pads are not difficult to make, and are a useful gift for most any adult. They're good for muscle aches or when you feel a chill and don't want to turn up the thermostat. You can also freeze them for a pleasant cooling effect. Although, just typing that made me shiver. Add a drop of essential oil if you'd like a little aromatherapy.
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            <!--break-->
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            Before you begin, measure the interior of your microwave oven just to be sure the lap/back pad will fit when folded in half (about 8" x 12"). Most microwaves will easily accommodate something of this size, but there are a few older and space-saver models that may require you to slightly reduce the size of the pad.
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            <a class="colorbox colorbox-insert-image" href="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/1552-Heating_Pad-3.jpg" rel="gallery-all" title="">
                             <img alt="" class="image-large-728" src="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/styles/large_728/public/1552-Heating_Pad-3.jpg" title=""/>
                            </a>
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            We show you dimensions and supplies for both a neck pad (22" x 8" flat) and a slightly larger lap/back pad (16" x 12" flat).
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            <a class="colorbox colorbox-insert-image" href="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/1552-Heating_Pad-4.jpg" rel="gallery-all" title="">
                             <img alt="" class="image-large-728" src="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/styles/large_728/public/1552-Heating_Pad-4.jpg" title=""/>
                            </a>
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            To give your heating pads as a gift, fold them up and tie them with a piece of natural twill tape. Pretty and practical!
                           </p>
                           <h1>
                            Sewing Tools You Need
                           </h1>
                           <ul>
                            <li>
                             Any Sewing Machine (we recommend the
                             <a href="http://content.janome.com/index.cfm/Machines/Sewing-Quilting/HD3000" target="_blank" title="Janome HD3000">
                              Janome HD3000
                             </a>
                             )
                            </li>
                           </ul>
                           <h1>
                            Fabric and Other Supplies
                           </h1>
                           <p>
                            This project is a great for fabric scraps. You can use flannel, as well as quilting or dcor-weight cotton. Just don't use anything with metal threads (unless you want a fireworks display in your microwave). We chose cotton ticking for its clean, fresh look and tight weave. Ticking is an old timefabric that was historically used for covering pillows and mattresses because its tight weave kept feathers, horse hair, and other fillers in place. Today, ticking has once again become trendy for design accessories like throw pillows, as well as for upholstery. There is something classically soothing about those woven stripes.
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            Ticking is available almost everywhere fabric is sold. We found good selections at both
                            <a href="http://www.fabric.com/SearchResults2.aspx?Source=Header&amp;SearchText=ticking&amp;CategoryID=1d5f47dc-9991-4088-93f3-26a376046a5e" target="_blank" title="fabric at fabric.com">
                             Fabric.com
                            </a>
                            and
                            <a href="http://www.fatquartershop.com/catalogsearch/result/?q=ticking" target="_blank" title="fabric at FQS">
                             Fat Quarter Shop
                            </a>
                            . The ticking we used is not the industrial-strength mattress variety, but a dcor-weight that has a soft drape after washing. As we always suggest,
                            <a href="http://www.sew4home.com/tips-resources/sewing-tips-tricks/simply-color-vanessa-christenson-preshrinking-101" title="pre shrink">
                             pre-wash
                            </a>
                            and press your fabric before beginning this and other projects.
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            <a class="colorbox colorbox-insert-image" href="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/1552-Ingredients-1.jpg" rel="gallery-all" title="">
                             <img alt="" class="image-large-728" src="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/styles/large_728/public/1552-Ingredients-1.jpg" title=""/>
                            </a>
                           </p>
                           <h2>
                            Neck Pad
                           </h2>
                           <p>
                            <img alt="Image" height="634" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-3.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Image" width="728"/>
                           </p>
                           <ul>
                            <li>
                              yard of 44-45" wide cotton ticking or similar;
                             <em>
                              we used a red stripe
                             </em>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                              yard of 45"+ soft fleece or similar
                             <em>
                              ; we used winter white
                             </em>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             One package of jumbo rick rack in red:
                             <em>
                              this is optional, as is the size of rick rack you use
                             </em>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             7 cups of uncooked rice for filler
                             <br/>
                             NOTE:
                             <em>
                              Corn is another option for a filler, just don't use the popcorn variety... feed corn is a economical option.
                             </em>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             <em>
                             </em>
                             All purpose thread to match fabric;
                             <i>
                              we used natural
                             </i>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             <i>
                             </i>
                             See-through ruler
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Fabric pen or pencil
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Iron and ironing board
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Scissors or rotary cutter and mat
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Seam gauge
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Seam ripper
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Straight pins
                            </li>

                             Optional: Essential oil
                             <em>
                              (we added a drop of vanilla spice or you can add a few lavender buds).
                             </em>
                             You should be able to easily find a selection of essential oils in the organic section of most grocery stores.
                            </li>
                           </ul>
                           <h2>
                            Lap/Back Pad
                           </h2>
                           <p>
                            <img alt="Image" height="332" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-4.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Image" width="728"/>
                           </p>
                           <ul>
                            <li>
                             yard of
                             <em>
                              <span style="font-style: normal;">
                               44-45" wide cotton ticking or similar;
                              </span>
                              <em>
                               we used a
                              </em>
                              classic navy stripe
                             </em>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                              yard of 45"+ soft fleece or similar

                              ; we used winter white

                           <h1>
                            Getting Started
                           </h1>
                           <h2>
                            <strong>
                             Neck Pad
                            </strong>
                           </h2>
                           <ol>
                            <li>
                             Cut ONE 9" x 23" rectangle from the red ticking.
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Cut ONE 9" x 23" rectangle from the fleece.
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Cut TWO 25" lengths of the red jumbo rick rack.
                            </li>
                           </ol>
                           <h2>
                            Lap/Back Pad
                           </h2>
                           <ol>
                            <li>
                             Cut ONE 13" x 17" rectangle from the navy ticking.
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Cut ONE 13" x 17" rectangle from the fleece.
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Cut TWO 18" lengths of the navy jumbo rick rack.
                            </li>
                           </ol>
                           <ol>
                           </ol>
                           <h1>
                            At Your Sewing Machine
                           </h1>
                           <p>
                            From here on, the instructions are the same for both the neck pad and the lap/back pads (except as noted). We use " seams throughout.
                           </p>
                           <ol>
                            <li>
                             If you want to add a label as we did, add that first to the center of one of the compartments. It should be about 2" from the bottom at a minimum.
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Pin the rick rack to the two long sides, centering it on the " seam line (ie. the center of the rick rack should be" from the raw edge). Machine baste in place.
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-5_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="384" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-5.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Pin the ticking and fleece rectangles right sides together. Sew all the way around leaving about a 3" opening on one end for turning and adding rice. Remember to pivot at the corners and to lock your seam at either side of the 3" opening.
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-6_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="328" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-6.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Trim the three sides without the opening, and
                             <a href="http://www.sew4home.com/tips-resources/sewing-tips-tricks/are-you-stitching-clipping-corners-correctly" title="corners">
                              clip the corners
                             </a>
                             .
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-8_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="344" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-8.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Turn right-side out and press, folding in the raw edges of the opening so they are flush with the sewn seam. Your rick rack should pop out along each edge.
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-16_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="344" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-16.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Find the center of the neck pad and and mark with a pin. From the center, measure 5" to either side and mark both of these points with a pin. These are the marks for the four rice compartments.
                             <br/>
                             NOTE:
                             <em>
                              <em>
                               For the lap/back pad, measure just 4" to either side of the center pin.
                              </em>
                             </em>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Adding the essential oil is an option, but if you want to add it, now is the time. I used ONE drop for each pad and my whole sewing room smells wonderfully like vanilla spice.
                             <em>
                             </em>
                             If you're using essential oils, put the rice in a large bowl, add the oil and stir. One drop goes a loooooog way.
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-7_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="344" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-7.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Add rice to fill the first compartment. You want to be able to conform the pad to your neck, so don't overfill.We used about 1 cup for each neck pad compartment and1 cupfor each lap/back compartment.I made a simple paper funnel to make pouring rice easier (a regular plastic funnel tends to clog). Hold the pad up vertically so the rice falls to the back of the first compartment you marked earlier, giving you a clear path to sew the compartment closed without sewing through any grains of rice.
                             <br/>
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-9_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="344" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-9.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Sew a vertical line of stitching at the first compartment line.If you used ticking, you have a line to follow from pin point to pin point. If not, you can use an erasable fabric pen to draw a straight line between the two points.Pin through both layers along the line and sew.Stop from time to time, with the needle in the down position, and shift the rice towards its compartment to keep the presser foot area clear. It's a bit time-consuming, but not difficult.
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox colorbox-insert-image" href="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-17.jpg" rel="gallery-all" title="">
                              <img alt="" class="image-medium-500" src="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/styles/medium_500/public/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-17.jpg" title=""/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             The first compartment should look about like this:
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-10_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="344" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-10.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             Repeat for the next three compartments.
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-11_b.jpg">
                              <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="344" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-11.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="500"/>
                             </a>
                            </li>
                            <li>
                             When the last compartment is filled with rice, hand sew the the opening closed with a tightly-spaced
                             <a href="http://www.sew4home.com/tips-resources/sewing-tips-tricks/hand-sewing-basics" title="hand sewing basics">
                              slip stitch
                             </a>
                             .
                             <br/>
                             <a class="colorbox colorbox-insert-image" href="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-12_b.jpg" rel="gallery-all" title="">
                              <img alt="" class="image-medium-500" src="http://www.sew4home.com/sites/default/files/styles/medium_500/public/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-12_b.jpg" title=""/>
                             </a>
                             <br/>
                             <a href="/tips-resources/sewing-tips-tricks/hand-sewing-basics">
                             </a>
                            </li>
                           </ol>
                           <h1>
                            Using Your Pads
                           </h1>
                           <p>
                            Since microwaves vary in how quickly they heat, start with 1 minute and see how it feels. The microwave we tested required about 2 minutes to achieve the desired toasty warmth. DO NOT overheat, you can scorch the rice. Once you know how long it takes your microwave to heat your pad, you can set that time for future uses.
                            <em>
                             Do not attempt heat the pad in a conventional oven.
                            </em>
                           </p>
                           <p>
                            <em>
                            </em>
                            You can also use this tutorial to make a simple cover for a heating pad. To do this, measure the heating pad and cut two rectangles from flannel or quilting cotton. Cut the rectangles two inches wider and five inches longer than the finished outside dimensions of the pad. With right sides together, sew along both long sides and across the bottom. Serge or zig zag the raw edges. On the top end, fold under " and press. Fold under an addition 1" to form a hem. Edgestitch the hem in place. You can add a ribbon tie or two if you'd like to hold the cover closed on the end after you slip the heating pad into place.
                            <br/>
                            <a class="colorbox" href="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-13_b.jpg">
                             <img alt="Click to Enlarge" height="500" src="/sites/sewmk.mervideo.com/files/images/articles/0943-Neck-lap-back_heating-pad-13.jpg" style="margin-top: 0px; margin-left: 0px; margin-bottom: 15px; margin-right: 0px;" title="Click to Enlarge" width="728"/>
                            </a>
                           </p>
