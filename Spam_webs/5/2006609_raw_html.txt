     <p>
      The
      <b>
       freedom to roam
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       everyman's right
      </b>
      is the general public's right to access certain public or privately owned land for
      <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">
       recreation
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">
       exercise
      </a>
      . The right is sometimes called the
      <b>
       right of public access to the wilderness
      </b>
      or the
      <b>
       right to roam
      </b>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_different_countries">
       In different countries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In different countries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
       Wales
      </a>
      public access rights apply to certain categories of mainly uncultivated landspecifically "mountain,
      <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">
       moor
      </a>
      , heath, down and registered common land." Developed land, gardens and certain other areas are specifically excluded from the right of access. Agricultural land is accessible if it falls within one of the categories described above. Most publicly owned forests have a similar right of access by virtue of a voluntary dedication made by the Forestry Commission. People exercising the right of access have certain duties to respect other people's rights to manage the land, and to protect nature.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">
       Nordic countries
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
       Finland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
       Iceland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
       Norway
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      as well as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltic_countries" title="Baltic countries">
       Baltic countries
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">
       Estonia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">
       Latvia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">
       Lithuania
      </a>
      the freedom to roam may take the form of general public rights which are sometimes codified in law. The access is ancient in parts of
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">
       Northern Europe
      </a>
      and has been regarded as sufficiently basic that it was not formalised in law until modern times. However, the right usually does not include any substantial economic exploitation, such as hunting or logging, or disruptive activities, such as making fires and driving offroad vehicles.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many tropical countries such as
      <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">
       Madagascar
      </a>
      have historic policies of open access to forest or wilderness areas. This practice in the
      <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">
       rainforests
      </a>
      of eastern Madagascar and in the
      <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_dry_deciduous_forests" title="Madagascar dry deciduous forests">
       Madagascar dry deciduous forests
      </a>
      has led to considerable destruction of habitat, much of which is effectively irreversible.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Nordic_countries">
       In the Nordic countries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In the Nordic countries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ancient traces provide evidence of the freedom to roam in many European countries, suggesting such a freedom was once a common norm. Today, the right to roam has survived in perhaps its purest form in Estonia, Finland, Iceland, Norway and Sweden. Here the right has been won through practice over hundreds of years
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and it is not known when it changed from mere 'common practice' to become a commonly recognised right. A possible explanation as to why the right has survived mainly in these four countries is that
      <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">
       feudalism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">
       serfdom
      </a>
      were not established there.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="source for that as explanation? (September 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Another factor is the survival of large areas of unenclosed forest. Elsewhere in
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europe
      </a>
      land was gradually
      <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">
       enclosed
      </a>
      for private use and enjoyment, with
      <a href="/wiki/Commons" title="Commons">
       commoners' rights
      </a>
      (for instance, rights to gather fuel or graze animals) largely eliminated.
     </p>
     <p>
      Today these rights underpin opportunities for outdoor recreation in several of the Nordic countries, providing the opportunity to hike across or camp on another's land (e.g. in Sweden for one or two nights), boating on someone else's waters, and picking
      <a href="/wiki/Wildflower" title="Wildflower">
       wildflowers
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">
       mushrooms
      </a>
      and berries. However, with these rights come responsibilities; that is, an obligation neither to harm, disturb, litter, nor to damage
      <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">
       wildlife
      </a>
      or crops.
     </p>
     <p>
      Access rights are most often for travel on foot. Rights to fish, hunt or take any other product are usually constrained by other customs or laws. Building a fire is often prohibited (though in Sweden and Norway fires are allowed with proper safety precautions). Making
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">
       noise
      </a>
      is discouraged. In some countries, putting up a
      <a href="/wiki/Tent" title="Tent">
       tent
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">
       forest
      </a>
      for one night is allowed, but not the use of a
      <a href="/wiki/Travel_trailer" title="Travel trailer">
       caravan
      </a>
      . Access does not extend to built up or developed land (such as
      <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">
       houses
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">
       gardens
      </a>
      ) and does not necessarily include commercial exploitation of the land. For example, workers picking berries may be legal only with the landowner's permit.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are some significant differences in the rules of different countries. In
      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      , there is a more restricted freedom to roam on privately held land. All dunes and beaches and all publicly owned forests are open to roaming. Not cultivated, un-fenced areas are open to daytime roaming irrespective of ownership status. Privately owned forest have access by roads and tracks only.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">
       Finland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Finland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Finland, the freedom to roam and related rights are called
      <i>
       "
       <span lang="fi" xml:lang="fi">
        jokamiehenoikeus
       </span>
       "
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
       Finnish
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       "
       <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv">
        allemansrtten
       </span>
       "
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
       Swedish
      </a>
      (lit. "the everyman's right"), similar to other Nordic countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      Everyone may walk, ski or cycle freely in the countryside where this does not harm the natural environment or the landowner, except in gardens or in the immediate vicinity of people's homes (yards). Fields and plantations, which may easily be harmed, may usually not be crossed except in the winter.
     </p>
     <p>
      One may stay or set up camp temporarily in the countryside, a reasonable distance from homes, pick mineral samples, wild berries, mushrooms and flowers (as long as they are not protected species). One may
      <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">
       fish with a rod and line
      </a>
      (only still waters), row, sail or use a motorboat on waterways (with certain restrictions), and swim or bathe in both inland waters and the sea. One can walk, ski and ice fish on frozen lakes, rivers and the sea. Income from selling picked berries or mushrooms is tax-free. Picking
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloudberry" title="Cloudberry">
       cloudberry
      </a>
      may be temporarily restricted
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      in parts of
      <a href="/wiki/Lapland_(Finland)" title="Lapland (Finland)">
       Lapland
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      One may not disturb others or damage property, disturb breeding birds (or their nests or young), or disturb reindeer or game animals. One may not cut down or damage living trees, or collect wood, moss or lichen on other people's property, nor may one light open fires without the landowner's permission (except in an emergency). It is acceptable, however, to use an alcohol burner, wood stove or similar device that has no hot parts touching the ground. One may not disturb the privacy of people's homes by camping too near to them or making too much noise, nor litter, drive motor vehicles off road without the landowner's permission, or fish (excluding
      <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">
       angling
      </a>
      ) or hunt without the relevant permits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the autonomous province of
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land_Islands" title="land Islands">
       land
      </a>
      the right to camp is not necessarily recognized.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-land_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-land-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-visitaland_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-visitaland-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The right is a positive right in the respect that only the government is allowed to restrict it as in the case of
      <a href="/wiki/Strict_nature_reserves_of_Finland" title="Strict nature reserves of Finland">
       strict nature reserves
      </a>
      . However, the exact definition remains mostly uncodified and based on the principle of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nulla_poena_sine_lege" title="Nulla poena sine lege">
        nulla poena sine lege
       </a>
      </i>
      (what is not illegal cannot be punished).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">
       Norway
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Norway">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Everyone in Norway enjoys the right of access to, and passage through, uncultivated land in the countryside. The right is an old
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consuetudinary" title="Consuetudinary">
       consuetudinary
      </a>
      law called the
      <i>
       allemannsrett
      </i>
      (lit. the everyman's right), that was codified in 1957 with the implementation of the Outdoor Recreation Act.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is based on respect for the countryside, and all visitors are expected to show consideration for farmers and landowners, other users and the environment. In Norway the terms
      <i>
       utmark
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       innmark
      </i>
      divide areas where the right is valid and where it is invalid or restricted. The law specifies innmark thoroughly,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and all areas not covered by this definition are defined as
      <i>
       utmark
      </i>
      , generally speaking uninhabited and uncultivated areas. Cultivated land may only be walked on when it is frozen and covered in snow.
     </p>
     <p>
      In later years the right has come under pressure particularly around the
      <a href="/wiki/Oslofjord" title="Oslofjord">
       Oslo Fjord
      </a>
      and in popular areas of Southern Norway. These areas are popular sites for holiday homes and many owners of coastal land want to restrict public access to their property. As a general rule, building and partitioning of property is prohibited in a 100-meter zone closest to the sea, but local authorities in many areas have made liberal use of their ability to grant exemptions from this rule. Even though a land owner has been permitted to build closer to the shore he can not restrict people from walking along the shore. Fences and other barriers to prevent public access are not permitted (but yet sometimes erected, resulting in heavy fines).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Canoeing" title="Canoeing">
       Canoeing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kayaking" title="Kayaking">
       kayaking
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rowing_(sport)" title="Rowing (sport)">
       rowing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">
       sailing
      </a>
      in rivers, lakes, and ocean are allowed. Motorised boats are only permitted in salt water. All waters are open for swimming - with the exception for lakes that are
      <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">
       drinking water
      </a>
      reservoirs (see for instance
      <a href="/wiki/Maridalen" title="Maridalen">
       Maridalsvannet
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Wild berry foraging is part of the right.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hunting rights belong to the landowner, and thus hunting is not included in the right of free access. In freshwater areas such as rivers and lakes, the fishing rights belong to the landowner. Regardless of who owns the land, fresh water fishing activities may only be conducted with the permission of the landowner or by those in possession of a fishing licence. Different rules apply for children under the age of 16. Children under the age of 16 have the right to fish without a licence, a right codified in 1992. This right was tried and upheld in a ruling from the Norwegian Supreme Court in 2004.
     </p>
     <p>
      In salt water areas there is free access to sports fishing using boats or from the shoreline. All fishing is subject to legislation to among other things protect biological diversity, and this legislation stipulates rules regarding the use of gear, seasons, bag or size limits and more.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sweden">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      <i>
       allemansrtten
      </i>
      (lit. "the everyman's right") is a freedom granted by the
      <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Sweden" title="Constitution of Sweden">
       Constitution of Sweden
      </a>
      . Since 1994 the Instrument of Government says that notwithstanding the right to own property "everyone shall have access to nature in accordance with
      <i>
       allemansrtten
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-regeringsformen2kap_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-regeringsformen2kap-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      What this means is not further explicated on in the constitution, and only sparsely in other legislation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NaturBB_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NaturBB-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In practice,
      <i>
       allemansrtten
      </i>
      is defined as actions that are not crimes, will not make a person liable to pay
      <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">
       damages
      </a>
      , nor can be prohibited by any authority.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As in other Nordic countries, the Swedish right to roam comes with an equal emphasis being placed upon the responsibility to look after the countryside; the maxim is "do not disturb, do not destroy".
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Allemansrtten
      </i>
      gives a person the right to access, walk, cycle, ride, ski, and camp on any landwith the exception of private gardens, the immediate vicinity of a dwelling house and land under cultivation. Restrictions apply for
      <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">
       nature reserves
      </a>
      and other protected areas. It also gives the right to pick wild flowers, mushrooms and berries (provided one knows they are not legally protected), but not to hunt in any way. Swimming in any lake and putting an unpowered boat on any water is permitted unless explicitly forbidden. Visiting beaches and walking by a shoreline is permitted, providing it is not a part of a garden or within the immediate vicinity of a residence (legally defined as the
      <i>
       hemfridszon
      </i>
      ). The
      <i>
       hemfridszon's
      </i>
      size depends on conditions but can be as large as 70 metres from an ordinary dwelling house.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NaturBB_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NaturBB-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To better protect access to water and the right to walk along beaches, it is since 1975 generally not permitted to build a new house near (generally 100 m) from a beach and/or shoreline.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Fishing remains essentially privateapart from on the biggest five lakes and the coast of the
      <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">
       Baltic Sea
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%96resund" title="resund">
       the Sound
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kattegat" title="Kattegat">
       Kattegat
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Skagerrak" title="Skagerrak">
       Skagerrak
      </a>
      . It is permitted to drive a car on a private road unless explicitly signposted otherwise. Small camp fires are generally permitted, but in some periods banned by local authorities due to wild fire risk. It is allowed to put up a tent on any uncultivated land for a night or two.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NaturUnique_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NaturUnique-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There has been some controversy on commercial use of the berry picking rights, when companies legally contract people to pick berries in the forests.
     </p>
     <p>
      Exercise of the rights is overseen by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_Administrative_Boards_of_Sweden" title="County Administrative Boards of Sweden">
       County Administrative Boards
      </a>
      which can, for example, force the removal of a fence if it obstructs access to areas that are important to the
      <i>
       allemansrtt
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NaturBB_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NaturBB-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Britain">
       In Britain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Britain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many land owners in Britain have, in the past, strongly defended their property rights. Even uncultivated and unenclosed land was formerly heavily protected in some areas, mostly to preserve the land owner's hunting or fishing rights. This in turn left the general public with little access to natural areas. Even such popular sites as
      <a href="/wiki/Chrome_Hill" title="Chrome Hill">
       Chrome Hill
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Parkhouse_Hill" title="Parkhouse Hill">
       Parkhouse Hill
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">
       Peak District
      </a>
       although of very little economic interest to the owner  had been out of access to the public, until the enactment of the
      <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">
       Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramblers%27_Association" title="Ramblers' Association">
       Ramblers' Association
      </a>
      works to increase the rights of walkers in the United Kingdom and has been a driving force behind the recent legislation increasing the public's access to the wilderness.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">
       England and Wales
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: England and Wales">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In England, after a polarised debate about the merits, rights and benefits of private landowners and public recreation, in 2000 the Government legislated to introduce a limited
      <i>
       right to roam
      </i>
      , without compensation for landowners. The
      <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">
       Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000
      </a>
      (CROW) was gradually implemented from 2000 onwards to give the general public the conditional right to walk in certain areas of the English and Welsh countryside: principally
      <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">
       downland
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">
       moorland
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heathland" title="Heathland">
       heathland
      </a>
      and coastal land.
     </p>
     <p>
      Traditionally the public could walk on established
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Footpath_(right_of_way)" title="Footpath (right of way)">
       public footpaths
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bridleways" title="Bridleways">
       bridleways
      </a>
      , on
      <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">
       common land
      </a>
      and on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshore" title="Foreshore">
       foreshore
      </a>
      , and land owners could prevent access to other areas (or charge a fee for access).
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">
       Angling
      </a>
      interests successfully lobbied for the exclusion of rivers in England and Wales from CROW, leaving other river users such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimmer" title="Swimmer">
       swimmers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canoeing" title="Canoeing">
       canoeists
      </a>
      with access restricted to less than 2% of navigable water. The
      <a href="/wiki/British_Canoe_Union" title="British Canoe Union">
       British Canoe Union
      </a>
      is running the
      <a href="/wiki/Rivers_Access_Campaign" title="Rivers Access Campaign">
       Rivers Access Campaign
      </a>
      , to highlight the level of restrictions the public face in gaining access to inland waterways in England and Wales.
     </p>
     <p>
      The new rights were introduced region by region through England and Wales, with completion in 2005. Maps showing accessible areas have been produced.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dartmoor_National_Park" title="Dartmoor National Park">
       Dartmoor National Park
      </a>
      enjoys Open Access rights which are significantly more extensive than the rest of the country for historic reasons which have been recently recodified with various legal changes following the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dartmoor_Commons_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dartmoor Commons Act (page does not exist)">
       Dartmoor Commons Act
      </a>
      (1985).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scotland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Scotland the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_Reform_(Scotland)_Act_2003" title="Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003">
       Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003
      </a>
      comprehensively codified into
      <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">
       Scots law
      </a>
      the ancient tradition of the right to universal access to the land in Scotland. The act specifically establishes a right to be on land for recreational, educational and certain other purposes and a right to cross land. The rights exist only if they are exercised responsibly, as specified in the
      <a href="/wiki/The_Country_Code#The_Scottish_Outdoor_Access_Code" title="The Country Code">
       Scottish Outdoor Access Code
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Access rights apply to any non-motorised activities, including walking, cycling, horse-riding and wild camping. They also allow access on inland water for canoeing, rowing, sailing and swimming. The rights confirmed in the Scottish legislation are greater than the limited rights of access created in England and
      <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
       Wales
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">
       Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000
      </a>
      (CRoW).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_rest_of_Europe">
       In the rest of Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In the rest of Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Estonia">
       Estonia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Estonia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Estonia, it is permitted to access natural and cultural landscapes on foot, by bicycle, skis, boat or on horseback.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Private property may be accessed from sunrise to sundown. If the private property is fenced or posted against trespassing, the permission of the owner is required to proceed. Land owners may not block access to land, roads or bodies of water that are public or designated for public use, including ice and shore paths.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      All bodies of water that are public or designated for public use have public shore paths that are up to 4 m wide. The shore path along a navigable body of water may extend to a distance of 10 m of the water line. The owner may not close this path even if the private property is posted or marked with no-trespassing signs. Grazing areas and other enclosed areas along the shore paths must have stiles. Ponds with no outlet located entirely on the land of one land owner and lakes smaller than five hectares located on land belonging to more than one land owner shall not be in public use. Permission from the landowner is required to access such bodies of water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Neither do bodies of water protected as sources of drinking water or which are in use by aquaculture or are in other special use have a shore path. All of the rights and responsibilities regarding humans interaction with nature are collectively termed everymans right. Everymans right does not pertain to the organizing of sporting events or other public events in open country. To organize these, the permission of the landowners or other possessors of land, and if necessary, of the local government, must be sought.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus_14-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Iga.C3.BChe.C3.B5igus-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The following is permitted in nature:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       accessing areas by foot, on bicycle, skis, boat or horseback in all places not prohibited on the basis of law(s);
      </li>
      <li>
       being present in any area where access is permitted;
      </li>
      <li>
       gathering wild berries, mushrooms, flowers, medicinal plants, hazelnuts and other natural products not under nature protection;
      </li>
      <li>
       fishing bodies of water that are public or designated for public use with a simple hand line.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The following is prohibited:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       accessing the immediate proximity of a persons yard, plantations, apiaries, sown crops, grain field and other cropland where damage is thereby incurred by the owner;
      </li>
      <li>
       lighting fires and camping without permission from the land owner or possessor;
      </li>
      <li>
       hunting and fishing without relevant license, except for simple hand line;
      </li>
      <li>
       injuring trees and bushes;
      </li>
      <li>
       disrupting the peace of local inhabitants;
      </li>
      <li>
       damaging the habitats and nests of forest animals and birds, gathering or removing their eggs, or otherwise doing harm to them;
      </li>
      <li>
       damaging nature protection objects and protected species;
      </li>
      <li>
       using motor vehicles where prohibited;
      </li>
      <li>
       polluting nature.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Austria">
       Austria
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Austria">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The right to roam in Austria, particularly in forests and mountainous areas, is called
      <b>
       Wegefreiheit
      </b>
      . Since 1975 the right to roam in forests is guaranteed by Federal law. In particular, walking, running, hiking, and resting are automatically allowed to the public in most forest areas. However, horse riding, bike riding, and camping are not, and may only be practised with the land owner's permission. A large proportion of the forest area in Austria is owned by government bodies such as the
      <i>
       sterreichische Bundesforste
      </i>
      , but the same restrictions still apply. In some circumstances forests may be closed to the public for environmental reasons. The situation in mountainous areas is less clear, and differs from state to state. Some states, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" title="Carinthia (state)">
       Carinthia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">
       Styria
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_(state)" title="Salzburg (state)">
       Salzburg
      </a>
      guarantee a right to roam in mountainous areas (usually defined as above the tree line), for all recreational activities. In other states, such as Tyrol, Lower Austria, and Burgenland, no explicit right to roam exists and land owners reserve the right to deny access. In practice, however, such restrictions are rarely enforced, since mountain tourism is an important industry in Austria.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">
       Czech Republic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The old legal institute of "right of the way" (imbedded in the Civil Code) has its roots in
      <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">
       Austria-Hungary
      </a>
      law. This legal institution is applied when one land owner has a need to go through alien lands for access to his own land.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the Nature and Countryside Preservation Act, there is a legal right to roam through countryside ("veejn pstupnost krajiny", public accessibility of countryside  excluding parcels owned by a
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_person" title="Natural person">
       natural person
      </a>
      ). Some types of land are excluded from compulsory public accessibility: settled and building grounds, courtyards, gardens, orchards, vineyards, hop gardens, grounds destined for animal husbandry. Fields and arable land are excluded during seasons when herbage or soil would be damaged, pasture lands are excluded during cattle grazing. In national natural preserves, national natural monuments, national parks and in the first zones of landscape protected areas, state authorities can restrict public access (ordinarily only to roads or only to marked routes). Special acts can exclude also other areas (e. g. military areas, rail tracks etc.).
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Forest Act, forests are publicly accessible ("obecn uvn lesa", common use of forest  including private ones). However, biking, sledge riding, skiing and horseback riding are allowed only on forest roads. Public motor vehicle riding is prohibited (highroads going through forest are not considered as parts of the forest). Common use of forest can be restricted by the owner in military forests, protected areas, forest nurseries, forest orchards, deer parks, pheasantries etc.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Road Act defines "obecn uvn pozemnch komunikac" (common use of roads  some road can be excluded), The Water Act defines "obecn uvn povrchovch vod" (common use of surface waters).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Switzerland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The freedom to roam is guaranteed in
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Civil_Code" title="Swiss Civil Code">
       Swiss Civil Code
      </a>
      . Some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantons_of_switzerland" title="Cantons of switzerland">
       cantons
      </a>
      however have more detailed regulations concerning the rights of access of otherwise not authorised people.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Swiss Civil Code provides that forest and pasture are accessible freely for everyone, as long as there is no excessive usage. Except in special cases like the protection of young forest or
      <a href="/wiki/Biotope" title="Biotope">
       biotopes
      </a>
      it is not allowed to fence in forest areas. This also applies to private property. However, it is possible to make activities with excessive usage and possible potential to cause damage (e.g. events in the woods, access with cars) dependent on special authorisation. Similar regulations are in place for land which is not usable (e.g. stretches of water, rock, snow and ice), regardless of the land being unowned (i.e. being under the control of the canton and not able to be claimed as private property) or being in private hands.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is further possible for the canton to restrict the freedom to roam to protect nature (e.g. the gathering of mushrooms, berries, wood, etc. in forests).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Belarus">
       Belarus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Belarus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Article 13 of Section I of the
      <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Belarus" title="Constitution of Belarus">
       Constitution of Belarus
      </a>
      guarantees that all forest and farm land is publicly owned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Forty percent of the country's territory is covered by forest,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and approximately the same amount devoted to agriculture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the Forest Code (Article 13
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) "citizens have the right to freely stay in the forest and collect wild fruits, berries, nuts, mushrooms, other food, forest resources and medicinal plants to meet their own needs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_United_States">
       In the United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Freedom_to_roam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In the United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      While no jurisdiction in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      has applied a true freedom-to-roam paradigm, many states have enacted
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Recreational_use_statute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Recreational use statute (page does not exist)">
       recreational use statutes
      </a>
      to encourage landowners to open their land to hiking, hunting, fishing, swimming and other recreational activities. These statutes shield landowners from liability in
      <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">
       negligence
      </a>
      actions should those using the land recreationally be injured by something on the land; without such statutes, those using the land for these purposes with permission would be
      <a href="/wiki/Invitee" title="Invitee">
       invitees
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Licensee" title="Licensee">
       licensees
      </a>
      depending on whether or not they had paid for the privilege, and would thus be able to sue the landowner in the event of injury under most circumstances. However, no state applies a general permission to enter private lands of any kind for recreational purposes.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      In recent years
      <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">
       population growth
      </a>
      has increased pressure on some areas popular for
      <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">
       hiking
      </a>
      and increased mobility and affluence has made previously remote areas more accessible. There is some concern that without ecological education, some recreational users have limited understanding of the economic and natural systems they are exploring, though significant harm or damage is unusual, the main concerns being disturbance of sensitive species of wildlife (particularly by dogs), and litter.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1992_Rio_Convention_on_Biodiversity" title="1992 Rio Convention on Biodiversity">
       1992 Rio Convention on Biodiversity
      </a>
      (subscribed to by 189 countries) expressed some caution about the potential effect of unlimited access, especially in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">
       tropical
      </a>
      forests, where
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">
       slash and burn
      </a>
      practices undermine
      <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">
       biodiversity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      For this reason, broad public access rights are challenged in some countries' resulting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">
       Biodiversity Action Plan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Critics from defenders of proprietorship sometimes assert that the All People's Right threatens the essence of ownership and the "management practices" of property owners, who may or may not have created and preserved environmentally important qualities
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Private owners and their representatives have also argued that newly created access rights ought to lead to financial compensation for private landowners.
     </p>
