      <h1 class="omc-post-heading-standard">
       Who owns the future?
      </h1>
      <p>
       <a id="dd_start">
       </a>
       A lot of tech articles about the future are about the near-term  and, of course, plenty of them sling the snake oil of how some magical bean harvested in Silicon Valley (or by our friends on the other side of LA at Silicon Beach) will cure all of your companys ills. When a tech solution fails, its often because buyers (perhaps at the sellers coaxing) remove themselves from the equation. Were repeatedly stressing
       <a href="/2013/01/you-need-a-collaboration-champion/" target="_blank">
        the importance of empowering a champion
       </a>
       to take ownership of your initiatives (like, say, a new collaboration solution) and
       <a href="/2012/10/eight-tips-on-successful-adoption-of-collaboration-solutions/" target="_blank">
        the absolute necessity of charting a clear course for adoption
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Once you solve your problems today and tomorrow, though, whats lurking on the horizon? Jaron Laniers
       <em>
        Who Owns the Future?
       </em>
       offers a few disturbing guesses. Its a wide-ranging book that sometimes strays outside of the topics we cover on this blog, but also spends a lot of time dealing with the cloud (and what Lanier calls Siren Servers), collaboration and big data. If youre living with these topics day-to-day  or if youre just someone who fears a dystopian future controlled by Mark Zuckerberg or some Zuckerberg 2.0  you should definitely check it out.
      </p>
      <p>
       Broadly speaking,
       <em>
        Who Owns the Future?
       </em>
       warns readers that they are fodder for manipulation by data-gobbling gatekeepers (Facebook and Google, naturally, pop up frequently). Warning against the economic impact of tech-fueled market disruption, he makes the case for a reimbursed middle class of users feeding into the servers. In a future of 3D printers and automated-everything, it will otherwise be easier than ever to be marginalized.The solutions dont always feel fully baked, but the glimpses into the future often read like the smart kind of sci-fi.
      </p>
      <h5>
       Whos Jaron Lanier?
      </h5>
      <p>
       So-called father of virtual reality. First person to see himself as an avatar. Protege of Marvin Minsky. Serial entrepreneur whose startups have become parts of Google, Adobe and Oracle.
       <a href="http://www.jaronlanier.com/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.jaronlanier.com/', 'Grower of serious dreadlocks']);" target="_blank">
        Grower of serious dreadlocks
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h5>
       <strong>
       </strong>
       Xanadu and the original collaborator
      </h5>
      <p>
       For those of us who spend a lot of time thinking about collaboration, its inspiring to hear the story of Ted Nelson dreaming up a more efficient and interconnected world before the buzzwords had even been created to describe it.
      </p>
      <p>
       He foresaw how digital information could become a new form of expression for people, Lanier writes. Instead of conceiving of only a single person in front of a computer, he imagined new networked forms of collaboration and culture. People would create information structures that could be shared, reused, collaborated on, and interacted with. These concepts are utterly ordinary today, but at the time very few could understand them at allTed Nelson was the first person to my knowledge to describe, starting in 1960, how you could actually implement new kinds of media in digital form, share them, and collaborate.
       <a id="dd_end">
       </a>
      </p>
