Chemical is one of the gases generated as bacteria disintegrate food in gut
Lethal in big doses, but small amounts can help preserve cells and battle illness
Exeter University experts state the gas has the power to be 'healthcare hero'
Scientists make a new compound to help the body make the right amount



The scent of flatulence carry hidden health rewards - and could help fend off cancer,
         </p>

        <div class="slide_img">

         <p>
          strokes, heart attacks and dementia, scientists have disclosed.

Hydrogen sulfide is one of a number of strong noxious gases made by bacteria as it disintegrate food in the gut.

It is lethal in big doses, but in small amounts it helps preserve cells and battle illness, say experts at Exeter University.

When cells turn stressed by disease, they try to pull in enzymes to make their own minuscule quantities of hydrogen sulfide.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">

          Viewing 3 of 7
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
         </h2>
         <p>
          The chemical aids in protecting mitochondria, which propel energy production in blood vessel cells and regulate inflammation, and in its absence cells can shut down and die.

Now researchers have created a new compound called AP39 to help the body in making just the right quantity of hydrogen sulfide.

They feel it will aid in preventing or reversing mitochondrial damage, which is a pivotal strategy in treating conditions like stroke, heart failure, diabetes, arthritis, dementia and ageing.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>

         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">

         </h2>
         <p>
          'This keeps the mitochondria running smoothly and lets cells to live.

'We have utilized this natural process by making a compound, named AP39, which slowly supply little amounts of this gas specifically to the mitochondria.

'Our results indicate that if stressed cells are treated with AP39, mitochondria are protected and cells stay alive.'

Before running trials on humans, researchers have run disease models to find out how effective AP39 is.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
