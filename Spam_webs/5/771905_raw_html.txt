       <h1 class="pagetitle">
        The Reason You Need to STOP Icing Your Running Injuries Right Now
        <span class="author-byline">
         Written by  John Davis - Get free updates
         <a href="https://my.leadpages.net/leadbox/141f6b773f72a2%3A14e0f6c9eb46dc/5668387241852928/">
          here
         </a>
        </span>
       </h1>
       <div class="entry">
        <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
        <p>
         Icing: theres probably no injury treatment thats more ubiquitous.
        </p>
        <p>
         At high school cross country meets, youll see ice bags and ice cups littered everywhere. At running stores, youll find reusable ice packs and specially-designed icing sleeves. And if you ask a doctor what to do about a running injury, the response will almost invariably include ice it.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, as researchers and coaches are starting to learn more about how inflammation works, our understanding of how to treat injuries is changing.
        </p>
        <p>
         For example, about 18 months ago we published this post on
         <a href="http://runnersconnect.net/running-injury-prevention/ibuprofen-and-running/" target="_blank">
          why you shouldnt use anti-inflammatories
         </a>
         (one of the go-to treatments when runners get hurt).
        </p>
        <p>
         In short, the research shows that
         <em>
          some
         </em>
         inflammation is a natural and good thing so inhibiting it actually hinders the healing process.
        </p>
        <p>
         But, what does the research say on icing? Is it the same effect as anti-inflammatories? Is icing useful in every situation? If so, whats the best way to go about it?
        </p>
        <h2>
         Icing and overuse running injuries
        </h2>
        <p>
         Given the near ubiquitous recommendation to ice when injured, youd expect there to be reams of studies on such a universal injury treatment. But, astoundingly, there arent.
        </p>
        <p>
         Theres a moderately-sized collection of controlled experiments on using icing (or cryotherapy as its known in medical circles) for acute injuries, like
         <a href="http://runnersconnect.net/running-injury-prevention/trailrunning-ankle-sprain-and-balance/" target="_blank" title="Trail Running: Sprain-Proof Your Ankles by Gaining The Right Balance">
          ankle sprains
         </a>
         and post-surgery recovery, but research into icing for
         <em>
          overuse
         </em>
         injuries, like the ones runners get, is quite limited.
        </p>
        <p>
         Some papers mention cryotherapy as a treatment for
         <a href="http://runnersconnect.net/running-injury-prevention/achilles-tendonitis-and-insertional-achilles-tendinopathy-in-runners/" target="_blank" title="The Ultimate Runners Guide to Achilles Tendon Injuries: The Scientific Signs, Symptoms, and Research Backed Treatment Options for Achilles Tendonitis and Insertional Achilles Tendinopathy">
          tendon injuries
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://runnersconnect.net/running-injury-prevention/plantar-fasciitis-in-runners/" target="_blank" title="Plantar Fasciitis: The Scientific Signs, Symptoms and Causes as well as Research-Backed Treatment Options for Runners">
          plantar fasciitis
         </a>
         , or
         <a href="http://runnersconnect.net/running-injury-prevention/the-ultimate-guide-to-shin-splints-for-runners/" target="_blank" title="The Ultimate Guide to Shin Splints for Runners">
          shin splints
         </a>
         , but its only in passingtheres been essentially no rigorous testing of icing as a treatment for running injuries.
        </p>
        <p>
         Icing was used as control treatment in a 2007 study on eccentric exercises for Achilles tendonitis; the rehab exercises were vastly more successful than icing. Aside from this, nothing!
        </p>
        <p>
         Because of the lack of research, we cant conclusively prove whether or not icing is a useful therapy.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, this doesnt mean theres nothing to take away from the research about icing.
        </p>
        <p>
         Instead of looking to direct studies on icing for running injuries, well have to learn from research on icing for acute injuries with the understanding that we are operating under the
         <em>
          assumption
         </em>
         that icing helps with overuse injuries.
        </p>
        <p>
         Going by the anecdotal experience of many thousands of runners in the real world, this seems to be a safe assumption, but until the science is in, its nothing more than that.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Quick icing therapy for ankle sprain
        </h2>
        <p>
         One thing appears certain: quick icing is definitely the proper response to an acute injury like an ankle sprain. (*acute injuries are those that occur suddenly, like when you turn your ankle, while overuse injuries are those that occur due to repeated stress)
        </p>
        <p>
         A 1982 study of 37 people with ankle sprains investigated the effects of ice and heat on time to recovery. The researchers reported that the patients who heated their injured ankle during the initial days following the sprain took over twice as long to recover as those who iced immediately.
        </p>
        <p>
         The timing of the icing had a big impact, toodelaying icing for a day and a half similarly resulted in a greater than two-fold increase in recovery time in serious ankle sprains when compared to icing within the first 36 hours following the injury.
        </p>
        <p>
         Other research suggests that doing intermittent icing is more effective than longer continuous blocks of icing. A clever 2006 study by three researchers in the UK split 89 people with ankle sprains into two groups.
        </p>
        <p>
         The first group was instructed to ice their injured ankle continuously for 20 minutes every two hours over the first three days following the injury. The second group also iced every two hours, but iced for ten minutes, removed the ice for 10 minutes, then iced for another 10 minutes.
        </p>
        <p>
         This small difference in icing strategy resulted in significantly less pain during activity in the first week following the injury. After this, both groups reported the same levels of pain, which dropped off over the next five weeks.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Takeaway message
        </h2>
        <p class="alert">
         If icing is beneficial for running injuries, it is likely
         <em>
          most
         </em>
         beneficial as a front-line treatment used immediately after an injury flares up.
        </p>
        <p>
         Acute injuries, like ankle sprains, are marked by inflammationicing is known to help limit localized inflammation by reducing blood flow.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Its been well-established that overuse injuries like tendonitis are
          <em>
           not
          </em>
          inflammatory after several week
         </strong>
         s, but some researchers believe there is a brief inflammatory phase that lasts a few days or a few weeks.
        </p>
        <p>
         If this exists, this is likely when icing would make a difference.
        </p>
        <p>
         The usefulness of icing in the treatment of running injuries is unclearon one hand, since there isnt a single study supporting the use of cryotherapy in the treatment of overuse injuries.
        </p>
        <p>
         On the other, there are ice bags and cups in nearly every athletic training room and physical therapy office in the countryignoring anecdotal evidence of that magnitude isnt a particularly attractive option either.
        </p>
        <p class="alert">
         What we
         <em>
          can
         </em>
         say is that
         <em>
          if
         </em>
         icing is beneficial, its going to be most effective if you apply ice immediately after you aggravate an injury, then do not ice again.
        </p>
        <p>
         Further, it is best to use an intermittent approach: ice for ten minutes, then remove it for 10 minutes, then reapply the ice for another 10 minutes.
        </p>
