      <h2>
       Remote Canadian Towns and Cities
      </h2>
      <p>
       Canada is a vast and remote land. When around 75 per cent of all Canadians live within 100 miles of the US border, it is clear to there are plenty of remote Canadian towns and cities to be discovered. From the frozen north of Nunavut and the Yukon to the remote and coastal British Columbia, there are lots of remote Canadian towns and cities out there. Here are a selection of remote Canadian Towns and Cities to get the wanderlust in you going.
      </p>
      <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_2673" style="width: 516px">
       <a href="http://www.nomadsontheroad.com/2013/03/remote-canadian-towns-and-cities/telegraph-cove-2/" rel="attachment wp-att-2673">
        <img alt="Remote Canadian Towns and Cities - Telegraph Cove" class="size-full wp-image-2673" height="300" src="http://www.nomadsontheroad.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/03/Telegraph-Cove1.jpg" title="Remote Canadian Towns and Cities - Telegraph Cove" width="506"/>
       </a>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">
        Telegraph Cove started life as a lumber mill and salmon saltery in the early 20th Century. The areaacquiredits name in 1912, when the Superintendent of Telegraphs was looking for a linemans station and northern terminal for the telegraph line from Campbell river. The protection cove was idea and the fishing village was named Telegraph Cove.
        <br/>
        Photo Credit: Tourism BC/JF Bergeron
       </p>
      </div>
      <h4>
       Tahsis, British Columbia
      </h4>
      <p>
       The little coastal village of Tahsis is one of the most remote Canadian towns in the country. Located on the west coast of Vancouver Island, the village is around 300km by air from the provincial capital of Victoria. With 300 residents the area is found at the head of the steep and forbidding Tahsis Inlet, part of the spectacular Nooka Sound. The inlet, while making access to Tahsis difficult, is actually a protector of sorts for the town, saving it from the vicious lashings of the Pacific Ocean. With the major income for the town derived from forestry, Tahsis enjoyed a healthy population at its peak of around 2,500. But as the forest industry has declined, so to has the towns population, it now numbers around 300 residents. Today, the town is trying to reinvent itself as an outdoor recreation adventure destination.
      </p>
      <h4>
       Old Crow, Yukon
      </h4>
      <p>
       A dry community in the middle of the Yukon, Old Crow has, at the last census, 267 residents, the majority of them belong to the Gwichin speaking Vuntut Gwitchin First Nation community. As the only community in the Yukon which cant be reached by car it earns its place in this list of remote Canadian towns and cities. Fly-in fly-out via the Old Crow airport is the only way to access this remote community. Old Crow is named after a Native American chief called Deetru Kavihdik, whose name literally translates as Crow May I Walk.
      </p>
      <h4>
       Grise Fiord, Nunavut
      </h4>
      <p>
       With a name that means place that never thaws it would be a good guess to say
       <a href="http://www.grisefiord.ca/eng/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.grisefiord.ca/eng/', 'Grise Fiord');">
        Grise Fiord
       </a>
       is cold and icy. The small Inuit hamlet in Nunavut is one of the northernmost towns in Canada and the world. Despite a population of just 130, Grise Fiord is still the largest community on Ellesmere Island. The town also holds the distinction as one of the coldest inhabited places in the world, with an average yearly temperature of -16.5 C.
      </p>
      <h4>
       Telegraph Cove, British Columbia
      </h4>
      <p>
       Telegraph Cove in British Columbia is a former fishing village which now serves as a launch point for eco-tourism in Blackfish Sound. Growing out of a one-room station of the northern tip of the Campbell River telegraph line, Telegraph Cove may be a remote Canadian town with apermanentpopulation of just 20. Despite this the area is a hive of activity, with up to 120,000 visitors descending on the area in the summer. Compact and bordered by rainforest and ocean, the town retains its rustic past.
      </p>
      <h4>
       Moosonee, Ontario
      </h4>
      <p>
       Located in remote northern Ontario on the Moose River, Moosonee is known as the Gateway to the Arctic. As Ontarios only salt-water port, Moosonee and nearby Moose Factory Island are only accessible by rail or water, despite being on the same latitude as Calgary. The town was originally the site of a fur trading company set up in 1903 by Revillon Freres and later incorporated into the more famous Hudsons Bay Company.
      </p>
      <h4>
       Kegashka, Quebec
      </h4>
      <p>
       Around 138 make this small Quebec town their home, Kegashka , or Kegaska as they pesky French-speaking Canadians like to spell it, was the site of a Hudsons Bay Company trading post in 1831.Yet it wasnt properly settled until 1898. Located at the entrance to the Lower North Shite, Kegaska is 37 miles past the end of the road. Literally the road ends in Natashquan, 37 miles shy of Kegaska.Now the area attracts outdoor enthusiasts, drawn by the excellent hiking, wildlife watching, iceberg viewing and sea kayaking.
      </p>
