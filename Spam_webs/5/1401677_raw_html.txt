         <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
          So Taylor Swift is louder than Motorhead, AC/DC and The Sex Pistols  wait, WHAT?
         </h1>
         <p class="entry-meta">
          <time class="entry-time" datetime="2015-03-27T14:47:37+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
           March 27, 2015
          </time>
          by
          <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
           <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/author/prodadvice/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
            <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
             Ian Shepherd
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="entry-comments-link">
           <a href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/taylor-swift-loudness/#comments">
            6 Comments
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
         <p>
          <a href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/DR-Infographic-20151.jpg">
           <img alt="Loudness War - Taylor Swift versus AC/DC" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8248" height="483" src="http://productionadvice.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/DR-Infographic-2015-Smaller1.jpg" width="580"/>
          </a>
          <br/>
          <em>
           (Click on the image for a larger version)
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          This is a 2015 re-boot of one of the first, and
          <a href="http://dynamicrangeday.co.uk/tag/DRD_Memes/" target="_blank">
           most popular
          </a>
          memes I created for
          <a href="http://www.DynamicRangeDay.co.uk/" target="_blank">
           Dynamic Range Day
          </a>
          
          <strong>
           not
          </strong>
          a Top 10 as some sites have reported, its actually the selected high (and low) -lights of recorded music levels over the last few decades.
         </p>
         <p>
          And the information it gives is just as crazy as it ever was.
         </p>
         <p>
          As a result of the so-called
          <a href="http://www.dynamicrangeday.co.uk/about/" target="_blank">
           loudness wars
          </a>
          , mainstream pop releases are being pushed onto CD and into mp3 files at such high levels theyre technically much hotter than some of the loudest acts in history, in an attempt to make them stand out from the competition.
         </p>
         <p>
          Why is it crazy ? Because it doesnt work.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           None
          </strong>
          of these loudness differences will be audiblein any of most popular places we listen to music.
         </p>
         <p>
          Not on
          <a href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/spotify-loudness-war/" target="_blank" title="How Spotify Will End The Loudness War">
           Spotify
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Not on
          <a href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/u2-21st-century-loudness-secret/" target="_blank" title="How to sound loud on iTunes Radio  the surprising secret of U2s new album">
           iTunes Radio
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Not even on
          <a href="http://dynamicrangeday.co.uk/youtube-versus-loudness/" target="_blank">
           Youtube
          </a>
          , any more !
         </p>
         <p>
          And certainly not on
          <a href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/loudness-means-nothing-on-the-radio/" target="_blank" title="Loudness means NOTHING on the radio  The Proof">
           radio
          </a>
          or
          <a href="http://dynamicrangeday.co.uk/calm-down-new-american-law-stops-tv-advertisers-from-turning-up-the-volume-the-independent/" target="_blank">
           TV
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Replay volume is normalised in all of these places, to improve the listener experience, because the number one source of complaints about audio is always big variations in loudness  we hate to have to keep adjusting the volume control.
         </p>
         <p>
          So if youre wondering  
          <a href="http://dynamicrangeday.co.uk/loudness-war-dirty-secret/" target="_blank">
           why do people still bother?
          </a>
            youve got a point!
         </p>
         <h4>
          Its not all bad news
         </h4>
         <p>
          Take another look at that infographic, though.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are some interesting features.
         </p>
         <p>
          Look at the 2015 releases.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span id="more-8112">
          </span>
          As well as ridiculous results like Taylor Swift being as loud as Oasis, and Nicki Minaj being almost as loud as Metallica  there are two massively successful pop albums by Daft Punk &amp; Mark Ronson that
          <a href="http://productionadvice.co.uk/massive-chart-hits/" target="_blank" title="What do these four massive chart hits all have in common, APART from Pharrell ?">
           have great dynamics
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          And DAngelos critically acclaimed album Black Messiah measures
          <strong>
           DR8
          </strong>
          . In a genre like R&amp;B where almost everything is clipped and crushed by default, thats a serious result ! And its not alone  in an
          <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/microphonecheck/2014/12/12/370148449/j-cole-it-aint-enough-of-us-trying" target="_blank">
           interview on NPRs Hip Hop show
          </a>
          ,
          <strong>
           J. Cole
          </strong>
          described how he and producer
          <a href="https://twitter.com/juromezdavis" target="_blank">
           Juro Mez Davis
          </a>
          deliberately chose
          <strong>
           not
          </strong>
          to compete in the loudness war  and
          <a href="https://storify.com/Ian_Shepherd/j-cole-versus-the-loudness-war" target="_blank">
           his fans loved the decision
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          (To hear the right section of the interview,
          <a href="https://soundcloud.com/npr-microphone-check/j-cole-it-aint-enough-of-us-trying#t=16m29s" target="_blank">
           click here
          </a>
          )
         </p>
         <p>
          And there have been a host of other great-sounding, dynamic releases in the last year, too  some of them are nominated for the
          <a href="http://dynamicrangeday.co.uk/award" target="_blank">
           Dynamic Range Day Award 2015
          </a>
          . Check them out, your ears will thank you.
         </p>
         <h4>
          It aint over yet
         </h4>
         <p>
          Of course these are the exceptions, rather than the rule.
         </p>
         <p>
          For every great-sounding success, there are ten more that have been smashed. As I said in my
          <a href="http://www.cepro.com/article/dynamic_range_days_shepherd_sees_progress_with_mastering_techniques/" target="_blank">
           interview for CE Pro
          </a>
          , the situation is getting more polarised, and will probably keep getting worse, before it gets better.
         </p>
         <p>
          But loudness normalisation is a fact, now  and gradually, the music world will wake up to the new reality. Just as U2 and Pharrell and DAngelo and Daft Punk and J.Cole and Opeth and Aphex Twin and Mark Ronson and Jack White and Paulo Nutini and The War On Drugs and many other already have.
         </p>
         <p>
          And when they do, our ears will thank
          <strong>
           them
          </strong>
          .
          <br/>
         </p>
         <h4>
          Update  mainstream press coverage
         </h4>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/arts/music/article4423123.ece">
           <img alt="Times Taylor Swift small" class="alignright size-full wp-image-8419" height="282" src="http://productionadvice.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Times-Taylor-Swift-small.jpg" width="250"/>
          </a>
          Im happy to say this post went viral recently  so much so, that I ended up being interviewed by
          <a href="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/arts/music/article4423123.ece" target="_blank">
           The Times
          </a>
          newspaper, and on BBC Radio 4 here in the UK about the topic. To read the article (re-blogged by The Australian),
          <a href="http://www.theaustralian.com.au/news/world/swift-end-to-pumping-up-the-volume/story-fnb64oi6-1227325401392" target="_blank">
           click here
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The story also showed up on the websites of Billboard and the NME, as well as many others. Great to see the story getting some attention in the popular press!
         </p>

            <p>
             Explaining the dynamic range issue to people is complicated. There is no rule that you must rip your face apart with a highly compressed recording, but if you are coming off of listening to a nice, dynamic recording, and you pop in Death Magnetic, you had better turn it down first.
            </p>
            <p>
             Incidentally, vinyl mixes tend to have better dynamics  but not because vinyl is inherently superior. Maybe vinyl is better, maybe not, but mastering engineers take into account the fact that you cant listen to a record in your car, on the subway, walking around a shop, etc. You pretty much need to stay put when listening to vinyl. Further, people willing to put up with the hassle of the big format, the physical handling, the care, and the stationary listening tend to also be willing to single-task and LISTEN. They tend to be caring more  at least in that case. I listen to vinyl  sometimes, when I have the time. Other times, as in at work, I have mp3s playing in the background, or Pandora, and I discover new music that way. If I want more, I will buy the record and sit down and dim the lights.
            </p>
            <p>
             Also fun: sometimes unexpected genres provide good dynamics  especially on vinyl. Check out Skeletonwitchs latest, Serpents Unleashed (black/thrash metal); Anciients Heart of Oak (progressive death/doom metal)(better on 2LP 45 RPM than the rated cd on the database); Clutchs Strange Cousins From the West (Heavy rock n roll); Anything from Mastodon on vinyl (more progressive metal)(Blood Mountain has an AVERAGE of 12 for the whole album  from a metal band in a recent album); Eviles Skull (thrash metal); etc.
            </p>

           <div class="comment-content" itemprop="commentText">
            <p>
             Aaaaaannnd (didnt want too long of a single post) I theorize that hard rock and metal genres tend to start at a disadvantage, dynamically speaking, because of heavily distorted guitar. You dont need post compression to make it sound squished  it is that way by design courtesy of guitar amplifiers and/or effects. It doesnt actually matter what level the engineers set them, they dont have much dynamic range. The classic Big Muff Pi fuzz pedal essentially outputs a square wave, and it is awesome.
            </p>
            <p>
             So wherein does heavy music find dynamics? Drums. And to a lesser extent, vocals. Do those snares, cymbals, hi-hats, etc. have genuine decay? Are they set so the peaks of the waveforms extend far beyond the very compressed guitar? Bands that are more guitar-driven tend to have less dynamics than impetuous drummers bands. TEND TO  see Metallica for a good destruction of my point.
            </p>
            <p>
             But if you listen to Master of Puppets and Ride the lightning, you will hear dynamic drums, and the distorted guitars basically have no dynamics aside from picking technique (staccato vs. legato). But the voice should sound like a human and not an over-processed robot in some club-mixed pop song.
            </p>
