     <b>
      Trochor
      <br/>
     </b>
    </font>
    <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
     Animated Virtual Harmonograph
    </font>
   </p>
   <p>
    Have fun, play around with the settings; that's probably the best way of 
    figuring out what's going on.
    <br/>
    It's pretty hard to give a concise description of it in English.
    <br/>
    So far, the best I've come up with is to say that it's two interacting ellipses, 
    a bit like a
    <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/Spirograph.html">
     spirograph
    </a>
    .
    <br/>
    'Eccentricity', by the way, is a measure of how non-circular an ellipse is 
    - an ellipse with 0 eccentricity is a circle, one with 1 is a line.
    <br/>
   </p>
   <p>
    <b>
     <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
      The Long Version
     </font>
    </b>
   </p>
   <p>
    I first stumbled on trochoids by playing around with plotting
    <a href="trig.htm">
     trigonometric
    </a>
    functions.
    <br/>
    I wondered what would happen if you took the basic parametric equations for 
    plotting a circle -
   </p>
   <p>
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     radius
    </i>
    * cos (
    <font face="Symbol">
     q)
    </font>
    <br/>
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     radius
    </i>
    * sin
    <font face="Symbol">
     (q)
    </font>
   </p>
   <p>
    - and added them to the equation for another circle, turning more than one 
    circle in the time it takes to draw the first one,
    <br/>
    so we get something like the trails left by a point on a wheel rolling around 
    another wheel (an
    <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/Epitrochoid.html">
     epitrochoid
    </a>
    or
    <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/Hypotrochoid.html">
     hypotrochoid
    </a>
    ), 
    with these equations:
   </p>
   <p>
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
    </i>
    * cos (
    <font face="Symbol">
     q)
    </font>
    +
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      2
     </sub>
    </i>
    * cos (
    <i>
     ratio
    </i>
    *
    <font face="Symbol">
     q
    </font>
    )
    <br/>
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
    </i>
    * sin
    <font face="Symbol">
     (q)
    </font>
    +
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      2
     </sub>
    </i>
    * sin (
    <i>
     ratio
    </i>
    *
    <font face="Symbol">
     q
    </font>
    )
   </p>
   <p>
    This makes some pleasing shapes, so I thought I would try animating it.
    <br/>
    The most obvious thing to do is to change the relative phase of the first 
    and second circles, but this just turns the whole thing round.
    <br/>
    What's more interesting is to animate the phase of the x component (the cosine) 
    in the opposite direction to the y component (sine):
   </p>
   <p>
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
    </i>
    * cos (
    <font face="Symbol">
     q)
    </font>
    +
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      2
     </sub>
    </i>
    * cos (
    <i>
     ratio
    </i>
    *
    <font face="Symbol">
     q + 
    f
    </font>
    )
    <br/>
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      1
     </sub>
    </i>
    * sin
    <font face="Symbol">
     (q)
    </font>
    +
    <i>
     radius
     <sub>
      2
     </sub>
    </i>
    * sin (
    <i>
     ratio
    </i>
    *
    <font face="Symbol">
     q - 
    f
    </font>
    )
   </p>
   <p>
    This makes the circle shrink to a line, then grow into a circle flowing the 
    opposite way, and then go through the same cycle again.
    <br/>
    Combined with the first circle, this makes the sort of animations you can 
    see above.
   </p>
   <p>
    Pleased with the results of this, in 2001 I made an applet to let people 
    play with it, and that's where things stood till September 2004.
   </p>
   <p>
    Then I read a gorgeously produced wee book called
    <i>
     <a href="http://search.barnesandnoble.com/booksearch/isbnInquiry.asp?userid=uF7F5NAARh&amp;isbn=0802714099&amp;itm=1">
      Harmonograph: 
    A Visual Guide to the Mathematics of Music
     </a>
     .
     <br/>
     Harmonograph
    </i>
    provides an impressively clear and thorough introduction 
    to the basics of music theory, and ways of visualising music.
    <br/>
    It does this in 50-odd pages, replete with beautiful illustrations of harmony 
    made by
    <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/Harmonograph.html">
     harmonographs
    </a>
    ,
    <a href="http://www.lumen.nu/rekveld/texts/symhar.html">
     kaleidophones
    </a>
    ,
    <a href="http://www.phys.unsw.edu.au/%7Ejw/patterns3.html">
     Chladni plates
    </a>
    and the like.
   </p>
   <p>
    A harmonograph is an instrument invented in the mid-nineteenth century, using 
    two or more pendulums to produce beautiful pictures.
    <br/>
    The pictures are especially beautiful when the ratios of the frequencies of 
    the pendulums are close to whole numbers,
    <br/>
    just as chords are especially beautiful when the ratios of the frequencies 
    notes making them up are close to whole numbers.
    <br/>
    The pictures I was making with Trochor were almost the same pictures produced 
    by a rotary harmonograph,
    <br/>
    except that the pendulums of a harmonograph steadily wind down as they draw, 
    and Trochor was restricted to whole numbers.
   </p>
   <p>
    A kaleidophone, like a harmonograph, creates images of harmonics, but these 
    are made only fleetingly, in light.
    <br/>
    It consists of a metal rod fixed in a stand, with a reflective bead on top, 
    which is struck and then stroked with a bow.
    <br/>
    A beam of light reflected from the top of it casts patterns onto a screen, 
    rather like those of a rotary harmonograph, but more complex.
    <br/>
    The biggest difference, judging by the illustrations I have seen, is that 
    it is not restricted to circular motion:
    <br/>
    Free to vibrate in any direction, it produces patterns composed of interacting 
    linear waves and ellipses.
   </p>
   <p>
    I have incorporated the idea of using ellipses, together with the damped 
    spiralling motion and non-integer ratios of the harmonograph, into Trochor.
    <br/>
    With 0 eccentricity, we are back to the rotary harmonograph; with 1, we have 
    the linear harmonograph, producing
    <a href="http://mathworld.wolfram.com/LissajousCurve.html">
     Lissajous
    </a>
    -type 
    figures.
    <br/>
   </p>
   <p>
    The equations of this version are as follow:
   </p>
   <p>
    <i>
     x
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     (1-damping)
     <sup>
      n
     </sup>
     * (axis
     <sub>
      1a
     </sub>
    </i>
    * cos 
    (
    <font face="Symbol">
     q)
    </font>
    +
    <i>
     axis
     <sub>
      2a
     </sub>
    </i>
    * cos (
    <i>
     ratio
    </i>
    *
    <font face="Symbol">
     q + f
    </font>
    ))
    <br/>
    <i>
     y
    </i>
    =
    <i>
     (1-damping)
     <sup>
      n
     </sup>
    </i>
    * (
    <i>
     axis
     <sub>
      1b
     </sub>
    </i>
    * sin
    <font face="Symbol">
     (q)
    </font>
    +
    <i>
     axis
     <sub>
      1b
     </sub>
    </i>
    * sin (
    <i>
     ratio
    </i>
    *
    <font face="Symbol">
     q 
    - f
    </font>
    ))
    <br/>
    <br/>
   </p>
