           <h1 class="entry-title">
            Gapology and 2014 election
           </h1>
           <p class="entry-categories">
            <strong class="hidden">
             View related content:
            </strong>
            <a href="http://www.aei.org/policy/politics-and-public-opinion/elections/" rel="category" title="View all entries in Elections">
             Elections
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.aei.org/policy/politics-and-public-opinion/" rel="category" title="View all entries in Politics and Public Opinion">
             Politics and Public Opinion
            </a>
           </p>
           <div class="article-controls">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="content">
           <div class="entry-featured-image">
            <img alt="Feat_F_electionresults_11-5-14" class="attachment-post-thumbnail wp-post-image" height="282" src="http://www.aei.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/Feat_F_electionresults_11-5-14.jpg" width="446"/>
           </div>
           <p>
            <strong>
             <p>
              What did we learn about political gaps on Election Day this year? And what might they tell us about future election outcomes?
             </p>
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            In 2006, Laura Olson of Clemson University and John Green of the University of Akron published an
            <a href="http://oldapsa.apsanet.org/imgtest/PSJuly06OlsonGreenIntro.pdf">
             article
            </a>
            about gaps in the political world. Their introduction to gapology looked at some of the most famous gaps in our politics: gender, race, age, and church attendance. They argued that understanding gaps was a potent way to understand election results. Like batting averages in baseball, such simple statistics offer the power of language in describing the political world.
           </p>
           <p>
            So what did we learn about political gaps on Election Day this year? And what might they tell us about future election outcomes if we marry the information with demographic changes?
           </p>
           <p>
            The
            <strong>
             gender gap
            </strong>
            has been a prominent feature of our politics since the 1980 campaign. That year, women voted narrowly for Ronald Reagan while men voted for him heavily. Since that time, in all presidential and off-year elections, men and women have voted differently. Men look more Republican while women favor the Democrats.
           </p>
           <p>
            In 2014, women supported Democratic candidates in House races by 52 to 47 percent. Men, by contrast, supported Republican candidates by 56 to 42 percent for a gap of 19 points. Thats not the largest gender gap we have ever seen in our politics, but it is significant. The gender gap is a permanent feature of our politics, and it is patterned. White men are more Republican than white women, black men are more Republican than black women, and so on. Women were a slightly larger share of the electorate than men this year, but the GOPs big advantage among men trumped womens demographic strength.
           </p>
           <p>
            The
            <strong>
             marriage gap
            </strong>
            in our politics is larger than the gender gap. In this election it was a whopping 31 percentage points. Married voters voted 58 percent for Republican candidates and 41 percent for Democratic ones, while non-married voters (defined as widowed, divorced, or never married) looked like a mirror image. They voted 56 percent for Democratic candidates and 42 percent for Republican ones. Going forward, Democrats are betting on the fact that singles will be a larger share of the electorate and will propel the party ahead. In this election, Democrats worked especially hard to woo single women, trumpeting the GOPs so-called war on women, but they were not very successful. Gary Langer, ABCs polling ace, wrote that they gave the party their smallest margin, 60 to 38 percent, in exit polls back to 1992. Single women in particular are a growing share of the electorate. If they dont turn out in significant numbers, their Democratic leanings wont matter.
           </p>
           <p class="pullquote" style="font-weight: bold; color: #5275ad; font-size: 19px;">
            The marriage gap was a whopping 31 percentage points.
           </p>
           <p>
            The exit pollsters asked voters about race relations on Election Day. Forty percent of voters said race relations in the country had stayed about the same in the last few years, while more of the remainder said they had gotten worse (38 percent) than better (20 percent). The
            <strong>
             race gap
            </strong>
            in voting is consistently one of the largest in our politics. The black vote is one of the few monolithic blocs; in 2014, blacks voted 89 percent for Democratic candidates and gave only 10 percent of their votes to Republicans. Whites by contrast, a much larger group, voted 60 to 38 percent for Republican candidates, for a huge gap of more than 100 points. The share of the white vote is declining (whites were three-quarters of all voters on Election Day, down from around 90 percent in the early 1970s). If the black vote remains overwhelmingly Democratic, Republicans are going to have to do a lot better with Hispanic voters going forward. Latinos were 8 percent of the electorate on Tuesday, but in future years, they will punch closer to their demographic weight.
           </p>
           <p>
            Pollsters look at denomination and degree of religious commitment (measured roughly by a question about how often the voter attends church) on Election Day. Denomination is important, but the overlay of religiosity tells us more about politics now. Today a Catholic who goes to mass every week has more in common with a Protestant who attends church every week and a Jew who attends services every week than that Catholic has in common with a Catholic who never goes to church at all. Weekly churchgoers (40 percent of voters in this election) voted for GOP candidates over Democrats by 58 to 41 percent while those who never attend church (18 percent this year) voted for Democratic candidates by 62 to 28 percent, for a
            <strong>
             religiosity gap
            </strong>
            of 41 points. America has always had a vibrant religious marketplace with significant changes in denominational strength over time. Going forward we should watch the size and political preferences of the group that never attends religious services, which appears to be growing, and those who attend regularly, a larger and more stable group.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its possible to look at a lot of other groups in our politics and see gaps. There are differences between young and old, rich and poor, and now, more than ever, Democrats and Republicans. Reports from the Pew Research Center in 2012 and 2014 focused on the stability of some of the familiar gaps alongside a big increase in the partisan gap. Its not clear that most of us think of ourselves first as members of a subgroup, but for political junkies it is a gold mine. All of this makes gapology, albeit a crude political measure, another way to understand Americas complex and heterogeneous electorate.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Karlyn Bowman is a senior fellow at AEI.
            </strong>
           </p>
