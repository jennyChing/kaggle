         <p>
          In the last few years, Ive been pretty good with New Years resolutions. One year, I promised to have a golf handicap in the mid teens. With plenty of practice and a little coaching, I made it happen. Another year, I lost 10 pounds.
         </p>
         <p>
          My 2014 resolution is far more daunting than shaving a few golf strokes or cutting out fast food. This year, Im promising to change education forever.
         </p>
         <p>
          Im going to encourage teachers everywhere to stop grading their students, using points, percentages and letters.
         </p>
         <h3>
          It may be crazy, but. . .
         </h3>
         <p>
          Sure, this may seem like an inconceivable, ridiculous, completely crazy idea.
         </p>
         <p>
          After all, wasnt
          <a href="http://www.brilliant-insane.com/2013/12/hey-orville-and-wilbur-you-cant-fly.html" target="_blank" title="Hey Orville and Wilbur, you cant fly!">
           flying a plane inconceivable
          </a>
          ? Wasnt the microwave a ridiculous idea? I mean who in his right mind would eat something exposed to radiation? Wasnt the Smartphone a completely crazy thought? Could an internet-ready computer and high-functioning video camera really be placed inside a four-inch mobile device?
         </p>
         <p>
          If these outrageous inventions could become realities, revolutionizing travel, eating and communication, why cant assessment be changed?
         </p>
         <h3>
          The plan
         </h3>
         <p>
          Any successful resolution or goal needs a plan. Heres how I intend to stop traditional grading in its tracks in 2014:
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           <b>
            Share this
           </b>
           <a href="http://www.brilliant-insane.com/2013/12/eliminate-grades-heres-your-5-step-blueprint.html" target="_blank">
            <b>
             5-step blueprint for eliminating grades
            </b>
           </a>
           with teachers nationwide often.
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            Enlist the help of educators in my
            <a class="external" href="https://twitter.com/markbarnes19" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'https://twitter.com/markbarnes19', 'Personal Learning Network']);" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
             Personal Learning Network
            </a>
           </b>
           . This must be a widespread grassroots effort.
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            Create open forums with parents and school leaders
           </b>
           about the value of
           <b>
            <a href="http://www.brilliant-insane.com/2014/05/se2r-feedback-revolutionizes-how-we-assess-learning.html" target="_blank" title="SE2R Feedback Revolutionizes How We Assess Learning">
             SE2R feedback
            </a>
           </b>
           over traditional grades (more on this later).
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            Write about the power of feedback and the failure of grades
           </b>
           . Theres plenty of prose coming on this subject.
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            Get the publishing lobby to buy in
           </b>
           . Ive been hard on education publishers, like Pearson, who capitalize on education reforms, like the Common Core. What if a powerhouse publisher decided to put its resources behind narrative feedback? Imagine the possibilities.
          </li>
         </ol>
         <p>
          So, in 2014, Im going to war, and my enemy is traditional grades.
         </p>
         <p>
          Are you ready to join the fight?
         </p>

           <p>
            This is a bad idea. You have middle school teachers who teach more than 100 kids; many districts have a majority of parents who dont even speak English. How are you going to provide continual feedback to those parents? Even if you display their kids work online, they still have to know how to interpret the work for proficiency.
           </p>
           <p>
            Grades are very efficient, so eliminating them will make life even harder on teachers. Parents have a right to know how their kids are doing, so now you will have teachers spending countless hours trying to come up with statements to send out to parents in lieu of traditional grades. That reason alone raises serious concerns for me.
           </p>
           <p>
            Another reason this is a bad idea:  Parents are already suspicious enough about the teaching profession, and now you are going to take away the one thing they understand in terms of knowing how their children in doing in school. Well-intentioned or not, the cry is going to be that teachers are trying to escape accountability.
