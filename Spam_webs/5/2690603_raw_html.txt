       <h1 class="entry-title">
        Dark, dense, and oh-so-delicious: Chocolate Decadence
       </h1>

      <div class="entry-content">
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42576" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3789.jpg" title="IMG_3789"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Now THIS, friends, is what I call chocolate ice cream.
       </p>
       <p>
        Spare me the wimpy, beige-tone frozen desserts that masquerade as chocolate.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mass-market ice cream with an ingredient label like this: Milk, Cream, Sugar, Corn Syrup, High Fructose Corn Syrup, Cocoa  Processed with Alkali, Whey, Buttermilk, Mono &amp; Diglycerides, Guar  Gum, Flavors, Carob Bean Gum, Polysorbate 80, Carrageenan.
       </p>
       <p>
        With cocoa the sixth ingredient down, its no wonder the ice cream is more milk than chocolate.
       </p>
       <p>
        Hey, if you happen to like milk chocolate, then store-bought ice cream is probably right up your alley.
       </p>
       <p>
        But me? Im a dark chocoholic, and the only chocolate that passes my lips is dark as well, as a vanilla bean, in a serendipitous pairing of complementary flavors.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span id="more-42232">
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        The following recipe started out as
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes/chocolate-intemperance-recipe">
         Chocolate Intemperance,
        </a>
        a chocolate smoothie. And indeed, the two share the same ingredients.
       </p>
       <p>
        But where Intemperance is served as is, or just slightly frozen (the intemperance comes from the vodka youre invited to stir in), Decadence is fully frozen: smooth, spoonable, and shamelessly seductive.
       </p>
       <p>
        And, what about the name, Chocolate Decadence? Its overworked, for sure; it seems every restaurant dessert featuring even a hint of chocolate has been given the over-the-top decadence label.
       </p>
       <p>
        But this ultra-smooth, dense ice cream truly is decadent, meeting at least one of the words definitions: downfall. If youre dieting  this ice cream will surely be your downfall!
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42578" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3617.jpg" title="IMG_3617"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Whisk together the following, in a medium-sized saucepan:
       </p>
       <p>
        1/2 cup
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/double-dutch-dark-cocoa-16-oz">
         Dutch-process cocoa
        </a>
        <br/>
        1 cup sugar
        <br/>
        3 tablespoons
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/instant-clearjel-8-oz">
         Instant ClearJel
        </a>
        or cornstarch
        <br/>
        1/4 teaspoon salt
       </p>
       <p>
        Gradually whisk in 1 cup cold water, stirring until smooth.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42580" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3619.jpg" title="IMG_3619"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Add 1 tablespoon
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/madagascar-bourbon-vanilla-extract-4-oz">
         vanilla;
        </a>
        or 1 tablespoon
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/espresso-powder-8-oz">
         espresso powder
        </a>
        , as Im doing here; or 1 teaspoon
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/vietnamese-cinnamon-2-oz">
         cinnamon
        </a>
        (all optional).
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42582" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3625.jpg" title="IMG_3625"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Whisk in 2 cups (1 pint) whole milk. Thats right, this ice cream has no cream; its mostly chocolate, which is why its so delectably dark.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42584" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3629.jpg" title="IMG_3629"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Bring the mixture to a boil over medium heat, whisking frequently as it warms, then almost constantly as it becomes hot; you dont want anything sticking to the bottom of the pan.
       </p>
       <p>
        Next  more chocolate!
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42586" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3620.jpg" title="IMG_3620"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Im going to use
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/peters-burgundy-semisweet-chocolate-chunks-16-oz">
         Peters Burgundy Chunks
        </a>
        here, my favorite all-purpose semisweet chocolate. Since the cocoa is unsweetened, I didnt want to go TOO dark with the solid chocolate; and Peters has really nice flavor, IMHO.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42588" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3633.jpg" title="IMG_3633"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Remove the pan from the heat, and add 1 cup chopped semisweet or bittersweet chocolate, or chocolate chips; those are the aforementioned Burgundy chunks Im stirring in. Stir until the chocolate melts completely.
       </p>
       <p>
        Pour the chocolate into a bowl, and stir occasionally as it cools, to prevent a skin from forming.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42590" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3636.jpg" title="IMG_3636"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Lay a piece of plastic wrap on the surface of the chocolate, again to prevent a skin from forming, and refrigerate until its well chilled; overnight is good.
       </p>
       <p>
        Make sure the canister for your ice cream maker is in the freezer, too; it needs to be as cold as it can be.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42592" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3685.jpg" title="IMG_3685"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Next day, pour the chocolate into the
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/ice-cream-maker">
         ice cream maker,
        </a>
        and freeze according to the manufacturers directions.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42594" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3686.jpg" title="IMG_3686"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Youll see the ice cream gradually thicken, over the course of about 20 to 25 minutes. When its as thick as you like, add 1/4 cup coffee liqueur (e.g., Kahlua) or the liqueur of your choice.
       </p>
       <p>
        This step is optional, and you can certainly skip it if you prefer your ice cream alcohol-free. But liqueur not only enhances the flavor of the ice cream, it helps its  texture.
       </p>
       <p>
        Most homemade ice cream becomes rock-hard when stored in the  freezer longer than 6 hours or so; this one, with the addition of  liqueur, remains nicely scoopable indefinitely. Why? The alcohol in the  liqueur acts as anti-freeze.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42596" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3708.jpg" title="IMG_3708"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Serve the ice cream immediately, if desired; it will be very soft. Ive added a sprinkle of
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/shop/items/sparkling-white-sugar-1-lb">
         coarse white sparkling sugar,
        </a>
        just for appearance, and a bit of crunch.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-42598" src="/blog/files/2011/05/IMG_3788.jpg" title="IMG_3788"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        For harder ice cream, store in the freezer. Ice cream without liqueur  will become nicely hard in 3 hours. With the addition of liqueur, it  will take 6 hours or more to become quite solid.
       </p>
       <p>
        Ice cream without  liqueur will become rock-hard within 5 hours or so; to soften,  let it sit at room temperature for 30 minutes or so before serving.
       </p>
       <p>
        Interested in more ice cream recipes? Check out

         Strawberry Sorbet,
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes/sorbet-two-ways-recipe">
         Sorbet Two Ways,
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes/coconut-ice-cream-recipe">
         Coconut Ice Cream,
        </a>
        <a href="http://www.kingarthurflour.com/recipes/mocha-madness-ice-cream-recipe">

            <p>
             Debi- Creamy ice cream depends on the lactose crystals freezing evenly and smoothly, to leave a smooth texture. Additives like chocolate or espresso powder that fail to dissolve fully can cause the lactose crystals nuclei to form around the particles, creating a sandy texture. Another cause is a warm base that is put in the ice cream machine. Hope this helps understand the cause- and how to fix it for the next delicious batch- Laurie@KAF
            </p>
