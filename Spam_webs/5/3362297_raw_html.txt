     <p>
      Robert Bryden is a mountain of a man with a mind that makes him act like a child.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 26-year-old from Waikanae can't recall most of his past or the names of his parents.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 1.92m tall man used to be a "gifted" builder, but has had to learn to walk, talk and feed himself again after he nearly lost his life in a beating in Tory St, Wellington, in September.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Basically his filing cabinet is on a bit of a lean and it can't hold anything and until it's standing up then it's not going to be able to retain anything," his mother Vicki Bryden told The Dominion Post this week.
     </p>
     <p>
      "We've had to teach him absolutely everything again."
     </p>
     <p>
      Mr Bryden had been drinking in the central city with a friend when he was involved in an altercation with a man that led to him being chased, punched to the ground and kicked repeatedly in the head. He was taken to Wellington Hospital with life-threatening injuries.
     </p>
     <p>
      He spent nearly a month in intensive care on life support before his condition improved and he was transferred to a rehabilitation centre in Porirua.
     </p>
     <p>
      He returned home permanently in April and has a team of people, including a carer, occupational therapist, physiotherapist, speech and language therapist, psychologist and personal trainer, working to return his life to normal.
     </p>
     <p>
      He's learned to walk, talk and feed himself again, but his memory retention is still a major concern.
     </p>
     <p>
      "He doesn't call us by name or anything. We normally get called monkey," Mrs Bryden said. "It's pretty heartbreaking. I assumed I would be one of the first people he would name.
     </p>
     <p>
      "I just can't wait for the day he starts calling me mum."
     </p>
     <p>
      ABI-Rehabilitation occupational therapist Stephanie Slater said Mr Bryden may know his parents' names, but may not be able to find the words for them. "It may be memory, but we may never know."
     </p>
     <p>
      The brain was a complicated organ and several things contributed to memory loss, Miss Slater said.
     </p>
     <p>
      It was unknown whether he would recover fully from the "severe" injury, which had affected large parts of his brain, she said.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, Mr Bryden's progress had been phenomenal since he returned home. "The family are looking for miracles and they do happen."
     </p>
     <p>
      Ashleigh Bryden, 23, lived with her older brother in Otaihanga before he was attacked and said he had played a huge part in her two-year-old son Kahn's life.
     </p>
     <p>
      Now she can't leave the pair alone because of safety concerns. "He doesn't know his own strength."
     </p>
     <p>
