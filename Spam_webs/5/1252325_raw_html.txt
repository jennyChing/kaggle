     <p>
      <b>
       Refeeding syndrome
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Syndrome" title="Syndrome">
       syndrome
      </a>
      consisting of metabolic disturbances that occur as a result of reinstitution of nutrition to patients who are
      <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">
       starved
      </a>
      or severely
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malnourishment" title="Malnourishment">
       malnourished
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18583681_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid18583681-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The syndrome was first
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      described after
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">
       Americans
      </a>
      who, held by the
      <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">
       Japanese
      </a>
      as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war#World_War_II" title="Prisoners of war">
       prisoners of war
      </a>
      , had become malnourished during captivity and who were then released to the care of United States personnel in the
      <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
       Philippines
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid14847450_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pmid14847450-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Syndrome">
       Syndrome
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Refeeding_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Syndrome">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Any individual who has had negligible nutrient intake for more than 5 consecutive days is at risk of refeeding syndrome. Refeeding syndrome usually occurs within four days of starting to feed. Patients can develop fluid and
      <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
       electrolyte
      </a>
      disorders, especially
      <a href="/wiki/Hypophosphatemia" title="Hypophosphatemia">
       hypophosphatemia
      </a>
      , along with neurologic, pulmonary, cardiac, neuromuscular, and hematologic complications.
     </p>
     <p>
      During prolonged fasting the body aims to conserve muscle and protein breakdown by switching to
      <a href="/wiki/Ketone_bodies" title="Ketone bodies">
       ketone bodies
      </a>
      derived from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">
       fatty acids
      </a>
      as the main energy source. The liver decreases its rate of
      <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">
       gluconeogenesis
      </a>
      thus conserving muscle and protein. Many intracellular minerals become severely depleted during this period, although serum levels remain normal. Importantly,
      <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">
       insulin
      </a>
      secretion is suppressed in this fasted state and
      <a href="/wiki/Glucagon" title="Glucagon">
       glucagon
      </a>
      secretion is increased.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During refeeding, insulin secretion resumes in response to increased
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_sugar" title="Blood sugar">
       blood sugar
      </a>
      ; resulting in increased glycogen, fat and protein synthesis. This process requires phosphates, magnesium and potassium which are already depleted and the stores rapidly become used up. Formation of phosphorylated carbohydrate compounds in the liver and skeletal muscle depletes intracellular ATP and 2,3-diphosphoglycerate in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">
       red blood cells
      </a>
      , leading to cellular dysfunction and inadequate oxygen delivery to the body's organs. Refeeding increases the
      <a href="/wiki/Basal_metabolic_rate" title="Basal metabolic rate">
       basal metabolic rate
      </a>
      . Intracellular movement of electrolytes occurs along with a fall in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_serum" title="Blood serum">
       serum
      </a>
      electrolytes, including
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">
       phosphate
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
       potassium
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">
       magnesium
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">
       Glucose
      </a>
      , and levels of the B
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      vitamin
      <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">
       thiamine
      </a>
      may also fall. Cardiac
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhythmias" title="Arrhythmias">
       arrhythmias
      </a>
      are the most common cause of death from refeeding syndrome, with other significant risks including confusion,
      <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">
       coma
      </a>
      and convulsions and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_failure" title="Cardiac failure">
       cardiac failure
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      This syndrome can occur at the beginning of treatment for
      <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">
       anorexia nervosa
      </a>
      when patients have an increase in calorie intake and can be lethal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The shifting of electrolytes and fluid balance increases cardiac workload and heart rate. This can lead to acute heart failure. Oxygen consumption is also increased which strains the respiratory system and can make weaning from ventilation more difficult.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
       Treatment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Refeeding_syndrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Treatment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Refeeding syndrome can be fatal if not recognized and treated properly. An awareness of the condition and a high
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Index_of_suspicion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Index of suspicion (page does not exist)">
       index of suspicion
      </a>
      are required in order to make the diagnosis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Refeeding syndrome occurs most commonly in those who have lost weight rapidly. The electrolyte disturbances of the refeeding syndrome can occur within the first few days of refeeding, which can be undertaken through the oral or nasogastric routes. Close monitoring of blood biochemistry is therefore necessary in the early refeeding period.
      <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">
       Milk
      </a>
      is often the refeeding food of choice in this early period as it is naturally high in phosphate and easily tolerated by those who have been starved. If
      <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
       potassium
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">
       phosphate
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">
       magnesium
      </a>
      are low then this should be corrected via the oral route, or if severe with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parenteral" title="Parenteral">
       parenteral
      </a>
      treatment. Prescribing
      <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">
       thiamine
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B" title="Vitamin B">
       vitamin B
      </a>
      complex (strong) and a
      <a href="/wiki/Multivitamin" title="Multivitamin">
       multivitamin
      </a>
      and mineral is recommended. Biochemistry should be monitored regularly until it is stable.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_balance_(biology)" title="Energy balance (biology)">
       Energy intake
      </a>
      should remain only 50-70% that of normally required for the first 35 days.
     </p>
     <p>
      Patients who have been starved for some time often experience gastrointestinal disturbance during refeeding, in particular colicky abdominal pain,
      <a href="/wiki/Reflux" title="Reflux">
       reflux
      </a>
      symptoms, nausea and early
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satiety" title="Satiety">
       satiety
      </a>
      . To help manage this, patients are often prescribed pro-kinetic agents such as
      <a href="/wiki/Domperidone" title="Domperidone">
       domperidone
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Metoclopramide" title="Metoclopramide">
       metoclopramide
      </a>
      , as well as acid suppressants such as
      <a href="/wiki/Omeprazole" title="Omeprazole">
       omeprazole
      </a>
      . This may be of particular importance in patients suffering from
      <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">
       anorexia nervosa
      </a>
      , who can find these symptoms particularly distressing.
     </p>
