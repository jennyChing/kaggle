       <h3 class="post_title">
        Understanding is Remembering in Disguise
       </h3>
       <p class="byline">
        August 30th, 2011 | Debbie Stier
       </p>
       <div class="post-content">
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-3647" height="340" src="http://perfectscoreproject.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/map.jpg" title="map" width="420"/>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         If youre anything like me..
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <strong>
          .
          <em>
           E V E R Y T H I N G
          </em>
          takes
          <em>
           way
          </em>
          longer than you think it will.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         With regard to the SAT (or I should say, with regard to learning anything,
         <strong>
          <em>
           and
          </em>
         </strong>
         remembering what you learned), multiply that idea by at least 50.
        </p>
        <p>
         Thankfully, at this stage in my life, the SAT stakes are about as high as the public humiliation I will have caused myself if I dont improve.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, if you
         <em>
          actually
         </em>
         need a good score for a reason more valid than thinking this is a good time, follow this mans advice:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <strong>
           As far as anyone knows, the only way to develop mental facility is to repeat the target process again and again and again.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           So, apparently, studying hard doesnt protect against forgetting  But something else does: continued practice.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Practice is another significant contributor to a good transfer. Working lots of problems of a particular type makes it more likely that you will recognize the underlying structure of the problem, even if you havent seen this particular version of the problem before.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           It is virtually impossible to become proficient at a mental task without extended practice.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Children do differ in intelligence, but intelligence can be changed through sustained hard work.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Intelligence is malleable. It can be improved.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           the amount of information you retain depends on what you already have.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Understanding is remembering in disguise. We understand new ideas by relating them to things we already know.
          </strong>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         These are a few of the many many passages I highlighted from
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Why-Dont-Students-Like-School/dp/047059196X/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1314646032&amp;sr=8-1">
          Daniel Willinghams book
         </a>
         about how we learn.
        </p>
        <p>
         If you dont believe me, believe
         <em>
          him.
         </em>
         I promise, hes not wrong.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>

         </em>
        </p>

                    <div class="dsq-comment-header" id="dsq-comment-header-1152">
                     <cite id="dsq-cite-1152">
                      <span id="dsq-author-user-1152">

                      </span>
                     </cite>
                    </div>
                    <div class="dsq-comment-body" id="dsq-comment-body-1152">
                     <div class="dsq-comment-message" id="dsq-comment-message-1152">
                      <p>
                       I dont advise students to game the test  in fact, I dont think it can be gamed.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       Many students approach a math problem only one way. And if they dont know how to do it the right way, they stop and turn off their brains. Or they solve it out the right way and it takes too much time and they cant finish the math section.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       The SAT rewards students who can find the right answer *in the shortest amount of time*. The College Board doesnt care *how* you arrive at the answer (whereas in school you often must show your work of solving the question correctly in order to get full credit)
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       One of the best examples of this is spring problem  Test 5, Section 2, Question 5 in the Blue Book. The force required to stretch a spring beyond its natural length is proportional to how far the spring is being stretched. If a force of 15 pounds stretches a spring 8 centimeters beyond its natural length, what force is needed to stretch the spring 20 centimeters? Answer choices are A) 23, B) 27, C) 30.5, D) 35, E) 37.5
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       If you know how to do the algebra, you just set up the proportion and solve it. 15 lbs/8 cm = x lbs/20 cm. x = 37.5 lbs.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       But for some reason a lot of my students struggle with this problem. So I ask them to *draw* the problem (Trick number one.)
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       If that doesnt spark an insight, I ask them what would happen if they doubled the force  poke the problem. (Trick number two.) (I work with a lot of math phobic students)
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       If you applied 30 lbs, the spring would stretch 16 cm. Hmm. Not far enough. But they can eliminate answers A-C.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       From here they can guess or backsolve with the two remaining answer choices, set up the proportion and solve it directly, or poke it again.
                      </p>
                      <p>
