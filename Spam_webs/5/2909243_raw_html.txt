         <h1 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://modernreject.com/2011/10/jesus-a-lover-or-a-fighter/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Is Jesus a Lover or a Fighter?">
           Is Jesus a Lover or a Fighter?
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="byline">
           By
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://modernreject.com/nicole-cottrell/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Nicole Cottrell">
             Nicole Cottrell
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
          
          <span class="cat-links">
           Posted in
           <a href="http://modernreject.com/category/dying-daily/" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in Dying Daily">
            Dying Daily
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://modernreject.com/category/featured/jesus/" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in Jesus">
            Jesus
           </a>
           ,
           <a href="http://modernreject.com/category/featured/theology/" rel="category tag" title="View all posts in Theology">
            Theology
           </a>
          </span>
         </div>

        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <a href="http://modernreject.com/wp-content/uploads/lover-fighter.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-1249092843" height="225" src="//modernreject.com/wp-content/uploads/lover-fighter-300x225.jpg" title="lover-fighter" width="300"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           from the archives
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          When I thought of Jesus, I used to think of a lover. He is the lover of our souls, after all. When we see images or interpretations of Jesus in Biblical times, He is always portrayed as gentle, kind, and loving. I always used to imagine Him almost whispering His parables, as if to say,
          <em>
           Hush, still your hearts, quiet yourselves before me, and listen to what I am sayingshhh.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          We have all seen the stereotypical Jesus painting: The Son of Man, in a white flowing robe, with children, sheep, or both, strewn around His feet.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           And then I read the Book of Revelation for the first time
          </strong>
          <span id="more-1249089984">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          I did not know what to expect as I read, but I heeded the words in the very beginning of the book which say, Blessed is the one who reads aloud the words of this prophecy. So I read it aloud and was awakened to a new vision of my Lord.
         </p>
         <p>
          Then I saw heaven opened, and behold, a white horse!
          <strong>
           The one sitting on it called Faithful and True, and in righteousness he judges and makes war.
          </strong>
          His eyes are like a flame of fire, and on his head are many diadems, and he has a name written that no one knows but himself. He is clothed in a robe dripped in blood, and the name by which he is called is The Word of God. Revelation 19:11-13
         </p>
         <p>
          I was suddenly struck with an image of Jesus that I had never had before, not only was He to be covered in blood, but He bears a name written on Him that only He knows. What would that name be? In vain, I tried to imagine what He would name Himself.
          <strong>
           I was left with no answer to that question, but instead, for the first time, I saw Him as He is: gentleness and strength, fierce and freeing a lover
           <em>
            and
           </em>
           a fighter.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          It is difficult for some Christians to recognize Jesus as both a meek and sympathetic Messiah come to bring peace
          <em>
           and
          </em>
          a Righteous King ruling with a rod of iron. Many outside the church have difficulty reconciling these opposing ideas, as well, and use them as a means with which to call into question the character of God.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The dichotomy of God in scripture is striking.
          </strong>
          Author
          <a href="http://rachelheldevans.com/blog">
           Rachel Held Evans
          </a>
          , in a
          <a href="http://rachelheldevans.com/bible-hard">
           post on her blog
          </a>
          , described the Bible as teeming with conflict and contrast, brimming with paradox, held together by creative tension. It is difficult for me to grasp the duality of God within scripture, and yet, the more I grow to know the Lord, the more this seemingly huge juxtaposition is lessened.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Jesus is God and, as God, He can be both:
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           The Prince of Peace
           <strong>
            <em>
             and
            </em>
           </strong>
           The Deliverer
          </li>
          <li>
           A Fountain of Living Waters
           <strong>
            <em>
             and
            </em>
           </strong>
           a consuming fire
          </li>
          <li>
           The Preserver of men
           <strong>
            <em>
             and
            </em>
           </strong>
           a jealous God
          </li>
          <li>
           A rewarder of those who diligently seek Him
           <strong>
            <em>
             and
            </em>
           </strong>
           a rock of offense
          </li>
          <li>
           A Father of mercies
           <strong>
            <em>
             and
            </em>
           </strong>
           He that judges righteously
          </li>
          <li>
           God of patience and consolation
           <strong>
            <em>
             and
            </em>
           </strong>
           He that ought to be feared
          </li>
          <li>
           A strong tower from the enemy
           <em>
            <strong>
             and
            </strong>
           </em>
           the Lord mighty in battle
          </li>
          <li>
           My hope
           <em>
            <strong>
             and
            </strong>
           </em>
           my judge
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          I take comfort in knowing my God not only loves me, but He fights for me. He does not just forgive me, but He seeks justice on my behalf. He pours out Grace, but also hates evil and abhors sin. He does not call me to battle to then leave me there, defenseless and unarmed. He engages in the battle right beside me with sword and shield, all the while loving me with His everlasting love.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Do you generally think of Jesus as a lover or a fighter? Is the dichotomy difficult for you? What is your favorite God juxtaposition? Why?
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           <a href="http://according2g.com/tag/im-a-lover-not-a-fighter/" target="_blank">
            image props
           </a>
          </em>
         </p>

            </p>

             <br/>
             until he pleads my cause and executes judgment for me. He will bring me out to the light; I shall look upon his vindication.

            </p>

In recent months I have begun to adopt a personal philosophy that I dont have to come to a quick and final conclusion about many aspects of doctrine or the Christian life.  In many cases, I simply dont have enough data to do so, and my perspective is not broad enough.  I have also come to be more and more wary of folks who seem to adamantly believe that they do have things figured out.
            </p>
            <p>
             Nicole, your paragraph on taking comfort in knowing my God not only loves me, but He fights for me is a good summary of the new direction my spiritual life is taking.  I am learning to trust that God has good plans for me, and that my own efforts to make things happen often serve only to frustrate his efforts.  My trust for God also extends to the belief that if he wants my behavior or perspective changed on some issue, he will find some way to communicate this to me or change my convictions to match his holiness.  I trust that as long as I continue to trust him, I need not live in fear of his judgement.  He will guide me to a place where he can both make use of my life, as well as a place where he can bless me with good things that satisfy my soul.
