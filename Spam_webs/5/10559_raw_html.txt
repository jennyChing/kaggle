         <h1 class="entry-title">
          When It Comes To Your Food Portion Size, Less
          <i>
           Really
          </i>
          Is More
         </h1>
         <ul class="post-details clearfix">
          <li>
           1 year ago by
           <a href="http://www.blisstree.com/author/dinahashem/" rel="author" title="Posts by Dina Hashem">
            Dina Hashem
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a href="http://www.blisstree.com/2014/07/01/food/smaller-food-portion-size-better-for-you/#comments" title="Comment on When It Comes To Your Food Portion Size, Less Really Is More">
            <span class="dsq-postid" rel="306026 http://www.blisstree.com/?p=306026">
             2 Comments 
            </span>
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a href="#top" onclick="show_modal_window('share-tip-window');">
            Share a Tip
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p>
          <a href="http://cdn2.blisstree.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/kid-eating-big-stack-of-pancakes.jpg">
           <img alt="kid eating big stack of pancakes" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-306032" height="427" src="http://cdn2.blisstree.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/kid-eating-big-stack-of-pancakes.jpg" title="kid eating big stack of pancakes" width="640"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          A new study has come out that shows the Greeks were onto something when they said 
          <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/cleanplate/2011/01/11/what_is_moderation.html" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.slate.com']);" target="_blank">
           Everything in moderation
          </a>
          . At least when it comes to your portion size. Research has demonstrated that people who ate a smaller amount of food
          <a href="http://blogs.scientificamerican.com/food-matters/2014/06/30/large-portion-sizes-bad-for-business/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://blogs.scientificamerican.com']);" target="_blank">
           enjoyed their meal more
          </a>
          than people who ate a larger portion. Too bad this research wasnt out when former Mayor Bloomberg was
          <a href="http://www.blisstree.com/2013/03/11/sex-relationships/soda-ban-2/" target="_blank">
           declaring war on big sodas
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The study, published in
          <a href="http://pss.sagepub.com/content/early/2014/06/02/0956797614534268.abstract" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://pss.sagepub.com']);" target="_blank">
           Psychological Science
          </a>
          , examined how memory plays a role in food consumption. Stanford University students were directed to eat either a small or large portion of Nut Thin crackers, and then rate their enjoyment of each. If
          <a href="http://www.blisstree.com/2006/09/22/mental-health-well-being/super-size-me-in-a-lab-setting/" target="_blank">
           Supersizing
          </a>
          is to retain
          <em>
           any
          </em>
          meaning in our society, then the result should skew positively toward larger portions. But its bad news for fast food, because it turns out the smaller portions meant more joy. I know, I know; this study is way too unpatriotic to come out so close to the Fourth of July.
         </p>
         <p>
          Sensory-specific satiety, the phenomenon whereinmore food decreases a persons enjoyment of it, is thought to be caused bya recency bias in memory. That mouthful of big words basically means that yourmemory of the final moments of a meal, when youre most satisfied, interferes with your memory of the initial moments. So as exciting as those first bites of your birthday cake are, if you over-indulge and eat the whole thing, youre more likely to remember the end where youre having trouble breathing.
         </p>
         <p>
          This phenomenon also affects how long someone will wait to consume food again. In the experiment, the students were asked when theyd like to receive a free box of the crackers. Those who had the larger portion opted to wait longer for the box than those who had the smaller, more enjoyable portion. Thats where the study confounds me, because no amount of intestinal pain has ever made me postpone free food. Moderation may not be my strong suit.
         </p>
         <p>
          (Image: ahturner/Shutterstock)
         </p>
