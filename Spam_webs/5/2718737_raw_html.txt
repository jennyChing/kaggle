           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2007/11/05/071105fa_fact_grafton">
              <img alt="Newyorkerill" border="0" height="310" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/11/01/newyorkerill.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="Newyorkerill" width="233"/>
             </a>
             Anthony Grafton in this week's
             <a href="http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2007/11/05/071105fa_fact_grafton">
              <strong>
               New Yorker
              </strong>
             </a>
             provides a lucid and level-headed analysis of the many fervid efforts to digitize the universe of the printed word, such as the
             <strong>
              <a href="http://books.google.com/googlebooks/library.html">
               Google Library Project
              </a>
             </strong>
             and Microsoft's
             <a href="http://publisher.live.com/">
              <strong>
               Live Search Books Publishers Program
              </strong>
             </a>
             , as well as more focused (and frequently non-profit) ventures such as the
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.opencontentalliance.org/">
               Open Content Alliance
              </a>
             </strong>
             and more subject-focused efforts such as
             <a href="http://aluka.org">
              <strong>
               Aluka
              </strong>
             </a>
             (about which you've previously
             <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/06/digital_library.html">
              read in this space
             </a>
             ).
            </p>
            <p>
             <em>
              In fact,
             </em>
             writes Grafton,
             <em>
              the Internet will not bring us a universal library, much less
an encyclopedic record of human experience. None of the firms now
engaged in digitization projects claim that it will create anything of
the kind. The hype and rhetoric make it hard to grasp what Google and
Microsoft and their partner libraries are actually doing ...
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Google and Microsoft pursue their own interests, in ways that they
think will generate income, and this has prompted a number of major
libraries to work with the Open Content Alliance, a nonprofit
book-digitizing venture. Many important books will remain untouched:
Google, for example, has no immediate plans to scan books from the
first couple of centuries of printing ... Other sectors of the worlds book production are not even catalogued
and accessible on site, much less available for digitization. The
materials from the poorest societies may not attract companies that
rely on subscriptions or on advertising for cash flow.
             </em>
            </p>
            <p>
             Closer to home Grafton also provides a concise and complimentary history of libraries from third millennium B.C. Mesopotamia to the present. The conclusion includes an encomium to libraries that might remind readers of the
             <em>
              retardaire
             </em>
             Nicholson Baker. But unlike Baker Grafton makes a case for libraries being an enduring participant in the research process, not the sole player:
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              <em>
               For now and for the foreseeable future, any serious reader will have to
know how to travel down two very different roads simultaneously. No one
should avoid the broad, smooth, and open road that leads through the
screen. But if you want to know what one of Coleridges annotated books
or an early Spider-Man comic really looks and feels like, or if you
just want to read one of those millions of books which are being
digitized, you still have to do it the old way, and you will have to
for decades to come.
              </em>
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             Apart from the article,
             <em>
              "Future reading: digitization and its discontents,"
             </em>
             there is a
             <a href="http://www.newyorker.com/online/2007/11/05/071105on_onlineonly_grafton">
              complementary online-only article
             </a>
             with links to many of the web resources cited in the article.
            </p>
            <h6 id="credit">
             <strong>
              ILLUSTRATION: TOM GAULD
             </strong>
            </h6>

        <div class="entry-category-ancient_america entry-category-archaeology entry-category-books entry-category-eresources entry-category-facsimiles entry-category-native_america entry-category-rare_book_room entry-category-ross_day entry-author-ross_day entry-type-post entry" id="entry-40088484">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/10/peruvian-antiqu.html">
            Illustrations from "Antigedades Peruanas" on Flickr
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <span style="font-size: 1.1em;">
              An interesting new development in the 'digitization' of books is reported in a
              <a href="http://bibliodyssey.blogspot.com/2007/09/peruvian-antiquities.html">
               recent post
              </a>
              by
              <a href="http://bibliodyssey.blogspot.com/">
               BibliOdyssey
              </a>
              , one of particular interest to our readers.
             </span>
            </p>
            <div style="text-align: center;">
             <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/antiguedadesperuanas/1428705833/in/set-72157602124747818">
              <img alt="1428705833_d3fda7b782" border="0" height="354" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/10/11/1428705833_d3fda7b782.jpg" title="1428705833_d3fda7b782" width="500"/>
             </a>
            </div>
            <p>
             Sixty-two plates from
             <a href="http://www.worldcat.org/oclc/3027283&amp;referer=brief_results">
              <em>
               Antig
              </em>
              <em>
               
               <o:p>
               </o:p>
              </em>
              <em>
               edades Peruanas
              </em>
             </a>
             (1851) by Rivera and Tschudi have been
             <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/antiguedadesperuanas/sets/72157602124747818/">
              uploaded to flickr
             </a>
             and are freely available for researchers and admirers of this gorgeously illustrated work. (For those nearer to the Goldwater Library, you can also consult the original:
             <a href="http://library.metmuseum.org/record=b1304729">
              YH R62a SpecColl-Folio
             </a>
             )
            </p>
            <div style="text-align: center;">
             <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/antiguedadesperuanas/1429592044/in/set-72157602124747818/">
              <img alt="1429592044_156462c14a" border="0" height="360" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/10/11/1429592044_156462c14a.jpg" title="1429592044_156462c14a" width="500"/>
             </a>
            </div>
            <p>
             As peakay of BibliOdyssey glosses it,
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              <em>
               In the 1830s and 1840s, Peruvian museum curator Mariano Eduardo de
Rivero and Swiss naturalist Dr Johann Jakob von Tschudi undertook a
survey of all known relics, ruins, records, bones, artefacts and
artworks relating to the pre-Columbian civilisations of Peru.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The resultant 1851 book,
               <span style="font-style: italic;">
                'Antigedades Peruanas'
               </span>
               ,
was a thorough and critical archaeological, ethnographic and
anthropological review for its time, although their conclusions about,
for instance, racial groupings have been superseded.
              </em>
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             A digitized version of the
             <a href="http://www.archive.org/search.php?query=TSCHUDI%20peru">
              English-language translation
             </a>
             (1855), which is less lavishly illustrated, is currently freely available through the
             <a href="http://www.archive.org/index.php">
              Internet Archive
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             This carries on the continuing work of BibliOdyssey to highlight historic illustrations available on the web. Previous posts include
             <a href="http://bibliodyssey.blogspot.com/2005/11/chief-celebratory-portraits.html">
              portraits of Native North American chiefs
             </a>
             , McKenney and Hall's
             <a href="http://bibliodyssey.blogspot.com/2005/10/north-american-indians-in-1800s.html">
              <em>
               History of the Indian Tribes of North America
              </em>
             </a>
             (1836-1844), Guaman Poma's
             <a href="http://bibliodyssey.blogspot.com/2006/10/andean-chronicle.html">
              <em>
               Nueva Coronica y Buen Gobierno
              </em>
             </a>
             , and Captain James Cook's
             <a href="http://bibliodyssey.blogspot.com/2005/10/cooks-voyage-to-pacific-ocean.html">
              <em>
               Voyage to the Pacific Ocean
              </em>
             </a>
             of 1784.
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         October 02, 2007
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-africa entry-category-alisa_lagamma entry-category-books entry-category-exhibitions_in_aaoa entry-category-new_york entry-category-ross_day entry-author-ross_day entry-type-post entry" id="entry-39615098">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/10/eternal-ancesto.html">
            Eternal Ancestors: The Art of the Central African Reliquary
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/.shared/image.html?/photos/uncategorized/2007/10/02/nlo_bieri.jpg" onclick="window.open(this.href, '_blank', 'width=300,height=470,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Nlo_bieri" border="0" height="235" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/10/02/nlo_bieri.jpg" style="margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px; float: right;" title="Nlo_bieri" width="150"/>
             <span style="font-size: 1.1em;">
              Beginning October 2, 2007, The Metropolitan Museum of Art presents a special exhibition of
acclaimed sculptural masterpieces from the heart of Africa's
equatorial rainforest. The exhibition explores not only the significance of the
works presented in their countries of origin but also how their reception in
the West led them to enter the mainstream of universal art.
             </span>
            </p>
            <p>
             <span style="font-size: 0.8em;">
              at right:
              <strong>
               Female Reliquary Figure (Nlo Bieri)
              </strong>
              , 19th-20th century, Gabon or Equatorial Guinea; Fang, Okak group; The Michael C. Rockefeller Memorial Collection, Gift of Nelson A. Rockefeller, 1965 (1978.412.441), Catalog no. 23
             </span>
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <span style="font-size: 0.8em;">
               View
               <a href="http://www.metmuseum.org/special/eternal_ancestors/images.asp">
                other objects
               </a>
               on exhibit
              </span>
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             Organized
thematically,
             <strong>
              <em>
               Eternal Ancestors: The Art of the Central African Reliquary
              </em>
             </strong>
             explains the sources of cultural and spiritual inspiration that led to their
creation in equatorial Africa. Drawn from the most
important collections of African art in Europe and the 

United States, the more than 150 works featured in
the exhibition relate to 14 distinct traditions in Cameroon, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, the Republic of Congo, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. They were created to
celebrate the lives of an extended family's most notable ancestors and to give
expression to their ongoing role as advocates with the divine.
            </p>
            <p>
             Since sacred relics have served as the catalysts for some of the most exalted and revered creations in the history of Western, Eastern, and African civilizations, the exhibition considers
             <strong>
              reliquaries from other world cultures
             </strong>
             alongside those produced in Africa, thereby drawing upon related works from other parts of the Metropolitans encyclopedic collections.
            </p>
            <p>
             Many of the works on view won renown as fresh sources of inspiration for
early 20th-century Western avant-garde artists, who collected them and kept
them in their studios.
             <a href="http://library.metmuseum.org/record=b1579611">
              Pablo Picasso
             </a>
             , Andr Derain, Maurice de Vlaminck, and
Henri Matisse were among the many artists who not only collected African
sculpture but who also carefully studied it in the newly formed ethnographic
museums of the day.
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              <span style='font-size: 12pt; font-family: "Times New Roman";'>
              </span>
              "Given that Western artists' engagement with these African works was essentially concerned with their formal qualities  namely the dynamic portrayal of the human form as it was distilled into essential elements  and given this emphasis on their originality, both the origins and spiritual meaning of these great works for their creators were subsequently largely ignored," said
              <strong>
               Alisa LaGamma
              </strong>
              , Curator in the Department of the Arts of Africa, Oceania, and the Americas at the Metropolitan and organizer of this exhibition. "Eternal Ancestors seeks to reveal the mystery behind the original spiritual and social imperatives that led to their creation by examining the history of the reception of these African artifacts in the West. I believe that an awareness of what inspired them allows us to appreciate Africa's heritage in a meaningful context."
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             <o:p>
             </o:p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Film footage
             </strong>
             in the exhibition will emphasize the importance of
performance rites as devotional forms of expression and demonstrate the
music and dance that are integral parts of those ceremonies. While
acknowledging that these works have ultimately transcended their
original cultural contexts, the exhibition seeks to examine them on
their own terms.
             <br/>
             <o:p>
             </o:p>
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/.shared/image.html?/photos/uncategorized/2007/10/02/catalogue_cover_front_3.jpg" onclick="window.open(this.href, '_blank', 'width=640,height=846,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
              <img alt="Catalogue_cover_front_3" border="0" height="330" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/10/02/catalogue_cover_front_3.jpg" style="margin: 0px 10px 0px 0px; float: left;" title="Catalogue_cover_front_3" width="250"/>
             </a>
             The
             <strong>
              illustrated catalogue
             </strong>
             <em>
              Eternal Ancestors: The Art of the Central African Reliquary -
             </em>
             with essays by specialists in various fields - will accompany the
exhibition. Published by The Metropolitan Museum of Art and distributed
by Yale University Press, it will be available at the Museum's book
shops for $65. It is also
             <a href="http://library.metmuseum.org/record=b1632007">
              available for consultation
             </a>
             in the Goldwater Library.
            </p>
            <p>
             The exhibition will close March 2, 2008.
             <br/>
            </p>
            <p>
             The exhibition is made possible in part by the William Randolph Hearst
Foundation. The catalogue is made possible by The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation and the
Doris Duke Fund for Publications.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              From the Metropolitan Museum of Art Web Site
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             --
             <a href="http://www.metmuseum.org/special/se_event.asp?OccurrenceId={7DFC0947-4620-4525-B6DA-2ACC0127B465}">
              Special Exhibitions
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             --
             <a href="http://www.metmuseum.org/press_room/full_release.asp?prid={A73D2A3B-AFB0-410F-B054-93BFCE27DC1D}">
              Press Release
             </a>
             (June 2007)
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         September 19, 2007
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-africa entry-category-books entry-category-exhibitions_elsewhere entry-category-lectures entry-category-new_york entry-category-photography entry-category-ross_day entry-author-ross_day entry-type-post entry" id="entry-39102965">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/09/makonde-masquer.html">
            Makonde masquerade @ Wallach Art Gallery
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <strong>
              <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/.shared/image.html?/photos/uncategorized/2007/09/19/revolutions.jpg" onclick="window.open(this.href, '_blank', 'width=640,height=598,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
               <img alt="Revolutions" border="0" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/09/19/revolutions.jpg" style="margin: 0px 10px 0px 5px; float: left; width: 265px; height: 248px;" title="Revolutions"/>
              </a>
             </strong>
             <span style="font-size: 0.8em;">
              Mapiko
              <em>
               dancer entering a village accompanied by a
              </em>
              lipalipanda
              <em>
               player. Matambalale Village, Cabo Delgado Province, Mozambique. Photograph by A. Bortolot, 2004.
              </em>
             </span>
             <strong>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <span style="font-size: 1.1em;">
               <a href="http://www.columbia.edu/cu/wallach/htm/exhibitions.html">
                Revolutions: A Century of Makonde Masquerade in Mozambique
               </a>
              </span>
             </strong>
             <span style="font-size: 1.1em;">
              , an exhibition of masks and other performance objects from East Africa, will be on view at the Columbia University
              <a href="http://www.columbia.edu/cu/wallach/index.html">
               Miriam and Ira D. Wallach Art Gallery
              </a>
              from 19 September through 8 December 2007.
             </span>
            </p>
            <p>
             The exhibition features more than 60 examples of
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makonde">
              Makonde
             </a>
             art, on loan from public and private collections, including a comprehensive selection of masks, figural statuary, puppets, drums, dance scepters, and ornate metal bracelets dating from the late 1800s to the present day.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Alexander Bortolot
             </strong>
             , a doctoral candidate in art history and archeology at Columbia University -- and a former graduate intern in the Department of the Arts of Africa, Oceania, and the Americas at the Metropolitan Museum of Art -- is the curator of the exhibit and author of the forthcoming accompanying catalog. In addition to the curatorial essay based on Bortolot's research, the publication will include more than 60 color images that provide a rare record of Makonde visual culture.
            </p>
            <p>
             Alex will deliver a
             <strong>
              gallery talk
             </strong>
             on Thursday, 25 October, at 6:30 p.m.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Miriam &amp; Ira D. Wallach Art Gallery
             </strong>
             <br/>
             826 Schermerhorn Hall, Columbia University (
             <a href="http://www.columbia.edu/about_columbia/map/">
              campus map

           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/08/from-the-ny-tim.html">
            Timbuktu digital library project
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             From the NY
             <em>
              Times,
             </em>
             8/7/2007
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/.shared/image.html?/photos/uncategorized/2007/08/10/koranms.jpg" onclick="window.open(this.href, '_blank', 'width=640,height=428,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
              <img alt="Koranms" border="0" height="428" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/08/10/koranms.jpg" title="Koranms" width="640"/>
             </a>
             <br/>
             <em>
              <span style="font-size: 0.8em;">
               A copy of the Koran from the 12th century. According to notes in the text, it was bought for a Moroccan king for a sum of gold. (Candace Feit for The New York Times)
              </span>
             </em>
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/08/07/world/africa/07mali.html?ex=1344139200&amp;en=bff7dd6855272c4d&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
              <span style="font-size: 1.2em;">
               <strong>
                Timbuktu Hopes Ancient Texts Spark a Revival
               </strong>
              </span>
             </a>
             <br/>
             by LYDIA POLGREEN
            </p>
            <p>
             TIMBUKTU, Mali  Ismal Diadi Hadara held a treasure in his slender fingers that has somehow endured through 11 generations  a square of battered leather enclosing a history of the two branches of his family, one side reaching back to the Visigoths in Spain and the other to the ancient origins of the
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Songhai_Empire">
              Songhai
             </a>
             emperors who ruled this city at its zenith.
            </p>
            <p>
             This is our familys story, he said, carefully leafing through the unbound pages. It was written in 1519.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/.shared/image.html?/photos/uncategorized/2007/08/10/timbuktu2.gif" onclick="window.open(this.href, '_blank', 'width=640,height=480,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
              <img alt="Timbuktu2" border="0" height="120" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/08/10/timbuktu2.gif" style="margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px; float: right;" title="Timbuktu2" width="160"/>
             </a>
             <span style="font-size: 0.6em;">
              <a href="http://www.sum.uio.no/timbuktu/">
               Photo source
              </a>
              &gt;
             </span>
             <br/>
             The musty collection of fragile, crumbling pages, written in the florid Arabic script of the sixteenth century, is also this once forgotten outposts future.
            </p>
            <p>
             A surge of interest in ancient books, hidden for centuries in houses along Timbuktus dusty streets and in leather trunks in nomad camps, is raising hopes that Timbuktu  a city whose name has become a staccato synonym for nowhere  may once again claim a place at the intellectual heart of Africa.
            </p>
            <p>
             I am a historian, Mr. Hadara said. I know from my research that great cities seldom get a second chance. Yet here we have a second chance because we held on to our past.
            </p>
            <p>
             This ancient city, a prisoner of the relentless sands of the Sahara and a changing world that prized access to the sea over the grooves worn by camel hooves across the dunes, is on the verge of a renaissance.
            </p>
            <p>
             We want to build an Alexandria for black Africa, said Mohamed Dicko, director of the Ahmed Baba Institute, a government-run library in Timbuktu. This is our chance to regain our place in history.
            </p>
            <p>
             The South African government is building a new library for the institute, a state-of-the-art facility that will house, catalog and digitize tens of thousands of books and make their contents available, many for the first time, to researchers. Charities and governments from Europe, the United States and the Middle East have poured hundreds of thousands of dollars into the citys musty family libraries, which are being expanded and transformed into research institutions, drawing scholars from around the world eager to translate and interpret the long forgotten manuscripts.
            </p>
            <p>
             The Libyan government is planning to transform a dingy 40-room hotel into a luxurious 100-room resort, complete with Timbuktus only swimming pool and space to hold academic and religious conferences. Libya is also digging a new canal that will bring the Niger River to the edge of Timbuktu.
            </p>
            <p>
             Timbuktus new seekers have a variety of motives. South Africa and Libya are vying for influence on the African stage, each promoting its vision of a resurgent Africa. Spain has direct links to some of the history stored here, while American charities began giving money after Henry Louis Gates Jr., the Harvard professor of African studies, featured the manuscripts in a television documentary series in the late 1990s.
            </p>
            <p>
             This new chapter in the story of Timbuktu, whose fortunes fell in the twilight of the Middle Ages, is almost as extraordinary as those that preceded it ...
             <strong>
              <em>
               [
               <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/08/07/world/africa/07mali.html?ex=1344139200&amp;en=bff7dd6855272c4d&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
                Read more
               </a>
               ]
              </em>
             </strong>
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Library of Congress
              <a href="http://www.loc.gov/exhibits/mali/">
               online exhibition
              </a>
              of Malian manuscripts from Timbuktu
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://www.sum.uio.no/research/mali/timbuktu/project/index.html">
               Timbuktu Manuscript Project
              </a>
              (University of Oslo Centre for Development and the Environment)
              <ul>
               <li>
                <a href="http://www.sum.uio.no/research/mali/timbuktu/libraries.html">
                 <em>
                  Visit the Timbuktu Libraries
                 </em>
                </a>
               </li>
              </ul>
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://library.metmuseum.org/search~/X?">

        <h2 class="date-header">
         July 09, 2007
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-blogs entry-category-books entry-category-contemporary entry-category-current_affairs entry-category-joy_garnett entry-category-libraries entry-category-local_vernacular entry-category-new_york entry-category-opinion entry-category-research entry-category-webtech entry-category-weblogs entry-author-newsgrist entry-type-post entry" id="entry-36272872">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/07/too-cool-for-th.html">
            Too Cool for Their Shush-shirts
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2007/07/09/08libr6001.jpg">
              <img alt="08libr6001" border="0" height="300" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/07/09/08libr6001.jpg" title="08libr6001" width="600"/>
             </a>
             <br/>
             <span style="color: #666666;">
              EAT, DRINK, BE LITERARY From left, Jessica Pigza, Maria Falgoust, Jeff
Buckley and Sarah Murphy at a social event for librarians where the
author Robert Sullivan, far right, spoke. photo: Michael Nagle for The New York Times
             </span>
            </p>
            <p>
             <em>
              via NYTimes
             </em>
             :
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/07/08/fashion/08librarian.html?ex=1341547200&amp;en=b7c01214ad013074&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
               <strong>
                A Hipper Crowd of Shushers
               </strong>
              </a>
              <br/>
              By KARA JESELLA
              <br/>
              Published: July 8, 2007
              <br/>
              ON a Sunday night last month at Daddys, a bar in Williamsburg,
Brooklyn, more than a dozen people in their 20s and 30s gathered at a
professional soiree, drinking frozen margaritas and nibbling
store-bought cookies. With their thrift-store inspired clothes and
abundant tattoos, they looked as if they could be filmmakers, Web
designers, coffee shop purveyors or artists.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/07/08/fashion/08librarian.html?pagewanted=2&amp;ei=5090&amp;en=b7c01214ad013074&amp;ex=1341547200&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
              <img alt="08libr1903" border="0" height="285" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/07/09/08libr1903.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="08libr1903" width="190"/>
             </a>
             <span style="font-size: 0.8em;color: #666666;">
              SOCIAL BOOKWORMS Maria Falgoust helped start Desk Set to meet like-minded librarians. Photo: Chester Higgins Jr./The New York Times
             </span>
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               <strong>
                <span style="font-size: 1.4em;color: #666666;">
                 
                </span>
                <span style="font-size: 1.2em;color: #000000;">
                 Maria Falgoust, 31, is also a founder of Desk Set, which took its name from the 1957 Katharine Hepburn-Spencer Tracy romantic comedy. A student who works part time at the library at Saint Anns School, she was inspired to become a librarian by a friend, a public librarian who works with teenagers and goes to rock shows regularly.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 Since matriculating to Palmer, Ms. Falgoust has met plenty of other like-minded librarians at places such as Brooklyn Label, a restaurant, and at Punk Rope, an exercise class. Theyre everywhere you go, she said.
                </span>
               </strong>
               <strong>
                <span style="font-size: 1.4em;color: #666666;">
                 
                </span>
               </strong>
              </p>
             </blockquote>
            </blockquote>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              When talk turned to a dance party the group had recently given at a nearby restaurant, their profession became clearer.
             </p>
             <p>
              Did you try the special drinks? Sarah Gentile, 29, asked Jennifer Yao, 31, referring to the colorfully named cocktails.
             </p>
             <p>
              I got the Joy of Sex, Ms. Yao replied. I thought for sure it was French Women Dont Get Fat.
             </p>
             <p>
              Ms.
Yao could be forgiven for being confused: the drink was numbered and
the guests had to guess the name. 613.96 C, said Ms. Yao,
cryptically, then apologized: Sorry if I talk in Dewey.
             </p>
             <p>
              That would be the Dewey Decimal System. The groups members were librarians. Or, in some cases, guybrarians.
             </p>
             <p>
              He
hates being called that, said Sarah Murphy, one of the evenings
organizers and a founder of the Desk Set, a social group for librarians
and library students.
             </p>
             <p>
              Ms. Murphy was speaking of Jeff Buckley,
a reference librarian at a law firm, who had a tattoo of the logo from
the Federal Depository Library Program peeking out of his black T-shirt
sleeve.
             </p>
             <p>
              Librarians? Arent they supposed to be bespectacled women
with a love of classic books and a perpetual annoyance with talkative
patrons  the ultimate humorless shushers?
             </p>
             <p>
              Not any more. With so
much of the job involving technology and with a focus now on finding
and sharing information beyond just what is available in books, a new
type of librarian is emerging  the kind that, according to the Web
site Librarian Avengers, is looking to put the hep cat in
cataloguing.
             </p>
             <p>
              When the cult film Party Girl appeared in 1995,
with Parker Posey as a night life impresario who finds happiness in the
stacks, the idea that a librarian could be cool was a joke.
             </p>
             <p>
              Now,
there is a public librarian who writes dispatches for McSweeneys
Internet Tendency, a favored magazine of the young literati.
Unshelved, a comic about librarians  yes, there is a comic about
librarians  features a hipster librarian character. And, in real life,
there are an increasing number of librarians who are notable not just
for their pink-streaked hair but also for their passion for pop
culture, activism and technology.
             </p>
             <p>
              Were not the typical librarians anymore, said Rick Block, an adjunct professor at the
              <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/organizations/l/long_island_university/index.html?inline=nyt-org" title="More articles about Long Island University">
               Long Island University
              </a>
              Palmer School and at the Pratt Institute School of Information and
Library Science, both graduate schools for librarians, in New York
City.
             </p>
             <p>
              When I was in library school in the early 80s, the students werent as interesting, Mr. Block said.
             </p>
             <p>
              Since
then, however, library organizations have been trying to recruit a more
diverse group of students and to mentor younger members of the
profession.
             </p>
             <p>
              I think were getting more progressive and hipper, said Carrie Ansell, a 28-year-old law librarian in Washington.
             </p>
             <p>
              In
the last few years, articles have decried the graying of the
profession, noting a large percentage of librarians that would soon be
retiring and a seemingly insurmountable demand for replacements. But
worries about a mass exodus appear to have been unfounded.
             </p>
             <p>
              Michele Besant, the librarian at the School of Library and Information Studies at the
              <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/organizations/u/university_of_wisconsin/index.html?inline=nyt-org" title="More articles about University of Wisconsin">
               University of Wisconsin
              </a>
              -Madison,
said the Association of Library and Information Science statistics show
a steady increase in library information science enrollments over the
last 10 years. Further, at hers and other schools there is a trend for
students to be entering masters programs at a younger age.
             </p>
             <p>
              The
myth prevails that librarians are becoming obsolete. Theres Google,
no one needs us, Ms. Gentile said, mockingly, over a drink at Daddys.
             </p>
             <p>
              Still,
these are high-tech times. Why are people getting into this profession
when libraries seem as retro as the granny glasses so many of the
members of the Desk Set wear?
             </p>
             <p>
              Because its cool, said Ms. Gentile, who works at the
              <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/organizations/b/brooklyn_museum/index.html?inline=nyt-org" title="More articles about Brooklyn Museum">
               Brooklyn Museum
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/07/08/fashion/08librarian.html?ex=1341547200&amp;en=b7c01214ad013074&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
               <em>
                read on...
               </em>
              </a>
             </p>
            </blockquote>

           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2007/05/may_11_2007harl.html">
            A Schomburg Renaissance
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <em>
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/05/11/arts/design/11harl.html?ex=1336536000&amp;en=768cde719d1046a9&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
               <img alt="11harlem4501" border="0" height="450" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/05/11/11harlem4501.jpg" title="11harlem4501" width="397"/>
              </a>
             </em>
            </p>
            <p>
             <span style="font-size: 0.6em;">
              Ruby Washington/The New York Times
             </span>
            </p>
            <div class="credit">
             <span style="font-size: 0.6em;">
              A 1925 sign from the New York Public Librarys Negro division.
             </span>
            </div>
            <p>
             <em>
              via the NY Times:
             </em>
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              <strong>
               <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/05/11/arts/design/11harl.html?ex=1336536000&amp;en=768cde719d1046a9&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
                Harlems Cultural Anchor in a Sea of Ideas
               </a>
              </strong>
              <br/>
              By FELICIA R. LEE
              <br/>
              Published: May 11, 2007
             </p>
             <p>
              YOU could almost see the ghosts among the new furniture and modern recessed lighting. It was a few days before the staff at the
              <a href="http://www.nypl.org/research/sc/sc.html">
               Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture
              </a>
              , at 135th Street and Malcolm X Boulevard in Harlem, finished hanging two exhibitions and stripping the paper off the doors at its bigger, brighter new entrance. Amid the sounds of hammers and drills, they prepared for tomorrows public celebration of the centers two-year, $11 million renovation.
             </p>
             <p>
              The Schomburg is as much a monument to an idea as it is a building. So those ghosts, workaday and luminous, inhabit a space of many incarnations, tracing its roots back to the 135th Street New York Public Library branch that opened there in 1905. Predominantly Jewish then, Harlem was mostly black by 1924. Over the years, Alex Haley researched Roots at the Schomburg; James Baldwin and Gordon Parks both found it a refuge; a young Ossie Davis honed his craft there.
             </p>
             <p>
              By the time it officially became the Schomburg in 1972, taking its name from
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arturo_Alfonso_Schomburg">
               Arturo Alfonso Schomburg
              </a>
              , the Puerto Rican-born black bibliophile who donated his collection, it was a one-stop connection with the global black experience. Its wonders include a rare recording of a Marcus Garvey speech, documents signed by Toussaint LOuverture, a signed first edition of Phillis Wheatleys poetry, daguerreotypes of African-Americans from the 1830s, Benjamin Bannekers almanacs. Its exhibitions have tracked black migration and displayed the contents of Malcolm Xs pocket when he was gunned down at the Audubon Ballroom.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/.shared/image.html?/photos/uncategorized/2007/05/11/11harlem4502.jpg" onclick="window.open(this.href, '_blank', 'width=301,height=450,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
               <img alt="11harlem4502" border="0" height="225" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/05/11/11harlem4502.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="11harlem4502" width="150"/>
              </a>
              The center has increasingly become one of the cultural anchors of the greater Harlem community, one of the top three tourist destinations, along with the Apollo and the
              <a href="http://studiomuseum.org/">
               Studio Museum in Harlem
              </a>
              , said Howard Dodson, the Schomburg director. The kind of change thats taking place in Harlem is of political, social and historical interest to the center, and well be here to document it. We are not going anywhere.
             </p>
             <p>
              As the Schomburg unveils its facelift, Harlem itself is also undergoing one of its periodic renaissances. Theres new real estate development, new stores and restaurants, new places to imbibe culture. The association with Harlem has been the constant for the Schomburg trove of more than five million items: art, manuscripts, films, photographs. The center has been a place for community meetings and for local politicians, for schoolchildren and eminent researchers like the historian John Hope Franklin.
             </p>
             <p>
              Thelma Golden, the director and chief curator of the Studio Museum,
is among those who see the renovated Schomburg as an emblem of a Harlem
at the top of its game. Hundreds of thousands of tourists pour into
Harlem annually to shop in the stores on 125th Street, sit in the pews
of the historic Abyssinian Baptist Church or revel in the serendipity
of finding new cafes or dowager buildings.
             </p>
             <p>
              The Schomburg stands as a bearer of the idea that our history and
culture are important, said Ms. Golden, who is African-American. The
renovation will reinvent the sense of the institution as living,
breathing space. All the cultural institutions in Harlem are going
through a period of incredible growth, and its not just about physical
renovation.
             </p>
             <p>
              A walk through Harlem makes vivid its embrace of many worlds. There
are the tiny African braiding shops, mom-and-pop restaurants with an
African or Caribbean flavor, as well as Citarella and Starbucks amid
the cacophony of 125th Street, the areas commercial spine. It is
dotted with stores like Old Navy, as well as the Apollo and the Studio
Museum. The streets are cleaner and safer than they have been in years. (
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2007/05/11/arts/design/11harl.html?ex=1336536000&amp;en=768cde719d1046a9&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss">
               read on
              </a>
              ...)
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             <strong>
              <em>
               And take note of the following:
              </em>
             </strong>
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #990000;">
               <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2007/05/11/stereotypes.jpg">
                <img alt="Stereotypes" border="0" height="255" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/2007/05/11/stereotypes.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="Stereotypes" width="110"/>
               </a>
               <a href="http://www.nypl.org/research/sc/abolition.html">
                Two exhibitions
               </a>
               will be on view through Oct. 28:
               <a href="http://www.nypl.org/research/calendar/exhib/sch/schexhibdesc.cfm?id=456">
                Stereotypes vs. Humantypes: 
Images of Blacks in the 19th and 20th Centuries
               </a>
               and Black Art: Treasures From 
the Schomburg.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #990000;">
               The Stereotypes exhibition is meant to show the prevalence of caricatured 
images of blacks for most of the early 19th and early 20 centuries. It uses 
items like sheet music, posters, advertisements and postcards to show how words 
like darktown and coon were casual companions to depictions of blacks with 
distorted features.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #990000;">
               Some of the items are on loan from the Jim Crow Museum of Racist Memorabilia 
at Ferris State University in Big Rapids, Mich.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #990000;">
               A small amount of this is from the South, Mr. Dodson noted. A significant 
amount of the stereotypical ads come from New York.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #990000;">
               The propaganda is contrasted with real-life black images from that period: 
couples in their wedding finery, 1920s bathing beauties, formal banquets.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              <span style="color: #990000;">
               Black Treasures is an eclectic display that includes the 1868 marble and 
bronze Portrait of Ira Aldridge as Othello, by Pietro Calvi, as well as the 
1969 collage Black Manhattan, by Romare Bearden, and dozens of other work by 
Elizabeth Catlett, Augusta Savage, Jacob Lawrence, Benny Andrews, Henry Ossawa 
Tanner, Horace Pippin, Faith Ringgold and others.
              </span>
             </p>
            </blockquote>
           </div>

        <h2 class="date-header">
         October 05, 2006
        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-books entry-category-contemporary entry-category-current_affairs entry-category-joy_garnett entry-category-local_vernacular entry-category-native_america entry-category-photography entry-author-newsgrist entry-type-post entry" id="entry-13203863">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/2006/10/jonathan_moller.html">
            Jonathan Moller: Our Culture Is Our Resistance
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <em>
              <a href="http://www.artnet.com/magazineus/books/smith/smith10-4-06_detail.asp?picnum=1">
               <img alt="Smith1041" border="0" height="460" src="http://newsgrist.typepad.com/robertgoldwaterlibrary/images/smith1041.jpg" title="Smith1041" width="404"/>
              </a>
             </em>
            </p>
            <p>
             <span style="font-size: 0.8em;color: #666666;">
              Jonathan Moller,
              <em>
               Our Culture Is Our Resistance
              </em>
              , 2004 (
              <a href="http://www.powerhousebooks.com/index.html">
               powerHouse Books
              </a>
              )
             </span>
            </p>
            <p>
             <span style="color: #000000;">
              [
              <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Our-Culture-Resistance-Repression-Guatemala/dp/1576872122/sr=8-1/qid=1160064765/ref=pd_bbs_1/104-0872330-2167933?ie=UTF8&amp;s=books">
               Amazon Link
              </a>
              ]
             </span>
            </p>
            <p>
             <em>
              via Artnet magazine, Oct 5, 2006
             </em>
             :
             <br/>
             <a href="http://www.artnet.com/magazineus/books/smith/smith10-4-06.asp">
              <strong>
               CULTURE AND RESISTANCE
              </strong>
             </a>
             <br/>
             by P.C. Smith
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              The photographer and human-rights activist
              <a href="http://www.jonathanmoller.org/biography.htm">
               <strong>
                Jonathan Moller
               </strong>
              </a>
              (b. 1963)
first went to Central America in 1991, where he contributed to "El
Salvador in the Eye of the Beholder," the celebrated exhibition
documenting the war in El Salvador. In 1993, he began working with
indigenous Mayan communities in Guatemala who had been uprooted by the
countrys brutal civil war and were living in the remote countryside as
clandestine, mobile "Communities of Population in Resistance" (CPRs).
Though not rebels, the campesinos were targeted by the Guatemalan
military in a "scorched earth" campaign that wiped out over 450
villages and killed some 200,000 people.
             </p>
             <p>
              A member of the
              <strong>
               Foreign Press Club of Guatemala
              </strong>
              , in 2000-2001 Moller worked as staff
photographer for a forensic anthropology team of the
              <strong>
               Quich Catholic
Diocese's Office of Peace and Reconciliation
              </strong>
              , documenting the
exhumations of clandestine cemeteries. Though the Guatemalan civil war
was ended in 1996 by a UN-sponsored peace accord, Amnesty International
reports continuing human rights concerns and extreme social inequality
in the country.
             </p>
             <p>
              <em>
               Our Culture Is Our Resistance
              </em>
              ,
published in 2004 by powerHouse books, features 147 black-and-white
photographs from 1993-2001, interspersed with texts by novelist
              <strong>
               Francisco Goldman
              </strong>
              , anthropologist
              <strong>
               Ricardo Falla
              </strong>
              and historian
              <strong>
               Susanne
Jonas
              </strong>
              , as well as poems and prose by several writers, including an
introduction by
              <strong>
               Rigoberta Mench Tum
