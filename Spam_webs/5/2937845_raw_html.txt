    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Intelligent transportation systems
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       ITS
      </b>
      ) are advanced applications which, without embodying intelligence as such, aim to provide innovative services relating to different modes of transport and traffic management and enable various users to be better informed and make safer, more coordinated, and 'smarter' use of transport networks.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although ITS may refer to all modes of transport, EU Directive 2010/40/EU (7 July 2010) defines ITS as systems in which information and communication technologies are applied in the field of road transport, including infrastructure, vehicles and users, and in traffic management and mobility management, as well as for interfaces with other modes of transport.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Recent governmental activity in the area of ITS  specifically in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
       is further motivated by an increasing focus on
      <a href="/wiki/Homeland_security" title="Homeland security">
       homeland security
      </a>
      . Many of the proposed ITS systems also involve surveillance of the roadways, which is a priority of homeland security.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Funding of many systems comes either directly through homeland security organisations or with their approval. Further, ITS can play a role in the rapid
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_evacuation" title="Mass evacuation">
       mass evacuation
      </a>
      of people in urban centres after large casualty events such as a result of a natural disaster or threat. Much of the infrastructure and planning involved with ITS parallels the need for homeland security systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">
       developing world
      </a>
      , the migration from rural to urbanized habitats has progressed differently. Many areas of the developing world have
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urbanisation" title="Urbanisation">
       urbanised
      </a>
      without significant motorisation and the formation of suburbs. A small portion of the population can afford
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
       automobiles
      </a>
      , but the automobiles greatly increase congestion in these multimodal transportation systems. They also produce considerable
      <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">
       air pollution
      </a>
      , pose a significant safety risk, and exacerbate feelings of inequities in the society. High-population density could be supported by a
      <a href="/wiki/Multimodal_transport" title="Multimodal transport">
       multimodal
      </a>
      system of walking,
      <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">
       bicycle
      </a>
      transportation,
      <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">
       motorcycles
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">
       buses
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trains" title="Trains">
       trains
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Other parts of the developing world, such as
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      &amp;
      <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
       Brazil
      </a>
      remain largely rural but are rapidly urbanising and industrialising. In these areas a motorised infrastructure is being developed alongside motorisation of the population. Great disparity of wealth means that only a fraction of the population can motorise, and therefore the highly dense multimodal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation_system" title="Transportation system">
       transportation system
      </a>
      for the poor is cross-cut by the highly motorised transportation system for the rich.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligent_transport_technologies">
       Intelligent transport technologies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Intelligent transport technologies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Intelligent transport systems vary in technologies applied, from basic management systems such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_navigation" title="Car navigation">
       car navigation
      </a>
      ;
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_signal" title="Traffic signal">
       traffic signal
      </a>
      control systems; container management systems; variable message signs;
      <a href="/wiki/Automatic_number_plate_recognition" title="Automatic number plate recognition">
       automatic number plate recognition
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speed_cameras" title="Speed cameras">
       speed cameras
      </a>
      to monitor applications, such as security
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCTV" title="CCTV">
       CCTV
      </a>
      systems; and to more advanced applications that integrate live data and feedback from a number of other sources, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Parking_guidance_and_information" title="Parking guidance and information">
       parking guidance and information
      </a>
      systems;
      <a href="/wiki/Road_Weather_Information_System" title="Road Weather Information System">
       weather information
      </a>
      ; bridge de-icing (US
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deicing" title="Deicing">
       deicing
      </a>
      ) systems; and the like. Additionally, predictive techniques are being developed to allow advanced modelling and comparison with historical baseline data. Some of these technologies are described in the following sections.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wireless_communications">
       Wireless communications
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wireless communications">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Various forms of wireless communications technologies have been proposed for intelligent transportation systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Radio_modem" title="Radio modem">
       Radio modem
      </a>
      communication on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UHF" title="UHF">
       UHF
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">
       VHF
      </a>
      frequencies are widely used for short and long range communication within ITS.
     </p>
     <p>
      Short-range communications of 350 m can be accomplished using
      <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11" title="IEEE 802.11">
       IEEE 802.11
      </a>
      protocols, specifically
      <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11p" title="IEEE 802.11p">
       WAVE
      </a>
      or the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dedicated_Short_Range_Communications" title="Dedicated Short Range Communications">
       Dedicated Short Range Communications
      </a>
      standard being promoted by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligent_Transportation_Society_of_America" title="Intelligent Transportation Society of America">
       Intelligent Transportation Society of America
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">
       United States Department of Transportation
      </a>
      . Theoretically, the range of these protocols can be extended using
      <a href="/wiki/Mobile_ad_hoc_network" title="Mobile ad hoc network">
       Mobile ad hoc networks
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Mesh_networking" title="Mesh networking">
       Mesh networking
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Longer range communications have been proposed using infrastructure networks such as
      <a href="/wiki/WiMAX" title="WiMAX">
       WiMAX
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.16" title="IEEE 802.16">
       IEEE 802.16
      </a>
      ),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_System_for_Mobile_Communications" title="Global System for Mobile Communications">
       Global System for Mobile Communications
      </a>
      (GSM), or
      <a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">
       3G
      </a>
      . Long-range communications using these methods are well established, but, unlike the short-range protocols, these methods require extensive and very expensive infrastructure deployment. There is lack of consensus as to what business model should support this infrastructure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Auto Insurance companies have utilised ad hoc solutions to support eCall and behavioural tracking functionalities in the form of
      <a href="/wiki/Telematics_2.0" title="Telematics 2.0">
       Telematics 2.0
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_technologies">
       Computational technologies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Computational technologies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Recent advances in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehicle_electronics" title="Vehicle electronics">
       vehicle electronics
      </a>
      have led to a move towards fewer, more capable computer processors on a vehicle. A typical vehicle in the early 2000s would have between 20 and 100 individual networked
      <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">
       microcontroller
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Programmable_logic_controller" title="Programmable logic controller">
       Programmable logic controller
      </a>
      modules with non-real-time
      <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">
       operating systems
      </a>
      . The current trend is toward fewer, more costly
      <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">
       microprocessor
      </a>
      modules with hardware
      <a href="/wiki/Memory_management" title="Memory management">
       memory management
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real-Time_Operating_System" title="Real-Time Operating System">
       Real-Time Operating Systems
      </a>
      . The new
      <a href="/wiki/Embedded_system" title="Embedded system">
       embedded system
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platforms_(computing)" title="Platforms (computing)">
       platforms
      </a>
      allow for more sophisticated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_application" title="Software application">
       software applications
      </a>
      to be implemented, including model-based
      <a href="/wiki/Process_control" title="Process control">
       process control
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">
       artificial intelligence
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">
       ubiquitous computing
      </a>
      . Perhaps the most important of these for Intelligent Transportation Systems is
      <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">
       artificial intelligence
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

          citation needed

       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Floating_car_data.2Ffloating_cellular_data">
       Floating car data/floating cellular data
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Floating car data/floating cellular data">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Floating_car_data" title="Floating car data">
       Floating car data
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg/220px-TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg/330px-TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg/440px-TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TransCore_RFID_reader_and_antenna.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFID" title="RFID">
         RFID
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/E-ZPass" title="E-ZPass">
         E-ZPass
        </a>
        reader attached to the pole and its antenna (right) used in traffic monitoring in New York City by using vehicle re-identification method
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      "Floating car" or "probe" data collection is a set of relatively low-cost methods for obtaining travel time and speed data for vehicles travelling along streets, highways, motorways (freeways), and other transport routes. Broadly speaking, three methods have been used to obtain the raw data:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Triangulation method.
       </b>
       In developed countries a high proportion of cars contain one or more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_phones" title="Mobile phones">
        mobile phones
       </a>
       . The phones periodically transmit their presence information to the mobile phone network, even when no voice connection is established. In the mid-2000s, attempts were made to use mobile phones as anonymous traffic probes. As a car moves, so does the signal of any mobile phones that are inside the vehicle. By measuring and analysing network data using
       <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">
        triangulation
       </a>
       , pattern matching or cell-sector statistics (in an anonymous format), the data was converted into traffic flow information. With more congestion, there are more cars, more phones, and thus, more probes. In metropolitan areas, the distance between antennas is shorter and in theory accuracy increases. An advantage of this method is that no infrastructure needs to be built along the road; only the mobile phone network is leveraged. But in practice the triangulation method can be complicated, especially in areas where the same mobile phone towers serve two or more parallel routes (such as a motorway (freeway) with a frontage road, a motorway (freeway) and a commuter rail line, two or more parallel streets, or a street that is also a bus line). By the early 2010s, the popularity of the triangulation method was declining
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Vehicle re-identification.
       </b>
       Vehicle re-identification methods require sets of detectors mounted along the road. In this technique, a unique serial number for a device in the vehicle is detected at one location and then detected again (re-identified) further down the road. Travel times and speed are calculated by comparing the time at which a specific device is detected by pairs of sensors. This can be done using the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAC_addresses" title="MAC addresses">
        MAC addresses
       </a>
       from Bluetooth or other devices,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       or using the RFID serial numbers from Electronic Toll Collection (ETC) transponders (also called "toll tags").
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        GPS based methods.
       </b>
       An increasing number of vehicles are equipped with in-vehicle satnav/GPS (satellite navigation) systems that have two-way communication with a traffic data provider. Position readings from these vehicles are used to compute vehicle speeds. Modern methods may not use dedicated hardware but instead
       <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">
        Smartphone
       </a>
       based solutions using so called
       <a href="/wiki/Telematics_2.0" title="Telematics 2.0">
        Telematics 2.0
       </a>
       approaches.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Floating car data technology provides advantages over other methods of traffic measurement:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Less expensive than sensors or cameras
      </li>
      <li>
       More coverage (potentially including all locations and streets)
      </li>
      <li>
       Faster to set up and less maintenance
      </li>
      <li>
       Works in all weather conditions, including heavy rain
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sensing_technologies">
       Sensing technologies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sensing technologies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Technological advances in telecommunications and information technology, coupled with ultramodern/state-of-the-art microchip,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFID" title="RFID">
       RFID
      </a>
      (Radio Frequency Identification), and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inexpensive" title="Inexpensive">
       inexpensive
      </a>
      intelligent
      <a href="/wiki/Beacon" title="Beacon">
       beacon
      </a>
      sensing technologies, have enhanced the technical capabilities that will facilitate motorist safety benefits for intelligent transportation systems
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globally" title="Globally">
       globally
      </a>
      . Sensing systems for ITS are vehicle- and infrastructure-based networked systems, i.e.,
      <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_vehicle_technologies" title="Intelligent vehicle technologies">
       Intelligent vehicle technologies
      </a>
      . Infrastructure sensors are indestructible (such as in-road reflectors) devices that are installed or embedded in the road or surrounding the road (e.g., on buildings, posts, and signs), as required, and may be manually disseminated during preventive
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_construction" title="Road construction">
       road construction
      </a>
      maintenance or by sensor injection machinery for rapid deployment. Vehicle-sensing systems include deployment of infrastructure-to-vehicle and vehicle-to-infrastructure electronic beacons for identification communications and may also employ video
      <a href="/wiki/Automatic_number_plate_recognition" title="Automatic number plate recognition">
       automatic number plate recognition
      </a>
      or vehicle magnetic signature detection technologies at desired intervals to increase sustained monitoring of vehicles operating in critical zones.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inductive_loop_detection">
       Inductive loop detection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Inductive loop detection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1039" data-file-width="1393" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg/220px-Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg/330px-Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg/440px-Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saw_Cut_Loop_Detectors.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Saw cut loop detectors for vehicle detection buried in the pavement at this intersection as seen by the rectangular shapes of loop detector sealant at the bottom part of this picture.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inductive_loop" title="Inductive loop">
       Inductive loops
      </a>
      can be placed in a roadbed to detect vehicles as they pass through the loop's magnetic field. The simplest detectors simply count the number of vehicles during a unit of time (typically 60 seconds in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      ) that pass over the loop, while more sophisticated sensors estimate the speed, length, and class of vehicles and the distance between them. Loops can be placed in a single lane or across multiple lanes, and they work with very slow or stopped vehicles as well as vehicles moving at high-speed.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Video_vehicle_detection">
       Video vehicle detection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Video vehicle detection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Traffic-flow measurement and automatic incident detection using video
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cameras" title="Cameras">
       cameras
      </a>
      is another form of vehicle detection. Since video detection systems such as those used in
      <a href="/wiki/Automatic_number_plate_recognition" title="Automatic number plate recognition">
       automatic number plate recognition
      </a>
      do not involve installing any components directly into the road surface or roadbed, this type of system is known as a "non-intrusive" method of traffic detection. Video from cameras is fed into
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Processors" title="Processors">
       processors
      </a>
      that analyse the changing characteristics of the video image as vehicles pass. The cameras are typically mounted on
      <a href="/wiki/Utility_pole" title="Utility pole">
       poles
      </a>
      or structures above or adjacent to the roadway. Most video detection systems require some initial configuration to "teach" the processor the baseline background image. This usually involves inputting known measurements such as the distance between
      <a href="/wiki/Lane" title="Lane">
       lane
      </a>
      lines or the height of the camera above the roadway. A single video detection processor can detect traffic simultaneously from one to eight cameras, depending on the brand and model. The typical output from a video detection system is lane-by-lane vehicle speeds, counts, and lane occupancy readings. Some systems provide additional outputs including gap, headway, stopped-vehicle detection, and wrong-way vehicle alarms.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bluetooth_detection">
       Bluetooth detection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bluetooth detection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">
       Bluetooth
      </a>
      is an accurate and inexpensive way to measure travel time and make origin and destination analysis. Bluetooth is a wireless standard used to communicate between electronic devices like mobile phones, smart phones, headsets, navigation systems, computers etc. Bluetooth road sensors are able to detect Bluetooth
      <a href="/wiki/MAC_address" title="MAC address">
       MAC addresses
      </a>
      from Bluetooth devices in passing vehicles. If these sensors are interconnected they are able to calculate travel time and provide data for origin and destination matrices. Compared to other traffic measurement technologies, Bluetooth measurement has some differences:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Accurate measurement points with absolute confirmation to provide to the second travel times.
      </li>
      <li>
       Is non-intrusive, which can lead to lower-cost installations for both permanent and temporary sites.
      </li>
      <li>
       Is limited to how many Bluetooth devices are broadcasting in a vehicle so counting and other applications are limited.
      </li>
      <li>
       Systems are generally quick to set up with little to no calibration needed.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Since Bluetooth devices become more prevalent on board vehicles and with more portable electronics broadcasting, the amount of data collected over time becomes more accurate and valuable for travel time and estimation purposes.
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     </h3>
     <p>
      The in-vehicle
      <a href="/wiki/ECall" title="ECall">
       eCall
      </a>
      is an emergency call generated either manually by the vehicle occupants or automatically via activation of in-vehicle sensors after an accident. When activated, the in-vehicle eCall device will establish an emergency call carrying both voice and data directly to the nearest emergency point (normally the nearest E
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-1-2" title="1-1-2">
       1-1-2
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Public-safety_answering_point" title="Public-safety answering point">
       Public-safety answering point
      </a>
      , PSAP). The voice call enables the vehicle occupant to communicate with the trained eCall operator. At the same time, a minimum set of data will be sent to the eCall operator receiving the voice call.
     </p>

The pan-European eCall aims to be operative for all new type-approved vehicles as a standard option. Depending on the manufacturer of the eCall system, it could be mobile phone based (Bluetooth connection to an in-vehicle interface), an integrated eCall device, or a functionality of a broader system like navigation, Telematics device, or tolling device. eCall is expected to be offered, at earliest, by the end of 2010, pending standardization by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Telecommunications_Standards_Institute" title="European Telecommunications Standards Institute">

      </a>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variable_speed_limits">
       Variable speed limits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_limit#Variable_speed_limits" title="Speed limit">
       Speed limit  Variable speed limits
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Variable_speed_limit.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="318" data-file-width="174" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Variable_speed_limit.jpg/170px-Variable_speed_limit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Variable_speed_limit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Variable_speed_limit.jpg 2x" width="170"/>

     <p>
      Recently some jurisdictions have begun experimenting with variable speed limits that change with road congestion and other factors. Typically such speed limits only change to decline during poor conditions, rather than being improved in good ones. One example is on Britain's
      <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">
       M25 motorway
      </a>
      , which circumnavigates London. On the most heavily traveled 14-mile (23km) section (junction 10 to 16) of the M25 variable speed limits combined with automated enforcement have been in force since 1995. Initial results indicated savings in journey times, smoother-flowing traffic, and a fall in the number of accidents, so the implementation was made permanent in 1997. Further trials on the M25 have been thus far proven inconclusive.

     <h3>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_traffic_light_sequence">
       Dynamic traffic light sequence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Dynamic traffic light sequence">
        edit

        ]

     </h3>
     <p>
      A 2008 paper was written about using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFID" title="RFID">
       RFID
      </a>
      for dynamic
      <a href="/wiki/Traffic_light" title="Traffic light">
       traffic light
      </a>
      sequences. It circumvents or avoids problems that usually arise with systems that use image processing and beam interruption techniques. RFID technologyj;h with appropriate
      <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">
       algorithm
      </a>
      and database were applied to a multi-vehicle, multi-lane and multi-road junction area to provide an efficient time management scheme. A dynamic time schedule was worked out for the passage of each column. The simulation showed the dynamic sequence algorithm could adjust itself even with the presence of some extreme cases. The paper said the system could emulate the judgment of a traffic police officer on duty, by considering the number of vehicles in each column and the routing proprieties.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cooperative_systems_on_the_road">
       Cooperative systems on the road
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cooperative systems on the road">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">

     <p>
      Communication cooperation on the road includes car-to-car, car-to-infrastructure, and vice versa. Data available from vehicles are acquired and transmitted to a server for central fusion and processing. These data can be used to detect events such as rain (wiper activity) and congestion (frequent braking activities). The server processes a driving recommendation dedicated to a single or a specific group of drivers and transmits it wirelessly to vehicles. The goal of cooperative systems is to use and plan communication and sensor infrastructure to increase road safety. The definition of cooperative systems in road traffic is according to the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">
       European Commission
      </a>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       "Road operators, infrastructure, vehicles, their drivers and other road users will cooperate to deliver the most efficient, safe, secure and comfortable journey. The vehicle-vehicle and vehicle-infrastructure co-operative systems will contribute to these objectives beyond the improvements achievable with stand-alone systems."
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="ITS_World_Congress">
       ITS World Congress
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: ITS World Congress">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ITS_World_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ITS World Congress (page does not exist)">
       ITS World Congress
      </a>
      is a world-wide annual event to promote and showcase ITS technologies. ERTICO  ITS Europe, ITS America and ITS Japan work closely together in the preparation of the annual ITS World Congress and Exhibition attracting over 8,000 people. Each year the event takes place in a different region (Europe, Americas or Asia-Pacific).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">
       Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Intelligent_transportation_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.itsnetwork.org/" rel="nofollow">
       The Network of National ITS Associations
      </a>
      is a grouping of national ITS interests formed in order to ensure that ITS knowledge and information is transmitted to all actors at the local and national level  such as small and medium-sized companies  and support ITS promotion from the ground up. It was officially launched 7 October 2004 in London. The Network currently consists of 27 member organisations. The Network Secretariat is at ERTICO  ITS Europe.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.ertico.com/" rel="nofollow">
       ERTICO  ITS Europe
      </a>
      is a multi-sector, public/private partnership pursuing the development and deployment of Intelligent Transport Systems and Services (ITS). We connect public authorities, industry players, infrastructure operators, users, national ITS associations and other organisations together and work to bring Intelligence into Mobility. The ERTICO work programme focuses on initiatives to improve transport safety, security and network efficiency whilst taking into account measures to reduce environmental impact. Our vision is of a future transport system working towards zero accidents, zero delays and fully informed people, where services are affordable and seamless, the environment is protected, privacy is respected and security is provided.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
       United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

       </span>

     </h2>
     <p>
      In the United States, each state has an Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS) chapter that holds a yearly conference to promote and showcase ITS technologies and ideas. Representatives from each Department of Transportation (state, cities, towns, and counties) within the state attend this conference.
     </p>
