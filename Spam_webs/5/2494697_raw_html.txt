     <p>
      <b>
       Jonathan Stephen Ross
      </b>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">
       OBE
      </a>
      (born 17 November 1960) is an English television and radio presenter; best known for presenting the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
       BBC One
      </a>
      chat show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Friday_Night_with_Jonathan_Ross" title="Friday Night with Jonathan Ross">
        Friday Night with Jonathan Ross
       </a>
      </i>
      during the 2000s. Ross also hosted his own radio show on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">
       BBC Radio 2
      </a>
      , and acted as a film critic and presenter of the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_2014" title="Film 2014">
        Film
       </a>
      </i>
      programme. After leaving the BBC, Ross then began hosting a new chat show on
      <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">
       ITV
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Jonathan_Ross_Show" title="The Jonathan Ross Show">
        The Jonathan Ross Show
       </a>
      </i>
      . Other regular roles have included being a regular panellist on the comedy sports quiz
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/They_Think_It%27s_All_Over_(TV_series)" title="They Think It's All Over (TV series)">
        They Think It's All Over
       </a>
      </i>
      and being a regular presenter of the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Comedy_Awards" title="British Comedy Awards">
       British Comedy Awards
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Ross began his television career as a programme researcher, before dbuting as a television presenter for
      <i>
       The Last Resort with Jonathan Ross
      </i>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
       Channel 4
      </a>
      in 1987. Over the next decade he had several radio and television roles, many through his own production company, Channel X. In 1995 he sold his stake in Channel X, and embarked on a career with the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      . In 1999, Ross took over presenting the
      <i>
       Film
      </i>
      programme from
      <a href="/wiki/Barry_Norman" title="Barry Norman">
       Barry Norman
      </a>
      , and also began presenting his own radio show, while two years later he began hosting
      <i>
       Friday Night with Jonathan Ross
      </i>
      . For the chat show, Ross won three
      <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">
       BAFTA
      </a>
      awards for Best Entertainment Performance, in 2004, 2006 and 2007. By 2006 Ross was believed to be the BBC's highest paid star. In 2005, Ross was made an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">
       Officer of the Order of the British Empire
      </a>
      (OBE) for services to broadcasting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OBE_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OBE-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ross has been involved in controversies throughout his broadcasting career.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a result, in 2008 he wrote a semi-autobiographical work titled
      <i>
       Why Do I Say These Things?
      </i>
      , detailing some of his life experiences.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ross has been married to the author, journalist and broadcaster
      <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goldman" title="Jane Goldman">
       Jane Goldman
      </a>
      since 1988; they have three children. Ross and Goldman have together established the television production company Hotsauce TV. Ross is known as an avid fan and collector of comic books and memorabilia, and has written his own comic books,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Turf_(Image_Comics)" title="Turf (Image Comics)">
        Turf
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       America's Got Powers
      </i>
      . Ross is known for his distinctive voice, flamboyant style of dress,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OBE_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-OBE-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      light-hearted banter and his characteristic
      <a href="/wiki/Rhotacism" title="Rhotacism">
       difficulty in pronouncing the letter 'r'
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">
       Early life and career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The son of a lorry driver father and a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_extra" title="Film extra">
       film extra
      </a>
      mother,
      <a href="/wiki/Martha_Ross" title="Martha Ross">
       Martha
      </a>
      , Ross was born in
      <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras,_London" title="St Pancras, London">
       St Pancras, London
      </a>
      , England, on 17 November 1960, but grew up in
      <a href="/wiki/Leytonstone" title="Leytonstone">
       Leytonstone
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bio-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is the brother of journalist, television editor, and media personality
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ross" title="Paul Ross">
       Paul Ross
      </a>
      ; TV producer/actor
      <a href="/wiki/Miles_Ross_(producer)" title="Miles Ross (producer)">
       Miles Ross
      </a>
      ; TV producer
      <a href="/wiki/Simon_Ross" title="Simon Ross">
       Simon Ross
      </a>
      , and music industry professional Adam Ross.
     </p>
     <p>
      Their mother put all of her children forward for roles in television advertisements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_2003_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Independent_2003-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ross first appeared in a television advertisement for the
      <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_cereal" title="Breakfast cereal">
       breakfast cereal
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s_Rice_Krispies" title="Kellogg's Rice Krispies">
       Kellogg's Rice Krispies
      </a>
      in 1970, when he was 10 years old.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also appeared in an ad for the
      <a href="/wiki/Laundry_detergent" title="Laundry detergent">
       laundry detergent
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Persil" title="Persil">
       Persil
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ross was educated at
      <a href="/wiki/Norlington_School_for_Boys" title="Norlington School for Boys">
       Norlington School for Boys
      </a>
      , a comprehensive school and at
      <a href="/wiki/Leyton_Sixth_Form_College" title="Leyton Sixth Form College">
       Leyton County High School for Boys
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">
       comprehensive school
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He then studied Modern European History at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_Slavonic_and_East_European_Studies" title="School of Slavonic and East European Studies">
       School of Slavonic and East European Studies
      </a>
      (SSEES) a college of the federal
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">
       University of London
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCL_Fellowships_conferred_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-UCL_Fellowships_conferred-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ross began his adult career as a researcher on the
      <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
       Channel 4
      </a>
      show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Loose_Talk" title="Loose Talk">
        Loose Talk
       </a>
      </i>
      . After leaving this, he worked on various other shows before beginning another research job on
      <i>
       Soul Train
      </i>
      , which became
      <i>
       Solid Soul
      </i>
      . It is believed his first appearance on television was as an extra in the 1981
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/It_Ain%27t_Half_Hot,_Mum" title="It Ain't Half Hot, Mum">
        It Ain't Half Hot, Mum
       </a>
      </i>
      episode,
      <i>
       The Last Roll Call
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Presenter_and_producer">
       Presenter and producer
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Presenter and producer">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1987.E2.80.9395:_Channel_X">
       198795: Channel X
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 198795: Channel X">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Whilst on
      <i>
       Solid Soul
      </i>
      , he met fellow researcher Alan Marke, and the two devised what would prove to be a breakthrough hit for Ross in 1987,
      <i>
       The Last Resort with Jonathan Ross
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The two men based their concept on the successful American show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Night_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Night with David Letterman">
        Late Night with David Letterman
       </a>
      </i>
      , and formed a new production company called Channel X, to produce a pilot. Ross had not planned to be the show's host, but he presented the show from its debut in January 1987.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While the series was initially a co-production with Colin Calendar, ownership transferred to Marke and Ross, meaning that the latter retained a great deal of control as well as being presenter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-times-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The show was successful for both Ross and for Channel 4, making him one of the major personalities on the channel. A year later, his documentary series
      <i>
       The Incredibly Strange Film Show
      </i>
      introduced many to the works of
      <a href="/wiki/Cult_film" title="Cult film">
       cult filmmakers
      </a>
      like
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Raimi" title="Sam Raimi">
       Sam Raimi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Chan" title="Jackie Chan">
       Jackie Chan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1989, he co-presented the biennial BBC charity telethon
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_Relief_(charity)" title="Comic Relief (charity)">
        Comic Relief
       </a>
      </i>
      , the same year he launched
      <i>
       One Hour with Jonathan Ross
      </i>
      a short lived chat show on Channel 4. Its game show segment, "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knock_down_ginger" title="Knock down ginger">
       Knock down ginger
      </a>
      ", introduced comedians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Vic_Reeves" title="Vic Reeves">
       Vic Reeves
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bob_Mortimer" title="Bob Mortimer">
       Bob Mortimer
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Whitehouse" title="Paul Whitehouse">
       Paul Whitehouse
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Higson" title="Charlie Higson">
       Charlie Higson
      </a>
      to television. In December 1989, however, Ross (and
      <a href="/wiki/Cilla_Black" title="Cilla Black">
       Cilla Black
      </a>
      ) presented all four members of
      <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">
       Queen
      </a>
      with the "Top Band of the Eighties" prize in a broadcast for
      <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">
       ITV
      </a>
      which would turn out to be
      <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">
       Freddie Mercury
      </a>
      's penultimate public appearance before his death from AIDS in 1991.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1991, he presented the annual
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/British_Comedy_Awards" title="British Comedy Awards">
        British Comedy Awards
       </a>
      </i>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">
       ITV
      </a>
      . He has presented the event each year since, but in 2008 announced he would be stepping down from the role following his suspension from the BBC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1992 he presented an interview with
      <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">
       Madonna
      </a>
      about her
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Erotica_(Madonna_album)" title="Erotica (Madonna album)">
        Erotica
       </a>
      </i>
      album and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sex_(book)" title="Sex (book)">
        Sex Book
       </a>
      </i>
      promotion.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ross has appeared in numerous television entertainment programmes on several channels throughout the 1990s and 2000s. He was a regular panellist on the sports quiz
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/They_Think_It%27s_All_Over_(TV_series)" title="They Think It's All Over (TV series)">
        They Think It's All Over
       </a>
      </i>
      , and hosted the
      <a href="/wiki/Panel_game" title="Panel game">
       panel game
      </a>
      <i>
       It's Only TV...But I Like It
      </i>
      . Other projects include the BBC joke-quiz
      <i>
       Gagtag
      </i>
      , the Channel 4 variety show
      <i>
       Saturday Zoo
      </i>
      , new-acts showcase
      <i>
       The Big Big Talent Show
      </i>
      , and the ITV programme
      <i>
       Fantastic Facts
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1995 he left Channel X, despite its profitable nature. He was quoted in a 1998 article as stating:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       It was to do with a deliberate change in my life, moving away from TV as the core of my existence to focus on my family more. So I had to give up everything to do with Channel X, and I literally got only 1 for my share, which was unbelievable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-guardian-15">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         15
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1995.E2.80.932006">
       19952006
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19952006">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1995 he presented
      <i>
       Mondo Rosso
      </i>
      , a programme about old
      <i>
       cult
      </i>
      films. He took over presenting of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_2014" title="Film 2014">
       the Film programme
      </a>
      , the BBC's long-running cinema review series, in 1999 after
      <a href="/wiki/Barry_Norman" title="Barry Norman">
       Barry Norman
      </a>
      left the show. Ross himself has made a number of cameo appearances in films, playing himself in the
      <a href="/wiki/Spice_Girls" title="Spice Girls">
       Spice Girls
      </a>
      ' film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Spice_World_(film)" title="Spice World (film)">
        Spice World
       </a>
      </i>
      (1997) and voicing the character of Doris in the UK version of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shrek_2" title="Shrek 2">
        Shrek 2
       </a>
      </i>
      (2004). In 2001 he also played himself in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Only_Fools_and_Horses" title="Only Fools and Horses">
        Only Fools and Horses
       </a>
      </i>
      , presenting
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/If_They_Could_See_Us_Now" title="If They Could See Us Now">
        Goldrush
       </a>
      </i>
      , a fictional television quiz on which the main character,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derek_Trotter" title="Derek Trotter">
       Del
      </a>
      , was a contestant. In 2001 he voiced characters in two episodes of the animated comedy series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rex_the_Runt" title="Rex the Runt">
        Rex the Runt
       </a>
      </i>
      . He also appeared on the first pilot show for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shooting_Stars" title="Shooting Stars">
        Shooting Stars
       </a>
      </i>
      , acting as a team captain.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1987.2C_1999.E2.80.932010.2C_2015:_BBC_Radio">
       1987, 19992010, 2015: BBC Radio
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1987, 19992010, 2015: BBC Radio">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Ross' first radio work was on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">
       BBC Radio 1
      </a>
      in 1987, when he sat in for
      <a href="/wiki/Janice_Long" title="Janice Long">
       Janice Long
      </a>
      for two weeks. Ross began presenting a Saturday morning show on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">
       BBC Radio 2
      </a>
      in 1999. He has also presented radio shows for
      <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Radio" title="Virgin Radio">
       Virgin Radio
      </a>
      (having previously worked on
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Branson" title="Richard Branson">
       Richard Branson
      </a>
      's earlier venture,
      <i>
       Radio Radio
      </i>
      ), as well as the now-defunct
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_radio" title="Commercial radio">
       commercial radio
      </a>
      network service
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Superstation" title="The Superstation">
        The Superstation
       </a>
      </i>
      , where his producer was
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Evans_(presenter)" title="Chris Evans (presenter)">
       Chris Evans
      </a>
      . Ross' show on Radio 2 last aired on 17 July 2010 when his contract at the BBC ended.
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2014, he returned to Radio 2 as a stand-in presenter on Steve Wright's afternoon show for four days.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In March 2015 Jonathan sat in for Steve Wright again from 1627 March 2015.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="2015 dates not included in 2014 reference (April 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2001.E2.80.9310:_Friday_Night_with_Jonathan_Ross">
       200110:
       <i>
        Friday Night with Jonathan Ross
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 200110: Friday Night with Jonathan Ross">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="853" data-file-width="1280" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg/220px-JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg/330px-JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg/440px-JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JonathanRossRickyGervais.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Ross with
        <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Gervais" title="Ricky Gervais">
         Ricky Gervais
        </a>
        at
        <a href="/wiki/Live_8" title="Live 8">
         Live 8
        </a>
        in July 2005
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      On 2 November 2001, Ross began presenting his chat show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Friday_Night_with_Jonathan_Ross" title="Friday Night with Jonathan Ross">
        Friday Night with Jonathan Ross
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2005, Ross anchored the BBC television coverage of the
      <a href="/wiki/Live_8" title="Live 8">
       Live 8
      </a>
      concerts. Later that year he was made an Officer of the
      <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">
       Order of the British Empire
      </a>
      in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_honours_system" title="British honours system">
       Queen's Birthday Honours
      </a>
      for services to broadcasting. He celebrated the news by playing "
      <a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen_(Sex_Pistols_song)" title="God Save the Queen (Sex Pistols song)">
       God Save the Queen
      </a>
      " by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sex_Pistols" title="The Sex Pistols">
       The Sex Pistols
      </a>
      (which was banned by the BBC when released in 1977) on his
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">
       BBC Radio 2
      </a>
      Saturday morning show. On 21 June 2006, Ross was made a Fellow of
      <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">
       University College London
      </a>
      , where he studied.
     </p>
     <p>
      In early 2006, Ross announced that after eight years he was quitting his regular panellist seat on the sport/comedy quiz show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/They_Think_It%27s_All_Over_(TV_series)" title="They Think It's All Over (TV series)">
        They Think It's All Over
       </a>
      </i>
      explaining: "I need time now to focus on my other commitments and so regrettably I won't be back for the 20th series." After Ross's departure, only two more episodes of the show were made before it was cancelled.
     </p>
     <p>
      In January 2006 he presented
      <i>
       Jonathan Ross' Asian Invasion
      </i>
      , broadcast on
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">
       BBC Four
      </a>
      . The three-part documentary followed Ross as he explored the film industry in Japan, Hong Kong and South Korea, interviewing directors and showcasing clips. His interest in Asian culture and his self-confessed love for
      <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">
       anime
      </a>
      and video games led him to making three series of
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Three" title="BBC Three">
       BBC Three
      </a>
      show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Japanorama" title="Japanorama">
        Japanorama
       </a>
      </i>
      , as well as producing another series for the same channel called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Joe_Go_Tokyo" title="Adam and Joe Go Tokyo">
        Adam and Joe Go Tokyo
       </a>
      </i>
      , starring
      <a href="/wiki/Adam_Buxton" title="Adam Buxton">
       Adam Buxton
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joe_Cornish_(filmmaker)" title="Joe Cornish (filmmaker)">
       Joe Cornish
      </a>
      . He produced the latter programme through his own production company Hot Sauce.
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 2006, a bidding war was sparked between BBC and other broadcasters for Ross's services. Although other broadcasters were unsuccessful in poaching Ross, it is believed that their bids were higher than the BBC during negotiations. ITV, who bid for Ross, poached chat host
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Parkinson" title="Michael Parkinson">
       Michael Parkinson
      </a>
      around the same time. Ross became the highest paid television personality in Britain, when a new BBC contract secured his services until 2010, for a reported 18 million (6 million per year).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After little initial interest, a media story about the calls generated a high number of complaints. Brand resigned from the BBC, while Ross was suspended without pay. BBC director general
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Thompson_(media_executive)" title="Mark Thompson (media executive)">
       Mark Thompson
      </a>
      stated that Ross should take the disciplinary action as a "final warning".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The BBC was later fined 150,000 by Britain's broadcast regulator for airing the calls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On 21 November 2008, the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC_Trust" title="BBC Trust">
       BBC Trust
      </a>
      said that the phone calls were a "deplorable intrusion with no editorial justification".

       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The trust gave its backing to Ross's 12-week suspension but recommended that no further action be taken against him. He returned to work in January 2009 with a new series of
      <i>
       Friday Night
      </i>
      . From 23 May 2009, Ross' BBC Radio 2 show was recorded 24 hours before broadcast.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Homophobia_accusation">
       Homophobia accusation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Ross&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Homophobia accusation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On 13 May 2009, Ross was accused of
      <a href="/wiki/Homophobia" title="Homophobia">
       homophobia
      </a>
      after a comment he made on his radio show,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in which he said,
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       If your son asks for a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Montana" title="Hannah Montana">
         Hannah Montana
        </a>
       </i>
       MP3 player, then you might want to already think about putting him down for
       <a href="/wiki/LGBT_adoption" title="LGBT adoption">
        adoption
       </a>
       in later life, when they settle down with their partner.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ofcom-07-09_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ofcom-07-09-51">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         51
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      An incorrect version of this quote was also circulated, in which Ross was accused of saying:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       If your son asks for a
       <i>
        Hannah Montana
       </i>
       MP3 player, you might want to already think about putting him down for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_adoption" title="Child adoption">
        adoption
       </a>
       before he brings his  erm  partner home.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         52
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ofcom" title="Ofcom">
       Ofcom
      </a>
      received 61 complaints following the comment. On 7 July 2009, Ofcom ruled that Ross did not breach the broadcasting code. They wrote in their opinion that "the comment was clearly presented as a joke intended to make light of the reactions that some parents may have if their child chooses a toy that is very widely recognised to be designed and marketed for the opposite sex" and that the nature of the joke and tone and manner in which it was presented "made clear that it was not intended to be hostile or pejorative towards the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gay_community" title="Gay community">
       gay community
      </a>
      in general."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ofcom-07-09_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ofcom-07-09-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stonewall_(UK)" title="Stonewall (UK)">
       Stonewall
      </a>
      criticised the ruling; saying "the fact that a comment is light-hearted does not absolve it from perpetuating the stereotypes that lead to homophobic bullying."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Honours_and_awards">
       Honours and awards
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

     <ul>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">
        OBE
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">
        Fellow
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_College,_London" title="University College, London">
        University College, London (UCL)
       </a>
       , into which his alma mater, SSEES, had been absorbed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCL_Fellowships_conferred_10-1">

         <span>

       .
      </li>
     </ul>
