     <h2 id="post-2526">
      <a href="http://www.blogsnreviews.com/reviews/more-public-stock-offerings-plan-unveiled-by-sec.html" rel="bookmark">
       More Public Stock Offerings Plan Unveiled By SEC
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      Written by James |
      <a href="http://www.blogsnreviews.com/reviews" rel="category tag">
       Reviews
      </a>
      | Tuesday 11 February 2014  -
     </div>
     <div class="shareit">
      <a href="#share_article">
       Share This Article
      </a>
     </div>
     <div style="clear: both;text-align: center;">
     </div>
     <div class="storycontent">
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       According to a proposal announced by U.S. securities regulators, start-up companies will now be able to raise much more capital through certain public stock deals without facing costly regulatory burdens.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: center;">
       <img alt="More Public Stock Offerings' Plan Unveiled By SEC" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2531" height="426" src="http://www.blogsnreviews.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/More-Public-Stock-Offerings-Plan-Unveiled-By-SEC.jpg" style="float: none;" title="More Public Stock Offerings' Plan Unveiled By SEC" width="600"/>
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       This plan from the
       <a href="http://www.sec.gov/" target="_blank" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">
        Securities and Exchange Commission
       </a>
       is the last outstanding rule requirement from the Jumpstart Our Business Startups Act, a 2012 law that eases regulations for assisting small businesses raise capital and go public. This plan would be updating a rule on the books termed as Regulation A, which is a measure intended to spur capital-raising by giving exemptions to
       <a href="http://www.blogsnreviews.com/" target="_blank" title="specific public offerings">
        specific public offerings
       </a>
       from SEC registration requirements.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       Companies may now decide to increase the amount of money that can be raised by them under Regulation A deals from $5 million under current rules to $50 million in a one-year period. This proposal would exempt deals between $5 million and $50 million from oversight by the states though any deals above the $5 million mark would also be subject to some additional regulatory requirements. Deals below $5 million would be subject to state review, under the SECs plan.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       EC Chair Mary Jo White said the current proposed pre-emption of state law appears necessary as Regulation A has been so under-used in recent years. I will be closely watching the continued development of this program and would like to hear more about how this program could effectively resolve the challenges identified with the current approach to state securities law compliance, she added.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       North American Securities Administrators Association president Andrea Seidt panned the SECs proposal by saying this could lead to more fraud. It is not clear why the commission would remove state oversight in a high-risk area where both federal and state resources should be fully leveraged to provide sufficient, regular review, Seidt said.
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: justify;">
       It is not reasonable for the commission to expect the states to continue to clean up all of the mess left behind in the wake of preemptive measures like this.
      </p>
