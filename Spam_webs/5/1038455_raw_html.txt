     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-44318 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="44318" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="image-article_inline_full">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/image-article_inline_full/public/blogs/698/2010/06/44318-29777.jpg?itok=krsJxIut" title=""/>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            Bryan Caplan, a professor of economics at George Mason University, argues that "good
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/parenting" title="Psychology Today looks at parenting">
             parenting
            </a>
            is less work and more fun than people think" and that should be reason enough to have more children. In
            <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704289504575313201221533826.html?mod=rss_Today's_Most_Popular">
             his article
            </a>
            in the Saturday/Father's Day
            <em>
             Wall Street Journal,
            </em>
            he presents a flawed case: He primarily bases his plea on studies of twins in the United States and adopted children in Korea and concludes that what parents strive for and do to affect their children's outcomes doesn't work: "Parents often change their kids in the short-run, but as kids grow up, their parents' influence wears off." He wants parents to stop feeling
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/guilt" title="Psychology Today looks at guilty">
             guilty
            </a>
            for what they don't do and sit back and enjoy their children-a point I don't argue with.
           </p>
           <p>
            However,
            <a href="http://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/singletons/200809/children-are-big-ticket-items">
             the economic demand
            </a>
            on parents grows geometrically in today's financial world when they have more children to feed, shelter, and educate. What Caplan doesn't factor in is that multiple children (or even another child added to the one or two or more you already have) put
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/stress" title="Psychology Today looks at stress">
             stress
            </a>
            on parents-if only to earn enough money to cover basic costs. More children frequently increase the need to have two working parents, a condition many families are in already.
           </p>
           <p>
            Parents work 11 more hours than they did in the 1970s and
            <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/06/20/weekinreview/20parkerpope.html?scp=1&amp;sq=Now,%20Dad%20Feels%20as%20Stressed%20as%20Mom&amp;st=cse">
             dads today feel as stressed as moms
            </a>
            . In a
            <a href="http://familiesandwork.org/site/research/reports/Times_Are_Changing.pdf">
             2008 study of Study of the Changing Workforce
            </a>
            , The Families and Work Institute reports that for fathers "each additional hour worked per week increases the probability of experiencing some degree of work-life conflict." Instead of looking at the realities of family life, Caplan asks readers to project to a time when they are older, in their sixties and eighties, and will have lots of grandchildren (to enjoy) if they have more children now.
           </p>
           <p>
            His claim that "parents' sacrifice is much smaller than it looks" probably doesn't feel that way to those raising children today. His examples are supposed to convince parents contemplating having another child that they have a "modest" effect on their children's outcomes. He urges parents not to worry if they skip reading to their offspring, for example. As he states, "Your choices have little effect on your kids' development, so it's OK to relax...you don't have to go the extra mile to prepare your children for the future." But, you still have to provide for them.
           </p>
           <p>
            Adopting Caplan's approach to family size also requires a monumental shift in parenting
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/philosophy" title="Psychology Today looks at philosophy">
             philosophy
            </a>
            as we know it. We have a nation of
            <a href="http://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/singletons/200904/when-does-mothering-become-smothering-part-1">
             helicopter parents
            </a>
            with an overdose of competitiveness mixed in. When I speak with parents, they almost universally say that they want to give their children every advantage, and many do. They work hard to give their children a competitive edge. It seems unlikely that parents raising children today will "pull back" as Caplan advises. Do you envision parents you know "pulling back" anytime soon?
           </p>
           <p>
            "The Breeders' Cup," the title of Caplan's article, in itself connotes
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/sport-and-competition" title="Psychology Today looks at competition">
             competition
            </a>
            -the best thoroughbred horses
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/race-and-ethnicity" title="Psychology Today looks at race">
             race
            </a>
            for the championship-not the relaxed parenting he recommends. Personally, I would need a lot more convincing before bringing another child into the family. Wouldn't you?
           </p>
