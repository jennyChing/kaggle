            <h1 class="title light" itemprop="title headline">
             7 Simple Methods To Fight Against Your Unconscious Biases
            </h1>
            <div class="deck fc-deck" itemprop="deck description">
             <p>
              What you think you believe and what your unconscious associations reveal about your beliefs are often very different.
             </p>
            </div>
            <div class="byline">
             By
             <span itemprop="byline">
              Courtney Seiter
             </span>
            </div>
           </header>
           <div class="row collapse">
            <div class="body prose article-prose twelve columns" itemprop="body">
             <p>
              I believe in the power of women to build inspiring careers in all types of fields.
             </p>
             <p>
              At least, thats what I
              <em>
               thought
              </em>
              I believed. Its what my conscious mind thinks, at least.
             </p>
             <p>
              My unconscious mind, however, favors traditional Western gender roles: men focusing on careers while women focus on family.
             </p>
             <p>
              I learned about this dichotomy from taking an
              <a href="http://www.understandingprejudice.org/iat/genframe.htm" target="_blank">
               implicit association test
              </a>
              , a social psychology test designed to measure a persons unconscious or automatic associations between types of people and specific concepts or ideas.
             </p>
             <p>
              And Im not alone: The results of more than one million tests suggest that most people have these unconscious associations.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//c.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-1-implicit-association-gender-1.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              So I thought I would search for a few ways I could begin to correct my implicit biases and bring my unconscious mind on board with what the rest of me believes.
             </p>
             <p>
              The book
              <em>
               <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Blindspot-Hidden-Biases-Good-People/dp/0553804642" target="_blank">
                Blindspot: The Hidden Biases of Good People
               </a>
              </em>
              (the authors are the inventors of the implicit association test) has a ton of fascinating science on this topic. One bit in particular stood out to me:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               Eric Kandel, a neuroscientist at Columbia University who received a Nobel Prize for his work on memory, was once pressed to say how much of the mind works unconsciously; he gave an estimate of 80 to 90%.
              </p>
              <p>
               The actual number isnt important or even possible to derive. The point is that experts agree that the ability to have conscious access to our minds is quite low.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              So its especially important to focus on inclusivity in our conscious minds, because our unconscious has already put most of us (me included!) in quite a deficit.
             </p>
             <p>
              Though this list is by no means exhaustive, here are a few things I discovered that might help us to counteract our own unconscious and get closer to the people we truly want to be.
             </p>
             <h4>
              <a name="1_Use_Inclusive_Language">
               1. Use Inclusive Language
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              One thing weve been working on lately in Buffers virtual workplace, where most communication is written, is to be
              <a href="https://open.bufferapp.com/job-descriptions-diversity/" target="_blank">
               mindful of the language we use
              </a>
              and make sure its as inclusive as possible.
             </p>
             <p>
              For example, many of us have been cracking down on our use of the colloquial "hey guys" greeting as we address the team. It was, of course, never meant to exclude the women on the team and has always been intended as a general greeting.
             </p>
             <p>
              But
              <a href="https://open.bufferapp.com/buffer-values/" target="_blank">
               we value clarity in communication
              </a>
              at Buffer, and this greeting, friendly though its intentions might be, can be easily misconstrued.
             </p>
             <p>
              Pinterest engineer
              <a class="fc-plugin people-page" data-id="fastcompany|node|3045599" data-name="peoplePages" href="//www.fastcompany.com/person/tracy-chou">
               <img src="//f.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/320x240/tracy-chou.jpg"/>
               Tracy Chou
              </a>
              rightly gave us a little nudge on this recently, and we really appreciate it:
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-tweet inline">
              <iframe border="0" data-tweetid="580761602301026304" data-userheight="633" frameborder="0" height="633" src="/twitframe?url=https://twitter.com/triketora/status/580761602301026304" width="605">
              </iframe>
             </figure>
             <p>
              Its a great reminder to keep going on this improvement, and being aware of all our language choices. Plus we occasionally get to reference this awesome flowchart from
              <a href="http://techladymafia.com/" target="_blank">
               Tech LadyMafia
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//b.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-2-group-of-women-flowchart.png"/>
             </figure>
             <h4>
              <a name="2_Expose_Yourself_To_Counterstereotyping_Imagery_As_Simple_As_a_Screensaver">
               2. Expose Yourself To Counterstereotyping Imagery (As Simple As a Screensaver)
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              Even the creators of the implicit association tests still "fail" them.
             </p>
             <p>
              <em>
               Blindspot
              </em>
              co-author Mahzarin Banaji came up a simple and unique solution to combat some of her own "mindbugs:"
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               She created a screensaver for her computer that displays images of a diverse array of humanity. She assumes that these images may do little more than keep her alerted to the actual range of diversity in the world, as opposed to that of the more limited set of humans she encounters in her daily experience. She also favored images that represent counterstereotypes. Short bald men who are senior executives is one of her favorite counterstereotyping images. Another is a drawing from a New Yorker magazine cover, of a construction worker with hard hat on, breast-feeding her baby.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <h4>
              <a name="3_Consider_Your_Office_Furnishings">
               3. Consider Your Office Furnishings
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              If you have a physical office that you want to make more inclusive for both genders, this study might be of interest.
             </p>
             <p>
              At the University of Washington, Sapna Cheryan demonstrated that
              <a href="https://depts.washington.edu/sibl/Publications/Cheryan,%20Plaut,%20Davies,%20&amp;%20Steele%20%282009%29.pdf" target="_blank">
               adding more feminine decor to computer science classrooms
              </a>
              strengthened womens associations of female gender with the possibility of computer science careers.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//h.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-3-office-environment-3.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              By changing out the objects in a computer science classroom from things like a Star Trek poster and video games to objects not considered stereotypical of computer science like a nature poster, the experiment
              <span class="subhead">
               boosted female study participants interest in computer science to the level of their male peers
              </span>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              The study concluded:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               Environments can act like gatekeepers by preventing people who do not feel they fit into those environments from ever considering membership in the associated groups.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <h4>
              <a name="4_Empower_Mentors_For_Underrepresented_Groups">
               4. Empower Mentors For Underrepresented Groups
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              The researcher Buju Dasgupta has lots of interesting
              <a href="http://people.umass.edu/nd/research_page.html" target="_blank">
               studies going on about implicit prejudice and stereotypes
              </a>
              . One I really like is the
              <span class="subhead">
               Stereotype Inoculation Model
              </span>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              This is her theory that successful people in your group who look like you, like teachers and peers, can function as a
              <a href="http://www.lpfi.org/sites/default/files/steming_the_tide.pdf" target="_blank">
               "social vaccine" that inoculates you
              </a>
              from some of the self-doubt or alienation you might otherwise face in such a situation.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//f.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-4-mentor-study.png"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              So far she has found a strengthening of "female = leader" and "female = math" associations in women college students after they received sustained exposure via their college courses to women faculty members.
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               Results from several lab and field studies revealed that exposure to female STEM professors and experts enhanced womens positive implicit attitudes toward STEM, increased their identification with STEM, their confidence in STEM, and effort on tests and exams.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              This could means that having even a few visible members of underrepresented groups on your team could have a compounding effect, if your organization can encourage and support mentoring relationships.
             </p>
             <h4>
              <a name="5_Use_Social_Media_To_Amplify_New_Voices">
               5. Use Social Media To Amplify New Voices
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              Did you know, in its analytics section, Twitter will tell you the
              <a href="http://usvsth3m.com/post/95999998028/twitter-will-now-tell-you-what-the-gender-split-of-your" target="_blank">
               gender split of your followers
              </a>
              ?
             </p>
             <p>
              I was a bit surprised to discover my followers are majority male (though there is a bit of uncertainty about
              <a href="http://readwrite.com/2014/09/03/twitter-analytics-demographics-gender-algorithm-male-female-followers" target="_blank">
               how Twitter figures out those genders
              </a>
              ).
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//d.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-5-twitter-gender-split-5.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              I was even more surprised by my results from
              <a href="http://twee-q.com/" target="_blank">
               Twee-Q
              </a>
              , a tool that analyzes the gender of the voices you amplify through retweets. I have a lot of work to do in amplifying smart female voices!
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//a.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-6-twee-q-score.png"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              Of the 107,966 Twitter accounts that have been input into Twee-Q, theres an immense tendency to amplify men more often than women:
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//g.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-7-twee-q-totals-7.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              After discovering that he followed a nearly equal ratio of women and men, but retweeted men
              <span class="subhead">
               three times as often
              </span>
              as women, the blogger and entrepreneur Anil Dash tried an experiment.
             </p>
             <p>
              For a year he attempted to amplify different kinds of voices than he normally would by
              <a href="https://medium.com/the-web-we-make/the-year-i-didnt-retweet-men-79403a7eade1" target="_blank">
               retweeting women exclusively
              </a>
              . He ended up enjoying the experiment and recommending it to others:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               If youre inclined, try being mindful of whose voices you share, amplify, validate and promote to others we spend so very much of our time on these social networks, and theres so much we can do to right the wrongs weve seen in other media, through simple, small actions. This ones been a delightful and fun place to start.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <h4>
              <a name="6_Find_Members_Of_Underrepresented_Groups_That_You_Admire">
               6. Find Members Of Underrepresented Groups That You Admire
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              This is a really fun and simple one. What if you could fight your brains unconscious bias simply by admiring others?
             </p>
             <p>
              Another study from the very busy Dr. Buju Dasgupta found that when people are exposed to admired members of disadvantaged groups (African Americans, gays and lesbians, elderly, women), they express less implicit bias against these groups.
             </p>
             <p>
              In this
              <a href="http://faculty.washington.edu/agg/pdf/Dasgupta_Gwald._JPSP_2001.OCR.pdf" target="_blank">
               study of racial implicit bias
              </a>
              , participants revealed less bias after being shown "black examplars"pictures of famous and admired people like Martin Luther King Jr., Colin Powell, Michael Jordan and Denzel Washington.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//h.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-8-admired-members-study-8.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              This means one easy way to work on unconscious bias could be to simply seek out more admired members of underrepresented groups and focus on those peoples work more often.
             </p>
             <h4>
              <a name="7_Use_Your_Imagination_Counterprogram_Your_Brain">
               7. Use Your Imagination: Counterprogram Your Brain
              </a>
             </h4>
             <p>
              Possibly the simplest way of all to retrain your unconscious mind? Use your imagination.
             </p>
             <p>
              At the University of Colorado, researcher Irene Blair discovered that
              <a href="http://www.homepage.psy.utexas.edu/HomePage/Class/Psy394U/Bower/10%20Automatic%20Process/I.Blair-mod.%20stereotypes.pdf" target="_blank">
               simple imagination exercises
              </a>
              were enough to weaken some implicit stereotypes.
             </p>
             <p>
              She asked a mixed-gender group of college students to "take a few minutes to imagine what a strong woman is like, why she is considered strong, what she is capable of doing, and what kinds of hobbies and activities she enjoys."
             </p>
             <p>
              The participants came up with all sort of images, from bosses to athletes:
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//f.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2015/04/3044738-inline-i-9-srtrong-woman-study-9.jpg"/>
             </figure>
             <p>
              No matter what their image was, participants who engaged in the mental imagery exercise produced "substantially weaker implicit stereotypes" compared with participants who engaged in neutral mental imagery or no mental energy.
             </p>
             <p>
              So if you happen to be challenged by a particular implicit bias, discovered either through taking a test or your own intuition, you can try counterprogramming your brain with some simple visual exercises like this one.
             </p>
             <p>
              Being empathetic and inclusive to those of all walks of life is a skill it seems that most of us could work on for a lifetime.
             </p>
             <p>
              Im looking forward to putting these strategies into practice to see if I can move my unconscious mind in the right direction.
             </p>

             </p>
