          <h1 class="withTip entry-title">
           <a class="bookmark" href="/blog/2014/06/20/more-snowden-effect-grayson-amendment-protects-security-standards-from-nsa/">
            More Snowden Effect: Grayson amendment protects security standards from NSA
           </a>
          </h1>
          <div class="meta">
           <address class="author tip">
            by
            <a class="url fn" href="/team/jwonderlich/" rel="author">
             John Wonderlich
            </a>
           </address>
           <ul class="channels">
            <li class="channel policy textReplace">
             <a href="/blog/policy/">
              policy
             </a>
            </li>
           </ul>
           <time class="published" datetime="2014-06-20T10:19:43" pubdate="">
            June 20, 2014, 10:19 a.m.
           </time>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="clear">
          </div>
          <div class="share-buttons module" data-facebook-like-options="layout=button_count" data-options="linkBack=http://sunlightfoundation.com/blog/2014/06/20/more-snowden-effect-grayson-amendment-protects-security-standards-from-nsa/&amp;width=150&amp;text=More%20Snowden%20Effect%3A%20Grayson%20amendment%20protects%20security%20standards%20from%20NSA&amp;showCounts=right" data-services="twitter-tweet,facebook-like" data-socialite="auto" data-twitter-tweet-options="via=sunfoundation">
          </div>
         </header>
         <div class="entry-content clearfix">
          <figure class="right">
           <img alt="" src="https://horseradish.s3.amazonaws.com/CACHE/images/photos/35/ec/9893567e419c/National_Security_Agency_headquarters%2C_Fort_Meade%2C_Maryland-800.jpg"/>
           <figcaption>
            National Security Agency headquarters, Fort Meade, Maryland. Photo credit: Wikimedia Commons.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p>
           Add another notch to
           <a href="http://pressthink.org/2013/07/the-snowden-effect-definition-and-examples/">
            the Snowden Effect
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           In the wake of revelations that the NSA was
           <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/09/06/us/nsa-foils-much-internet-encryption.html?hp&amp;_r=0&amp;pagewanted=all">
            subverting technical standards
           </a>
           on which the Internet relies  making everyone less secure and undermining the encryption standards we rely on  Congress is pursuing a new batch of reforms, and not just through the
           <a href="http://www.opencongress.org/bill/s1599-113/show">
            USA FREEDOM Act
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           A few weeks ago, Sunlight helped
           <a href="http://sunlightfoundation.com/blog/2014/05/22/getting-secrecy-out-of-science/">
            get an amendment passed
           </a>
           to the
           <a href="https://scout.sunlightfoundation.com/item/bill/hr4186-113/first-act-of-2014">
            FIRST act
           </a>
           , removing the old requirement that the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) consult with the NSA for technical standards.
          </p>
          <p>
           Last night saw another significant step  again in an amendment  this time to the
           <a href="https://scout.sunlightfoundation.com/item/bill/hr4870-113/department-of-defense-appropriations-act-2015">
            Department of Defense appropriations bill
           </a>
           . The amendment limits the NSAs use of funds to activities that improve information security, building a significant barrier to its ability to weaken security and encryption standards.
          </p>
          <p>
           You can see the
           <a href="http://houselive.gov/MediaPlayer.php?view_id=2&amp;clip_id=10732">
            debate on the amendment here
           </a>
           (at 11:09 a.m. ET). Inexplicably, Rep. Rodney Frelinghuysen, R-N.J., Chair of the Defense Appropriations Subcommittee, denies that the NSA subverted technical standards at all, or that NISTs reputation was harmed.
          </p>
          <p>
           Regardless, an amendment introduced by Rep. Alan Grayson, D-Fla., has created an essential new consensus: Governments role in propagating reliable security standards should trump any mandate to undermine them.
          </p>
          <p>
           While we shouldnt expect any single amendment to gut the intelligence agencies subterfuge, even on essential security standards, theres reason to be optimistic.
          </p>
          <p>
           The legislative apathy about intelligence activities that empowered the worst of the Snowden revelations is starting to erode. Now it's time for the Senate to follow suit.
          </p>
