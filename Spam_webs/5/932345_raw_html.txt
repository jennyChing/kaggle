     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Sb sword
      </b>
      (also known as the
      <b>
       Thurmuth sword
      </b>
      ) is an early 9th-century
      <a href="/wiki/Viking_sword" title="Viking sword">
       Viking sword
      </a>
      , found in a barrow at Sb,
      <a href="/wiki/Vik" title="Vik">
       Vik
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sogn" title="Sogn">
       Sogn
      </a>
      , Norway in 1825. It is now held at the
      <a href="/wiki/Bergen_Museum" title="Bergen Museum">
       Bergen Museum
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">
       Bergen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
       Norway
      </a>
      . The sword has an enigmatic inscription on its blade, which has been identified as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runic_inscription" title="Runic inscription">
       runic inscription
      </a>
      incorporating a
      <a href="/wiki/Swastika_(Germanic_Iron_Age)" title="Swastika (Germanic Iron Age)">
       swastika symbol
      </a>
      . If so, this sword is a very rare example of a weapon with a runic inscription on its blade.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%A6b%C3%B8_sword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">

       </span>

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg">
        <img alt="Drawing of a complete sword and detail of the upper part of the blade." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="210" data-file-width="841" height="55" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg/220px-Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg/330px-Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg/440px-Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thurmuth_Rune_Sword.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Drawing by George Stephens of the Sb sword and detail of inlaid decoration on the reverse
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The sword itself is categorized as 'Type C' by Pedersen (1919), who notes that it is unique in showing remnants of a metal thread at the broadsides of the upper hilt,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      compared to other specimens of the type which show horizontal ridges or protruding edges, or less commonly inlaid forged stripes or protruding moldings that seem to be imitations of twisted or smooth thread. It is described as an imitation of a foreign [continental] sword inscription because of the lack of parallels in native tradition.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2010)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      There is an inscription realised in iron inlay along the center of the blade, close to the hilt.
     </p>

     <p>
      The sword was described in 1867 by
      <a href="/wiki/George_Stephens_(philologist)" title="George Stephens (philologist)">
       George Stephens
      </a>
      , an English archaeologist and philologist who specialised in the runic inscriptions of Scandinavia, in his book
      <i>
       Handbook of the Old-Northern Runic Monuments of Scandinavia and England
      </i>
      . In this work he showed a drawing of the sword with a very clear inscription comprising five runes or rune-like letters with a
      <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">
       swastika
      </a>
      symbol in the middle. According to Stephens the inscription reads
      <i>
       ohmu
      </i>
      from right to left. He interpreted the swastika as being used in
      <a href="/wiki/Rebus" title="Rebus">
       rebus
      </a>
      -writing to represent the syllable
      <i>
       ur
      </i>
      for the god
      <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">
       Thor
      </a>
      , and thus expanded the reading to
      <i>
       oh urmu
      </i>
      meaning "Owns [me], Thurmuth".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This reading was inspired by the idea that the swastika was used as a
      <a href="/wiki/Thor#Swastikas" title="Thor">
       symbol of Thor
      </a>
      (more precisely, of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thor%27s_hammer" title="Thor's hammer">
       Thor's hammer
      </a>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">
       Viking Age
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" title="Norse paganism">
       Norse paganism
      </a>
      . It was the subject of scholarly discussion at the International Congress of Anthropology and Prehistoric Archology at Budapest in 1876, where the prevalent opinion was that the swastika stood for "blessing" or "good luck".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1889, in a review of a book by A. L. Lorange, Stephens noted that the sword had been treated with acid whilst at the Danish Museum, with the result that the sword and its inscription were severely damaged, and consequently the inscription shown in a colour plate in Lorange's book was undecipherable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
