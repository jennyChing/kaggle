            <hgroup>
             <h1>
              Osteoporosis in Patients with Chronic Kidney Disease
             </h1>
            </hgroup>
           </header>
           <div class="byline">
            By Sanjai Sinha, MD, FACP, Assistant Professor, Department of Medicine, Weill Cornell Medical College, New York, NY
            <br/>
            Reviewed by Vrunda Bhavsar Desai, MD, FACOG, Assistant Professor of Obstetrics, Gynecology, and Reproductive Sciences, Yale University School of Medicine, New Haven, CT
           </div>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <aside class="sidebar">
            <div class="media infographic">
             <img alt="image" src="http://clf1.medpagetoday.com/assets/images/cuso/Osteopeorosis-and-Kidney-Disease-300.jpg"/>

            <section class="actionpoints">
             <header>
              <h2>
               Take Note
              </h2>
             </header>
             <div class="content">
              <ul>
               <li>
                Though standard criteria for diagnosing osteoporosis exist, patients in chronic kidney disease (CKD) stages 3-5 may have coexisting metabolic bone disease (CKD-MBD) that is also an important cause of fragility fracturesand distinct from osteoporosis.
               </li>
               <li>
                Examples of CKD-MBD include secondary hyperparathyrodism, osteomalacia, and adynamic bone disease.
               </li>
               <li>
                Treating the causes of CKD-MBD is important in preventing fragility fractures.
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
            </section>
           </aside>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Osteoporosis affects up to 200 million women worldwide and causes 9 million fractures annually. An osteoporosis-related fracture occurs every 3 seconds.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            Among people over age 50, an estimated 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men will experience a fragility fracture.
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            Osteoporosis is a disease with significant morbidity and mortality, so identification and treatment for those at greatest risk are crucial. Treatment decisions may be tempered, however, in patients with advanced kidney disease. Chronic kidney disease (CKD) stages 1-4 affects up to 20 million Americans, and stage 5 disease affects about 750,000.
            <sup>
             3
            </sup>
            To confound the diagnosis and treatment even further, as patients age, the prevalence of both osteoporosis and CKD increases.
           </p>
           <p>
            Patients with CKD are more likely to develop osteoporosis and fractures than age-matched controls without kidney disease.
            <sup>
             4
            </sup>
            In addition, the risk of fracture-related mortality increases as the severity of CKD increases.
            <sup>
             5
            </sup>
            Patients with chronic kidney disease-metabolic bone disease (CKD-MBD) may need different treatment of fragility fractures, and standard osteoporosis therapies may be precluded in some high-risk patients.
           </p>
           <p>
            Examples of CKD-MBD include osteomalacia (decreased mineralization of newly formed bone), secondary hyperparathyroidism, mixed uremic osteodystrophy, amyloid bone disease, and adynamic bone disease. CKD-MBD is an independent risk factor for fragility fractures.
           </p>
           <p>
            So what tests should we pursue to make a diagnosis of renal bone disease, and which stages of CKD are we most concerned about? To answer these questions, we turned to Paul Miller, MD, Distinguished Clinical Professor of Medicine at the University of Colorado Health Sciences Center and the Medical Director of the Colorado Center for Bone Research. Dr. Miller notes that in stage 3 CKD, its sufficient to use T-scores and/or fracture history to make the diagnosis of osteoporosiswithout looking for evidence of CKD-MBD. Treatment with standard agents for osteoporosis is recommended, he says.
           </p>
           <p>
            It's CKD stages 4 and 5 (eGFR
           </p>
           <p>
            In these later-stage patients, the 2 most important markers of CKD-MBD are parathyroid hormone (PTH) and bone specific alkaline phosphatase (BSAP). A high PTH (&gt;6x the upper limit of normal) rules out adynamic bone disease and suggests hyperparathyroidism. Treating this secondary hyperparathyroidism is the goal of care to prevent fragility fractures.
           </p>
           <p>
            Dr. Miller notes that a high BSAP also goes against the diagnosis of adynamic bone disease in most patients, except in those who have just had a recent large bone fracture.
           </p>
           <p>
            A high BSAP may be suggestive of Pagets Disease, bone metastasis, and osteomalacia. A low BSAP in a patient not on any antiresorptive therapy may suggest adynamic bone disease. The combination of a low PTH (
           </p>
           <p>
            According to Dr. Miller, other important tests typically ordered in patients with CKD stages 4-5 include determinations of calcium, vitamin D, and phosphorous, as the handling of all of these important minerals is deranged in advanced CKD and plays a significant role in bone turnover, mineralization and volume.
           </p>
           <p>
            CKD-MBD and osteoporosis can co-exist. There is no ICD-9 code for CKD-MBD, but it is suggested by persistent hyperphosphatemia, elevated PTH and "normal BSAP. The treatment for CKD-MBD is to manage hyperphosphatemia and secondary hyperparathyroidism, not typical osteoporosis therapies. However in high-risk patients (prior fragility fracture, T-scores 65 years), once the CKD-MBD metabolic parameters are managed, osteoporosis medications can be considered.
           </p>
           <p>
            Regarding the occasional need for bone biopsy in patients with suspected renal osteodystrophy, Dr. Miller says he only considers a bone biopsy in patients with CKD stages 4-5 when he suspects adynamic bone disease based on blood tests and needs to consider either an antiresorptive agent or, if adynamic, an anabolic agent first line in high-risk patients.
           </p>
           <p>
            In summary, CKD-MBD and osteoporosis may share some similar pathophysiologic processes leading to more fragile bones, but with a crucial difference in the amount of bone turnover that is occurring. Since many of the medications for osteoporosis are based on decreasing bone turnover, distinguishing states where no or low bone turnover is occurring, as in some forms of CKD-MBD, is important to make before considering osteoporosis therapy. The cohort of CKD patients where the mineral and bone disorder is most problematic is CKD stages 4 and 5.
           </p>
