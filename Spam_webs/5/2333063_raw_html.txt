        <p>
         When Australia
         <a href="http://www.humanservices.gov.au/customer/services/centrelink/parental-leave-pay" target="_hplink">
          passed a parental leave law
         </a>
         in 2010, it left the U.S. as the only industrialized nation not to mandate paid leave for mothers of newborns. Most of the rest of the world has paid maternity leave policies, too; Lesotho, Swaziland and Papua New Guinea are the only other countries
         <a href="http://www.ilo.org/wcmsp5/groups/public/@dgreports/@dcomm/@publ/documents/publication/wcms_124442.pdf" target="_hplink">
          that do not
         </a>
         . Many countries give new fathers paid time off as well or allow parents to share paid leave.
        </p>
        <p>
         New parents in the U.S. are guaranteed their jobs for 12 weeks after the arrival of a new baby, thanks to the Family Medical Leave Act of 1993, but they do not have to be paid during that time and exemptions apply for small companies.
         <a href="http://www.workingmother.com/best-companies/everyone-us-state-maternity-leave" target="_hplink">
          Only about 16 percent of employers
         </a>
         offer fully paid maternity leave and many families
         <a href="http://www.thenation.com/article/167897/too-often-new-baby-brings-big-debt#" target="_hplink">
          take on significant debt or turn to public assistance
         </a>
         around the birth of a child. As America's
         <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424127887323375204578270053387770718.html" target="_hplink">
          falling fertility rate
         </a>
         raises economic concerns, working families may long to procreate in Sweden, where parents
         <a href="http://www.wbur.org/npr/139121410/parental-leave-the-swedes-are-the-most-generous" target="_hplink">
          are given 480 paid days per child
         </a>
         , to be shared between them and used anytime before the kid turns eight.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img src="http://big.assets.huffingtonpost.com/0204pregnancyleave_final.png" width="570px"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Will the U.S. catch up with the rest of the world during President Obama's second term? Advocates are working to get a national law passed while some states are expanding family leave policies,
         <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/sexes/archive/2012/12/why-paid-leave-could-pass-in-obamas-second-term-americans-want-it/265831/" target="_hplink">
          the Atlantic reports
         </a>
         . See a new White House petition
         <a href="https://petitions.whitehouse.gov/petition/mandate-paid-maternity-leave/MtHK02QF" target="_hplink">
          here
         </a>
         . In the meantime, certain companies understand that keeping new parents happy makes more sense than replacing them, which generally costs somewhere
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/work-in-progress/2012/05/23/3-reasons-why-card-carrying-capitalists-should-support-paid-family-leave/3/" target="_hplink">
          between 50 and 200 percent
         </a>
         of a worker's salary. When Google
         <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2012/08/23/technology/in-googles-inner-circle-a-falling-number-of-women.html?pagewanted=all&amp;_r=0" target="_hplink">
          lengthened its maternity leave from three months to five
         </a>
         and made it fully paid, new-mom attrition fell by half.
         <em>
         </em>
        </p>

         , available Friday, March 1.
