       <div class="fixed-asset perfectPixel">

       <div class="fool">
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.fool.com/investing/general/2013/11/09/will-lockheed-martins-f-35-cost-america-11-trillio.aspx?source=epthstlnk0000001">
          Over-budget
         </a>
         , behind schedule, and -- reportedly -- not a particularly good fighter jet,
         <strong>
          Lockheed Martin
         </strong>
         's  ultramodern Joint Strike Fighter may be
         <a href="http://www.fool.com/investing/general/2014/07/12/is-it-time-to-kill-lockheed-martins-3986-billion-f.aspx?source=epthstlnk0000001">
          destined for the scrap heap
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         In a 2013 RAND Corporation report, one of the nation's foremost military analysts, blasted the F-35 for being a fighter that "
         <a href="http://www.stopthef35.com/rand-corp-f35-cant-turn-cant-climb-cant-run/">
          can't turn, can't climb, can't run
         </a>
         ." Proponents of the F-35 reply that because it's stealthy, it shouldn't have to do any of those things -- lobbing missiles at its foes from over the horizon, and long before they can even see it.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unfortunately, it turns out that the F-35 may not do the "invisibility" thing very well, either.
        </p>
        <p class="caption">
         <img alt="" src="http://g.foolcdn.com/editorial/images/146867/f35_large.jpg"/>
         <br/>
         Photo:
         <a href="http://www.lockheedmartin.com/us/products/f35.html">
          Lockheed Martin
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Invisible becomes visible -- voila!
          <br/>
         </strong>
         As
         <a href="http://www.defensenews.com/article/20141004/DEFREG03/310040023">
          DefenseNews.com
         </a>
         recently revealed, China has a new device that may be able to track Lockheed's F-35 fighter with "passive" radar detection technology. Dubbed the DWL002, China's equipment can apparently detect stealth aircraft at distances of up to 400 kilometers -- and 600 kilometers for larger "stealth" targets -- processing "pulse, frequency agility, pulse duration, tactical air navigation system, distance measuring equipment, jitter/stagger radar, and identification friend or foe" signals emitted by the otherwise stealthy aircraft to determine its location.
        </p>
        <p>
         To be clear, DWL002 is not an active radar system itself. As International Assessment and Strategy Center senior fellow Richard Fisher explains "Passive systems like these simply listen for any electronic emission," identifying an aircraft without having to ping it with an active radar transmitter. As such, whether or not the F-35 is itself invisible to "radar" may be a moot point.
        </p>
        <p>
         China can "see" it anyway.
        </p>
        <p class="caption">
         <img alt="" src="http://g.foolcdn.com/editorial/images/146867/china-radar_large.jpg"/>
         <br/>
         Don't look now, Lockheed Martin. But China is watching you. Photo:
         <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:ROCN_MPS-2001_Mobile_Radar_System_Radar_Vehicle_20130504a.jpg">
          Wikimedia Commons
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          And so can Russia
          <br/>
         </strong>
         This problem with the F-35's lack of invisibility, it turns out, is not limited to China. According to DN, both the Czechs and the Ukrainians have similar systems for passive intercept of electronic signals, capable of detecting stealth aircraft.
        </p>
        <p>
         Similarly,
         <a href="http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2014/04/28/new-u-s-stealth-jet-can-t-hide-from-russian-radar.html">
          Aviation Week
         </a>
         reported earlier this year that certain very high frequency (VHF) radar systems, such as Russia's P-14 Oborona VHF early warning system, and its 3D Nebo SVU active electronically scanned array (AESA), may also be capable of detecting the F-35. (A new Chinese naval radar system, Type 517M VHF, may be similarly effective against the F-35.)
        </p>
        <p>
         And of course, there is the Balkan War incident to keep in mind. On March 27, 1999, Serbian anti-aircraft forces used a 1960s vintage P-18 VHF acquisition radarsystem (working in conjunction with an SA-3 SAM system for proximity targeting) to detect and down a F-117 stealth fighter jet.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          What this all means for Lockheed Martin
          <br/>
         </strong>
         Now, experts differ on how effective these various aircraft detection systems will be against the F-35. For one thing, while passive detection systems can tell an opponent that there's an F-35 "out there," it still takes an active detection system to guide a missile to shoot it down. But if the critics are right, it could still undermine the aircraft's reputation for invisibility, and pose a significant threat to Lockheed Martin's business.
        </p>
        <p>
         It's been 13 years now since Lockheed Martin won the contract to build what was then known as the "joint strike fighter," beating out
         <strong>
          Boeing
         </strong>
         for that honor. Since then, Lockheed stock has risen an astonishing five times in value, from $35.29 (adjusted for dividends and stock splits) on Oct. 29, 2001,to more than $176 per share today. While many factors contributed to this outperformance, Lockheed's winning the F-35 franchise has certainly contributed mightily to the stock's success.
        </p>
        <p>
         After all, analysts estimate that over the program's estimate 60-year lifespan, the F-35 will bring Lockheed Martin as much as
         <a href="http://www.fool.com/investing/general/2013/11/09/will-lockheed-martins-f-35-cost-america-11-trillio.aspx?source=epthstlnk0000001">
          $1.1 trillion
         </a>
         worth of high-margin revenues --
         <a href="http://www.fool.com/investing/general/2009/06/19/lockheeds-trillion-dollar-warplane-redux.aspx?source=epthstlnk0000001">
          or more
         </a>
         . At the 11.2% profit margin that Lockheed Martin earns at its Aeronautics division (thanks to
         <a href="https://www.capitaliq.com/">
          S&amp;P Capital IQ
         </a>
         for the data), that works out to about $112 billion in profits the company could earn from this single product -- twice the market capitalization of
         <em>
          the whole company
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         But if the F-35 comes up short in the "invisibility" department, that franchise could be in jeopardy.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The upshot for investors
          <br/>
         </strong>
         The thing about technology is that
         <a href="http://www.fool.com/investing/general/2014/01/04/drones-are-the-future-of-war-so-why-is-the-pentago.aspx?source=epthstlnk0000001">
          it's always changing
         </a>
         . What was cutting edge tech in 2001 could be obsolete by 2021. If that happens, and if the F-35 is ultimately not built in the quantities originally anticipated (as was the case with Lockheed Martin's similarly high-tech F-22 Raptor fighter jet, you'll recall), then
         <a href="http://www.fool.com/investing/general/2014/09/02/3-reasons-lockheed-martin-corporations-stock-fall.aspx?source=epthstlnk0000001">
          Lockheed Martin stock could be worth less
         </a>
         than investors are counting on.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <div class="" id="pitch">
         <p>
          <strong>
           You can't afford to miss this
           <br/>
          </strong>
          "Made in China" -- an all too familiar phrase. But not for much longer: There's a radical new technology out there, one that's already being employed by the U.S. Air Force, BMW and even Nike. Respected publications like
          <em>
           The Economist
          </em>
          have compared this disruptive invention to the steam engine and the printing press;
          <em>
           Business Insider
          </em>
          calls it "the next trillion dollar industry." Watch The Motley Fool's
          <a href="http://www.fool.com/ecap/stock-advisor/declaration-ecap-70214-v2/?aid=4631&amp;source=epthstlnk0000001">
           shocking video presentation
          </a>
          to learn about the next great wave of technological innovation, one that will bring an end to "Made In China" for good.
          <a href="http://www.fool.com/ecap/stock-advisor/declaration-ecap-70214-v2/?aid=4631&amp;source=epthstlnk0000001">
           Click here
          </a>
          !
         </p>
