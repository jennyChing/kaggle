   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Programmable matter
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Programmable matter
      </b>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
       matter
      </a>
      which has the ability to change its physical properties (shape, density,
      <a href="/wiki/Moduli_(physics)" title="Moduli (physics)">
       moduli
      </a>
      , conductivity, optical properties, etc.) in a programmable fashion, based upon user input or autonomous sensing. Programmable matter is thus linked to the concept of a material which inherently has the ability to perform information processing.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Programmable matter is a term originally coined in 1991 by
      <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_Toffoli" title="Tommaso Toffoli">
       Toffoli
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Margolus" title="Norman Margolus">
       Margolus
      </a>
      to refer to an ensemble of fine-grained computing elements arranged in space (
      <a href="#CITEREFToffoliMargolus1991">
       Toffoli &amp; Margolus 1991
      </a>
      ). Their paper describes a computing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">
       substrate
      </a>
      that is composed of fine-grained compute nodes distributed throughout space which communicate using only nearest neighbor interactions. In this context, programmable matter refers to compute models similar to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellular_automata" title="Cellular automata">
       cellular automata
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_gas_automata" title="Lattice gas automata">
       lattice gas automata
      </a>
      (
      <a href="#CITEREFRothmanZaleski1997">
       Rothman &amp; Zaleski 1997
      </a>
      ). The CAM-8 architecture is an example hardware realization of this model.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This function is also known as "digital referenced areas" (DRA) in some forms of
      <a href="/wiki/Self-replicating_machine" title="Self-replicating machine">
       self-replicating machine
      </a>
      science.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dra_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dra-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early 1990s, there was a significant amount of work in reconfigurable modular robotics with a philosophy similar to programmable matter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dra_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-dra-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As
      <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">
       semiconductor
      </a>
      technology,
      <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">
       nanotechnology
      </a>
      , and self-replicating machine technology have advanced, the use of the term programmable matter has changed to reflect the fact that it is possible to build an ensemble of elements which can be "programmed" to change their physical properties in reality, not just in
      <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">
       simulation
      </a>
      . Thus, programmable matter has come to mean "any bulk substance which can be programmed to change its physical properties."
     </p>
     <p>
      In the summer of 1998, in a discussion on artificial atoms and programmable matter,
      <a href="/wiki/Wil_McCarthy" title="Wil McCarthy">
       Wil McCarthy
      </a>
      and G. Snyder coined the term "quantum wellstone" (or simply "wellstone") to describe this hypothetical but plausible form of programmable matter. McCarthy has used the term in his fiction.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2002, Seth Goldstein and Todd Mowry started the
      <a href="/wiki/Claytronics" title="Claytronics">
       claytronics
      </a>
      project at
      <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">
       Carnegie Mellon University
      </a>
      to investigate the underlying hardware and software mechanisms necessary to realize programmable matter.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004, the
      <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">
       DARPA
      </a>
      Information Science and Technology group (ISAT) examined the potential of programmable matter. This resulted in the 20052006 study "Realizing Programmable Matter", which laid out a multi-year program for the research and development of programmable matter.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2007, programmable matter was the subject of a DARPA research solicitation and subsequent program.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches_to_programmable_matter">
       Approaches to programmable matter
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>

     <p>
      In one school of thought the programming could be external to the material and might be achieved by the "application of light, voltage, electric or magnetic fields, etc." (
      <a href="#CITEREFMcCarthy2006">
       McCarthy 2006
      </a>
      ). For example, a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">
       liquid crystal display
      </a>
      is a form of programmable matter. A second school of thought is that the individual units of the ensemble can compute and the result of their computation is a change in the ensemble's physical properties. An example of this more ambitious form of programmable matter is
      <a href="/wiki/Claytronics" title="Claytronics">
       claytronics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      There are many proposed implementations of programmable matter. Scale is one key differentiator between different forms of programmable matter. At one end of the spectrum reconfigurable modular robotics pursues a form of programmable matter where the individual units are in the centimeter size range.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dra_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-dra-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the nanoscale end of the spectrum there are a tremendous number of different bases for programmable matter, ranging from shape changing molecules
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stoddart.chem.ucla_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-stoddart.chem.ucla-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_dot" title="Quantum dot">
       quantum dots
      </a>
      . Quantum dots are in fact often referred to as artificial atoms. In the micrometer to sub-millimeter range examples include claytronics,
      <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">
       MEMS
      </a>
      -based units, cells created using
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">
       synthetic biology
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Utility_fog" title="Utility fog">
       utility fog
      </a>
      concept.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_programmable_matter">
       Examples of programmable matter
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples of programmable matter">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are many conceptions of programmable matter, and thus many discrete avenues of research using the name. Below are some specific examples of programmable matter.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id=".22Simple.22_programmable_matter">
       "Simple" programmable matter
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: "Simple" programmable matter'>
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      These include materials that can change their properties based on some input, but do not have the ability to do complex computation by themselves.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Complex_fluids">
       Complex fluids
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Complex fluids">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_fluids" title="Complex fluids">
       Complex fluids
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The physical properties of several complex fluids can be modified by applying a current or voltage, as is the case with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystals" title="Liquid crystals">

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Metamaterials">
       Metamaterials
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metamaterials">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamaterials" title="Metamaterials">
       Metamaterials
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Metamaterials are artificial
      <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">
       composites
      </a>
      that can be controlled to react in ways that do not occur in nature. One example developed by David Smith and then by John Pendry and David Schuri is of a material that can have its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_of_refraction" title="Index of refraction">
       index of refraction
      </a>
      tuned so that it can have a different index of refraction at different points in the material. If tuned properly this could result in an "invisibility cloak."
     </p>
     <p>
      A further example of programmable -mechanical- metamaterial is presented by Bergamini et al.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adaptive_phononic_crystal_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-adaptive_phononic_crystal-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Here, a pass band within the phononic bandgap is introduced, by exploiting variable stiffness of piezoelectric elements linking aluminum stubs to the aluminum plate to create a phononic crystal as in the work of Wu et al.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The piezoelectric elements are shunted to ground over synthetic inductors. Around the resonance frequency of the LC circuit formed by the piezoelectric and the inductors, the piezoelectric elements exhibit near zero stiffness, thus effectively disconnecting the stubs from the plate. This is considered an example of programmable mechanical metamaterial.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adaptive_phononic_crystal_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-adaptive_phononic_crystal-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shape-changing_molecules">
       Shape-changing molecules
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shape-changing molecules">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      An active area of research is in molecules that can change their shape, as well as other properties, in response to external stimuli. These molecules can be used individually or en masse to form new kinds of materials. For example,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Fraser_Stoddart" title="James Fraser Stoddart">
       J Fraser Stoddart
      </a>
      's group at UCLA has been developing molecules that can change their electrical properties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stoddart.chem.ucla_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-stoddart.chem.ucla-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Electropermanent_magnets">
       Electropermanent magnets
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electropermanent magnets">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Electropermanent_magnet" title="Electropermanent magnet">
       Electropermanent magnet
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      An electropermanent magnet is a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">
       magnet
      </a>
      which consists of both an
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">
       electromagnet
      </a>
      and a dual material
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnet" title="Permanent magnet">
       permanent magnet
      </a>
      , in which the
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
       magnetic field
      </a>
      produced by the electromagnet is used to change the magnetization of the permanent magnet. The permanent magnet consists of magnetically hard and soft materials, of which only the soft material can have its magnetization changed. When the magnetically soft and hard materials have opposite magnetizations the magnet has no net field, and when they are aligned the magnet displays magnetic behaviour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      They allow creating controllable permanent magnets where the magnetic effect can be maintained without requiring a continuous supply of electrical energy. For these reasons, electropermanent magnets are essential components of the research studies aiming to build programmable magnets that can give rise to self-building structures.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-:0-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Self-reconfiguring_modular_robotics">
       Self-reconfiguring modular robotics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Self-reconfiguring modular robotics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Self-reconfiguring_modular_robot" title="Self-reconfiguring modular robot">
       Self-reconfiguring modular robot
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Self-Reconfiguring Modular Robotics is a field of robotics in which a group of basic robot modules work together to dynamically form shapes and create behaviours suitable for many tasks. Like Programmable matter SRCMR aims to offer significant improvement to any kind of objects or system by introducing many new possibilities for example: 1. Most important is the incredible flexibility that comes from the ability to change the physical structure and behavior of a solution by changing the software that controls modules. 2. The ability to self-repair by automatically replacing a broken module will make SRCMR solution incredibly resilient. 3. Reducing the environmental foot print by reusing the same modules in many different solutions. Self-Reconfiguring Modular Robotics enjoys a vibrant and active research community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Claytronics">
       Claytronics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Claytronics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Claytronics" title="Claytronics">
       Claytronics
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Claytronics is an emerging field of
      <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">
       engineering
      </a>
      concerning reconfigurable
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanoscale" title="Nanoscale">
       nanoscale
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">
       robots
      </a>
      ('claytronic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">
       atoms
      </a>
      ', or
      <i>
       catoms
      </i>
      ) designed to form much larger scale
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machines" title="Machines">
       machines
      </a>
      or mechanisms. The catoms will be sub-millimeter computers that will eventually have the ability to move around, communicate with other computers, change color, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">
       electrostatically
      </a>
      connect to other catoms to form different shapes.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_wells">
       Quantum wells
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Quantum wells">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_well" title="Quantum well">
       Quantum well
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Quantum wells can hold one or more electrons. Those electrons behave like
      <a href="/wiki/Artificial_atom" title="Artificial atom">
       artificial atoms
      </a>
      which, like real atoms, can form
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent_bonds" title="Covalent bonds">
       covalent bonds
      </a>
      , but these are extremely weak. Because of their larger sizes, other properties are also widely different.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_biology">
       Synthetic biology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Programmable_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Synthetic biology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">
       Synthetic biology
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Synthetic biology is a field that aims to engineer cells with "novel biological functions." Such
      <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">
       cells
      </a>
      are usually used to create larger systems (e.g.,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofilms" title="Biofilms">
       biofilms
      </a>
      ) which can be "programmed" utilizing synthetic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_networks" title="Gene networks">
       gene networks
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/James_Collins_(bioengineer)" title="James Collins (bioengineer)">
       genetic toggle switches
      </a>
      , to change their color, shape, etc. Such bioinspired approaches to materials production has been demonstrated, using self-assembling bacterial biofilm materials that can be programmed for specific functions, such as substrate adhesion, nanoparticle templating, and protein immobilization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
