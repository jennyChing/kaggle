     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">
       Birth
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Genealogy_of_Khadijah%27s_daughters" title="Genealogy of Khadijah's daughters">
       Genealogy of Khadijah's daughters
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fatimah was born in Mecca to Khadija, the first of
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad%27s_wives" title="Muhammad's wives">
       Muhammad's wives
      </a>
      . There are differences of opinion on the exact date of her birth, but the widely accepted view is that she was born five years before the first
      <a href="/wiki/Wahy" title="Wahy">
       Quranic revelations
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chittick_1981_136_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chittick_1981_136-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      during the time of the rebuilding of the
      <a href="/wiki/Kaaba" title="Kaaba">
       Kaaba
      </a>
      in 605,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parsa_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-parsa-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FENB_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FENB-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although this does imply she was over 18 at the time of her marriage, which was unusual in
      <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">
       Arabia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Twelver Shia sources, however, state that she was born either two or five years after the first
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">
       Qur'anic
      </a>
      revelations,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordoni_1990_pp.42-45_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ordoni_1990_pp.42-45-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but that timeline would imply her mother was over fifty at the time of her birth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Fatimah had three sisters namely Zainab, Umm Kulthum and Ruqayyah and two brothers Tayyab &amp; Qasim respectively. Shias believe that Ruqayyah and Umm Kulthum who the Sunnis believe to be the other daughters of Muhammad were actually the daughters of Hala, the sister of Khadijah, who were adopted by Muhammad and Khadijah at her death. A strong reason given by the Shi'a scholars for this belief is the event of (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mubahala" title="Mubahala">
        mubahala
       </a>
      </i>
      ) mentioned in the Quran, in which there is no reference of the presence of any other female apart from Fatimah, however Sunnis accept that Muhammad had four daughters all from Khadijah.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Iranians" title="Iranians">
       Iranians
      </a>
      celebrate Fatima Zahras birth anniversary which is also designated as
      <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day" title="Mother's Day">
       Mother's Day
      </a>
      in the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:147px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Binte_Muhammad.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="108" data-file-width="145" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/20/Binte_Muhammad.jpg" width="145"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Binte_Muhammad.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Arabic calligraphy reading
        <i>
         Fatimah az-Zahra
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Shi%27a_titles_for_Fatima_Zahra" title="List of Shi'a titles for Fatima Zahra">
       List of Shi'a titles for Fatima Zahra
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fatimah is given many titles by Muslims to show their admiration of her moral and physical characteristics. The most used title is "
      <i>
       al-Zahra
      </i>
      ", meaning "the shining one", and she is commonly referred to as
      <i>
       Fatimah Zahra
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She was also known as "
      <i>
       al-Batul
      </i>
      " (the chaste and pure one) as she spent much of her time in
      <a href="/wiki/Salat" title="Salat">
       prayer
      </a>
      , reciting the Qur'an and in other acts of
      <a href="/wiki/Ibadah" title="Ibadah">
       worship
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Besides, amongst 125 famous veneration titles, she has also been honored with the title of Umm-ul-Aaima (Mother of Imams).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al-Maaref_Islamic_Net_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Al-Maaref_Islamic_Net-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Following the birth of Fatimah, she was personally nursed, especially she was brought up by her father;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      contrary to local customs where the newborn were sent to "
      <a href="/wiki/Wet_nurse" title="Wet nurse">
       wet nurses
      </a>
      " in surrounding villages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghadanfar.2C_p_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ghadanfar.2C_p-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She spent her early youth under the care of her parents in Mecca in the shadow of the tribulations suffered by her father at the hands of the
      <a href="/wiki/Quraysh" title="Quraysh">
       Quraysh
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One time, while Muhammad was performing the
      <i>
       salat
      </i>
      (prayer) in the Kaaba,
      <a href="/wiki/Amr_ibn_Hish%C4%81m" title="Amr ibn Hishm">
       Amr ibn Hishm
      </a>
      (Abu Jahl) and his men poured camel
      <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">
       placenta
      </a>
      over him. Fatimah, upon hearing the news, rushed to her father and wiped away the filth while scolding the men.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">

      <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">

      </a>

        <span class="metadata">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Allah-green.svg">
          <img alt="Portal icon" data-file-height="215" data-file-width="206" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Allah-green.svg/15px-Allah-green.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Allah-green.svg/23px-Allah-green.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Allah-green.svg/31px-Allah-green.svg.png 2x" width="15"/>

      <tr>
       <td style="text-align:right;font-size:115%;padding-top: 0.6em;">
        <div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini">
         <ul>
          <li class="nv-view">
           <a href="/wiki/Template:Shia_Islam" title="Template:Shia Islam">
            <span title="View this template">

          </li>
          <li class="nv-talk">

     <p>
      Many of Muhammad's companions asked for Fatimah's hand in marriage, including Abu Bakr and Umar. Muhammad turned them all down, saying that he was awaiting a sign of her destiny.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ali,
      <a href="/wiki/Family_tree_of_Muhammad" title="Family tree of Muhammad">
       Muhammad's cousin
      </a>
      , also had a desire to marry Fatimah. When he went to see Muhammad, he could not vocalise his intention but remained silent. Muhammad understood the reason for his being there and prompted Ali to confirm that he had come to seek Fatimah in marriage. He suggested that Ali had a shield, which if sold, would provide sufficient money to pay the bridal gift (
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mahr" title="Mahr">
        mahr
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Muhammad put forward the proposal from Ali to Fatimah, who remained silent and did not reject the proposal like the previous ones. Muhammad took this to be a sign of affirmation and consent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The actual date of the marriage is unclear, but it most likely took place in 623, the
      <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
       second year of the hijra
      </a>
      , although some sources say it was in 622. The age of Fatimah is reported to have been 9 or 19 (due to differences of opinion on the exact date of her birth i.e. 605 or 615) at the time of her marriage while Ali was between 21 and 25.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordoni_1990_pp.42-45_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ordoni_1990_pp.42-45-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Muhammad told Ali that he had been ordered by God to give his daughter Fatimah to Ali in marriage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ali_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ali-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Muhammad said to Fatimah: "I have married you to the dearest of my family to me."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ali sold his shield to raise the money needed for the wedding, as suggested by Muhammad.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uthman_ibn_Affan" title="Uthman ibn Affan">
       Uthman ibn Affan
      </a>
      , to whom the shield was sold, gave it back to Ali saying it was his wedding gift to Ali and Fatimah.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Muhammad himself performed the wedding ceremony and two of his wives,
      <a href="/wiki/Aisha" title="Aisha">
       Aisha
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umm_Salama_Hind_bint_Abi_Umayya" title="Umm Salama Hind bint Abi Umayya">
       Umm Salama
      </a>
      , prepared the wedding feast with dates, figs, sheep and other food donated by various members of the Madinan community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Hossein_Nasr" title="Hossein Nasr">
       Hossein Nasr
      </a>
      , their marriage possesses a special spiritual significance for all Muslims because it is seen as the marriage between the greatest saintly figures surrounding Muhammad.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ali_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ali-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their marriage lasted about ten years and ended when Fatimah died. Although
      <a href="/wiki/Polygyny" title="Polygyny">
       polygyny
      </a>
      is permitted by Islam, Ali did not marry another woman while Fatimah was alive.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ali_27-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ali-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_before_the_death_of_Muhammad">
       Life before the death of Muhammad
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimah_marital_life" title="Fatimah marital life">
       Fatimah marital life
      </a>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty">
       Poverty
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After her marriage to Ali, the couple led a humble life in contrast to her sisters who were all married to wealthy individuals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ali had built a house not too far from Muhammad's residence where he lived with Fatimah. However, due to Fatimah's desire to be closer to her father, a Medinan (Haritha bin al-Numan) donated his own house to them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For several years after her marriage, she did all of the work by herself. The shoulder on which she carried pitchers of water from the well was swollen and the hand with which she worked the handmill to grind corn where often covered with blisters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fatimah vouched to take care of the household work, make dough, bake bread, and clean the house; in return, Ali vouched to take care of the outside work such as gathering firewood, and bringing food.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ali worked to irrigate other peoples lands by drawing water from the wells.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their circumstances were akin to many of the Muslims at the time and only improved following the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Khaybar" title="Battle of Khaybar">
       Battle of Khaybar
      </a>
      when the produce of Khaybar was distributed among the poor. When the economic situations of the Muslims become better, Fatimah gained some maids but treated them like her family and performed the house duties with them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another reference to their simple life comes to us from the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tasbih" title="Tasbih">
        Tasbih
       </a>
       of Fatimah
      </i>
      , a divine formula that was first given to Fatimah when she asked her father for a kaneez (servant girl) in order to help her with household chores. Her father asked her if she would like a gift instead that was better than a servant and worth more than everything in the world. Upon her ready agreement, he told her to recite at the end of every prayer the Great Exaltation,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Takbir" title="Takbir">
        Allahu Akbar
       </a>
      </i>
      34 times, the Statement of Absolute Gratitude,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alhamdulillah" title="Alhamdulillah">
        Alhamdulillah
       </a>
      </i>
      33 times and the Invocation of Divine Glory,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subhan%27Allah" title="Subhan'Allah">
        Subhan'Allah
       </a>
      </i>
      33 times, totalling 100. This collective prayer is called the Tasbih of Fatima.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Married_life">
       Married life
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An indication of their special relationship is found in the fact that Ali never married another woman as long as Fatima was alive. Some Sunnis believe that Ali allegedly asked for Abu Jahl's daughter in marriage, but Muhammad did not allow him to marry her because it would upset Fatimah, although this view is rejected by the Shia Muslims and is not considered a strong hadith by many Sunni scholars. The three most popular versions of this tradition are related by al-Miswar b. Makhrama, a Companion who was about nine years old when Muhammad died.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ali however did not marry another woman during Fatima's lifetime despite the difference in opinion among some Muslims about his proposal for Abu Jahl's daughter.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Denise L. Soufi, the purpose of the narration -in which Ali allegedly asks for Abu Jahl's daughter's hand- is to denigrate Ali or to establish the Sunni orthodoxy that the fourth Caliph, i.e. Ali, is lower in rank than the earlier ones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ali was given the name of Abu Turab (the man of dust) by Muhammad. Although some sources explain this by linking it to the disputes with Fatimah where, instead of arguing with Fatimah, Ali would go and put dust on his head.,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      other sources such as Ibn Hisham and Musnad Ahmad ibn Hanbal say this name was given to Ali before marriage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Islam" title="Encyclopedia of Islam">
        Encyclopedia of Islam
       </a>
      </i>
      , Fatimah had occasional disputes with her husband and often sought the intercession of her father who showed signs of great satisfaction upon reconciling the couple's differences. Denise L. Soufi on the other hand finds such tradition as "mere a narrative device to praise Ali". For example she says: "her complaints about Ali elicit high praise from the Prophet for him. In fact, whenever Fatima has any troubles or worries in her life, it is usually praise for Ali which is used to comfort her."
     </p>
     <p>
      In her view, these traditions are kept for the purpose of the Sunni-Shia debate over the Caliphate, even though they are in contrast with what she describes the Shia picture of the marriage to be "harmonious and divinely ordained".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In contrary with what Sunni says, in Shia literature there are some speeches from Ali that deny any problem with his spouse. For instance, Ali has sworn to the God that:" I never did any act that make Fatimah angry and she never made me angry too"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shia acknowledge the saying of Muhammad, "Fatimah is a part of me and whoever offends her offends me", however the context of the reporting in reference to Ali is disputed; like Abu Muhammad Ordoni quotes in his book: "Among the many fabricated stories told against Ali was that he had asked for Abu Jahl's (the chief of infidels) daughter's hand in marriage. When this news reached Fatimah, she rushed to her father who found out the falsity of the story."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shia say this statement was used by Fatimah herself when she spoke to Abu Bakr and Umar, stating that they had both displeased her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_battlefield">
       On the battlefield
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Uhud" title="Battle of Uhud">
       Battle of Uhud
      </a>
      , Fatimah tended to the wounds of her father and husband, and took it upon herself to regularly visit the graves of all those who died in the battle and pray for them. Fatimah, along with her husband, was also called upon by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Sufyan" title="Abu Sufyan">
       Abu Sufyan
      </a>
      to intercede on his behalf with Muhammad while attempting to make amends following the violation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Hudaybiyyah" title="Treaty of Hudaybiyyah">
       Treaty of Hudaybiyyah
      </a>
      . Abu Sufyan also asked for Fatimah's protection when she went to Mecca while it was under occupation which she refused under instruction from her father.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fatimah_in_the_Qur.27an">
       Fatimah in the Qur'an
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Ahl_al-Bayt" title="Ahl al-Bayt">
       Ahl al-Bayt
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Some verses in the Qur'an are associated to Fatimah and her household by classical
      <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">
       exegetes
      </a>
      , although she is not mentioned by name. According to J. D. McAuliffe, two of the most important verses include
      <a href="/wiki/The_verse_of_purification" title="The verse of purification">
       the verse of purification
      </a>
      , which is the 33rd
      <a href="/wiki/Ayah" title="Ayah">
       ayah
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sura" title="Sura">
       sura
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Al-Ahzab" title="Al-Ahzab">
       al-Ahzab
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the 61st ayah in sura
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-i-Imran" title="Al-i-Imran">
       Al-i-Imran
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoQ_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EoQ-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the first verse, the phrase "people of the house" (
      <i>
       ahl al-bayt
      </i>
      ) is ordinarily understood to consist of Muhammad, Fatimah, her husband Ali and their two sons (
      <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Jarir_al-Tabari" title="Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari">
       al-Tabari
      </a>
      in his exegesis also mentions a tradition that interprets "people of the house" as Muhammad's wives; for
      <a href="/wiki/Abu%27l-Faraj_ibn_al-Jawzi" title="Abu'l-Faraj ibn al-Jawzi">
       Ibn al-Jawzi
      </a>
      , the order of these options is reversed).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoQ_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EoQ-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The second verse refers to an episode in which Muhammad proposed an ordeal of mutual adjuration (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mubahala" title="Mubahala">
        mubahala
       </a>
      </i>
      ) to a delegation of Christians. Fatimah, according to the "occasion for the revelation" of this verse, was among those offered by Muhammad as witnesses and guarantors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoQ_42-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EoQ-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Muslim exegesis of the Qur'anic verse
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.usc.edu/org/cmje/religious-texts/quran/verses/003-qmt.php#003.042" rel="nofollow">
       3:42
      </a>
      , links the praise of Mary, the mother of
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      , with that of Fatimah based on a quote attributed to Muhammad that lists the outstanding women of all time as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_views_on_Mary" title="Islamic views on Mary">
       Mary
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Asiya,_wife_of_the_Pharaoh" title="Asiya, wife of the Pharaoh">
       Asiya
      </a>
      (the wife of
      <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">
       Pharaoh
      </a>
      ), Khadija and Fatimah (the all Muslim commentaries insists upon the absolute superiority of Fatimah).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoQ_42-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EoQ-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_the_death_of_Muhammad">
       Life after the death of Muhammad
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Caliphate_of_Abu_Bakr">
       Caliphate of Abu Bakr
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Succession_to_Muhammad" title="Succession to Muhammad">
       Succession to Muhammad
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Umar_at_Fatimah%27s_house" title="Umar at Fatimah's house">
       Umar at Fatimah's house
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      For the few months that she survived following the death of her father, Fatimah found herself at the center of political disunity. Differing accounts of the events surrounding the commencement of the caliphate exist which were the cause of the Shia and Sunni split.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sunni_View">
       Sunni View
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to the Sunnis the majority of Muslims at the time of Muhammed's death favoured Abu Bakr as the Caliph while a portion of the population supported Fatimah's husband, Ali.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following his selection to the caliphate after a meeting in
      <a href="/wiki/Saqifah" title="Saqifah">
       Saqifah
      </a>
      , Ali (R.A.) and Zubair (R.A.) took allegiance on the hands of Abu bakar without any coercion.
     </p>
     <p>
      Umar associated with Fatima in a very respectful manner and mentioned that she was more beloved to him than his own children. When Umar left Fatima's house, both Fatima and her home were sound and intact. No harm was afflicted on either of them. Later when Ali arrived, Fatima did not complain of Umar behaving in a disrespectful manner, rather she advised him not to oppose Umar and not to conspire against the caliphate of Abu Bakr in her house in future.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Twelver_Shi.27a_view">
       Twelver Shi'a view
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Shi'a tradition portrays a differing narrative though impartial sources are either less readily available or non-existent, in contrast to the rigorously authenticated historical accounts relied upon by the substantial majority of Muslims. Shia historians hold that Umar called for Ali and his men to come out and swear allegiance to Abu Bakr. When they did not, Umar broke in, resulting in Fatimah's ribs being broken by being pressed between the door and the wall causing her to miscarry Muhsin which led to her eventual death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordoni205_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordoni205-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qurashi217_44-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-qurashi217-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another Shia version of the events says that Umar sent a force led by his slave-boy Qunfud to Fatimah's house instructing them to bring Ali to the
      <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">
       mosque
      </a>
      . Arriving at the house, Qunfud requested permission to enter, which was refused by Ali causing Qunfud to return to Abu Bakr and Umar and relate the events, who instructed them to go back and enter the house by force if necessary. Qunfud and his men returned but were this time refused permission by Fatimah which caused Qunfud to send his men back to Abu Bakr and Umar for further instructions who told them to burn the house down if necessary in order to bring Ali to them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ordoni205_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ordoni205-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qurashi217_44-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-qurashi217-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shi'as hold the beliefs that she was involved in three significant political actions. First, after the conquest of Mecca, she refused her protection to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Sufian" title="Abu Sufian">
       Abu Sufian
      </a>
      ; Second, after Muhammad's death, she defended Alis cause, fiercely opposed the election of
      <a href="/wiki/Abu_Bakr" title="Abu Bakr">
       Abu Bakr
      </a>
      , and had violent disputes with him and particularly with Umar; Third, she laid claim to the property rights of her father and challenged Abu Bakrs categorical refusal to cede them, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Fadak" title="Fadak">
       Fadak
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and a share in the produce of
      <a href="/wiki/Khaybar" title="Khaybar">
       Khaybar
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iranica_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Iranica-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">

       </a>
      </sup>
      ) and a share of Khaybar would be passed onto her as part of her inheritance. However, Abu Bakr rejected her request citing a narration where Muhammad stated that
      <a href="/wiki/Hadith_of_Muhammad%27s_inheritance" title="Hadith of Muhammad's inheritance">
       prophets do not leave behind inheritance
      </a>
      and that all their possessions become
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sadaqah" title="Sadaqah">
        sadaqah
       </a>
      </i>
      to be used for charity. Fatimah was upset at this flat refusal by Abu Bakr and did not speak to him until her death (however some Sunni sources claim she had reconciled her differences with Abu Bakr before she died).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shias contend that Fadak had been given to Fatimah by Muhammad and Abu Bakr was wrong in not allowing her to take possession of it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sunni_View_2">
       Sunni View
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunnis" title="Sunnis">
       Sunnis
      </a>
      believe that Fatimah reconciled her differences with Abu Bakr prior to her death, while
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">
       Shia
      </a>
      Muslims believe that her anger with him remained.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FENB_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FENB-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Authentic historical accounts suggest Fatima died as a result of separation from her beloved father. The scholar Shaykh Muzaffer Ozak al-Jerrahi writes:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       'After our Master had honored the world of the Hereafter, Fatima would neither eat nor drink and she forgot all laughter and joy. She had an apartment built for her in which she stayed by night and day, weeping her heart out for her beloved father."
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shi.27a_View">
       Shi'a View
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Farewell_Pilgrimage" title="The Farewell Pilgrimage">
       the Farewell Pilgrimage
      </a>
      , Muhammad summoned Fatimah and informed her that he would die soon, and also told her that she would be the next of his household to die.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOIUSC_9-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-EOIUSC-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After Mohammad's subsequent passing, Fatimah was grief-stricken and remained so until she herself died less than six months later, on 10th,
      <a href="/wiki/Jumada_al-awwal" title="Jumada al-awwal">
       Jumada al-awwal
      </a>
      (as per Fatimid).
     </p>
     <p>
      She passed the time sobbing and sighing and nothing could take away her grief. As soon as she had done her household chores and taken care of her husband, 'Ali, and their sons, Hasan and Husain, she would continue weeping: "O my beloved father! To whom have you left your Fatima?" Six months went by in this fashion till Fatima got so thin that there was nothing left of her but skin and bones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USC-MSA-BIO_10-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-USC-MSA-BIO-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shia believe that Fatima died as a result of 'injuries' sustained after her house was raided by
      <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">
       Umar ibn al-Khattab
      </a>
      who shi'a accuse of 'threatening to set fire to the house'. The door was supposedly rammed open by one of the assailants knocking Fatimah to the ground. This attack is said to have cracked her rib-cage whilst she was pregnant, causing her to miscarry. According to Shia tradition, Muhammed appeared in a dream and informed Fatimah that she would be passing away the next day. Fatimah informed her husband Ali of her impending death, and asked him not to allow the oppressors to be involved in her ceremonial prayers
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Salat_al-Janazah" title="Salat al-Janazah">
        janazah
       </a>
      </i>
      (prayer performed in congregation after the death of a Muslim) or take part in the burial.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">
       Sunni Muslims
      </a>
      , who regard Abu Bakr and Umar as revered figures, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Zaidiyyah" title="Zaidiyyah">
       Zaidiyyah
      </a>
      Shia reject this version of events.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to some sources
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2013)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , on the morning of her death, she took a bath, put on new clothes and lay down in bed. She asked for Ali and informed him that her time to die was very close. Upon hearing this news, Ali began to cry but was consoled by Fatimah who asked him to look after her two sons and for him to bury her without ceremony. It is further stated that her two sons were the first family members to learn of her death and immediately proceeded to the mosque to inform their father. Upon hearing the news, Ali fell unconscious. After recovering he followed Fatima's wishes and performed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Janazah" title="Janazah">
       janazah
      </a>
      . He buried her during the night on 13
      <a href="/wiki/Jumada_al-awwal" title="Jumada al-awwal">
       Jumada al-awwal
      </a>
      11AH (632 AD), also making three false graves to ensure her real grave could not be identified. With him were his family and a few of his close companions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After her death, Ali followed her wishes and buried her without informing the Medinan people.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Lesley_Hazleton" title="Lesley Hazleton">
       Lesley Hazleton
      </a>
      also describes Fatimah' death as follows:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       But perhaps most painful of all in those months after the loss of her third son was the ostracism she suffered ordered by Abu Bakr to force Ali into line. [...] When she knew death was close she asked Ali for a clandestine burial [...] Abu Bakr was not to be informed of her death she said. he was to be given no chance to officiate at her funeral.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         51
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Twelver Shi'a, especially
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_of_Iran" title="People of Iran">
       Iranians
      </a>
      , hold ceremonies every year for 20 days in
      <a href="/wiki/Jumada_al-awwal" title="Jumada al-awwal">
       Jumada al-awwal
      </a>
      to commemorate the anniversary of the martyrdom of Fatimah. Mourners march in procession through the streets to reaffirm their allegiance to the ideals of Fatima.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Views">
       Views
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Muslims regard Fatimah as a loving and devoted daughter, mother, wife, a sincere Muslim, and an exemplar for women.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bukhari4-56-819_61-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-bukhari4-56-819-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordoni_1990_p_62-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ordoni_1990_p-62">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        61
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is believed that she was very close to her father and her distinction from other women is mentioned in many
      <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">
       hadith
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        62
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After Khadijah, Muslims regard Fatimah as the most significant historical figure, considered to be the leader (Arabic:
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">
        Sayyidih
       </a>
      </i>
      ) of all women in this world and in
      <a href="/wiki/Jannah" title="Jannah">
       Paradise
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bukhari4-56-819_61-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bukhari4-56-819-61">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        63
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        64
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is because of her moral purity that she occupies an analogous position in
      <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
       Islam
      </a>
      to that
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">
       Mary
      </a>
      occupies in Catholic
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      . She was the first wife of Ali, whom Sunnis consider the fourth
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rashidun" title="Rashidun">
        Rashidun
       </a>
      </i>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">
       caliph
      </a>
      and Shias consider the first infallible
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imamah_(Shi%27a_doctrine)" title="Imamah (Shi'a doctrine)">
       Imamah
      </a>
      , the mother of the second and third
      <a href="/wiki/Imam" title="Imam">
       Imams
      </a>
      , and the ancestor of all the succeeding Imams; indeed, the
      <a href="/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate" title="Fatimid Caliphate">
       Fatimid Caliphate
      </a>
      is named after her.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shia_view">
       Shia view
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shi%27a_view_of_Fatimah" title="Shi'a view of Fatimah">
       Shi'a view of Fatimah
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/The_Fourteen_Infallibles" title="The Fourteen Infallibles">
       The Fourteen Infallibles
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">
       Fatimid
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fatimah, regarded as "the Mother of the Imams", plays a special role in Shia piety. She has a unique status as Muhammad's only surviving child, the wife of Ali, their first Imam, and the mother of Hasan and Husayn. The chapter of Quran 'Abundance' mentions significance of her birth and recognizes her as the only surviving child of Muhammad.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">
       Fatimid
      </a>
      Caliphate/ Imamate is based on her name. Fatimid faith continue further in Ismaili/Bohras (refer Tree on right). She is believed to have been
      <a href="/wiki/Infallibility#Additional_Shi.27a_teachings" title="Infallibility">
       immaculate
      </a>
      , sinless and a pattern for Muslim women. Although leading a life of poverty, the Shia tradition emphasizes her compassion and sharing of whatever she had with others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shias greatly respect and adore her sublime personality. In fact her character outshines as one of the most brave and courageous personality in Islamic history. Fatimah stood as the lone defender of Muhammad's declaration of Ghadeer. She forwarded her arguments to prove Fadak as her right and undisputed property amongst those who had killed her unborn child Mohsin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Mahmoud Ayoub, the two main images of Fatimah within the Shia tradition is that of "Eternal Weeper" and "the Judge in the hereafter".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Shia tradition, the suffering and death of Fatimah was the first tragedy of Islam. She spent her last days mourning at the death of her father. Fatimah eternally weeps at the death of her two sons, who were murdered by the
      <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">
       Umayyads
      </a>
      . Shias believe they share in Fatimah's suffering by weeping for her sorrows. The tears of the faithful is also believed to console Fatimah.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Shias hold that Fatimah will play a redemptive role as the mistress of the day of judgment in the hereafter as a reward for her suffering in this world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
