                <span class="hs_cos_wrapper hs_cos_wrapper_meta_field hs_cos_wrapper_type_rich_text" data-hs-cos-general-type="meta_field" data-hs-cos-type="rich_text" id="hs_cos_wrapper_post_body" style="">
                 <div class="hs-migrated-cms-post">
                  <p>
                   The Dharmesh Test isa quick, simple set of yes/no questions togauge thelikelihood of  long-term successof a SaaS (Software as a Service) company. It is  inspiredby the highly popular 
                   <a href="http://www.joelonsoftware.com/articles/fog0000000043.html">
                    Joel Test
                   </a>
                     for measuring software development teams. [Disclosure: I am an angel  investorinJoels company
                   <a href="http://stackoverflow.com" target="_self" title="Stack Overflow">
                    Stack Overflow
                   </a>
                   ]
                  </p>
                  <!--more-->
                  <p>
                   <img alt="OnStartups YesNo" border="0" class="alignRight" src="//cdn2.hubspot.net/hub/150/file-13149536-jpg/images/yesno.jpg?t=1435784204223" style="float: right;"/>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Now, if you know me, and you know Joel, you know that I'm no Joel. He's smarter and is a better writer. So, why did I have the audacity to write the "Dharmesh Test"? Because I'm passionate about SaaS companies and have spent the better part of the last 6 years
                   <em>
                    studying
                   </em>
                   them and learning from folks that
                   <em>
                    should
                   </em>
                   have written this article. And, I've taken what I've learned and applied it to my own company,
                   <a href="http://www.HubSpot.com" target="_self" title="HubSpot">
                    HubSpot
                   </a>
                   -- which is doing very well and scores a respectable 13/16 on the test (yes, I've got work to do).
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This is alpha Version 0.80 of this test. Will be iterating on it based on community feedback.
                  </p>
                  <h2>
                   The Dharmesh Test: 16 Questions For Better SaaS Companies
                  </h2>
                  <strong>
                   1. Is there
                   <em>
                    exactly
                   </em>
                   one version of your software that  services all users?
                  </strong>
                  <p>
                   Or stated differently, have you resisted the temptation to have custom code  for an individual customer or small group of customers? Ideally, in steady  state, the same master code-base would be servicing
                   <em>
                    all
                   </em>
                   customers. Any  customizations are done through application configuration or via APIs and  plugins. If you have different versions running temporarily to test new  versions with asubset of customers, thats fine.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    2. Do you have a free trial?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Not only does a free trial increase your likelihood of getting more customers, it  often improves the simplicity of the product. Many companies
                   <em>
                    cant
                   </em>
                   have free trials because the product is just too complicated and requires too  much human assistance to really try it. That's a bad thing. Freemium (where you have a free version of your product that's feature-limited, not time-limited) counts too.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    3. Do you track key metrics like CAC, LTV and cancellation  rate?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   CAC = Customer Acquisition Cost. This is the total sales expense and  marketing expense (salaries, programs, etc.) over a specific period (lets say a  month) divided by the number of customers acquired in that month.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Cancellation Rate / Churn Rate: A percentage calculated as the number of  customers that cancel in a month divided by the number of customer at the  beginning of the month.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   LTV = Lifetime Value of a customer. Based on how long the average customer  is expected to stay (a function of cancellation rate) and the average  revenue.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   The reason these numbers are important is simple: They are the key  metrics that will help you drive a SaaS company. It answers questions around how  much capital you need, how much you should be investing in product, whether  customers are generally happy (or not), etc.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Example:
                   <a href="http://kissmetrics.com" target="_self" title="KissMetrics">
                    KissMetrics
                   </a>
                   gets this stuff (because that's what they do. And, if you're not yet reading David Skok's
                   <a href="http://forentrepreneurs.com" target="_self" title="For Entrepreneurs">
                    For Entrepreneurs
                   </a>
                   blog, you should be. Brilliant material on this topic.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    4. Are your prices published on your website?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   It is decidedly sub-optimal if people have to get a custom quote or talk to a  human just to know what your price is. Increasingly, the market values  simplicity and transparency. If youre not providing easy access to information,  potential customers will go elsewhere.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    5. Canpeople start paying you (become a customer)  withoutinteracting with a human?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   The way of the world is frictionless web sales. There's nothing quite like the joy of having your revenues go up at 4:00 a.m. in the morning on a Saturday -- without anyone having to talk to anyone.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    6. Do you make more money fromusers that are getting more  value?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Is there some variable component to your pricing? Do you charge based on number of users, storage, frequency of use or some other metric? If  you have the ability to make
                   <em>
                    more revenue
                   </em>
                   from customers over time  (without having to upsell them), thats high margin business and very  desirable. By the way, the ideal state is when there are "in product" upgrade paths. Customer reaches a certain limit and "upgrades" (starts paying you more) without you having to do anything.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    7. Does your application have a simple, clean API (Application Programming  Interface)?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   The web is not just about connecting static websites  its now about  connecting applications. Without an API, your application is an island. Its  unlikely that you will be able to meet
                   <em>
                    all
                   </em>
                   the needs of your  customers. An API allows you to integrate with third-parties (and they to you)  to deliver a more complete solution for the customer. These kinds of integrations are also great ways to spread the word about your product (the more people that integrate with you, the more reach you have).
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    8. Can customers export their data out of your system?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   To get more people to use/buy your product, you need to reduce not just the barrier to entry, but also the barrier to  exit. The
                   <em>
                    product
                   </em>
                   should be what keeps customers to stay with you   not data lock-in. If you try to use data lock-in, fewer people will pick you in  the first place.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    9. Can you deploy (and rollback) a new version of the software in one-step  without downtime?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This is to measure the effectiveness of your technical operations and  software release tooling. Companies that have easy ways to deploy/rollback software tend to have more confidence and release more often. Companies that release often tend to do better than those that don't.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    10. Is there an online community where users can interact with your  team
                    <em>
                     and
                    </em>
                    with other users?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Ideally
                   <strong>
                   </strong>
                   , everyone in your company should be interacting with customers. Especially developers. And, customers should be able to talk about you (even if it's negative) online and interact with other customers. [Note: I'm not talking here about "automatic" online venues that form -- but something that
                   <em>
                    you
                   </em>
                   are actively fostering and encouraging]
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    11. Do new developers commit code on their first day at  work?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This is partly to measure how difficult your development environment is to  setup and what controls you have in place. If it takes 4 days for a developer to get their development environment setup, something's wrong. If youre too scared to have a  rookie commit code to production, because it might bring the entire system down, something's wrong (you havent built a strong enough safety net yet). It shouldnt be that easy to  break your system. [Nod to
                   <a href="http://startuplessonslearned.com" target="_self" title="Eric Ries">
                    Eric Ries
                   </a>
                   for this one].
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Example: Cheezburger Network doesn't show new employees where the bathroom is
                   <a href="http://www.scottporad.com/2010/11/01/cheezburger-network-doesnt-show-its-new-employees-the-bathroom-until-theyve-checked-in-code/" target="_self" title="until they've committed code">
                    until they've committed code
                   </a>
                   .
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    12. Do you release code to production at least once a  month?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This is to check whether or not youre agile. The challenge these days is  that
                   <em>
                    everyone
                   </em>
                   thinks theyre agile, so asking "are you agile" is ineffective. The proof is in the pudding. Real  agile teams release code  frequently. Everything else is secondary.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    13. Do you maintain a centralized backlog of new product ideas, bugs  and issues?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Ideally, not only would you have a central database of ideas, but you'd provide the ability for customers to submit ideas and
                   <em>
                    vote
                   </em>
                   on other people's ideas.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    14. Do you regularly run A/B (split) tests?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Great SaaS companies are great at testing and deciding with data. You should be running regular tests (as in several times a month). Example tests: Should we remove this feature (does anyone care)? Should we increase our price to $75?
                  </p>
                  Example: A company that does this really well is
                  <a href="http://freshbooks.com" target="_self" title="FreshBooks">
                   FreshBooks
                  </a>
                  . If you run into Mike McDerment, ask him about how they built their product to facilitate testing. (He probably won't tell you, but doesn't hurt to try). I also think
                  <a href="http://seomoz.org" target="_self" title="SEOmoz">
                   SEOmoz
                  </a>
                  (hi Rand!) does a great job at this.
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    15. Do you invest in design and user experience?
                    <br/>
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Customer expectations (even for B2B companies) are high on the web. Having crappy design
                   <em>
                    might
                   </em>
                   be OK if you happen to have a product that people can't help but use -- but most of us are not in such forgiving circumstances. Great design not only helps you attract customers, it helps you keep them -- and get referrals.
                   <em>
                    Profitable
                   </em>
                   design is when you spend effort in design that is centered around your business objectives.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Examples: I like Campaign Monitor a lot. Heck, even their
                   <a href="http://www.campaignmonitor.com/api/" target="_self" title="API documentation">
                    API documentation
                   </a>
                   site is beautiful. But there are other examples like the ever-popular
                   <a href="http://37signals.com" target="_self" title="37signals">
                    37signals
                   </a>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <strong>
                    16. Does your company or product have a personality?
                   </strong>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   This is the squishiest of the questions -- but it's important. Great software companies today have a personality and a
                   <em>
                    voice
                   </em>
                   . They're distinctive and remarkable. Whether it's through their blog, through the copywriting in the product (like error messages), or through in-person interactions, some companies just have personality.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Examples: Take a look at what
                   <a href="http://wufoo.com" target="_self" title="Wufoo">
                    Wufoo
                   </a>
                   or
                   <a href="http://zendesk.com" target="_self" title="ZenDesk">
                    ZenDesk
                   </a>
                   does. Quirky? Yes. Effective? Absolutely.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   ----------
                  </p>
                  So, how did you do? Any questions you think are unimportant and should have been left out? Any you'd add?
                 </div>
