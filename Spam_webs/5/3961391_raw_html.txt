           <h2 class="title">
            <a href="http://blog.lifeextension.com/2015/05/the-microbes-that-make-us.html">
             The Microbes That Make Us
            </a>
           </h2>
           <div class="post-header-line-1">
           </div>
           <div class="entry">
            <p>
             <meta content=" When you think of bacteria, images of swarming, filth-loving microorganisms probably come to mind, ..." name="description"/>
            </p>
            <div style="overflow: hidden;">
             <div style="float:left">
              <g:plusone href="http://blog.lifeextension.com/2015/05/the-microbes-that-make-us.html" size="medium">
              </g:plusone>
             </div>

             </div>
             <div style="float: left;font-size:0;margin-left:20px;">
              <a href="http://www.facebook.com/sharer.php" name="fb_share" share_url="http://blog.lifeextension.com/2015/05/the-microbes-that-make-us.html" type="button_count">

            </div>
            <br/>
            <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
             <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-8JKmlrD_tew/VVtXGc9f8BI/AAAAAAAAoiM/tQ3zw4PkviQ/s1600/eyes-virus-final.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
              <img border="0" height="167" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-8JKmlrD_tew/VVtXGc9f8BI/AAAAAAAAoiM/tQ3zw4PkviQ/s1600/eyes-virus-final.jpg" width="320"/>
             </a>
            </div>
            When you think of bacteria, images of swarming, filth-loving microorganisms probably come to mind, accompanied by thoughts of tuberculosis, pneumonia, plague, and other deadly diseases. But in actuality, its a lot more complicated than that.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Yes, it's wise to protect yourself from potentially lethal microbes, either by boosting your immune system or by avoidance.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            However, if you're concerned about the presence of bacteria that surround you, you might be surprised to know that your own body has about
            <i>
             ten times
            </i>
            more microbial cells than human cells, which may prompt the question of exactly "who" we are.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <h3>
             Humans Have 10 Times More Microbes Than Human Cells
            </h3>
            The human body can be compared to a city that contains ten thousand or more different living species. The roles played by these life forms, both good and bad, are of growing interest to researchers and laypersons alike.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            These bacteria, which number in the trillions, coexist in relatively peaceful communities on planet Human. The National Institutes of Health Common Fund Human Microbiome Project is sequencing the genes of these diverse microorganisms. The mission of the project is to analyze the role microbes have on human health and disease.
            <sup>
             2
            </sup>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <h3>
             Why Are Certain Bacteria Good for You?
            </h3>
            Bacteria play important roles in the human body. Gut microbiota ensure healthy digestion and prevent malnutrition. The composition of gut bacteria may also play a role in
            <a href="http://www.lifeextension.com//Magazine/2013/SS/Surprising-Effects-of-Probiotics-Outside-the-Intestinal-Tract/Page-01" target="_blank">
             obesity
            </a>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            In a study examining sewage from 71 U.S. cities, it was discovered that bacterial patterns were able to predict if a population was lean or obese with up to 89% accuracy.
            <sup>
             3
            </sup>
            Beneficial bacteria release hormones which help to control appetite and modulate levels of inflammation.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            The cardiovascular system can also benefit from probiotics. The bacterial strain,
            <i>
             Lactobacillus reuteri
            </i>
            30242, for example, has been demonstrated to
            <a href="http://www.lifeextension.com//Magazine/2014/10/Probiotic-Targets-Cardiovascular-Disease/Page-01" target="_blank">
             lower cholesterol
            </a>
            .
            <sup>
             4,5
            </sup>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Other research involving this strain shows it may help to increase vitamin D blood levels.
            <sup>
             6
            </sup>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <h3>
             The Problem with Antibiotics
            </h3>
            Although antibiotics have contributed significantly to modern medicine, the price of that progress has been the development of "superbugs" such as MRSA that have evolved to resist the strongest antibiotic therapies.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Even without this threat, the extent of the damage to the human microbiome incurred by needless antibiotic prescriptions is only now beginning to be understood.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            It has long been observed that women treated with antibiotics often develop yeast infections. This overgrowth of
            <i>
             Candida albicans
            </i>
            , which is normally found in the vagina and digestive tract, is caused by the destruction of protective lactobacilli that keep
            <i>
             Candida
            </i>
            in check
            <sup>
             7
            </sup>
            .
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Antibiotics that target a broad group of bacteria frequently result in the death of so-called "good" bacteria. This is why women who experience
            <a href="http://www.lifeextension.com/Protocols/Infections/Fungal-Infections-Candida/Page-01" target="_blank">
             candidiasis
            </a>
            are recommended to consume yogurt or encapsulated probiotics that contain
            <i>
             Lactobacillus acidophilus
            </i>
            , bifidobacteria, and/or other strains to restore the vagina's healthy flora.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <h3>
             Is Yogurt Really a Good Source of Probiotics?
            </h3>
            Although yogurt may contain live bacteria
            <i>
             ,
            </i>
            those sold in the U.S. do not always contain organisms that are actually
            <i>
             still alive
            </i>
            . However, research shows that it may be the bacteria's DNA that is protective.
            <sup>
             8
            </sup>
            <br/>
            <br/>
            Nevertheless, the highly sweetened yogurt commonly seen on supermarket shelves is a less than optimal way to obtain health-promoting bacteria. For this reason, supplementing with a probiotic is typically much more reliable.
            <br/>
