     <h1 class="row col-sm-12 col-md-10 article-title mt-30 mt-xs-0">
      The Unexpected Fees to Expect in Japan
     </h1>
     <h3 class="clear text-h3 mt-xs-10 mb-50 mb-xs-20 text-color2 text-uppercase">
      By
      <a class="h2 text-color2" href="http://blog.gaijinpot.com/author/wandertokyo/" rel="author">
       Lynda Deaver
      </a>
     </h3>
    </div>
    <img alt="fees" class="full-width wp-post-image" height="767" src="http://cdn.gaijinpot.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2014/07/fees.jpg" width="1200"/>
    <div class="h4 article-container text-color2 mt-50 mt-xs-20 mb-30">
     July 11, 2014
     <div class="pull-right">
      <a class="gpjs-social-share social facebook" href="https://www.facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?s=100&amp;p[url]=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.gaijinpot.com%2Ffees-in-japan%2F&amp;p[images][0]=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.gaijinpot.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2Fsites%2F4%2F2014%2F07%2Ffees-300x191.jpg&amp;p[title]=The+Unexpected+Fees+to+Expect+in+Japan&amp;p[summary]=Japan+is+the+land+of+the+rising+fee%21+Everywhere+you+look+you+will+be+faced+with+open+and+hidden+fees.+" target="_blank">
       Facebook
      </a>
      <a class="gpjs-social-share social twitter" href="https://twitter.com/share?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.gaijinpot.com%2Ffees-in-japan%2F&amp;text=The Unexpected Fees to Expect in Japan&amp;hashtags=GaijinPotBlog&amp;related=GaijinPot&amp;via=GaijinPot" target="_blank">

     </div>
    </div>
    <div class="article have-first-letter">
     <p>
      Japan is a country full of interesting culture, great food, and
      <strong>
       lots and lots of fees
      </strong>
      . Some expenses, such as apartment gift money (, reikin), are well known, but there are many others that will creep up on unsuspecting new residence to Japan.
     </p>
     <p>
      If you are thinking of moving to Japan, prepare yourself by reading about a few of the lesser-known fees below.
     </p>
     <h3>
      National Pension
      <br/>
      (, kokumin nenkin hokenry)
     </h3>
     <p>
      Most know about the Japanese health insurance system, but many people moving to Japan dont realize that paying for national pension is an obligation as well. As much of a hassle as it is, if National Health Insurance and National Pension arent automatically taken from your paycheck, youll have to sign up at city hall.
     </p>
     <p>
      The good news is that many countries have a pension exchange treaty with Japan, which means that you might see the money again even if you dont live in Japan until retirement. You can also choose to withdraw a lump-sum upon leaving Japan if you prefer.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Pension: 15,250 yen/month (in 2014)
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Reducing pension payments:
      </strong>
      One of the best ways to cut down on pension payments is to pay in advance via bank transfer (savings of 14,800 yen if paying 2 years at once). If you are having financial troubles, you can also request a delay or reduction in payment, depending on your situation.
     </p>
     <p>
      More info:
      <br/>
      <a href="http://www.nenkin.go.jp/n/www/english/detail.jsp?id=37">
       Japanese International Social Security Agreements
      </a>
      <br/>
      <a href="http://www.nenkin.go.jp/n/www/english/result.jsp?genre=12">
       Japanese Pension Lump-sum Withdrawal
      </a>
     </p>
     <h3>
      Japan Broadcast Corporation (NHK)
      <br/>
      (NHK, NHK jushinry)
     </h3>
     <p>
      Paying the national television station fee is another obligation that you might not know about until the NHK fee collector comes to your door. Like many Japanese and non-Japanese residents, you can argue that you dont watch NHK, but if you have television equipment that is capable of receiving NHK, then you are technically supposed to pay the fee.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       NHK fee: 2,520 yen/every 2 months
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Reducing the NHK fee:
      </strong>
      The NHK fee is most expensive for those with satellite television (4,460 yen/every two months). You can bring down your NHK fee by only receiving terrestrial broadcasting (2,520 yen/every 2 months). Also, be sure to pay by bank or credit card transfer to get the lowest rate (2,520 yen/every 2 months rather than 2,620 yen/every 2 months). One last trick is to pay your NHK fee upfront for one year (13,990 yen/1 year, saving 1,130 yen for the year).
     </p>
     <h3>
      Bureaucratic Fees
     </h3>
     <p>
      You probably realize that extending your period of stay will cost a fee (currently 4000 yen). In addition to this fee, you may have to pay from 300 yen to 500 yen per copy to receive official documents, such as your certificate of residence (, juminhyo) and tax certificate (, zeishomeisho).
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Reducing bureaucratic fees:
      </strong>
      The main ways to reduce these fees is to avoid procedures that require the fees in the first place. Of course, this suggestion isnt always feasible, so your best bet is probably to reduce incidental fees by going in person to get the documents (and avoid paying for the SASE) and going to offices close to you (to reduce train fare).
     </p>
     <h3>
      Apartment Initial Fees
     </h3>
     <p>
      The deposit (, shikikin) and gift money (, reikin) given to the apartment owner upon moving in are well known, but did you know that another half dozen fees exist?
     </p>
     <p>
      To move into your apartment you will be hit with:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       agency fee for the realtor (, chkai tesury)
      </li>
      <li>
       damage insurance (, sonpo hokenry)
      </li>
      <li>
       key exchange fee (, kagi kkandai)
      </li>
      <li>
       guarantor company fee (, hosh gaisha riyry)
      </li>
      <li>
       cleaning fee (, kurningu hiy)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Whether youll have to pay these fees depends on the apartment owner, but preparing a decent amount of money for moving in is a good idea.
     </p>
     <p>
      To find out more about renting an apartment in Tokyo, check out the
      <a href="http://blog.gaijinpot.com/gpod-12-rent-apartment-tokyo/">
       GaijinPot Podcast #12
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Reducing apartment fees:
      </strong>
      Naturally, one of the best ways to reduce these upfront fees is by choosing a place that doesnt charge the fees in the first place. Guest houses and student dormitories dont tend to charge as many fees, but its also worth visiting a realtor who specializes in apartments with low upfront fees.
     </p>
     <p>
      This list is far from exhaustive but hopefully can give you a head start on saving for coming to Japan.
     </p>
     <p>
      Have you been hit with any unexpected fees in Japan?
     </p>

             </p>
