     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       TESS
      </b>
      ) is a planned
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">
       space telescope
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
       NASA
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Explorers_program" title="Explorers program">
       Explorers program
      </a>
      , designed to search for
      <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">
       exoplanets
      </a>
      using the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_method" title="Transit method">
       transit method
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The primary mission objective for TESS is to survey the brightest stars near the Earth for transiting exoplanets over a two-year period. The TESS project will use an array of wide-field cameras to perform an all-sky survey. It will scan nearby stars for exoplanets. With TESS, it will be possible to study the mass, size, density and orbit of a large cohort of small planets, including a sample of rocky worlds in the habitable zones of their host stars. TESS will provide prime targets for further characterization by the
      <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">
       James Webb Space Telescope
      </a>
      , as well as other large ground-based and space-based telescopes of the future.
     </p>
     <p>
      Previous sky surveys with ground-based telescopes have mainly picked out giant exoplanets. In contrast, TESS will examine a large number of small planets around the very brightest stars in the sky. TESS will record the nearest and brightest main sequence stars hosting transiting exoplanets, which will forever be the most favorable targets for detailed investigations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-2014_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NASA-2014-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Led by the
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
       Massachusetts Institute of Technology
      </a>
      with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_funding" title="Seed funding">
       seed funding
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">
       Google
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chandler-2008_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-chandler-2008-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      TESS was one of 11 proposals selected for NASA funding in September 2011, down from the original 42 submitted in February of that year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-study_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-study-4">
        <span>

      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Neutron_Star_Interior_Composition_Explorer" title="Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer">
       Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer
      </a>
      (NICER), had been selected for launch in 2017.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_overview">
       Mission overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      TESS is designed to carry out the first space-borne
      <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_survey" title="Astronomical survey">
       all-sky
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transiting_exoplanet" title="Transiting exoplanet">
       transiting exoplanet
      </a>
      survey.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selection_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-selection-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ricker2014_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Ricker2014-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is equipped with four wide-angle telescopes and associated
      <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
       charge-coupled device
      </a>
      (CCD) detectors. Science data will be transmitted to Earth every two weeks. Full-frame images with an effective exposure time of two hours will be transmitted as well, enabling scientists to search for unexpected, transient phenomena, such as the optical counterparts to
      <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">
       gamma-ray bursts
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_dynamics">
       Orbital dynamics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbital dynamics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In order to obtain unobstructed imagery of both the
      <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
       northern and southern hemispheres
      </a>
      of the sky, TESS will utilize a 2:1
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       lunar
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
       resonant orbit
      </a>
      called P/2, a never-before-used orbit. The spacecraft's 373,000km (232,000mi)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
       apogee
      </a>
      is timed to keep the craft away from the Moon, which acts as a
      <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
       destabilizing agent
      </a>
      . This highly elliptical orbit should remain stable for decades, and will keep TESS's cameras in a stable temperature range. The majority of the orbit is spent outside the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_belts" title="Van Allen belts">
       Van Allen belts
      </a>
      to avoid radiation damage to TESS. Every 13.7 days at its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
       perigee
      </a>
      of 108,000km (67,000mi), TESS will
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downlink" title="Downlink">
       downlink
      </a>
      the data it has collected during the orbit to Earth over a period of approximately three hours.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_objectives">
       Scientific objectives
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific objectives">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      TESS's survey will focus on
      <a href="/wiki/G-type_main-sequence_star" title="G-type main-sequence star">
       G
      </a>
      - and
      <a href="/wiki/K-type_main-sequence_star" title="K-type main-sequence star">
       K
      </a>
      -
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_type" title="Spectral type">
       type
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
       stars
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
       apparent magnitudes
      </a>
      brighter than magnitude 12.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seager-2011_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-seager-2011-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Approximately 500,000 stars will be studied, including the 1,000 closest
      <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">
       red dwarfs
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skytel20130530_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-skytel20130530-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      across an area of sky 400 times larger than that covered by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">
        Kepler
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tess-nasa-faq_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tess-nasa-faq-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      TESS is expected to discover more than 3,000 transiting exoplanet candidates, including those which are
      <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
       Earth sized
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Super-Earth" title="Super-Earth">
       larger
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tess-nasa-faq_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tess-nasa-faq-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The survey is broken up into 26 observation sectors, each sector being
      <span class="nowrap">
       24 x 96
      </span>
      , with an overlap of sectors at the ecliptic poles to allow for additional sensitivity toward smaller and longer-period exoplanets in that region of the celestial sphere. The spacecraft will spend two 13.7 day orbits observing each sector, mapping the northern hemisphere of sky in its first year of operation and the southern hemisphere in its second year. Each of TESS's cameras will take one-minute exposures focused on specifically targeted stars as part of its transit search, and 30-minute full-frame exposures to search for other transient events.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tess-nasa-faq_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-tess-nasa-faq-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Exoplanet candidates could later be investigated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Automated_Planet_Finder" title="Automated Planet Finder">
       Automated Planet Finder
      </a>
      telescope, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HARPS" title="HARPS">
       HARPS
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
       spectrometer
      </a>
      and both the future
      <a href="/wiki/ESPRESSO" title="ESPRESSO">
       ESPRESSO
      </a>
      spectrometer and
      <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">
       James Webb Space Telescope
      </a>
      . The development team at MIT has suggested that the first manned
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space_mission" title="Interstellar space mission">
       interstellar space missions
      </a>
      may be to planets discovered by TESS.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gilster-2008_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gilster-2008-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">
       Spacecraft
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      TESS uses an
      <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">
       Orbital Sciences
      </a>
      LEOStar-2
      <a href="/wiki/Satellite_bus" title="Satellite bus">
       satellite bus
      </a>
      , capable of three-axis stabilization using four
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">
       hydrazine
      </a>
      thrusters plus four
      <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">
       reaction wheels
      </a>
      providing better than three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc-second" title="Arc-second">
       arc-second
      </a>
      fine spacecraft pointing control. Power is provided by two single-axis solar arrays generating 400 watts. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ka-band" title="Ka-band">
       Ka-band
      </a>
      dish antenna will provide a 100 Mbit/s science downlink.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tess-nasa-faq_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-tess-nasa-faq-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tess-orbital-faq_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tess-orbital-faq-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">
       Launch
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Launch">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      TESS will be launched in August 2017 on a
      <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">
       SpaceX
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9_v1.1" title="Falcon 9 v1.1">
       Falcon 9 v1.1
      </a>
      rocket from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">
       Cape Canaveral Air Force Station
      </a>
      . The total value of the contract is
      <span style="white-space: nowrap">
       US$87 million
      </span>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20141216_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nasa20141216-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Science_instrument">
       Science instrument
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Science instrument">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The sole instrument on TESS is a package of four wide-field-of-view CCD cameras. Each camera features a low-noise, low-power 16.8 megapixel CCD detector created by the
      <a href="/wiki/MIT_Lincoln_Laboratory" title="MIT Lincoln Laboratory">
       MIT Lincoln Laboratory
      </a>
      . Each has a
      <span class="nowrap">
       24  24
      </span>
      <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
       field of view
      </a>
      , a 100mm (4in) effective
      <a href="/wiki/Entrance_pupil" title="Entrance pupil">
       pupil diameter
      </a>
      , a lens assembly with seven optical elements, and a
      <a href="/wiki/Band-pass_filter" title="Band-pass filter">
       bandpass
      </a>
      range of
      <span class="nowrap">
       600 to 1000 nm
      </span>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tess-nasa-faq_11-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-tess-nasa-faq-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
