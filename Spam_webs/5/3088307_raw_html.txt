     <p>
      <b>
       Rahsaan Roland Kirk
      </b>
      (August 7, 1935
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
       December 5, 1977) was an American
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
       jazz
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Multi-instrumentalist" title="Multi-instrumentalist">
       multi-instrumentalist
      </a>
      who played
      <a href="/wiki/Tenor_saxophone" title="Tenor saxophone">
       tenor saxophone
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">
       flute
      </a>
      and many other instruments. He was renowned for his onstage vitality, during which virtuoso improvisation was accompanied by comic banter, political ranting, and the ability to play several instruments simultaneously.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rahsaan_Roland_Kirk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Kirk was born Ronald Theodore Kirk
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grove_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Grove-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">
       Columbus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">
       Ohio
      </a>
      , where he lived in a neighborhood known as
      <a href="/wiki/Flytown" title="Flytown">
       Flytown
      </a>
      . He felt compelled by a dream to transpose two letters in his first name to make
      <i>
       Roland.
      </i>
      He became blind at an early age as a result of poor medical treatment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1970, Kirk added "Rahsaan" to his name after hearing it in a dream.
     </p>
     <p>
      Preferring to lead his own bands, Kirk rarely performed as a sideman, although he did record with arranger
      <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">
       Quincy Jones
      </a>
      and drummer
      <a href="/wiki/Roy_Haynes" title="Roy Haynes">
       Roy Haynes
      </a>
      and had notable stints with bassist
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mingus" title="Charles Mingus">
       Charles Mingus
      </a>
      . One of his best-known recorded performances is the lead flute and solo on Jones' "
      <a href="/wiki/Soul_Bossa_Nova" title="Soul Bossa Nova">
       Soul Bossa Nova
      </a>
      ", a 1964 hit song repopularized in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Austin_Powers_(film_series)" title="Austin Powers (film series)">
        Austin Powers
       </a>
      </i>
      films (Jones 1964; McLeod et al. 1997).
     </p>
     <p>
      His playing was generally rooted in
      <a href="/wiki/Soul_jazz" title="Soul jazz">
       soul jazz
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Hard_bop" title="Hard bop">
       hard bop
      </a>
      , but Kirk's knowledge of jazz history allowed him to draw from many elements of the music's past, from
      <a href="/wiki/Ragtime" title="Ragtime">
       ragtime
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swing_(genre)" title="Swing (genre)">
       swing
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Free_jazz" title="Free jazz">
       free jazz
      </a>
      . Kirk also absorbed
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">
       classical
      </a>
      influences, and his artistry reflected elements of
      <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">
       pop music
      </a>
      by composers such as
      <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Robinson" title="Smokey Robinson">
       Smokey Robinson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">
       Burt Bacharach
      </a>
      , as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">
       Duke Ellington
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Coltrane" title="John Coltrane">
       John Coltrane
      </a>
      and other jazz musicians. The live album
      <i>
       Bright Moments
      </i>
      (1973) is an example of one of his shows. His main instrument was the tenor saxophone, supplemented by other saxes, and contrasted with the lighter sound of the flute. At times he would play a number of these horns at once, harmonizing with himself, or sustain a note for lengthy durations by using
      <a href="/wiki/Circular_breathing" title="Circular breathing">
       circular breathing
      </a>
      , or play the rare, seldom heard
      <a href="/wiki/Nose_flute" title="Nose flute">
       nose flute
      </a>
      . A number of his instruments were exotic or homemade, but even while playing two or three saxophones at once, the music was intricate, powerful jazz with a strong feel for the blues.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kirk was politically outspoken. During his concerts, between songs he often talked about topical issues, including black history and the civil rights movement. His monologues were often laced with satire and absurdist humor. According to comedian
      <a href="/wiki/Jay_Leno" title="Jay Leno">
       Jay Leno
      </a>
      , when Leno toured with Kirk as Kirk's opening act, Kirk would introduce him by saying, "I want to introduce a young brother who knows the black experience and knows all about the white devils .... Please welcome Jay Leno!"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1975, Kirk suffered a major stroke which led to partial paralysis of one side of his body. However, he continued to perform and record, modifying his instruments to enable him to play with one arm. At a live performance at
      <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Scott%27s_Jazz_Club" title="Ronnie Scott's Jazz Club">
       Ronnie Scott's Jazz Club
      </a>
      in London he even managed to play two instruments, and carried on to tour internationally and even appear on television.
     </p>

      </a>
      Student Union in
      <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Indiana" title="Bloomington, Indiana">

     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments_and_technique">
       Instruments and technique
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rahsaan_Roland_Kirk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instruments and technique">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Kirk played and collected a number of musical instruments, mainly various
      <a href="/wiki/Saxophone" title="Saxophone">
       saxophones
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">
       clarinets
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Flute" title="Flute">
       flutes
      </a>
      . His main instruments were
      <a href="/wiki/Tenor_saxophone" title="Tenor saxophone">
       tenor saxophone
      </a>
      and two obscure saxophones: the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stritch_(saxophone)" title="Stritch (saxophone)">
       stritch
      </a>
      (a straight alto sax lacking the instrument's characteristic upturned bell) and a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manzello" title="Manzello">
       manzello
      </a>
      (a modified
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxello" title="Saxello">
       saxello
      </a>
      soprano sax, with a larger, upturned bell). Kirk modified these instruments himself to accommodate his simultaneous playing technique.
     </p>
     <p>
      He typically appeared on stage with all three horns hanging around his neck, as well as a variety of other instruments, including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flutes" title="Flutes">
       flutes
      </a>
      and whistles, and often kept a gong within reach. Kirk also played
      <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">
       clarinet
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Harmonica" title="Harmonica">
       harmonica
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_horn" title="English horn">
       English horn
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Recorder_(musical_instrument)" title="Recorder (musical instrument)">
       recorders
      </a>
      , and was a competent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trumpeter" title="Trumpeter">
       trumpeter
      </a>
      . He often had unique approaches, using a saxophone mouthpiece on a trumpet or playing
      <a href="/wiki/Nose_flute" title="Nose flute">
       nose flute
      </a>
      . He additionally used many non-musical devices, such as alarm clocks, sirens, or a section of common garden hose (dubbed "the black mystery pipes"). His studio recordings also used tape-manipulated
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Musique_concr%C3%A8te" title="Musique concrte">
        musique concrte
       </a>
      </i>
      and primitive electronic sounds (before such things became commonplace).
     </p>

     <p>
      Kirk was also an influential
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flautist" title="Flautist">
       flautist
      </a>
      , employing several techniques that he developed himself. One technique was to
      <a href="/wiki/Singing" title="Singing">
       sing
      </a>
      or hum into the flute at the same time as playing. Another was to play the standard transverse flute at the same time as a
      <a href="/wiki/Nose_flute" title="Nose flute">
       nose flute
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Some
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2015)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      have suggested that Kirk's unique onstage appearance and simultaneous
      <a href="/wiki/Multi-instrumentalist" title="Multi-instrumentalist">
       multi-instrumentalism
      </a>
      were gimmicks to the point of linking these to his blindness
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="needs a reliable source (June 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      , but Kirk's playing and performance tended to silence these ideas. He used the multiple horns to play true chords, essentially functioning as a one-man saxophone section. Kirk insisted that he was only trying to emulate the sounds he heard in his head.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kirk was a major exponent of
      <a href="/wiki/Circular_breathing" title="Circular breathing">
       circular breathing
      </a>
      . Using this technique, he was not only able to sustain a single note for an extended period; he could also play sixteenth-note runs of almost unlimited length, and at high speeds. His circular breathing ability enabled him to record "Concerto For Saxophone" on the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prepare_Thyself_to_Deal_With_a_Miracle" title="Prepare Thyself to Deal With a Miracle">
        Prepare Thyself to Deal With a Miracle
       </a>
      </i>
      LP in one continuous take of about 20 minutes' playing with no discernible "break" for inhaling. His long-time producer at Atlantic Jazz,
      <a href="/wiki/Joel_Dorn" title="Joel Dorn">
       Joel Dorn
      </a>
      , believed he should have received credit in
      <i>
       The Guinness Book of World Records
      </i>
      for such feats (he was capable of playing continuously "without taking a breath" for far longer than exhibited on that LP), but this never happened.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Case_of_the_3_Sided_Dream_in_Audio_Color" title="The Case of the 3 Sided Dream in Audio Color">
        The Case of the 3 Sided Dream in Audio Color
       </a>
      </i>
      was a unique album in jazz and popular music recorded annals. It was a two-LP set, with Side 4 apparently "blank", the label not indicating any content. However, once word of "the secret message" got around among Rahsaan's fans, one would find that about 12 minutes into Side 4 appeared the first of two telephone answering machine messages recorded by Kirk, the second following soon thereafter (but separated by more blank grooves). The surprise impact of these segments appearing on "blank" Side 4 was lost on the CD reissue of this album.
     </p>
     <p>
      He gleaned information on what was happening in the world via audio media like radio and the sounds coming from TV sets. His later recordings often incorporated his spoken commentaries on current events, including
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
       Richard Nixon
      </a>
      's involvement in the
      <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">
       Watergate scandal
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       3-Sided Dream
      </i>
      album was a "concept album" which incorporated of "found" or environmental sounds and tape loops, tapes being played backwards, etc. Snippets of
      <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">
       Billie Holiday
      </a>
      singing are also heard briefly. The album even confronts the rise of influence of computers in society, as Rahsaan threatens to pull the plug on the machine trying to tell him what to do.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Other_Folks%27_Music" title="Other Folks' Music">
        Other Folks' Music
       </a>
      </i>
      the spoken words of
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Robeson" title="Paul Robeson">
       Paul Robeson
      </a>
      , another outspoken black artist, can be briefly heard.
     </p>
