     <p>
      A
      <b>
       pyroclastic flow
      </b>
      (also known scientifically as a
      <b>
       pyroclastic density current
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) is a fast-moving current of hot
      <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_gas" title="Volcanic gas">
       gas
      </a>
      and rock (collectively known as
      <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">
       tephra
      </a>
      ), which reaches speeds moving away from a volcano of up to 700km/h (450mph).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The gas can reach temperatures of about 1,000C (1,830F). Pyroclastic flows normally hug the ground and travel downhill, or spread laterally under gravity. Their speed depends upon the density of the current, the volcanic output rate, and the gradient of the slope. They are a common and devastating result of certain explosive
      <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
       volcanic eruptions
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_term">
       Origin of term
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pyroclastic_flow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of term">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       pyroclast
      </i>
      is derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%E1%BF%A6%CF%81" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      , meaning "fire", and
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CE%BB%CE%B1%CF%83%CF%84%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      , meaning "broken in pieces". A name for some pyroclastic flows is
      <i>
       <b>
        nue ardente
       </b>
      </i>
      (French for "Glowing Cloud"); this was first used to describe the disastrous 1902 eruption of
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pel%C3%A9e" title="Mount Pele">
       Mount Pele
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">
       Martinique
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the dark, these pyroclastic flows glowed red.
     </p>
     <p>
      Pyroclastic flows that contain a much higher proportion of gas to rock are known as "fully dilute pyroclastic density currents" or
      <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_surge" title="Pyroclastic surge">
       pyroclastic surges
      </a>
      . The lower density sometimes allows them to flow over higher topographic features such as ridges and hills. They may also contain steam, water and rock at less than 250C (482F); these are called "cold" compared with other flows, although the temperature is still lethally high. Cold pyroclastic surges can occur when the eruption is from a vent under a shallow lake or the sea. Fronts of some pyroclastic density currents are fully dilute; for example, during the eruption of
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pel%C3%A9e" title="Mount Pele">
       Mount Pele
      </a>
      in 1902 a fully dilute current overwhelmed the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Saint-Pierre,_Martinique" title="Saint-Pierre, Martinique">
       Saint-Pierre
      </a>
      and killed nearly 30,000 people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A pyroclastic flow is a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Gravity_current" title="Gravity current">
       gravity current
      </a>
      ; in scientific literature they are sometimes abbreviated to PDC (pyroclastic density current).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
       Causes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pyroclastic_flow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are several factors which can produce a pyroclastic flow:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        Fountain collapse
       </i>
       of an
       <a href="/wiki/Eruption_column" title="Eruption column">
        eruption column
       </a>
       from a
       <a href="/wiki/Plinian_eruption" title="Plinian eruption">
        Plinian eruption
       </a>
       (e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vesuvius" title="Mount Vesuvius">
        Mount Vesuvius
       </a>
       's destruction of
       <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">
        Pompeii
       </a>
       , see
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger" title="Pliny the Younger">
        Pliny the Younger
       </a>
       ). In such an eruption, the material ejected from the vent heats the surrounding air and the turbulent mixture rises, through
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       , for many kilometres. If the erupted jet is unable to heat the surrounding air sufficiently, convection currents will not be strong enough to carry the plume upwards and it falls, flowing down the flanks of the volcano.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Fountain collapse
       </i>
       of an
       <a href="/wiki/Eruption_column" title="Eruption column">
        eruption column
       </a>
       associated with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcanian" title="Vulcanian">
        vulcanian
       </a>
       eruption (e.g., Montserrat's
       <a href="/wiki/Soufri%C3%A8re_Hills" title="Soufrire Hills">
        Soufrire Hills
       </a>
       volcano has generated many of these deadly pyroclastic flows and surges.) The gas and projectiles create a cloud which is denser than the surrounding air and becomes a pyroclastic flow.
      </li>
      <li>
       Frothing at the mouth of the vent during degassing of the erupted lava. This can lead to the production of a rock called
       <a href="/wiki/Ignimbrite" title="Ignimbrite">
        ignimbrite
       </a>
       . This occurred during the eruption of
       <a href="/wiki/Novarupta" title="Novarupta">
        Novarupta
       </a>
       in 1912.
      </li>
      <li>
       Gravitational collapse of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lava_dome" title="Lava dome">
        lava dome
       </a>
       or spine, with subsequent avalanches and flow down a steep slope (e.g., Montserrat's
       <a href="/wiki/Soufri%C3%A8re_Hills" title="Soufrire Hills">
        Soufrire Hills
       </a>
       volcano, which caused nineteen deaths in 1997).
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_eruption" title="Lateral eruption">
        directional blast
       </a>
       (or jet) when part of a volcano explodes or collapses (e.g., the
       <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">
        May 18, 1980, eruption of Mount St. Helens
       </a>
       ). As distance from the volcano increases, this rapidly transforms into a gravity-driven current.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_effects">
       Size and effects
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pyroclastic_flow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Size and effects">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:181px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="410" data-file-width="600" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg/179px-Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg/269px-Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg/358px-Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg 2x" width="179"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pyroclastic_Flow_St._Helens.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A scientist examines pumice blocks at the edge of a pyroclastic flow deposit from
        <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">
         Mount St. Helens
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The volumes range from a few hundred cubic meters to more than a thousand cubic kilometres. The larger ones can travel for hundreds of kilometres, although none on that scale have occurred for several hundred thousand years. Most pyroclastic flows are around one to ten cubic kilometres and travel for several kilometres. Flows usually consist of two parts: the
      <i>
       basal flow
      </i>
      hugs the ground and contains larger, coarse boulders and rock fragments, while an extremely hot
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ash_plume" title="Ash plume">
       ash plume
      </a>
      lofts above it because of the turbulence between the flow and the overlying air, admixes and heats cold atmospheric air causing expansion and convection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
       kinetic energy
      </a>
      of the moving boulders will flatten trees and buildings in their path. The hot gases and high speed make them particularly lethal, as they will incinerate living organisms instantaneously:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The towns of
       <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">
        Pompeii
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Herculaneum" title="Herculaneum">
        Herculaneum
       </a>
       , Italy, for example, were engulfed by pyroclastic surges in 79 AD with many lives lost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       A pyroclastic surge killed
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanology" title="Volcanology">
        volcanologists
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Katia_and_Maurice_Krafft" title="Katia and Maurice Krafft">
        Katia and Maurice Krafft
       </a>
       and 41 other people on
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Unzen" title="Mount Unzen">
        Mount Unzen
       </a>
       , in Japan, on June 3, 1991. The surge started as a pyroclastic flow and the more energised surge climbed a spur on which the Kraffts and the others were standing; it engulfed them, and the corpses were covered with about 5mm of ash.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The 1902 eruption of
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pel%C3%A9e" title="Mount Pele">
        Mount Pele
       </a>
       destroyed the
       <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">
        Martinique
       </a>
       town of
       <a href="/wiki/Saint-Pierre,_Martinique" title="Saint-Pierre, Martinique">
        St. Pierre
       </a>
       . Despite signs of impending eruption, the government deemed St. Pierre safe due to hills and valleys between it and the volcano, but the pyroclastic flow charred almost the entirety of the city, killing all but two of its 30,000 residents.
      </li>
      <li>
       On 25 June 1997 a pyroclastic flow travelled down Mosquito Ghaut on the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       island of
       <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">
        Montserrat
       </a>
       . A large energised pyroclastic surge developed. This surge could not be restrained by the Ghaut and spilled out of it, killing 19 people who were in the Streatham village area (which was officially evacuated). Several others in the area suffered severe burns.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_with_water">
       Interaction with water
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pyroclastic_flow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interaction with water">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Testimonial evidence from the
      <a href="/wiki/1883_eruption_of_Krakatoa" title="1883 eruption of Krakatoa">
       1883 eruption of Krakatoa
      </a>
      , supported by experimental evidence,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      shows that pyroclastic flows can cross significant bodies of water. One flow reached the
      <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">
       Sumatran
      </a>
      coast as much as 48km (30mi) away.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2006 documentary film,
      <i>
       Ten Things You Didn't Know About Volcanoes
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      demonstrated tests by a research team at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiel_University" title="Kiel University">
       Kiel University
      </a>
      , Germany, of pyroclastic flows moving over water.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When the reconstructed pyroclastic flow (stream of mostly hot ash with varying densities) hit the water two things happened: The heavier material fell into the water, precipitating out from the pyroclastic flow and into the liquid; The temperature of the ash caused the water to evaporate, propelling the pyroclastic flow (now only consisting of the lighter material) along at an even faster pace than before on a bed of steam.
     </p>
     <p>
      During some phases of the Soufriere Hills volcano on Montserrat, pyroclastic flows were filmed about 1km offshore. These show the water boiling as the flow passed over it. The flows eventually built a delta which covered about 1km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A pyroclastic flow can interact with a body of water to form a large amount of mud, which can then continue to flow downhill as a
      <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">
       lahar
      </a>
      . This is one of several mechanisms that can create a lahar.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_Moon">
       On the Moon
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pyroclastic_flow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: On the Moon">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1963, NASA astronomer Winifred Cameron proposed that sinuous
      <a href="/wiki/Rille" title="Rille">
       rilles
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
       Moon
      </a>
      may have been formed by the lunar equivalent of terrestrial pyroclastic flows. In a lunar volcanic eruption, a pyroclastic cloud would follow local relief resulting in an often sinuous track. The Moon's
      <a href="/wiki/Schroter%27s_Valley" title="Schroter's Valley">
       Schroter's Valley
      </a>
      is one example.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
