     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Frankfurt School
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
       <i>
        Frankfurter Schule
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a school of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_theory" title="Social theory">
       social theory
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">
       philosophy
      </a>
      associated in part with the
      <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Social_Research" title="Institute for Social Research">
       Institute for Social Research
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Goethe_University_Frankfurt" title="Goethe University Frankfurt">
       Goethe University
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">
       Frankfurt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      . The school initially formed during the interwar period in Germany and consisted of dissidents who were at home neither in the existent capitalist, fascist, nor communist systems that had formed during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interwar" title="Interwar">
       interwar
      </a>
      period. Meanwhile, many of these theorists believed that traditional theory could not adequately explain the turbulent and unexpected development of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitalist" title="Capitalist">
       capitalist
      </a>
      societies in the twentieth century. Critical of both capitalism and
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet
      </a>
      socialism, their writings pointed to the possibility of an alternative path to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_development" title="Social development">
       social development
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although sometimes only loosely affiliated, Frankfurt School theorists spoke with a common
      <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">
       paradigm
      </a>
      in mind, thus sharing the same assumptions and being preoccupied with similar questions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To fill in the perceived omissions of traditional Marxism, they sought to draw answers from other schools of thought, hence using the insights of
      <a href="/wiki/Antipositivism" title="Antipositivism">
       antipositivist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">
       psychoanalysis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">
       existential philosophy
      </a>
      , and other disciplines.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The school's main figures sought to learn from and synthesize the works of such varied thinkers as
      <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
       Kant
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">
       Hegel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
       Marx
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">
       Freud
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">
       Weber
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Luk%C3%A1cs" title="Georg Lukcs">
       Lukcs
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following Marx, they were concerned with the conditions that allow for
      <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">
       social change
      </a>
      and the establishment of rational institutions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Held.2C_David_1980.2C_p._15_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Held.2C_David_1980.2C_p._15-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their emphasis on the
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory#In_social_theory" title="Critical theory">
       "critical" component of theory
      </a>
      was derived significantly from their attempt to overcome the limits of
      <a href="/wiki/Positivism" title="Positivism">
       positivism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">
       materialism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">
       determinism
      </a>
      by returning to Kant's
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_philosophy" title="Critical philosophy">
       critical philosophy
      </a>
      and its successors in
      <a href="/wiki/German_idealism" title="German idealism">
       German idealism
      </a>
      , principally Hegel's philosophy, with its emphasis on
      <a href="/wiki/Dialectic#Modern_philosophy" title="Dialectic">
       dialectic
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Contradiction#Dialectical_materialism" title="Contradiction">
       contradiction
      </a>
      as inherent properties of human reality.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the 1960s, Frankfurt School critical theory has increasingly been guided by
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jrgen Habermas">
       Jrgen Habermas
      </a>
      's work on
      <a href="/wiki/Communicative_rationality" title="Communicative rationality">
       communicative reason
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      linguistic
      <a href="/wiki/Intersubjectivity" title="Intersubjectivity">
       intersubjectivity
      </a>
      and what Habermas calls "the philosophical discourse of
      <a href="/wiki/Modernity#In_sociological_thought" title="Modernity">
       modernity
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Critical theorists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Geuss" title="Raymond Geuss">
       Raymond Geuss
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nikolas_Kompridis" title="Nikolas Kompridis">
       Nikolas Kompridis
      </a>
      have voiced opposition to Habermas, claiming that he has undermined the aspirations for social change that originally gave purpose to critical theory's various projectsfor example the problem of what
      <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">
       reason
      </a>
      should mean, the analysis and enlargement of "conditions of possibility" for social
      <a href="/wiki/Emancipation" title="Emancipation">
       emancipation
      </a>
      , and the critique of modern
      <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">
       capitalism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <tr>
       <td style="text-align:right;font-size:115%;padding-top: 0.6em;">
        <div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini">
         <ul>
          <li class="nv-view">
           <a href="/wiki/Template:Marxism" title="Template:Marxism">
            <span title="View this template">
             v
            </span>
           </a>
          </li>
          <li class="nv-talk">
           <a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Marxism" title="Template talk:Marxism">
            <span title="Discuss this template">
             t
            </span>
           </a>
          </li>
          <li class="nv-edit">
           <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Marxism&amp;action=edit">
            <span title="Edit this template">
             e
            </span>
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      The term "Frankfurt School" arose informally to describe the thinkers affiliated or merely associated with the Frankfurt
      <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Social_Research" title="Institute for Social Research">
       Institute for Social Research
      </a>
      ; it is not the title of any specific position or institution
      <i>
       per se
      </i>
      , and few of these theorists used the term themselves. The Institute for Social Research (
      <i>
       Institut fr Sozialforschung
      </i>
      ) was founded in 1923 by
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gr%C3%BCnberg" title="Carl Grnberg">
       Carl Grnberg
      </a>
      , a Marxist legal and political professor at the University of Vienna,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iep_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-iep-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as an adjunct of the
      <a href="/wiki/Goethe_University_Frankfurt" title="Goethe University Frankfurt">
       University of Frankfurt
      </a>
      ; it was the first Marxist-oriented research center affiliated with a major German university.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the school can trace its earliest roots back to
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_Weil" title="Felix Weil">
       Felix Weil
      </a>
      , who used money from his father's grain business to finance the
      <i>
       Institut
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Weil (1898-1975), a young Marxist, had written his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thesis_or_dissertation" title="Thesis or dissertation">
       doctoral thesis
      </a>
      (published by
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Korsch" title="Karl Korsch">
       Karl Korsch
      </a>
      ) on the practical problems of implementing socialism. With the hope of bringing different trends of Marxism together, Weil organized a week-long symposium (the
      <i>
       Erste Marxistische Arbeitswoche
      </i>
      ) in 1922, a meeting attended by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Luk%C3%A1cs" title="Georg Lukcs">
       Georg Lukcs
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Korsch" title="Karl Korsch">
       Karl Korsch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_August_Wittfogel" title="Karl August Wittfogel">
       Karl August Wittfogel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Pollock" title="Friedrich Pollock">
       Friedrich Pollock
      </a>
      and others. The event was so successful that Weil set about erecting a building and funding salaries for a permanent institute. Weil negotiated with the Ministry of Education that the Director of the Institute would be a full professor from the state system, so that the
      <i>
       Institut
      </i>
      would have the status of a University institution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marxist_Internet_Archive_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Marxist_Internet_Archive-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gyrgy Lukcs and Karl Korsch both attended the
      <i>
       Arbeitswoche
      </i>
      , which had included a study of Korsch's
      <i>
       Marxism and Philosophy
      </i>
      but both were too committed to political activity and Party membership to join the
      <i>
       Institut
      </i>
      , though Korsch participated in publishing ventures for a number of years. The way Lukcs was obliged to repudiate his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/History_and_Class_Consciousness" title="History and Class Consciousness">
        History and Class Consciousness
       </a>
      </i>
      , published in 1923 and probably a major inspiration for the work of the Frankfurt School, indicated that independence from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party" title="Communist Party">
       Communist Party
      </a>
      was necessary for genuine theoretical work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marxist_Internet_Archive_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Marxist_Internet_Archive-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The philosophical tradition now referred to as the "Frankfurt School" is perhaps particularly associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Horkheimer" title="Max Horkheimer">
       Max Horkheimer
      </a>
      (philosopher, sociologist and social psychologist), who took over as the institute's director in 1930 and recruited many of the school's most talented theorists, including
      <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno" title="Theodor W. Adorno">
       Theodor W. Adorno
      </a>
      (philosopher, sociologist, musicologist),
      <a href="/wiki/Erich_Fromm" title="Erich Fromm">
       Erich Fromm
      </a>
      (psychoanalyst), and
      <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Marcuse" title="Herbert Marcuse">
       Herbert Marcuse
      </a>
      (philosopher).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_German_prewar_context">
       The German prewar context
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The German prewar context">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The political turmoil of
      <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">
       Germany's troubled interwar years
      </a>
      greatly affected the School's development. Its thinkers were particularly influenced by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%931919" title="German Revolution of 19181919">
       failure of the working-class revolution in Western Europe
      </a>
      (precisely where Marx had predicted that a communist revolution would take place) and by the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">
       Nazism
      </a>
      in such an economically and technologically advanced nation as Germany. This led many of them to take up the task of choosing what parts of Marx's thought might serve to clarify contemporary social conditions that Marx himself had never seen. Another key influence also came from the publication in the 1930s of Marx's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_and_Philosophical_Manuscripts_of_1844" title="Economic and Philosophical Manuscripts of 1844">
        Economic-Philosophical Manuscripts
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_German_Ideology" title="The German Ideology">
        The German Ideology
       </a>
      </i>
      , which showed the continuity with
      <a href="/wiki/Hegelianism" title="Hegelianism">
       Hegelianism
      </a>
      that underlay Marx's thought.
     </p>
     <p>
      As the growing influence of National Socialism became ever more threatening, its founders decided to prepare to move the Institute out of the country.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machtergreifung" title="Machtergreifung">
       Adolf Hitler's rise to power
      </a>
      in 1933, the Institute left Germany for
      <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">
       Geneva
      </a>
      , before moving to
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      in 1935, where it became affiliated with
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia University
      </a>
      . Its journal
      <i>
       Zeitschrift fr Sozialforschung
      </i>
      was accordingly renamed
      <i>
       Studies in Philosophy and Social Science
      </i>
      . It was at this moment that much of its important work began to emerge, having gained a favorable reception within American and English
      <a href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">
       academia
      </a>
      . Horkheimer, Adorno and Pollock eventually resettled in
      <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">
       West Germany
      </a>
      in the early 1950s, although Marcuse, Lowenthal, Kirchheimer and others chose to remain in the United States. It was only in 1953 that the Institute was formally re-established in Frankfurt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theorists">
       Theorists
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theorists">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_critical_theorists" title="List of critical theorists">
       List of critical theorists
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Which "theorists" to include in what is now called the "Frankfurt School" may vary among different scholars. Indeed, the title of "school" can often be misleading, as the Institute's members did not always form a series of tightly woven, complementary projects. Some scholars have therefore limited their view of the Frankfurt School to Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse, Lowenthal and Pollock.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Held.2C_David_1980.2C_p._15_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Held.2C_David_1980.2C_p._15-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, most pre-war theorists can be considered as having shared a very similar paradigm. Although he was initially part of the School's inner circle,
      <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jrgen Habermas">
       Jrgen Habermas
      </a>
      is generally considered as the first to have diverged from Horkheimer's research program, thus giving rise to a new generation of
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">
       critical theorists
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Early members of the Frankfurt School were:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Horkheimer" title="Max Horkheimer">
        Max Horkheimer
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno" title="Theodor W. Adorno">
        Theodor W. Adorno
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Marcuse" title="Herbert Marcuse">
        Herbert Marcuse
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Pollock" title="Friedrich Pollock">
        Friedrich Pollock
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Erich_Fromm" title="Erich Fromm">
        Erich Fromm
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Otto_Kirchheimer" title="Otto Kirchheimer">
        Otto Kirchheimer
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_L%C3%B6wenthal" title="Leo Lwenthal">
        Leo Lwenthal
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Franz_Leopold_Neumann" title="Franz Leopold Neumann">
        Franz Leopold Neumann
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      People who were associated with the Institute or its theorists include:
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_work">
       Theoretical work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Theoretical work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_theory_and_the_critique_of_ideology">
       Critical theory and the critique of ideology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical theory and the critique of ideology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Frankfurt School's work cannot be fully comprehended without equally understanding the aims and objectives of critical theory. Initially outlined by
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Horkheimer" title="Max Horkheimer">
       Max Horkheimer
      </a>
      in his
      <i>
       Traditional and Critical Theory
      </i>
      (1937), critical theory may be defined as a self-conscious
      <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">
       social
      </a>
      critique that is aimed at change and emancipation through enlightenment, and does not cling dogmatically to its own doctrinal assumptions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carr.2C_Adrian_2000_p._208-220_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carr.2C_Adrian_2000_p._208-220-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The original aim of critical theory was to analyze the true significance of "the ruling understandings" generated in bourgeois society, in order to show how they
      <i>
       misrepresented
      </i>
      actual human interaction in the real world, and in so doing functioned to
      <i>
       justify
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       legitimize
      </i>
      the domination of people by capitalism. A certain sort of story (a narrative) was provided to explain what was happening in society, but the story concealed as much as it revealed. The Frankfurt theorists generally assumed that their task was mainly to interpret the areas of society Marx had not dealt with, especially in the
      <a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">
       superstructure
      </a>
      of society.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Horkheimer opposed it to
      <i>
       traditional theory
      </i>
      , which refers to theory in the positivistic,
      <a href="/wiki/Scientism" title="Scientism">
       scientistic
      </a>
      , or purely observational mode  that is, which derives generalizations or "
      <a href="/wiki/Scientific_law" title="Scientific law">
       laws
      </a>
      " about different aspects of the world. Drawing upon Max Weber, Horkheimer argued that the
      <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">
       social sciences
      </a>
      are different from the
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">
       natural sciences
      </a>
      , inasmuch as generalizations cannot be easily made from so-called experiences, because the understanding of a "social" experience itself is always fashioned by ideas that are in the researchers themselves. What the researcher does not realize is that he is caught in a historical context in which ideologies shape the thinking; thus theory would be conforming to the ideas in the mind of the researcher rather than the experience itself:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        The facts which our senses present to us are socially performed in two ways: through the historical character of the object perceived and through the historical character of the perceiving organ. Both are not simply natural; they are shaped by human activity, and yet the individual perceives himself as receptive and passive in the act of perception.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          17
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      For Horkheimer, approaches to understanding in the social sciences cannot simply imitate those in the natural sciences. Although various theoretical approaches would come close to breaking out of the ideological constraints that restricted them, such as positivism,
      <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">
       pragmatism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Neo-Kantianism" title="Neo-Kantianism">
       neo-Kantianism
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">
       phenomenology
      </a>
      , Horkheimer argued that they failed, because all were subject to a "logico-mathematical" prejudice that separates theoretical activity from actual life (meaning that all these schools sought to find a logic that always remains true, independently of and without consideration for ongoing human activities). According to Horkheimer, the appropriate response to this dilemma is the development of a critical theory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The problem, Horkheimer argued, is
      <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">
       epistemological
      </a>
      : we should not merely reconsider the scientist but the knowing individual in general.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Unlike
      <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Marxism" title="Orthodox Marxism">
       orthodox Marxism
      </a>
      , which merely applies a ready-made "template" to both critique and action, critical theory seeks to be self-critical and rejects any pretensions to
      <a href="/wiki/Universality_(philosophy)" title="Universality (philosophy)">
       absolute truth
      </a>
      . Critical theory defends the primacy of neither matter (materialism) nor consciousness (
      <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">
       idealism
      </a>
      ), arguing that both epistemologies distort reality to the benefit, eventually, of some small group. What critical theory attempts to do is to place itself outside of philosophical strictures and the confines of existing structures. However, as a way of thinking and "recovering" humanity's self-knowledge, critical theory often looks to Marxism for its methods and tools.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carr.2C_Adrian_2000_p._208-220_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Carr.2C_Adrian_2000_p._208-220-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Horkheimer maintained that critical theory should be directed at the totality of society in its
      <a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist" title="Zeitgeist">
       historical specificity
      </a>
      (i.e., how it came to be configured at a specific point in time), just as it should improve understanding of society by integrating all the major social sciences, including geography, economics, sociology, history, political science, anthropology, and psychology. While critical theory must at all times be self-critical, Horkheimer insisted that a theory is only critical if it is explanatory. Critical theory must, therefore, combine practical and normative thinking to "explain what is wrong with current social reality, identify actors to change it, and provide clear norms for criticism and practical goals for the future."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Whereas traditional theory can only mirror and explain reality as it presently is, critical theory's purpose is to
      <i>
       change
      </i>
      it; in Horkheimer's words the goal of critical theory is "the emancipation of human beings from the circumstances that enslave them".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Frankfurt School theorists were explicitly linking up with the critical philosophy of
      <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
       Immanuel Kant
      </a>
      , where the term
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Critique" title="Critique">
        critique
       </a>
      </i>
      meant philosophical reflection on the limits of claims made for certain kinds of knowledge and a direct connection between such critique and the emphasis on
      <a href="/wiki/Autonomy#Philosophy" title="Autonomy">
       moral autonomy
      </a>
       as opposed to traditionally deterministic and static theories of human action. In an intellectual context defined by dogmatic positivism and scientism on the one hand and dogmatic "
      <a href="/wiki/Scientific_socialism" title="Scientific socialism">
       scientific socialism
      </a>
      " on the other, critical theorists intended to rehabilitate Marx's ideas through a philosophically critical approach.
     </p>
     <p>
      Whereas both
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist-Leninist" title="Marxist-Leninist">
       Marxist-Leninist
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">
       Social-Democratic
      </a>
      orthodox thinkers viewed Marxism as a new kind of positive science, Frankfurt School theorists, such as Horkheimer, rather based their work on the epistemological base of Karl Marx's work, which presented itself as critique, as in Marx's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Das_Kapital" title="Das Kapital">
        Capital: A Critique of Political Economy
       </a>
      </i>
      . They thus emphasized that Marx was attempting to create a new kind of critical analysis oriented toward the unity of theory and
      <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">
       revolutionary practice
      </a>
      rather than a new kind of positive science. Critique, in this Marxian sense, meant taking the ideology of a society  e.g. the belief in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Individual_freedom" title="Individual freedom">
       individual freedom
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">
       free market
      </a>
      under capitalism  and critiquing it by comparing it with the social reality of that very society  e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">
       social inequality
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploitation" title="Exploitation">
       exploitation
      </a>
      . The methodology on which Frankfurt School theorists grounded this critique came to be what had before been established by Hegel and Marx, namely the dialectical method.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialectical_method">
       Dialectical method
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dialectical method">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Institute also attempted to reformulate dialectics as a concrete method. The use of such a dialectical method can be traced back to the philosophy of Hegel, who conceived dialectic as the tendency of a notion to pass over into its own negation as the result of conflict between its inherent contradictory aspects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dialectic1_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dialectic1-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In opposition to previous modes of thought, which viewed things in abstraction, each by itself and as though endowed with fixed properties, Hegelian dialectic has the ability to consider ideas according to their movement and change in time, as well as according to their interrelations and interactions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dialectic1_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-dialectic1-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      History, according to Hegel, proceeds and evolves in a dialectical manner: the present embodies the rational
      <a href="/wiki/Aufheben" title="Aufheben">
       sublation
      </a>
      , or "synthesis", of past contradictions. History may thus be seen as an intelligible process (which Hegel referred to as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Geist#The_Weltgeist" title="Geist">
        Weltgeist
       </a>
      </i>
      ), which is the
      <a href="/wiki/Idea_of_Progress" title="Idea of Progress">
       moving towards a specific condition
      </a>
      the rational realization of human freedom.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HegelStanford_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HegelStanford-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However,
      <a href="/wiki/Problem_of_future_contingents" title="Problem of future contingents">
       considerations about the future
      </a>
      were of no interest to Hegel,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for whom philosophy cannot be
      <a href="/wiki/Normative#Philosophy" title="Normative">
       prescriptive
      </a>
      because it understands only in hindsight. The study of history is thus limited to the description of past and present realities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HegelStanford_23-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HegelStanford-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Hence for Hegel and his
      <a href="/wiki/Right_Hegelians" title="Right Hegelians">
       successors
      </a>
      , dialectics inevitably lead to the approval of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">
        status quo
       </a>
      </i>
      indeed, Hegel's philosophy served as a justification for
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">
       Christian theology
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">
       Prussian state
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      This was fiercely criticized by Marx and the
      <a href="/wiki/Young_Hegelians" title="Young Hegelians">
       Young Hegelians
      </a>
      , who claimed that Hegel had gone too far in defending his abstract conception of "absolute Reason" and had failed to notice the "real" i.e.
      <i>
       undesirable
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       irrational
      </i>
       life conditions of the
      <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">
       working class
      </a>
      . By turning Hegel's
      <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">
       idealist
      </a>
      dialectics upside-down, Marx advanced his own theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Dialectical_materialism" title="Dialectical materialism">
       dialectical materialism
      </a>
      , arguing that "it is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their consciousness."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Marx's theory follows a
      <a href="/wiki/Materialism#Marx.27s_social_materialism" title="Materialism">
       materialist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Historicism" title="Historicism">
       law of history
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marxist_geography" title="Marxist geography">
       space
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where the development of the productive forces is seen as the primary motive force for historical change, and according to which the social and material
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contradictions#Dialectical_materialism" title="Contradictions">
       contradictions
      </a>
      inherent to capitalism inevitably lead to its negationthereby replacing capitalism with a new rational form of society:
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       communism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Marx thus extensively relied on a form of dialectical analysis. This methodto know the truth by uncovering the contradictions in presently predominant ideas and, by extension, in the social relations to which they are linkedexposes the underlying struggle between opposing forces. For Marx, it is only by becoming
      <a href="/wiki/Class_consciousness" title="Class consciousness">
       aware
      </a>
      of the dialectic of such opposing forces, in a struggle for power, that individuals can liberate themselves and change the existing social order.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For their part, Frankfurt School theorists quickly came to realize that a dialectical method could only be adopted
      <i>
       if it could be applied to itself
      </i>
      that is to say, if they adopted a self-correcting methoda dialectical method that would enable them to correct previous false dialectical interpretations. Accordingly, critical theory rejected the dogmatic
      <a href="/wiki/Historicism" title="Historicism">
       historicism
      </a>
      and materialism of
      <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Marxism" title="Orthodox Marxism">
       orthodox Marxism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Indeed, the material tensions and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle">
       class struggles
      </a>
      of which Marx spoke were no longer seen by Frankfurt School theorists as having the same
      <a href="/wiki/Communist_revolution" title="Communist revolution">
       revolutionary potential
      </a>
      within contemporary Western societiesan observation that indicated that Marx's dialectical interpretations and predictions were either incomplete or incorrect.
     </p>
     <p>
      Contrary to orthodox Marxist
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">
        praxis
       </a>
      </i>
      , which solely seeks to implement an unchangeable and narrow idea of "communism" into practice, critical theorists held that praxis and theory, following the dialectical method, should be interdependent and should mutually influence each other. When Marx famously stated in his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Theses_on_Feuerbach" title="Theses on Feuerbach">
        Theses on Feuerbach
       </a>
      </i>
      that "philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is to change it", his real idea was that philosophy's
      <i>
       only
      </i>
      validity was in how it informed action. Frankfurt School theorists would correct this by claiming that when action fails, then the theory guiding it must be reviewed. In short, socialist philosophical thought must be given the ability to criticize itself and "overcome" its own errors. While theory must inform praxis, praxis must
      <i>
       also
      </i>
      have a chance to inform theory.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_influences">
       Early influences
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early influences">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The intellectual influences on and theoretical focus of the first generation of Frankfurt School critical theorists can be summarized as follows:
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable">
      <tr>
       <td width="150pt">
        <b>
         Historical context
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Periodizations_of_capitalism" title="Periodizations of capitalism">
         Transition
        </a>
        from small-scale entrepreneurial capitalism to
        <a href="/wiki/State_monopoly_capitalism" title="State monopoly capitalism">
         monopoly capitalism
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">
         imperialism
        </a>
        ; socialist labor movement grows, turns
        <a href="/wiki/Reform_movement" title="Reform movement">
         reformist
        </a>
        ; emergence of the
        <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">
         welfare state
        </a>
        ;
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_(1917)" title="Russian Revolution (1917)">
         Russian revolution
        </a>
        and the rise of
        <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
         Communism
        </a>
        ;
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotechnic" title="Neotechnic">
         neotechnic
        </a>
        period; emergence of
        <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">
         mass media
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">
         mass culture
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">
         "modern" art
        </a>
        ; rise of
        <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">
         Nazism
        </a>
        .
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">
          Weberian theory
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Comparative_history" title="Comparative history">
         Comparative historical analysis
        </a>

     <p>
      Responding to the intensification of
      <a href="/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation" title="Marx's theory of alienation">
       alienation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Irrationality" title="Irrationality">
       irrationality
      </a>
      in an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_capitalist_society" title="Advanced capitalist society">
       advanced capitalist society
      </a>
      , critical theory is a comprehensive, ideology-critical, historically self-reflective body of theory aiming simultaneously to explain domination and point to the possibilities of bringing about a rational, humane, and free society. Frankfurt School critical theorists developed numerous theories of the economic, political, cultural, and psychological domination structures of advanced industrial civilization.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Institute made major contributions in two areas relating to the possibility of human
      <a href="/wiki/Subject_(philosophy)" title="Subject (philosophy)">
       subjects
      </a>
      to be rational, i.e., individuals who could act rationally to take charge of their own
      <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">
       society
      </a>
      and their own
      <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">
       history
      </a>
      . The first consisted of social phenomena previously considered in Marxism as part of the "
      <a href="/wiki/Base_and_superstructure" title="Base and superstructure">
       superstructure
      </a>
      " or as
      <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">
       ideology
      </a>
      :
      <a href="/wiki/Personality_psychology" title="Personality psychology">
       personality
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">
       family
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">
       authority
      </a>
      structures (one of the earliest works published bore the title
      <i>
       Studies of Authority and the Family
      </i>
      ), and the realm of
      <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">
       aesthetics
      </a>
      and mass culture. Studies saw a common concern here in the ability of capitalism to destroy the preconditions of critical, revolutionary
      <a href="/wiki/Political_consciousness" title="Political consciousness">
       political consciousness
      </a>
      . This meant arriving at a sophisticated awareness of the depth dimension in which social
      <a href="/wiki/Oppression" title="Oppression">
       oppression
      </a>
      sustains itself. It also meant the beginning of critical theory's recognition of ideology as part of the foundations of social structure.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critique_of_Western_civilization">
       Critique of Western civilization
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critique of Western civilization">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialectic_of_Enlightenment_and_Minima_Moralia">
       <i>
        Dialectic of Enlightenment
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Minima Moralia
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dialectic of Enlightenment and Minima Moralia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The second phase of Frankfurt School critical theory centres principally on two works:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodor_Adorno" title="Theodor Adorno">
       Adorno
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Horkheimer" title="Max Horkheimer">
       Horkheimer
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dialectic_of_Enlightenment" title="Dialectic of Enlightenment">
        Dialectic of Enlightenment
       </a>
      </i>
      (1944) and Adorno's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Minima_Moralia" title="Minima Moralia">
        Minima Moralia
       </a>
      </i>
      (1951). The authors wrote both works during the Institute's exile in America. While retaining much of a Marxian analysis, in these works critical theory shifted its emphasis. The critique of capitalism turned into a critique of
      <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">
       Western civilization
      </a>
      as a whole. Indeed, the
      <i>
       Dialectic of Enlightenment
      </i>
      uses the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">
        Odyssey
       </a>
      </i>
      as a paradigm for the analysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">
       bourgeois
      </a>
      consciousness. Horkheimer and Adorno already present in these works many themes that have come to dominate the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_thought" title="Social thought">
       social thought
      </a>
      of recent years; indeed, their exposition of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nature#Human_interrelationship" title="Nature">
       domination of nature
      </a>
      as a central characteristic of
      <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_rationality" title="Instrumental rationality">
       instrumental rationality
      </a>
      in Western civilization was made long before
      <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">
       ecology
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">
       environmentalism
      </a>
      had become popular concerns.
     </p>
     <p>
      The analysis of reason now goes one stage further. The
      <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">
       rationality
      </a>
      of Western civilization appears as a fusion of domination and of technological rationality, bringing all of external and internal nature under the power of the human subject. In the process, however, the subject itself gets swallowed up, and no social force analogous to the
      <a href="/wiki/Proletariat" title="Proletariat">
       proletariat
      </a>
      can be identified that enables the subject to emancipate itself. Hence the subtitle of
      <i>
       Minima Moralia
      </i>
      : "Reflections from Damaged Life". In Adorno's words,
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        For since the overwhelming
        <a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(philosophy)" title="Objectivity (philosophy)">
         objectivity
        </a>
        of historical movement in its present phase consists so far only in the dissolution of the subject, without yet giving rise to a new one, individual
        <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">
         experience
        </a>
        necessarily bases itself on the old subject, now historically condemned, which is still for-itself, but no longer in-itself. The subject still feels sure of its
        <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/autonomy" title="wiktionary:autonomy">
         autonomy
        </a>
        , but the nullity demonstrated to subjects by the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" title="Concentration camp">
         concentration camp
        </a>
        is already overtaking the form of
        <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">
         subjectivity
        </a>
        itself.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
         <a href="#cite_note-31">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          31
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Consequently, at a time when it appears that reality itself has become the basis for ideology, the greatest contribution that critical theory can make is to explore the dialectical contradictions of individual subjective experience on the one hand, and to preserve the truth of theory on the other. Even dialectical progress is put into doubt: "its truth or untruth is not inherent in the method itself, but in its intention in the historical process." This intention
      <i>
       must
      </i>
      be oriented toward integral freedom and happiness: "The only philosophy which can be responsibly practiced in face of despair is the attempt to contemplate all things as they would present themselves from the standpoint of
      <a href="/wiki/Salvation#Redemption" title="Salvation">
       redemption
      </a>
      ." Adorno goes on to distance himself from the "optimism" of orthodox Marxism: "beside the demand thus placed on thought, the question of the reality or unreality of redemption [i.e. human emancipation] itself hardly matters."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From a sociological point of view, both Horkheimer's and Adorno's works contain a certain ambivalence concerning the ultimate source or foundation of social domination, an ambivalence that gave rise to the "pessimism" of the new critical theory over the possibility of human emancipation and freedom.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This ambivalence was rooted, of course, in the historical circumstances in which the work was originally produced, in particular, the rise of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Socialism" title="National Socialism">
       National Socialism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/State_capitalism" title="State capitalism">
       state capitalism
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_culture" title="Mass culture">
       mass culture
      </a>
      as entirely new forms of social domination that could not be adequately explained within the terms of traditional Marxist sociology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For Adorno and Horkheimer,
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_interventionism" title="Economic interventionism">
       state intervention
      </a>
      in the economy had effectively abolished the tension in capitalism between the "
      <a href="/wiki/Relations_of_production" title="Relations of production">
       relations of production
      </a>
      " and "material
      <a href="/wiki/Productive_forces" title="Productive forces">
       productive forces
      </a>
      of society"a tension that, according to traditional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist_theory" title="Marxist theory">
       Marxist theory
      </a>
      , constituted the primary contradiction within capitalism. The previously "free" market (as an "unconscious" mechanism for the distribution of goods) and "irrevocable"
      <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">
       private property
      </a>
      of Marx's epoch have gradually been replaced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Planned_economy" title="Planned economy">
       centralized state planning
      </a>
      and socialized ownership of the
      <a href="/wiki/Means_of_production" title="Means of production">
       means of production
      </a>
      in contemporary Western societies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The dialectic through which Marx predicted the emancipation of modern society is thus suppressed, effectively being subjugated to a positivist rationality of domination.
     </p>
     <p>
      Of this second "phase" of the Frankfurt School, philosopher and critical theorist Nikolas Kompridis writes that:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        According to the now canonical view of its history, Frankfurt School critical theory began in the 1930s as a fairly confident interdisciplinary and materialist research program, the general aim of which was to connect normative social criticism to the emancipatory potential latent in concrete historical processes. Only a decade or so later, however, having revisited the premises of their philosophy of history, Horkheimer and Adorno's
        <i>
         Dialectic of Enlightenment
        </i>
        steered the whole enterprise, provocatively and self-consciously, into a skeptical
        <a href="/wiki/Cul-de-sac" title="Cul-de-sac">
         cul-de-sac
        </a>
        . As a result they got stuck in the irresolvable dilemmas of the "philosophy of the subject," and the original program was shrunk to a negativistic practice of critique that eschewed the very normative ideals on which it implicitly depended.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kompridis.2C_Nikolas._2006.2C_p._256_36-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Kompridis.2C_Nikolas._2006.2C_p._256-36">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          36
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Kompridis claims that this "sceptical cul-de-sac" was arrived at with "a lot of help from the once unspeakable and unprecedented barbarity of European fascism," and could not be gotten out of without "some well-marked [exit or]
      <i>
       Ausgang
      </i>
      , showing the way out of the ever-recurring nightmare in which Enlightenment hopes and Holocaust horrors are fatally entangled." However, this
      <i>
       Ausgang
      </i>
      , according to Kompridis, would not come until later  purportedly in the form of Jrgen Habermas's work on the intersubjective bases of
      <a href="/wiki/Communicative_rationality" title="Communicative rationality">
       communicative rationality
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kompridis.2C_Nikolas._2006.2C_p._256_36-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kompridis.2C_Nikolas._2006.2C_p._256-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_modern_music">
       Philosophy of modern music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Philosophy of modern music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Adorno, a trained musician, wrote
      <i>
       The Philosophy of Modern Music
      </i>
      (1949), in which he, in essence, polemicizes against
      <a href="/wiki/Beauty" title="Beauty">
       beauty
      </a>
      itself  because it has become part of the ideology of
      <a href="/wiki/Advanced_capitalism" title="Advanced capitalism">
       advanced capitalist society
      </a>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (September 2010)">
          pageneeded
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/False_consciousness" title="False consciousness">
       false consciousness
      </a>
      that contributes to social domination. It hence contributes to the present sustainability of capitalism by rendering it "aesthetically pleasing" and "agreeable". Only
      <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
       avant-garde
      </a>
      art and music may preserve the truth by capturing the reality of human suffering. Hence:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        What radical music perceives is the untransfigured suffering of man [...] The seismographic registration of traumatic shock becomes, at the same time, the technical structural law of music. It forbids continuity and development. Musical language is polarized according to its extreme; towards gestures of shock resembling bodily convulsions on the one hand, and on the other towards a crystalline standstill of a human being whom anxiety causes to freeze in her tracks [...] Modern music sees absolute oblivion as its goal. It is the surviving message of despair from the shipwrecked.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
         <a href="#cite_note-37">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          37
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      This view of
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">
       modern art
      </a>
      as producing truth only through the negation of traditional aesthetic form and traditional norms of beauty because they have become ideological is characteristic of Adorno and of the Frankfurt School generally. It has been criticized by those who do not share its conception of modern society as a false totality that renders obsolete traditional conceptions and images of beauty and harmony.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_theory_and_domination">
       Critical theory and domination
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_dialectics">
       Negative dialectics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      With the growth of advanced industrial society during the
      <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
       Cold War
      </a>
      era, critical theorists recognized that the path of capitalism and history had changed decisively, that the modes of oppression operated differently, and that the industrial
      <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">
       working class
      </a>
      no longer remained the determinate negation of capitalism. This led to the attempt to root the dialectic in an absolute method of negativity, as in Marcuse's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/One-Dimensional_Man" title="One-Dimensional Man">
        One-Dimensional Man
       </a>
      </i>
      (1964) and Adorno's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Negative_Dialectics" title="Negative Dialectics">
        Negative Dialectics
       </a>
      </i>
      (1966). During this period the Institute of Social Research re-settled in
      <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">
       Frankfurt
      </a>
      (although many of its associates remained in the United States) with the task not merely of continuing its research but of becoming a leading force in the sociological education and
      <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">
       democratization
      </a>
      of West Germany. This led to a certain systematization of the Institute's entire accumulation of empirical research and theoretical analysis.
     </p>
     <p>
      During this period, Frankfurt School critical theory particularly influenced some segments of the
      <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">
       left wing
      </a>
      and leftist thought, particularly the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">
       New Left
      </a>
      . Herbert Marcuse has occasionally been described as the theorist or intellectual progenitor of the New Left. Their critique of technology, totality, teleology and (occasionally) civilization is an influence on
      <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">
       anarcho-primitivism
      </a>
      . Their work also heavily influenced intellectual discourse on popular culture and scholarly popular culture studies.
     </p>
     <p>
      More importantly, however, the Frankfurt School attempted to define the fate of reason in the new historical period. While Marcuse did so through analysis of structural changes in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_(economics)" title="Labour (economics)">
       labor
      </a>
      process under capitalism and inherent features of the
      <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">
       methodology
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
       science
      </a>
      , Horkheimer and Adorno concentrated on a re-examination of the foundation of critical theory. This effort appears in systematized form in Adorno's
      <i>
       Negative Dialectics
      </i>
      , which tries to redefine dialectics for an era in which "philosophy, which once seemed obsolete, lives on because the moment to realize it was missed". Negative dialectics expresses the idea of critical thought so conceived that the apparatus of domination cannot co-opt it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its central notion, long a focal one for Horkheimer and Adorno, suggests that the
      <a href="/wiki/Original_sin" title="Original sin">
       original sin
      </a>
      of thought lies in its attempt to eliminate all that is other than thought, the attempt by the subject to devour the object, the striving for
      <a href="/wiki/Identity_(philosophy)" title="Identity (philosophy)">
       identity
      </a>
      . This
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction_(philosophy)" title="Reduction (philosophy)">
       reduction
      </a>
      makes thought the accomplice of domination.
      <i>
       Negative Dialectics
      </i>
      rescues the "preponderance of the object", not through a naive
      <a href="/wiki/Epistemological_realism" title="Epistemological realism">
       epistemological
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_realism" title="Philosophical realism">
       metaphysical realism
      </a>
      but through a thought based on
      <a href="/wiki/Differentiation_(sociology)" title="Differentiation (sociology)">
       differentiation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">
       paradox
      </a>
      , and ruse: a "logic of disintegration". Adorno thoroughly criticizes
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">
       Heidegger
      </a>
      's fundamental
      <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">
       ontology
      </a>
      , which he thinks reintroduces idealistic and identity-based concepts under the guise of having overcome the philosophical tradition.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Negative dialectics
      </i>
      comprises a monument to the end of the tradition of the individual subject as the locus of criticism. Without a revolutionary working class, the Frankfurt School had no one to rely on but the individual subject. But, as the
      <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">
       liberal
      </a>
      capitalist social basis of the autonomous individual receded into the past, the dialectic based on it became more and more abstract.
     </p>

     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">

       Jrgen Habermas
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Habermas's work takes the Frankfurt School's abiding interests in rationality, the human subject,
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">
       democratic socialism
      </a>
      , and the dialectical method and overcomes a set of contradictions that always weakened critical theory: the contradictions between the materialist and
      <a href="/wiki/Transcendence_(philosophy)" title="Transcendence (philosophy)">
       transcendental
      </a>
      methods, between Marxian social theory and the
      <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">
       individualist
      </a>
      assumptions of critical
      <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">
       rationalism
      </a>
      between technical and social rationalization, and between cultural and psychological phenomena on the one hand and the
      <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
       economic
      </a>
      structure of society on the other.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Frankfurt School avoided taking a stand on the precise relationship between the materialist and transcendental methods, which led to ambiguity in their writings and confusion among their readers. Habermas's epistemology synthesizes these two traditions by showing that phenomenological and transcendental analysis can be subsumed under a materialist theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Social_evolution" title="Social evolution">
       social evolution
      </a>
      , while the materialist theory makes sense only as part of a quasi-transcendental theory of emancipatory knowledge that is the self-reflection of cultural evolution. The simultaneously empirical and transcendental nature of emancipatory knowledge becomes the foundation stone of critical theory.
     </p>
     <p>
      By locating the conditions of rationality in the social structure of
      <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
       language
      </a>
      use, Habermas moves the locus of rationality from the autonomous subject to subjects in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_interaction" title="Social interaction">
       interaction
      </a>
      . Rationality is a property not of individuals per se, but rather of structures of undistorted
      <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">
       communication
      </a>
      . In this notion Habermas has overcome the ambiguous plight of the subject in critical theory. If capitalistic technological society weakens the autonomy and rationality of the subject, it is not through the domination of the individual by the apparatus but through technological rationality supplanting a describable rationality of communication. And, in his sketch of communicative
      <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">
       ethics
      </a>
      as the highest stage in the internal logic of the evolution of ethical systems, Habermas hints at the source of a new
      <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">
       political
      </a>
      practice that incorporates the imperatives of evolutionary rationality.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_psychoanalytic_categorizations">
       Criticism of psychoanalytic categorizations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism of psychoanalytic categorizations">
        edit
       </a>

        ]
       </span>

     <p>
      In an interview with Casey Blake and
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Phelps" title="Christopher Phelps">
       Christopher Phelps
      </a>
      , historian
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lasch" title="Christopher Lasch">
       Christopher Lasch
      </a>
      criticized the Frankfurt School's initial tendencies towards "automatically" rejecting opposing political criticisms on "psychiatric" grounds:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Authoritarian_Personality" title="The Authoritarian Personality">
          The Authoritarian Personality
         </a>
        </i>
        had a tremendous influence on
        <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hofstadter" title="Richard Hofstadter">
         Hofstadter
        </a>
        and other liberal intellectuals, because it showed them how to conduct political criticism in psychiatric categories, to make those categories bear the weight of political criticism. This procedure excused them from the difficult work of judgment and argumentation. Instead of arguing with opponents, they simply dismissed them on psychiatric grounds.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
         <a href="#cite_note-38">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          38
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Horkheimer_and_Adorno.27s_pessimism">
       Horkheimer and Adorno's pessimism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Horkheimer and Adorno's pessimism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An early criticism, originating from the left, argues that Frankfurt School critical theory is nothing more than a form of "bourgeois idealism" devoid of any actual relation to political practice, and is hence totally isolated from the reality of any ongoing revolutionary movement. This criticism was captured in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Luk%C3%A1cs" title="Georg Lukcs">
       Georg Lukcs
      </a>
      's phrase "Grand Hotel Abyss" as a syndrome he imputed to the members of the Frankfurt School:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        A considerable part of the leading German intelligentsia, including Adorno, have taken up residence in the
        <i>
         Grand Hotel Abyss
        </i>
        which I described in connection with my critique of
        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">
         Schopenhauer
        </a>
        as "a beautiful hotel, equipped with every comfort, on the edge of an abyss, of nothingness, of absurdity. And the daily contemplation of the abyss between excellent meals or artistic entertainments, can only heighten the enjoyment of the subtle comforts offered."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
         <a href="#cite_note-39">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          39
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">
       Karl Popper
      </a>
      equally believed that the school did not live up to Marx's promise of a better future:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        Marx's own condemnation of our society makes sense. For Marx's theory contains the promise of a better future. But the theory becomes vacuous and irresponsible if this promise is withdrawn, as it is by Adorno and Horkheimer.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
         <a href="#cite_note-40">
          <span>
           [
          </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Habermas.27s_solutions:_critical_theory_.22between_past_and_future.22">
       Habermas's solutions: critical theory "between past and future"
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Habermas's solutions: critical theory &quot;between past and future&quot;">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2006, Nikolas Kompridis (who undertook a post-doctoral fellowship with Jrgen Habermas) published new criticisms of Habermas's approach to critical theory, calling for a dramatic break with the proceduralist ethics of communicative rationality. He writes:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        For all its theoretical ingenuity and practical implications, Habermas's reformulation of critical theory is beset by persistent problems of its own In my view, the depth of these problems indicate just how wrong was Habermas's expectation that the paradigm change to linguistic intersubjectivity would render "objectless" the dilemmas of the philosophy of the subject.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
         <a href="#cite_note-41">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          41
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
        Habermas accused Hegel of creating a conception of reason so "overwhelming" that it solved
        <i>
         too well
        </i>
        the problem of modernity's [need for] self-reassurance.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
         <a href="#cite_note-42">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          42
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
        It seems, however, that Habermas has repeated rather than avoided Hegel's mistake, creating a theoretical paradigm so comprehensive that in one stroke it
        <i>
         also
        </i>
        solves too well the dilemmas of the philosophy of the subject
        <i>
         and
        </i>
        the problem of modernity's self-reassurance.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
         <a href="#cite_note-43">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          43
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      In addition, he writes that:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        The change of paradigm to linguistic intersubjectivity has been accompanied by a dramatic change in critical theory's self-understanding. The priority given to questions of justice and the normative order of society has remodeled critical theory in the image of liberal theories of justice. While this has produced an important contemporary variant of liberal theories of justice, different enough to be a challenge to liberal theory, but not enough to preserve sufficient continuity with critical theory's past, it has severely weakened the identity of critical theory and inadvertently initiated its premature dissolution.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
         <a href="#cite_note-44">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          44
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      To prevent that dissolution, Kompridis suggests that critical theory should "reinvent" itself as a "possibility-disclosing" enterprise, incorporating Heidegger's controversial insights into
      <a href="/wiki/World_disclosure" title="World disclosure">
       world disclosure
      </a>
      and drawing from the sources of normativity that he feels were blocked from critical theory by its recent change of paradigm. Calling for what
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Taylor_(philosopher)" title="Charles Taylor (philosopher)">
       Charles Taylor
      </a>
      has named a "new department" of reason,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with a possibility-disclosing role that Kompridis calls "
      <a href="/wiki/Reflective_disclosure" title="Reflective disclosure">
       reflective disclosure
      </a>
      ", Kompridis argues that critical theory must embrace its neglected
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_romanticism" title="German romanticism">
       German romantic inheritance
      </a>
      and once again imagine alternatives to existing social and political conditions, "if it is to have a future worthy of its past."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_and_media_critiques">
       Economic and media critiques
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Economic and media critiques">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the eighties, anti-authoritarian socialists in the United Kingdom and New Zealand criticised the rigid and determinist view of popular culture deployed within the Frankfurt School theories of capitalist culture, which seemed to preclude any prefigurative role for social critique within such work. They argued that
      <a href="/wiki/EC_Comics" title="EC Comics">
       EC Comics
      </a>
      often did contain such cultural critiques.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Recent criticism of the Frankfurt School by the
      <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">
       libertarian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CATO_Institute" title="CATO Institute">
       CATO Institute
      </a>
      focused on the claim that culture has grown more sophisticated and diverse as a consequence of free markets and the availability of niche cultural text for niche audiences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conspiracy_theory">
       Conspiracy theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Frankfurt_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Conspiracy theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A notable 21st-century conspiracy theory regards the Frankfurt School as the origin of a contemporary movement in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_left" title="Political left">
       political left
      </a>
      to subvert traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">
       western cultural norms
      </a>
      , referred to as "
      <b>
       Cultural Marxism
      </b>
      " by theory proponents. It advocates the idea that
      <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">
       multiculturalism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">
       political correctness
      </a>
      are products of
      <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">
       critical theory
      </a>
      , which originated with the Frankfurt School. The theory is associated with American conservative thinkers such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Lind" title="William Lind">
       William Lind
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pat_Buchanan" title="Pat Buchanan">
       Pat Buchanan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Weyrich" title="Paul Weyrich">
       Paul Weyrich
      </a>
      , and has received institutional support from the
      <a href="/wiki/Free_Congress_Foundation" title="Free Congress Foundation">
       Free Congress Foundation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berkowitz_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berkowitz-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lind_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lind-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A copy of
      <i>
       Political Correctness: A Short History of an Ideology
      </i>
      by the FCF was included in a document titled
      <i>
       2083: A European Declaration of Independence
      </i>
      by Norwegian terrorist
      <a href="/wiki/Anders_Behring_Breivik" title="Anders Behring Breivik">
       Anders Behring Breivik
      </a>
      , which was e-mailed to 1,003 addresses about 90 minutes before
      <a href="/wiki/2011_Norway_attacks" title="2011 Norway attacks">
       the 2011 bomb blast in Oslo
      </a>
      Breivik was responsible for.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAYLOR_53-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TAYLOR-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although it became more widespread in the late 1990s and 2000s, the theory originated with Michael Minnicino's 1992 essay "New Dark Age: Frankfurt School and 'Political Correctness'", published in
      <i>
       Fidelio
      </i>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Schiller_Institute" title="Schiller Institute">
       Schiller Institute
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schillerinstitute.org_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-schillerinstitute.org-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jay_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jay-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Schiller Institute, a branch of the
      <a href="/wiki/LaRouche_movement" title="LaRouche movement">
       LaRouche movement
      </a>
      , further promoted the idea in 1994.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Minnicino article charges that the Frankfurt School promoted
      <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">
       Modernism
      </a>
      in the arts as a form of
      <a href="/wiki/Cultural_pessimism" title="Cultural pessimism">
       Cultural pessimism
      </a>
      , and played a role in shaping the
      <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">
       1960s counterculture
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schillerinstitute.org_54-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-schillerinstitute.org-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1999 Lind led the creation of an hour-long program,
      <i>
       Political Correctness: The Frankfurt School
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jay_55-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jay-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The documentary
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "... spawned a number of condensed textual versions, which were reproduced on a number of radical right-wing sites. These in turn led to a welter of new videos now available on You Tube, which feature an odd cast of pseudo-experts regurgitating exactly the same line. The message is numbingly simplistic: all the ills of modern American culture, from feminism, affirmative action, sexual liberation and gay rights to the decay of traditional education and even environmentalism are ultimately attributable to the insidious influence of the members of the Institute for Social Research who came to America in the 1930's. The origins of "cultural Marxism" are traced back to
       <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Luk%C3%A1cs" title="Gyrgy Lukcs">
        Lukcs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">
        Gramsci
       </a>
       , but because they were not actual migrs, their role in the narrative is not as prominent."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jay_55-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jay-55">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         55
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/Chip_Berlet" title="Chip Berlet">
       Chip Berlet
      </a>
      , who specializes in the study of extreme
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-wing" title="Right-wing">
       right-wing movements
      </a>
      , the Cultural Marxism conspiracy theory found fertile ground with the development of the
      <a href="/wiki/Tea_Party_movement" title="Tea Party movement">
       Tea Party movement
      </a>
      in 2009, with contributions published in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/American_Thinker" title="American Thinker">
        American Thinker
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/WorldNetDaily" title="WorldNetDaily">
        WorldNetDaily
       </a>
      </i>
      highlighted by some Tea Party websites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collectivists_58-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Collectivists-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Philosopher and political science lecturer Jrme Jamin has stated "Next to the global dimension of the Cultural Marxism conspiracy theory, there is its innovative and original dimension, which lets its authors avoid racist discourses and pretend to be defenders of democracy."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAMIN_59-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-JAMIN-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
