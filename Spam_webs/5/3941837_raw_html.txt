            <article id="article-body" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Article" section="articleBody">
             <meta content="http://cnet2.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2011/10/18/9b4fc99b-f0f1-11e2-8c7c-d4ae52e62bcc/thumbnail/770x433/18667cb1db4f9c77a4641220f7e0626c/1_57.jpg" itemprop="thumbnailUrl"/>
             <meta content="Car Tech Video, Car Tech, Tech Culture" itemprop="keywords"/>
             <meta content="http://www.cnet.com/news/the-electric-dmc-12-time-travel-optional/" itemprop="url"/>
             <div class="article-main-body row" data-component="lazyloadImages" data-use-autolinker="true" itemprop="articleBody">
              <figure class="image image-large pull-none" section="shortcodeImage">
               <span class="imageContainer">
                <img alt="DeLorean DMC-12 EV" class="" itemprop="image" src="http://cnet1.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2011/10/18/9d152981-f0f1-11e2-8c7c-d4ae52e62bcc/resize/620x/4dd68de5c5c9489dc30279ada0bff38c/1_57.jpg"/>
               </span>
               <figcaption>
                <span class="caption">
                 Epic Electric Vehicles converted this DMC-12 to run on electricity.
                </span>
                <span class="credit">
                 DeLorean
                </span>
               </figcaption>
              </figure>
              <p>
               DeLorean Motor Company showed off a prototype of an electrically driven DMC-12, which it promises to put into production in 2013.
              </p>
              <p>
               You won't need a bolt of lightning to charge it up, and you won't be able to run it on eggshells and coffee grounds. Unlike the "Back to the Future" time machine, this DMC-12 electric conversion gets its electricity from the grid.
              </p>
              <p>
               The DeLorean Motor Company (yes, it still exists), showed off the prototype at an owners' event last week.
              </p>
              <!--pagebreak-->
              <p>
               <figure class="image image-medium pull-right" section="shortcodeImage">
                <a class="imageContainer" href="http://i.i.cbsi.com/cnwk.1d/i/tim/2011/10/18/3_34480078.jpg" target="_blank">
                 <img alt="DeLorean DMC-12 EV" class="lazy " data-original="    http://cnet3.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2011/10/18/da283021-fdc4-11e2-8c7c-d4ae52e62bcc/resize/370xauto/5380c55eaafe058ce4020b99be717bf2/3_34480078.jpg" height="auto" src="" width="370"/>
                 <noscript>
                  <img alt="DeLorean DMC-12 EV" class="" height="auto" itemprop="image" src="http://cnet3.cbsistatic.com/hub/i/r/2011/10/18/da283021-fdc4-11e2-8c7c-d4ae52e62bcc/resize/370xauto/5380c55eaafe058ce4020b99be717bf2/3_34480078.jpg" width="370"/>
                 </noscript>
                </a>
                <figcaption>
                 <span class="caption">
                  DeLorean updated the cabin of the DMC-12 EV with modern electronics.
                 </span>
                 <span class="credit">
                  DeLorean
                 </span>
                </figcaption>
               </figure>
              </p>
              <p>
               DeLorean currently maintains a parts warehouse and offers services to repair and restore existing DMC-12s. The company can even build a new DMC-12 using 80 percent original parts.
              </p>
              <p>
               Partnering with
               <a data-component="externalLink" href="http://www.evepic.com" target="_blank">
                Epic Electric Vehicles
               </a>
               , the company developed the prototype DMC-12 that, according to an
               <a data-component="externalLink" href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2011/10/18/idUS270028144720111018" target="_blank">
                article at Reuters
               </a>
               , has a range of 70 to 100 miles. A 125-horsepower electric motor drives the wheels to a top speed of 125 mph, well above the 88 mph needed to travel through time.
              </p>
