     <p>
      <b>
       New-age music
      </b>
      is a genre of music intended to create
      <a href="/wiki/Artistic_inspiration" title="Artistic inspiration">
       artistic inspiration
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Relaxation_technique" title="Relaxation technique">
       relaxation
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Optimism" title="Optimism">
       optimism
      </a>
      . It is used by listeners for
      <a href="/wiki/Yoga" title="Yoga">
       yoga
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Massage" title="Massage">
       massage
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">
       meditation
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicnewage_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-allmusicnewage-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Reading_(process)" title="Reading (process)">
       reading
      </a>
      as a method of
      <a href="/wiki/Stress_management" title="Stress management">
       stress management
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or to create a peaceful atmosphere in their home or other environments, and is associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">
       environmentalism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Age" title="New Age">
       New Age spirituality
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusicnewage_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-allmusicnewage-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmonies" title="Harmonies">
       harmonies
      </a>
      in new-age music include a
      <a href="/wiki/Drone_(music)" title="Drone (music)">
       drone
      </a>
      bass, and are often structured as variations on a theme. The music often contains recorded sounds of nature which are often used as an introduction to a track or throughout the piece.
     </p>
     <p>
      New age includes both
      <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">
       electronic
      </a>
      forms, frequently relying on sustained
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synth_pad" title="Synth pad">
       synth pads
      </a>
      or long
      <a href="/wiki/Music_sequencer" title="Music sequencer">
       sequencer
      </a>
      -based runs, and
      <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_music" title="Acoustic music">
       acoustic
      </a>
      forms, featuring instruments such as flutes, piano, acoustic guitar and a wide variety of non-western acoustic instruments. Vocal arrangements were initially rare in new age, but as it has evolved vocals have become more common, especially vocals featuring
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
       Native American
      </a>
      -,
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      -, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetan_Music" title="Tibetan Music">
       Tibetan
      </a>
      -influenced chants, or lyrics based on
      <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">
       mythology
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Celtic_mythology" title="Celtic mythology">
       Celtic legends
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      New-age music was influenced by a wide range of artists from a variety of genres. Irv Teibel's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environments_(series)" title="Environments (series)">
        Environments
       </a>
      </i>
      series (196979) featured natural
      <a href="/wiki/Soundscape" title="Soundscape">
       soundscapes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tintinnabulation" title="Tintinnabulation">
       tintinnabulation
      </a>
      , and "
      <a href="/wiki/Om" title="Om">
       Om
      </a>
      " chants and were some of the first publicly available
      <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">
       psychoacoustic
      </a>
      recordings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1973,
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Oldfield" title="Mike Oldfield">
       Mike Oldfield
      </a>
      's progressive rock album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tubular_Bells" title="Tubular Bells">
        Tubular Bells
       </a>
      </i>
      became one of the first albums to be referred to under the genre description of new age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birosik_1989_138_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Birosik_1989_138-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Halpern" title="Steven Halpern">
       Steven Halpern
      </a>
      's 1975
      <i>
       Spectrum Suite
      </i>
      was a key work that began the new-age music movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spectrum_Suite_.E2.80.93_Steven_Halpern_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Spectrum_Suite_.E2.80.93_Steven_Halpern-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 1989, there were over 150 small independent record labels releasing new-age music.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New-age_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      New-age music was influenced by a wide range of artists from a variety of genresfor example, folk-instrumentalists
      <a href="/wiki/John_Fahey_(musician)" title="John Fahey (musician)">
       John Fahey
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Leo_Kottke" title="Leo Kottke">
       Leo Kottke
      </a>
      , minimalists
      <a href="/wiki/Terry_Riley" title="Terry Riley">
       Terry Riley
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Reich" title="Steve Reich">
       Steve Reich
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/La_Monte_Young" title="La Monte Young">
       La Monte Young
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_Glass" title="Philip Glass">
       Philip Glass
      </a>
      , synthesizer performers
      <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">
       Pink Floyd
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">
       Brian Eno
      </a>
      , and impressionistic jazz artists
      <a href="/wiki/Keith_Jarrett" title="Keith Jarrett">
       Keith Jarrett
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Horn_(musician)" title="Paul Horn (musician)">
       Paul Horn
      </a>
      (beginning with 1968's
      <a href="/wiki/Inside_(Paul_Horn_album)" title="Inside (Paul Horn album)">
       <i>
        Inside
       </i>
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Pat_Metheny" title="Pat Metheny">
       Pat Metheny
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motherjones_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-motherjones-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many different styles and combinations of electronic, experimental and acoustic new age were introduced in the 1970s including music from Asia, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitaro" title="Kitaro">
       Kitaro
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Magic_Orchestra" title="Yellow Magic Orchestra">
       Yellow Magic Orchestra
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:153px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kitaro_5.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="1713" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Kitaro_5.jpg/151px-Kitaro_5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Kitaro_5.jpg/227px-Kitaro_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Kitaro_5.jpg/302px-Kitaro_5.jpg 2x" width="151"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kitaro_5.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Kitar%C5%8D" title="Kitar">
         Kitaro
        </a>
        , one of the most influential electronic/new-age artists in Asia
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Halpern" title="Steven Halpern">
       Steven Halpern
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Spectrum Suite
      </i>
      , released in 1975, is generally credited as the album that began the new-age music movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spectrum_Suite_.E2.80.93_Steven_Halpern_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Spectrum_Suite_.E2.80.93_Steven_Halpern-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      New age was initially produced and sold only by independent labels. The sales reached significant numbers in unusual outlets such as bookstores, gift stores, health-food stores and boutiques, as well as direct mail. Another prominent example of an early new-age album was when in 1979,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%26B_music" title="R&amp;B music">
       R&amp;B musician
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">
       Stevie Wonder
      </a>
      created the
      <a href="/wiki/Soundtrack_album" title="Soundtrack album">
       soundtrack
      </a>
      for the documentary (
      <a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Life_of_Plants" title="The Secret Life of Plants">
       based on the book
      </a>
      )
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journey_Through_the_Secret_Life_of_Plants" title="Journey Through the Secret Life of Plants">
       The Secret Life of Plants
      </a>
      , which in turn was the first
      <a href="/wiki/Digital_recording" title="Digital recording">
       digital recording
      </a>
      of a new-age album. In 1981,
      <a href="/wiki/Tower_Records" title="Tower Records">
       Tower Records
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">
       Mountain View, California
      </a>
      added a "new age" bin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard1986_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-billboard1986-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By 1985, independent and chain record retail stores were adding sections for new age, and major labels began showing interest in the genre, both through acquisition of some existing new-age labels such as
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Winter" title="Paul Winter">
       Paul Winter
      </a>
      's Living Music and through signing of new-age artists such as Kitaro and jazz crossover artist Pat Metheny, both signed by Geffen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billboard1986_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-billboard1986-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On Valentine's Day in 1987, the former Los Angeles rock radio station KMET changed to a full-time new-age-music format with new call letters
      <a href="/wiki/KTWV" title="KTWV">
       KTWV
      </a>
      , branded as
      <i>
       The Wave
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balfe_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Balfe-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During The Wave's new-age period, management told the station employees to refer to The Wave as a "mood service" rather than a "radio station". DJs stopped announcing the titles of the songs, and instead, to maintain an uninterrupted mood, listeners could call a 1-800 phone number to find out what song was playing. News breaks were also re-branded and referred to as "wave breaks".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balfe_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Balfe-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Stations in other cities followed this lead
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">
         <span title="1983 precedes 1987. Please help to fix this. (September 2013)">
          contradiction
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and in 1983,
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hill_(broadcaster)" title="Stephen Hill (broadcaster)">
       Stephen Hill
      </a>
      's radio show
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hearts_of_Space" title="Hearts of Space">
        Music From The Hearts of Space
       </a>
      </i>
      was picked up by
      <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">
       NPR
      </a>
      for syndication to 230 affiliates nationally.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balfe_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Balfe-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other new-age-specialty radio programs included Forest's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Musical_Starstreams" title="Musical Starstreams">
        Musical Starstreams
       </a>
      </i>
      and John Diliberto's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Echoes_(radio_program)" title="Echoes (radio program)">
        Echoes
       </a>
      </i>
      . Most major
      <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">
       cable television
      </a>
      networks have channels that play music without visuals, including channels for new age, such as the "Soundscapes" channel on
      <a href="/wiki/Music_Choice" title="Music Choice">
       Music Choice
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      By 1989, there were over 150 small independent record labels releasing new-age music, while new-age and adult-alternative programs were carried on hundreds of commercial and college radio stations in the U.S., and over 40 distributors were selling new-age music through mail-order catalogs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pjyoga_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pjyoga-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
       Definitions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New-age_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="295" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg/150px-Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg/225px-Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6d/Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vangelis_HonoraryDoct.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Works of Greek composer
        <a href="/wiki/Vangelis" title="Vangelis">
         Vangelis
        </a>
        , noted for his film scores, exemplify the progressive side of new-age music
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          10
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      New-age music is defined more by the effect or feeling it produces rather than the instruments used in its creation; it may be electronic, acoustic, or a mixture of both. New-age artists range from solo or ensemble performances using
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">
       classical-music
      </a>
      instruments ranging from the piano, acoustic guitar, flute or harp to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_musical_instruments" title="Electronic musical instruments">
       electronic musical instruments
      </a>
      , or from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_music" title="Eastern music">
       Eastern instruments
      </a>
      such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Sitar" title="Sitar">
       sitar
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tabla" title="Tabla">
       tabla
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanpura_(instrument)" title="Tanpura (instrument)">
       tamboura
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a significant overlap of sectors of new-age music,
      <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">
       ambient music
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">
       classical music
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
       jazz
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">
       electronica
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">
       world music
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chill_out_(music)" title="Chill out (music)">
       chillout
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Space_music" title="Space music">
       space music
      </a>
      and others. The two definitions typically associated with the new-age genre are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       New-age music with an ambient sound that has the explicit purpose of aiding meditation and relaxation, or aiding and enabling various alternative spiritual practices, such as alternative healing, yoga practice, guided meditation,
       <a href="/wiki/Chakra" title="Chakra">
        chakra
       </a>
       auditing, and so on. The proponents of this definition are almost always musicians who create their music expressly for these purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAV_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NAV-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Prominent artists who create new-age music expressly for healing and/or meditation include
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Horn_(musician)" title="Paul Horn (musician)">
        Paul Horn
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Deuter" title="Deuter">
        Deuter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Steven_Halpern" title="Steven Halpern">
        Steven Halpern
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dean_Evenson" title="Dean Evenson">
        Dean Evenson
       </a>
       who in 1979 was one of the first to combine his peaceful flute music with the sounds of nature,
       <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Ball" title="Lawrence Ball">
        Lawrence Ball
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Karunesh" title="Karunesh">
        Karunesh
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Music which is found in the new-age sections of record stores.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAV_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NAV-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is largely a definition of practicality, given the breadth of music that is classified as "new age" by retailers who are often less interested in finely-grained distinctions between musical styles than are fans of those styles. Music which falls into this definition is usually music which cannot be easily classified into other, more common definitions, but often includes well-defined music such as
       <a href="/wiki/Worldbeat" title="Worldbeat">
        worldbeat
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">
        Flamenco
       </a>
       guitar. It also includes expressly spiritual new-age music as a subset. There are retail outlets, that specify subcategories such as "nature sounds", "healing", "piano", "chants", and so on.
       <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">
        Amazon
       </a>
       includes also "Celtic", "meditation", "world-dance", and "relaxation", and
       <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">
        iTunes Store
       </a>
       includes "nature", "environmental", "healing", and "travel", besides other subgenres. The German new-age site Silenzio lists almost 70 subgenres.
      </li>
     </ul>

        Irish singer/composer
        <a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">
         Enya
        </a>
        received four
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards">
         Grammy Awards
        </a>
        for Best New Age Album
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      From 1968 to 1973, German musicians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Froese" title="Edgar Froese">
       Edgar Froese
      </a>
      (founder of
      <a href="/wiki/Tangerine_Dream" title="Tangerine Dream">
       Tangerine Dream
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Holger_Czukay" title="Holger Czukay">
       Holger Czukay
      </a>
      (one of the founders of
      <a href="/wiki/Can_(band)" title="Can (band)">
       Can
      </a>
      and a former student of
      <a href="/wiki/Karlheinz_Stockhausen" title="Karlheinz Stockhausen">
       Karlheinz Stockhausen
      </a>
      ),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh_(German_band)" title="Popol Vuh (German band)">
       Popol Vuh
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ash_Ra_Tempel" title="Ash Ra Tempel">
       Ashra
      </a>
      released a number of works featuring experimental sounds and textures built with electronics, synthesizers, acoustic and electric instruments which were referred to as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosmische_musik" title="Kosmische musik">
       cosmic music
      </a>
      . This experimentation provided early foundational influences for the
      <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">
       ambient music
      </a>
      and new-age music genres. In the late 1970s
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">
       Brian Eno
      </a>
      's defining explorations in ambient music further influenced the formation of the new-age-music genre, as developed in the styles of musicians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fripp" title="Robert Fripp">
       Robert Fripp
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jon_Hassell" title="Jon Hassell">
       Jon Hassell
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Laraaji" title="Laraaji">
       Laraaji
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Harold_Budd" title="Harold Budd">
       Harold Budd
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cluster_(band)" title="Cluster (band)">
       Cluster
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jah_Wobble" title="Jah Wobble">
       Jah Wobble
      </a>
      (of
      <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">
       post-punk
      </a>
      band
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Image_Limited" title="Public Image Limited">
       Public Image Limited
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1973,
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Oldfield" title="Mike Oldfield">
       Mike Oldfield
      </a>
      's unconventional progressive rock album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tubular_Bells" title="Tubular Bells">
        Tubular Bells
       </a>
      </i>
      became one of the first albums to be referred to under the genre description of new age.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birosik_1989_138_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Birosik_1989_138-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other influences are early electronic music,
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">
       classical music
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_music" title="Ethnic music">
       ethnic music
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">
       world music
      </a>
      . The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minimalist_music" title="Minimalist music">
       minimalism
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Terry_Riley" title="Terry Riley">
       Terry Riley
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Reich" title="Steve Reich">
       Steve Reich
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       Indian
      </a>
      influenced in the former case) can also be cited as an influence, along with artists like
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Conrad" title="Tony Conrad">
       Tony Conrad
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/La_Monte_Young" title="La Monte Young">
       La Monte Young
      </a>
      who utilized drones since the early 1960s. Connected to the creation of new-age music is the resurgence of interest in
      <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_chant" title="Gregorian chant">
       Gregorian chant
      </a>
      during the second half of the 20th century. Now, new-age music has branched out and also includes chanting of "spiritual" or ancient languages, and includes, but is not limited to Sanskrit, Latin, Gaelic, Hebrew and Gurmukhi. Popular artists in this genre include
      <a href="/wiki/Krishna_Das_(singer)" title="Krishna Das (singer)">
       Krishna Das
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Deva_Premal" title="Deva Premal">
       Deva Premal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bhagavan_Das_(yogi)" title="Bhagavan Das (yogi)">
       Bhagavan Das
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Snatam_Kaur" title="Snatam Kaur">
       Snatam Kaur
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The solo
      <a href="/wiki/ECM_Records" title="ECM Records">
       ECM
      </a>
      performances by artists like
      <a href="/wiki/Keith_Jarrett" title="Keith Jarrett">
       Keith Jarrett
      </a>
      (especially his record
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_K%C3%B6ln_Concert" title="The Kln Concert">
        The Kln Concert
       </a>
      </i>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Towner" title="Ralph Towner">
       Ralph Towner
      </a>
      (especially his records
      <i>
       Blue Sun
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Solo Concert
      </i>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Mays" title="Lyle Mays">
       Lyle Mays
      </a>
      's first eponymous album, are usually thought to be an influence on ambient/new-age music. The acoustic solo and group performances by the early
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windham_Hill" title="Windham Hill">
       Windham Hill
      </a>
      artists such as
      <a href="/wiki/William_Ackerman" title="William Ackerman">
       William Ackerman
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alex_de_Grassi" title="Alex de Grassi">
       Alex de Grassi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/George_Winston" title="George Winston">
       George Winston
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Hedges" title="Michael Hedges">
       Michael Hedges
      </a>
      were called
      <i>
       new age
      </i>
      for much of the last 30 years.
     </p>
     <p>
      Popular themes in new-age music include space and the cosmos, environment and nature, wellness in being, harmony with one's self and the world,
      <a href="/wiki/Dream" title="Dream">
       dreams
      </a>
      or dreaming and journeys of the mind or spirit. Titles of new-age albums and songs are frequently descriptive: examples include
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_Moons" title="Shepherd Moons">
        Shepherd Moons
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">
       Enya
      </a>
      ),
      <i>
       Straight' a Way to Orion
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kitaro" title="Kitaro">
       Kitaro
      </a>
      ),
      <i>
       Touching the Clouds
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis_(musical_ensemble)" title="Symbiosis (musical ensemble)">
       Symbiosis
      </a>
      ), and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/One_Deep_Breath" title="One Deep Breath">
        One Deep Breath
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Bradley_Joseph" title="Bradley Joseph">
       Bradley Joseph
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_terms">
       Alternative terms
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New-age_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternative terms">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:YanniPressPhoto.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="2584" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/YanniPressPhoto.jpg/200px-YanniPressPhoto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/YanniPressPhoto.jpg/300px-YanniPressPhoto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/YanniPressPhoto.jpg/400px-YanniPressPhoto.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:YanniPressPhoto.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Greek musician and composer
        <a href="/wiki/Yanni" title="Yanni">
         Yanni
        </a>
        has had several albums reach #1 on the
        <i>
         Billboard
        </i>
        new-age rankings.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      As described in this article, the borders of this genre are not well defined; however music retail stores will include artists in the "new age" category even if the artists themselves use different names for their style of music. Here are some other terms used instead of "new age".
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_instrumental">
       Contemporary instrumental
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New-age_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contemporary instrumental">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The term "contemporary instrumental" can include artists that do not use electronic instruments in their music, such as solo pianist
      <a href="/wiki/David_Lanz" title="David Lanz">
       David Lanz
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, pianists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Yanni" title="Yanni">
       Yanni
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yanni_in_Words_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Yanni_in_Words-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bradley_Joseph" title="Bradley Joseph">
       Bradley Joseph
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indie_Journal_Interview_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Indie_Journal_Interview-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      both use this term as well, although they use
      <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_instrument" title="Keyboard instrument">
       keyboards
      </a>
      to incorporate layered
      <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">
       orchestral
      </a>
      textures into their compositions. Yanni has distinguished the music genre from the spiritual movement bearing the same name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CourierJournal20120426_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CourierJournal20120426-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_adult_instrumental">
       Contemporary adult instrumental
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New-age_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Contemporary adult instrumental">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This term was suggested by Steven Halpern in the June 1999 issue of
      <i>
       New Age Voice
      </i>
      as an alternative catch-all label for music which is classified by retailers as "new age", but which is not expressly spiritual in nature.
     </p>
