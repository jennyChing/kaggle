        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         Spicy Thai Noodles (Pad Kee Mao) (Drunken Noodles)
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2014-07-20T17:56:31+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          July 20, 2014
         </time>
         <span class="entry-comments-link">
          <a href="http://www.recipetineats.com/pad-kee-mao-thai-drunken-noodles/#comments">
           15 Comments
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <div class="ssbp-set--one ssbp--state-hidden ssbp-wrap ssbp-- ssbp--theme-1" data-ssbp-counts="true">

         <div class="ssbp-container" data-ssbp-post-id="3590" data-ssbp-share-text="" data-ssbp-short-url="" data-ssbp-title="Spicy Thai Noodles (Pad Kee Mao) (Drunken Noodles)" data-ssbp-url="http://www.recipetineats.com/pad-kee-mao-thai-drunken-noodles/">
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         <em>
          The literal translation of Pad Kee Mao is Drunken Noodles becausethe theory is that these spicy noodles are perfectly accompanied with an ice cold beer and also that they are a great cure for hangover. I can confirm both cases to be true!
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Authentic recipe for this popular Thai street food, made with everyday ingredients. #thai #stir_fry #noodles #fast" class="alignnone wp-image-3598 size-full" height="1024" src="http://recipetineats.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Thai-Drunken-Noodles-1.jpg" width="680"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Get these noodles from the streets of Thailand and unless you have an exceptional spice-o-meter, youll be chugging down the beer in an attempt to cool the burn in your mouth. Make this at home and you can control the heat! The amount of chilli Ive included in the recipe is mild enough for most people (I think), but enough so you can taste the heat. By all means, feel free to turn up the spice dial!
        </p>
        <p>
         There are all sorts of variations of Pad Kee Mao in Thailand and even more in the western world.In Thailand the two constants arechicken and Thai Basil, and quite often it came with baby corn as well, though from my research I couldnt confirm that this was a must have in this dish. Thai Basil is different to ordinary sweet / Italian basil, it has a mild aniseed and mint flavour. Die-hard Thai purists will tell you that you must use Thai Basil or Thai Holy Basil to make this recipe, otherwise dont bother. I say that its not a make or break. Whileyou can definitely taste the difference if you eat the leaves plain, once stir fried with the other ingredients and the sauce, I bet most people wouldnt be able to tell whether its ordinary or Thai Basil.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Authentic recipe for this popular Thai street food, made with everyday ingredients. #thai #stir_fry #noodles #fast" class="alignnone wp-image-3599 size-full" height="1024" src="http://recipetineats.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Thai-Drunken-Noodles-2.jpg" width="680"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         In Western restaurants these noodles are usually very saucy, oily and salty  too much so in my opinion, and very different from the streets of Thailand. The sauce of this recipe is lighter in colour and not as sweet asthat of
         <a href="http://recipetineats.com/thai-stir-fried-noodles-pad-see-ew/" title="Thai Stir Fried Noodles (Pad See Ew)">
          Pad See Ew
         </a>
         (Thai Stir Fried Noodles), one of the mostpopular recipeson my blog.This recipe is also faster and easier to make because there are less ingredients.I often throw vegetables into it  like sliced carrots, cabbage, snow peas and Chinese greens  to make a complete one wok meal.
        </p>
        <p>
         Make sure you prepare all the ingredients, ready to throw into the wok becauseit only takes a few minutes to cook and you dont want to be scrambling around the kitchen. And remember tocrack open an ice cold beer to enjoy these noodles with!
         <br/>
        </p>

         <div class="ERSSummary" itemprop="description">
          The literal translation of Pad Kee Mao is "Drunken Noodles" because the theory is that these spicy noodles are perfectly accompanied with an ice cold beer and also that they are a great cure for hangover. This is a popular Thai takeout dish but it's much healthier and just as tasty made at home. If you throw in Asian greens like Chinese broccoli or bok choy, you can turn this into a one-wok meal. Make sure you have all ingredients ready to toss into the wok as once you start cooking, things happen quickly!
         </div>
         <div class="divERSHeadItems">
          <div class="ERSAuthor">
           Author:
           <span itemprop="author">
            Nagi | RecipeTin Eats
           </span>
          </div>
          <div class="ERSCategory">
           Recipe type:
           <span itemprop="recipeCategory">
            Noodles, Stir Fries
           </span>
          </div>
          <div class="ERSCuisine">
           Cuisine:
           <span itemprop="recipeCuisine">
            Asian, Thai
           </span>
          </div>
          <div class="ERSServes">
           Serves:
           <span itemprop="recipeYield">
            2-3
           </span>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="ERSIngredients">
          <div class="ERSIngredientsHeader ERSHeading">
           Ingredients
          </div>
          <div class="ERSSectionHead">
           Noodles
          </div>
          <ul>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            7oz/200g pad thai noodles, dried (see notes)
           </li>
          </ul>
          <div class="ERSSectionHead">
           Stir Fry
          </div>
          <ul>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            200g/10oz chicken thigh fillet, cut into bite size pieces (2 small or 1 normal size fillets)
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 cup basil leaves (packed), preferably Thai holy basil but normal basil is fine too
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            2 shallot/scallion stems, cut into 3cm/2" pieces
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            3 large cloves of garlic, minced
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 birds eye chilli, deseeded (or to taste), very finely chopped
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            2 tbsp oil (peanut, vegetable or canola)
           </li>
          </ul>
          <div class="ERSSectionHead">
           Sauce
          </div>
          <ul>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            3 tbsp oyster sauce
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 tbsp light soy sauce (see notes)
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            1 tbsp (normal dark soy sauce)
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            2 tsp sugar
           </li>
           <li class="ingredient" itemprop="ingredients">
            2 tbsp water
           </li>
          </ul>
          <div class="ERSClear">
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="ERSInstructions">
          <div class="ERSInstructionsHeader ERSHeading">
           Instructions
          </div>
          <ol>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Pour boiling water over noodles in a large bowl (or pot) and set aside for 5 minutes (or as per packet instructions), and drain when ready.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Put Sauce ingredients in a small bowl and mix to combine.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Heat oil in wok or pan over high heat.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Add garlic and chilli and cook for 10 seconds. Don't inhale - the chilli will make you cough!
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Add chicken and fry until cooked, around 2 minutes.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Add the shallots/scallions and about 1 tbsp of the sauce and stir fry for 30 seconds, just to coat the chicken.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Add the noodles and sauce and cook for 1 minute until the water evaporates and the sauce has coated the noodles.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            If your noodles absorbs all the sauce liquid very quickly, this is probably because your noodles were a bit underdone. If this occurs, just add a splash of water (preferably hot) and this not only helps finish cooking the noodles but also revitalises the sauce.
           </li>
           <li class="instruction" itemprop="recipeInstructions">
            Remove from heat and immediately add basil leaves, stir until just wilted, then serve immediately.
           </li>
          </ol>
          <div class="ERSClear">
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="ERSNotesDiv">
          <div class="ERSNotesHeader">
           Notes
          </div>
          <div class="ERSNotes">
           1. Light soy sauce is lighter in colour that the normal dark soy sauce but it is actually saltier. If you don't have/can't find light soy sauce, you can substitute with normal soy sauce but the colour of your noodles will be slightly darker and the flavour slightly less salty.
           <br/>
           2. If you can't find dried Pad Thai noodles, you can use any other wide flat rice noodles or even egg noodles, dried or fresh. However, I don't recommend making this with vermicelli noodles (the very thin rice noodles) - it clumps together and you can't stir fry it to disperse the sauce evenly throughout (I tried it once. Disaster.)
          </div>
          <div class="ERSClear">
