      <h2>
       Military Fact: Renewable Energy Saves Lives
      </h2>
      <div style="font-size:15px; border-top:1px dashed gray; border-bottom:1px dashed gray; padding:5px;">
       <div style="float:right; height:18px; margin-top:3px;">

      <p>
       U.S. Senator Jim Inhofe is an asshole.
      </p>
      <p>
       Why?
      </p>
      <p>
       Because in an effort to play politics as usual, he's second-guessing our military's top brass.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the U.S. military is seeking to integrate more renewable energy into its operations  in an effort to help save lives and save taxpayer dollars, Inhofe wants Congress to enact legislation that would make it impossible for the U.S. military to purchase alternative fuels if they cost a penny more than gasoline and diesel.
      </p>
      <p>
       Question is, while the military was creating and perfecting the Internet  which wasn't cheap  would Inhofe made a ruckus?  Not a chance.  You know why?  Because the fossil fuel interests that line his campaign coffers had no interest in slowing the development of the Internet.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bottom line: Inhofe is a scumbag politician that's playing political theatre while having zero regard for the lives of our soldiers.  This is unacceptable.  Especially when we know for a fact that our nation's military leaders  uniformed and civilian  believe that using greater amounts of renewable energy saves lives and money, increases operational effectiveness and enhances the nation's energy security by reducing dependence on foreign oil.
      </p>
      <p>
       In another fact check published by the American Council on Renewable Energy we find the following. . .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <p>
         The military has several good 	reasons to move away from petroleum-based fuels:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <p>
             One out of eight U.S. Army 			casualties in Iraq (2003-2007) resulted from protecting fuel 			convoys.
            </p>
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             The Department of Defense is the 			world's largest single consumer of oil and has a goal of getting 			25% of its energy from renewable sources by 2025.
            </p>
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             Because it does not have 			alternatives to oil, the DOD spent $15 billion on petroleum-based 			fuel for military operations in 2011.
            </p>
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             It costs the Department of 			Defense an additional $1.3 billion  nearly equal to the 			Marines' entire procurement budget  every time oil prices rise 			by $10.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <p>
         Since 2010, nine different U.S. 	Navy vessels and aircraft have been successfully powered by 	advanced, domestic biofuels, including the super-sonic biofuel 	flight of the F/A-18 Green Hornet, the MH-60S Seahawk, the 	MV-22 Osprey, the T-45 Goshawk, AV-8B Hariier, the Fire Scout 	unmanned vehicle, the Riverine Command Boat (RCB-X), the USS Paul F. 	Foster destroyer and the USS Ford frigate.
        </p>
       </li>
       <li>
        <p>
         Nevada's Nellis Air Force Base has 	a 14 MW solar facility, which supplies more than 25% of the power 	used at the base.
        </p>
       </li>
       <li>
        <p>
         7,000megawatts of additional solar 	energy can be generated on military installation rooftops in the 	Mojave Desert, according to a DOD analysis.
        </p>
       </li>
      </ul>
