        Home Design Software: An Integrated Workflow For Architects
       </h1>
       <div class="clear">
       </div>

       <h2 id="page_description" itemprop="description">
        Home design has morphed into something digital over the years. Here is a good quick workflow for architectural designs.
       </h2>
       <div class="clear">
       </div>
       <div style="margin:30px 0 35px">
        <ins class="adsbygoogle " data-ad-client="pub-1119802406799739" data-ad-format="auto" data-ad-type="" data-color-link="0078ca" data-color-text="444444" data-color-url="777777" data-font-face="arial,verdana" style="display:block">
        </ins>
        <script>
         (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({ params: {google_ad_channel: ""} });

       <div class="text" style="text-align:justify;margin:15px 0 0 3px">
        <p>
         There are tens of design software on the market today, all promising faster and better results. It can be difficult to decide which one to settle for if you are new to the whole area. But, a few have been on the throne for a long time now and for a good reason. It is hard for new ones to get the attention of designers even though they might be better. Anyway, in this article, allow me to introduce you to my own home design workflow, and the software I use from start to finish. This will only focus on the actual creation, and not the design phase. The home design software I use are as follows: Revit, 3Ds Max and finally AutoCad.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>

      <div class="module module_fullsize" id="module_316431">
       <div class="no_module_color">
        <h3>
         Starting Out in Revit
        </h3>
        <h5>
         Revit Architecture promises a user friendly interface, with an easy learning curve, but it has its own limitations.
        </h5>
        <div class="text float_images clearfix">
         <p>
          After all the planning and having made sketches of my new project, I fire up Revit and start laying the walls. Revit is a BIM software developed in 1999. It allows users to work with their building directly in 3Dwhile at the same time producing2D views. It is actually a one stop design software, meaning it will do yourconstruction drawings and your 3Ds as well, but it has limitations and if things are to be professional, I choose to use it in collaboration with other software. The beauty about Revit is that once you add a window in your plan view, it shows up in all the views- elevations, sections, and even in schedules. The same applies to doors, roofs, and even plumbing fittings. There is a certain intelligence to it all and it will not do things that won't be done in real-life. This can sometimes be a hindrance if it goes too far.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once I have added everything that needs to be done- walls, doors, roofs, plumbing, floors, curtains and other additions, I proceed to add the landscape. The vegetationin Revit is not all that good so I leave it out except for grass. I then add all colours, texture and patterns to the surfaces and do a test render to see how the building will look once done. If I am pleased, I proceed to the next phase with 3Ds Max.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>

       <div class="no_module_color">
        <h3>
         Phase II : Exporting into 3Ds Max.
        </h3>
        <h5>
         Once the 3D is done, I move to 3Ds Max to finish up the rendering.
        </h5>
        <div class="text float_images clearfix">
         <p>
          Revit is not really good for rendering so for better results, I transfer my work into 3Ds Max. This has to be done after all the essential modelling has been done, especially the ones that will show up in the construction drawings. 3Ds max has a thorough and more advanced modelling process. There is no end to what you can model with it, but it is a bit complicated. It is an advanced software used in the graphic design, architectural, and animation industry.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are what are called rendering engines within Revit and 3Ds max. The beauty is Revit has a rendering engine called Mental Ray, which is also found in 3Ds max. However, 3Ds Max also popularly uses two others called Iray and Vray. If you want things to be seamless, then use mental ray in 3Ds max.
          <span style="line-height: 21px;">
           There is a tool within Revit that enables you to make the transfer into Max. You will move all the data that is in your Revit project including sizes, colours and textures. This also extends to lights, sky, sun and cameras.
          </span>
          For better results, I choose to add sun and sky from 3Ds max though. Once this is done, I adjust the exposure controller to control the brightness of the render and finally press 'render'.
         </p>
         <p>
          The render is almost always OK and the next step is to adjust the sharpness, add vegetation, cars, people, and even animals like dogs to make thingslook more realistic. For this, I use Adobe Photoshop.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="module module_halfsize" id="module_316433">
       <div class="no_module_color">
        <h3>
         Phase III : Touch Ups in Photoshop
        </h3>
        <h5>
         You don't need any pro-skill for starters, just basic cuting and pasting.
        </h5>
        <div class="text float_images clearfix">
         <p>
          Photoshop is like one of the top software on the graphic design market. It can do anything a graphic designer would want to do. I find it usable by people of any skill level. What I do is search for free cutouts of people, trees, cars and animals like dogs, then paste them into my rendering. You can find them for sale or just free online. I use the free ones for now. Sometimes the lighting on the cutout is different from the rendering so you need to tweak that up.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once that's done, the 3D is ready to be given to the client and I proceed with the construction drawings. I could use Revit for this, but I find it easier to add details using AutoCad.
         </p>
        </div>

       <div class="no_module_color">
        <h3>
         Phase IV: Construction Drawings in AutoCad
        </h3>
        <h5>
         Revit can be used for construction drawings as well, but I prefer AutoCad as I find it easier to detail my drawings.
        </h5>
        <div class="text float_images clearfix">
         <p>
          I have used Revit for construction drawings a few times before, but those are too few to mention. AutoCad is the industry standard for construction drawings. I think it is a bit harder than Revit but for basic draughting, it is OK.
         </p>
         <p>
          For my workflow, I go back to Revit and export mywork to DWG format. There is a plugin for this within Revit. After opening the drawing in AutoCad, I will adjust line thicknesses, line colours and hatchings. The dimensions are inserted within Revit and all I do is change their colours and type. With my title blocks done, all that remains is exporting the drawing to PDF. I then send the client an email or print the drawings myself then deliver.
         </p>
         <p>
          I enjoy modelling three Ds more than any stage in the whole process, and hate the cad part most. But, all in all, I like my workflow.
         </p>
