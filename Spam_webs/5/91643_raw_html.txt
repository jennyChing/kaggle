     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Forbidden Games
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
       <i>
        Jeux interdits
       </i>
      </span>
      ), is a 1952 French
      <a href="/wiki/War_film" title="War film">
       war
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">
       drama film
      </a>
      directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Cl%C3%A9ment" title="Ren Clment">
       Ren Clment
      </a>
      and based on
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Boyer" title="Franois Boyer">
       Franois Boyer
      </a>
      's novel,
      <i>
       Jeux interdits
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      While not initially successful in France, the film was a hit elsewhere. It won the
      <a href="/wiki/Golden_Lion" title="Golden Lion">
       Golden Lion
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">
       Venice Film Festival
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Honorary_Award" title="Academy Honorary Award">
       Special Award
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">
       Best Foreign Language Film
      </a>
      in the United States, and a
      <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film">
       Best Film from any Source
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="British Academy Film Awards">
       British Academy Film Awards
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forbidden_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      It is June 1940, during the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">
       Battle of France
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CritCol_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CritCol-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After five-year-old Paulette's parents and pet dog die in a German air attack on a column of refugees fleeing
      <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
       Paris
      </a>
      , the traumatized child meets 10-year-old Michel Doll whose peasant family takes her in.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CritCol_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CritCol-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She quickly becomes attached to Michel. The two attempt to cope with the death and destruction that surrounds them by secretly building a small cemetery among the ruins of an abandoned watermill,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CritCol_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CritCol-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where they bury her dog and start to bury other animals, marking their graves with crosses stolen from a local graveyard, including one belonging to Michel's brother. Michel's father first suspects that Michel's brother's cross was stolen from the graveyard by his neighbour. Eventually, the father finds out that Michel has stolen the cross.
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile, the French gendarmes come to the Doll household in order to take Paulette. Michel cannot bear the thought of her leaving and tells his father that he would tell him where the stolen crosses are, but in return he should not give Paulette to the gendarmes. His father doesn't keep his promise: Michel destroys the crosses and Paulette ends up going to a Red Cross camp, but at the end of the movie is seen running away into a crowd of people in the Red Cross camp, crying for Michel and then for her mother.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
       Reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forbidden_Games&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The film was widely praised among critics, whose "howling protests" were heard at the
      <a href="/wiki/1952_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="1952 Cannes Film Festival">
       1952 Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      where it was not an "official entry of France";
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt52_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt52-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      instead, it was "screened on the fringe of the Competition."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The film was entered into competition at the
      <a href="/wiki/13th_Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="13th Venice International Film Festival">
       13th Venice International Film Festival
      </a>
      ; festival organizers at first considered the film ineligible because it had been screened at Cannes;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt52_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt52-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it ended up receiving the
      <a href="/wiki/Golden_Lion" title="Golden Lion">
       Golden Lion
      </a>
      , the Festival's highest prize.
     </p>
     <p>
      Upon its release, it was lambasted by some, who said it was a "vicious and unfair picture of the
      <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">
       peasantry
      </a>
      of France";
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt52_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt52-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in France, 4,910,835 theater tickets were sold.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPBox_Office_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-JPBox_Office-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following its December 1952 release in the United States,
      <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">
       Bosley Crowther
      </a>
      called it a film with "the irony of a
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Illusion_(film)" title="Grand Illusion (film)">
        Grand Illusion
       </a>
      </i>
      , the authenticity of a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Random_Harvest" title="Random Harvest">
        Harvest
       </a>
      </i>
      and the finesse of French films at their best"; according to Crowther, the film is a "brilliant and devastating drama of the tragic frailties of men, clear and uncorrupted by sentimentality or dogmatism in its candid view of life."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt52_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt52-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the
      <a href="/wiki/25th_Academy_Awards" title="25th Academy Awards">
       25th Academy Awards
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Forbidden Games
      </i>
      won an out-of-competition
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Honorary_Award" title="Academy Honorary Award">
       Special Award
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Foreign_Language_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film">
       Best Foreign Language Film
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CritCol_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-CritCol-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In December 1952, at the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Board_of_Review_Awards_1952" title="National Board of Review Awards 1952">
       24th National Board of Review Awards
      </a>
      it was chosen as one of that year's five top foreign films. At the
      <a href="/wiki/1952_New_York_Film_Critics_Circle_Awards" title="1952 New York Film Critics Circle Awards">
       1952 New York Film Critics Circle Awards
      </a>
      , it won for Best Foreign Language Film.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1954, it was BAFTA's
      <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Film" title="BAFTA Award for Best Film">
       Best Film from any Source
      </a>
      ; in 1955, at the
      <a href="/wiki/27th_Academy_Awards" title="27th Academy Awards">
       27th Academy Awards
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Boyer" title="Franois Boyer">
       Franois Boyer
      </a>
      was nominated for an
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Story" title="Academy Award for Best Story">
       Academy Award for Best Story
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Philip_Yordan" title="Philip Yordan">
       Philip Yordan
      </a>
      won, for his work on
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Broken_Lance" title="Broken Lance">
        Broken Lance
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Decades after its release,
      <a href="/wiki/David_Ehrenstein" title="David Ehrenstein">
       David Ehrenstein
      </a>
      called it "deeply touching" and wrote: "
      <a href="/wiki/Brigitte_Fossey" title="Brigitte Fossey">
       Fossey's
      </a>
      is quite simply one of the most uncanny pieces of acting ever attempted by a youngster. Clments sensitivity doubtless accounts for much of what we see here, but the rest is clearly Fosseys own."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CritCol_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-CritCol-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
