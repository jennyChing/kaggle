                            Ahh, this gives me the opportunity to showcase my favourite food. The humble bean. Far and away, the bean, be it, black, october, navy, whatever, is the ultimate inexpensive superfood. It contains scads of fiber, good protein, no sugars, and no fat. And dried beans are cheap, cheap, cheap.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Some great ways to prepare:
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in_feed" master_h="302" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-0734d78be29bb4ff0c05a54049e4ead4?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="381" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-0734d78be29bb4ff0c05a54049e4ead4?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            Black Bean Soup (Use a low-fat cream of celery, Rotel, and a can of Black beans, onions and cumin...yum!)
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in_feed" master_h="420" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-beef2c07ea92b4ffd8aa85d456554e52?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="420" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-beef2c07ea92b4ffd8aa85d456554e52?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            Turkey Chili
                            <br/>
                            It's one of the best lean proteins, and one of the cheapest.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in_feed" master_h="343" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-f16fba695fcc68b295e41ef92248b7ad?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="468" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-f16fba695fcc68b295e41ef92248b7ad?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            5-Bean Salad
                            <br/>
                            Add a bit of hot sauce and enjoy on a hot day!
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in zoomable_in_feed" master_h="347" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-64a37be98da6fad399aecd4d95e2adf0?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="520" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-a2d1beabcd1b7f645c2f3dcfc4f8b53f?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            Black-Eyed Pea Salad
                            <br/>
                            Still a bean, and wilted Kale and curry makes this!
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in zoomable_in_feed" master_h="333" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-ef18f9e82c38b9074dc4af7f39666ec7?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="500" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-717e33d2087ee26bb95ed8e7cd3dc671?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            Refried Bean Casserole
                            <br/>
                            Refried beans, salsa, chilis, avocado, a scant of cheese, olives and fresh tomatoes. Add liberal amounts of cumin and chili powder, and this is perfect!
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            All of the above can easily be made for under $5, typically for 3-4 servings. All are healthy, will make you wealthy, and leave you wise! Email me if you need additional ways to make anything,.

                          <div id="__w2_gqJtoVe_outer">
                           <div id="__w2_gqJtoVe_container">
                            Many of the answers here seem to be aiming for the least expensive meal possible using "real food" (i.e. not fast food or highly processed food). While that's certainly a worthy topic, the author of the question allows for $5 per person, which is actually a decent amount of money -- enough to buy some truly nutrient dense foods without having to resort to calorie-rich, nutrient-poor fillers.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Lately for lunch (which I need to carry with me and eat cold), I've been eating a can of sardines packed in olive oil, along with some carrots, spinach, and maybe some raw almonds.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <b>
                             Calorie for calorie, sardines are nutritional powerhouses, though they are often overlooked.
                            </b>
                            They are affordable (even some of the more expensive brands come in around $3/can), easy to store and, if you like the taste (like I do) require no preparation to eat (just have breath mint for afterward). Personally, I prefer the two-layer brisling sardines as they are smaller and, I think, the most flavorful.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in zoomable_in_feed" master_h="431" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-e62596d1c3d8db052577c0571b818dfe?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="500" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-a51164fbadd4e525231c2de1fbfab01c?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            A tin of sardines with bones and skin typically contains 50% of the recommended daily value of calcium, 100% DV vitamin D, 200% DV B12, 100% DV selenium, 25% DV iron, a hefty dose of EPA and DHA, and as much protein as steak.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            UPDATE: Since there are some sardine fans out there who have liked this answer, I thought I should share with you my most recent canned seafood adoption: canned oysters.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                             <img class="landscape qtext_image zoomable_in zoomable_in_feed" master_h="500" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-915ad5279eed98ee79cf4bb6b558d032?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="500" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-6dfcc372c04108a30b901bb6cce29ce0?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                            </div>
                            <b>
                             Oysters are incredibly rich in nutrients. They are particularly rich in zinc and iron while being very low in calories.
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Sadly, I have to admit that I've never been a raw oyster fan. I've tried them in several different locales and in different seasons and they still just remind me of salty snot. Moreover, raw oysters are usually an expensive delicacy, not exactly an easy, inexpensive lunch.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            I finally realized that I needed to try canned oysters and I think they are delicious. The ones pictured above are the only I've found that are canned in olive oil (as opposed to cottonseed oil, which I think is pretty gross). They can be difficult to find in grocery stores, but
                            <b>
                             Trader Joe's sells them for less than $3 a tin.
                            </b>

                            Crock pot cooking is where it at. As mentioned above, you can go vegetarian with dried beans and veggies super cheaply. 5lbs of potatoes are a couple of dollars and will make many meals. Other veggies such as turnips, carrots, cabbage, kale, onions, etc. are similarly cheap and obviously very healthy. It is amazing how good these un-sexy veggies taste after sitting all day on low in a crock pot. And, before anyone says anything, there is real debate about potatoes and whether they are healthy. They are undeniably full of nutrients and carbs. I think they are fine slow cooked or baked but some feel potatoes are the devil because of the carbs. You have to make that decision for yourself but I recommend not deciding based solely on fried potatoes. See how your body reacts to them when they aren't covered in salt, fat, or butter.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            For non-vegetarians (like me) crockpots really excel at cheap meat meals because the cheapest meats turn magically delicious in 8 hours of cooking. Generally, you can find roasts for about $2/lb. I have one cooking right now. I have 5lbs of meat ($10), about less than a $1 or two in potatoes, carrots, and onions. I also have .50 in canned mushrooms and probably another .50 in onion soup mix. (If you want to really splurge you can add broth for around another $1.) That works out to $12 but will feed a family of 5 at least twice. That is just over your $5 but this is for meat that will taste better than most steaks you've ever had.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            There are a million ways to mix this up with all different flavors. Of course, the downside to crockpots are the time. I work from home so I start it in the AM. I know plenty of people who will leave their crockpots running on low while they are at work. You just have to make sure there is enough liquid but, if you do have problems, don't sue me because I warned you. Another option is to let the meal cook overnight while you are home (you do have a smoke alarm right?). Put the contents in the fridge while at work and then re-heat the next day. This is how people lived back when they had a stone hearth, a big kettle, and had to work the fields all day.

                          <div id="__w2_e8mY3H7_outer">
                           <div id="__w2_e8mY3H7_container">
                            <b>
                             Lentils with vegetables (Kale, Collard, Cauliflower. Spice: Ginger, Turmeric, garlic).
                            </b>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            I once had a nutrition start-up. We sold premium tailored-nutrition products, but were looking into a low-cost product for low-income individuals. For this particular product, we'd set our target price to $2.50 for a single, healthy meal.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            The only nutritionally-complete option at this price point was lentils with vegetables. Greens like kale and collard particularly added to the nutritional value, although cruciferous veggies like cauliflower were also important. At this price range incorporating cooking liquid for any vegetables was vital, as the cooking liquid for vegetables often contains most of the nutrition from those vegetables.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Using spices like ginger, turmeric, and garlic was also useful to tune the nutritional content and palatability of the food.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Ultimately, we didn't proceed with the product because we couldn't come up with enough texture and flavor variations to make this diet any fun if that's what you were eating at least once every day. But if you want to mix in a few days a week where a meal is cheap, I'd go with lentils every time.
