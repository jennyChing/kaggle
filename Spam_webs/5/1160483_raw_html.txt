     <p>
      A
      <b>
       nightclub
      </b>
      (also known as a
      <b>
       discothque
      </b>
      , dance clubs, or simply a
      <b>
       club
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       disco
      </b>
      ) is an entertainment
      <a href="/wiki/Music_venue" title="Music venue">
       venue
      </a>
      which usually operates late into the night. A nightclub is generally distinguished from
      <a href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">
       bars
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">
       pubs
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Tavern" title="Tavern">
       taverns
      </a>
      by the inclusion of a
      <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">
       dance floor
      </a>
      and a
      <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">
       DJ
      </a>
      booth, where a DJ plays recorded music.
     </p>
     <p>
      The music in nightclubs is either
      <a href="/wiki/Concert" title="Concert">
       live
      </a>
      bands or, more commonly, a mix of songs played by a
      <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">
       DJ
      </a>
      through a powerful
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_address" title="Public address">
       PA
      </a>
      system. Most clubs or club nights cater to certain
      <a href="/wiki/Music_genre" title="Music genre">
       music genres
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CardGrunewaldCave.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1042" data-file-width="1590" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CardGrunewaldCave.jpg/220px-CardGrunewaldCave.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CardGrunewaldCave.jpg/330px-CardGrunewaldCave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CardGrunewaldCave.jpg/440px-CardGrunewaldCave.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CardGrunewaldCave.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        "The Cave" in the basement of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Roosevelt_Hotel_New_Orleans" title="The Roosevelt Hotel New Orleans">
         the Gruenwald (later Roosevelt) Hotel
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">
         New Orleans
        </a>
        opened in 1912; said by some to be one of the first "nightclubs" in the United States
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      From about 1900 to 1920,
      <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">
       working class
      </a>
      Americans would gather at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honky_tonk" title="Honky tonk">
       honky tonks
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Juke_joint" title="Juke joint">
       juke joints
      </a>
      to dance to music played on a piano or a
      <a href="/wiki/Jukebox" title="Jukebox">
       jukebox
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Webster_Hall" title="Webster Hall">
       Webster Hall
      </a>
      is credited as the first modern nightclub,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      being built in 1886 and starting off as a "social hall", originally functioning as a home for dance and political activism events. During US
      <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">
       Prohibition
      </a>
      , nightclubs went underground as illegal
      <a href="/wiki/Speakeasy" title="Speakeasy">
       speakeasy
      </a>
      bars, with
      <a href="/wiki/Webster_Hall" title="Webster Hall">
       Webster Hall
      </a>
      staying open, with rumors circulating of
      <a href="/wiki/Al_Capone" title="Al Capone">
       Al Capone
      </a>
      's involvement and police bribery. With the
      <a href="/wiki/Twenty-first_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-first Amendment to the United States Constitution">
       repeal of Prohibition
      </a>
      in February 1933, nightclubs were revived, such as New York's
      <a href="/wiki/21_Club" title="21 Club">
       21 Club
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Copacabana_(nightclub)" title="Copacabana (nightclub)">
       Copacabana
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/El_Morocco" title="El Morocco">
       El Morocco
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Stork_Club" title="Stork Club">
       Stork Club
      </a>
      . These nightclubs featured
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_bands" title="Big bands">
       big bands
      </a>
      (there were no DJs).
     </p>
     <p>
      In Germany, possibly the first discothque was
      <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Club" title="Scotch-Club">
       Scotch-Club
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupied_France" title="Occupied France">
       Occupied France
      </a>
      , jazz and
      <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">
       bebop
      </a>
      music, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Jitterbug" title="Jitterbug">
       jitterbug
      </a>
      dance were banned by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazis" title="Nazis">
       Nazis
      </a>
      as
      <i>
       decadent American influences
      </i>
      , so as an act of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_resistance" title="French resistance">
       French resistance
      </a>
      , people met at hidden basements called
      <i>
       discothques
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      where they danced to jazz and
      <a href="/wiki/Swing_music" title="Swing music">
       swing music
      </a>
      , which was played on a single turntable when a jukebox was not available. These
      <i>
       discothques
      </i>
      were also patronized by anti-
      <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">
       Vichy
      </a>
      youth called
      <a href="/wiki/Zazou" title="Zazou">
       zazous
      </a>
      . There were also underground discothques in
      <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">

      </a>
      patronized by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-nazism" title="Anti-nazism">

      youth called the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swing_kids" title="Swing kids">

     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">
       Harlem
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Connie%27s_Inn" title="Connie's Inn">
       Connie's Inn
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Cotton_Club" title="Cotton Club">
       Cotton Club
      </a>
      were popular venues for white audiences. Before 1953 and even some years thereafter, most bars and nightclubs used a jukebox or mostly live bands. In Paris, at a club named Whisky  Gogo, founded in 1947,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9gine_Zylberberg" title="Rgine Zylberberg">
       Rgine
      </a>
      in 1953 laid down a dance-floor, suspended coloured lights and replaced the jukebox with two turntables which she operated herself so there would be no breaks between the music. The Whisky  Gogo set into place the standard elements of the modern post
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      <i>
       discothque
      </i>
      -style nightclub. At the end of the 1950s, several of the coffee bars in Soho introduced afternoon dancing and the most famous, at least on the continent, was Les Enfants Terribles at 93 Dean St. These original discothques were nothing like the night clubs, as they were unlicensed and catered to a very young publicmostly made up of French and Italians working illegally, mostly in catering, to learn English as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Au_pair" title="Au pair">
       au pair
      </a>
      girls from most of western Europe. In the early 1960s,
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Birley" title="Mark Birley">
       Mark Birley
      </a>
      opened a members-only discothque nightclub,
      <a href="/wiki/Annabel%27s" title="Annabel's">
       Annabel's
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Peppermint_Lounge" title="Peppermint Lounge">
       Peppermint Lounge
      </a>
      in New York City became popular and is the place where
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Go-Go_dancer" title="Go-Go dancer">
       go-go dancing
      </a>
      originated. However, the first
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">
       rock and roll
      </a>
      generation preferred rough and tumble bars and taverns to nightclubs, and the nightclub did not attain mainstream popularity until the 1970s
      <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">
       disco
      </a>
      era.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sybil_Burton" title="Sybil Burton">
       Sybil Burton
      </a>
      , former wife of actor
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burton" title="Richard Burton">
       Richard Burton
      </a>
      , opened the "Arthur" discothque in 1965 on East 54th Street in Manhattan on the site of the old El Morocco nightclub and it became the first, foremost and hottest disco in New York City through 1969.

       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="1970s:_Disco">
       1970s: Disco
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nightclub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1970s: Disco">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">
       Disco
      </a>
      has its roots in the underground club scene. During the early 70s in New York City, disco clubs were places where oppressed groups such as homosexuals, Blacks, Latinos, and Jews could party without following male to female dance protocol or exclusive club policies. It brought together people from all walks of life and backgrounds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These clubs acted as safe havens for homosexual partygoers to dance in peace and away from public scrutiny.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Disco was a genre for people to explore sexuality and push the envelope on the dancefloor.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Disco clubs acted as an escape from such depressing environments and acted as the fantasy marginalized peoples could escape to forget oppression and racism.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Disco clubs originally functioned as liberated party spaces and were seen as places of political statement.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By the late 1970s many major US cities had thriving disco club scenes which were centered around discothques, nightclubs, and private loft parties where
      <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">
       DJs
      </a>
      would play
      <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">
       disco
      </a>
      hits through powerful
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PA_system" title="PA system">
       PA systems
      </a>
      for the dancers. The DJs played "... a smooth mix of long single records to keep people 'dancing all night long'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some of the most prestigious clubs had elaborate lighting systems that throbbed to the beat of the music.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some cities had disco dance instructors or
      <a href="/wiki/Dance_studio" title="Dance studio">
       dance schools
      </a>
      which taught people how to do popular disco dances such as "touch dancing", the "
      <a href="/wiki/Hustle_(dance)" title="Hustle (dance)">
       hustle
      </a>
      " and the "
      <a href="/wiki/Cha-cha-cha_(dance)" title="Cha-cha-cha (dance)">
       cha-cha-cha
      </a>
      ". There were also disco fashions that discothque-goers wore for nights out at their local disco, such as sheer, flowing
      <a href="/wiki/Halston" title="Halston">
       Halston
      </a>
      dresses for women and shiny polyester
      <a href="/wiki/Qiana" title="Qiana">
       Qiana
      </a>
      shirts for men. Disco clubs and "...hedonistic loft parties" had a club culture which had many
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian-American" title="Italian-American">
       Italian-American
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">
       African American
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">
       gay
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">
       Hispanic
      </a>
      people.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to the dance and fashion aspects of the disco club scene, there was also a thriving
      <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">
       drug
      </a>
      subculture, particularly for
      <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">
       recreational drugs
      </a>
      that would enhance the experience of dancing to the loud music and the flashing lights, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">
       cocaine
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (nicknamed "blow"), amyl nitrite "
      <a href="/wiki/Poppers" title="Poppers">
       poppers
      </a>
      ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the "...other quintessential 1970s club drug
      <a href="/wiki/Methaqualone" title="Methaqualone">
       Quaalude
      </a>
      , which suspended motor coordination and turned one's arms and legs to Jell-O".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braunstein_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Braunstein-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The "massive quantities of drugs ingested in discothques by newly liberated gay men produced the next cultural phenomenon of the disco era: rampant
      <a href="/wiki/Promiscuity" title="Promiscuity">
       promiscuity
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Public_sex" title="Public sex">
       public sex
      </a>
      . While the dance floor was the central arena of seduction, actual sex usually took place in the nether regions of the disco: bathroom stalls, exit stairwells, and so on. In other cases the disco became a kind of "main course" in a
      <a href="/wiki/Hedonism" title="Hedonism">
       hedonist's
      </a>
      menu for a night out."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braunstein_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Braunstein-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Famous 1970s discothques included celebrity hangouts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">
       Manhattan
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Studio_54" title="Studio 54">
       Studio 54
      </a>
      , which was operated by
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Rubell" title="Steve Rubell">
       Steve Rubell
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ian_Schrager" title="Ian Schrager">
       Ian Schrager
      </a>
      . Studio 54 was notorious for the hedonism that went on within; the balconies were known for sexual encounters, and drug use was rampant. Its dance floor was decorated with an image of the "Man in the Moon" that included an animated
      <a href="/wiki/Cocaine_spoon" title="Cocaine spoon">
       cocaine spoon
      </a>
      . Other famous 1970s discothques in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      included "
      <a href="/wiki/Xenon_(nightclub)" title="Xenon (nightclub)">
       Xenon
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Loft" title="The Loft">
       The Loft
      </a>
      ", the "
      <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Garage" title="Paradise Garage">
       Paradise Garage
      </a>
      ", a recently renovated "
      <a href="/wiki/Copacabana_(nightclub)" title="Copacabana (nightclub)">
       Copacabana
      </a>
      ," and "
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aux_Puces&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aux Puces (page does not exist)">
       Aux Puces
      </a>
      ", one of the first gay disco bars. In
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      , there was the
      <a href="/wiki/Trocadero_Transfer" title="Trocadero Transfer">
       Trocadero Transfer
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/I-Beam_(nightclub)" title="I-Beam (nightclub)">
       I-Beam
      </a>
      , and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_Up" title="End Up">
       End Up
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      By the early 1980s, the term "disco" had largely fallen out of favour in most of the
      <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">
       English-speaking world
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>

       Nightclub entry criteria

     <p>
      Many nightclubs choose who can enter, on bases other than just age, e.g. dress code and guest list. This is used to make their status as a nightclub more "exclusive". Quite often, there are no clear policies governing entry to a nightclub, thereby allowing the doormen to deny entry to anybody at their discretion.

     <p>
      Many nightclubs enforce a dress code in order to ensure a certain type of clientele is in attendance at the venue. Some upscale nightclubs ban attendees from wearing trainers (sneakers) or jeans, while other nightclubs will advertise a vague "dress to impress" dress code that allows the bouncers to discriminate at will against those vying for entry to the club. Many exceptions are made to nightclub dress codes, with denied entry usually reserved for the most glaring rule breakers or those thought to be unsuitable for the party. Certain nightclubs like fetish nightclubs may apply a dress code (
      <a href="/wiki/Fetish_fashion" title="Fetish fashion">
       BDSM

       Exclusive boutique nightclubs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     <p>
      Large cosmopolitan cities that are home to large affluent populations (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">
       Atlanta
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">
       Chicago
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
       Los Angeles
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">
       Miami
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      ) often have what are known as exclusive boutique nightclubs. This type of club typically has a capacity of less than 200 occupants and a very strict entrance policy, which usually requires an entrant to be on the club's guest list.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">
          citation needed

       ]
      </sup>
      While not explicitly members only clubs, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Soho_House_(club)" title="Soho House (club)">
       Soho House
      </a>
      , exclusive nightclubs operate with a similar level of exclusivity. As they are off limits to most of the public and ensure the privacy of guests, many celebrities favor these types of clubs to other, less exclusive, clubs which do not cater as well to their needs.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Another differentiating feature of exclusive nightclubs is, in addition to being known for a certain type of music, they are known for having a certain type of crowd, for instance, a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fashion-forward&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fashion-forward (page does not exist)">
       fashion-forward
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affluent" title="Affluent">
       affluent
      </a>
      crowd or a crowd with a high concentration of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_models" title="Fashion models">
       fashion models
      </a>
      . Many exclusive boutique clubs market themselves as being a place where one can socialize with models and celebrities. Affluent patrons who find this marketing message appealing are often willing to purchase
      <a href="/wiki/Bottle_service" title="Bottle service">
       bottle service
      </a>
      at a markup of several times the retail cost of the liquor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      London's most exclusive boutique nightclubs include Amika, Cirque Du Soir, Project, The Box, and The Rose Club. These venues are frequently visited by an array of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-List" title="A-List">
       A-List
      </a>
      celebrities from the fashion, film, and music industries. All are located in London's prestigious Mayfair, except Cirque Du Soir and The Box, which are both located in London's sex capital, Soho,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      and the both have a more risque theme.

          <q>
           Amyl, butyl and isobutyl nitrite (collectively known as alkyl nitrites) are clear, yellow liquids which are inhaled for their intoxicating effects. Nitrites originally came as small glass capsules that were popped open. This led to nitrites being given the name 'poppers' but this form of the drug is rarely found in the UK. The drug became popular in the UK first on the disco/club scene of the 1970s and then at dance and
           <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">
            rave
           </a>
           venues in the 1980s and 1990s.
