     <div class="col-md-6 col-lg-7 col-sm-9 col-xs-12 post_content">
      <p>
       The new album from Michael Angelakos' once dorm room project, now Madison Square Garden headliner feels like his most cohesive yet. The glossy twinkles, trademark falsetto, and swirling synths are all still present, but
       <em>
        Kindred
       </em>
       (although the shortest
       <a href="http://www.chartattack.com/tag/passion_pit/" target="_blank">
        Passion Pit
       </a>
       album to date) gives itself time to settle in and grow. On each past release, Angelakos has shown he knows how to throw someone in an album and not want to get out. See the opening tracks of
       <em>
        Manners'
       </em>
       "
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-EOx6_7I_OU" target="_blank">
        Make Light
       </a>
       ,"
       <em>
        Gossamer's
       </em>
       "
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dZX6Q-Bj_xg" target="_blank">
        Take A Walk
       </a>
       ," and now the infectious and bouncing "
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wAvr6h7QvJY" target="_blank">
        Lifted Up (1985)
       </a>
       " that opens
       <em>
        Kindred
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Passion Pit paved the way for hundreds of bands trying to re-create the hyper, colourful synth-pop that Angelakos seems to be able to create in his sleep. At this point, he could play an entire festival set of hits. Just try to not smile while listening to "
       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GX3ub_s8Gb8" target="_blank">
        Until We Can't (Let's Go)
       </a>
       ." That is, until you dig into the lyrics.
      </p>
