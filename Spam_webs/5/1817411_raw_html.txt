     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Pink Panther
      </b>
      is the main and title character in the opening and closing credit sequences of every film in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther" title="The Pink Panther">
        The Pink Panther
       </a>
      </i>
      series except for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Shot_in_the_Dark_(1964_film)" title="A Shot in the Dark (1964 film)">
        A Shot in the Dark
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Inspector_Clouseau_(film)" title="Inspector Clouseau (film)">
        Inspector Clouseau
       </a>
      </i>
      . His popularity spawned a series of
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Pink_Panther_cartoons" title="List of The Pink Panther cartoons">
       theatrical shorts
      </a>
      , merchandise, a
      <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">
       comic book
      </a>
      , and television cartoons. He starred in 124 short films, 10 television shows and three
      <a href="/wiki/Prime_time" title="Prime time">
       prime time
      </a>
      specials.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="DePatie-Freleng.2FUnited_Artists_cartoons">
       DePatie-Freleng/United Artists cartoons
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink_Panther_(character)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: DePatie-Freleng/United Artists cartoons">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Pink_Panther_cartoons" title="List of The Pink Panther cartoons">
       List of The Pink Panther cartoons
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The animated Pink Panther character's initial appearance in the live action film's title sequence, directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Friz_Freleng" title="Friz Freleng">
       Friz Freleng
      </a>
      , was such a success with audiences and
      <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">
       United Artists
      </a>
      that the studio signed Freleng and his
      <a href="/wiki/DePatie-Freleng_Enterprises" title="DePatie-Freleng Enterprises">
       DePatie-Freleng Enterprises
      </a>
      studio to a multi-year contract for a series of
      <i>
       Pink Panther
      </i>
      theatrical cartoon shorts. The first entry in the series, 1964's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Phink" title="The Pink Phink">
        The Pink Phink
       </a>
       , featured the Panther harassing his foil, a little white mustachioed man who is actually a caricature of Friz Freleng (this character is officially known as "The Little White Guy" and/or "The Man"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottawaxpress.ca_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ottawaxpress.ca-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), by constantly trying to paint the Little Man's blue house pink.
      </i>
      The Pink Phink
      <i>
       won the 1964
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Animated_Short_Film" title="Academy Award for Animated Short Film">
        Academy Award for Animated Short Film
       </a>
       , and subsequent shorts in the series, usually featuring the Pink Panther opposite the Little Man, were successful releases.
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      In an early series of Pink Panther animated cartoons, the Panther generally remained silent, speaking only in two theatrical shorts,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sink_Pink" title="Sink Pink">
        Sink Pink
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pink_Ice" title="Pink Ice">
        Pink Ice
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Rich_Little" title="Rich Little">
        Rich Little
       </a>
       provided the Panther's voice in the latter shorts, modelling it on that of
       <a href="/wiki/David_Niven" title="David Niven">
        David Niven
       </a>
       (who had portrayed Clouseau's jewel thief nemesis in the original live-action film). Years later, Little would overdub Niven's voice for
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Trail_of_the_Pink_Panther" title="Trail of the Pink Panther">
       Trail of the Pink Panther
      </a>
      <i>
       and
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/Curse_of_the_Pink_Panther" title="Curse of the Pink Panther">
       Curse of the Pink Panther
      </a>
      <i>
       , due to Niven's ill health. All of the animated
      </i>
      Pink Panther
      <i>
       shorts utilized
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_Theme" title="The Pink Panther Theme">
        the distinctive jazzy theme music
       </a>
       composed by
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Mancini" title="Henry Mancini">
        Henry Mancini
       </a>
       for the 1963 feature film, with additional scores composed by
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Greene" title="Walter Greene">
        Walter Greene
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lava" title="William Lava">
        William Lava
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-book1-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>

        <a href="#cite_note-book3-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4

        <a href="#cite_note-5">

      </i>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pink_Panther_Show">
       <i>
        The Pink Panther Show
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink_Panther_(character)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Pink Panther Show">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_Show" title="The Pink Panther Show">
       The Pink Panther Show
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In the fall of 1969, the
      <i>
       Pink Panther
      </i>
      cartoons made their way to
      <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">
       NBC
      </a>
      television shown
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_morning_cartoon" title="Saturday morning cartoon">
       Saturday mornings
      </a>
      via
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_Show" title="The Pink Panther Show">
        The Pink Panther Show
       </a>
      </i>
      . NBC added a
      <a href="/wiki/Laugh_track" title="Laugh track">
       laugh track
      </a>
      to the original cartoons, with
      <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Miller_(actor)" title="Marvin Miller (actor)">
       Marvin Miller
      </a>
      brought on as an off-camera narrator talking to the Pink Panther during
      <a href="/wiki/Bumper_(broadcasting)" title="Bumper (broadcasting)">
       bumper
      </a>
      segments featuring the Pink Panther and The Inspector together.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Pink Panther
      </i>
      shorts made after 1969 were produced for both broadcast and film release, typically appearing on television first, and released to theaters by United Artists. One version of the show was called
      <i>
       The Think Pink Panther Show
      </i>
      . A number of sister series joined
      <i>
       The Pink Panther
      </i>
      on movie screens and on the airwaves, among them
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Ant_and_the_Aardvark" title="The Ant and the Aardvark">
        The Ant and the Aardvark
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tijuana_Toads" title="Tijuana Toads">
        The Tijuana Toads
       </a>
      </i>
      (a.k.a.
      <i>
       The Texas Toads
      </i>
      ),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hoot_Kloot" title="Hoot Kloot">
        Hoot Kloot
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Misterjaw" title="Misterjaw">
        Misterjaw
       </a>
      </i>
      (a.k.a.
      <i>
       Mr. Jaws and Catfish
      </i>
      ). There were also a series of animated shorts called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Inspector" title="The Inspector">
        The Inspector
       </a>
      </i>
      , with the Clouseau-inspired Inspector and his sidekick Sgt. Deux-Deux, whom the Inspector is forever correcting. Other DePatie-Freleng series included
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Roland_and_Rattfink" title="Roland and Rattfink">
        Roland and Rattfink
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Dogfather" title="The Dogfather">
        The Dogfather
       </a>
      </i>
      (a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Godfather" title="The Godfather">
        Godfather
       </a>
      </i>
      pastiche), with a canine Corleone family and two
      <i>
       Tijuana Toads
      </i>
      spinoffs,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Racer" title="The Blue Racer">
        The Blue Racer
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Crazylegs_Crane" title="Crazylegs Crane">
        Crazylegs Crane
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The German television version which started airing in 1973 in
      <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">
       ZDF
      </a>
      was presented in 30-minute episodes, composed of one Pink Panther cartoon, one episode of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Inspector" title="The Inspector">
       The Inspector
      </a>
      and one episode of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Ant_and_the_Aardvark" title="The Ant and the Aardvark">
       The Ant and the Aardvark
      </a>
      . Most notably, the difference between the German and the English version of the Pink Panther is a rhymed narration in the German version (spoken by voice actor
      <a href="/wiki/Gert_G%C3%BCnther_Hoffmann" title="Gert Gnther Hoffmann">
       Gert Gnther Hoffmann
      </a>
      ), commenting and describing the plot. For this show, custom intro and end sequences were cut together from existing pieces of animation.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1976, the half-hour series was revamped into a 90-minute format, as
      <i>
       The Pink Panther Laugh and a Half Hour and a Half Show
      </i>
      ; this version included a live-action segment, where the show's host, comedian Lenny Schultz, would read letters and jokes from viewers. This version flopped, and would change back to the original half-hour version in 1977.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1978,
      <i>
       The Pink Panther
      </i>
      moved to ABC and was rebranded
      <i>
       The All New Pink Panther Show
      </i>
      , where it lasted one season before leaving the network realm entirely. The ABC version of the series featured sixteen episodes with 32 new Pink Panther cartoons, and 16 of
      <a href="/wiki/Crazylegs_Crane" title="Crazylegs Crane">
       Crazylegs Crane
      </a>
      . The 32 entries were later released theatrically by
      <a href="/wiki/United_Artists" title="United Artists">
       United Artists
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_television_shows_and_specials">
       Later television shows and specials
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink_Panther_(character)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later television shows and specials">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      During the final years of the Panther's theatrical run, DePatie-Freleng produced a series of three primetime Pink Panther
      <a href="/wiki/Television_special" title="Television special">
       television specials
      </a>
      for ABC. The first was 1978's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_in:_A_Pink_Christmas" title="The Pink Panther in: A Pink Christmas">
        A Pink Christmas
       </a>
      </i>
      . It featured the panther in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York
      </a>
      being cold and hungry looking for a holiday dinner. The other two specials premiered on ABC after the shorts officially ended in theaters, 1980's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_in:_Olym-Pinks" title="The Pink Panther in: Olym-Pinks">
        Olym-Pinks
       </a>
      </i>
      and 1981's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_in:_Pink_at_First_Sight" title="The Pink Panther in: Pink at First Sight">
        Pink at First Sight
       </a>
      </i>
      . In November 2007, the three specials were released on a single disc DVD collection,
      <i>
       The Pink Panther: A Pink Christmas
      </i>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/MGM_Home_Entertainment" title="MGM Home Entertainment">
       MGM Home Entertainment
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox_Home_Entertainment" title="20th Century Fox Home Entertainment">
       20th Century Fox Home Entertainment
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The studio was sold to
      <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">
       Marvel Comics
      </a>
      in 1981, and became
      <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Productions" title="Marvel Productions">
       Marvel Productions
      </a>
      (now a part of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">
       The Walt Disney Company
      </a>
      ). In 1984, a new Saturday morning series was produced entitled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pink_Panther_and_Sons" title="Pink Panther and Sons">
        Pink Panther and Sons
       </a>
      </i>
      . In this incarnation (produced by Hanna-Barbera Productions with Freleng serving as creative producer for the series), the still-silent Pink Panther was a father of his two talking sons, Pinky and Panky. While popular, critics complained that there was not enough Pink Panther to maintain interest for a full 30 minutes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A new series of cartoon were created in 1993, simply titled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pink_Panther_(1993_TV_series)" title="The Pink Panther (1993 TV series)">
        The Pink Panther
       </a>
      </i>
      , produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer_Animation" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Animation">
       Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Animation
      </a>
      , premiered in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_syndication" title="Television syndication">
       syndication
      </a>
      in 1993, and had the Pink Panther speaking with the voice of
      <a href="/wiki/Matt_Frewer" title="Matt Frewer">
       Matt Frewer
      </a>
      (of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Headroom_(TV_series)" title="Max Headroom (TV series)">
        Max Headroom
       </a>
      </i>
      fame). Unlike the original shorts, not all episode titles contained the word "pink," although many instead contained the word "panther." Voice impressionist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Byner" title="John Byner">
       John Byner
      </a>
      returned to voice both
      <a href="/wiki/The_Ant_and_the_Aardvark" title="The Ant and the Aardvark">
       the Ant and the Aardvark
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In July 2007,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer_Inc." title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Inc.">
       Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Inc.
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">
       Jordan
      </a>
      's Rubicon animation company began co-production of the animated series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pink_Panther_and_Pals" title="Pink Panther and Pals">
        Pink Panther and Pals
       </a>
      </i>
      portraying a teenaged panther and his friends. The 26 episode TV series premiered worldwide in spring 2010 on
      <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">
       Cartoon Network
      </a>
      . In 2011, a new 22-minute holiday special entitled
      <i>
       A Very Pink Christmas
      </i>
      aired on ABC Family and later Boomerang in the U.S., YTV in Canada, and Boomerang in the U.K. in December.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After acquiring United Artists in the early 1980s, MGM continues to own the ancillary rights and trademarks to the Pink Panther franchise.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Charity">
       Charity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink_Panther_(character)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Charity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Pink Panther is associated with a number of cancer awareness and support organisations. The Pink Panther is the mascot of the New Zealand Child Cancer foundation
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCF_Mascot_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CCF_Mascot-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and for a line of clothing to promote breast cancer awareness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PiNKiTUDE_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PiNKiTUDE-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      California based children's cancer charity The Gary L. Hoop Foundation humorously places The Pink Panther in various locations on its website and in its advertisements, paying homage to both the cartoon and their late namesake Gary Hoop, who once carried "The Pink Panther" as a nickname.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GarysKids.org_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-GarysKids.org-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
       Critical reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pink_Panther_(character)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Animation historian
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Beck" title="Jerry Beck">
       Jerry Beck
      </a>
      has called the Pink Panther "the last great Hollywood cartoon character", noting that "Classic animation pretty much died in the '60s, everyone had kind of bailed out. But his creators didn't rest on their laurels. They didn't make the cartoons to look like
      <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Cartoons" title="Warner Bros. Cartoons">
       Warner Bros.
      </a>
      cartoons, or
      <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">
       Disney
      </a>
      cartoons, or the
      <a href="/wiki/UPA_(animation_studio)" title="UPA (animation studio)">
       UPA
      </a>
      look of
      <a href="/wiki/Mister_Magoo" title="Mister Magoo">
       Mister Magoo
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gerald_McBoing-Boing" title="Gerald McBoing-Boing">
       Gerald McBoing-Boing
      </a>
      . They came up with their own clever new style. The only other important cartoon of the '60s was
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_Submarine_(1968_film)" title="Yellow Submarine (1968 film)">
        Yellow Submarine
       </a>
      </i>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottawaxpress.ca_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ottawaxpress.ca-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pink Panther was a notable contribution to the animation art form. Top animation directors such as
      <a href="/wiki/Hawley_Pratt" title="Hawley Pratt">
       Hawley Pratt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Chiniquy" title="Gerry Chiniquy">
       Gerry Chiniquy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_McKimson" title="Robert McKimson">
       Robert McKimson
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sid_Marcus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sid Marcus (page does not exist)">
       Sid Marcus
      </a>
      contributed to a distinctive style, supported by master story writer
      <a href="/wiki/John_W._Dunn_(animator)" title="John W. Dunn (animator)">
       John W. Dunn
      </a>
      . Produced after theatrical cartooning's golden age of the 1940s and 50s, they were constrained to the
      <a href="/wiki/Limited_animation" title="Limited animation">
       limited animation
      </a>
      techniques applied to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_morning_cartoons" title="Saturday morning cartoons">
       Saturday morning cartoons
      </a>
      of 1960s and after. Within these limitations, the Pink Panther made creative use of absurd and surreal themes and visual puns and an almost completely wordless pantomime style, set to the ubiquitous Pink Panther theme and its variations by Henry Mancini. The overall approach is reminiscent of the classic silent movies of
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">
       Charlie Chaplin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Buster_Keaton" title="Buster Keaton">
       Buster Keaton
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beck_3-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-beck-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cultural references were more muted and stylized, resulting in a cartoon with longer-term, more cross-cultural appeal not shared by contemporaries such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Bear" title="Yogi Bear">
        Yogi Bear
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Flintstones" title="The Flintstones">
        The Flintstones
       </a>
      </i>
      , with their greater reliance on contemporary American pop culture. The Pink Panther also remained constrained to the classic six-minute form of theatrical shorts, while contemporaries expanded into longer, sitcom-like storylines, up to a full 30 minutes of broadcast TV in the case of
      <i>
       The Flintstones
      </i>
      . Freleng's colleagues credit his sense of creative timing as a key element to the cartoon's artistic success. Freleng himself regarded the Pink Panther as his finest achievement and the character he most identified with, according to family and colleagues interviewed on the 2006 DVD release.
     </p>
