            <p class="first-text" data-textannotation-id="82f1743e754079ddc8cc37366865cf4a">
             Everybody acknowledges the huge influence that writer J.G. Ballard continues to have on science fiction and our visions of the future  but we don't pay enough attention to one of Ballard's own main influences. Over in
             <a href="http://www.theartsdesk.com/visual-arts/sci-fi-week-through-eyes-jg-ballard" target="_blank">
              The Arts Desk
             </a>
             , there's a great look at the visual artists who influenced Ballard.
            </p>
            <p data-textannotation-id="80a97466232e03167c78fc5341ae10b4">
             Above is the poster for a pop art exhibition that Ballard visited in 1956 called This Is Tomorrow, which had a huge impact on his visual sensibility as a writer. Ballard himself organized a museum exhibition of crashed cars, called Crashed Cars, in 1970, causing a huge scandal  and this led to his novel
             <em>
              Crash
             </em>
             a few years later. The Art Desk
             <a href="http://www.theartsdesk.com/visual-arts/sci-fi-week-through-eyes-jg-ballard" target="_blank">
              adds
             </a>
             :
            </p>
            <blockquote data-textannotation-id="dc25a5777d1b85cf32247471ebe0ee04">
             <p data-textannotation-id="02ce0c73f02f50db4b7b770f8870232d">
              More directly,
              <a href="http://www.theartsdesk.com/visual-arts/tacita-dean-frith-street-gallery" target="_blank">
               Tacita Dean's 2013 film
               <em>
                JG
               </em>
              </a>
              explored Ballard's fascination with artist Robert Smithson, and was  inspired by her correspondence with the writer in his last years. Their  letters explored the connections between Smithson's great earthwork
              <em>
               Spiral Jetty
              </em>
              , 1970, built on the north-eastern shore of Utah's Great Salt Lake
              <strong>
              </strong>
              , and Ballard's short story
              <em>
               The Voices of Time
              </em>
              ,  published 10 years earlier, in which the main character builds a  mandala in a dried saline landscape. The collection featuring this story  was found among Smithson's belongings after his death in a plane crash  in 1973. Like Ballard, Smithson was fascinated by the idea of entropy,  and Ballard was in turn fascinated by
              <em>
               Spiral Jetty
              </em>
              , though Ballard's twin obsessions encompassed psychological as well as environmental disintegration.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p data-textannotation-id="885fbc2eb381b5d6b9d3d47641df2e0d">
             As the article points out, quoting Martin Amis' obituary, Ballard wasn't particularly oriented towards dialogue or character  instead, he was remorselessly visual. He was influenced by a generation of visual artists, and in turn he influenced a generation that followed.
            </p>
