     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">
       music
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       sharp
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       dise
      </b>
      (from
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      ), or
      <b>
       diesis
      </b>
      (from
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        a
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      means higher in pitch and the
      <b>
       sharp symbol
      </b>
      raises a
      <a href="/wiki/Musical_note" title="Musical note">
       note
      </a>
      by a half tone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(music)" title="Intonation (music)">
       Intonation
      </a>
      may be
      <a href="/wiki/Flat_(music)" title="Flat (music)">
       flat
      </a>
      , sharp, or both, successively or
      <a href="/wiki/Simultaneity_(music)" title="Simultaneity (music)">
       simultaneously
      </a>
      . More specifically, in
      <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">
       musical notation
      </a>
      , sharp means "higher in
      <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(music)" title="Pitch (music)">
       pitch
      </a>
      by a
      <a href="/wiki/Semitone" title="Semitone">
       semitone
      </a>
      (half step)," and has an associated symbol (
      <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">
       
      </span>
      ), which may be found in
      <a href="/wiki/Key_signature" title="Key signature">
       key signatures
      </a>
      or as an
      <a href="/wiki/Accidental_(music)" title="Accidental (music)">
       accidental
      </a>
      , as may flats.
     </p>
     <p>
      Under twelve-tone
      <a href="/wiki/Equal_temperament" title="Equal temperament">
       equal temperament
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       B sharp
      </i>
      , for instance, sounds the same as, or is
      <a href="/wiki/Enharmonic" title="Enharmonic">
       enharmonically
      </a>
      equivalent to,
      <i>
       C
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_sign" title="Natural sign">
        natural
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       E sharp
      </i>
      is enharmonically equivalent to
      <i>
       F
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_sign" title="Natural sign">
        natural
       </a>
      </i>
      . In other
      <a href="/wiki/Musical_tuning#Tuning_systems" title="Musical tuning">
       tuning systems
      </a>
      , such enharmonic equivalences in general do not exist. To allow extended
      <a href="/wiki/Just_intonation" title="Just intonation">
       just intonation
      </a>
      , composer
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Johnston_(composer)" title="Ben Johnston (composer)">
       Ben Johnston
      </a>
      uses a sharp to indicate a note is raised 70.6
      <a href="/wiki/Cent_(music)" title="Cent (music)">
       cents
      </a>
      (ratio 25:24), or a flat to indicate a note is lowered 70.6 cents.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Musical_tuning#Tuning_practice" title="Musical tuning">
       tuning
      </a>
      , sharp can also mean "slightly higher in pitch" (by some unspecified amount). If two simultaneous notes are slightly out of tune, the higher-pitched one (assuming the lower one is properly pitched) is said to be sharp with respect to the other. Furthermore, the verb
      <i>
       sharpen
      </i>
      means "raise the frequency of a note, typically by a
      <a href="/wiki/Comma_(music)" title="Comma (music)">
       small musical interval
      </a>
      ".
     </p>

     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/Number_sign" title="Number sign">
       number (hash or pound) sign
      </a>
      (#). Both signs have two sets of parallel double-lines. However, a correctly drawn sharp sign must have two slanted parallel lines which rise from left to right, to avoid being obscured by the staff lines. The number sign, in contrast, has two compulsory horizontal strokes in this place. In addition, while the sharp also always has two perfectly vertical lines, the number sign (#) may or may not contain perfectly vertical lines (according to typeface and writing style).
