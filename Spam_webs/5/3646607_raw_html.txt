        <h1 class="article-title">
         Can I build muscle without eating meat?
        </h1>
        <p class="article-byline">
         You dont have to be a carnivore to pack on muscle. Heres how to get in great shape as a vegetarian or vegan
        </p>
        <p class="article-meta has-image">
         <a class="article-meta--avatar" href="/author/Edward-Lane" title="Edward Lane">
          <img alt="Edward Lane" src="http://menshealth.cdnds.net/main/st/members/teddy.jpg"/>
         </a>
         By
         <span class="article-meta--author">
          <a href="/author/Edward-Lane" title="Edward Lane">
           Edward Lane
          </a>
         </span>
         <time class="article-meta--published" datetime="27-27-November" pubdate="" title="27 Thu November 2014">
          Posted on November 27, 2014
         </time>
        </p>
        <div class="social-sharing">
         <button class="facebook-sharer" data-href="http://www.menshealth.co.uk/food-nutrition/muscle-foods/can-I-build-muscle-without-eating-meat" title="Share on Facebook">

       <div class="article-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="ALTTEXT" src="http://assets.menshealth.co.uk/main/embedded/29582/veganmain.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Plates piled high with poultry are a mainstay of the gym-goers diet. The notion that meat is essential to build muscle is widespread and, as a result, vegetarians looking to bulk up can feel disadvantaged.
        </p>
        <p>
         But there are plenty of ways to get the protein you need without renouncing your principles or breaking the bank on supplements. Bodybuilder Alex Mitchell (
         <a href="http://veganbodybuilding.org/home.htm%20" target="_blank">
          veganbodybuilding.org
         </a>
         ) first hit the gym as a vegetarian and now as a vegan is continuing to see impressive muscle gains.
        </p>
        <p>
         The protein pyramid of nuts, cereals (such as oats and quinoa) and legumes should be the cornerstone of any vegan diet. The tricky part is making sure you balance your meals to deliver a complete amino acid profile. A good rule of thumb is to ensure every meal contains at least two members of this protein-providing triumvirate. Mitchell's post-workout meal of choice: boiled quinoa with sliced green beans, a mixture of kidney, butter and haricot beans fried and then mashed, served with a spinach salad topped with chopped almonds.
        </p>
        <p>
         Another commonly used product by vegetarians who train is
         <a href="http://www.realfoods.co.uk/product/897/real-foods-tvp-light-chunks-gmo-free-bulk-wholesale%20" target="_blank">
          Textured Vegetable Protein
         </a>
         , which, despite the unappetising name, is an inexpensive processed soya that you can buy minced. "As a vegetarian I used to
         <a href="http://www.menshealth.co.uk/food-nutrition/healthy-recipes/egg-recipes-for-breakfast" target="_blank">
          rely heavily on eggs
         </a>
         but since I became a vegan soya is now my go-to protein source," says Mitchell.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Other good sources of protein for those who don't eat meat include
         </strong>
         :
         <br/>

         </em>
        </p>
        <p>

          <br/>
          Tofu

          <br/>

          <br/>

         </em>
        </p>
