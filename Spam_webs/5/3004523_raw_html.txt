            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <span style="font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: 'Verdana','sans-serif'">
              The inflation rate is an incredibly important macroeconomic factor. It is so important that it is the main focus of both the Federal Reserve and the European Central Bank. Price stability is a crucial factor, in fact, both the aforementioned central banks have the statutory mission to keep the inflation rate close but not above the 2% threshold. A slightlylessthan 2% inflation rate is often associated with a stable and sustainable economic expansion and this is precisely why developed economies monitor so closely the percentage change in prices level. The first part of the analysis shows the quarterly divided inflation rates in the largest European economies:
             </span>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <img alt="HyperVolatility End Year Report 2014: Inflation Rate EU" border="0" src="http://media.barchart.com/cm/users/36850/EYR2014INFL1.png" title="HyperVolatility End Year Report 2014: Inflation Rate EU"/>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <span style="font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: 'Verdana','sans-serif'">
              The above reported chart clearly highlights the fact that the Euro area is rather far from the 2% target. In fact, the annual average was +0.38% and the inflation rate declined over time going from a +0.6% in the 1
              <sup>
               st
              </sup>
              quarter to only +0.1% in the 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              one. The only European large economy that managed to experience an inflation rate close to 2% was the UK with an annual average of +1.43% (although figures passed from +1.7% in the 1
              <sup>
               st
              </sup>
              quarter to +0.9% in the 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              one). The second highest annual average inflation was registered in Germany (+0.88%), although, even in this case, the increment in prices level moved from +1.2% in the first quarter to +0.5% in the 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              one. The third place is occupied by France (+0.5% annual average) but the down trend in the national inflation is evident also in this case (from +0.7% in the 1
              <sup>
               st
              </sup>
              quarter to +0.3% in the 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              quarter). The lowest inflation rates have been registered in Italy (+0.23% on an annual basis) and Spain (0.15 annually). The increment in Italian inflation rate throughout the 2014 has never been higher than +0.4% while in Spain it has never violated the +0.2% threshold. The next chart provides a broader view of the inflation rate in the socalled global economies (Euro area is treated as a standalone country):
             </span>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <img alt="HyperVolatility End of the Year Report 2014: Global Economies" border="0" src="http://media.barchart.com/cm/users/36850/EYR2014INFL2.png" title="HyperVolatility End of the Year Report 2014: Global Economies"/>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <span style="font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: 'Verdana','sans-serif'">
              The Euro area, as previously mentioned, did not experience a robust increment as far as inflation rate is concerned and its performances have been clearly surpassed by the United States: average annual inflation was +1.6%. Nevertheless, the highest inflationary environments have been identified in Australia (+2.5% annual average) and Japan (+2.5% annual average). The Australian economy prices level has been growing at a 3% pace for the first 6 months of the 2014 but it slowed down in the 3
              <sup>
               rd
              </sup>
              (+2.3%) and 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              quarter (+1.7%). Japan, on the other hand, has gone through a monetary earthquake because of the Abenomics (a large QE programme launched by the Prime Minister Abe in the attempt to end the stagnation of the GDP which lasts since the 90s) and this has caused the inflation to move up. In fact, the rate has touched +3.5% in the 2
              <sup>
               nd
              </sup>
              quarter and +3.3% in the 3
              <sup>
               rd
              </sup>
              and closed the year at +2.5%.
             </span>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <span style="font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: 'Verdana','sans-serif'">
              The last section of the current macroeconomic analysis focuses on emerging economies:
             </span>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <img alt="HyperVolatility End of the Year Report 2014:Inflation Rate Emerging Economies" border="0" src="http://media.barchart.com/cm/users/36850/EYR2014INFL3.png" title="HyperVolatility End of the Year Report 2014:Inflation Rate Emerging Economies"/>
            </p>
            <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify; line-height: 150%">
             <span style="font-size: 12pt; line-height: 150%; font-family: 'Verdana','sans-serif'">
              Emerging countries have fast growing, but still not well established, economic structures and, consequently, they tend to experience wilder swings in prices level. In fact, the average annual inflation rate for all countries, with the exception of China, is above +6%. Russia annual average inflation was +7.8% and the peak was reached with a +9.6% in the 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              quarter. The Russian high inflationary environment is due to the severe drop in oil prices (primary source of income for Moscow) which provoked an aggressive depreciation in the Ruble and forced the Central Bank to increment interest rates (from 11.5% to 17% in December) in the attempt to prevent the currency from depreciating even further with respect to the US Dollar. India (+7.1% annually) is the country that, along with Russia, went through the most aggressive changes in prices level. In fact, the inflation rate was above 8% for almost 6 months and above 7% for almost 9 months. Brazil (+6.3% on an annual basis) and South Africa (+6% annually) rank 3
              <sup>
               rd
              </sup>
              and 4
              <sup>
               th
              </sup>
              from the bottom while China is definitely the economy that has showed the most stable growth in inflation over the 2014. The percentage change in prices level for the giant Asian country was +2.3% in the 1
              <sup>
               st
              </sup>
              quarter, +2.2% in the 2
              <sup>
               nd
              </sup>
              quarter, it dropped to +1.9% in the 3
              <sup>
               rd
              </sup>
              and closed the year at +1.5%.
             </span>
            </p>
