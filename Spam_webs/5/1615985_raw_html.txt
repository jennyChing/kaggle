            <li class="contributor" data-reactid=".in7bpmdn28.5.1:2.1.0.0.3.$Author.1.$54cb25477e4b004120bd7143">
             <a class="author" data-reactid=".in7bpmdn28.5.1:2.1.0.0.3.$Author.1.$54cb25477e4b004120bd7143.0" href="/contributor/dan-pashman" rel="author">
              Dan Pashman
             </a>
            </li>
           </ul>
          </div>

         <span class="content" data-reactid=".in7bpmdn28.5.1:2.1.0.0.5">
          <p>
           There are simply not enough foods on the market containing pumpkin spice.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sure, every chain that serves coffee now serves a pumpkin-spice latte. And we have pumpkin-spice coffee creamer. Pumpkin-spice coconut milk. Pumpkin-spice seltzer. Pumpkin-spice Kahlua. Pumpkin-spice M&amp;Ms. Pumpkin-spice Hersheys Kisses. Pumpkin-spice Oreos. Pumpkin-spice Milanos. Pumpkin-spice ice cream. Pumpkin-spice Eggo waffles. Pumpkin-spice Jell-O pudding. Pumpkin-spice marshmallows. Pumpkin-spice English muffins. Pumpkin-spice regular muffins. Pumpkin-spice bagels. Pumpkin-spice instant oatmeal. Pumpkin-spice yogurt. Pumpkin-spice butter. Pumpkin-spice peanut butter. Pumpkin-spice margarine. Pumpkin-spice almonds. Pumpkin-spice energy bars. Pumpkin-spice gum. And pumpkin-spice fettuccini.
          </p>
          <p>
           And thats it!
          </p>
          <p>
           Still, when I survey todays coffee drinks, confections, spreads, and anti-depressants, all I see are missed opportunities. Food manufacturers of the world are holding out on us.
          </p>
          <p>
           One company
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/10/the-company-that-tested-out-pumpkin-spice-foie-gras-mashed-potatoes/381192/?single_page=true">
            tested
           </a>
           pumpkin-spice foie-gras mashed potatoes and came to the unbelievable conclusion that people might not want to eat that. (Good luck explaining that move to shareholders, fellas.) Sure, its great that Philadelphia introduced pumpkin-spice cream cheese. But why would you only add the aromatic goodness of pumpkin spice to plain cream cheese? Why not the scallion variety? What could go wrong?
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/10/the-company-that-tested-out-pumpkin-spice-foie-gras-mashed-potatoes/381192/?single_page=true">
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Now I know what youre thinking. But Dan, youll say, not everything is better with pumpkin spice. Besides, the term itself is deceiving. Most things labeled pumpkin spice are just full of sugar, corn syrup, and lab-tested compounds vaguely reminiscent of ginger, cinnamon, nutmeg, and clove. Few of them actually contain pumpkin, let alone the spices that normally accompany it, and if they did contain pumpkin youd hardly notice it anyway, because pumpkin has no flavor.
          </p>
          <p>
           Youll point out that Starbucks and Dunkin Donuts
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/10/the-company-that-tested-out-pumpkin-spice-foie-gras-mashed-potatoes/381192/?single_page=true">
            make it very hard
           </a>
           to find out exactly what they use to achieve the pumpkin-spice-latte bliss point. (It seems the secret ingredient, beyond the chemicals, is sweetened condensed milkthe nectar of the gods, if the gods all had diabetes.)
          </p>
          <p>
           And youll send me the link to Hugh Merwins
           <a href="http://www.grubstreet.com/2014/09/starbucks-pumpkin-spice-latte-syrup-diet.html">
            piece on Grub Street
           </a>
           , in which he paid $65 for a black-market bottle of Starbucks pumpkin-spice latte syrup and started putting it in everything he ate and drank until he made himself sick.
          </p>
          <p>
           You may even go so far as to suggest that I could attain the same level of pumpkin-spice-induced delirium by lighting a pumpkin-spice candle in my house and eating anything.
          </p>
          <p>
           Perhaps. But as an American there are two things I lovesugar and artificial scarcity.
          </p>
          <p>
           Stamp the words LIMITED EDITION on a package of something sweet and I will buy it immediately. A few years ago, General Mills pulled Count Chocula and Franken Berry cereals from shelves, save for a few months each year around Halloween. Now people are
           <a href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/thesalt/2013/10/20/228193138/unleashed-on-halloween-monsters-cereal-haunts-hoarders">
            selling them on eBay
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Why? Because artificial scarcity tastes better! Just ask Michael Pollan, who has written so eloquently about the importance of seasonality in food.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ill admit I became a bit concerned recently when Starbucks declared that autumn starts in August and moved up the release of its pumpkin-spice latte accordingly. Will artificial abundance taste as good as scarcity? Time will tell.
          </p>
          <p>
           Fortunately, one executive from a pumpkin-spice-producing company put me at ease when she
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/10/the-company-that-tested-out-pumpkin-spice-foie-gras-mashed-potatoes/381192/">
           </a>
           <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/10/the-company-that-tested-out-pumpkin-spice-foie-gras-mashed-potatoes/381192/">
            said
           </a>
           , I dont think well go into July. Thats summer.
          </p>
          <p>
           Crisis averted.
          </p>
          <p>
           So wash me in the waterer, bright, viscous, aromatic, saccharine elixirof pumpkin spice. For a limited time only, let it rain down over all of us, and may we all act now and purchase it in great quantities. Before its too late.
          </p>
