          <h1 class="entry-title">
           What My Dog Trainer Reminded Me About Leadership
          </h1>
         </header>
         <!-- .entry-header -->

         <div class="entry-content">
          <p class="first-child ">
           <span class="dropcap" title="R">
            R
           </span>
           ecently, my wife bought me a Labradoodle puppy. I had wanted one ever since my daughter got hers. My grandson named him Charlie Brown, since his fur was a beautiful chocolate brown color.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="Sitting on the Front Porch with Charlie" class="featured-image wp-post-image" height="592" src="http://michaelhyatt.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/sitting-on-the-porch-with-charlie-760x592.jpg" width="760"/>
          </p>
          <div class="photo-credit">
           Sitting on the Front Porch with My New Labradoodle, Charlie
          </div>
          <p>
           Though he was born in January, we didnt get to meet Charlie until last week. He spent the first eleven weeks at the breeder with his mother and siblings. Then he went straight to a trainer for Companion Training. (Yes, we believe in outsourcing!)
           <br/>
           <span id="more-26007">
           </span>
           <br/>
           Last Monday, after what seemed like forever, we drove five hours to Indianapolis to pick him up. He was everything I had hoped he would becute, calm, and compliant.
          </p>
          <p>
           The trainer spent three hours with us, going over the training. We watched with delight as Charlie responded obediently to nineteen different commands. Then the trainer let us work with him, as she coached and corrected us.
          </p>
          <h3>
           The Foundation of Successand Failure
          </h3>
          <p>
           Though he was house-broken, I had to ask her, What happens if he has an accident and eliminates indoors. How should we respond? (This is the part I always dread about getting a puppy.)
          </p>
          <p>
           No problem, she assured me. Just take a newspaper and roll it up real tightlike its a stick. Now hit yourself on the forehead and say, Bad owner!
          </p>
          <p>
           We laughed out loud. She went on to explain. If he has an accident, its your fault. Either you got distracted or ignored the signals. She let that sink in and continued, Charlies trained. Now we just have to train you.
          </p>
          <p>
           I had to smile. She was giving me a dose of my own medicine. When I teach on leadership, I always begin with self-leadership. To get better results, you have to become a better leader.
          </p>
          <p>
           In any situation, we can practice self-leadership by taking these actions.
          </p>
          <h3>
           Action #1: Take responsibility for the results.
          </h3>
          <p>
           This is a challenge for many leaders. Its much easier to offer an excuse or blame someone else or something else.
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Its the economy. Things are just slow right now.
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             Its my sales managers fault. She didnt get that order from Wal-Mart.
            </p>
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             We missed the deadline, because the vendor didnt come through.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           The problem is that as long as you are making excuses you will not grow as a leader. You will also not earn the respect of others, whether its your boss, your peers, or your subordinates.
          </p>
          <p>
           Instead, you have look in the mirror and say, I own this. I dont like it. I wish it were different, but it is what it is. I take full responsibility for this outcome.
          </p>
          <h3>
           Action #2: Ask yourself a hard question.
          </h3>
          <p>
           One of the greatest leadership lessons I ever learned was from my executive coach,
           <a href="http://www.gapinternational.com/Ilene-Muething" title="Ilene Muething">
            Ilene
           </a>
           from
           <a href="http://www.gapinternational.com/" title="Gap International Website">
            Gap International
           </a>
           . After missing our revenue plan one August, she asked, Why do you think you fell short of your budget?
          </p>
          <p>
           I stammered around, then offered a parade of excuses (actually, the very ones I cited earlier). Except, I didnt think of them as excuses. Instead, I saw them as
           <em>
            the reasons
           </em>
           we had missed our target.
          </p>
          <p>
           She then assured me she understood. I get that all those things were factors. Then she hit me with this zinger: What was it about your leadership that led to this outcome?
          </p>
          <p>
           I did not like that question at all. In fact, I was offended by it. But she persisted.
          </p>
          <p>
           Look, if its someone elses fault, then you are just a victim. But if you accept responsibility, you can change your behavior. And if you can change your behavior, you change the outcome next time around.
          </p>
          <p>
           This question has revolutionized the way I lead. I feel it is a major reason I get the results I get. But its not enough to ask the question. You also have to 
          </p>
          <h3>
           Action #3: Change your behavior.
          </h3>
          <p>
           This is the hard part. Leaders talk a lot about the challenges of organizational change. Most of the focus is on changing other peoples behavior.
          </p>
          <p>
           Granted, changing other peoples behavior is tough. But its a whole lot easier if, as leaders, we are willing to change our own behavior.
          </p>
          <p>
           For example,
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            Leaders complain about their teammates being late to meetings but usually arrive late themselvesunless they have called the meeting.
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             Leaders insist on more analysis before green-lighting a subordinates project, but are content to follow their intuition when its their own.
            </p>
           </li>
           <li>
            <p>
             Leaders challenge their team to explore the use of social media in their organizations marketing efforts, but refuse to engage themselves.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           The Russian saint,
           <a href="http://orthodoxwiki.org/Seraphim_of_Sarov" title="Seraphim of Sarov">
            Seraphim of Sarov
           </a>
           , once said, Save yourself, and thousands around you will be saved. This reflects deep insight into the essence of leadership and the key to organizational change.
          </p>
          <h3>
           Lead By Example
          </h3>
          <p>
           By taking these these three actions, you are in a much better position to lead by example. It doesnt mean you cant hold others accountable. You can. But you must start with yourself and then teach others to do the same.
          </p>
          <p class="comment-reminder reminder">
           <span class="preface">
            Question:
           </span>
           How well are you leading yourself?
