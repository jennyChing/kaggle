    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       ludic fallacy
      </b>
      , identified by
      <a href="/wiki/Nassim_Nicholas_Taleb" title="Nassim Nicholas Taleb">
       Nassim Nicholas Taleb
      </a>
      in his 2007 book
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Black_Swan_(Taleb_book)" title="The Black Swan (Taleb book)">
        The Black Swan
       </a>
      </i>
      , is "the misuse of
      <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">
       games
      </a>
      to model real-life situations."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Taleb explains the fallacy as "basing studies of chance on the narrow world of games and dice."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The adjective
      <i>
       ludic
      </i>
      originates from the Latin noun
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ludus" title="wikt:ludus">
        ludus
       </a>
      </i>
      , meaning "play, game, sport, pastime."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The alleged fallacy is a central argument in the book and a rebuttal of the predictive mathematical models used to predict the future  as well as an attack on the idea of applying nave and simplified statistical models in complex domains. According to Taleb, statistics is applicable only in some domains, for instance
      <a href="/wiki/Casino" title="Casino">
       casinos
      </a>
      in which the odds are visible and defined. Taleb's argument centers on the idea that predictive models are based on
      <a href="/wiki/Platonic_realism" title="Platonic realism">
       platonified forms
      </a>
      , gravitating towards mathematical purity and failing to take various aspects into account:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       It is impossible to be in possession of the entirety of available information
      </li>
      <li>
       Small unknown variations in the data could have a huge impact. Taleb differentiates his idea from that of mathematical notions in
       <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">
        chaos theory
       </a>
       , e.g. the
       <a href="/wiki/Butterfly_effect" title="Butterfly effect">
        butterfly effect
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Theories/models based on empirical data are claimed to be flawed as they may not be able predict events which are previously unobserved, but have tremendous impact, e.g., the
       <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">
        9/11 terrorist attacks
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_automobile" title="History of the automobile">
        invention of the automobile
       </a>
       , a.k.a
       <a href="/wiki/Black_swan_theory" title="Black swan theory">
        black swan theory
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
       Examples
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Example_1:_Suspicious_coin">
       Example 1: Suspicious coin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Example 1: Suspicious coin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      One example given in the book is the following
      <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">
       thought experiment
      </a>
      . Two people are involved:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Dr. John who is regarded as a man of science and logical thinking
      </li>
      <li>
       Fat Tony who is regarded as a man who lives by his wits
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A third party asks them to "assume a
      <i>
       fair
      </i>
      coin is flipped 99 times, and each time it comes up heads. What are the odds that the 100th flip would also come up heads?"
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Dr. John says that the odds are not affected by the previous outcomes so the odds must still be
       <a href="/wiki/Odds#Even_odds" title="Odds">
        50:50
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Fat Tony says that the odds of the coin coming up heads 99 times in a row are so low that the initial assumption that the coin had a 50:50 chance of coming up heads is most likely incorrect. "The coin gotta be loaded. It can't be a fair game."
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The ludic fallacy here is to assume that in real life the rules from the purely hypothetical model (where Dr. John is correct) apply. Would a reasonable person bet on black on a
      <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">
       roulette
      </a>
      table that has come up red 99 times in a row (especially as the reward for a correct guess is so low when compared with the probable odds that the game is fixed)?
     </p>
     <p>
      In classical terms,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistically_significant" title="Statistically significant">
       statistically significant
      </a>
      events, i.e. unlikely events, should make one question one's model assumptions. In
      <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_statistics" title="Bayesian statistics">
       Bayesian statistics
      </a>
      , this can be modelled by using a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prior_distribution" title="Prior distribution">
       prior distribution
      </a>
      for one's assumptions on the fairness of the coin, then
      <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_inference" title="Bayesian inference">
       Bayesian inference
      </a>
      to update this distribution.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Example_2:_Job_interview">
       Example 2: Job interview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example 2: Job interview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A person considers whether to attend a
      <a href="/wiki/Job_interview" title="Job interview">
       job interview
      </a>
      . He recently studied
      <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
       statistics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">
       utility
      </a>
      theory in college and performed well in the exams. Considering whether to take the interview, he tries to calculate the
      <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">
       probability
      </a>
      that he will get accepted. Furthermore, he estimates the length of the interview, and calculates an expected utility cost accordingly. Using this information, he performs a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risk-benefit_analysis" title="Risk-benefit analysis">
       risk-benefit analysis
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      This particular job seeker does not take into account that
      <a href="/wiki/Real_life" title="Real life">
       real life
      </a>
      has more variables than the subset he has chosen from. Even with a low probability of success of getting accepted, an attractive job may be worth the effort of attending to the interview. Will he enjoy the process of the interview? Will his interview technique improve regardless of whether he gets the job or not? Even the statistics of the job business are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">
       non-linear
      </a>
      . What other jobs could come the man's way by meeting the interviewer? Might there be a possibility of a very high pay-off in this company that he has not thought of? The point being that there may be hidden payoffs in accepting options that have a lower apparent utility than alternatives.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Example_3:_Stock_returns">
       Example 3: Stock returns
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Example 3: Stock returns">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Any
      <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">
       decision theory
      </a>
      based on a fixed universe or model of possible outcomes ignores and minimizes the impact of events which are "outside the model." For instance, a simple model of daily
      <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">
       stock market
      </a>
      returns may include extreme moves such as
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987)" title="Black Monday (1987)">
       Black Monday (1987)
      </a>
      but might not model the market breakdowns following the
      <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Thoku earthquake and tsunami">
       2011 Japanese tsunami
      </a>
      and its consequences. A fixed model considers the "known unknowns," but ignores the "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unknown_unknowns" title="Unknown unknowns">
       unknown unknowns
      </a>
      ."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Example_4:_Chess_and_Warfare">
       Example 4: Chess and Warfare
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Example 4: Chess and Warfare">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Warfare is often reduced and compared to strategic games such as chess, or the Chinese game Go. A battle may be described as a chess match, "and the field of chess is projected upon the field of warfare."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_Platonicity">
       Relation to Platonicity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludic_fallacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relation to Platonicity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The ludic fallacy is a specific case of the more general problem of Platonicity, defined by Taleb as:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       the focus on those pure, well-defined, and easily discernible objects like triangles, or more social notions like friendship or love, at the cost of ignoring those objects of seemingly messier and less tractable structures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
