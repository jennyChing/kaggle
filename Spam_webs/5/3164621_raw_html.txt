     <section id="article-section-1">
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         The show is as riveting as it is enlightening, humorous as it is dark. From its raw lesbianism and gnawing poverty to its meth-laden Jesus worshipping and tribalistic racial segregation,
        </span>
        <em>
         <span>
          Orange is the New Black
         </span>
        </em>
        <span>
         has managed to captivate
        </span>
        <a href="https://www.recordedfuture.com/orange-is-the-new-black-viewership/">
         <span>
          as many as 3 million
         </span>
        </a>
        <span>
         Netflix users in just two seasons. Its back-to-back episodes can easily consume whole weekends, the blessingor curseof Netflixs decision to release an entire seasons trove of installments all at once.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         But watching an entire season of television in one sitting can be productive if its homeworkas it is for one lucky group of college students in the suburban town of University Center, Michigan. Saginaw Valley State University is slated to offer a new writing class next semester that will give students an opportunity to do something constructive with that binge-watching: get college credit.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         <em>
          Orange is the New Black
         </em>
         
        </span>
        <span>
         the Netflix TV series, not the memoir off of which it was basedis being featured as the main "textbook" in Assistant Professor Kim Laceys general-education English course next semester on writing about oppression.
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <aside class="pullquote instapaper_ignore" dir="ltr">
       <span>
        The story provides an illuminating window into prison, humanizing hardened criminals and exposing just how inhumane jail can be.
       </span>
      </aside>
      <p dir="ltr">
       The show was first released as a Netflix original series last summer and almost immediately became a near-cult sensation. Its the brainchild of creator and producer Jenji Kohanbest known previously for her hit show
       <em>
        Weeds
       </em>
       , a similarly dark comedy seriesand the storyline, or at least its groundwork, is ostensibly the product of reality.
       <a href="#correction">
        *
       </a>
       Thats because its based on the memoir of Piper Kerman, a woman who spent 13 months in federal womens prison in Danbury, Connecticut, after getting convicted for a crime she had committed five years prior: laundering money and trafficking drugs on behalf of her then-girlfriend, a large-scale, globe-trotting heroin dealer.
      </p>
      <p dir="ltr">
       <span>
        <span>
         "Some people may scoff at the idea of [the show] not being highbrow enough, but thats just not true," Lacey said, referring to the series as the courses "text." "Its not like these texts arent sophisticated toolsyou just have to discuss these tools intelligently."

        <p class="dek">
         The Islamic State is no mere collection of psychopaths. It is a religious group with carefully considered beliefs, among them that it is a key agent of the coming apocalypse. Heres what that means for its strategyand for how to stop it.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">

           <span class="smallcaps">

           </span>

          </span>

          <i>

          </i>

Nagata, the Special Operations commander for the United States in the Middle East, admitting that he had hardly begun figuring out the Islamic States appeal. We have not defeated the idea, he said. We do not even understand the idea. In the past year, President Obama has referred to the Islamic State, variously, as not Islamic and as al-Qaedas jayvee team, statements that reflected confusion about the group, and may have contributed to significant strategic errors.

         More people with low incomes now live outside of cities, and some areas are ill-equipped to deal with the influx of the poor.

        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          NORCROSS, Ga.Every weekday around 3:15 p.m., a big, yellow school bus stops on Pelican Drive outside Norcross Extended Stay, near the intersection with Best Friend Drive.
         </p>
         <p>
          Dozens of children file out, carrying their heavy backpacks away from the Wendys and the AutoZone, towards the cluster of aging three-story yellow buildings where they live. Some are met by waiting parents, others trek by themselves to the shabby motel rooms, marching past broken-down cars
          <b>
           ,
          </b>

         </p>
         <p>
          That families are living in extended-stay motels like this one may seem surprising in a town like Norcross, founded in 1870 and named one of the

           best places to live
          </a>
          in Georgia by
          <em>
           Movoto
          </em>
          , a real estate blog, last year. Gwinnett County, where Norcross is located, is, in parts, a collection of well-off towns like Duluth, home to
          <a href="http://www.celebrityhousepictures.com/nene-leakes.php">
           NeNe Leaks
          </a>
          , of
          <em>
           Real Housewives of Atlanta
          </em>
          fame. Its unemployment rate is just 5.7 percent and one of its schools, the Gwinnett School of Mathematics, Science, and Technology, was recently named one of the
          <a href="http://www.wsbtv.com/news/news/local/gwinnett-school-ranks-among-top-3-nation/nXT2y/">

          by
