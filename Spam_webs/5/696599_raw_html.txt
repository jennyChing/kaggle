          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/10/wicked-diy-arca.html">
            Wicked DIY Arcade Cabinet Kits
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="Mini dk" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/10/23/minidk.jpg" title="DK seems smaller in person..."/>
            </p>
            <p>
             Mameroom Designs in Cincinnati, Ohio makes an impressive line of arcade cabinets, ranging from massive standup MAME cabs down to incredibly compact bartop units. Their
             <a href="http://www.mameroom.com/ProductDetail.asp?ID=UABARTOPKIT">
              Ultimate Arcade Bartop Kit
             </a>
             (above) sells for $149.99. It includes professionally milled panels that are pre-drilled and ready for assembly without requiring power tools. All of the necessary assembly hardware is included, eliminating the need for expensive hardware store visits.
            </p>
            <p>
             This little arcade console has room for a 15" CRT monitor and includes a removable rear panel, clear marquee plastic and a tinted monitor cover. You'll have to add your own panel art, control buttons, and electronics to bring this little beast to life. It looks like the perfect home for my
             <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/03/diy-build-a-mam.html">
              $150 MAME Brain
             </a>
             project, although their $249.99
             <a href="http://www.mameroom.com/Categories.asp?ID=COCKTAILKIT">
              head-to-head cocktail unit
             </a>
             looks great, too.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.mameroom.com/home.asp">
              MAMEroom Designs Arcade Kits
             </a>
            </p>

          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/10/diego-stoccos-m.html">
            Diego Stocco: DIY Musical Machines
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <embed allowfullscreen="true" allowscriptaccess="always" height="332" src="http://blip.tv/play/AdP8eIj3dg" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="470">
            </embed>
            <p>
             Diego Stocco is a composer and sound designer who's not afraid to
             <a href="http://www.behance.net/Gallery/Diego-Stocco--The-Burning-Piano/131439">
              incinerate
             </a>
             a piano to get a few good sound samples. He's also a prolific instrument designer, although you're unlikely to find his crazy contraptions in any orchestra.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Stocco sampling" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/10/17/stoccosample.jpg" title="Stocco sampling the TypoSonic"/>
             <br/>
            </p>
            <p>
             My favorite is the
             <a href="http://www.behance.net/Gallery/Diego-Stocco--Typosonic-Machine/133781">
              TypoSonic
             </a>
             - a custom built musical typewriter. He tells us, "I made this instrument by taking apart parts of an old typewriter and then adding a new mechanism, bass strings and mechanics and a sound board." Needless to say, it looks and sounds wicked.
            </p>
            <p>
             <object height="265" width="470">
              <param name="allowfullscreen" value="true">
              </param>
              <param name="allowscriptaccess" value="always">
              </param>
              <param name="movie" value="http://vimeo.com/moogaloop.swf?clip_id=1747614&amp;server=vimeo.com&amp;show_title=1&amp;show_byline=1&amp;show_portrait=0&amp;color=&amp;fullscreen=1">
              </param>
              <embed allowfullscreen="true" allowscriptaccess="always" height="265" src="http://vimeo.com/moogaloop.swf?clip_id=1747614&amp;server=vimeo.com&amp;show_title=1&amp;show_byline=1&amp;show_portrait=0&amp;color=&amp;fullscreen=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="470">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.behance.net/Gallery/Diego-Stocco--The-Burning-Piano/131439">
              The Burning Piano
             </a>
             is also strikingly impressive, especially since few of us would have the nerve to do something so destructively creative. "I set the piano on fire and sampled it," he explains mischievously. If you're after something a tad less dangerous, the
             <a href="http://www.behance.net/Gallery/Diego-Stocco--Bedside-Table-Bass/134763">
              Bedside Table Bass
             </a>
             - crafted from a 1960s vintage night table - has a hauntingly beautiful tone. His other designs include a musical clothes drying rack and the Sonic Washer - the result of an unholy union between an electric guitar and a washing machine.
            </p>
            <p>
             When he's not designing whimsical musical instruments, Stocco serves as Director of Sound for Epic Score, a Los Angeles-based music production company. His sound design credits include work for Nokia, BMW, Samsung, Panasonic and General Motors. And - as if he wasn't busy enough - Stocco is one of the creative geniuses behind Spectrasonics'
             <a href="http://www.spectrasonics.net/instruments/omnisphere.html">
              Omnisphere
             </a>
             virtual instrument software, which he presented earlier this month at the AES show in San Francisco.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.diegostocco.com/">
              Diego Stocco's official site
             </a>
             <br/>
             <a href="http://www.behance.net/DiegoStocco">
              Explore Stocco's instrument portfolio on the Behance Network
             </a>
            </p>

          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/07/modding-a-fishe.html">
            Modding a Fisher-Price Movie Viewer
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="F-P movie viewer" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/07/17/fisherpricemovieviewer.jpg" title="F-P movie viewer"/>
             <br/>
             The Fisher-Price Movie Viewer Theater shows just how far kids' entertainment has progressed since 1978. The compact plastic film viewer shows silent Super 8 movies on a blurry little screen. It can also project a 10-inch image onto the wall.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Open cart" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/07/17/fpviewer_2.jpg" style="margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px; float: right;" title="Open cart"/>
             Its best feature (speaking from personal experience) is that the film is hand-cranked, allowing kids to morph everyday Warner Brothers cartoons into bizarre reverse slow-motion cinematic works of art.
            </p>
            <p>
             The only thing cooler than owning one of these would be to have one loaded with your own movies. Super8man was up to the challenge, and loaded his own Super 8 home movies into the F-P viewer, enabling his kids to manipulate their flickery images on-screen.
            </p>
            <p>
             Conveniently, the viewer is compatible with standard Super 8 film. Super 8 film is still available, and it'll cost about $25 to shoot and process a 50 foot cartridge.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Home movies!" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/07/17/fphomemovie.jpg" title="Home movies!"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             The next step is to carefully pry open one of the old yellow cartridges and replace the film inside with your own Oscar-worthy footage. Super8man reports that the cartridges come preloaded with about 12 feet of film, but he thinks they'll accommodate up to 50 feet, giving a maximum running time of 3 1/2 minutes at 18 frames per second.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Projection gate" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/07/17/fpfilm.jpg" style="margin: 0px 10px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="Projection gate"/>
             Before it can be loaded, the new film needs to be spliced into a continuous loop. Loading it into the cartridge is guaranteed to be a fiddly process, and paying careful attention to how the prepackaged film is positioned will definitely make the replacement process easier.
            </p>
            <p>
             The Fisher-Price Movie viewer was also available in a hand-held version that resembled mom &amp; dad's real Super 8 camera. It requires no batteries, you simply point it at a bright window of lamp and turn the crank. Both models
             <a href="http://rover.ebay.com/rover/1/711-53200-19255-0/1?type=3&amp;campid=5335835488&amp;toolid=10001&amp;customid=&amp;ext=fisher-price+movie+viewer&amp;satitle=fisher-price+movie+viewer">
              turn up frequently
             </a>
             on eBay, although prices vary considerably.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://home.pacbell.net/mnyberg/super8mm/super8_41.html">
              Visit Super8man's how-to page for full instructions
             </a>
             <br/>
             <a href="http://rover.ebay.com/rover/1/711-53200-19255-0/1?type=3&amp;campid=5335835488&amp;toolid=10001&amp;customid=&amp;ext=fisher-price+movie+viewer&amp;satitle=fisher-price+movie+viewer">
              Browse Fisher-Price movie viewers on eBay
             </a>
             <br/>
            </p>

          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/06/build-your-own.html">
            Build Your Own Mechanical TV
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="Televisor" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/06/29/televisor.jpg" title="Televisor"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             Here's an opportunity to build and own a televisor similar to the device created by Scottish inventor John Logie Baird in 1924. Middlesex University Teaching Resources have released a clever 28.56 kit that includes everything necessary to build your own vintage mechanical television.
            </p>
            <p>
             The unit measures 260 x 195 x 70mm and includes a CD encoded with compatible TV footage. Simply plug your CD player or iPod into the televisor to view the video. The kit design is vaguely reminiscent of the most popular commercial televisor, manufactured by
             <a href="http://www.earlytelevision.org/baird_televisor.html">
              Plessey
             </a>
             in the early 1930s. Around 1,000 of these tin-encased devices were sold at a price of 18, qualifying it as the first commercial television.
             <br/>
            </p>
            <p>
             <object allowfullscreen="false" data="http://blip.tv/scripts/flash/showplayer.swf?enablejs=true&amp;feedurl=http%3A%2F%2Fretrothingtv%2Eblip%2Etv%2Frss&amp;file=http%3A%2F%2Fblip%2Etv%2Frss%2Fflash%2F1041009%3Freferrer%3Dblip%2Etv%26source%3D1&amp;showplayerpath=http%3A%2F%2Fblip%2Etv%2Fscripts%2Fflash%2Fshowplayer%2Eswf" height="300" id="showplayer" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="470">
              <param name="movie" value="http://blip.tv/scripts/flash/showplayer.swf?enablejs=true&amp;feedurl=http%3A%2F%2Fretrothingtv%2Eblip%2Etv%2Frss&amp;file=http%3A%2F%2Fblip%2Etv%2Frss%2Fflash%2F1041009%3Freferrer%3Dblip%2Etv%26source%3D1&amp;showplayerpath=http%3A%2F%2Fblip%2Etv%2Fscripts%2Fflash%2Fshowplayer%2Eswf">
              </param>
              <param name="quality" value="best">
              </param>
              <embed height="300" name="showplayer" quality="best" src="http://blip.tv/scripts/flash/showplayer.swf?enablejs=true&amp;feedurl=http%3A%2F%2Fretrothingtv%2Eblip%2Etv%2Frss&amp;file=http%3A%2F%2Fblip%2Etv%2Frss%2Fflash%2F1041009%3Freferrer%3Dblip%2Etv%26source%3D1&amp;showplayerpath=http%3A%2F%2Fblip%2Etv%2Fscripts%2Fflash%2Fshowplayer%2Eswf" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="470">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             The British Broadcasting Corporation transmitted experimental televisor broadcasts between late 1929 and 1932. The tall 30 line image measured 2 1/4 inches high by a mere 3/4 inch wide. Televisor images were a ghostly shade of black and red, thanks to the neon lamp used for illumination.
            </p>
            <p>
             An unimpressed
             <a href="http://www.earlytelevision.org/baird_image_description.html">
              1928 viewer
             </a>
             remarked,
             <em>
             </em>
             "The Baird machine may be said to give a recognisable human head. It is curiously unlike any particular face. I suspect that the eyebrows were heavily made up. Only very slow movements are possible, any thing of even normal speed producing a wild blurr. The impression is of a curiously ape like head, decapitated at the chin, swaying up and down in a streaky stream of yellowy light."
            </p>
            <p>
             Check out
             <a href="http://blip.tv/file/1034738">
              the video
             </a>
             to see the device built and operated before your very eyes.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.mutr.co.uk/product_info.php?cPath=148_150&amp;products_id=1376">
              The MUTR Televisor Kit Product Page
             </a>

          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/05/the-original-co.html">
            Retroputing: Build A Classic RCA COSMAC ELF System
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="Elf2k" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/05/09/elf2k.jpg" title="Elf2k"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             The original RCA COSMAC ELF appeared on the front cover of the August
1976 issue of Popular Electronics. It was a complete computer based on
the RCA 1802 CPU and cost under $100 to build from a bare board.
Another $20 would get you a B&amp;W video display. Outstanding value,
and these things lingered in the classified section of magazines like
BYTE throughout the early 1980s. Spare Time Gizmos has recreated the machine, and
even offers discrete logic replacements for some parts that might be
hard to find. The price for a partial kit is around $100, but be warned
that you'll have to key in programs using 12 switches on the front
panel.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Elf video" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/05/09/elfvideo.jpg" style="margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px; float: right;" title="The classic ELF video demo"/>
             The secret to the ELF's success was its incredibly low price. It was sold through detailed
             <a href="http://homepage.mac.com/ruske/cosmacelf/images/netronicsad_rejan79.gif">
              display ads
             </a>
             , catering to budget computing enthusiasts who wanted to try their hands at programming but couldn't afford something more advanced. The base unit featured a two digit 7-segment LED display and a mere 256 bytes of program memory.
            </p>
            <p>
             By early 1979, Netronics Research
and Development was offering an assembled ELF II with built-in B&amp;W video and hexadecimal keypad for $99.95. Accessories included a $39.95 "Giant Board" with cassette I/O and various serial and parallel connectors. 4K of static RAM cost $89.95 and an ASCII keyboard was another $64.95. With a bit of forethought, it was possible to build a respectable system over the course of a few months without breaking the bank.
            </p>
            <p>
             The 1802 processor is still manufactured by Intersil, leading Spare Time Gizmos to release an updated design as the Cosmac ELF 2000. The new machine includes 32K RAM and an optional 32K EPROM containing utilities and BASIC, FORTH and CHIP-8 languages. There's a six-digit LED display. It also supports the original video chip (no longer in production) and there are fully decoded I/O ports. A bare board costs a mere $20 and a partial kit full of the most important components can be yours for a very reasonable $100. Should you wish to add 80 column video, flash program storage or extended I/O, that's possible too.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.sparetimegizmos.com/Hardware/Elf2K.htm">
              Check out the Spare Time Gizmos COSMAC ELF 2000
             </a>
             <br/>
             <a href="http://www.cosmacelf.com/history1.htm">
              The history of the COSMAC ELF
             </a>
            </p>

My fear is that as the label "educational" is applied to more and more inane things, fun and cheap Presto Magix kits could start putting on airs.              The Sinclair ZX80 set the computing world on fire as the first computer available for under 100 ($200 in the USA). You could buy a kit version for a mere 79.95 including postage.            <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/04/build-your-ow-1.html">
            Minimig: Build Your Own Amiga 500 Clone
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="Minimig" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/04/10/minimig.jpg" title="DIY Amiga"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             The Amiga 500, released in 1987, was Commodore's most successful Amiga model, thanks to its all-in-one good looks and reasonable $595.95 price tag. Dutch electrical engineer Dennis van Weeren never forgot his Amiga 500 and launched an ambitious project to build an FPGA-based version of the machine in early 2005.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Minimig proto" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/04/10/minimigproto.jpg" style="margin: 0px 0px 5px 5px; float: right;" title="The prototype"/>
             The end result was the Minimig, a 12cm x 12cm board based on a modern 3.3V version of the Motorola 68000 processor. The Amiga's custom chipset was implemented in a Xilinx Spartan-3 FPGA chip, and the board includes 2MB of RAM and an MMC flash memory card connector for program storage. van Weeren thoughtfully included a standard PS/2 keyboard and mouse connectors, along with a 9-pin joystick port.
            </p>
            <p>
             It's amazing that someone managed to successfully recreate a
machine as complicated as the Amiga. There are a few little glitches
that still need to be worked out (some sprite bugs and a few file system
limitations), but basically everything works as you'd expect.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Lemmings" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/04/10/lemmings.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="Lemmings"/>
             The Minimig board design and software has been released under the GNU
General Public License, so anyone with fairly decent soldering skills can build their own Amiga clone.To get the system running, you'll need an image of the original Amiga Kickstart ROM loaded into a memory card, along with a copy of the Workbench. Now just sit back, plug in your favorite Commodore-compatible joystick and enjoy a game of Lemmings (which was the first game to successfully run on the machine).
            </p>
            <p>
             If you're not up to soldering together your own motherboard,
             <a href="http://www.acube-systems.biz/eng/minimig.php">
              Acube Systems Srl
             </a>
             in Italy offers a fully assembled board with 1GB SD flash card for 138 plus shipping. One thing you should be aware of is that van Weeren's design includes ports on several sides, making it challenging to house. Luckily, Swedish enthusiast
             <a href="http://web.comhem.se/illuwatar/project_pages/minimig/minimig.htm">
              Illuwatar
             </a>
             stepped in to produce a version in a standard Mini-ITX form factor, allowing you to house your little Amiga clone in a standard PC case.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://home.hetnet.nl/~weeren001/">
              Minimig - An Amiga in an FPGA
             </a>
            </p>

             <img alt="Gakken tube amp" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/03/06/gakkenamp.jpg" title="This thing's tiny"/>
             <br/>
             Japanese kit builder Gakken sells a stunningly affordable Vacuum Tube Amplifier kit. It retails for about $125, including a set of four surplus Chinese vacuum tubes (2 x 2P3 and 2 x 1B2). The kit comes with a pre-built circuit board, so no soldering is required. All you have to do is screw the various parts together, plug in the vacuum tubes and enjoy your new toy. The kit even includes a pin straightener to ensure you don't accidentally bend a pin while inserting a tube.
            </p>
            <p>
             I suspect that the audio output is extremely low, since the unit is powered by a couple of D-Cell batteries. My first mod would be the addition of a power jack on the back panel for a regulated 3V wall wart. My second "mod" would be to substitute some slightly higher quality speakers. The plastic horns included with the kit look really cool, but I doubt they sound anything but awful. Also, you should be warned that the instructions are in Japanese only, but there are lots of detailed pictures for illiterate westerners like me.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Gakken kit" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/03/06/gakkenkit.jpg" title="Ready to build"/>
             <br/>
             Purchasing one of these could be a bit of a problem for those of us in North America. Engadget featured it this morning with a link to a Korean online retailer, but
             <a href="http://www.funshop.co.kr/vs/detail.aspx?no=0657134906">
              the product page
             </a>
             isn't in English. The same goes for the official Japanese Gakken site. Luckily, there seem to be a a few of these
             <a href="http://rover.ebay.com/rover/1/711-53200-19255-0/1?type=3&amp;campid=5335835488&amp;toolid=10001&amp;customid=&amp;ext=Gakken&amp;satitle=Gakken">
              on eBay
             </a>
             at very reasonable prices.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.funshop.co.kr/vs/detail.aspx?no=0657134906">
              Lots of assembly photos at funshop.co.kr
             </a>
             [via
             <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2008/03/05/gakken-amplifier-kit-brings-vacuum-tubes-to-your-ipod/">
              Engadget
             </a>
             ]
             <br/>
             <a href="http://rover.ebay.com/rover/1/711-53200-19255-0/1?type=3&amp;campid=5335835488&amp;toolid=10001&amp;customid=&amp;ext=Gakken&amp;satitle=Gakken">
              Gakken kits on eBay
             </a>
            </p>

          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>

            <p>
             Trust the Japanese to combine the old-school mechanical charm of a player piano with modern electronics. Elekit's Otokibako is a hand-cranked electronic music box that plays melodies from paper rolls using optical sensors to detect the black "notes."              <a href="http://translate.google.com/translate?hl=en&amp;u=http://www.elekit.co.jp/product/54552d3837334c454949">
              Google translation
             </a>
             of their Japanese pages.

        <div class="entry-category-doityourself entry-category-musical_instruments entry-author-james_grahame entry-type-post entry" id="entry-45626936">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/02/paia-affordable.html">
            PAiA: Affordable DIY Synthesizers
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             Somehow I've forgotten to mention the wonderful DIY devices from PAiA Corporation. Company founder John Simonton is often credited with creating the world's first programmable drum machine - the
             <a href="http://www.synthmuseum.com/paia/paipdrset01.html">
              PAiA Programmable Drum Set
             </a>
             . Its distinctive analog sound underpinned Peter Gabriel's
             <em>
              Games Without Frontiers
             </em>
             and
             <em>
              Biko
             </em>
             , among countless others. Although Simonton passed away several years ago, the company is still going strong.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="PAiA fatman" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/02/14/paiafatman.jpg" title="Desktop synthesis"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             The
             <a href="http://www.paia.com/fatman.asp">
              PAiA Fatman
             </a>
             analog monosynth continues PAiA's long tradition of bringing affordable analog synthesis to the masses. This kit-based instrument
is offered in rack-mount or table top versions for under $250. Over the years, it has
earned the honor of becoming the worlds most modded synth, with dozens
of useful tweaks available online for intrepid builders, including
VCO hard sync and a subharmonic generator. Build one and you'll quickly discover that nothing beats a front panel filled with knobs. Don't despair if you're not the DIY type - they frequently appear
             <a href="http://rover.ebay.com/rover/1/711-53200-19255-0/1?type=3&amp;campid=5335835488&amp;toolid=10001&amp;customid=&amp;ext=PAiA+synth&amp;satitle=PAiA+synth">
              on eBay
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="Paia 9700" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/02/14/paia9700.jpg" title="Mini modular"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             Way back in the mid-1970s, PAiA offered a series of monstrous analog modular synthesizer kits at then-unheard of prices. The modern descendant of these ancient beasts is the
             <a href="http://www.paia.com/p9700s.asp">
              9700 analog modular system
             </a>
             . Unlike the massive and temperamental instruments of decades past, the 9700 combines multiple sound elements into each module. The result is a surprisingly compact and affordable system starting at under $600.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.paia.com/index.asp">
              Explore the PAiA product catalog
             </a>
            </p>

        <h2 class="date-header">

        <div class="entry-category-doityourself entry-category-timepieces entry-author-james_grahame entry-type-post entry" id="entry-45331604">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/02/diy-almost-affo.html">
            DIY: Almost Affordable Nixie Clocks
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="Ramsey Nixie Clocks" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/02/08/ramseynixies.jpg" title="I prefer the wooden version"/>
            </p>
            <p>
             Before the world was flooded with cheap LED and LCD panels, there were Nixies -- neon-filled glass tubes. They usually
contain ten cathodes in the shape of the numbers 0 through 9, and a
wire mesh anode. When electricity is passed between one of the cathodes
and the anode, the corresponding number shines with an endearing orangey-red
glow. I think the only Nixies I've ever used in real life were on an ancient spectrum analyzer, which adds to the mystique for me.
            </p>
            <p>
             There have been a number of Nixie clock projects in recent years, but most of them cost around $500 -- too much for most casual enthusiasts. Conveniently, Ramsey Electronics has stepped up to the plate with a pair of $170 clock kits. The wooden version features six IN14 Nixie tubes on a teak and maple base, while the aluminum model offers a half dozen IN12 tubes for a more modern look. If your electronics chops are a bit rusty, you can purchase either model factory assembled for an extra $40.
            </p>
            <p>
             I find it amusing that both designs incorporate a low-cost PIC microcontroller as their brains, making these timepieces a wonderful example of vintage and modern technologies that complement each other perfectly.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.ramseyelectronics.com/cgi-bin/commerce.exe?preadd=action&amp;key=NIXIE#clocks">
              Classic Nixie Tube Clocks
             </a>
             [via
             <a href="http://www.redferret.net/?p=9978">
              The Red Ferret Journal
             </a>
             ]
             <br/>
            </p>

        </h2>
        <div class="entry-category-classic_computing entry-category-doityourself entry-author-james_grahame entry-type-post entry" id="entry-45269468">
         <div class="entry-inner">
          <h3 class="entry-header">
           <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2008/02/diy-turn-your-o.html">
            DIY: Turn Your Oscilloscope Into An Old-School Terminal
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="entry-content">
           <input name="zemanta-related" type="hidden" val=""/>
           <div class="entry-body">
            <p>
             <img alt="AVR terminal" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/02/07/avrterminal.jpg" title="A serious Matrix vibe"/>
             <br/>
             Jan de Rie introduced his $24.95 Dutchtronix AVR Oscilloscope Clock kit almost a year ago. Now he's back with the AVR Scope terminal, an open source software upgrade that turns his clock into a seriously retro 20 x 12 display driver. It's a great way to repurpose your test bench oscilloscope to display serial information from your latest microcontroller-controlled widget or
             <a href="http://www.retrothing.com/2007/11/briel-kim-1-8-b.html">
              vintage computer
             </a>
             project.
            </p>
            <p>
             <img alt="AVR Clock" border="0" src="http://retrothing.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/02/07/avrclocksmall.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="How does one wind it up?"/>
             This project is a brilliant reminder of the early days of computing, when the Digital Equipment Corporation PDP-1 was programmed to play futuristic games like
             <a href="http://www.digibarn.com/history/06-09-21-Spacewar/index.html">
              Spacewar
             </a>
             on an oscilloscope display. Of course, vector display technology was eventually surpassed by less expensive raster displays (similar to those used in old-school TVs, where the screen is scanned line by line) built into dumb terminals.
            </p>
            <p>
             Jan tells us,
             <em>
              "The code is currently targeted for the Dutchtronix AVR Oscilloscope
Clock hardware but could be easily ported to an alternate platform
consisting of an AVR and some form of a DAC. The Scope Terminal displays 12 lines of 20 characters each. There is
also a line showing the current time (it's a clock hardware after all).
Configuration is possible using a "Command Mode" where you can use a
terminal (usually a PC) to set options (currently Time Change and baud rate change are supported). This alternate application can be downloaded to the AVR Oscilloscope
Clock using the built-in bootloader; no AVR programmer required."
             </em>
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="http://www.dutchtronix.com/">
              Dutchtronix AVR Oscilloscope Terminal [discontinued]
             </a>
            </p>
