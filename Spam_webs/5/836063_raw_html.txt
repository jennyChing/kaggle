     <p>
      <b>
       Mount Hood
      </b>
      , called
      <i>
       Wy'east
      </i>
      by the
      <a href="/wiki/Multnomah_people" title="Multnomah people">
       Multnomah tribe
      </a>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">
       stratovolcano
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Volcanoes" title="Cascade Volcanoes">
       Cascade Volcanic Arc
      </a>
      of northern
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      . It was formed by a
      <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
       subduction
      </a>
      zone on the
      <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">
       Pacific coast
      </a>
      and rests in the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">
       Pacific Northwest
      </a>
      region of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      . It is located about 50 miles (80km) east-southeast of
      <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">
       Portland
      </a>
      , on the border between
      <a href="/wiki/Clackamas_County,_Oregon" title="Clackamas County, Oregon">
       Clackamas
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hood_River_County,_Oregon" title="Hood River County, Oregon">
       Hood River
      </a>
      counties. In addition to being Oregon's highest mountain, it is one of the loftiest mountains in the nation based on its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_of_the_most_prominent_summits_of_the_United_States" title="Table of the most prominent summits of the United States">
       prominence
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The height assigned to Mount Hood's snow-covered peak has varied over its history. Modern sources point to three different heights: 11,249 feet (3,429m), a 1991 measurement by the
      <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Geodetic_Survey" title="U.S. National Geodetic Survey">
       U.S. National Geodetic Survey
      </a>
      (NGS),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngs_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ngs-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      11,240 feet (3,426m) based on a 1993 scientific expedition,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Register-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and 11,239 feet (3,426m)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trafford_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trafford-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of slightly older origin. The peak is home to 12 named
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
       glaciers
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowfield" title="Snowfield">
       snowfields
      </a>
      . It is the highest point in Oregon and the fourth highest in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">
       Cascade Range
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mount Hood is considered the Oregon volcano most likely to erupt,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though based on its history, an explosive eruption is unlikely. Still, the odds of an eruption in the next 30 years are estimated at between 3 and 7percent, so the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Geological_Survey" title="U.S. Geological Survey">
       U.S. Geological Survey
      </a>
      (USGS) characterizes it as "potentially active", but the mountain is informally considered
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dormant_volcano" title="Dormant volcano">
       dormant
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_Hazards_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-USGS_Hazards-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Establishments">
       Establishments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Establishments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Hood,_Oregon_by_William_Keith,_c1881-83.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="768" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Mount_Hood%2C_Oregon_by_William_Keith%2C_c1881-83.jpg/240px-Mount_Hood%2C_Oregon_by_William_Keith%2C_c1881-83.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Mount_Hood%2C_Oregon_by_William_Keith%2C_c1881-83.jpg/360px-Mount_Hood%2C_Oregon_by_William_Keith%2C_c1881-83.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Mount_Hood%2C_Oregon_by_William_Keith%2C_c1881-83.jpg/480px-Mount_Hood%2C_Oregon_by_William_Keith%2C_c1881-83.jpg 2x" width="240"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Hood,_Oregon_by_William_Keith,_c1881-83.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        William Keith (American, 18381911).
        <i>
         Mount Hood, Oregon
        </i>
        , ca. 18811883.
        <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">
         Brooklyn Museum
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Timberline_Lodge" title="Timberline Lodge">
       Timberline Lodge
      </a>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">
       National Historic Landmark
      </a>
      located on the southern flank of Mount Hood just below
      <a href="/wiki/Palmer_Glacier" title="Palmer Glacier">
       Palmer Glacier
      </a>
      , at approximately 6,000 feet (1,800m) above sea level.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The mountain has six
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ski_area" title="Ski area">
       ski areas
      </a>
      :
      <a href="/wiki/Timberline_Lodge_ski_area" title="Timberline Lodge ski area">
       Timberline
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hood_Meadows" title="Mount Hood Meadows">
       Mount Hood Meadows
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hood_Skibowl" title="Mount Hood Skibowl">
       Ski Bowl
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Spur_ski_area" title="Cooper Spur ski area">
       Cooper Spur
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Snow_Bunny" title="Snow Bunny">
       Snow Bunny
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Summit_Ski_Area" title="Summit Ski Area">
       Summit
      </a>
      . They total over 4,600 acres (7.2sqmi; 19km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) of skiable terrain; Timberline offers the only year-round lift-served skiing in North America.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-summerski_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-summerski-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Mount Hood is within the
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hood_National_Forest" title="Mount Hood National Forest">
       Mount Hood National Forest
      </a>
      , which comprises 1,067,043 acres (1,667sqmi; 4,318km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) of landincluding four designated
      <a href="/wiki/National_Wilderness_Preservation_System" title="National Wilderness Preservation System">
       wilderness areas
      </a>
      that total 314,078 acres (491sqmi; 1,271km
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      )and more than 1,200 miles (1,900km) of hiking trails.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoodfacts1_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hoodfacts1-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The most northwestern pass around the mountain is called Lolo Pass. Native Americans crossed the pass while traveling between the
      <a href="/wiki/Willamette_Valley" title="Willamette Valley">
       Willamette Valley
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Celilo_Falls" title="Celilo Falls">
       Celilo Falls
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
       Name
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="401" data-file-width="640" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg/220px-Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg/330px-Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg/440px-Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mounthoodoregonwithsnow.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        View of Mount Hood from the west
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mthoodtimothylake.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/94/Mthoodtimothylake.jpg/220px-Mthoodtimothylake.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/94/Mthoodtimothylake.jpg/330px-Mthoodtimothylake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/94/Mthoodtimothylake.jpg/440px-Mthoodtimothylake.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mthoodtimothylake.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Mt. Hood as seen from Timothy Lake.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multnomah_(tribe)" title="Multnomah (tribe)">
       Multnomah
      </a>
      name for Mount Hood was Wy'east. In one version of the legend, the two sons of the Great Spirit Sahale fell in love with the beautiful maiden Loowit, who could not decide which to choose. The two braves, Wy'east and Klickitat, burned forests and villages in their battle over her. Sahale became enraged and smote the three lovers. Seeing what he had done, he erected three mountain peaks to mark where each fell. He made beautiful
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">
       Mount St. Helens
      </a>
      for Loowit, proud and erect Mount Hood for Wy'east, and the somber
      <a href="/wiki/Mount_Adams_(Washington)" title="Mount Adams (Washington)">
       Mount Adams
      </a>
      for the mourning Klickitat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are other versions of the legend. In another telling, Wy'east (Hood) battles Pahto (Adams) for the fair La-wa-la-clough (St. Helens). Or again Wy'east, the chief of the Multnomah tribe, competed with the chief of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klickitat_(tribe)" title="Klickitat (tribe)">
       Klickitat tribe
      </a>
      . Their great anger led to their transformation into volcanoes. Their battle is said to have destroyed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bridge_of_the_Gods_(geologic_event)" title="Bridge of the Gods (geologic event)">
       Bridge of the Gods
      </a>
      and thus created the great
      <a href="/wiki/Cascades_Rapids" title="Cascades Rapids">
       Cascades Rapids
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River" title="Columbia River">
       Columbia River
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The mountain was given its present name on October29, 1792, by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Robert_Broughton" title="William Robert Broughton">
       Lt. William Broughton
      </a>
      , a member of Captain
      <a href="/wiki/George_Vancouver" title="George Vancouver">
       George Vancouver
      </a>
      's discovery expedition. Lt. Broughton observed its peak while at Belle Vue Point of what is now called
      <a href="/wiki/Sauvie_Island" title="Sauvie Island">
       Sauvie Island
      </a>
      during his travels up the Columbia River, writing, "A very high, snowy mountain now appeared rising beautifully conspicuous in the midst of an extensive tract of low or moderately elevated land [location of today's
      <a href="/wiki/Vancouver,_Washington" title="Vancouver, Washington">
       Vancouver, Washington
      </a>
      ] lying S 67 E., and seemed to announce a termination to the river." Lt. Broughton named the mountain after
      <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hood,_1st_Viscount_Hood" title="Samuel Hood, 1st Viscount Hood">
       Lord (Samuel) Hood
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">
       British
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">
       Admiral
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Chesapeake" title="Battle of the Chesapeake">
       Battle of the Chesapeake
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark" title="Lewis and Clark">
       Lewis and Clark
      </a>
      spotted the mountain on October18, 1805. A few days later at what would become
      <a href="/wiki/The_Dalles,_Oregon" title="The Dalles, Oregon">
       The Dalles
      </a>
      , Clark wrote, "The pinnacle of the round topped mountain, which we saw a short distance below the banks of the river, is South 43-degrees West of us and about 37 miles (60km). It is at this time topped with snow. We called this the Falls Mountain, or Timm Mountain." Timm was the native name for Celilo Falls. Clark later noted that it was also Vancouver's Mount Hood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firsttouch_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-firsttouch-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Two French explorers from the
      <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson's Bay Company">
       Hudson's Bay Company
      </a>
      may have traveled into the
      <a href="/wiki/Dog_River_(Oregon)" title="Dog River (Oregon)">
       Dog River
      </a>
      area east of Mount Hood in 1818. They reported climbing to a glacier on "Montagne de Neige" (
      <i>
       Mountain of Snow
      </i>
      ), probably Eliot Glacier.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firsttouch_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-firsttouch-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      There have been two
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      ammunition ships named for Mount Hood, despite the mountain's namesake having been an enemy commander.
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Mount_Hood_(AE-11)" title="USS Mount Hood (AE-11)">
       USS
       <i>
        Mount Hood
       </i>
       (AE-11)
      </a>
      was commissioned in July 1944 and was destroyed in November 1944 while at anchor in Manus Naval Base,
      <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_Islands" title="Admiralty Islands">
       Admiralty Islands
      </a>
      . Her explosive cargo ignited, resulting in 45 confirmed dead, 327 missing and 371 injured.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A second ammunition ship,
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Mount_Hood_(AE-29)" title="USS Mount Hood (AE-29)">
       AE-29
      </a>
      , was commissioned in May 1971 and decommissioned in August 1999.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">

       </a>

     <p>
      The glacially eroded summit area consists of several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andesitic" title="Andesitic">
       andesitic
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dacitic" title="Dacitic">
       dacitic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Lava_dome" title="Lava dome">
       lava domes
      </a>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">
       Pleistocene
      </a>
      collapses produced avalanches and
      <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">
       lahars
      </a>
      (rapidly moving mudflows) that traveled across the
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River" title="Columbia River">
       Columbia River
      </a>
      to the north. The eroded volcano has had at least four major eruptive periods during the past 15,000 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volcanoinformation_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-volcanoinformation-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The last three eruptions at Mount Hood occurred within the past 1,800 years from vents high on the southwest flank and produced deposits that were distributed primarily to the south and west along the
      <a href="/wiki/Sandy_River_(Oregon)" title="Sandy River (Oregon)">
       Sandy
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Zigzag_River" title="Zigzag River">
       Zigzag
      </a>
      rivers. The last eruptive period took place around 220 to 170 years ago, when dacitic lava domes, pyroclastic flows and mudflows were produced without major explosive eruptions. The prominent Crater Rock just below the summit is hypothesized to be the remains of one of these now-eroded domes. This period includes the last major eruption of 1781 to 1782 with a slightly more recent episode ending shortly before the arrival of Lewis and Clark in 1805. The latest minor eruptive event occurred in August 1907.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volcanoinformation_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-volcanoinformation-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_hood_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vulcan_hood-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The glaciers on the mountain's upper slopes may be a source of potentially dangerous lahars when the mountain next erupts. There are vents near the summit that are known for emitting gases such as
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       carbon dioxide
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">
       sulfur dioxide
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lavadomes_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-lavadomes-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Prior to the
      <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">
       1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens
      </a>
      , the only known fatality related to volcanic activity in the Cascades occurred in 1934, when a climber suffocated in oxygen-poor air while exploring ice caves melted by
      <a href="/wiki/Fumarole" title="Fumarole">
       fumaroles
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Coalman_Glacier" title="Coalman Glacier">
       Coalman Glacier
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 1950, there have been several
      <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_swarm" title="Earthquake swarm">
       earthquake swarms
      </a>
      each year at Mount Hood, most notably in July 1980 and June 2002.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Seismic activity is monitored by the USGS
      <a href="/wiki/Cascades_Volcano_Observatory" title="Cascades Volcano Observatory">
       Cascades Volcano Observatory
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Vancouver,_Washington" title="Vancouver, Washington">
       Vancouver, Washington
      </a>
      , which issues weekly updates (and daily updates if significant eruptive activity is occurring at a Cascades volcano).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The most recent evidence of volcanic activity at Mount Hood consists of fumaroles near Crater Rock and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_springs" title="Hot springs">
       hot springs
      </a>
      on the flanks of the volcano.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DONF_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DONF-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Mount Hood was first seen by European explorers in 1792 and is believed to have maintained a consistent summit elevation, varying by no more than a few feet due to mild seismic activity. Elevation changes since the 1950s are predominantly due to improved survey methods and model refinements of the shape of the Earth (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
       vertical reference datum
      </a>
      ). Despite the physical consistency, the estimated elevation of Mount Hood has varied substantially over the years.
     </p>
     <p>
      Early explorers on the Columbia River estimated the elevation to be 10,000 to 12,000 feet (3,000 to 3,700m). Two persons in Thomas J. Dryer's 1854 expedition calculated the elevation to be 18,361 feet (5,596m) and that the
      <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">
       tree line
      </a>
      was at about 11,250 feet (3,430m). Two months later, a Mr. Belden claimed to have climbed the mountain during a hunting trip and determined it to be 19,400 feet (5,900m) upon which "pores oozed blood, eyes bled, and blood rushed from their ears." Sometime by 1866, Reverend G. H. Atkinson determined it to be 17,600 feet (5,400m). A Portland engineer used surveying methods from a Portland baseline and calculated a height of between 18,000 and 19,000 feet (5,500 and 5,800m). Many maps distributed in the late 19thcentury cited 18,361 feet (5,596m), though
      <a href="/wiki/Mitchell%27s_School_Atlas" title="Mitchell's School Atlas">
       Mitchell's School Atlas
      </a>
      gave 14,000 feet (4,300m) as the correct value. For some time, many references assumed Mount Hood to be the highest point in North America.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grauer_29-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-grauer-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern height surveys also vary, but not by the huge margins seen in the past. A 1993 survey by a scientific party that arrived at the peak's summit with 16 pounds (7.3kg) of electronic equipment reported a height of 11,240 feet (3,430m), claimed to be accurate to within 1.25 inches (32mm).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Register_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Register-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many modern sources likewise list 11,240 feet (3,430m) as the height.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, numerous others place the peak's height one foot lower, at 11,239 feet (3,426m).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trafford_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trafford-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Finally, a height of 11,249 feet (3,429m) has also been reported.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngs_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-ngs-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">

      </sup>

     <p>
      Mount Hood is host to 12
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      named glaciers or
      <a href="/wiki/Snow_field" title="Snow field">
       snow fields
      </a>
      , the most visited of which is Palmer Glacier, partially within the
      <a href="/wiki/Timberline_Lodge_ski_area" title="Timberline Lodge ski area">
       Timberline Lodge ski area
      </a>
      and on the most popular climbing route. The glaciers are almost exclusively above the 6,000-foot (1,800m) level, which also is about the average
      <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">
       tree line
      </a>
      elevation on Mount Hood. More than 80percent of the glacial surface area is above 7,000 feet (2,100m).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tree_line_40-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tree_line-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspp1365_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gspp1365-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The glaciers and permanent snow fields have an area of 3,331 acres (1,348ha) and contain a volume of about 282,000 acre feet (0.348km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      ).
      <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Glacier" title="Eliot Glacier">
       Eliot Glacier
      </a>
      is the largest glacier by volume at 73,000 acre feet (0.09km
      <sup>
       3
      </sup>
      ), and has the thickest depth measured by ice radar at 361 feet (110m). The largest glacier by surface area is the Coe-Ladd Glacier system at 531 acres (215ha).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gspp1365_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gspp1365-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Glaciers and snowfields cover about 80percent of the mountain above the 6,900-foot (2,100m) level. The glaciers declined by an average of 34percent from 19072004. Glaciers on Mount Hood retreated through the first half of the 20thcentury, advanced or at least slowed their retreat in the 1960s and 1970s, and have since returned to a pattern of retreat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The neo-glacial maximum extents formed in the early 18thcentury.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the last major glacial event between 29,000 and 10,000 years ago, glaciers reached down to the 2,600-to-2,300-foot (790 to 700m) level, a distance of 9.3 miles (15.0km) from the summit. The retreat released considerable outwash, some of which filled and flattened the upper
      <a href="/wiki/Hood_River_Valley" title="Hood River Valley">
       Hood River Valley
      </a>
      near
      <a href="/wiki/Parkdale,_Oregon" title="Parkdale, Oregon">
       Parkdale
      </a>
      and formed
      <a href="/wiki/Dee,_Oregon" title="Dee, Oregon">
       Dee Flat
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_8-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Older glaciation produced moraines near
      <a href="/wiki/Brightwood,_Oregon" title="Brightwood, Oregon">
       Brightwood
      </a>
      and distinctive cuts on the southeast side; they may date to 140,000 years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swanson_8-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Swanson-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climbing">
       Climbing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climbing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="717" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg/220px-Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg/330px-Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg/440px-Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Hood_Landmarks.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Landmarks along the southern climbing route of Mount Hood
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Hood_summit.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="2400" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mount_Hood_summit.jpg/220px-Mount_Hood_summit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mount_Hood_summit.jpg/330px-Mount_Hood_summit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mount_Hood_summit.jpg/440px-Mount_Hood_summit.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Hood_summit.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Aerial photograph of Mt. Hood's summit
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Mount Hood is Oregon's highest point and a prominent landmark visible up to 100 miles (160km) away. It has convenient access and a minimum of technical climbing challenges. About 10,000 people attempt to climb Mount Hood each year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goesright_45-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-goesright-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are no trails to the summit. Even the "easier" southside climbing route is a technical climb with crevasses, falling rocks, and often inclement weather. Ropes,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_axes" title="Ice axes">
       ice axes
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Crampons" title="Crampons">
       crampons
      </a>
      and other technical
      <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">
       mountaineering
      </a>
      gear are necessary. Peak climbing season is generally from April to mid-June.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are six main routes to approach the mountain with about 30 total variations for summiting. The climbs range in difficulty from class 2 to class 5.9+ (for
      <i>
       Arachnophobia
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The most popular route, dubbed the south route, begins at Timberline Lodge and proceeds up Palmer Glacier to Crater Rock, the large prominence at the head of the glacier. The route goes east around Crater Rock and crosses Coalman Glacier on the Hogsback, a ridge spanning from Crater Rock to the approach to the summit. The Hogsback terminates at a
      <a href="/wiki/Bergschrund" title="Bergschrund">
       bergschrund
      </a>
      where Coalman Glacier separates from the summit rock
      <a href="/wiki/Headwall" title="Headwall">
       headwall
      </a>
      , and then to Pearly Gates, a gap in the summit rock formation, then right onto the summit plateau and the summit proper.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Technical ice axes, fall protection, and experience are now recommended in order to attempt the left chute variation or Pearly Gates ice chute. The Forest Service is recommending several other route options due to these changes in conditions (e.g. "Old Chute", West Crater Rim, etc.).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climbing_accidents">
       Climbing accidents
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climbing accidents">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
        Main article:
        <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hood_climbing_accidents" title="Mount Hood climbing accidents">
         Mount Hood climbing accidents
        </a>
       </div>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      As of May 2002, more than 130 people had died in climbing-related accidents since records have been kept on Mount Hood, the first in 1896.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Incidents in May 1986, December 2006, and December 2009 attracted intense national and international media interest. Though avalanches are a common hazard on other glaciated mountains, most Mount Hood climbing deaths are the result of falls and
      <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">
       hypothermia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite a quadrupling of forest visitors since 1990, fewer than 50 people require rescue per year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Only 3.4percent of search and rescue missions in 2006 were for mountain climbers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hiking">
       Hiking
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Hood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hiking">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Timberline_Trail" title="Timberline Trail">
       Timberline Trail
      </a>
      , which circumnavigates the entire mountain, was built in the 1930s by the
      <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">
       Civilian Conservation Corps
      </a>
      . Typically, the 40.7-mile (65.5km) hike is snow-free from late July until the autumn snows begin. A portion of the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Crest_Trail" title="Pacific Crest Trail">
       Pacific Crest Trail
      </a>
      is coincident with the Timberline Trail on the west side of Mount Hood.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timberlinetrail_54-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-timberlinetrail-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorp_55-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gorp-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The predecessor of the Pacific Crest Trail was the
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Skyline_Trail" title="Oregon Skyline Trail">
       Oregon Skyline Trail
      </a>
      , established in 1920, which connected Mount Hood to
      <a href="/wiki/Crater_Lake" title="Crater Lake">
       Crater Lake
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FS1921_56-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FS1921-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
