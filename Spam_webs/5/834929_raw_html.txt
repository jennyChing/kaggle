          <article class="node full-view kind-article slug-innovation-by-design " data-resource-id="3036035" id="post-3036035" itemscope="" itemtype="http://nik.io/v1/schema/Article">
           <span content="http://www.fastcodesign.com/3036035/ipod-mastermind-tony-fadell-on-the-death-of-the-ipod-you-cant-get-too-nostalgic" itemprop="permalink">

           <h4 class="supertag slug-is-innovation-by-design" itemscope="" itemtype="http://data-vocabulary.org/Breadcrumb">
            <a href="/section/innovation-by-design" itemprop="url">
             <em itemprop="title">
              Innovation By Design
             </em>
            </a>
           </h4>
           <header>
            <div class="summary row collapse">
             <div class="twelve columns">
              <h1 class="title" itemprop="title headline">
               iPod Mastermind Tony Fadell On The Death Of The iPod: "You Can't Get Too Nostalgic"
              </h1>
             </div>
             <span class="deck pull-three" itemprop="deck description">
              <p>
               After 13 years, the iPod Classic is dead. Google's Tony Fadell tells us what killed the iPod, and why we shouldn't be bummed about it.
              </p>
             </span>
            </div>
            <div class="tools row collapse">
             <div class="twelve columns hide-on-print">
              <div class="share-box">
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </header>
           <div class="row collapse">
            <div class="body prose twelve columns" itemprop="body articleBody">
             <p>
              What does it feel like to lose your design child?
             </p>
             <aside class="info">
              <div class="info-inner">
               <p>
                Join us at the
                <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/innovation-by-design-awards/2014#ibd2014-post-3026404" target="_self">
                 2014 Innovation By Design Awards and Conference
                </a>
                October 15 in New York for an intimate conversation with Nest CEO
                <a class="fc-plugin people-page" data-id="fastcompany|node|3020443" data-name="peoplePages" href="//www.fastcompany.com/person/tony-fadell">
                 <img src="//e.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/320x240/Tony-Fadell-P4A0760.jpg"/>
                 Tony Fadell
                </a>
                .
                <a href="https://www.etouches.com/ereg/newreg.php?eventid=93355&amp;utm_source=FastCompany.com&amp;utm_medium=Website&amp;utm_content=IBD2014&amp;utm_campaign=IBD2014" target="_blank">
                 Sign up today
                </a>
                .
               </p>
              </div>
             </aside>
             <p>
              Ask Google's Tony Fadell. Before he started
              <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/3035239/innovation-by-design-2014/nest-hatches-a-connected-home-boom" target="_self">
               supercharging our thermostats
              </a>
              , Fadell was mostly known for his work at Apple. His breakout success back in 2001 was the iPod, Apple's revolutionary digital media player, a product he
              <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/1665275/the-ipods-inventor-strikes-out-on-his-own-and-inventsa-thermostat" target="_self">
               oversaw
              </a>
              as senior vice president until his departure from the company in 2008.
             </p>
             <p>
              Last week, Apple
              <a href="http://www.popmatters.com/post/185765-requiem-for-perfection-r.i.p.-ipod-classic/" target="_blank">
               killed off the iPod Classic
              </a>
              , the last remaining iPod model that still resembled Fadell's original design: a big laptop hard drive full of MP3s with a click wheel on front.
             </p>
             <figure class="inline-large inline">
              <img alt="" src="//d.fastcompany.net/multisite_files/fastcompany/imagecache/inline-large/inline/2014/09/3036035-inline-3035239-poster-p-2-nest-hatches-a-connected-home-boom.jpg"/>
              <figcaption>
               Tony Fadell, father of the iPod. Photo: Pari Dukovic
              </figcaption>
             </figure>
             <aside class="pullquote">
              <q>
               Fadell acknowledges that the iPod was born to die.
              </q>
             </aside>
             <p>
              "I'm sad to see it go," Fadell admits in a phone interview. "The iPod's been a huge part of my life for the last decade. The team that worked on the iPod poured literally everything into making it what it was." Eighteen months after launch, the iPod owned the portable media player category, and for the next decade, it continued to do so. "Products just don't come around like that often," laments Fadell. "The iPod was one-in-a-million."
             </p>
             <p>
              At the same time, Fadell acknowledges that the iPod was born to die. "It was inevitable something would take its place. You know, in 2003 or 2004, we started asking ourselves what would kill the iPod," Fadell says. "And even back then, at Apple, we knew it was streaming. We called it the 'celestial jukebox in the sky.' And we have that now: music in the cloud."
             </p>
             <p>
              But streaming music wasn't the only thing that killed the iPod Classic, Fadell says. The people who bought iPod Classics for their cars are now using hand-me-down smartphones instead. And for the people who want to have every single song they own with them at all times, the new iPhone 6 comes with 128GB storage, which is about the same amount of space the iPod Classic shipped withand unlike the Classic, the iPhone 6 can pull any songs it can't fit on its massive drive from the cloud.
              <br/>
              <aside class="pullquote">
               <q>
                There are people out there who still want the Commodore 64 or the Amiga to come back. That's cute, but time marches on.
               </q>
              </aside>
             </p>
             <p>
              "I'll miss the iPod. I loved it," says Fadell. "But you know, that's just how it is. I also loved my Apple II, and also saw it come and go. You can't get too nostalgic. I mean, there are people out there who still want the Commodore 64 or the Amiga to come back. That's cute, but time marches on. It's better to be excited for the future."
             </p>
             <p>
              As for the future of music: It's not iPods, iPhones, or iPads. It's
              <a href="http://www.fastcodesign.com/3025991/the-next-big-thing-in-music-apps-that-read-your-mind" target="_self">
               apps that read your mind
              </a>
              . "Now that we all have access to all the music we could ever want, discoverability is the new Holy Grail," Fadell says. "Using machine learning and AI to figure out context, so that the celestial jukebox knows the perfect song for every occasion."
             </p>
             <p>
              Like, say, the perfect funeral dirge for the portable media player he created. But since that technology doesn't yet exist, Fadell joked that he plans on loading up the "Zoloft - Sad Music" playlist on his iPod instead.
             </p>
            </div>

Steaming is a money maker for all interested parties; cell phone data charges, and your monthly credit card percentage, etc. fortunately, there are some alternatives, albeit not quite as smooth in terms of ease of use; I have just transferred my music library to a Fiio X5, and in spite of some hiccups I really like it- the sound quality is better than the iPods. And, I won't be purchasing as much music from Apple, which was a substantial amount of money per year. I am investigating HD Audio downloads. Apple is becoming a mass consumer appliance company, and, the minority of classical and jazz listeners will have to move up to audiophile quality equipment.
                    </p>
