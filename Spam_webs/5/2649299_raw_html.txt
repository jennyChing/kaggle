  <h2 class="Q">
   According to Bohrs theory, the angular momentum of an electron in 5th orbit is:
  </h2>
  <p align="right" class="Q">
   (AIEEE 2006)
  </p>
  <p>
   a) 25h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   b) 1.0h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   c) 10h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   d) 2.5h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p class="Q">
   <b>
    Logic:
   </b>
  </p>
  <p>
   The angular momentum (mvr) of electron in nth orbit is equal to nh/2
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     .
    </font>
   </b>
  </p>
  <p class="Q">
   <b>
    Solution:
   </b>
  </p>
  <p>
   For the electron in 5th orbit,
  </p>
  <p>
   angular momentum = mvr = 5h/2
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     
    </font>
   </b>
   = 2.5h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    
   </font>
  </p>
  <p class="Q">
   Conclusion:
  </p>
  <p>
   Correct option is "d".
  </p>

   Extra information
  </p>
  <p>
   Note: nh/2
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     
    </font>
   </b>
   gives angular momentum of electron revolving in a circular
   <i>
    orbit
   </i>

  <h4 class="Q">
   4) The angular momentum of an electron present in the excited state of hydrogen is 1.5h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
   . The electron is present in:
  </h4>
  <p align="right" class="Q">
   (EAMCET 2006-M)
  </p>
  <p>
   a) Third orbit
  </p>
  <p>
   b) Second orbit
  </p>
  <p>
   c) Fourth orbit
  </p>
  <p>
   d) Fifth orbit
  </p>
  <p class="Q">
   Solution:
  </p>
  <p>
   1.5h/
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     
    </font>
   </b>
   = 3 x h/2
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   Therefore n = 3 and the electron is in the third orbit of excited state.
  </p>
  <h3 class="Q">
   5) In a hydrogen atom, the electron is at a distance of 4.768  from the nucleus. The angular momentum of the electron is:
  </h3>
  <p align="right" class="Q">
   (EAMCET 2010-M)
  </p>
  <p>
   a) 3h/2
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     
    </font>
   </b>
  </p>
  <p>
   b) h/2
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     
    </font>
   </b>
  </p>
  <p>
   c) h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   d) 2h/
   <font face="Times New Roman">
    <b>
     
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p class="Q">
   Logic:
  </p>
  <p>
   First find out the n value for the orbital using equation:
  </p>
  <p>
   <img alt="radius of bohr's orbit" border="0" height="48" src="q2-1.png" width="315"/>
  </p>
  <p>
   Then calculate the angular momentum using the formula:
  </p>
  <p>
   <font color="#0000FF">
    mvr = nh/2
    <b>
     <font face="Times New Roman">
      
     </font>
    </b>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p class="Q">
   Solution:
  </p>
  <p>
   Radius of orbit (r) = 0.529 x n
   <sup>
    2
   </sup>
   /Z 
  </p>
  <p>
   or
  </p>
  <p>
   4.768  = 0.529 x n
   <sup>
    2
   </sup>
   /1 
  </p>
  <p>
   or
  </p>
  <p>
   n
   <sup>
    2
   </sup>
   = 4.768 / 0.529 = 9.013
  </p>
  <p>
   Therefore,
  </p>
  <p>
   n = 3
  </p>
  <p>
   And the angular momentum, mvr = 3h/2
   <b>
    <font face="Times New Roman">
     
    </font>
   </b>
  </p>
