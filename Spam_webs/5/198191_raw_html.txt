     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-168162 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="168162" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="article-inline-half">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half/public/blogs/1595/2015/01/168162-174392.jpg?itok=pJVC4dwb" title="Andy Dean Photography/Shutterstock"/>
              </div>
              <div class="caption">
               <p class="image-caption">
                Andy Dean Photography/Shutterstock
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            George Bernard Shaw famously said, Patriotism is your conviction that this country is superior to all others because you were born in it.
           </p>
           <p>
            I would extend his theme to cover something that comes naturally to us all, which Ill call
            <em>
             Pseudo-exceptionalism
            </em>
            the unearned conviction that
            <em>
             we
            </em>
            are exceptional, superior to others because we were born...
            <em>
             us
            </em>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            We simply assume that were kinder, more honest, more realistic, more wholesome than those around us. After all, were married to ourselves for life, so we make accommodations: We cut ourselves slack. Were fast to forgive ourselves. When challenged, were much better at making our case than our opponents. We spot injustices to ourselves far faster than we spot our injustices to others.
           </p>
           <p>
            All living organisms serve themselves, of course, but through language, we humans have the capacity to rationalize our self-service. Were masters at coming up with any argument we need in order to keep
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/forgiveness" title="Psychology Today looks at forgiving">
             forgiving
            </a>
            , accepting, and liking ourselves. This tendency can be overcome but it takes conscious work that doesnt come naturally. (Perhaps encouraging people to do this work should be a higher priority in
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/education" title="Psychology Today looks at education">
             education
            </a>
            , certainly in
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/ethics-and-morality" title="Psychology Today looks at moral">
             moral
            </a>
            education.)
           </p>
           <p>
            If youve ever suggested that someone has behaved badly, and gotten a response like, I would never do that. My intentions are good, you've witnessed pseudo-exceptionalism at work. The accused looks inward in response to your challenge, but gingerly and selectively. He doesnt look for possible ulterior motives; he looks for the beliefs that he has embraced in principle:
            <em>
             Im someone who believes that one should be honest
            </em>
            is all he needs to find in order to respond with, That couldnt be me. Im not the kind who does that. Others? Sure. But not me.
           </p>
           <p>
            Pseudo-exceptionalism subsidizes our sense of self-confidence, and while many people harbor self-doubt, that does not temper pseudo-exceptionalism so much as feed it. If youre filled with self-doubt already, someone
            <em>
             else
            </em>
            doubting you could make your pseudo-exceptionalism work overtime to keep you feeling okay about yourself. You become that much more defensive.
           </p>
           <p>
            If thats you, people wont know how to deal with you. On the one hand, they can see that youre full of self-doubt; on the other, you come across as a know-it-all, very full of yourself. Out of respect for your hypersensitivity, they may walk on eggshells around you. But in response to your hyperactive compensatory pseudo-exceptionalism, theyll want to get through to you.
           </p>
           <p>
            Theres a lot of popular concern these days about dealing with narcissists and psychopaths. Such people exist, but are far rarer than pseudo-exceptionalists. Its safe to assume were
            <em>
             all
            </em>
            pseudo-exceptionalists to some extent. (And chances are, the person youre inclined to label as a
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/narcissism" title="Psychology Today looks at narcissist ">
             narcissist
            </a>
            or
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/conditions/antisocial-personality-disorder" title="Psychology Today looks at sociopath">
             sociopath
            </a>
            is just a garden-variety pseudo-exceptionalist.)
           </p>
           <p>
            The biggest side effect of pseudo-exceptionalism is something Ill call passive-exploitive behaviorbasically taking up whatever room one can get away with taking, not as a consciously active campaign, but as a passive outcome of thinking, as we all do, that were special, and therefore
            <em>
             entitled to a bit more than others
            </em>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            Passive-aggressiveness is just one form that passive-exploitive behavior takes, chiefly when ones exaggerated sense of entitlement is thwarted, and one senses that he or she is therefore entitled to
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/punishment" title="Psychology Today looks at revenge">
             revenge
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            More generally, passive-exploitive behavior includes any behavior pursued in the dark about ones own tendency toward pseudo-exceptionalism. It can include talking over others; giving less attention to their preferences than yours; justifying yourself more readily than you consider other peoples justifications; and rationalizing why you deserve more by whatever means are availableand a great many means are available to our rationalizing minds. The pseudo-exceptionalist's prayer is, Grant me one good reason why I can do what I want to do." Given our imaginations, that prayer is invariably granted.
           </p>
           <p>
            Do you know anyone who engages in passive-exploitive behaviors? The better question may be, Do you know anyone who doesnt?
           </p>

                 <p>
                  Hi Valentina,
                  <br/>
                  It's a great question and one I've been wondering about myself over the years and actually a lot in recent weeks. First, I'd say that pseudo-exceptionalism is often highest in people who beat themselves up a lot, because the more you blame yourself the more you tend to accumulate pride and self-justification to compensate for it. When someone challenges you, you'll be quick to protect and justify yourself  because you are already self-critical and can't take any more criticism. That quick self-protection and self-justification is at the heart of passive exploitive behavior. Defensiveness has an offensive quality. When I say a quick, automatic "No you're wrong about me," I'm showing an unwillingness to hear other people, which borders on exploiting them.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I think we start to overcome our pseudo-exceptionalism by noticing that we have the same flaws and prospects as other people have.  That can be very disappointing. We like to go around pointing out other people's flaws. It gives us the impression that we don't have them and therefore are bound to do better in the world. But it can also be a relief to notice that our personal flaws and prospects are everyone's. In other words that our personal condition is the human condition.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  To the extent that you have built a relatively safe and secure life in which you can keep food on the table, satisfying activities on the to do list and keep your health, you are safe and free enough to ignore status questions.  I think some of it comes with age. When we're younger we don't know what to expect from ourselves, and we oscillate between self-loathing and self-aggrandizement. With time, our expectations align pretty well with our prospects, and if you've got a decent life you can get over the whole question of where you stand.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I've worked to pre-grieve all sorts of negative prospects for myself. Could I end up like that loser? Yes I could. I could fail in all the ways other people fail.  I'm not an exception.  At first that's a scary thought, but eventually you get to where it's OK. And OK doesn't mean you're resigned to losing. It means that if someone on some event hints that you might be heading toward that losing status, you don't freak out. You've already considered that possibility. It's not news that you could lose. It makes you more receptive than if you're busy keeping all hints of personal failing at bay. That's the gift of really feeling down to your bones your own humanness, warts and all. And you don't use your humanness as a rationale for failing (hey, we're all human). You still strive to be better but in a more realistic context about who you are.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Something like that. Here are a few articles related to that:
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <a href="http://www.mindreadersdictionary.com/can-i-change-him/communication/pre-grief/">
                   http://www.mindreadersdictionary.com/can-i-change-him/communication/pre-grief/
                  </a>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <a href="https://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/ambigamy/201307/metastability-necessity-happiness-and-evolution">
                   https://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/ambigamy/201307/metastability-necessity-happiness-and...
                  </a>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Feel free to write back if I didn't answer your question or you have other questions. And thanks for thinking with me too.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Jeremy
