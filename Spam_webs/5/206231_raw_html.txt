     <span id="print_title">
      <h2>
       Which Came First, the Chicken or the Egg? ?? 
      </h2>
     </span>
     <p class="title">
      <span id="print_date">
       2014-10-31
      </span>
      <span style="padding-right:10px;">
       <a href="javascript:printArticle();">
        <img alt="print" src="http://www.visitbeijing.com.cn/upload/cn_visitbj/new/print_btn.gif"/>
       </a>
      </span>
     </p>
     <div class="cont" id="cont">
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        There are two famous riddles about 
chickens. One investigates the reasoning behind the chickens desire to cross 
the road (to get to the other side), while the other poses the ontological 
quandary: which came first, the chicken or the egg?
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        We shant attempt to answer the 
question in a philosophical or biologicalmanner, but we can answer it 
lexicographically. And, looking in the Oxford English Dictionary (OED), we can 
reveal that the answer is... not conclusive.
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        Chicken
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        Interesting, the earliest recorded 
use of chicken in English refers to the young of the domestic fowl in a 
simile. The word dates to an Old English interlinear gloss of the Lindisfarne 
Gospels: Su henne somnigas cicceno hire. This is taken from Matthew 23:37, 
where Jesus says of Jerusalem that He has longed to gather your children 
together as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings (in the New International 
Version).
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        chicken2337
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        In this quotation, chicken is used 
to describe the young of the fowl, rather than the adult (chicken as a domestic 
fowl of any age is first found, according to current OED research, as late as 
the 19th century) - but the same verse is also one of the earliest recorded 
examples of hen (the adult female of the common fowl).
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        chicken19chickenhen 

       </font>
      </p>
      <table align="center" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0">
       <tbody>
        <tr>
         <td align="center">
          <table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0">
           <tr>
            <td>
             <img alt="Which Came First, the Chicken or the Egg? ?? " src="http://images.visitbeijing.com.cn/20141031/Img215088036.jpg"/>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </table>
          <a href="http://images.visitbeijing.com.cn/20141031/Img215088036.jpg">
          </a>
         </td>
        </tr>
       </tbody>
      </table>
      <br/>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        Egg
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        Things get a bit more complex when 
we turn our attention to egg. One of the oldest examples of the word in English 
is found in an Old English excerpt that translates as But if it be a fast-day, 
they are to be given a wey of cheese, and of fish, butter, and eggs, as much as 
they can get. In this instance, listed alongside cheese, fish, and butter, it 
is clear that the egg is (as the OED definition phrases it) an egg of a 
domestic fowl as an article of food.
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        eggeggegg 
egg
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        All well and good. But we are more 
interested (for the purposes of this riddle) in egg meaning the (more or less) 
spheroidal body produced by the female of birds and other animal species, and 
containing the germ of a new individual, enclosed within a shell or firm 
membrane. Well, this is found in another Old English document: On ge bi 
gioleca on middan (In an egg there is a yolk in the middle). Although the 
egg-as-offspring must predate the egg-as-culinary-fare, we do not know which was 
written about first.
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        egg 

       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        A draw
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        So, when somebody asks you Which 
came first, the chicken or the egg?, you can question what they mean by chicken 
and what they mean by egg, but you cant offer a conclusive lexicographical 
answer; both date from the Old English period, so well call it a draw. It is 
also worth noting that neither chicken nor egg have yet been revised in the 
ongoing creation of the third edition of the OED. Its possible that this 
question will be resolved when the entries are updated, but for now we cant be 
certain.
       </font>
      </p>
      <p>
       <font face="arial, helvetica, sans-serif">
        chicken 
egg
       </font>
      </p>
