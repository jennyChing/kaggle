               Why Bother Being A Good Parent At All?
              </h2>

                  <div style="float: left; margin-right: 5px;">
                   <a class="external-link" href="http://view.picapp.com/pictures.photo/creative/photo-caricature-blonde/image/8507271?term=woman+halo" target="_blank">
                    <img alt="photo caricature of a blonde angel with wings and a halo as she points at the camera" border="0" height="341" oncontextmenu="return false;" ondrag="return false;" onmousedown="return false;" src="http://view.picapp.com/pictures.photo/image/8507271/photo-caricature-blonde/photo-caricature-blonde.jpg?size=234&amp;imageId=8507271" title="photo caricature of a blonde angel with wings and a halo as she points at the camera" width="234"/>

                  <p>
                   I had a theory going into parenting:
                   <em>
                    If I was, or at least acted like, the best parent ever, I would have the best behaved children ever.
                   </em>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <em>
                   </em>
                   I told myself:
                   <em>
                    I must model the behavior I seek.
                   </em>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   If I dont want my children to grab, I dont grab things out of their hands unless there is danger.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   If I want my children to be polite, I use please and thank you even when I want to scream a startling amount of curse words at them.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I use a gentle voice and hands.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I ask first.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I apologize.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   And I managed to yell only a handful of times in my son's first three years of life, which awes my sharp tongue and
                   <a class="external-link elf-add-back-link" href="http://www.blogher.com/frame.php?url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.lateenough.com%2F2010%2F05%2Fmy-miracle-is-that-i-have-never-hit-my-children%2F" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                    bright temper
                   </a>
                   .
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   But with baby number two and
                   <a class="external-link elf-add-back-link" href="http://www.blogher.com/frame.php?url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.lateenough.com%2F2010%2F07%2Fgod-i-cannot-handle-it%2F" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                    Es continued tantrums
                   </a>
                   , I began to backslide.  I yell more.  But I catch myself quickly and apologize.  I am tired and overwhelmed, but still strongly clinging to my good parent mantra.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Until now.  When I gaze enviously at the parents shouting, grabbing, ignoring their children.  And those kids seem no worse than mine.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I become more careless and self-justifying as I wade through the exhaustion and disappointment of motherhood.  I begin to snap and grab and assume and demand.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I keep thinking:
                   <em>
                    Why spend so much time and effort reining myself in?  My children still fuss and struggle and ignore and fight.  Haven't four years of parenting taught me how little I matter?
                   </em>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Because when I raise my voice, my children listen.  When I grab something, the problem is over in five seconds rather than five minutes.  And when I ignore them, they show me more love.  Or go away and let me finish what I'm doing.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   So I think:
                   <em>
                    See.  It's okay.  It's worth it.  They deserve it.  I deserve it.
                   </em>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   But I've carried an ache this week.  I dont want my children to remember me yelling at them.  Pushing them around with my voice and adultness.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   So I pray.  Because I feel so lost.  Because I am afraid of who I'll become.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   And in my fear and brokenness, a thought surfaces as it often does when I pay attention to that spark of God or love or whatever it is that makes us human and empathetic.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   The words crowd out the cacophony of guilt and self-righteousness.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   <em>
                    You work to be a good parent because it is the right thing to do.
                   </em>
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   And I remember that parenting is not about the outcome.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Its not even about my children.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Sure Id love well-adjusted, gentle, God-loving, liberal children.  But there are too many factors out of my control.  I did not birth lumps of clay no matter how strange
                   <a class="external-link elf-add-back-link" href="http://www.blogher.com/frame.php?url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.lateenough.com%2F2010%2F05%2Fa-mama-who-doesn%25E2%2580%2599t-like-babies-also-known-as-me%2F" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                    newborns look to me
                   </a>
                   . My children have tendencies and character that will place them on journeys with amazing highs and terrible lows of which I have no control.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Yes, I can nurture certain qualities in them and teach them how to cope with others.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Yes, I can be an example of love and possibility.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   But I cannot force them to be anything other than who they are meant to be.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   And I can only be who I am meant to be.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Im meant to be a good mom.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Mostly for me.
                  </p>
