        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Episode 122: Good Egress
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta postauthordate">
         <span class="byline">

       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         When designing a commercial structure, there isone safety componentthat must be designed right into the building from the start: egress.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-3464">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/10276043525_6c530a58ab_z.jpg">
          <img alt="10276043525_6c530a58ab_z" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3468" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/10276043525_6c530a58ab_z.jpg?resize=640%2C426"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          [Credit:
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/smartsignbrooklyn/10276043525" target="_blank">
           MySafetySign.com
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Egress refers to anentire exit system from a building: stairs, corridors, and evacuation routes outside the building. Each states building code specifiesa certain number of means of egress, depending on the size and purpose of the structure.
        </p>
        <p>
         Simply put, therehave to be enough doors, corridors, and stairs for every occupant to exit in an orderly manner in the event of an emergency.
        </p>
        <p>
         Historically, the biggest threat to architecture has been fire, and architecture has evolved to resistit. In the 1700s, the best that building occupants could do in the event of a fire was to shoutfor firemen, who wouldbring the fire escapeessentiallya cart with a ladder on it.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/ahlco_truck10_web.jpg">
          <img alt="ahlco_truck10_web" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3475" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/ahlco_truck10_web.jpg?resize=640%2C373"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          [The earliest fire escapes were mobile. Credit:
          <a href="http://www.reliancefirecompany.org/history.html" target="_blank">
           Reliance Fire Company
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Fire escape methods becameincorporated
         <i>
          into
         </i>
         architecture with the invention of the scuttle. The scuttle looked like a modern skylight with an attached ladder, allowing one to access the roof, at which point that person could walk onto a neighbors roof and climb down through their scuttle.
        </p>
        <p>
         Many cities required thatscuttles be incorporated in new construction, and it was the first time that architecture wasregulated for the sake of fire safety.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/roof_scuttle_s_la-1.jpg">
          <img alt="2014-07-07 05.38.54 pm" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-3510" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-05.38.54-pm.png?resize=640%2C323"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Credit:
          <a href="http://www.bilco.com/foundations/store/shopdetail.asp?product=1GS-1&amp;ta" target="_blank">
           Bilco.com
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         By about 1860, New York began to require means of egress in tenement buildings.Landlords, of course, often went with the least expensive egress option: rope
         <i>
          .
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Houghtons_Fire_Escape_1877.jpg">
          <img alt="Houghton's_Fire_Escape_1877" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3470" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/Houghtons_Fire_Escape_1877.jpg?resize=640%2C880"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Courtesy of
          <a href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Houghton%27s_Fire_Escape_1877.jpg" target="_blank">
           Scientific American
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         There were ropes and ropes with baskets, with which people were supposed tolower themselves to the ground. There wereeven advertisements for fake cabinets, hollow refrigerators, and empty washing machines in which to stow awaya rope baskets.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/rope-escape.jpg">
          <img alt="rope escape" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3366" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/rope-escape.jpg?resize=640%2C658"/>
         </a>
         [
         <em>
          Image courtesy of Sara Wermiel]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         One engineer actually thought that, instead of dispatching the ropes from indoors, archers could shoot the ropes up to the higher floors.
        </p>
        <p>
         Another patent proposed individual parachute hats, with accompanying rubber shoes to break the fall.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/parachute-hat.jpg">
          <img alt="parachute hat" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3362" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/parachute-hat.jpg?resize=453%2C629"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          [Imagecourtesy of Sara Wermiel]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         There were also fire escape slides, which were marketed to schools as both emergency devices
         <i>
          and
         </i>
         playground equipment.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/fireescapeslide.jpg">
          <img alt="fireescapeslide" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3488" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/fireescapeslide.jpg?resize=600%2C427"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Credit:
          <a href="http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~stanfam/nostalgia/fireescapeslide.html">
           Rootsweb
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         By the 1870s, fire escapes had become permanentiron structures. Some were just straight ladders clamped to walls, others werethe angled ladders more resembling stairs. Butin true disasters, fire escapes didnt suffice.
        </p>
        <p>
         New Yorks Asch Building was required to have three means of egress. The developer insisted the property would be just be used as warehousing, so rather than installing three stairs, he was allowed to put in two stairs and a thin fire escape.
        </p>
        <p>
         The owner rented the top three floors of the Asch Building to the Triangle Shirtwaist Company.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/9thfloor.gif">
          <img alt="9thfloor" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3370" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/9thfloor.gif?resize=453%2C480"/>
         </a>
         <a href="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-05.52.16-pm.png">
          <img alt="2014-07-07 05.52.16 pm" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-3512" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-05.52.16-pm.png?resize=640%2C487"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Floorplan of the Asch Building. Credit:
          <a href="http://www.ilr.cornell.edu/trianglefire/supplemental/3Dmodel.html" target="_blank">
           Cornell University
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         OnMarch25,1911, a fire broke out in the Asch Building and spread quickly.
        </p>
        <p>
         Workers on the tenth floor wereable to survive by taking the stairs up through a fire exit to the roof. Workers on the eighth floor wereby and large able to get out by takingthe stairs down.
        </p>
        <p>
         But workers on the ninth floor were trapped. Only a few peopleon the ninthfloor knew about the tenthfloor exit, and mostdidnt know to go upstairs. Allegedly, one of the doors out of the building was lockedthough even if it wasnt, the staircases that would have provided egress were toonarrow and winding to hold the number of people who needed to escape.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          <a href="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-06.03.04-pm.png">
           <img alt="2014-07-07 06.03.04 pm" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3515" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-06.03.04-pm.png?resize=547%2C507"/>
          </a>
          [The Asch Building fire escape. Courtesy ofthe
          <a href="http://www.csun.edu/~ghy7463/mw2.html" target="_blank">
           The California State University of Northridge
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         A number of workers tried to use the outside fire escape, but itcollapsed under their weight. It was from the windows of the ninth floor that many workers, desperate to escape the flames and smoke, fell or jumped to their deaths.
        </p>
        <p>
         146 people died, most of them women, right in the middle of Greenwich Village.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/asch-building2.jpg">
          <img alt="asch building2" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3483" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/asch-building2.jpg?resize=640%2C506"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Courtesy of New York Public Library]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-06.41.28-pm.png">
          <img alt="2014-07-07 06.41.28 pm" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3519" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07-06.41.28-pm.png?resize=640%2C212"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Aftermath of the Triangle fire. Courtesy of
          <a href="http://www.ilr.cornell.edu/trianglefire/supplemental/3Dmodel.html" target="_blank">
           Cornell University
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         The building, however, was fine. It was a fireproof structure, which is why, at the time, no one really thought it needed egress.The Asch building, now called the Brown Building, is part of New York University.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/371358527_7b1e1c1f2d_z.jpg">
          <img alt="371358527_7b1e1c1f2d_z" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3486" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/371358527_7b1e1c1f2d_z.jpg?resize=426%2C640"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          [The Brown Building of Science. Credit:
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/wallyg/371358527/in/photolist-yPiXe-bZMj73-gRQKC7-gRQEe9-gRQJEJ-gRRzLt-biWchR-9fgJfp-7yBq-djm3R4-djkYgS-yPiyr-93vYD4-9ZtwvC-93vWaF-93y7cf-93xKQy-7NkjpC-73vdgA-9Mh7Xa-93uZur-93xKT3-93uZCH-93uZR2-93v18R-93y7qb-93uZKD-93xKvd-kJMdYK-ajkqGZ" target="_blank">
           Wally Gobetz
          </a>
          .]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Exits and egress were a problem, people thought, for the tenements and poor quality buildings. Popularlogic was that if a building was first class and non-combustable, theoccupants could be safely locked inside.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Triangle fire proved that architecture couldnt protect people. People had to protect themselves from architecture. After the Triangle Fire, the National Fire Protection Association started collecting data and studyingeffective egress.
        </p>
        <p>
         Fire escapes, it turns out, just didnt work.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/ornamental-fire-escape.jpg">
          <img alt="ornamental fire escape" class="alignnone wp-image-3372 size-full" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/ornamental-fire-escape-e1404784153556.jpg?resize=640%2C642"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          [Courtesy of Sarah Wermiel]
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         Because theywerent commonly used, fire escapes were often in states of disrepair or eroded by the elements. Even if they were maintained, fire escapes were not accessible to people with disabilities, the young, the elderly, and women, who were hamstrung by their long skirts.
        </p>
        <p>
         Most importantly, since people were not accustomed to using fire escapes, they often didnt know where they were located.
        </p>
        <p>
         Generally speaking, people try to leave a building the same way they entered it, and themodern fire escape is designed for this logic. They are the firstplace you would think to go in an emergency. They
         <i>
          are
         </i>
         the stairs. Or rather, they look like normal staircases, but they are truly pieces ofemergency equipment: enclosed in fire proof walls, sealed with a self-closing door, and covered in sprinklers and alarms.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/SSPX0047.jpg">
          <img alt="SSPX0047" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-3480" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/SSPX0047.jpg?resize=640%2C853"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          [The fire stairs in Oaklands Tribune Tower. Credit:Alex Kelley.]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07.jpg">
          <img alt="2014-07-07" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3503" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i2.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/2014-07-07.jpg?resize=612%2C816"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Fire Stairs in San Francisco. Credit:Malik Adan]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Because the fire stairswork perfectly well as stairways, theyre often
         <em>
          the
         </em>
         stairway in a building. Rather than spending the money and space onanopulent lobby with agrand, sweeping stairway, new construction tends to just haveelevators and fire stairs.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/11161611185_234703dece_z.jpg">
          <img alt="11161611185_234703dece_z" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3481" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/11161611185_234703dece_z.jpg?resize=640%2C425"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          [These stairs in Rome are a rarity. Credit:
          <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/duluoz_cats/11161611185/in/photolist-i1jb9a-yufE5-yuee7-fL9gEN-7EyNBU-7EftU7-4ZsjHa-4JzWBR-vm69B-dLDUC7-4eTwqh-6UJL7U-gtbts-5CfWFt-6vPb2c-nfAV-8xH2eX-71MhF2-jFY2HH-hxmVZ-Hd4s4-hSNQMo-d9Wo3n-2s2yqA-6BMLcS-amG2kd-4bnTL-fLVSMM-c7oAxW-815ZcU-ALALn-8PXaqk-aW7P2n-dor1hA-2DEDy-9FtU1P-5PRCMt-9iUzaT-dYfZ9N-3dQaP7-6DZoU8-7KvYhU-5NasrS-bjJeWz-4cAaA4-4QHscJ-9vK7ct-6qHntd-eiWFBD-6vPb2D" target="_blank">
           Duluoz Cats
          </a>
          ]
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Today, firestairways need to be rated, meaning they need to be enclosed in a construction that wont melt or allow the fire to penetrate as quickly as a non-rated wall.This is why the stairsare always shoved off into a cold, industrial-looking tower, no matter what the building looks like from the outside.
        </p>
        <p>
         The rated towers and other emergency structures are now modeled with egress software such asExodus, which allows architectsandconsultants toplug in the measurements of the building, its emergency equipment, the maximum number of occupants, click play, and watch digital people escape the pixel flames.
        </p>
        <p>
         <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/6E0VO6dTUBA" width="420">
         </iframe>
        </p>
        <p>
         This software works because humans generally behave predictably in emergencies. Ina state of panic, people dont want to go places they havent gone before, or use devices theyve never seen, or suddenly see if they can catch a rope shot with a bow and arrow. The way egress works now is in keeping with the way we use buildings normally.
        </p>
        <p>
         Rated towers might be ugly, expensive, and space-consuming, but they help save lives. In 2012, there were
         <a href="http://www.nfpa.org/research/reports-and-statistics" target="_blank">
          65 deaths in non-residential structures
         </a>
         , which arethe buildings with the heavy regulations and rated stairways. This number is already down from 2003, where there were 220 deaths in non-residential structures.
        </p>
        <p>
         Advances in egress make external fire escapes look primordial, but theres still something beautiful about them,even the ones no longer in use. Fire escapes are a physical reminder of how we evolved past being a culture that says, Heres a rope. Good luck, buddy!
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Tall-escape.jpg">
          <img alt="Tall escape" class="alignnone wp-image-3371 size-full" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/99percentinvisible.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/Tall-escape-e1404784342154.jpg?resize=468%2C629"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          [Fire escape on a fourteen-story, 1907 San Francisco office building. Courtesy of Sara Wermiel]
         </em>
        </p>

          <div class="comment-content">
           <p>
            The way egress works now is in keeping with the way we use buildings normally. Um except that the onerous regulations that remove staircases from general use also mean no one knows where they are. In many cases, they are prohibited except in emergencies.
           </p>
           <p>
            I worked in one of the tallest buildings in San Francisco, and the FD came by to make sure we all knew how to evacuate. They did a quiz, and I was one of maybe 10% of the people on the floor who knew where the fire stairs were. And they werent even hidden! Right there, with a lit sign, next to the bathroom and supply closet where everyone stares at it all day.
           </p>
           <p>
            I know the WTC was built long ago, this disaster was a lifetime ago to many, and it was not thoroughly updated, but it is a useful case:
           </p>
           <p>
            Forced out through the 78th floors sky lobby, where express elevators go to the main lobby, a building employee didnt know any other way down, said Heineman. Frantic, he discovered a second stairwell on the buildings east side, and continued downward
           </p>
           <p>
            Almost all the escapes from above the impact zone were people who explored a bit (or followed others doing the same) and found another staircase they had never been aware of. Similar events are from other serious disasters, like cruise ship fires or capsizing, where emergency exits are not familiar so cannot be easily recalled or used during the emergency.
           </p>
           <p>
            Lest you think I disagree with fireproof stair towers, I just want there to also be regulations requiring they be open (unlocked) for general building traffic, and something to encourage/require they be readily discoverable, and easy to access.
           </p>
          </div>
