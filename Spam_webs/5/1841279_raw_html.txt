          <h1 class="pos-title">
           Creating Budget-Friendly Photo Sets with Seamless Paper
          </h1>
          <p class="pos-meta">
           <span class="element element-relateditems first">
            Written by:
            <a href="/blog/featured-photographers/alana-tyler-slutsky" title="Alana Tyler Slutsky">

              <p>
               <em>
                <strong>
                 Note from Savage:
                </strong>
                Alana reached out to us earlier this month happy to share her unique and eye-catching conceptual work she's been creating using Savage seamless paper. Needless to say, we were thrilled to see our backdrops being used in a creative and interesting way in her photo sets, and thought it would be an awesome topic to share with our followers to help inspire them in their own work. This blog has been republished here with Alana's permission,
                <a href="http://alanatylerslutsky.blogspot.com/2014/03/building-sets-on-budget.html" target="_blank">
                 see her original post here
                </a>
                . If you want to check out more of Alana's work, follow her on
                <a href="https://twitter.com/alanaslutsky" target="_blank">
                 Twitter
                </a>
                and
                <a href="https://www.facebook.com/alanatylerphotography" target="_blank">
                 Facebook
                </a>
                !
               </em>
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               ---
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                So, you're relatively new to photography, you want to build a killer portfolio and above all, you want to stand out from everyone else. When you're just graduating from school with $50k in loans and paying New York City rent prices, how in the world does one do this? Simple. Get crafty.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               What sets all of the big guys apart from emerging or amateur photographers?
               <strong>
                Concept
               </strong>
               . And with concept often comes set design. Sure, it would be nice to have unlimited funds and access to the best set designers money can buy. But let's be real, that's not the reality of things. I've found that the best way to create amazing sets on a shoe string budget is to think outside of the box. Home Depot is an amazing source of raw materials and inspiration for sets. You never know what you'll run into. Just keep an open mind and try to look at any and every material from all angles. My favorite material to work with when building a set is paper.
               <a href="/products/seamless-paper">
                Savage Seamless Paper
               </a>
               has become my go-to set building material. They have a ton of different colors to choose from. I recently got to play around with a bunch of their pastels and they look absolutely amazing!
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
              <p>
               Many people have asked how some of my sets have been built so, let's break down the details on two set's I've created for under $250.
              </p>
              <h2>
               White Mountain -
               <a href="http://kentonmagazine.com/" target="_blank">
                Kenton Magazine
               </a>
              </h2>
              <p>
               <img alt="White Mountain by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/White%20Mountain%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutsky.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="White Mountain by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                For "White Mountain" my set designer,
                <a href="http://amelialorensmith.com/" target="_blank">
                 Amelia Loren Smith
                </a>
                , and I sat down and discussed what we wanted to achieve. Essentially we wanted to create some sort of frozen tundra inspired by the craftsmanship and abstract set design that you often see in ballets or operas. With this we came across the idea of building a set entirely out of paper products.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 Materials Used:
                </span>
               </span>
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 Roll of Savage 107"
                </span>
                <a href="/products/seamless-paper/super-white-seamless-paper" style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 Super White Seamless Paper
                </a>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 - $45
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 3 Pieces of 4' x 8' White Foamcore - $24 each
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 Bottle of India Ink - $6
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 Roll of clear packing tape - $7
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 Fog Machine - $30
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1,000,000 bags of fake snow (or 3)
                </span>
               </li>
              </ul>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Total: $160
               </strong>
               (minus snow)
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                First things first, we wanted to create some sort of concrete looking material to be used as the walls of this cold environment we were building. To achieve this, we used foam core and India ink. By distressing the foam core, using none other than my kitchen utensils, we broke through the smooth, treated surface of the foam core to the rough textured foam inside. After creating a large grid (aka concrete blocks) and poking close to three million holes in the surface (be warned, this will result in tired arms and bent forks... But is a great source for relieving aggression!) you'll have a very sad looking piece of foam core. Coat this in a thin, uneven wash of India ink - it may take a couple applications- but as the India ink builds up, so will the illusion of concrete.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                <img alt="White Mountain by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/White%20Mountain%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutsky%202.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="White Mountain by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               Next we had to create glaciers. By taking large pieces of paper from a roll of Savage seamless paper and crushing it just right, we created a hollow glacier. It took quite a bit of finessing and a lot of tape to hold it to the armoire that was inside to give it its massive size, but after enough playing around a glacier appeared! For variety in size, and for the model's safety, every large paper glacier had a piece of furniture positioned inside of it so it could be leaned on and used as a prop. We repeat this process with a smaller sheets of paper for baby glaciers that were able to be moved around the set.
              </p>
              <p>
               Lay down some sort of plexi or reflective surface on the ground for the effect of ice then coat everything with a million bags of fake snow. (And hope you have a very effective vacuum.) Voila! A set on a budget.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="White Mountain by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/White%20Mountain%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutsky%203.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="White Mountain by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               By the way - did I mention that this was shot in my bedroom? Don't let space, or lack thereof, be an excuse. If I did it, so can you!
              </p>
              <h2>
               UltraViolet -
               <a href="http://facticemagazine.com/" target="_blank">
                Factice Magazine
               </a>
              </h2>
              <p>
               <img alt="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutksy" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/Ultraviolet%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutksy.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutksy" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                When shooting "UltraViolet" for Factice Magazine, Amelia and I teamed up again. This shoot grew out of the desire to shoot with backlights. When you think UV, you think futurism. So we decided to run with it!
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                We didn't want to be clich and bring in chromes and other metallics to a futurism shoot. Instead we decided to go a minimalism route and build larger than life shapes. Now that Amelia and I had a grip on how to manipulate paper, we wanted to do something more complex. After figuring out that Savage Super White paper glows in the dark, we knew that it was game on!
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 Material Used:
                </span>
               </span>
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 3 Rolls of Savage 53" Super White Seamless Paper - $25 each
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 3 Sonotubes - $8 each
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 4 'x 8' piece of plywood - $26
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 6 2" x 4" x 8' planks of wood - $6 each
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 36 Square wooden dowels - $1 each
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 Hot Glue Gun + Glue Sticks - $22
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 1 Roll of Fishing Wire - $5
                </span>
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                 3 36" Pure White Beach Balls - $7 each
                </span>
               </li>
              </ul>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Total: $245
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                On a mission to hunt down sonotubes (in order to build cylinders), Amelia and I found ourselves at a Home Depot in Queens. It's here where we ran into a contractor that took pity on our attempts to figure out how to construct a big triangle that can hold the weight of a person. He agreed to come over the next day and build one for us (for free!) so we bought all of the materials needed right then and there. Not the brightest of ideas, as we had to carry our haul back to Manhattan. But hey, you gotta do what you gotta do!
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                <img alt="Building a Photo Set - Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/Building%20a%20Photo%20Set%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutsky.jpg" style="display: block; border: 0px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Building a Photo Set - Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="400"/>
               </span>
              </p>
              <h3>
               Triangle
              </h3>
              <p>
               First we had the contractor construct a triangle/ramp type shape which was sturdy enough to support a person. First we tried to paint the ramp but found that the white paint didn't glow. Fail. So instead we covered it with seamless paper on all sides.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/Ultraviolet%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutksy%205.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <h3>
               Cubes
              </h3>
              <p>
               To create cubes, we glued together wooden dowels to create the skeleton of a cube. We then cut the paper to fit each side and fixed the paper to the skeleton. Easy-peasy. But VERY fragile.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/Ultraviolet%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutksy%202.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <h3>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Spheres
               </span>
              </h3>
              <p>
               Hunt down some gigantic white beach balls, rush order, almost die trying to inflate by mouth, then convince the local bike shop to inflate them. If only all the shapes were this easy.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/Ultraviolet%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutksy%203.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <h3>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Cylinders
               </span>
              </h3>
              <p>
               Using the sonotubes as a base, Amelia and I would wrap paper around the sonotube, tape the ends then slide it off. Repeat - 16 times using varying paper lengths. Then we traced the top of the tube and created 16 lids to glue on to the top of the cylinders.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" border="0" src="/images/blogs/alana-tyler-slutsky/building-set-on-budget/Ultraviolet%20-%20Alana%20Tyler%20Slutksy%204.jpg" style="border: 0px; display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Ultraviolet by Alana Tyler Slutsky" width="650"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                (All of our props were built in my apartment then carried to set.)
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                Using fishing wire, strategically hidden photo assistants and light stands, we were able to get everything to stand upright or appear as if it were floating.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.3em;">
                The most difficult part of this editorial was nailing the lighting! If anyone has tried to balance black light, strobe (white light) and blue gels before, you would know this is no easy feat! The black lights were VERY easily overpowered or the effect was significantly dulled down depending on how it was balanced with the strobe. The tiniest of adjustments and the shot would be ruined. It took three lighting tests for my assistants and I to totally nail what we were doing and feel confident bringing it on set. If you're trying something new, ALWAYS TEST YOUR LIGHT. No one wants to show up on set and look like a fool.
               </span>
              </p>
              <p>
               Don't be afraid of building a set. It's not nearly as difficult or expensive as you may think. Just get creative and think outside of the box; source unusual materials and you'll pull together something amazing. Make your work stand out. As best put by Jeff Bark, "
               <em>
                If I create an environment that's only mine, then it's my picture.
               </em>
               "
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
