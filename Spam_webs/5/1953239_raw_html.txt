      <div class="article-content clearfix" itemprop="articleBody">
       <figure class="intro-image image full" style="width:980px">
        <img height="551" src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/2014-11-26_00017-980x551.jpg" width="980"/>
        <figcaption class="caption">
         <div class="caption-text">

       <!-- cache miss 4121:single/related:e02bf8ed74432290eae694f5b1c7ede5 -->
       <!-- empty -->
       <p>
        You'd be forgiven for forgetting about
        <em>
         Goat Simulator
        </em>
        afterit launched this April. The tiny broken-sandbox game was
        <a href="http://arstechnica.com/gaming/2014/04/double-your-goat-ars-reviews-the-latest-in-goat-video-games/">
         a funny lark that earned its $10 price
        </a>
        whether you viewed it as a high-minded, satirical examination of over-serious video games... or as a good excuse to see what happens when a goat rams its headinto anexploding sedan. Either way, you played, you goated, you moved on. Right?
       </p>
       <p>
        Butwith little fanfare, and no advance notice, the developers at Coffee Stain Studios snuck an update into the game last Thursday, complete with a brand new splash screen at launch: "Goat MMO Simulator"! We at Ars Technica were quite excited at the prospect of a giant, out-of-nowhere
        <em>
         Goat Simulator
        </em>
        transformationa free one for current and future owners, at thatand we rallied to schedule a session in which we might all join forces and get our collective goat on, but when we booted the game as a group, we looked all over the place for a "server" or "IP address" or "find your friends" option, only to find... there wasn't one. As it turns out, the word order is important: It's not
        <em>
         Goat Simulator MMO
        </em>
        , but rather a simulatorof a Goat MMO!
       </p>
       <p>
        "My dreams of becoming a goat with you guys have been shattered," one staffer wrote via e-mail, and indeed, we want to cut readersoff at the pass who might have expected Coffee Stain Studios to have assembled a server farm (you know, farm, goats...) and created a giant chain of quests to be shared among friends. No matter.
        <em>
         Goat MMO Simulator
        </em>
        , in spite of neither being massive, multiplayer, nor online, might still be the MMO experience of the year.
       </p>

       <p>
        Your quest begins with a character-selection screen that rolls out the usual, boring MMO classes: hunter, magician, tank, rouge (intentionally misspelled),goat, or microwave. Most of those slap silly costumes on your goatwe're partial to "hunter" dressing as a Cabela's-loving fishermanwhile "microwave" is literally a microwave with giant human legs.
       </p>
       <p>
        Each class also receives a few cosmetic bonuses, including invisibilityand the ability to launch "attacks" like playing cards and microwaved pizza, but these don't really matter, since the game doesn't have any health meters or recharge times. Pick your favorite silly look, then jump into the game, where you're dropped into a knights-and-wizards realm full of quest-givers with exclamation points above their heads.
       </p>
       <p>
        The first thing you may notice is an endlessly scrolling chat box, full of ridiculous notices aboutitem trades, complaints about patches ("why did they nerf berserkers they are totally useless now") and even realistic-looking requests for people to find each other in the game world, but don't be fooled. The game runs entirely offline, and its creators went to great lengths to fill its chat portion with so many usernames and MMO-sounding phrases that you might find yourself wondering mid-game if it somehow logged online after all. (Go on, perusethe chat boxes in our giant
        <em>
         Goat MMO Simulator
        </em>
        gallery.)
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Goat MMO Simulator
        </em>
        doesn't go so far as to code fake users within the game itself, at least. Every goat, sheep, ram, "bear" (which look more like monkeys with bear masks), knight, witch, oversized ant, and more runs on the game's rudimentary AI system, and they're all there for you to slam into or "lick" to carry around as you please. Which is good, because the game still revolves around barely organized chaos. Quest givers dole out tasks that range from utterly simplehand me that apple two feet awayto intentionally ridiclousgo on, lick 10,000 trees, you know you want to.
       </p>
       <p>
        Should you complete these quests, which open with delightfully moronic text promptsthat seemto have been written by a college freshman, you'll cash in experience points that, from what we can gather, unlock new goat outfits for use in the game's original mode. You also get XP for doing pretty much anything in the game, from jumping and spinning in mid-air to hitting a table with your head to standing entirely still. XP for contemplation? We can dig it. (The game's
        <a href="http://store.steampowered.com/news/15024/">
         patch notes
        </a>
        indicate that its characters can reach a level max of 101 as a way to openly mock the designersat Blizzard.)
       </p>
       <p>
        We're not sure exactly how big the game world is, considering it hides a few hard-to-find passages, but the "main" world is maybe four times the original game's sandbox, and secret paths will unlock places like a castle andholy goata server room that players can destroy to actually
        <a href="https://imgur.com/a/xdIv4">
         break portions of the running game's code
        </a>
        . Yet for a series that revels in mocking its technological weaknesses and glitches, this MMO update goes to pretty great lengths in terms of polish and level density;we particularly enjoyed the soundtrack changes when entering new zones, especially the area of the main map that openly mocks the
        <em>
         Diablo
        </em>
        series.
       </p>
       <h2>
        G.O.A.T. = Greatest of all time
       </h2>
       <p>
        Because much of the game's joy comes from its out-of-nowhere quests, we're not interested in spoiling a lot of its comedic gold; rest assured that it's all painfully stupid stuff, written with MMO addicts in mind. Just so we're clear, other than the aesthetics and the silly quests, there's nothing particularly MMO-ey about how the game plays, meaning no crafting, no guilds, and no raids.
       </p>
       <p>
        Fine by us; we still had a blast. Alot of care went into tucking secrets and discoveries around the game's new MMO-branded world, and while we can't guarantee more than a couple of hours of fresh content, we do think this is an absolutely worthy reason to load the game once more if you've left it dormant for a whileand certainly the over-the-top bonus that should convince fence-sitters to take the silly
        <em>
         Goat Simulator
        </em>
        plunge for as little as $10 (currently less thanks to the usual holiday-themed Steam sale as of press time).
       </p>
      </div>
