   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Golden age hip hop
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      Hip hop's
      <b>
       golden age
      </b>
      is a name given to a period in mainstream
      <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">
       hip hop
      </a>
      , usually cited as the mid-1980s to the mid-1990s. It is said to be characterized by its diversity, quality, innovation and influence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone1995_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rollingstone1995-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated132_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated132-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There were various types of
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Subject_matter" title="Subject matter">
       subject matter
      </a>
      , while the music was experimental and the
      <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">
       sampling
      </a>
      eclectic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      The artists most often associated with the phrase are
      <a href="/wiki/Run%E2%80%93D.M.C." title="RunD.M.C.">
       RunD.M.C.
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">
       Public Enemy
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">
       Beastie Boys
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Boogie_Down_Productions" title="Boogie Down Productions">
       Boogie Down Productions
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_B._%26_Rakim" title="Eric B. &amp; Rakim">
       Eric B. &amp; Rakim
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">
       Big Daddy Kane
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">
       De La Soul
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gang_Starr" title="Gang Starr">
       Gang Starr
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pete_Rock_%26_C.L._Smooth" title="Pete Rock &amp; C.L. Smooth">
       Pete Rock &amp; C.L. Smooth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/EPMD" title="EPMD">
       EPMD
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">
       A Tribe Called Quest
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Slick_Rick" title="Slick Rick">
       Slick Rick
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramagnetic_MC%27s" title="Ultramagnetic MC's">
       Ultramagnetic MC's
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linhardt_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Linhardt-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/MC_Lyte" title="MC Lyte">
       MC Lyte
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Brothers" title="Jungle Brothers">
       Jungle Brothers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Releases by these acts co-existed in this period with, and were as commercially viable as, those of early
      <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">
       gangsta rap
      </a>
      artists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ice-T" title="Ice-T">
       Ice-T
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Geto_Boys" title="Geto Boys">
       Geto Boys
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">
       N.W.A
      </a>
      , the sex raps of
      <a href="/wiki/2_Live_Crew" title="2 Live Crew">
       2 Live Crew
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Too_Short" title="Too Short">
       Too Short
      </a>
      , and party-oriented music by acts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Kid_%27n_Play" title="Kid 'n Play">
       Kid 'n Play
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Fat_Boys" title="The Fat Boys">
       The Fat Boys
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/DJ_Jazzy_Jeff_%26_The_Fresh_Prince" title="DJ Jazzy Jeff &amp; The Fresh Prince">
       DJ Jazzy Jeff &amp; The Fresh Prince
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/MC_Hammer" title="MC Hammer">
       MC Hammer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Cotton_Club_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Cotton_Club-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Style">
       Style
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_age_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Style">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The golden age is noted for its innovation  a time "when it seemed that every new single reinvented the genre,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rollingstone1995_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rollingstone1995-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      according to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      . Referring to "hip-hop in its golden age",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday2005_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatoday2005-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">
        Spin
       </a>
      </i>
      's editor-in-chief Sia Michel said, "there were so many important, groundbreaking albums coming out right about that time",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday2005_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatoday2005-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/MTV" title="MTV">
       MTV
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Sway_Calloway" title="Sway Calloway">
       Sway Calloway
      </a>
      added: "The thing that made that era so great is that nothing was contrived. Everything was still being discovered and everything was still innovative and new".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post-gazette2004_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-post-gazette2004-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Writer William Jelani Cobb said, "what made the era they inaugurated worthy of the term golden was the sheer number of stylistic innovations that came into existence... in these golden years, a critical mass of mic prodigies were literally creating themselves and their art form at the same time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated47_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated47-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the definitive characteristics of the golden age of hip-hop is the proliferation of sample-heavy music. The ability to sample different beats from a wide variety of sources gave birth to a new breed of producers, DJs who did not necessarily need formal musical training or instruments, just an ear for sound collages.These samples were derived from a number of genres, ranging from jazz, funk and soul to rock &amp; roll. For example,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Paul%27s_Boutique" title="Paul's Boutique">
        Paul's Boutique
       </a>
      </i>
      , the Beastie Boy's second studio album, drew from over 105 individual samples, 24 of which were featured on the last track of the album.
     </p>
     <p>
      During this time, sound bites were not limited to just music. The
      <a href="/wiki/RZA" title="RZA">
       RZA
      </a>
      , the de facto leader of the
      <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">
       Wu-Tang Clan
      </a>
      , a hip hop collective from the 90s, sampled sound clips from his own collection of 70s kung-fu flicks to bolster and frame the group's gritty lyrical content. Many of the sample-laden albums released during this time would not be able to receive legal clearance today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The era also provided some of the greatest advances in
      <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">
       rapping
      </a>
      technique.
      <a href="/wiki/Kool_G_Rap" title="Kool G Rap">
       Kool G Rap
      </a>
      , referring to the golden age in the book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/How_to_Rap" title="How to Rap">
        How to Rap
       </a>
      </i>
      said, "that era bred rappers like a
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">
       Big Daddy Kane
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">
       KRS-One
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Rakim" title="Rakim">
       Rakim
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Chuck_D" title="Chuck D">
       Chuck D
      </a>
      . . . their rapping capability and ability these dudes were phenomenal".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many of hip hop's biggest artists were also at their creative peak.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">
       Allmusic
      </a>
      said the golden age "witnessed the best recordings from some of the biggest rappers in the genre's history... overwhelmingly based in New York City, golden age rap is characterized by skeletal beats, samples cribbed from hard rock or soul tracks, and tough dis raps... rhymers like
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">
       PE
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Chuck_D" title="Chuck D">
       Chuck D
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Big_Daddy_Kane" title="Big Daddy Kane">
       Big Daddy Kane
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/KRS-One" title="KRS-One">
       KRS-One
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rakim" title="Rakim">
       Rakim
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">
       LL Cool J
      </a>
      basically invented the complex wordplay and lyrical kung-fu of later hip-hop".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllMusic_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AllMusic-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to lyrical self-glorification, hip hop was also used as a form of social protest. Lyrical content from the era often drew attention to a variety of social issues including afrocentric living, drug use, crime and violence, religion, culture, the state of the American economy, and the modern man's struggle. Conscious and political hip hop tracks of the time were a response to the effects of American capitalism and former President Reagan's conservative political economy. According to Rose Tricia, " In rap, relationships between black cultural practice, social and economic conditions, technology, sexual and racial politics, and the institution policing of the popular terrain are complex and in constant motion. Even though hip hop was used as a mechanism for different social issues it was still very complex with issues within the movement itself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There was also often an emphasis on black nationalism. Hip hop scholar
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Eric_Dyson" title="Michael Eric Dyson">
       Michael Eric Dyson
      </a>
      stated, "during the golden age of hip hop, from 1987 to 1993, Afrocentric and black nationalist rap were prominent",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and critic Scott Thill described the time as "the golden age of hip hop, the late '80s and early '90s when the form most capably fused the militancy of its Black Panther and Watts Prophets forebears with the wide-open cultural experimentalism of
      <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">
       De La Soul
      </a>
      and others".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Stylistic variety was also prominent.
      <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">
       MSNBC
      </a>
      said that in the golden age, "rappers had an individual sound that was dictated by their region and their communities, not by a marketing strategist"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Village_Voice" title="Village Voice">
        Village Voice
       </a>
      </i>
      referred to the golden age's "eclecticism".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-villagevoice2002_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-villagevoice2002-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Along with focusing on black nationalism, hip hop artists often talked about urban poverty. This brought many listeners to the genre who were struggling with poverty and were coping with the scourges of alcohol, drugs, and gangs in their communities.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">
       Public Enemy
      </a>
      's most influential song, "
      <a href="/wiki/Fight_the_Power" title="Fight the Power">
       Fight the Power
      </a>
      ," came out at the time of urban poverty. The song speaks up to the government, proclaiming that people in the ghetto have freedom of speech and rights like every other American. One line in the song, "We got to pump the stuff to make us tough from the heart," grabbed listeners' attention and gave them motivation to speak out for themselves.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Time_period">
       Time period
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_age_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Time period">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">
       Allmusic
      </a>
      writes, "Hip-hop's golden age is bookended by the commercial breakthrough of
      <a href="/wiki/Run%E2%80%93D.M.C." title="RunD.M.C.">
       RunD.M.C.
      </a>
      in 1986 and the explosion of gangsta rap with NWA in the late 80s and Dr. Dre and Snoop Doggy Dogg in 1993."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllMusic_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AllMusic-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, the specific time period that the golden age covers varies among different sources. The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">
        New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      also defines hip-hop's golden age as the "late 1980's and early 90's".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ed Simmons of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Chemical_Brothers" title="The Chemical Brothers">
       The Chemical Brothers
      </a>
      said, "there was that golden age of hip-hop in the early 90s when the
      <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Brothers" title="Jungle Brothers">
       Jungle Brothers
      </a>
      made
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight_Out_the_Jungle" title="Straight Out the Jungle">
        Straight Out the Jungle
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">
       De La Soul
      </a>
      made
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Feet_High_and_Rising" title="Three Feet High and Rising">
        Three Feet High and Rising
       </a>
      </i>
      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (though these records were in fact made in 1988 and 1989 respectively).
      <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">
       MSNBC
      </a>
      called the 80s the "Golden Age" of hip-hop music.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the book
      <i>
       Contemporary Youth Culture
      </i>
      , the "golden age era" is described as being "from 19871999", coming after "the old school era: from 1979 to 1985".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated361_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated361-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <i>
       Icons of Hip Hop: An Encyclopedia of the Movement, Music, and Culture
      </i>
      , the golden age is described by scholar Mickey Hess as "circa 1986-1994."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated361_24-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated361-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Music critic Tony Green, in the book
      <i>
       Classic Material
      </i>
      , refers to the two-year period 19931994 as "a second Golden Age" that saw influential, high-quality albums using elements of past classicism
      <a href="/wiki/E-mu_SP-1200" title="E-mu SP-1200">
       E-mu SP-1200
      </a>
      drum sounds, turntable scratches, references to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_school_hip_hop" title="Old school hip hop">
       old school hip hop
      </a>
      hits, and "tongue-twisting triplet verbalisms" while making clear that new directions were being taken. Green lists as examples the Wu-Tang Clan's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">
        Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)
       </a>
      </i>
      , Nas'
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Illmatic" title="Illmatic">
        Illmatic
       </a>
      </i>
      , De La Soul's 1993 release
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Buhloone_Mindstate" title="Buhloone Mindstate">
        Buhloone Mindstate
       </a>
      </i>
      , Snoop Doggy Dogg's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">
        Doggystyle
       </a>
      </i>
      , A Tribe Called Quest's third album
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_Marauders" title="Midnight Marauders">
        Midnight Marauders
       </a>
      </i>
      and the Outkast debut
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik" title="Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik">
        Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated132_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated132-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to copyright, music, and pop culture scholars Kembrew Mcleod and Peter DiCola, the golden age of hip-hop sampling spans from 1987-1992. Artists and record labels were not yet aware of the permanence of hip-hop culture in mainstream media, and did not yet accept it as a legitimate institution. They believe the ruling made in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Upright_Music,_Ltd._v._Warner_Bros._Records_Inc." title="Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc.">
        Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc.
       </a>
      </i>
      marked the end of the golden age of hip hop and its sampling practices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Grand_Upright_Music_v._Warner_Brothers_Records">
       <i>
        Grand Upright Music v. Warner Brothers Records
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_age_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grand Upright Music v. Warner Brothers Records">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This lawsuit was known for effectively ending the "Wild West" period for sampling during the golden age of hip hop.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1991,
      <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_O%27Sullivan" title="Gilbert O'Sullivan">
       Gilbert O'Sullivan
      </a>
      's song publisher sued Warner Brothers Records over the use of the original in
      <a href="/wiki/Biz_Markie" title="Biz Markie">
       Biz Markie
      </a>
      's song "Alone Again." No copyright case precedents were cited in the ruling of the final verdict, and the presiding judge's opinion was prefaced with the words "Thou Shalt not Steal."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiCola.2C_Peter_p._132_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiCola.2C_Peter_p._132-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Turtles_v._De_La_Soul">
       <i>
        The Turtles v. De La Soul
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_age_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Turtles v. De La Soul">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The sixties pop band
      <a href="/wiki/The_Turtles" title="The Turtles">
       The Turtles
      </a>
      filed a lawsuit in 1989 against hip hop group
      <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">
       De La Soul
      </a>
      for the uncleared use of a sampled element derived from their original 1968 track "You Showed Me." The lawsuit was settled out of court for a reported $1.7 million, though group members later claimed that the actual payout was significantly less.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiCola.2C_Peter_p._132_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiCola.2C_Peter_p._132-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
