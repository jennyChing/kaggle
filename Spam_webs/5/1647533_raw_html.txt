     <p>
      A
      <b>
       violin concerto
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">
       concerto
      </a>
      for solo
      <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">
       violin
      </a>
      (occasionally, two or more violins) and instrumental ensemble, customarily
      <a href="/wiki/Orchestra" title="Orchestra">
       orchestra
      </a>
      . Such works have been written since the
      <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">
       Baroque
      </a>
      period, when the solo concerto form was first developed, up through the present day. Many major composers have contributed to the violin concerto repertoire, with the best known works including those by Vivaldi,
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">
       Bach
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Bla Bartk">
       Bartk
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">
       Beethoven
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">
       Brahms
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Bruch" title="Max Bruch">
       Bruch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">
       Mendelssohn
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">
       Mozart
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Paganini" title="Niccol Paganini">
       Paganini
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokofiev" title="Prokofiev">
       Prokofiev
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shostakovich" title="Shostakovich">
       Shostakovich
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">
       Sibelius
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">
       Tchaikovsky
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaldi" title="Vivaldi">
       Vivaldi
      </a>
      . Traditionally a three-movement work, the violin concerto has been structured in four movements by a number of modern composers, including
      <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">
       Dmitri Shostakovich
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">
       Igor Stravinsky
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">
       Alban Berg
      </a>
      (in the latter, the first two and last two movements are connected, with the only break coming between the second and third). In some violin concertos, especially from the Baroque and modern eras, the violin (or group of violins) is accompanied by a
      <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">
       chamber ensemble
      </a>
      rather than an orchestrafor instance, Vivaldi's
      <i>
       L'estro armonico
      </i>
      , originally scored for four violins, two violas, cello, and
      <a href="/wiki/Figured_bass" title="Figured bass">
       continuo
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Allan_Pettersson" title="Allan Pettersson">
       Allan Pettersson
      </a>
      's first concerto, for violin and string quartet.
     </p>
