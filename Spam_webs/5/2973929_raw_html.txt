         <h1>
          <a href="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/2014/07/09/a-new-view-of-europa-2/">
           A New View of Europa
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="post-author">
          <span class="by">
           by
          </span>
          Nadia Drake
         </div>
        </header>
        <div class="article-text">
         <p>
          Europa is like a spherical ice cube that has partially melted. In fact, if you shook this small moon of Jupiter, you might hear a sloshing sound.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats because a deep, global ocean lies beneath the moons frozen, criss-crossed crust  an ocean that might be
          <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/topics/solarsystem/features/europa20130305.html" target="_blank">
           as much as 100 kilometers deep
          </a>
          . In fact, Europas ocean is so vast that it contains between two and three times
          <a href="http://apod.nasa.gov/apod/ap120524.html" target="_blank">
           as much water as all the oceans on Earth, combined
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          What swims in that alien sea? We dont yet know. Maybe nothing. But
          <a href="http://www.nationalgeographic.com/astrobiology/" target="_blank">
           astrobiologists have placed Europa
          </a>
          at  or near  the top of their visitation wish-list for decades. That massive ocean, which is likely filled with minerals from the alien ocean floor, plus the possibility of hydrothermal sea vents, makes this small moon one of the best places to look for life beyond Earth in the solar system.
         </p>
         <p>
          Piercing that icy crust and sinking a spacecraft into the ocean is no trivial matter, though. But not to worry:
          <a href="https://www.sciencenews.org/article/ice-distant-moon">
           Scientists are working on solving that problem
          </a>
          . And maybe in the next decade or so, Earth will send a spacecraft to this faraway moon, a
          <a href="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/missions/europa-clipper/">
           probe tasked with sniffing around
          </a>
          and possibly scouting out landing sites for future spacecraft.
         </p>
         <p>
          It wouldnt be the first time a robotic emissary had the moon in sight, though. In the 1990s,
          <a href="https://solarsystem.nasa.gov/galileo/">
           the Galileo spacecraft
          </a>
          zipped through the Jovian system and studied the solar systems most massive world. Part of that campaign included taking pictures of the many moons in Jupiters gravitational clutches.
         </p>
         <p>
          If you thought youd seen all the images Galileo had to offer, youd be wrong. NASA
          <a href="http://photojournal.jpl.nasa.gov/catalog/pia18413">
           released a new view of Europa today
          </a>
          . Its a reprocessed version of images taken on November 6, 1997, and spans an area thats roughly 160 square kilometers. Those red streaks that look like highways are formed by sulfur-containing compounds oozing through the ice. And if the streaks are anything like what we see on Earth 
          <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/vision/universe/solarsystem/europaf-20060908.html">
           up in the Canadian Arctic
          </a>
           then maybe theyre
          <a href="http://www.ifa.hawaii.edu/UHNAI/bioast07oral/Gleeson.pdf" target="_blank">
           the mark of life beneath the surface
          </a>
          .
         </p>
