             <header>
              <span>
              </span>
              <h1>
               Exploring HIV Treatment Options
              </h1>
             </header>
             <aside class="disclaimer">
             </aside>
             <h2>
              New HIV treatments are making it easier to live a fuller and longer life with HIV. Here's what you need to know about advances in antiretroviral medications for HIV.
             </h2>
             <div class="bottom">
              <div class="byline">
               By
               <a href="/contributing-writers-and-editors.aspx">
                Jennifer Warner
               </a>
              </div>
              <div class="reviewedby">
               Medically reviewed by
               <a href="/medical-reviewers.aspx">
                Lindsey Marcellin, MD, MPH
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="clear">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div id="video-player-top">
            </div>
            <aside class="aside">
             <img alt="HIV medications" class="left" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/cs-exploring-hiv-treatment-options-article.jpg"/>
            </aside>
            <p>
             Within a
generation, highly effective treatments have transformed the human
immunodeficiency virus (HIV) from a death sentence to a chronic disease that
can often be managed by taking a single pill a day.
            </p>
            <p>
             Although
theres no cure for
             <a href="/health-report/hiv-health.aspx">
              HIV
             </a>
             or AIDS (acquired immunodeficiency syndrome), todays
antiretroviral drugs can suppress the virus to undetectable levels in the body and
cause far fewer side effects than earlier treatments. Whats more, the right
HIV treatment can make living with HIV easier and decrease the risk of
transmission of the virus to others.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              How HIV Treatment Has Changed
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             When the
HIV/AIDS epidemic began in the 1980s, people with HIV were often given only a
few years to live. Today, there are 31 antiretroviral medications approved to
treat HIV, and with prompt
             <a href="/health-report/hiv-health/hiv-diagnosis-who-to-tell.aspx">
              diagnosis
             </a>
             and proper treatment, people with HIV can
expect to live nearly as long as people without the virus.
            </p>
            <p>
             Whats
changed in the last 5 to 10 years is the increased attention on developing
drugs that are more convenient with fewer side effects, says Paul Volberding,
MD, a professor in the department of medicine at the University of California
in San Francisco and the director of the universitys AIDS research institute.
The single-pill regimen has transformed HIV treatment.
            </p>
            <p>
             In the past,
people with HIV had to take a handful of pills every day to treat their
condition, plus
             <a href="/health-report/hiv-health/manage-hiv-treatment-side-effects.aspx">
              manage the side effects
             </a>
             caused by the potent drugs. Now, many
therapies are being combined into a single pill.
            </p>
            <p>
             The newer HIV
medication options have limited side effects, which allows people to take their
medications for a long time while really being able to get on with their
lives, says Dr. Volberding.
            </p>
            <p>
             The other
main development in HIV treatment, he says, is new research that suggests
current therapies not only suppress the virus but can also prevent its
transmission to others.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              HIV Treatment Options
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Current HIV
treatment guidelines from the National Institutes of Health recommend antiretroviral
drugs for everyone with the HIV infection, regardless of CD4 count, the
indicator of immune function in people with HIV.
            </p>
            <p>
             Until 2012,
treatment was considered optional for people with HIV with normal CD4 counts
who were at lower risk of disease progression. The new guideline is designed to
reduce the risk of progression to AIDS as well as prevent the spread of the
disease.
            </p>
            <p>
             We no
longer wait to start treatment, says Volberding. Theres benefit to the people
with HIV themselves as well as to the general public by way of stopping
transmission of the virus.
            </p>
            <p>
             Most HIV
treatment regimens consist of three different drugs, which often can be
combined into a single daily pill. Combination antiretroviral therapy attacks
the HIV virus at different points of its life cycle. These drugs create a
synergistic effect in suppressing the virus thats greater than the sum of its
parts, says Volberding.
            </p>
            <p>
             The main types
of antiretroviral medications used to treat HIV treatment include:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Nucleoside reverse transcriptase
    inhibitors (NRTIs)
              </strong>
              ,
    which block one of the enzymes that HIV needs to replicate itself in a cell.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Non-nucleoside reverse
    transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs)
              </strong>
              ,
    which target the same enzyme as NRTIs, but with a different chemical structure.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Protease inhibitors (PIs)
              </strong>
              , which stop the production of
    one component of HIV.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Entry inhibitors
              </strong>
              , which block the entry of HIV
    into CD4 cells.
             </li>
             <li>
              <strong>
               Integrase inhibitors
              </strong>
              , which block HIV from inserting
    its viral DNA into host cells.
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Choosing HIV Treatment Thats Right for You
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Discussing
the benefits and risks of different HIV treatment options with your doctor can
help you choose
             <a href="/health-report/hiv-health/how-to-know-if-hiv-treatment-isnt-working.aspx">
              treatment thats right for you
             </a>
             based on your individual health
status and lifestyle. The choice is based on whats effective and convenient,
says Volberding. Factors that can come into play include your stress level,
eating habits, and any other medications youre taking. Your doctor should also
check for HIV drug resistance with a blood test.
            </p>
            <p>
             Once youve been prescribed
HIV treatment, its vital to stick with your antiretroviral therapy to get the
best results. Whatever the prescription says, do it every single day, says Volberding.
The drugs work when taken. When people get in trouble, its usually because
theyre not taking their medication.
            </p>
