         <h2 class="posttitle">
          5 Digital Marketing Lessons You Can Learn From Politics
         </h2>
         <div class="entry">
          <img alt="Digital Marketing Lessons From Politics" class="display_none wp-post-image" height="256" src="http://www.mdgadvertising.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/1265-blog-social-media-politics.jpg" title="5 Digital Marketing Lessons You Can Learn From Politics" width="475"/>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.mdgadvertising.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/1265-blog-social-media-politics.jpg">
            <img alt="Digital Marketing Lessons From Politics" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-34743" height="256" src="http://www.mdgadvertising.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/1265-blog-social-media-politics.jpg" title="5 Digital Marketing Lessons You Can Learn From Politics" width="475"/>
           </a>
           A decade ago, U.S. politics was a technological hinterland, exemplified by Ted Stevens, the head of the Senate Committee on Commerce, referring to the Internet as series of tubes that could somehow get jammed up.
          </p>
          <p>
           Today that has clearly changed. Politicians and the groups affiliated with them use the full spectrum of digital tools availablefrom websites and social media accounts to sophisticated databases and analytics suites.
          </p>
          <p>
           <span id="more-34741">
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, not all of these forays have been successful (case in point: Anthony Weiner). Still, theres plenty digital marketers can learnboth good and badfrom politics. In particular, here are five recent lessons:
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            1. Respect Your Email List Subscribers
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           When the midterm election predictions started looking grim for Democrats earlier this year, someone in the party must have decided to go all-in with email messaging. The outreach started with a few extra emails to subscribers each week and grew into a fevered pitch of seemingly never-ending solicitations.
          </p>
          <p>
           The flood of desperate messaging prompted Jon Stewart to go on a lengthy rant against the tactic on
           <em>
            The Daily Show
           </em>
           . A choice line from the broadcast: Then there are the emails, oh God the emails. With a frequency matched only by a Nigerian prince selling Groupon coupons for boner pills, the emails from the Democrats will not stop.
          </p>
          <p>
           The emails were clearly part of a conscious strategy to fight tooth-and-nail to retain control of the Senate this year. The problem, of course, is that while it may lead to a short-term boost in donations, long-term it is a terrible strategy. This sort of extremely aggressive messagingsometimes two or three times a dayis certain to lead to significant unsubscribes, as well as general antipathy
           <strong>
            .
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            2. Your 404 Error Page is an Opportunity
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           After the last presidential election, the Republicans were seen as being the less tech-savvy party. In 2012, while the Obama camp was making the most of Big Data, Mitt Romneys get-out-the-vote system couldnt even function when he needed it most.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Grand Old Party has recovered well since then. It has closed the technology gap by focusing on both big projects and small tweaks. One of the latter is the 404 error page for GOP.com, which cleverly takes a swipe at Hillary Clintons dead broke comment earlier this year.
          </p>
          <p>
           The small move paid off well, garnering laughs from the faithful and plenty of good press.
          </p>
          <p>
           The lesson? Every page on your website matters. Also, a second lesson: Sometimes just have fun.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.mdgadvertising.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/1265-article-Hillary-404-error.jpg">
            <img alt="Digital Marketing Lessons to Learn From Politics" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-34756" height="284" src="http://www.mdgadvertising.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/1265-article-Hillary-404-error.jpg" title="5 Digital Marketing Lessons You Can Learn From Politics" width="475"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            3. Your Social Profile Elements are an Opportunity, Too
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The Republicans may have gotten the better of Hillary with their 404 page, but shes shown that shes plenty digital-savvy as well.
          </p>
          <p>
           Case in point: Hilarys social media pages, especially her Twitter profile. Rather than use a standard politician headshot (like Bill does), she cleverly references the Texts From Hillary meme with her profile photo and header.
          </p>
          <p>
           Moreover, she makes a winking reference to her future presidential plans/non-plans in the description (pantsuit aficionado, glass ceiling cracker, TBD.).
          </p>

           <div id="blog-sm-advertisement-top">

          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            4. Find Your Own Tone on Twitter
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Representative John Dingell was born in 1925, yet has somehow mastered social media better than many Millennials.
          </p>
          <p>
           How? He (or someone on his staff) found a wonderful middle ground between ignoring social networks and trying to look more hip than would be natural.
          </p>
          <p>
           Dingell tweets regularly about a mix of topics (politics, sports, technology, etc.), almost always with a dry, self-deprecating tone. His self-swipes about his age and looks are often brilliantly mixed with pop-culture referenceswhich is one of the reasons BuzzFeed labeled him as probably the best person on Twitter.
          </p>

           <strong>
            5. Consumers Media Consumption Habits Have Changed
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Finally, some stats and insights from a recent report about voters media consumption habits.
          </p>
          <p>
           The researchfrom Google, Targeted Digital (a Republican digital consulting firm), and Well &amp; Lighthouse (a Democrat digital consulting firm)found that less than half of likely voters say live television is their primary way of viewing video content, yet the vast majority of political video budgets go toward traditional television ads.
          </p>
          <p>
           Basically, theres a huge gap between where spends are going and where consumers are actually spending their time.
          </p>
          <p>
           The reports conclusion: Campaigns on both sides of the aisle must now adjust to the proliferation of methods to watch video content.
          </p>
          <p>
           Those, of course, are important words for not just campaigns, but for all of us.
