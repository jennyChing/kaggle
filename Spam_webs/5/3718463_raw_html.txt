      <h1 class="entry-title">
       Confederate Motorcycles
      </h1>
      <small>
       Updated November 7th, 2009
       <!-- by Luke Van Deman -->
      </small>
      <div class="entry-content entry">
       <p>
        <script type="text/javascript">
         &lt;!--
google_ad_client = "pub-1409797665217528";
/* 468x15, created 10/15/09 */
google_ad_slot = "9736129147";
google_ad_width = 468;
google_ad_height = 15;
//--&gt;
        </script>
        <script src="http://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/show_ads.js" type="text/javascript">
        </script>
       </p>
       <p>
        Motorcycles are machines. They offer transportation and supply functionality to their commander. But they are also independent creations. Marvels of their engineers and owners. They are the output of hours of consideration, crafting and creation, of welding, oiling and inking.
       </p>
       <p>
        So when I first saw a picture of a Confederate motorcycle, I was stuck by the railyard ruthlessness and industrial inspired construction of these machines. They appear conceived not from a directors chair in Hollywood, but from a revolutionary living in a left-for-dead town as he struggles to put pieces and polish on scrap metal and left to his warehouse and his genius creates an unapologetic beast that breathes fire and design and looks like no one else. And I want one.
       </p>
       <p>
        The current years production include the
        <a href="http://www.confederate.com/wraith/" target="_blank">
         Wraith
        </a>
        , the
        <a href="http://www.confederate.com/confederate3/fighter.php" target="_blank">
         Fighter
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="http://www.confederate.com/confederate3/hellcat.php" target="_blank">
         Hellcat
        </a>
        . My personal favorite is the Wraith, although Ill have to wait a few years before I can cash my entire 401k to cover the $92,000 list price. What strikes me about Confederate, besides their mission statement to deemphasize volume which feels sorta throwback American in its effort to sell fewer, higher priced items than the consumerism jungle created by Sam Walton is their ability to actually maintain a business.
       </p>
       <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_194" style="width: 640px">
        <a href="http://www.lukevandeman.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/11/confederate-wraith-2009.jpg">
         <img alt="Confedearte - Wraith" class="size-full wp-image-194" height="451" src="http://www.lukevandeman.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/11/confederate-wraith-2009.jpg" title="confederate-wraith-2009" width="630"/>
        </a>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Confederate - Wraith
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        Im all for expensive toys. And Ive already admitted that Id love to ride one of these untamed wildebeests. But what this tells me is that while functionality is important, Im sure the 1967cc engine fires on the first go, that the chain powers the rear wheel and that the bike certainly lunges forward, design, from concept to visual, thought to appeal carry a very real sense of value. So while not everyone may be in agreement over the Wraiths design, there is a truth to how design affects our decisions, attitudes and perceived worth. Car manufacturers continue to roll out almost identical designs with Siamese-like trim and engine options, but motorcycles have found a way to transcend this monotony with unique approaches to the same problems.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span id="intelliTXT">
         <span id="ctl00_ctl07_ctl00_lblArticle">
          Nesbitt, of Confederate comments,
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <span id="intelliTXT">
          <span id="ctl00_ctl07_ctl00_lblArticle">
           But Ive come to realize what I was yearning for was to study vehicle design, which they didnt have in the curriculum and I didnt know to ask. But Im so fortunate I didnt find out it existed because if Id run through the mill like everyone else Id wind up doing stuff like everyone elses, too. My design approach is much more art-form-based, more inspirational.
          </span>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span>
          <span>
           - JT Nesbitt
          </span>
         </span>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        <span>
         <span>
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
       <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_195" style="width: 581px">
        <a href="http://www.lukevandeman.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/11/confederate-001.jpg">
         <img alt="Confederate - Hellcat" class="size-full wp-image-195" height="317" src="http://www.lukevandeman.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/11/confederate-001.jpg" title="confederate-001" width="571"/>
        </a>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Confederate - Hellcat
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        <span>
         <span>
          Artful and inspirational for sure, and even thoughtful. Matt Chambers, the founder of Confederate Motorcycles, even writes
          <a href="http://confederate.wordpress.com/2009/10/21/five-critical-elements-of-american-way-motorcycle-design/" target="_blank">
           great articles
          </a>
          on the
          <a href="http://confederate.wordpress.com/" target="_blank">
           companys blog
          </a>
          .
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span>
         <span>
          Confederate Motorcycles is a fresh reminder of the new America, the idea that copying only creates more of the same, that craftsmenship is a dedication to building a better planet and that innovation as a concept is for everyone even if the final product is only in the hands of a few.
          <br/>
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
