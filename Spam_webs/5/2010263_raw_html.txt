     <p>
      <b>
       Amunet
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'a' in 'bad'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'m' in 'my'">
           m
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// secondary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'n' in 'no'">
           n
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'e' in 'bed'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
           t
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      ; also spelled Amonet or Amaunet) was a primordial goddess in
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">
       Ancient Egyptian religion
      </a>
      . She is a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ogdoad" title="Ogdoad">
       Ogdoad
      </a>
      and the consort of
      <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">
       Amun
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Her name, meaning "the female hidden one", was simply the feminine form of Amun's own name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is possible that she was never an independent deity, as the first mention of either of them is in a pair.
     </p>
     <p>
      By at least the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelfth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twelfth dynasty of Egypt">
       Twelfth dynasty
      </a>
      (c. 19911803 BC) she was overshadowed as Amun's consort by
      <a href="/wiki/Mut" title="Mut">
       Mut
      </a>
      , but she remained locally important in the region of
      <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Egypt" title="Thebes, Egypt">
       Thebes
      </a>
      where Amun was worshipped, and there she was seen as a protector of the
      <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">
       pharaoh
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hart_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hart-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At
      <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">
       Karnak
      </a>
      , Amun's cult center, priests were dedicated to Amunet's service. The goddess also played a part in royal ceremonies such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Sed_festival" title="Sed festival">
       Sed festival
      </a>
      . Amunet was depicted as a woman wearing the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Crown" title="Red Crown">
       Red Crown
      </a>
      and carrying a staff of
      <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">
       papyrus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In late texts from Karnak she was syncretized with
      <a href="/wiki/Neith" title="Neith">
       Neith
      </a>
      , although she remained a distinct deity as late as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_period" title="Ptolemaic period">
       Ptolemaic period
      </a>
      (32330 BC).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hart_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hart-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
