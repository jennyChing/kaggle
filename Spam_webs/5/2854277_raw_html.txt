  $.fn.naviDropDown = function(options) {

    dropDownClass: 'dropdown', //the class name for your drop down
    dropDownWidth: 'auto',  //the default width of drop down elements
    slideDownEasing: 'easeInOutCirc', //easing method for slideDown
    slideUpEasing: 'easeInOutCirc', //easing method for slideUp

  }; 
    
  var opts = $.extend({}, defaults, options);   

    return this.each(function() {  
    var $this = $(this);
    $this.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).css('width', opts.dropDownWidth).css('display', 'none');
    
    var buttonWidth = $this.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).parent().width() + 'px';
    var buttonHeight = $this.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).parent().height() + 'px';
    if(opts.orientation == 'horizontal') {
    $this.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).css('left', '0px').css('top', buttonHeight);
    }
    if(opts.orientation == 'vertical') {
    $this.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).css('left', buttonWidth).css('top', '0px');
    }
    
    $this.find('li').hoverIntent(getDropDown, hideDropDown);
    });
    function getDropDown(){
    activeNav = $(this);
    showDropDown();
    $(this).addClass("hovering");
  }
  
  function showDropDown(){
    activeNav.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).fadeIn({duration:opts.slideDownDuration, easing:opts.slideDownEasing});

  }
  
  function hideDropDown(){
    activeNav.find('.'+opts.dropDownClass).fadeOut({duration:opts.slideUpDuration, easing:opts.slideUpEasing});//hides the current dropdown
    $(this).removeClass("hovering");
  }
  
  
  };

           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7466" style="width: 570px">
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/index.php/art-gallery-inspiration-for-interiors-7460.html/1g" rel="attachment wp-att-7466">
             <img alt="A section of the Pre-Raphaelite collection at the Walker Art Gallery in Liverpool. www.wikipedia.org" class=" wp-image-7466 " height="420" src="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/1g.jpg" width="560"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             A section of the Pre-Raphaelite collection at the Walker Art Gallery in Liverpool. www.wikipedia.org
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            <a href="http://storify.com/eFrame/top-10-must-visit-art-galleries-this-christmas">
             Art Galleries, museums and exhibitions
            </a>
            all seem to have a certain magic about them. Most of this will be attributed to the actual items that are being displayed. Take for example the current exhibition at
            <a href="http://www.nationalgallery.org.uk/">
             The National Gallery
            </a>
            on Venetian Renaissance art (Veronese: Magnificence in Renaissance Venice runs at the National Gallery in London until the 15th of June 2014). There is no doubt that the main pull will be these
            <a href="http://storify.com/eFrame/contemporary-vs-traditional">
             precious and very classical looking works of art
            </a>
            that have stood the test of time.Not only are they stunning examples of the creative, they also serve as a kind of visual record of past political and social events. Some will say that the works have made history sociologically as well as in design.
           </p>
           <p>
            However another part of any successful exhibition is the actual displaying itself.Its only right that these master pieces are shown in a way that they can be seen and appreciated from all angles. These methods are also part of what can make or break a museum or gallery exhibit.
           </p>
           <p>
            It doesnt need to be said that a lot of thought and planning goes into exhibiting a show, especially those on the larger scales. There are many obvious aspects that need to be taken into consideration. including how any people will be visiting the show on average, will there be any height or space issues in conjunction with showing the work? Perhaps some of the visitors will have vision problems and might like to be closer to the work. Then there are also health and safety issues to be looked into.
           </p>
           <p>
            This applies to whether it is a 300 year old masterpiece or a piece from a collage art show. Elements of this could be also applied in a more domestic setting too. Although most of us will not want our living rooms to resemble the Tate Moderns Turbine hall, but there is something very stylish about the methods of display that are found in these larger institutions.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7462" style="width: 711px">
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/custom-frames/view?nrq=1&amp;h=0&amp;ref=MLDA718&amp;w=0">
             <img alt="A Gold Picture Frame in a Baroque or Renaissance style will add more than a hint of high drama to a work of art!" class="size-full wp-image-7462" height="366" src="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/1D.jpg" width="701"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/custom-frames/view?nrq=1&amp;h=0&amp;ref=MLDA718&amp;w=0">
              A Gold Picture Frame in a Baroque or Renaissance style will add more than a hint of high drama to a work of art!
             </a>
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            <b>
             Frame it
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            A
            <a href="http://eframe.co.uk">
             picture frame
            </a>
            alone adds a special feel to the
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/index.php/art-for-the-home-7709.html">
             art
            </a>
            or
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/index.php/framed-photo-of-the-week-andy-brown-7826.html">
             photograph
            </a>
            that is encased within. It could be to do with the act that there are visible boundaries in the form of the frame itself. One is also able to play with the decorative aspects of a picture frame. The style of the frame will naturally have a big impact on the overall effect of the art work. It pays to decide on the effect one is wishing to achieve when framing.
           </p>
           <p>
            A
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/swept-frames">
             bright gold baroque styles swept frame
            </a>
            will add high drama if that is what you want. The desired focus could be kept on the art work itself, in cases such as these it is recommended that a subtle more
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/custom-frames/collections/browse?nrq=1&amp;id=cosmopolitancollection&amp;idstyle=3&amp;nav=1&amp;t=The%20Cosmopolitan%20Picture%20Frames%20Collection&amp;x=1">
             modern-like frame
            </a>
            is used. Think a
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/custom-frames/view?nrq=1&amp;h=0&amp;ref=MLDA331&amp;w=0">
             sleek 1920s black lacquered
            </a>
            or
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/custom-frames/browse?nrq=1&amp;colstr=Silver&amp;idcolour=4&amp;layout=6&amp;materialstr=Wood&amp;nav=1&amp;x=1">
             silver picture frame
            </a>
            to add a subtle but very noticeable outer edge to your work.
           </p>
           <p>
            Will you be framing several pieces at once? Will of these framed images be featured in one room? Perhaps one will want to incorporate the more three-dimensional pieces into the gallery-like setting? You will have the option to experiment with the layout of the room before settling on a set place for both hanging and standing works. The final arrangement will be all important when it comes to getting this look just right.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7463" style="width: 660px">
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/index.php/art-gallery-inspiration-for-interiors-7460.html/1e" rel="attachment wp-att-7463">
             <img alt="A series of smaller frames along a hallway will bring that gallery feel to the narrower of spaces. www.bleuepiece.com" class="size-full wp-image-7463" height="486" src="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/1e.jpg" width="650"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             A series of smaller frames along a hallway will bring that gallery feel to the narrower of spaces. www.bleuepiece.com
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Positioning and spacing
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            The more public exhibitions will normally require plenty of space. Even the permanent collections at the Tate Britain and Tate Modern will include wide expanses of white wall and floor space. Visitors will be able to move freely and easily around these exhibits. This allows for visitors to fully take in hat is on show. The same can be said for the hanging frames in your home.That large living room wall may only need one or two moderately sized framed works of art. Hanging these at a roughly eye level will mean that they are accessible to most. Too high or low and guests will possibly loose interest. It is also a good idea to keep these areas free of furniture as well as any other accessory that may distract attention.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Whether your homes art is a quality reproduced print from a Renaissance master or something that you have crafted yourself, it deserves to be in a place where it can be proudly displayed. Suitable ideas could be a dramatic triptych hanging from a passageway wall. Perhaps your art work could be viewed as one ascends the stairs?
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            The spacing out of these works will also be important. This all really depends on how large the works are as well as how much room is available. A smaller hallway wall that features a very large
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/index.php/framed-art-of-the-week-katharine-rowe-7857.html">
             framed painting
            </a>
            will not be a suitable place. Simply because there wont be the chance to stand back and enjoy the whole image. However a series of smaller paintings could be just right to break up the monotony of this area. Too much space between paintings will mean they may not have much relation or connection with one another. Yet placed too closely together and they could over crowd a room. A space of perhaps half a metre maybe just right in cases such as these. But as with arrangement, the spacing and positioning can be experimented with.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_7464" style="width: 342px">
            <a href="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/index.php/art-gallery-inspiration-for-interiors-7460.html/1f" rel="attachment wp-att-7464">
             <img alt="The Great Court at the British Museum, London. www.commons.wikimedia.org" class=" wp-image-7464 " height="442" src="http://www.eframe.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/1f.jpg" width="332"/>
            </a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">
             The Great Court at the British Museum, London. www.commons.wikimedia.org
            </p>
           </div>
           <p>
            <b>
             Light
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            While all homes will have more than adequate artificial lighting, the sources of natural light are just as important. For the perfect example of how natural light can work in a display space, take a look at the Great Court in Londons British Museum as example of this. Thousands of seamless panes of glass have been joined together to create what looks like sweeping bio-inspired roof. But not only does it serve aesthetic purposes, it also brings lots of natural light into this public exhibition space. No matter what the weather conditions happen to be, the roof is used to full effect in this large circular room and magnifies the available light. A similar idea could be projected in the home, one example could be to have paintings hung in the brightest parts of the home. Conservatories could be a great place to show one or two pieces.
           </p>
