       <div class="article-body-content">
        <p class="normal">
         Theres a big gender gap in architecture but we never talk about it, even though it has an impact on every building, cityscape and urban space we inhabit.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         But now, theres an under-the-radar movement of powerful female architects who are changing the way things get done. Theyre challenging norms within the profession, developing new networks and building a different kind of industry  one that better reflects the communities it serves and represents a more diverse approach to what constitutes a great city.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         The gender gap among architects is bigger than the one youd find at many tech companies like
         <a href="http://time.com/3068015/ebay-diversity-women-blacks-hispanics/">
         </a>
         <a href="http://time.com/3068015/ebay-diversity-women-blacks-hispanics/">
          ebay
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://time.com/3068015/ebay-diversity-women-blacks-hispanics/">
         </a>
         <a href="http://time.com/3068015/ebay-diversity-women-blacks-hispanics/">
          Twitter
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://thinkprogress.org/economy/2014/05/29/3442514/google-diversity-report/">
         </a>
         <a href="http://thinkprogress.org/economy/2014/05/29/3442514/google-diversity-report/">
          Google
         </a>
         , yet we talk about it far less. Only 17% of the members of the American Institute of Architecture (AIA) are women,
         <a href="http://www.aia.org/press/AIAS077761">
          up from 9%
         </a>
         in 2000, and only about
         <a href="http://aiadc.com/calendar/event/women-architecture-lecture-series-lecture-1-business-architecture">
          17%
         </a>
         of principals and partners at architecture firms are women. But a search for gender gap in architecture brings up 225 search results. A similar search for gender gap in technology brings up 132,000 results.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         <img src="http://media2.policymic.com/2c93204810bc2650980c6b0cc6fdff44.png" width="650"/>
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         Yet hidden behind the statistics, theres a rising group of women challenging the status quo. Far from household names, Courtney Brett and Rosannah Sandoval represent the kind of shift taking place. In 2012, Brett became the youngest licensed architect in the history of the American Institute of Architecture (AIA)
         <a href="http://curbed.com/archives/2014/03/18/aia-wunderkind-courtney-brett-bridges-architectural-worlds.php">
          at the age of 24
         </a>
         . Her record was broken a year later by Sandoval, who
         <a href="http://aiasf.org/news/press/2013/03/04/a-conversation-with-rosannah-sandoval-aia/">
          won the honor
         </a>
         by becoming the youngest licensed architect at 23. Its unusual in an industry where only 18% of the licensed architects are women.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img height="256" src="http://media2.policymic.com/e17b4c7df8dd911ea4c49468ec85058c.png" style="float: left; margin-left: 5px; margin-right: 5px;" width="152"/>
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         "The day I received the license, word spread like wildfire through the AIA, Sandoval told
         <i>
          Mic
         </i>
         in an interview (pictured here). The outpour of inspiration from across the country was so gratifying!
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         Its a powerful trend given the incredible additions female architects are making to our landscapes. Today, ones able to rattle off names like Zaha Hadid, Kazuyo Sejima, Maya Lin, Jeanne Gang, Michelle Kaufmann and others, who have designed some incredibly innovative spaces around the world and are now getting the recognition they deserve. The Pritzker Prize, another well-known award for architecture, has been
         <a href="http://www.pritzkerprize.com/laureates/year">
          awarded to a woman
         </a>
         several times since 2005. This year was the first time that a woman, Julia Morgan,
         <a href="http://www.aia.org/practicing/awards/2014/gold-medal/julia-morgan/">
          received the AIAs gold medal award
         </a>
         for achievements in architecture.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         <img src="http://media2.policymic.com/483f115f5e9d663d7cf507ddc2fcdfe4.png" width="800"/>
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         <i>
          Image credit: Wikipedia. The Pabellon Puente y Puente del Tercer Milenio in Spain designed by Zaha Hadid.
         </i>
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         I think my generation has benefited greatly from the female pioneers in our profession,
         <a href="http://aiasf.org/news/press/2013/03/04/a-conversation-with-rosannah-sandoval-aia/">
          said Sandoval
         </a>
         , who will be joining the studio of Diller Scofidio + Renfro in September. Unlike decades before, I know that its possible to be at the top  its just a matter of getting there.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         <i>
          <img src="http://media2.policymic.com/ad83dffdbdb8a59c53ed1735fcb7f200.jpg" width="800"/>
         </i>
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         <i>
          Image credit: RosannahSandoval, courtesy of Perkins + Will.Sandovalwas one of the designers for a new land port of entry in Calexico, CA.
         </i>
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         There are many structural issues that women (and men to a degree) encounter in the profession, from work-life balance, to childcare, to cost of education and others, but now theres a movement to address them. The AIA and
         <a href="http://themissing32percent.com/origins/">
          The Missing 32%
         </a>
         Project, an organization based out of San Francisco whose
         <a href="http://themissing32percent.com/origins/">
          mission
         </a>
         is to ensure that the architectural profession [reflects] the diversity of the communities, users, or clients it serves, are teaming up to do a comprehensive survey on the issue of gender and diversity and architecture to better diagnose and address the issues and build a more inclusive profession. The results are due out in October.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         Its great news for our cities. Greater diversity in the profession leads to a broader understanding of how to approach design and ultimately affects the spaces we inhabit.
        </p>
        <p>
         Its important to involve many sensibilities [in architecture so theres] a much wider spectrum of what constitutes a good city, says Eva Franch i Gilabert, the executive director of the
         <a href="http://www.storefrontnews.org/">
          Storefront for Art and Architecture
         </a>
         in New York.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">
         As the profession is transformed, thanks to the pioneering work of Sandoval and many others, so too will the cityscapes of the future  for the good of us all.
        </p>
        <p class="normal">

         </a>
        </p>
