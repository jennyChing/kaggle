                            I haven't seen this mentioned till now, so I'll put it in this answer.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <h2>
                             We poop wrong
                            </h2>
                            In his 1966 book
                            <i>
                             The Bathroom,
                            </i>
                            Cornell professor Alexander Kira called the modern, sit-down toilet the most ill-suited fixture ever designed. Kira believedand subsequent studies have confirmedthat toilets work against our bodies by forcing us into unnatural angles when we sit down to defecate.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            The solution to hunched-over posture, Kira wrote, is squattinga more natural position that opens the anal sphincter, moves the bodys plumbing into proper alignment, and allows us to evacuate more freely. A 2003 study found that squatters took an average of 51 seconds to move their bowels, as opposed to 130 seconds for those sitting on a standard high toilet.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            <h2>
                             Mechanism
                            </h2>
                            <br/>
                            People can control their defecation, to some extent, by contracting or releasing the anal sphincter. But that muscle can't maintain continence on its own. The body also relies on a bend between the rectumwhere feces builds upand the anuswhere feces comes out. When we're standing up, the extent of this bend, called the anorectal angle, is about 90 degrees, which puts upward pressure on the rectum and keeps feces inside. In a squatting posture, the bend straightens out, like a kink ringed out of a garden hose, and defecation becomes easier.
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            Conventional toilets produce an anorectal angle that's ill-suited for defecation. By squatting, we can achieve of the colon, ridding our bowels of
                            <span class="qlink_container">
                             <a class="external_link" href="http://www.relfe.com/toilet_seat_constipation.html" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "relfe.com")' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                              disease-causing toxins
                             </a>
                            </span>
                            . However, more than this, there is another important reason to squat. It comes in the form of hemorrhoid prevention. Hemorrhoids may be brought on by pregnancy, obesity, and receiving anal sex. But the main cause is straining during bowel movement.
                            <span class="qlink_container">
                             <a class="external_link" href="http://www.nlm.nih.gov/medlineplus/ency/article/000292.htm" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "nih.gov")' target="_blank">
                              Straining increases the pressure in your abdomen
                             </a>
                            </span>
                            , causing the veins that line your anus to swell. In hemorrhoid patients, those veins stay swollen and sometimes bleed. In theory, squatting might stave off hemorrhoids by making defecation easier, reducing the need to strain and decreasing abdominal pressure.

                           <div id="__w2_cfy2AHC_container">
                            <ul>
                             <li>
                              We eat wrong: Too much sugar and processed foods. Eat whole grains. Shop only from fresh aisles (outside aisles in grocery store). We think eating fat is bad, but there are many good fats we should eat (avocado, olive oil, etc).
                              <br/>
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We work out wrong: Work out early in AM to burn more calories throughout the whole day. Even when we do work out, we don't push ourselves via interval training so we don't get gains in muscle/aerobic. Try intervals. We don't mix up your routine, but do the same things every week over and over again. We don't get a healthy mix of activities that include: weights (lifting weights, kettle bell, pilates, TRX, etc), aerobic (running, biking, any sport with a ball), and flexibility (yoga)
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We wear the wrong things: Just watch what not to wear and you can get a sense of clothes that look good for you or make you look heavy.
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We sit wrong: If you sit with your legs only crossed on one side (cross right over left or vice-versa) your body will develop a sideways bent and at one point you'll need body work to get evened out. Bad posture will get you over time.
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We see wrong: Computer folks often only look forward. We don't stare out horizon or peripheral and our muscles atrophy. Checko out how we should see:
                              <span class="qlink_container">
                               <a class="external_link" href="http://www.fireitupwithcj.com/spirituality/show-archives/exercises-to-improve-eyesight/" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "fireitupwithcj.com")' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                                Exercises to improve eyesight (Meir Schneider) - Fire it up with cj
                               </a>
                              </span>
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We don't listen: We listen so we can come back with quick retort, versus listen to truly hear and communicate with another.
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We sit too long: It's important to keep active during the day. Easiest thing to do is to take stairs or walk in place of elevator. Get off the bus a few stops early. Make a point to get a glass of water and walk around every few hours.
                             </li>
                             <li>
                              We wake up wrong: Try to think positively. Set intention first thing in morning. Before you sleep think of 3 things you are grateful for.
                             </li>
                            </ul>
                            And here's the thing. We can do all these things wrong and still be perfect as is. That is the ultimate quandary isn't it?

                            The most important one of them all: Breathing! People tend to have shallow quick breaths which are bad for a variety of reasons. Read more below:
                            <br/>
                            <br/>
                            "For example, once you go below 10 breaths a minute you start to engage the parasympathetic nervous system," says Muench, "which helps the body relax when it has been injured. Slow breathing activates the vagus nerve, the primary cranial nerve, which is associated with a recuperative state." University of Phoenix studies have also demonstrated that slow breathing increases alpha waves in the brain, calming mid-range waves that foster a relaxed yet alert state of mind.
                            <br/>
                            Perhaps more important, slow breathing tends to increase heart-rate variability, a measurement of the fluctuation in heartbeat during an activity. "If your heart rate fluctuates 60 to 80 beats per minute, cardiac-wise that's healthier than someone whose heart rate varies between only 70 and 75 beats per minute," says Muench. "It means your system is not so rigid. Someone like Lance Armstrong has a massive swing in heart-rate variability, whereas an unhealthy or older person has a much smaller one. The way to increase variability is to breathe slowly."
