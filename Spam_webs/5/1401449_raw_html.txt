      <span class="paragraph 0">
       <p>
        Lets get started! the Jazzercise instructor shouted to her class as One Directions What Makes You Beautiful blared through the speakers. Two dozen women from college students to business women filled the dance floor and started shaking their hips.
       </p>
      </span>
      <div class="ad-position standard">
       <div class="advertisement" id="ad-mobile-article-instory">
        <div class="tncms-region-ads" id="tncms-region-ads-mobile-article-instory">

      <span class="paragraph 1">
       <p>
        Jazzercise is no longer your spandex-clad moms aerobics class. The dance exercise program, which incorporates resistance training, Pilates, yoga and kickboxing into each workout, is a juggernaut in the fitness industry. There are 32,000 classes each week taught by some 7,800 instructors worldwide, with 200 of them in Nebraska.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 2">
       <p>
        People think of the leg warmers and the leotards  which is where we started and is awesome to look back on  but we have evolved so much, said Terri Kehrli, who owns three of 10 Jazzercise studios in Omaha.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 3">
       <p>
        Natalie VanKat, a clinical dietitian and Jazzercise instructor in Omaha, thinks Jazzercise has thrived because it adapts to attract a younger clientele.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 4">
       <p>
        Modern Jazzercise uses the latest music that sometimes hasnt even been heard on the radio yet, said VanKat, 27, who started teaching Jazzercise five years ago in Lincoln. We wear modern workout clothes like LuLuLemon and Nike.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 5">
       <p>
        When Jazzercise debuted in Omaha, classes were confined to church basements, said Kehrli, a 13-year Jazzercise veteran. Now there are Jazzercise classes in more than a dozen cities in Nebraska.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 6">
       <p>
        Iowa native Judi Sheppard Missett created Jazzercise in 1969. It had spread to all 50 states by 1983, rooting itself in the fitness industry. Jazzercise generated $100 million in revenue in 2013.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 7">
       <p>
        Not bad for an aerobics class.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 8">
       <p>
        Its a sizable chunk of the $60 billion that Americans spend annually on gym memberships, diet books, workout DVDs and fitness classes, according to market research firm Marketdata Enterprises.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 9">
       <p>
        So how has Jazzercise managed to stay afloatand stand out in a billion-dollar industry? The answer is simple, Kehrli says: It works.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 10">
       <p>
        Participants can burn more than 600 calories in a one-hour class. Primarily a cardio workout, each class starts with a gentle warm-up followed by a 30-minute aerobic and muscle-toning session and ends with light stretching.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 11">
       <p>
        The choreography incorporates low-impact movements like marching in place to high-energy dance movements such as sashays, knee lifts, salsa dancing and body rolls.
       </p>
      </span>
      <span class="paragraph 12">
       <p>
