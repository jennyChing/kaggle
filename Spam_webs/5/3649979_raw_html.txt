         <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="name">
          Amphipolis Tomb Timeline: What We Know So Far About the Magnificent Ancient Greek Monument
         </h1>
         <div class="meta-info">
          <ul class="td-category">
           <li class="entry-category">

             <p>
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amphipolis-caryatids-e1410616841557.jpg">
               <img alt="amphipolis caryatids" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-141366" height="467" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amphipolis-caryatids-e1410616841557.jpg" width="700"/>
              </a>
              <em>
               <br/>
               This article gets updated constantly,as new findings of the Alexander the Great-era tomb surface from the Casta hill in Amphipolis, Greece.
              </em>
             </p>
             <p>
              The recent archaeological discoveries of
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/tag/amphipolis/">
               Amphipolis
              </a>
              have become known acrossthe world. At first, archaeologists wonderedwhether Alexander the Great was buried in the largesttomb that has even been discovered in Greece. However, that theory was ruled out and people started wondering what secrets lie deep under the Casta hill.
             </p>
             <p>
              Here is the timeline of the latest Ancient Greek discoveries and the excavation progress, spearheadedby Greek archaeologist
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/tag/katerina-peristeri/">
               Katerina Peristeri
              </a>
              :
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -January 19, 2015 [
               <span style="color: #ff0000">
                Latest
               </span>
               ]
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Amfipolis.jpg">
                <img alt="Amfipolis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-155352" height="390" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Amfipolis.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              Greece s Ministry of Culture made an official announcement today saying that the laboratory results of the examination of the human skeletal material found inside the grave in the Amphipolis tomb show that four dead were buried in the tomb, along with the remains of a cremated dead. The ministry has also issued several pictures of the
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2015/01/19/five-dead-inside-amphipolis-tomb/">
               bone material found
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -November 12, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Amphipolis_tafos.jpg">
                <img alt="Amphipolis_tafos" class="aligncenter wp-image-147778 size-full" height="429" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Amphipolis_tafos.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              The new discovery of a grave made of limestone allegedly containing a wooden coffin with an integral human skeleton inside the Kasta Hill tomb in Amphipolis, Greece, brings archaeologists closer to solving the mystery of the person buried in the monument.
              <br/>
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/11/12/integral-skeleton-found-in-amphipolis-tomb/">
               The grave
              </a>
              was found 1.60 meters beneath the third chamber floor.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 31, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amfipoli4_473_355.jpg">
                <img alt="amfipoli4_473_355" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-146371" height="454" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amfipoli4_473_355.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              According to an official announcement by the Greek Ministry of culture, excavation works in the Amphipolis tomb continue with the removal of the backfilling earth from the fourth chamber.
              <br/>
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/10/31/eight-square-meter-vault-and-marble-door-found-in-amphipolis-tomb/">
               The crew exposed a vault dug on the natural slate floor. The vault has a surface of 4X2.10 meters and its floor is sealed with limestone.
              </a>
              <br/>
              Also, a marble door that belongs to the third chamber with dimensions 2X0.90X0.15 meters, weighing 1.5 tons was found.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 21, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Missing-Head-of-Amphipolis-Discovered.jpg">
                <img alt="Missing Head of Amphipolis Discovered" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-145345" height="426" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Missing-Head-of-Amphipolis-Discovered.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              Another amazing discovery has surfaced on the Amphipolis dig, Greece.
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/10/21/missing-amphipolis-sphinx-head-discovered/">
               The missing head of the Sphinx guarding the tombs entrance was finally discovered inside the third chamber
              </a>
              .
              <br/>
              The Sphinxs head is intact, with minimal breakage on the nose.
              <br/>
              In addition, fragments of the Sphinxs wings were discovered at the same chamber.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 20, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              Archaeologists trying to reach the next chamber in the
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/10/21/archaeologists-hit-dirt-wall-in-amphipolis-tomb/">
               Amphipolis tomb have hit a wall
              </a>
              of dirt and reaching the next chamber will take longer.
              <br/>
              Minister of Culture and Sports Kostas Tasoulas gave the Greek Parliament a cost analysis of the excavation works.
              <br/>
              Specifically, 390,000 euros were allocated by the Ministry of Culture and Sports, 50,000 euros by the Ministry of Macedonia and Thrace, and the National Bank of Greece donated 150,000 euros
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 16, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Persefoni_Amfipolis.jpg">
                <img alt="Persefoni_Amfipolis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-144691" height="390" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Persefoni_Amfipolis.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              The Greek ministry of culture announced today that the rest of the Amphipolis tomb mosaic has been revealed and that there is a womans face completing the picture.
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/10/16/amphipolis-mosaic-depicts-plutos-abduction-of-persephone/">
               Archaeologists are certain that the woman is Persephone.
              </a>
              <br/>
              In a series of impressive pictures released by the ministry, we see that the female figure has fiery red hair, wears a white robe held by a red ribbon over her breast. She raises her left hand and wears a bracelet. This leads archaeologists to the assumption that the 4.5X3 meters mosaic depicts the abduction of Persephone by Pluto.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 12, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amfipoli_psifidoto.jpg">
                <img alt="amfipoli_psifidoto" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-144129" height="390" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amfipoli_psifidoto.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              Archaeologists have at last publicized
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/10/12/stunning-mosaic-floor-revealed-in-amphipolis-tomb/">
               photos of a stunning mosaic floor discovered in the second chamber of the tomb
              </a>
              , the site of the Caryatids discovery. According to the Greek Ministry of Culture, the colorful floor was laid with white, black, grey, blue, red and yellow pebbles and depicts a chariot in motion. Hermes, the messenger of the gods, is pictured riding the chariot.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 7, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              Speaking to Mega Channel yesterday, Katerina Peristeri, head archaeologist of the Kasta tomb excavations, conjectured that the tombs occupant is a high-level Macedonian figure, possibly one of Alexander the Greats generals. But the secret of the tomb will only be fully revealed in one months time, when the excavations are complete, she added.
              <br/>
              Also speaking on Mega, Theodoros Mavrogiannis, professor of History and Archaeology at the University of Cyprus, reiterated his theory that Hephaestion, close friend of Alexander the Great, lies in the Kasta tomb.
              <br/>
              Mr. Mavrogiannis agrees with Peristeris dating of the tomb to the last quarter of the 4th century BC. He added that the Caryatids discovered in the tombs second chamber may be a direct reference to Athens, Hephaestions hometown.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -October 2, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              After removing a section of soil from the third chamber,
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/10/02/amphipolis-marble-door-found-in-third-chamber/">
               archaeologists have uncovered three fragments of a marble door,
              </a>
              a door hinge and several bronze and iron nails. The new findings have archaeologists convinced that Casta Hill is in fact a tomb.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 30, 2014
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Caryatids-are-on-plinths.jpg">
               <img alt="Caryatids are on plinths" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-142973" height="390" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Caryatids-are-on-plinths.jpg" width="700"/>
              </a>
              New photographs released by the Ministry of Culture show that the Caryatids rest on pedestals. They stand guard over the third chamber, lofting 3.5 meters above the ground. Their bases are each 1.4 meters in height. At two meters tall, the figures themselves stand larger than life; combined with their bases, each towers a total of 3.67 meters into the air. The floor of the second chamber is raised seven centimeters above that of the first. It appears to preserve traces of its original blue paint.
              <br/>
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/09/30/is-the-mother-of-alexander-the-great-in-the-tomb-at-amphipolis-part-3/">
               The third section of Andrew Chuggs commentary
              </a>
              on the baskets resting above the Amphipolis Caryatids heads argues that the baskets resemble those that were actually used in the worship of Dionysus.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 26, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              The head of the team excavating the Casta tomb at ancient Amphipolis, archaeologist Katerina Peristeri, told Athens News Agency yesterday morning that the excavations progress has started to slow. Entering the third and fourth chambers of the tomb is particularly difficult because it requires extended support work in order to prevent roof collapse. But Peristeri reiterated that the complete excavation of the tumulus will be over in one month.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 21, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Karyatide_All.jpg">
                <img alt="Karyatide_All" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-142093" height="416" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Karyatide_All.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              The Culture Ministry releases
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/09/21/greek-culture-ministry-releases-caryatids-full-body-photographs/">
               official photographs of the caryatids guarding the Amphipolis tomb
              </a>
              . Standing at 2.27 meters high, the statues are placed on marble pedestals, reaching up to 1.33 meters in length, 0.68 meters in width and, for now, 0.30 meters in height. They are both sculpted with great detail and are dressed in long chitons and long fringed dresses with folds.
              <br/>
              Furthermore, it is announced that a door was found in the third chamber, which probably leads to another, fourth chamber. Archaeologists working on the site have noted that excavations in the fourth chamber are expected to be very difficult.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 20, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              Peristeri says that she is convinced the tomb was constructed between 300 and 325 B.C.
              <br/>
              However, archaeology professor Olga Palaggia states her strong belief that the tomb is not Macedonian but Roman and that it is falsely associated with the time of the Amphipolis lion statues construction.
              <br/>
              This on-going battle of
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/2014/09/20/13-alexander-era-figures-who-may-be-buried-in-the-amphipolis-tomb/">
               whose remains are hidden in Ancient Amphipolis
              </a>
              will not end until excavations reach the tombs main chamber.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Ancient-Amphipolis.jpg">
               <img alt="Ancient Amphipolis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-142018" height="394" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Ancient-Amphipolis.jpg" width="700"/>
              </a>
              <strong>
               -September 17, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              In an article published on his
              <a href="www.peritexnisologos.blogspot.gr">
               blog
              </a>
              , architect and painter Gerasimos G. Gerolymatos presents a detailed reconstruction of the mound and inner tomb chambers. His plans provide a careful analysis of the tumulus.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 16, 2014
               <br/>
              </strong>
              At this stage, archaeologists are unable to remove soil from the third chamber until protective measures have been taken to support the tombs walls and ceiling in order to prevent collapse. Retaining walls and steel beams will be installed across the length and breadth of the chamber. Soil will be then removed in small sections at a time.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 15, 2014
               <br/>
               <a href="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/third-chamber-in-Amphipolis.jpg">
                <img alt="third chamber in Amphipolis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-141645" height="301" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/third-chamber-in-Amphipolis.jpg" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </strong>
              Archaeologists discover a third chamber afterthe soil was removed behind the Karyatids and an Ionic-style marble lintel gate was gradually exposed. A 13-meter long wall blocks the way to the third chamber.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 14, 2014
              </strong>
              <br/>
              Archaeologists findout that the same marble from Thasos was used to construct not only the Caryatids but the rest of the tomb as well. It is believed to be most likely delivered from the same ancient Macedonian workshop.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 10, 2014
               <img alt="Amphipolis" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-140699" height="357" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/OlympiasAtAmphipolis1.jpg" width="700"/>
              </strong>
              Three red and blue colored epistyles arediscovered, approximately 80 to 100 centimeters long, with a height of 15 cm. They arefound nearthe second
              <span style="color: #4b4b4b">
               diaphragmatic
              </span>
              wall, behind the caryatids, covered in soil, and arequickly transferred to the Archaeological Museum of Amphipolis for security reasons.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -September 6, 2014
               <img alt="Caryatids Amf 1" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-140747" height="332" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/Caryatids-Amf-1.jpg" width="700"/>
              </strong>
              Two caryatids (sculpted female figures) of exceptional artistic value areunearthed. Specialists note that the new discovery further supports the view that the monument is of major Archaeological importance.According to a Culture Ministry announcement, the western caryatids face is saved almost intact, whereas the easterns face is missing.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -August 31, 2014
              </strong>
              <br/>
              Afloor section made of irregular pieces of white marble on red background isdiscovered in the antechamber behind the sphinxes wall. The floor section is in excellent condition. The remains of a fresco with traces of blue coloring arealso found on the first diaphragmatic wall behind the sphinxes.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -August 26, 2014
               <img alt="amphipolis 1" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-140025" height="500" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amphipolis-11.jpg" width="700"/>
              </strong>
              Part of the mosaic floor isrevealed after the last piece of the sealing wall wasremoved. The floor is pebbled in front of the entrance, consisting of rectangular and square shapes, surrounded by black and white rhombs.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -August 21, 2014
               <img alt="amphipolis-sphinxe" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-141381" height="618" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amphipolis-sphinxe.jpg" width="700"/>
              </strong>
              <br/>
              Under the sphinxes base, archaeologists findthe upper portion of a marble doorway, covered with fresco, mimicking the Ionic epistyle, decorated with red, blue and black colors.It rests on two Ionic capitals, also painted with the same colors, crowning the pilasters that form the door frame. Culture Minister Konstantinos Tasoulasconfirms that parts of the Sphinxes wings have also beenfound.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -August 20, 2014
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <img alt="amfipoli1" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-141382" height="420" src="http://greece.greekreporter.com/files/amfipoli1.jpg" width="700"/>
              The Culture Ministry announces that the entrance is guarded by twomarble sphinxes that have been completely unearthed. In addition they announce the discovery of another statue, a part of a Lions back, as well as a small part of the monuments superstructure.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -August 10, 2014
              </strong>
              <br/>
              Archaeologists locate, and reach, the entrance of the ancient Greek tomb.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -2012
               <br/>
              </strong>
              Katerina Peristeri and her team bringsthe grand tomb circle to light, proving that Dimitris Lazaridis theory about Amphipoliswas correct.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -2009
               <br/>
              </strong>
              KaterinaPeristeriand her team restarts excavations at the Casta Hill in Amphipolis, Greece.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               -1956
              </strong>
              <br/>
              Prominent Greek archaeologist Dimitris Lazaridis starts the first excavation atthe site and later suggests that there is an important burial complex under the Casta hill. Lack of funds never allowed the operation to continue. However, by the time of his death in 1985, Lazaridis had discovered at least 70 ancient tombs in other locations.
             </p>
