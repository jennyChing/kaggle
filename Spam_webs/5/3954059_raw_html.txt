         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           Common compounds added to processed food may be a key factor in the obesity epidemic that's swept the country, a new study shows.
          </p>
          <p>
           Researchers found that emulsifiers, which improve the texture of processed foods and extend their shelf life, alter the normal environment of the stomach, inducing intestinal inflammation. That, in turn, promotes inflammatory bowel disease and obesity related disorders that can lead to type-2 diabetes, cardiovascular and even liver disease.
          </p>
          <p>
           The study marks a wake-up call about the potential dangers of a diet that relies too much on processed food, said Andrew Gewirtz, professor at the
           <a href="http://biomedical.gsu.edu/">
            Institute of Biomedical Sciences at Georgia State University
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           "The upshot is eat less processed food," he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           There are various emulsifiers in food. Just look for the word "gum," as in guar gum. The study was focused on two emulsifiers -- polysorbate 80 and carboxymethylcellulose. Researchers added them to the food or water fed the mice in doses equivalent to quantities in processed food.
          </p>
          <p>
           The mice were kept on the diet for three months but the emulsifiers had an impact within eight weeks, becoming ravenous and packing on weight. Most of it was fat. Their bodies also changed in other unhealthy ways.
          </p>
          <p>
           "They had an increase in their fasting glucose levels," Gewirtz said. "They had elevated blood sugar. They were prediabetic."
          </p>
          <p>
           They had what's known as metabolic syndrome, a collection of conditions that increase the risk of heart disease, stroke and diabetes. The added emulsifiers were also associated with genetically engineered mice developing colitis, the rodent equivalent of irritable bowel syndrome.
          </p>
          <p>
           IBD and metabolic syndrome have been on the rise since about the mid-20th century, about the same time that the American diet shifted towards more processed food.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.nrdc.org/about/staff/erik-olson">
            Erik Olson
           </a>
           , director of food and agriculture at the Natural Resources Defense Council, said no one knows how much of these additives people are consuming. There's also a lack of good information about their toxicity, he said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Food and Drug Administration oversees additives. Some are officially approved, others are classified as
           <a href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/IngredientsPackagingLabeling/GRAS/">
            GRAS, generally recognized as safe
           </a>
           . The FDA said in a statement it "closely monitors the scientific literature for information that might indicate a potential public health concern with a food substance."
          </p>
          <p>
           "At this time, the FDA does not have sufficient evidence to alter its previous conclusion that polysorbate 80 and carboxymethyl cellulose are considered safe under their intended conditions of use in food," the statement said.
          </p>
          <p>
           But the study, published in the journal Nature, found that the emulsifiers altered the makeup of the gut microbiota, a varied group of 100 trillion bacteria that normally are separated from the intestine by a mucus layer. The emulsifier-spiked diet enabled bacteria to essentially eat their way through the protective layer, coming into contact with the epithelial cells in the intestine.
          </p>
          <p>
           That is not a good thing, Gewirtz said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The researchers discovered that the changes in the bacteria provoked an inflammatory response from the immune system. That response might interfere with the body's mechanism for signaling satiety, thus spurring the mice to eat more, Gewirtz said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Among a group of mice genetically predisposed to colitis, 60 percent of those on the diet came down with the disease. That compares with about 10 percent without the diet, Gewirtz said.
          </p>
          <p>
           The researchers are now looking at the impact of other emulsifiers.
          </p>
          <p>
           -- Lynne Terry
          </p>
