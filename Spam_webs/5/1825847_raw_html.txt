     <div class="entry">
      <p>
      </p>
      <center>
       <div>
        <table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" width="100">
         <tr>
          <td class="tableborder" style="border-bottom-style: none; border-bottom-width: medium" width="100%">
           <p>
            <img alt="with Abu Khaled al Suri.png" class="mt-image-center" height="314" src="http://www.longwarjournal.org/with%20Abu%20Khaled%20al%20Suri.png" style="text-align: center; display: block; margin: 0 auto 20px;" width="350"/>
           </p>
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td class="tableborder" style="border-top-style: none; border-top-width: medium" width="100%">
           <p align="center" class="imagetext">
            <strong>
             Hassan Abboud, who was killed in todays car bombing, posted this picture on his Twitter page earlier this year. It shows Abboud (right) walking alongside a top al Qaeda operative known as Abu Khalid al Suri (left). Al Suri was killed in a suicide attack in February.
            </strong>
           </p>
          </td>
         </tr>
        </table>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
      </center>
      <p>
       The Islamic Front, a coalition of several rebel groups fighting in Syria,
       <a href="http://justpaste.it/h18t">
        announced today
       </a>
       that more than one dozen of its senior leaders have been killed in a car bombing in Idlib. Among them is Hassan Abboud, who was the head of the Islamic Fronts political office. Abboud was also the head of Ahrar al Sham, an al Qaeda-linked group that holds key positions within the Islamic Front alliance.
      </p>
      <p>
       There has been no claim of responsibility as of this writing. Their deaths are a major blow to the Islamic Front and Ahrar al Sham.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Islamic Front gives the Muslim world the good news of the martyrdom of the head of Ahrar Al-Sham Islamic Movement (Head of the Political Bureau of the Islamic Front) Hassan Abboud  and some of his companions, a statement from the Islamic Front posted on its official Twitter account in English reads.
      </p>
      <p>
       The statement names 11 other slain leaders who are described as some of [Abbouds] companions. They include Abu Abdulmalek, the Islamic Fronts top sharia official. In addition, several other leaders of Ahrar al Sham were killed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ahrar al Sham and the Islamic Front have long been considered one of the most effective fighting forces on the ground in Syria. Western officials had even considered the possibility of working with the group as a supposed counterweight to extremists.
      </p>
      <p>
       But Ahrar al Sham and the Islamic Front are closely allied with the Al Nusrah Front, al Qaedas official branch in Syria. A prominent al Qaeda figure known as Sanafi al Nasr has revealed on his Twitter account that al Qaedas senior leadership in Pakistan dispatched seasoned veterans to serve in the ranks of both Ahrar al Sham and Al Nusrah.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Islamic Front, Ahrar al Sham, and Al Nusrah are opposed to the Islamic State, the al Qaeda offshoot that has captured large swaths of territory stretching across Iraq and Syria since earlier this year.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Abboud was close to Zawahiris representative in Syria
       </strong>
      </p>
      <p>
       Abboud was especially close to a senior al Qaeda operative known as Abu Khalid al Suri, whose real name was Mohamed Bahaiah. Alongside Abboud, Bahaiah cofounded Ahrar al Sham.
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        The Long War Journal
       </em>
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2013/12/aq_courier_rebel_leader_zawahiri.php">
        first reported Bahaiahs dual-hatted role as both an Ahrar al Sham leader and al Qaeda operative
       </a>
       in December 2013. That same month the US Treasury Department reported that Bahaiah was al Qaedas 
       <a href="http://www.treasury.gov/press-center/press-releases/Pages/jl2249.aspx">
        representative in Syria
       </a>
        and was
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2013/12/treasury_designation.php">
        receiving donations from Gulf donors
       </a>
       that were then passed on to other parts of the al Qaeda network.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both Abboud and Bahaiah were staunch opponents of the Islamic State and repeatedly criticized the group and its emir, Abu Bakr al Baghdadi. After the infighting between the Islamic State and the Al Nusrah Front became public in April 2013, Ayman al Zawahiri
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2013/06/analysis_alleged_let.php">
        named Bahaiah as his chief representative
       </a>
       in the region and tasked him with mediating the dispute.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bahaiahs reconciliation efforts failed and he subsequently
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2014/01/statement_from_zawah.php">
        released an audio recording denouncing Baghdadis organization
       </a>
       . He also urged al Qaedas general command to disown the Islamic State, which
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2014/02/al_qaedas_general_co.php">
        they did in early February 2014
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Later that same month, Bahaiah was
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2014/02/zawahiris_chief_repr.php">
        killed in a suicide attack
       </a>
       . It is widely believed that the Islamic State was responsible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several weeks later, in early April 2014, Zawahiri issued a eulogy for his fallen comrade in an audio recording. A video produced by al Qaeda to accompany Zawahiris eulogy highlighted Bahaiahs al Qaeda role, showing
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2014/04/zawahiri_eulogizes_a.php">
        footage of him attending the Al Farouq training camp in Afghanistan
       </a>
       in 2000. The images included a scene of Bahaiah walking between Osama bin Laden and Zawahiri.
      </p>
      <p>
       Senior al Qaeda leaders in Syria, such as Sanafi al Nasr and Abu Firas al Suri, openly mourned Bahaiahs death as well. Both Nasr and Abu Firas said that they had warned Bahaiah that the Islamic State was seeking to kill him.
      </p>
      <p>
       Only after Bahaiah was killed did Abboud make their relationship public. Abboud
       <a href="http://www.longwarjournal.org/archives/2014/02/islamic_front_offici.php">
        posted a series of pictures of the pair of jihadists working side-by-side
       </a>
       . The photos were clearly intended to illustrate Abbouds close working relationship with Bahaiah. One of the pictures can be seen above.
      </p>
      <p>
       Abboud also changed the avatar on his official Twitter page to a photo of Bahaiah. He kept the photo on top of his Twitter feed for weeks after Bahaiahs death.
      </p>
      <p>
       And now Abboud has met the same fate as his longtime companion.
      </p>
