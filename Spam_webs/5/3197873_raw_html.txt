         <div class="m-entry__body" id="article-body">
          <p>
           <a href="http://assets.sbnation.com/assets/31576/ishida1.gif" target="_blank">

           As you may or may not know, my martial arts training is limited to some karate lessons in the mid-90s and getting my ass-kicked badly in high school wrestling.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, I have watched  thousands of hours of fights and even recently started reading some books on fighting technique.
          </p>
          <div class="m-ad m-ad__article-body">
           <div class="dfp_ad" data-cb-ad-id="Mobile article body" data-cb-dfp-id="unit=mobile_article_body" id="div-gpt-ad-mobile_article_body">

          <p>
           Therefore, I believe I'm eminently qualified to start a new column on MMA techniques: The Judo Chop.
          </p>
          <p>
           Today's technique is a really sweet transition that Japanese ace
           <a href="http://sherdog.com/fighter/Mitsuhiro-Ishida-2225">
            Mitsuhiro Ishida
           </a>
           pulled off at his Strikeforce debut against
           <a href="http://sherdog.com/fighter/Justin-Wilcox-16879">
            Justin Wilcox
           </a>
           . Ishida's known for his wrestling, not his submission acumen (only 2 submissions in 17 wins) so this was a bit of a surprise.
          </p>
          <p>
           Here's my attempt to describe what's going on. The gif starts out with an interesting positional battle, Wilcox is going for a single leg and Ishida has a kimura grip. (In traditional Judo that's called an
           <a href="http://www.judoinfo.com/quiz0297_2.htm">
            Ude Garami
           </a>
           grip).
          </p>
          <p>
           From there Ishida rolls into a technique that is similar to
           <a href="http://www.judoinfo.com/images/animations/blue/sumigaeshi.htm">
            Sumi-Gaeshi
           </a>
           -- in Judo its normally done by grabbing the opponent's gi. The kimura grip is a nice way to make the throw work in MMA.
          </p>
          <p>
           We've seen that move in MMA before --
           <a href="http://sherdog.com/fighter/Karo-Parisyan-5153">
            Karo Parisyan
           </a>
           used it on
           <a href="http://sherdog.com/fighter/Dave-Strasser-615">
            Dave Strasser
           </a>
           at UFC 44. Karo describes the move in his book "
           <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Judo-Mixed-Martial-Arts-Parisyan/dp/097773157X/ref=pd_bbs_sr_1?ie=UTF8&amp;s=books&amp;qid=1222869614&amp;sr=8-1">
            Judo for Mixed Martial Arts
           </a>
           " as a "sumi-gaeshi with kimura grip" (p. 151).
          </p>
          <div class="m-ad m-ad__native_ad_mobile">
           <div class="dfp_ad" data-cb-ad-id="Native ad mobile" data-cb-dfp-id="unit=native_ad_mobile" id="div-gpt-ad-native_ad_mobile">
            <script type="text/javascript">
             SBN.Ads.showAd("native_ad_mobile");
            </script>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>
           The difference is, where Karo went ahead and finished Strasser with a kimura, Wilcox spins in mid-air to escape the Kimura. Therefore Ishida rolls into a lightening fast straight arm-bar. I'd love to know if he's trained that sequence before or if he just improvised it in the fight.
          </p>
          <p>
           I assume Ishida has always had a pretty solid Judo background and just hasn't had the chance to use it much in Japan. I think the ring prevents a lot of throws just like it stops a lot of double-leg takedowns (not that I don't love the ring, but just like the cage its an artificial environment that distorts the fight). I also think the commonality of judo training in Japan makes it harder to catch opponents with sweet throws.
          </p>
          <p>
           And that's the Judo Chop of the Week!
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Bonus:
           </b>
           The reason that we call the Ude Garami a Kimura is because Judo legend
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masahiko_Kimura">
            Masahiko Kimura
           </a>
           used the hold to break
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%A9lio_Gracie">
            Helio Gracie's
           </a>
           shoulder in
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masahiko_Kimura#Kimura_vs._H.C3.A9lio_Gracie">
            their famous 1951 match
           </a>
           . You can see Kimura using the Ude Garami grip to land a Sumi-Gaeshi at 0:20 of the video I've put in the extended entry.
          </p>
