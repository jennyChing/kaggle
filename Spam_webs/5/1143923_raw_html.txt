        <div class="diy-stry-body__content">
         <p>
          A ceramic floor is one of the most durable, attractive and low-maintenance floors available. Although it may be a time-consuming, backbreaking job, the finished project will be worth it. Below is a basic outline of the installation steps.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Getting Ready
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Remove the old flooring and baseboard molding and inspect the subfloor. The subfloor should be at least 1 1/8 thick and have no flexibility. It is best to install cement board on top of the plywood subfloor to ensure its stability.
          <br/>
          Measure the square footage of the room be multiplying one side of the room by the other. For example: a 12 by 12 room is 144 square feet. Then add 10% to that number to make up for tile breakage. The final number is the amount of tile needed to complete the project. In this instance the final number will be 158.4 square feet.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Find a Starting Point
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div id="vwoContent">
          <script type="text/javascript">
           if(window.vwo){
    vwoCustomTestAd();
}
          </script>
         </div>
         <p>
          Start tiling in the middle of the room so that any small, cut pieces end up on the edges of the room. To find the middle of the room snap a chalk line from the center of opposite walls. The lines should cross in the middle of the room, marking your starting point.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Laying the tile
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Lay out a line of tile along each of the chalk lines before using mortar. Get an idea of the pattern before committing.
         </p>
         <p>
          After deciding on a layout, mix the mortar per the manufacturers directions. Using a notched trowel, spread a thin coat of mortar in a 2x2 foot section. Place the tiles carefully on the mortar and use tile spacers to keep the distance between each tile precise. You have to use a notched trowel for two reasons: 1. the ridges that it produces in the mortar suction the tile to the floor and 2. the air space in between the ridges help the mortar dry. To ensure that the tiles are level use a rubber mallet and tap the tile into place. Use a level to double check your work.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Cutting Tile
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          The most accurate way to cut tile is with a wet saw. A wet saw works like a table saw, but has a water supply running on the blade to keep it cool. A tile cutter is also an option, but there is a knack to using them and it will take some practice to get a perfect cut.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Grouting
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Let the tile and mortar dry for at least 24 hours before grouting. Mix grout according to the manufacturers directions. Push the grout into every crevice at a 45 degree angle using a grout float. Wipe off any excess with a tile sponge. Rinse the sponge often. Work in small sections. Try not to get too much on the tile, since it is virtually impossible to get it off once dried. There will be a haze left on the tile, but that can be washed off once the grout is cured.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Dry and Seal
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          It may sound funny, but grout needs some moisture to dry correctly. To cure it properly wash the floor with a sponge mop once a day for three days. Apply the sealer after the third day to the grout only.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Finishing Touches
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Put the base board back on and you're done! Now stand back and admire your work!
         </p>
