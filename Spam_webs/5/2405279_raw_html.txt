   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Dental terminology
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Commonly_used_terms_of_relationship_and_comparison_in_dentistry&amp;redirect=no" title="Commonly used terms of relationship and comparison in dentistry">
       Commonly used terms of relationship and comparison in dentistry

     </span>
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      This is a list of
      <b>
       commonly used terms of location and direction in dentistry
      </b>
      . This set of terms provides orientation within the oral cavity, much as
      <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location" title="Anatomical terms of location">
       anatomical terms of location
      </a>
      provide orientation throughout the body.
     </p>
     <p>
      <span id="Anterior">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Anterior" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Anterior
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       the direction towards the front of the head or the lips, as opposed to
       <i>
        posterior
       </i>
       , which refers to the directions towards the back of an individual's head. The term
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior_teeth_(disambiguation)" title="Anterior teeth (disambiguation)">
        anterior teeth
       </a>
       refers to
       <a href="/wiki/Incisor" title="Incisor">
        incisors
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">
        canines
       </a>
       , as opposed to premolars and molars, which are
       <i>
        posterior teeth
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Apical">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Apical" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Apical
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the root tip(s) of a tooth, as opposed to
       <i>
        coronal
       </i>
       , which refers to the direction towards the crown. It may also refer to something relating to the roots, such as
       <i>
        apical support
       </i>
       . When referring to direction in relation to entities on or of the crown, this term can be synonymous with both
       <i>
        cervical
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gingival
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Axial">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Axial" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Axial
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">
        plane
       </a>
       parallel to the surface of a tooth. For example, if a drill bur would be inserted into a tooth from any side (
       <i>
        proximal
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        facial
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lingual
       </i>
       ), the depth of the hole is defined by the axial wall of the hole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Buccal">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Buccal" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Buccal
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The side of a tooth that is adjacent to (or the direction towards) the inside of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">
        cheek
       </a>
       , as opposed to
       <i>
        lingual
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        palatal
       </i>
       , which refer to the side of a tooth adjacent to (or the direction towards) the
       <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
        tongue
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">
        palate
       </a>
       , respectively. Although technically referring only to posterior teeth (where the cheeks are present instead of lips, use of this term may extend to all teeth, anterior and posterior), this term may be employed to describe the
       <i>
        facial
       </i>
       surface of (or directions in relation to) anterior teeth as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Cervical">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Cervical" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Cervical
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       Means
       <i>
        neck
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       (as in
       <a href="/wiki/Cervical_vertebrae" title="Cervical vertebrae">
        cervical vertebrae
       </a>
       ), and refers to the narrowing of the contours of the tooth surface at or near the
       <a href="/wiki/Cementoenamel_junction" title="Cementoenamel junction">
        CEJ
       </a>
       , where the crown meets the root. When referring to direction in relation to entities on or of the crown, it is nearly synonymous with both
       <i>
        apical
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gingival
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Coronal">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Coronal" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Coronal
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the crown of a tooth, as opposed to
       <i>
        apical
       </i>
       , which refers to the direction towards the tip(s) of the root(s). It may also refer to something relating to the crown, such as
       <i>
        coronal forces
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Distal">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Distal" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Distal
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the last tooth in each quadrant of a dental arch, as opposed to
       <i>
        mesial
       </i>
       , which refers to the direction towards the anterior
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_midline" title="Dental midline">
        midline
       </a>
       . Each tooth can be described as having a distal surface and, for
       <i>
        posterior
       </i>
       teeth, a
       <i>
        distobuccal (DB)
       </i>
       and a
       <i>
        distolingual (DL)
       </i>
       corner or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cusp_(dentistry)" title="Cusp (dentistry)">
        cusp
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>

     <p>
      <span id="Facial">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Facial" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Facial
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The side of a tooth that is adjacent to (or the direction towards) the inside of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cheek" title="Cheek">
        cheek
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Lip" title="Lip">
        lips
       </a>
       , as opposed to
       <i>
        lingual
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        palatal
       </i>
       , which refer to the side of a tooth adjacent to (or the direction towards) the
       <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
        tongue
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">
        palate
       </a>
       , respectively. This term is an
       <a href="/wiki/Umbrella_term" title="Umbrella term">
        umbrella term
       </a>
       for both the term
       <i>
        buccal
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        labial
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Gingival">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Gingival" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Gingival
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Gingiva" title="Gingiva">
        gingiva
       </a>
       (gums), synonymous with
       <i>
        cervical
       </i>
       and similar to apical. However, locations on teeth already more
       <i>
        apical
       </i>
       to the interface of the crown and root, referred to as the
       <a href="/wiki/Cementoenamel_junction" title="Cementoenamel junction">
        CEJ
       </a>
       , tend not to be described using this term, as it would lead to confusion, as the exact definition is ambiguous. Additionally, this term would not be used when referring to a tooth
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ex_vivo" title="Ex vivo">
         ex vivo
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Incisal">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Incisal" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Incisal
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       Either the direction towards the biting edge of
       <i>
        anterior
       </i>
       teeth, or to something relating to this edge, such as the terms
       <i>
        incisal guidance
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        incisal edge
       </i>
       . This is the sister term to
       <i>
        occlusal
       </i>
       , which related to the analogous location on
       <i>
        posterior
       </i>
       teeth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Inferior">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Inferior" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Inferior
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the feet of a human's body, as opposed to
       <i>
        superior
       </i>
       , which refers to the direction towards the head. However, use of these terms should enjoy only limited usage when discussing features of a tooth, as, for example, something more inferior on a
       <a href="/wiki/Human_mandible" title="Human mandible">
        mandibular
       </a>
       tooth will be situated more superior on a
       <a href="/wiki/Maxilla" title="Maxilla">
        maxillary
       </a>
       tooth, as they exhibit an inverted relationship. It is for this reason that the terms
       <i>
        coronal
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        apical
       </i>
       are substituted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Interproximal">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Interproximal" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Interproximal
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       An adjective meaning between teeth. For example,
       <i>
        interproximal teeth
       </i>
       refers to the space between adjacent teeth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADAterms_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ADAterms-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheelers_1-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wheelers-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      <span id="Labial">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Labial" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Labial
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The side of a tooth that is adjacent to (or the direction towards) the inside of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lip" title="Lip">
        lip
       </a>
       , as opposed to
       <i>
        lingual
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        palatal
       </i>
       , which refer to the side of a tooth adjacent to (or the direction towards) the
       <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
        tongue
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">
        palate
       </a>
       , respectively. Although technically referring only to anterior teeth (where the lips are present instead of cheeks), use of the term
       <i>
        buccal
       </i>
       may extend to all teeth, anterior and posterior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADAterms_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ADAterms-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>

        Marginal
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       A number of different 'margins' that are involved in dentistry. The edge of tooth structure that is prepared to meet the edge of a prosthetic
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_(dentistry)" title="Crown (dentistry)">
        crown
       </a>

       </a>

       <i>
        occlusal
       </i>

       <i>
        marginal
       </i>

       </i>
       and a
       <i>
        mesiolingual (ML)

       corner or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cusp_(dentistry)" title="Cusp (dentistry)">
        cusp

      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the back of an individual's head, as opposed to

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

      <span id="Superior">
      </span>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt class="glossary" id="Superior" style="margin-top: 0.4em;">
       <dfn class="glossary">
        Superior
       </dfn>
      </dt>
      <dd class="glossary">
       The direction towards the head of a human's body, as opposed to
       <i>
        inferior
       </i>
       , which refers to the direction towards the feet. However, use of these terms should enjoy only limited use when discussing features of a tooth, as, for example, something more superior on a
       <a href="/wiki/Human_mandible" title="Human mandible">
        mandibular
       </a>
       tooth will be situated more inferior on a
       <a href="/wiki/Maxilla" title="Maxilla">
        maxillary
       </a>
       tooth, as they exhibit an inverted relationship. It is for this reason that the terms
       <i>
        coronal
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        apical
       </i>
       are substituted

         </span>

      </dd>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Combining_of_terms">
       Combining of terms
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dental_terminology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Combining of terms">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Most of the principal terms can be combined using their corresponding
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_compound" title="Classical compound">
       combining forms
      </a>
      (such as
      <i>
       mesio-
      </i>
      for
      <i>
       mesial
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       disto-
      </i>
      for
      <i>
       distal
      </i>
      ). They provide names for directions (vectors) and axes; for example, the coronoapical axis is the long axis of a tooth. Such combining yields terms such as those in the following list. The abbreviations should be used only in restricted contexts where they are explicitly defined and help avoid extensive repetition (for example, a journal article that uses the term "mesiodistal" dozens of times might use the abbreviation "MD"). The abbreviations are ambiguous: (1) they are not specific to these terms; (2) they are not even one-to-one specific within this list; and (3) some of the combined terms are little used, and the abbreviations of the latter are even less used. Therefore spelling out is best.
     </p>
     <p>
      The combined terms include apicocoronal (AC), buccoapical (BA), buccocervical (BC), buccogingival (BG), buccolabial (BL), buccolingual (BL), bucco-occlusal (BO), buccopalatal (BP), coronoapical (CA), distoapical (DA), distobuccal (DB), distocervical (DC), distocoronal (DC), distogingival (DG), distolingual (DL), disto-occlusal or distoclusal (DO), distopalatal (DP), linguobuccal (LB), linguo-occlusal (LO), mesioapical (MA), mesiobuccal (MB), mesiocervical (MC), mesiocoronal (MC), mesiodistal (MD), mesiogingival (MG), mesio-occlusal or mesioclusal (MO), mesiopalatal (MP).
     </p>
