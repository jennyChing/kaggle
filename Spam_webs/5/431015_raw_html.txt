         <p>

         <h2>
          Weave Traditional Chair Seats
          <br/>
         </h2>
         <h3>
          Traditional techniques and modern materials produce attractive, durable results.
          <br/>
         </h3>
         <p>
          by Tim Johnson
         </p>
         <p>
          Visit any antique or second-hand furniture store and youll find great old chairssuch as these solid cherry ladderbacksthat are bargain priced because their woven seats have worn out. In fact, any chair with round rails or dowels used to form the seat frame can have a splint or a rush seat installed.
         </p>
         <p>
          The choice is mainly an aesthetic one. Splint seats (above left) are created by weaving together flat lengths of wood that have been soaked in water to make them pliable. Rush seats (above right) are installed by wrapping twisted strands of fiber around each corner of a chair in a continuous loop. Both seats take about the same amount of time to install and the cost for materials is similar. With proper use and occasional maintenance, both seats can provide ten or more years of service.
         </p>
         <p>
          I find installing these seats satisfying, quiet work. Yes, it takes time, but the techniques arent difficult and mastering them connects you to an ancient craft.
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
             </p>
             <h3>
              How to weave a splint seat
             </h3>
             <p>
              Traditionally, splints were split by hand from native hardwoods such
              <br/>
              as ash, oak and hickory. Flat reed splint made from the stem of the
              <br/>
              tropical rattan palm is much more common today. The rattan palm is a
              <br/>
              climbing vine-like tree; its stems can grow up to 600 ft. long!
             </p>
             <p>
              A uniform creamy-white color, flat reed splint comes in different
              <br/>
              widths and is machined to uniform thickness. Flat oval reed splint is
              <br/>
              also available. It has a rounded top surface. The amount youll need
              <br/>
              depends on the size of your chair seat and the width you choose. I used
              <br/>
              just over one bundle of 1/2 in. splint for this chair, which has an
              <br/>
              average-size seat. Bundles cost $15 or less (see Source, page 40).
             </p>
             <p>
              Flat reed splints should be soaked in hot water for at least twenty
              <br/>
              minutes prior to use. Each splint has a top and bottom facealways
              <br/>
              weave with the top face up. The top face is smoother to the touch. You
              <br/>
              can also bend the splint into a tight U; small fibers breaking away
              <br/>
              on the outside of the curve indicate the bottom face. Thinned varnish
              <br/>
              (equal parts mineral spirits and varnish) applied to both the top and
              <br/>
              bottom sides helps to protect splint seats from drying out due to
              <br/>
              seasonal changes in humidity. Splint seats can also be stained; seal
              <br/>
              with varnish to keep the stain from rubbing off onto clothing.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               Click any image to view a larger version.
              </b>
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seat1.jpg">
               <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seat.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               Splint seats are created by weaving together flat lengths of wood that have been soaked in water to make them pliable.
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               A uniform creamy-white color, flat reed splint comes in different widths and is machined to uniform thickness. Flat oval reed splint is also available. It has a rounded top surface.
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_cords1.jpg">
               <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_cords.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
              </a>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                1. Chair seats are usually wider
               </b>
              </b>
              at the front, so the first step is to establish a rectangular seat area. Mark the front rail while holding a square against the back rail, with its corner against the back leg.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_11.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_1.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                2. Nail the first splint
               </b>
              </b>
              to the back rail. Draw it over the rail and forward around the front rail at your reference mark. Take out the slack, wrap the splint back around the back rail and draw it forward again. Continue this process to wrap the entire length of splint.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_21.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_2.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                3. Connect new lengths of splint
               </b>
              </b>
              on the bottom. Use a spring clamp to keep the wrapped splint under tension. Overlap the previous length by about 6 in. and staple all along the overlap. Orient the staples crown-side-out.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_31.jpg">
               <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_3.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
              </a>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                4. Keep wrapping splint
               </b>
              </b>
              front-to back until you reach the opposite reference reference mark on the front rail. Cut the splint and staple it to the adjacent underside loop. This layer, now completed, is called the warp.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_41.jpg">
               <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_4.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
              </a>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                5. Weaving is the next step.
               </b>
              </b>
              Experiment with different patterns, using short splints. Simply alter the over-under counts between the weave and warp splints. Well proceed with the diagonal herringbone pattern.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_51.jpg">
                 <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_5.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
                </a>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                6. Establish the back edge.
               </b>
              </b>
              Weave a length of splint, called a weaver, across the warp, using a 3-under, 3-over pattern thats centered, so the weaver passes under the same number of warp splints at both ends. Cut the ends flush.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_61.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_6.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                7. To create the diagonal pattern
               </b>
              </b>
              , shift the 3-under, 3-over pattern by one warp splint with each new row: Here, the back weaver started 2-under, so this weaver starts 3-under. On the next row, the weaver will start 1-over.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_71.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_7.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                8. Wrap the weaver
               </b>
              </b>
              around the rail and weave it across the seats bottom, using the same pattern as on the top. Secure the next weaver by overlapping the end of the first weaver by 6 to 8 inches and weaving it in.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_81.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_8.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                9. When the seat is about half finished
               </b>
              </b>
              , fill in the corners by weaving in additional warp splints. Lock them in place on the top by looping their ends around weaver splints and drawing out the slack. On the bottom, just weave them in.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_91.jpg">
                 <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_9.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
                </a>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                10. Completing the last few rows
               </b>
              </b>
              is challenging, because the seat becomes more taut as you weave. A table knife spreads the warp and helps to guide the splint.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_101.jpg">
                 <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_10.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
                </a>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <h3>
              How to weave a rush seat
              <br/>
             </h3>
             <p>
              Natural rush strands are made from cattail leaves. Fiber rush, made
              <br/>
              from kraft paper (think grocery store bags), is an economical
              <br/>
              substitute thats more widely available than natural rush and much
              <br/>
              easier to use.
             </p>
             <p>
              Fiber rush comes in a variety of colors and diameter sizes. The
              <br/>
              smaller the diameter, the longer it takes to complete a seat; larger
              <br/>
              diameters are stiffer and harder to work with. Fiber rush is commonly
              <br/>
              available in 2 lb. coils and costs $10 to $15 per coil (see Source,
              <br/>
              below). I always buy two coils for every chair seat, to make sure I
              <br/>
              have enough. I also check the color, to make sure the coils match.
             </p>
             <p>
              Make fiber rush more pliable before use by misting it with water
              <br/>
              from a pump spray bottle. Two keys to success are to keep the strands
              <br/>
              consistently twisted and consistently taut as you weave. Use short
              <br/>
              strands at first, to build out the front corners, then work with
              <br/>
              strands that are 20 to 25 ft. long. Use a spring clamp to maintain the
              <br/>
              tension when you tie in new stands. Finish fiber rush seats with two
              <br/>
              coats of shellac thinned to a 1 lb. cut.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seat1.jpg">
               <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seat.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               Rush seats are installed by wrapping twisted
               <br/>
               strands of fiber around each corner of a chair in a continuous loop.
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               Natural rush strands are made from cattail leaves. Fiber rush, made from kraft paper (think grocery store bags), is an economical substitute thats more widely available than natural rush and much easier to use.
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_cord1.jpg">
               <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_cord.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
              </a>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                1. Chair seats are usually wider
               </b>
              </b>
              at the front, so the first step is to establish a rectangular seat area. Mark the front rail while holding a square against the back rail, with its corner against the back leg.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_11.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/splint-seats_5F00_1.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                2. Rush seats are woven
               </b>
              </b>
              by repeating one wrapping pattern. Starting from the left rail, draw the rush strand over and around the front rail. Continue up, over and around the left rail, turning 90 degrees and crossing over the strand. Draw the strand to the top of the right rail and repeat the pattern. Its that simple.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_21.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_2.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                3. Create the rectangular seat area
               </b>
              </b>
              by filling the front corners, using short, individual strands wrapped around both front legs and tacked to the side rails. Each successive strand builds the seat toward the center.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_31.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_3.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                4. Once the rectangular seat area is established,
               </b>
              </b>
              wrapping begins in earnest. Tack a long strand to the left rail and begin a continuous counter-clockwise loop that wraps around all four corners.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_41.jpg">
                 <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_4.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
                </a>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                5. Tie in new strands
               </b>
              </b>
              on the underside of the seat, using a square knot. Always keep the strands taut.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_51.jpg">
                 <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_5.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
                </a>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                6. As you wrap each corner,
               </b>
              </b>
              its important to keep the rush strands at right angles and to straighten the diagonal line formed by the crossover joints. Pinching each crossover joint makes the job easier by compressing the strands.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_61.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_6.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                7. Press the rush strands
               </b>
              </b>
              to the corners to fill gaps and keep uniform spacing. Fiber rush comes in different sizes. Larger sizes build the seat faster, but theyre harder to pinch and press, so a few gaps are likely to remain.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_71.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_7.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                8. When the seat is about two-thirds complete,
               </b>
              </b>
              fill the space between the top and bottom strands to add support. Install two or three layers of cardboard thats been cut to fit inside the seats four triangular-shaped sections.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_81.jpg">
                <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_8.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
               </a>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                9. When the seat area is wider
               </b>
              </b>
              than it is deep, the final wrapping goes front to back, through the ever-shrinking hole in the middle of the seat. If the seat is deeper than wide, the wrapping goes side to side.
             </p>
            </td>
            <td width="20">
            </td>
            <td width="280">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                <a href="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_91.jpg">
                 <img alt="" border="0" src="/wp-content/uploads/rush-seats_5F00_9.jpg" style="border:0;vertical-align:top;"/>
                </a>
               </b>
              </b>
             </p>
            </td>
           </tr>
          </tbody>
         </table>
         <p>
         </p>
         <table>
          <tbody>
           <tr>
            <td width="230">
             <p>
              <b>
               <b>
                10. Tack the last strand
               </b>
              </b>
              to the bottom rail. Unravel the end and stuff it under the strands on either side
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
