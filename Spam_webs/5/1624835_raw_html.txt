   <b>
    Explanation:
   </b>
   What does the Martian moon Phobos look like?

To better visualize
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_(moon)">
    this unusual object
   </a>
   , 
images from
   <a href="http://www.esa.int/About_Us/Welcome_to_ESA/ESA_history/A_history_of_the_European_Space_Agency">
    ESA
   </a>
   's
   <a href="http://www.esa.int/Our_Activities/Space_Science/Mars_Express/Mars_Express_mission_facts">
    Mars Express
   </a>
   orbiter have been combined into a
   <a href="ap130916.html">
    virtual
   </a>
   <a href="ap130612.html">
    rotation
   </a>
   movie.

The rotation is actually a digital illusion --
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_locking">
    tidally-locked
   </a>
   Phobos always keeps the same
face toward its home planet, as does Earth's moon.

The
   <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7MMOCw21D4w">
    above video
   </a>
   highlights Phobos' chunky shape and an unusually dark surface covered
with craters and grooves.

What lies beneath the surface is a
   <a href="http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2013DPS....4531301A">
    topic of research
   </a>
   since the moon is not dense enough to be filled with solid rock.
   <a href="http://www.universetoday.com/107446/groovy-martian-moon-shows-off-its-weird-stripes-in-new-video/">
    Phobos
   </a>
   is losing about of 
centimeter of altitude a year and is expected to break up and
   <a href="ap121028.html">
    crash onto Mars
   </a>
   within the next 50 million years.

To better understand this unusual world,
   <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Express">
    Mars Express
   </a>
   is on course to make the
   <a href="http://www.esa.int/Our_Activities/Space_Science/Mars_Express/Mars_Express_heading_towards_daring_flyby_of_Phobos">
    closest flyby ever
   </a>
   on Sunday.
  </p>
