        <header>
         <h2>
          Silence is Agreement
         </h2>
        </header>
       </div>
       <div class="blog-more-content">
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          We are making this too difficult! Everyone
          <i>
           talks
          </i>
          about how we are not
          <i>
           communicating
          </i>
          . Hundreds of workshops are held to help people converse. Everyone and her grandmother is in on the act.
          <a href="http://www.iexecuvision.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/Silence-is-Agreement.jpg">
           <img alt="Silence is Agreement" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-1320" height="204" src="http://www.iexecuvision.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/Silence-is-Agreement-300x204.jpg" width="300"/>
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          Over the past 15 years, ExecuVision International has worked with nearly 200 companies. One component of every engagement has been around the topic of communication. In all that time, I have never met a single company or team that told me they had mastered the art. Every single person has acknowledged that the communication channel was not working.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          Of course, I have also heard a lot of reasons for the lack of communication. Those reasons usually have to do with the
          <i>
           other persons
          </i>
          inability to pay attention, or with the senior executive levels inability to communicate openly. Somehow, it is always the other person who has some missing quality that, if it were present, would magically alter the state of the relationship.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          The most important communication in any
          <i>
           organization
          </i>
          , though, is not the individual, one-on-one conversation. It is, rather, the conversation we have as a group. It is how we handle group conversations that truly define the culture of any organization.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          Why is it that we cannot say publicly what everyone already knows? Why is it so hard to make transparent the truths that every single employee has divined for themselves? And how stupid do we have to make ourselves in the eyes of everyone around us before we face up to a few simple truths? Arent we supposed to be the leaders here?
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          Years ago, when I was still running a large company, I learned some Rules of Engagement for meetings that I have used ever since. Simple rules, understood by all, posted on the walls of our conference room.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          1)
          <b>
           <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
            No spectators allowed
           </span>
          </b>
          (posted outside the conference room)
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          If you have been invited to the meeting, it is because we believe that you have something to contribute. Our meetings are
          <i>
           inspirational, not informational
          </i>
          . We do not hold meetings to simply transfer information. We interpret the information provided prior to the meeting. You are here to participate. If you cannot do that, please dont take up space in this room.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          <b>
           2)
          </b>
          <b>
           <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
            Silence is Agreement
           </span>
          </b>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          If you dont agree with what is being said, you have an obligation to either ask for clarification or share your point of view. You dont get to passively ride the time out, and then later tell everyone, I knew it wouldnt work. If you know it wont work, youd better tell us now.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          <b>
           3)
          </b>
          <b>
           <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
            Any issue that affects the group is a group issue
           </span>
          </b>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          This is the most important rule of group dynamics. If the behavior of a team member is negatively affecting the groups performance, we need to talk about that as a team, because we are all affected. Most negative behavior is unintentional. That doesnt make it less negative, but it does mean that the perpetrator may not be aware of the consequences of that behavior. We have to make that apparent,
          <i>
           and we have to make it public.
          </i>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          4)
         </b>
         <b>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           Whats said in the room stays in the room
          </span>
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          The Las Vegas rule is critical. If we are to have an open dialogue, then safety is essential. We can only establish that safety when we know that every idea and possibility can be explored without fear of reprisal
          <i>
           or ridicule
          </i>
          . It is the facilitators responsibility to create that safety, in part, by enforcing this rule.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          5)
         </b>
         <b>
          <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
           A voice is not a veto
          </span>
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          Yes, everyone has a need to be heard. Being heard is not the same thing as always having your point of view accepted. Democracy revolves around an open dialogue, followed by a decision. The majority rules only after the minority has been heard. The minority accepts that we now have a decision and, as part of this team, I am obligated to support it. If I cannot support it, then I no longer belong on this team.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          Following these five simple rules wont guarantee the success of every organizational conversation. It will, however, significantly improve your odds for getting said what needs to be said. Wouldnt that be an improvement?
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="color: #8b4513;">
          All it requires is that you do not remain silent!
         </span>
        </p>

         <p>
          While I agree that wasted time at meetings should be reduced, points 1 and 4 of this article seem very questionable, and certainly at
          <br/>
          odds with open book principles.  My points follow the text of both below:
         </p>
         <p>
          1)      No spectators allowed (posted outside the conference room) If you have been invited to the meeting, it is because we believe that
          <br/>
          you have something to contribute. Our meetings are inspirational, not informational. We do not hold meetings to simply transfer information. We interpret the information provided prior to the meeting. You are here to participate. If you cannot do that, please dont take up space in this room.
         </p>
         <p>
          There is nothing wrong with information sharing at a meeting, as is certainly the case in open book companies  And open door policies, including most meetings, leads to a level of transparency that tends to be much more inspirational than rah rah meeting, as I saw first-hand at Southwest Airlines.
         </p>
         <p>
          4)      Whats said in the room stays in the room The Las Vegas rule is critical. If we are to have an open dialogue, then safety is essential. We can only establish that safety when we know that every idea and possibility can be explored without fear of reprisal or ridicule. It is the facilitators responsibility to create that safety, in part, by enforcing this rule.
         </p>
         <p>
          Candid dialogue is useful, and sometimes confidentiality is important where sensitive personnel or market information are involved.  But in general, The Las Vegas rule is a really bad idea, creating a sense of secrecy and exclusion.  Sunlight is a great disinfectant that works well in company meetings.
