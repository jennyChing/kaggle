         The Definitive Guide to Canning Tomatoes (for Those Afraid of Poisoning Themselves)
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="tags">
        Tags:

        </a>

       <p>
        <img align="right" alt="Ultimate Guide to Canning Tomatoes (for Those Afraid of Poisoning Themselves)" hspace="5" src="http://www.tomatocasual.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/09/canned-tomatoes.jpg" title="Ultimate Guide to Canning Tomatoes (for Those Afraid of Poisoning Themselves)"/>
        By Tomato Queen
       </p>
       <p>
        Ill admit that eying the stacks of gleaming tomato jars in my cabinet has brought almost as much satisfaction over the long, bleak months of winter as eating them in a delicious sauce or stew.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Canning (really, jarring) preserves the texture and flavors of tomatoes like nothing else, and is an affordable way to eat organic and/or heirloom tomatoes year-round.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        But make no mistake: canning definitely takes time and equipment.
       </p>
       <p>
        Those who havent canned find it daunting, but have no fear; following a few basic essentialssterility, acidity, heatwill ensure safe and delicious tomatoes for 1-2 years.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span id="more-151">
        </span>
        Equipment youll need:
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
                 Water Bath with Rack
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Tomatoes are acidic enough that you dont need a pressure cooker. If you dont own a covered water bath (with rack) and mason jars, theyre often easily scored in rural/farm area garage sales and thrift stores. But for those who dont have time to scour garage sales or your local craigslist, consider purchasing a water bath on eBay; where you can find a $60 piece of equipment for $16 or so.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
                  Mason Jars and Bands
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        I recommend wide-mouthed quart jars for tomatoes, as theyre easier to fill and a good size for cooking. Make sure the jars have no chips or cracks. These, too, you can often find in garage sales and thrift stores; or you can buy sets with rings and lids in mega mart stores and general stores alike. Its pricy to buy jars new, but worthwhile if youre using multiple times.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
                  Lids
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        These are the rubber-coated discs that the bands secure. These are inexpensive, and should always be used new.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         	Canning Tongs
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        You can also use regular kitchen tongs, but they are unwieldy and take extra care.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
                 Optional
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        There are canners wands, which are little plastic pen-sized tools with a magnet on the end, for fishing the lids out of hot water.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Additionally:
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
                 Loads of Ripe Tomatoes!
        <br/>
                 Canning/pickling salt (or kosher salt). No iodine.
        <br/>
                 A pint of fresh lemon juice (bottled works)
        <br/>
                 Several basil leaves, cleaned and patted dry
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step 1: Prep
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
                 Fill water bath 2/3rds full and bring water to a boil; then keep at a near-simmer until ready to can. Bring a second large stock pot of water to boil for blanching/peeling later.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Take clean mason jars and bands and sterilize in the hot water bath by boiling for 3-5 minutes. Remove with tongs and let dry on clean towels.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Wash all tomatoes thoroughly. Best not to use any tomatoes that have rotten parts at all.
       </p>
       <p>
                 You might want to separate your tomatoes, by type, variety, and/or color if you care about such things, so that you can use your paste tomatoes for sauces and your green zebras for stews, etc.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Keep the coated lids in a small pot of hot water on the stove (do not boil).
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step 2: Blanch, Peel and Trim
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        To keep skins from floating and marring sauces, soups and stews:
       </p>
       <p>
                 Drop several tomatoes into the stock pot for 30-60 seconds. (This is easiest in a wire basket, but also works to scoop out with a strainer.) Drop into bowl/pot of cold water.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Then just peel the skins right off.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Cut the cores and any inedible spots from the tomatoes. Optional: if using paste tomatoes, which are full of pectin and not much juice, you may wish to squeeze/scrape out the seeds from the center.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step 3: Fill the Jars
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Now youre ready to can!
       </p>
       <p>
                 Fill the jars with tomatoes, leaving no more than 1 inch of space.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Use a chopstick or slender rubber spatula to press the tomatoes down so that any air pockets fill with juices. Fill with more tomatoes if need be.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Top off the jar with 2 Tbsp of lemon juice and a basil leaf to each quart (1 Tbsp of lemon to a pint). If theres food residue on the top of the jar, wipe off with a clean paper towel.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Take a clean lid from the lid pot and place onto the filled jar. Screw on the band (no need to make this too tight) and put aside.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Repeat until youve used up all your jars or tomatoes! Jars really do need to be full to seal; you dont want to can jars with too much air in them.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Step 4: Bathe and Cool
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
                 Bringing the water bath back to boil, bring the rack up so that the handles are resting over the sides of the pot. Load the jars onto the rack and gently lower the rack into the water. The water should cover the jars by at least 1-2 inches.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Cover the canner and let process for 45 minutes for quarts; 40 minutes for pints.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Raise the rack back up and carefully remove the hot jars, leaving them to cool on a clean towel (cooling racks are nice, too, on top of towels).
       </p>
       <p>
                 Repeat with the rest of the jars until all have been canned.
       </p>
       <p>
                 Let cool: youll hear popping noises for the next   12 hours as the jars cool and seal, one by one. You may wish to remove the bands while the jars dry so as not to get rusty. You can re-screw them on again later, if youd like, or not; theyre not necessary after your jar has sealed.
       </p>
       <p>
        If you have any jars that have not sealed after 12 hours, refrigerate! Occasionally through a defect in the lid or jar, you may get one or two that refuse to seal.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Store the jars out of direct sunlight, and your tomatoes and juice should bring you pleasure for months to come.
        </em>
       </p>

         <p>
          I havent canned in about 5 years, but have just put up 26 1 1/2 pints of stewed (so versatile), 4 quarts chopped &amp; 16 jars of salsa. If you have questions, go online &amp; look at websites for Ball or Kerr or the USDA site for helpful hints. The salt is for flavoring only (although generally salt does act as a slight perservative). The vinegar or bottled lemon juice is very important! Waterbath canning, as opposed to Pressure canning, is dependent upon proper acidity levels. Does it change flavor, yes slightly, but not worth throwing a weekends worth of work away or constantly wondering if everything is going to be okay. Tomatoes are acidic, but if they are overripe they will have less acidity (counter intuative huh?)I think the pH should be around 4.5 or so for safety concerns. If you add non-acidic items (herbs, onions, peppers) you should increase the amount of added acidifier to offset decreased acidity. Always clean jars w/ hot soapy water &amp; sterilize for 10 mintues in boiling canning kettle. Rings can go in also, but for the lids: wash &amp; place in sauce pan w/ hot (not boiling)water until needed. Add salt &amp; vinehar to jars 1st, then fill to 1/2-1 of tops. Wipes rims w/clean papertowel or cloth. Place lid on jar &amp; hold lid down while screwing on ring to ensure proper seating. Dont overfill jars or contents will bubble up under the lid &amp; prevent proper sealing. Start time when water has returned to gentle boil &amp; process for time recommended (I go by 30 min/pint or 45min/qt). Let set until cool &amp; then I give the jars a shake to redistribute the contents (cooking causes separation). You wont always hear the click when sealing. Test the top with your finger, if it doesnt move, your good to go. If it still pops up&amp;down, reseal w/in 24 hrs with new lid(!), use right away or put in freezer bag &amp; freeze. Fresh canned tomoes are better than anything you can buy!!!!!! Happy canning!!!

         <p>
          So many people writing here about leaving out the lemon juice and/or not water-bathing the filled jars.  I am really getting worried about your safety!
         </p>
         <p>
          Theres a reason that ALL scientific experts in canning, such as Ball, Kerr, and university extension offices, insist upon lemon juice and prescribed times for water bathing. Its to prevent bacteria from growing inside those sealed jars.  The water-bathing attempts to kill any bacteria present.  However, water boils at 212 degrees and some bacteria can survive that.  The lemon juice increases acidity, which provides a hostile environment in the jar for those bacteria that survive the temperature.
         </p>
         <p>
          Thats for just tomatoes.  When you add other ingredients that are not acidic  herbs (including basil), onions, peppers, garlic, meat  it becomes much more dangerous because of the greatly lower acidity.  Those bacteria that survive 212 degrees can also survive in the less acid environment inside your jar.  In that case, pressure canning is recommended.  The increased pressure means the boiling temperature will be higher than 212 degrees, killing those tougher bacteria.
         </p>
         <p>
          Just because jars are sealed does not mean theyre safely canned.  The jars have been filled with hot stuff and lidded, so when they cool a partial vacuum forms and sucks down the lids.  That doesnt mean bacteria are killed.  Youre planning to put these jars on a shelf for a year or more, at room temperatures, so its important that no bacteria be able to grow in there.
         </p>
         <p>
          So, although youve been canning w/o and its been fine so I\m not going to start  youve just been lucky so far.  All it would take is one bacteria-contaminated jar and you could be very, very ill or worse.  Please, folks, if youre going to home can anything, take the time to find out the safe way to do it.  Dont take shortcuts or do it the way your grandmother did.

          Onions and carrots are not acidic, and MUST be pressure canned. The boiling point of water (212 degrees) is not hot enough to kill Boutilism.
         </p>
         <p>
          It would be safe to re-process them if you just canned them yesterday.
         </p>
