    <td valign="top">
     <p>
      Welcome to the American Languages pages devoted to German  dialects in the United States, including Ltzebuergesch (Luxembourgish), which is officially recognized as a language in its own right.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to a number of
      <a href="#essays">
       short essays
      </a>
      on  various aspects of German-American linguistics, we are currently featuring  sound clips from the following categories:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="states/wisconsin/german_wi.htm">
        Wisconsin German Dialects
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="../search_clip_type.php?clip_type=PennDutch">
        Pennsylvania Dutch
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="../search.php?clip_type=TexasGerman&amp;state=tx">
        Texas German
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Our recordings of German-American dialects come from the Max  Kade Institutes North American German Dialect Archive, which contains several  hundred digitized interviews with speakers going back over half a century.
     </p>
     <p>
      The  oldest recordings were made in the 1940s by Professor Lester W. J. Seifert  (UWMadison). These consist mainly of Low German dialects of southeastern  Wisconsin, as well as Wisconsin High German. During the late 1960s, Prof.  Jrgen Eichhoff (UWMadison; later Penn State University) recorded interviews with a  number of speakers in nearly all major areas of German settlement in Wisconsin.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Texas German recordings were made by Prof. Glenn G. Gilbert (Southern  Illinois University at Carbondale) in the 1960s and 1970s.
     </p>
     <p>
      Also in the Gilbert  collection are a number of Kansas German interviews that will be featured soon.  Most of the Pennsylvania Dutch recordings were made in the 1980s by Prof.  Wolfgang W. Moelleken and colleagues at SUNY-Albany, as well as Jrgen  Eichhoff, who interviewed a number of Amish and Mennonite Pennsylvania Dutch  speakers in Indiana and Wisconsin.
     </p>
     <p>
      These German dialect pages are being regularly updated and  expanded, so please visit often!
     </p>
