       On Saturday, amid the cold and rain, an estimated
       <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-26903931">
        7 million Afghans
       </a>
       headed to the polls to choose from a
       <a href="http://www.tolonews.com/elections2014/candidates">
        roster of presidential candidates
       </a>
       whose average age is 63. This in a country where
       <a href="http://www.undp.org.af/whoweare/undpinafghanistan/Projects/dcse/prj_youth.htm">
        68 percent
       </a>
       of the population is under the age of 25where seven in 10 people were 12 or younger when the U.S. invaded Afghanistan.
      </p>
      <figure class="right" style="max-width: 300.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:93.3333333333%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/04/Screen_Shot_2014_04_05_at_10.36.16_AM/f0862785b.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="credit">
        <a href="https://www.cimicweb.org/Documents/CFC%20AFG%20Social%20Well-being%20Archive/CFC_Afg_Youth_Bulge_Oct03.pdf">
         CimicWeb.org
        </a>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Arguably the most important storyline in this weekend's electionwhich marks the first democratic transfer of power in Afghanistan's modern historyis the participation of Afghan youth, who will
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2014/03/afghanistan-from-the-air/284409/">
        play an outsize role
       </a>
       in shaping the country's future as U.S. troops withdraw. Ahead of this year's poll, Afghan election officials
       <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/story/news/world/2014/04/05/afghanistan-elections/7343425/">
        predicted
       </a>
       that the majority of voters would be young people (the voting age is 18), which explains why presidential frontrunners like Ashraf Ghani and Zalmay Rassoul
       <a href="http://www.tolonews.com/en/afghanistan/14226-youth-movement-endorses-rassoul">
        pledged
       </a>
       to carve out more than half the positions in their government for the younger generationand why the race has produced initiatives like a contest to
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2014/03/the-afghan-election-gets-its-own-hip-hop-anthem/284325/">
        develop a hip-hop anthem
       </a>
       for the election.
      </p>
      <p>
       But in Afghanistan, campaign promises and rap songs aren't enough to court the youth votethe challenges in engaging young people go well beyond apathy about the political process. The men jockeying to replace longtime President Hamid Karzai are an unappealing, all-too-familiar mix of warlords and government insiders. And in recent weeks, the Taliban repeatedly vowed to violently disrupt the election, in part by reprising a 2009 tactic of attacking people with blue ink on their fingera measure to prevent voter fraud that also makes it easier for the militant group to target voters.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_international&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=20" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_international&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=20"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       What's remarkable is that many young people have treated these obstacles as an opportunity to get
       <em>
        more
       </em>
       involved in the election, not less. On Monday, representatives of the Kabul-based
       <a href="https://www.facebook.com/youngactivistsnetwork">
        Young Activist Network for Reform and Change
       </a>
       (YARC), an umbrella organization of social and civil-society groups that includes some 300,000 members, inked their fingers a week ahead of the vote to encourage Afghans to participate in the election despite the Taliban's threats.
      </p>
      <figure class="left" style="max-width: 300.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:65.6666666667%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/04/BlueInk/952f82d26.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        YARC members show off their blue fingers (YARC)
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       "Even if the Taliban and al-Qaeda cut off my hand or finger, as a youth, I will still take part in the elections," YARC member Sangar Amirzaizada
       <a href="http://www.tolonews.com/en/afghanistan/14405-afghan-youth-ink-fingers-to-promote-participation">
        told
       </a>
       Afghanistan's Tolo News. (This year,
       <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/04/06/world/asia/afghanistan-voting.html?_r=0">
        <em>
         The New York Times
        </em>
       </a>
       reports, voters are getting invisible ultraviolet ink on one finger and blue silver-nitrate ink on another, meaning people "in troubled areas could be identified by insurgents for some three days.")
      </p>
      <p>
       The younger generation's emergence as a powerful political force has gone beyond symbolic actions. The National Democratic Institute
       <a href="https://www.ndi.org/files/AFG-2014-Election-Mission-Update-March-19.pdf">
        notes
       </a>
       that they are the reason campaigning via social media and mobile technology have, for the first time in Afghan electoral history, become critical components of the race (in 2013 there were 2.4 million Internet users in the country, up from 2,000 during the 2004 election). In March, Afghanistan's election commission
       <a href="http://www.tolonews.com/en/afghanistan/14165-youth-constitute-70-percent-of-provincial-council-candidates">
        reported
       </a>
       that young people constituted a staggering 70 percent of provincial council candidates across the country (YARC itself has put forward more than 35 candidates for provincial councils).
      </p>
      <p>
       And these young people appear to be more concerned with building the country's future than litigating its past, which has been dominated by warlords, ethnic conflicts, and civil wars. "Our elders are divided, but the youth are more united," the youth activist Israr Karimzai
       <a href="http://www.stripes.com/news/ethnic-divisions-playing-lesser-role-in-afghan-elections-1.275850">
        told
       </a>
       <em>
        Stars and Stripes
       </em>
       . "There are certain candidates that are playing the ethnic card in this election, but Afghans have been through the process of national solidarity and they are aware of national interest."
      </p>
      <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/special-report/democracy" name="&amp;lid=Democracy-Bug&amp;lpos=Promo-Bug" style="float:right;margin: 0 0 10px 10px;" title="The Democracy Report">
       <img alt="The Democracy Report" src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/static/front/images/specialreports/democracy/bug.png"/>
      </a>
      <p>
       <span>
        Ehsanullah Hikmat
       </span>
       , the head of YARC's international-relations committee, expressed similar views when we spoke on Friday, just hours before the election. He explained that the organization, which has not endorsed a presidential candidate, cares most about the contenders' policies for young people, women, and Afghans as a wholenot factors like the candidates' ages or what side they were on in the country's civil wars.
      </p>
      <p>
       "About 1.5 million Afghan youth are unemployed. What are the schemes for this unemployment?" he asked. "What are the schemes for the economics of our country?"
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector2">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_international&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=19" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_international&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=19"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-3">
      <p>
       "We need peace. We need stability. We need infrastructure. We need schools, roads, hospitals," added Hikmat, a 25-year-old from Logar province who studied diplomatic relations at Kabul University. He said that while young people in cities like Kabul and Herat are especially focused on economic issues, their counterparts in rural areas are more worried about security. He wants Afghanistan to sign a pending
       <a href="http://america.aljazeera.com/opinions/2014/4/afghanistan-bilateralsecurityagreementtalibankarzai.html">
        Bilateral Security Agreement
       </a>
       with the United States, and he's opposed to the so-called "zero option" that the Obama administration is considering, in which no U.S. troops would remain in the country by the end of 2014.
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:70.5263157895%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2014/04/RTR3K0V7/a9667dea6.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        Afghans queue to vote at a polling station in Kabul on Saturday. (Reuters/Ahmad Masood)
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       But, as Hikmat's comments suggest, Afghanistan's younger generation isn't a monolith. And not everyone in his age cohort is embracing democratic values.
      </p>
      <p>
       Earlier this month, Borhan Osman, a political analyst in Afghanistan,
       <a href="http://www.afghanistan-analysts.org/afghan-youth-for-democracy-not-all-of-them">
        wrote
       </a>
       that less educated young people in rural areas tend to be "politically inactive," while a growing number of more educated young people in conservative urban areas are flocking to Islamist groups that oppose democracy and elections and favor sharia-based forms of governance:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        In many cases, scepticism towards elections stems from experiences in the post-Taleban democracy era, which was riddled with war, corruption, manipulation, political exclusion and the rule of warlords and patronage politics. Dissatisfaction with the government seems to have tarnished the reputation of all democratic politics that produced the government, driving people to seek alternatives they perceive as better. In conservative segments of society, religious groups have often offered as an alternative the form of an Islamic state based on sharia law. This discourse pits an Islamic system against a democratic system  in the marred form Afghans experienced during the past decade. In Khost, for example, in many discussions with young people, from school teachers to madrassa tutors and university graduates, the dominant discourse was that elections were un-Islamic because they have come from the West and are not based on Islamic principles of governance. Digging deeper in conversation, however, the author understood that opposition to election was based more on grievances with the politics of the past decade than an authentic and detailed religious argument. These young Afghans appeared more supportive of the Taleban than of the government, recalling grievances they and their communities had with the government and Western forces in recent years, for example, night raids and the killing and detention of villagers. The Taleban themselves have never been supportive of an electoral system  and have not spelled out or developed their general ideas about the countrys future political system, apart from vaguely seeking an Islamic state.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      These findings hint at one of the key questions in this weekend's historic election. As the United States leaves Afghanistan, what lessons will young Afghansthe country's future leadersdraw from the last decade and a half of war and instability? Will they work to improve Afghan democracy and elect more effective and accountable leaders, or will they spurn the democratic process in favor of a system they perceive as superior?
     </section>

        <h3 class="hed">
         Why the Iran Deal Makes Obama's Critics So Angry
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/peter-beinart/" title="Peter Beinart">
          Peter Beinart
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         The nuclear agreement highlights the limits of American powersomething the presidents opponents wont accept.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Mankind faces a crossroads,
          <a href="http://www.skeptic.ca/Woody_Allen_Speech_to_Graduates.htm">
           declared
          </a>
          Woody Allen. One path leads to despair and utter hopelessness. The other, to total extinction. Let us pray we have the wisdom to choose correctly.
         </p>
         <p>
          The point is simple: In life, what matters most isnt how a decision compares to your ideal outcome. Its how it compares to
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/alternatives-obama-iran-nuclear-deal-israel/389751/">
           the alternative at hand
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The same is true for the Iran deal,
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-33518524">
           announced Tuesday
          </a>
          <span>
           between Iran and six world powers
          </span>
          . As Congress begins debating the agreement, its opponents have three real alternatives. The first is to kill the deal, and the
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/the-iran-nuclear-deal-by-the-numbers/389592/">
           interim agreement
          </a>
          that preceded it, and do nothing else, which means few restraints on Irans nuclear program. The second is war. But top American and Israeli officials have warned that military action against Iranian nuclear facilities could ignite a catastrophic regional conflict and would be ineffective, if not counterproductive, in delaying Irans path to the bomb. Meir Dagan, who oversaw the Iran file as head of Israels external spy agency, the Mossad, from 2002 to 2011,
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/04/world/middleeast/04mossad.html">
           has said
          </a>
          an attack would mean regional war, and in that case you would have given Iran the best possible reason to continue the nuclear program. Michael Hayden, who ran the CIA under George W. Bush from 2006 to 2009,
          <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2012/01/19/bushs-cia-director-we-determined-attacking-iran-was-a-bad-idea/">
           has warned
          </a>
          that an attack would guarantee that which we are trying to prevent: an Iran that will spare nothing to build a nuclear weapon.
         </p>

        <h3 class="hed">
         The Iran Deal Upends U.S. Politics
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/david-frum/" title="David Frum">
          David Frum
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Five ways Tuesdays announcement reshapes the terms of the domestic political debate
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          The Iran nuclear deal upends the security of the Middle East region. Old alliances have been shoved aside. Massive new resources have been put at the disposal of the Iranian regime. The security implications of the agreement will take time to be felt. The U.S. political consequences will be immediate.
         </p>
         <p>
          Here are five:
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           The Rand Paul Candidacy for the Republican Nomination Is Over
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          The Iran deal presents Republicans with a sharp binary choice. Line up with President Obama in support of a treaty achieved mostly by American concessions that puts Iran on the path to an internationally accepted nuclear weapon sometime in the 2020s? A treaty that bypasses Congress role and that is opposed by every U.S. ally in the region, including but not limited to Israel? No cash prize for predicting how the Republican primary electorate will align.
         </p>

        <h3 class="hed">

        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/jeffrey-goldberg/" title="Jeffrey Goldberg">
          Jeffrey Goldberg
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         <span>
          Making sense of a morally dubious agreement that might be a practical necessity
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">

          </a>

          <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2015/04/05/politics/netanyahu-iran-deal/">
           calling it a a very bad deal,
          </a>
