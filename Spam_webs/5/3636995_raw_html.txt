   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Umbral calculus
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
       mathematics
      </a>
      before the 1970s, the term
      <i>
       <b>
        umbral calculus
       </b>
      </i>
      referred to the surprising similarity between seemingly unrelated
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynomial_equation" title="Polynomial equation">
       polynomial equations
      </a>
      and certain shadowy techniques used to 'prove' them. These techniques were introduced by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Blissard" title="John Blissard">
       John Blissard
      </a>
      (
      <a href="#CITEREFBlissard1861">
       1861
      </a>
      ) and are sometimes called
      <b>
       Blissard's symbolic method
      </b>
      . They are often attributed to
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lucas" title="douard Lucas">
       douard Lucas
      </a>
      (or
      <a href="/wiki/James_Joseph_Sylvester" title="James Joseph Sylvester">
       James Joseph Sylvester
      </a>
      ), who used the technique extensively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1930s and 1940s,
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Temple_Bell" title="Eric Temple Bell">
       Eric Temple Bell
      </a>
      attempted to set the umbral calculus on a rigorous footing.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1970s,
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Roman" title="Steven Roman">
       Steven Roman
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gian-Carlo_Rota" title="Gian-Carlo Rota">
       Gian-Carlo Rota
      </a>
      , and others developed the umbral calculus by means of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_functional" title="Linear functional">
       linear functionals
      </a>
      on spaces of polynomials. Currently,
      <i>
       umbral calculus
      </i>
      refers to the study of
      <a href="/wiki/Sheffer_sequence" title="Sheffer sequence">
       Sheffer sequences
      </a>
      , including polynomial sequences of
      <a href="/wiki/Binomial_type" title="Binomial type">
       binomial type
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Appell_sequence" title="Appell sequence">
       Appell sequences
      </a>
      , but may encompass in its penumbra systematic correspondence techniques of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calculus_of_finite_differences" title="Calculus of finite differences">
       calculus of finite differences
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_19th-century_umbral_calculus">
       The 19th-century umbral calculus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbral_calculus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The 19th-century umbral calculus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The method is a notational procedure used for deriving identities involving indexed sequences of numbers by
      <i>
       pretending that the indices are exponents
      </i>
      . Construed literally, it is absurd, and yet it is successful: identities derived via the umbral calculus can also be properly derived by more complicated methods that can be taken literally without logical difficulty.
     </p>
     <p>
      An example involves the
      <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_polynomials" title="Bernoulli polynomials">
       Bernoulli polynomials
      </a>
      . Consider, for example, the ordinary
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binomial_expansion" title="Binomial expansion">
       binomial expansion
      </a>
      (which contains a
      <a href="/wiki/Binomial_coefficient" title="Binomial coefficient">
       binomial coefficient
      </a>
      ):
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="(y+x)^n=\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}y^{n-k} x^k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/3/2/032bd084e42536bbce65d8689fbc579e.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      and the remarkably similar-looking relation on the
      <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_polynomials" title="Bernoulli polynomials">
       Bernoulli polynomials
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="B_n(y+x)=\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}B_{n-k}(y) x^k." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/e/b/8eb4a84a6ed6eedd832c00b3dbfc298a.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Compare also the ordinary derivative
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \frac{d}{dx} x^n = nx^{n-1} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/5/d/e5dad2c9b57f239b3cadac712aa332ff.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      to a very similar-looking relation on the Bernoulli polynomials:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt=" \frac{d}{dx} B_n(x) = nB_{n-1}(x)." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/4/6843244da83e83d635fd4d49e379dd53.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      These similarities allow one to construct
      <i>
       umbral
      </i>
      proofs, which, on the surface, cannot be correct, but seem to work anyway. Thus, for example, by pretending that the subscript
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      
      <i>
       k
      </i>
      is an exponent:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="B_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n {n\choose k}b^{n-k}x^k=(b+x)^n," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/7/7a77c80db44bb042cc95c5c771e54ee3.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      and then differentiating, one gets the desired result:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="B_n'(x)=n(b+x)^{n-1}=nB_{n-1}(x).\, " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/d/0/6d0069fa1df58ae7a9dfaaf01ed34e57.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      In the above, the variable
      <i>
       b
      </i>
      is an "umbra" (
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      for
      <i>
       shadow
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      See also
      <a href="/wiki/Faulhaber%27s_formula" title="Faulhaber's formula">
       Faulhaber's formula
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Umbral_Taylor_series">
       Umbral Taylor series
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbral_calculus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Umbral Taylor series">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Similar relationships were also observed in the theory of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_differences" title="Finite differences">
       finite differences
      </a>
      . The umbral version of the
      <a href="/wiki/Taylor_series" title="Taylor series">
       Taylor series
      </a>
      is given by a similar expression involving the
      <i>
       k
      </i>
      'th
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_difference" title="Forward difference">
       forward differences
      </a>
      <img alt="\Delta^k [f]" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/e/5/1e58285f6ee0ef184011bdacd6933445.png"/>
      of a
      <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">
       polynomial
      </a>
      function
      <i>
       f
      </i>
      ,
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{\Delta^k [f](0)}{k!}(x)_k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/2/e/a2e3958d202a530e599e2c856ec9a41f.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="(x)_k=x(x-1)(x-2)\cdots(x-k+1)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/e/5/2e55866d359ad1f7e6c7269f929cac88.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Pochhammer_symbol" title="Pochhammer symbol">
       Pochhammer symbol
      </a>
      used here for the falling sequential product. A similar relationship holds for the backward differences and rising factorial.
     </p>
     <p>
      This series is also known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Finite_difference#Newton.27s_series" title="Finite difference">
       <i>
        Newton series
       </i>
      </a>
      or
      <b>
       Newton's forward difference expansion
      </b>
      . The analogy to Taylor's expansion is utilized in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calculus_of_finite_differences" title="Calculus of finite differences">
       calculus of finite differences
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bell_and_Riordan">
       Bell and Riordan
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbral_calculus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bell and Riordan">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the 1930s and 1940s,
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Temple_Bell" title="Eric Temple Bell">
       Eric Temple Bell
      </a>
      tried unsuccessfully to make this kind of argument logically rigorous. The
      <a href="/wiki/Combinatorics" title="Combinatorics">
       combinatorialist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/John_Riordan_(mathematician)" title="John Riordan (mathematician)">
       John Riordan
      </a>
      in his book
      <i>
       Combinatorial Identities
      </i>
      published in the 1960s, used techniques of this sort extensively.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_modern_umbral_calculus">
       The modern umbral calculus
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umbral_calculus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The modern umbral calculus">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Another combinatorialist,
      <a href="/wiki/Gian-Carlo_Rota" title="Gian-Carlo Rota">
       Gian-Carlo Rota
      </a>
      , pointed out that the mystery vanishes if one considers the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_functional" title="Linear functional">
       linear functional
      </a>
      <i>
       L
      </i>
      on polynomials in
      <i>
       y
      </i>
      defined by
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="L\left(y^n\right)= B_n(0)= B_n.\," class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/c/c/fcceb1e86ee70d1b8c096ed7b6bcacc5.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Then, using the definition of the Bernoulli polynomials and the definition and linearity of
      <i>
       L
      </i>
      , one can write
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="
B_n(x) =\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}B_{n-k}x^k = \sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}L\left(y^{n-k}\right)x^k = L\left(\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}y^{n-k}x^k\right) = L\left((y+x)^n\right).
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/3/5/33535479a9eafe9db0fa3e4b2de74763.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      This enables one to replace occurrences of
      <img alt="B_n(x)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/e/f/3efcf545ce9bca872d347805b46ba157.png"/>
      by
      <img alt="L((y+x)^n)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/b/e/ebeeca3e672c52bc3044ff956a3b7372.png"/>
      , that is, move the
      <i>
       n
      </i>
      from a subscript to a superscript (the key operation of umbral calculus). For instance, we can now prove that
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="B_n(y+x)=\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}B_{n-k}(y) x^k" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/5/e/d5e706d84c4b02995afbe3a456d5bfc4.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      by expanding the right-hand side as
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}B_{n-k}(y) x^k = \sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}L\left((2y)^{n-k}\right) x^k = L\left(\sum_{k=0}^n{n\choose k}(2y)^{n-k} x^k\right) = L\left((2y+x)^n\right) = B_n(x+y)." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/e/37eebca1d8c055977b26dc41a913be4c.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Rota later stated that much confusion resulted from the failure to distinguish between three
      <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_relation" title="Equivalence relation">
       equivalence relations
      </a>
      that occur frequently in this topic, all of which were denoted by "=".
     </p>
     <p>
      In a paper published in 1964, Rota used umbral methods to establish the
      <a href="/wiki/Recursion" title="Recursion">
       recursion
      </a>
      formula satisfied by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_numbers" title="Bell numbers">
       Bell numbers
      </a>
      , which enumerate
      <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_a_set" title="Partition of a set">
       partitions
      </a>
      of finite sets.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the paper of Roman and Rota cited below, the umbral calculus is characterized as the study of the
      <b>
       umbral algebra
      </b>
      , defined as the
      <a href="/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field" title="Algebra over a field">
       algebra
      </a>
      of linear functionals on the
      <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">
       vector space
      </a>
      of polynomials in a variable
      <i>
       x
      </i>
      , with a product
      <i>
       L
      </i>
      <sub>
       1
      </sub>
      <i>
       L
      </i>
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      of linear functionals defined by
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\langle L_1 L_2 \mid x^n \rangle = \sum_{k=0}^n {n \choose k}\langle L_1 \mid x^k\rangle \langle L_2 \mid x^{n-k} \rangle." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/2/3/123c6adaff24e9a8502dc2c2ba35fa01.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      When
      <a href="/wiki/Polynomial_sequence" title="Polynomial sequence">
       polynomial sequences
      </a>
      replace sequences of numbers as images of
      <i>
       y
      </i>
      <sup>
       <i>
        n
       </i>
      </sup>
      under the linear mapping
      <i>
       L
      </i>
      , then the umbral method is seen to be an essential component of Rota's general theory of special polynomials, and that theory is the
      <b>
       umbral calculus
      </b>
      by some more modern definitions of the term.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A small sample of that theory can be found in the article on
      <a href="/wiki/Binomial_type" title="Binomial type">
       polynomial sequences of binomial type
      </a>
      . Another is the article titled
      <a href="/wiki/Sheffer_sequence" title="Sheffer sequence">
       Sheffer sequence
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Rota later applied umbral calculus extensively in his paper with Shen to study the various combinatorial properties of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cumulant" title="Cumulant">
       cumulants
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
