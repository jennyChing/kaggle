        <h1 class="entry-title">
         History Slam Episode Forty-One: Race, Identity, and Newfoundland Culture in Robert Chafes Oil and Water
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="date">
          <a href="http://activehistory.ca/2014/05/history-slam-episode-forty-one-race-identity-and-newfoundland-culture-in-oil-and-water/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to History Slam Episode Forty-One: Race, Identity, and Newfoundland Culture in Robert Chafes Oil and Water">
           <time class="entry-date published" datetime="2014-05-21T05:00:17+00:00">
            May 21, 2014
           </time>
           <time class="updated" datetime="2015-06-09T17:13:13+00:00">
            June 9, 2015
           </time>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="author vcard">
          <a class="url fn n" href="http://activehistory.ca/author/sean-graham/" title="View all posts by Sean Graham">
           Sean Graham
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="comments-link">
          <a href="http://activehistory.ca/2014/05/history-slam-episode-forty-one-race-identity-and-newfoundland-culture-in-oil-and-water/#comments">
           1 Comment
          </a>
         </span>

       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <em>
          By Sean Graham
         </em>
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_13424" style="width: 310px">
         <a href="http://activehistory.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/alison_woolrdige_neema_bikersteth_jeremiah_sparks_web__large.jpg">
          <img alt="Alison Woolridge, Neema Bikersteth, and Jeremiah Sparks in Oil and Water at the Neptune Theatre. Photo via the National Arts Centre." class="size-medium wp-image-13424" height="185" src="http://activehistory.ca/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/alison_woolrdige_neema_bikersteth_jeremiah_sparks_web__large-300x185.jpg" width="300"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Alison Woolridge, Neema Bikersteth, and Jeremiah Sparks in Oil and Water at the Neptune Theatre. Photo via the National Arts Centre.
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         On February 18, 1942 off the coast of St. Lawrence, Newfoundland, the USS Truxton and the USS Pollux ran aground in the midst of a harsh winter storm. Of the 389 sailors on both ships, only 186 survived. Of those, one stood out: Lanier Phillips. After being rescued by a group of locals, Phillips became the first African American in St. Lawrence, an experience that forever changed him and the community.
        </p>
        <p>
         That transformation is the focus of playwright Robert Chafes
         <em>
          Oil and Water
         </em>
         , which is currently running at the
         <a href="http://nac-cna.ca/en/englishtheatre/event/5068">
          National Arts Centre
         </a>
         . The show examines both Laniers background and that of the St. Lawrence townspeople. The two parallel stories come together in a beautifully poignant moment that demonstrates how our preconceptions can be changed in an instant.
         <br/>
         <span id="more-13418">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         Until his death in 2012, Lanier Phillips remained an extremely
         <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/newfoundland-labrador/lanier-phillips-civil-rights-pioneer-laid-to-rest-1.1218991">
          popular figure in Newfoundland
         </a>
         . In an interview just before his death, he said that the experience changed him, eventually leading him to the Civil Rights Movement: I just had to join up with Dr. King and thats because of the change they did for me in St. Lawrence. After growing up in segregated Georgia, it was his brief time in Newfoundland that erased the hatred with which he grew up.
        </p>
        <p>
         The two days Phillips was in St. Lawrence proved transformational for the community as well. During a period of Newfoundlands history that was generally looked upon unfavourably, Phillips telling of the story of his time in St. Lawrence provided the community an opportunity to celebrate its history. For as much as the town changed Phillips, Phillips changed the town.
        </p>
        <p>
         In this episode of the History Slam, I talk with Robert Chafe about
         <em>
          Oil and Water
         </em>
         . We chat about Lanier Phillips legacy, the challenges of representing a true story on stage, and the development of Newfoundland culture. In particular, we discuss the Newfoundland accent and the rare occasions when it is presented in Canadian culture outside of comedy.
        </p>
        <p>
         <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="499" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/S3qmBhU0_XQ?feature=oembed" width="665">
         </iframe>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Sean Graham is a doctoral candidate at the University of Ottawa where he is currently working on a project that examines the early years of the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation. He has previously studied at Nipissing University, the University of the West Indies, and the University of Regina and like any red-blooded Canadian his ultimate dream is to be a curling champion while living on a diet of beer and poutine.
         </em>
        </p>
