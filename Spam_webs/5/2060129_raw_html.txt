        <h1 class="entry-title">
         See a 1-day-old Moon challenge
        </h1>
        <p class="meta post-meta">
         Posted on
         <span class="updated">
          21 January 2015
         </span>
         by
         <span class="vcard author">

        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry clearfix">

        <p>
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_2504" style="width: 630px">
         <a href="http://astronomynow.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Mars-Venus-Mercury-Moon_21Jan15_1730_UT.jpg">
          <img alt="If you wish to go for this one-day-old Moon challenge you'll need a very clear sky and a totally unobstructed view to the southwest with a level horizon. The graphic depicts the view one hour after sunset (5:30 pm for the central British Isles). The 1.2-day-old Moon will have a whisker-thin 2% crescent, while Venus and Mercury will lie a little over 6 apart. Mercury and the Moon will fit in the same low-power binocular field. AN graphic by Ade Ashford" class="size-full wp-image-2504" height="377" src="http://astronomynow.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Mars-Venus-Mercury-Moon_21Jan15_1730_UT.jpg" width="620"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          If you wish to go for this one-day-old Moon challenge youll need a very clear sky and a totally unobstructed view to the southwest with a level horizon. The graphic depicts the view one hour after sunset (5:30 pm for the central British Isles). The 1.2-day-old Moon will have a whisker-thin 2% crescent, while Venus and Mercury will lie a little over 6 apart. Mercury and the Moon will fit in the same low-power binocular field. AN graphic by Ade Ashford
         </p>
        </div>
        With the added challenge of poor weather predictions for the UK this evening, hopefully some fortunate observer(s) will have an opportunity to see a young crescent Moon just 28hoursold.
        <p>
         If you see a window of clear sky an
         <a href="http://astronomynow.com/almanac/" target="_blank" title="Almanac">
          hour after sunset
         </a>
         youll have to be quick, as the Moon will be
         <em>
          very low
         </em>
         in the southwest, so youll need a pair of binoculars and a flat, unobscured horizon. Let bright Venus be your guide and move two binocular fields (just over 7) to the right to find the whisker-thin 2% lunar crescent. If you succeed in viewing the Moon, low power binoculars (750, 735, etc.) will have Mercury in the same field.
        </p>
        <p>
         As the inclination of the ecliptic to the western horizon steepens over the spring months for Northern Hemisphere observers, opportunities to see the young crescent Moon for longer after sunset generally improve over the next few lunations. But for the next few months at least, January 21st offers your best bet at seeing a really young Moon from the British Isles  weather permitting, of course
        </p>
        <hr/>
        20th February at 6:15 pm  Venus close to Mars in conjunction with a 2-day-old Moon.
        <br/>
        21st March at 7:15 pm  Mars in conjunction with a 1.6-day-old Moon.
        <br/>
        19th April at 9:00 pm  Mars, Mercury and 1.2-day-old Moon (Moons altitude ~3).
