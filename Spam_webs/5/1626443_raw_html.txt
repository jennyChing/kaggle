     <p>
      <b>
       Subvocalization
      </b>
      , or silent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_communication" title="Speech communication">
       speech
      </a>
      , is defined as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_Monologue" title="Internal Monologue">
       internal speech
      </a>
      made when
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_(activity)" title="Reading (activity)">
       reading
      </a>
      a
      <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">
       word
      </a>
      , thus allowing the reader to imagine the
      <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">
       sound
      </a>
      of the word as it is read.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carter_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-carter-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is a natural process when reading and helps to reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_load" title="Cognitive load">
       cognitive load
      </a>
      , and it helps the
      <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">
       mind
      </a>
      to access meanings to enable it to comprehend and
      <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">
       remember
      </a>
      what is read.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rayner_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rayner-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although some people associate subvocalization with moving one's lips, the actual term refers primarily to the movement of muscles associated with speaking, not the literal moving of lips. Most subvocalization is undetectable (without the aid of machines) even by the person doing the subvocalizing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rayner_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rayner-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_speed_reading">
       Comparison to speed reading
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subvocalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Comparison to speed reading">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Advocates of
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_reading" title="Speed reading">
       speed reading
      </a>
      generally claim that subvocalization places extra burden on the cognitive resources, thus, slowing the reading down.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Speedreading courses often prescribe lengthy practices to eliminate subvocalizing when reading. Normal reading instructors often simply apply remedial teaching to a reader who subvocalizes to the degree that they make visible movements on the
      <a href="/wiki/Lip" title="Lip">
       lips
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jaw" title="Jaw">
       jaw
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Throat" title="Throat">
       throat
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcwhorter_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-mcwhorter-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      No evidence exists that normal non-observable subvocalizing will negatively affect any reading process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carter_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-carter-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At the more powerful rates (memorizing,
      <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">
       learning
      </a>
      , and reading for
      <a href="/wiki/Reading_comprehension" title="Reading comprehension">
       comprehension
      </a>
      ), subvocalizing by the reader is very detectable. At the less powerful, faster rates of reading, (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skimming_(reading)" title="Skimming (reading)">
       skimming
      </a>
      , and scanning) subvocalization is less detectable. For competent readers, subvocalizing to some extent even at scanning rates is normal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcwhorter_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-mcwhorter-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It may be impossible to totally and permanently eliminate subvocalization because people learn to read by associating the
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">
       sight
      </a>
      of words with their spoken sounds. Sound associations for words are indelibly imprinted on the
      <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">
       nervous system
      </a>
      even of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf" title="Deaf">
       deaf
      </a>
      people, since they will have associated the word with the mechanism for causing the sound or a sign in a particular
      <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
       sign language
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Subvocalizing is an inherent part of reading and understanding a word, and micro-muscle tests suggest that subvocalizing is impossible to permanently eliminate. At the more powerful
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_(activity)#Reading_rate" title="Reading (activity)">
       reading rates
      </a>
      (100-300 words per minute), subvocalizing can be used to improve comprehension.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rayner_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-rayner-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Subvocalizing or actual vocalizing can indeed be of great help when one wants to learn a passage verbatim. This is because the person is repeating the information in an auditory way, as well as seeing the piece on the paper.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Detection">
       Detection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Subvocalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Detection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Subvocal_recognition" title="Subvocal recognition">
       Subvocal recognition
      </a>
      involves monitoring actual movements of the
      <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
       tongue
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocal_cords" title="Vocal cords">
       vocal cords
      </a>
      that can be interpreted by
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">
       electromagnetic
      </a>
      sensors. Through the use of electrodes and
      <a href="/wiki/Nanocircuitry" title="Nanocircuitry">
       nanocircuitry
      </a>
      , synthetic telepathy can be achieved allowing people to communicate silently.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
