       <h4>
        ByJerome Burne
       </h4>
       <p>
        Its no secret that there are serious problems with the practice of scientific evidence based medicine (EBM). Its obviously a good idea to have a system for ensuring treatments are safe and effective. But as a defence against dangerous or poor drugs, the working of our current one makes the pre-crash banking regulation look rigorous.
       </p>
       <p>
        Even EBMs most energetic defenders are eloquent about its failings. Recently Lancet editor Richard Horton
        <a href="http://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(15)60696-1/fulltext?rss%3Dyes" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(15)60696-1/fulltext?rss%3Dyes', 'wrote an editorial']);" target="_blank">
         wrote an editorial
        </a>
        about a symposium held in London on the reproducibility and reliability of biomedical research.
       </p>
       <p>
        The case against science is straightforward, writes Horton much of the scientific literature, perhaps half, may simply be untrue. Afflicted by studies with small sample sizes, tiny effects, invalid exploratory analyses, and flagrant conflicts of interest  it has taken a turn towards darkness.
       </p>
       <p>
        But there is a more serious flaw. Even if tightened regulations could improve the testing, that does nothing to address another growing problem. More drugs are not the solution to the rise in chronic diseases thats threatening to bankrupt health services. However, demanding the same large randomised controlled trials (RCTs) used for drugs, to assess the benefits of lifestyle changes will simply reinforce the drug-driven status quo.

         <h1 class="su-post-title">
          Current Posts  19 June 2015
         </h1>
         <div class="su-post-meta">
          Posted: 10 Jul 2015 |
          <a class="su-post-comments-link" href="http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/07/10/current-posts-19-june-2015-2/#comments">
           0 comments
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="su-post-content">
          <p>
           Evidence based medicine (EBM), which is supposed to be a guarantee of safe and effective treatments, is proving more of a hindrance than a help to our health. A pioneering GP talks about his disenchantment with the follies of EBM and describes how encouraging life-style changes has made a huge difference to the health of his patients. EBM is too lax when dealing with dodgy drugs and ham fisted at assessing life-style changes.
           <a href="http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/07/10/how-evidence-based-medicine-is-failing-patients-what-needs-to-be-done-to-fix-it/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/07/10/how-evidence-based-medicine-is-failing-patients-what-needs-to-be-done-to-fix-it/', 'Read more']);" title="How evidence based medicine is failing patients. What needs to be done to fix it">
            Read more
           </a>
          </p>
          <div class="su-spoiler su-spoiler-style-default su-spoiler-icon-plus su-spoiler-closed">
           <div class="su-spoiler-title">
            <span class="su-spoiler-icon">
            </span>
            Read more...
           </div>
           <div class="su-spoiler-content su-clearfix" style="display:none">
            Statins can help fight cancer. This was the claim hailed as an important breakthrough by some last week and dismissed as over-hyped rubbish by others. So which is it? Here we have asked Godfrey Stoloff CEO of the ground-breaking Care Oncology Clinic in London
            <a href="http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/06/19/are-statins-the-new-cancer-killers-or-is-the-claim-overhyped-rubbish/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/06/19/are-statins-the-new-cancer-killers-or-is-the-claim-overhyped-rubbish/', 'to explain']);" title="Are statins the new cancer killers or is the claim overhyped rubbish?">
             to explain
            </a>
            how statin drugs are able to have an impact on cancer.
            <p>
             For years many cancer patients have felt hugely frustrated by their doctors lack of interest in any form of treatment that didnt fit the standard model. Now a pioneering clinic is offering an exciting third way that could break this log jam. In the process its
             <a href="http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/06/09/pioneering-clinic-points-to-new-directions-in-cancer-treatment/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/06/09/pioneering-clinic-points-to-new-directions-in-cancer-treatment/', 'highlighting some of the shortcomings']);">
              highlighting some of the shortcomings
             </a>
             of what is on offer at the moment.
            </p>
            <p>
             A deep and fiercely contested fault line runs down the middle of cancer treatment  SAM (standard authorized model) Vs. CAM (complementary and alternative medicine). Sophie Sabbage has been writing wisely and movingly about both the grim realities and the sweet moments of being a cancer patient.
             <a href="http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/05/26/caught-in-the-cross-fire-across-a-great-divide/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article-int', 'http://healthinsightuk.org/2015/05/26/caught-in-the-cross-fire-across-a-great-divide/', 'Here']);" title="Caught in the cross-fire across a great divide">
              Here
             </a>
             she makes plea for the warring sides in this running battle to abandon their polemic and build bridges to support their patients.
            </p>
           </div>
