     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Lost Gardens of Heligan
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">
       Cornish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="kw" xml:lang="kw">
       <i>
        <b>
         Lowarth Helygen
        </b>
       </i>
      </span>
      , meaning "willow tree garden"), near
      <a href="/wiki/Mevagissey" title="Mevagissey">
       Mevagissey
      </a>
      in Cornwall, are one of the most popular
      <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">
       botanical gardens
      </a>
      in the UK. The gardens are typical of the 19th century
      <a href="/wiki/Gardenesque" title="Gardenesque">
       Gardenesque
      </a>
      style with areas of different character and in different design styles.
     </p>
     <p>
      The gardens were created by members of the Cornish Tremayne family from the mid-18th century to the beginning of the 20th century, and still form part of the family's
      <a href="/wiki/Heligan_estate" title="Heligan estate">
       Heligan estate
      </a>
      . The gardens were neglected after the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">
       First World War
      </a>
      and restored only in the 1990s, a restoration that was the subject of several popular television programmes and books.
     </p>
     <p>
      The gardens include aged and colossal
      <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">
       rhododendrons
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Camellia" title="Camellia">
       camellias
      </a>
      , a series of lakes fed by a
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_ram" title="Hydraulic ram">
       ram pump
      </a>
      over 100 years old, highly productive flower and vegetable gardens, an Italian garden, and a wild area filled with subtropical
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_fern" title="Tree fern">
       tree ferns
      </a>
      called "The Jungle". The gardens also have Europe's only remaining
      <a href="/wiki/Pineapple_pit" title="Pineapple pit">
       pineapple pit
      </a>
      , warmed by rotting
      <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">
       manure
      </a>
      , and two figures made from rocks and plants known as the Mud Maid and the Giant's Head.
     </p>
     <p>
      The place name, properly pronounced 'h'LIG'n', and not the commonly heard 'HEL-i-gun', is derived from the Cornish word
      <i>
       helygen
      </i>
      , "willow tree".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
       Geography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lost_Gardens_of_Heligan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Lost Gardens of Heligan completely surround
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heligan_House&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heligan House (page does not exist)">
       Heligan House
      </a>
      and its private gardens. They lie some 1.5mi (2.4km) to the north-west of, and about 250ft (76m) above, the fishing village of
      <a href="/wiki/Mevagissey" title="Mevagissey">
       Mevagissey
      </a>
      . The gardens are 6mi (9.7km) by road from the town and railway station of
      <a href="/wiki/St_Austell" title="St Austell">
       St Austell
      </a>
      and are principally in the civil parish of
      <a href="/wiki/St_Ewe" title="St Ewe">
       St Ewe
      </a>
      , although elements of the eastern gardens are in Mevagissey parish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osl105_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-osl105-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The northern part of the gardens, which includes the main ornamental and vegetable gardens, are slightly higher than the house and slope gently down to it. The areas of the gardens to the west, south, and east of the house slope steeply down into a series of valleys that ultimately drain into the sea at Mevagissey. These areas are much wilder and include The Jungle and The Lost Valley.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osl105_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-osl105-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Heligan_estate" title="Heligan estate">
       Heligan estate
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Heligan estate was originally bought by the Tremaynes in the 16th century, and earlier members of the family were responsible for Heligan House and the (still private) gardens that immediately surround it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh113_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh113-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the more extensive gardens now open to the public were largely the result of the efforts of four successive squires of Heligan. These were:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh113_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh113-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh122_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh122-4">

         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

        Rev. Henry Hawkins Tremayne
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Hearle_Tremayne" title="John Hearle Tremayne">
        John Hearle Tremayne
       </a>
       , son of Henry Hawkins Tremayne
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Tremayne_(1825%E2%80%931901)" title="John Tremayne (18251901)">
        John Tremayne
       </a>
       , son of John Hearle Tremayne
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Claude_Lewis_Tremayne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Claude Lewis Tremayne (page does not exist)">
        John Claude Lewis Tremayne
       </a>
       , son of John Tremayne and better known as "Jack"
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Two estate plans, dating from 1777 and sometime before 1810, show the changes wrought to the Heligan estate during Henry Hawkins' ownership. The first plan shows a predominantly parkland estate, with the site of today's Northern Gardens occupied by a field. The second plan shows the development of shelter belts of trees surrounding the gardens, and the main shape of the Northern Gardens, the Mellon Yard and the Flower Garden are all readily discernible.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh96_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh96-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Henry Hawkins' descendants each made significant contributions to the development of the gardens, including the ornamental plantings along the estate's Long Drive, The Jungle, the
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">
       hybridising
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">
       rhododendrons
      </a>
      and their planting around Flora's Green, and the creation of the Italian Garden.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh122_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh122-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Before the First World War,the garden required the services of 22 gardeners to maintain it, but that war lead to the deaths of 16 of those gardeners, and by 1916,the garden was being looked after by only eight men. By the 1920s, Jack Tremayne's love of Italy, which had earlier inspired the Italian Garden, led him to set up permanent home there, and lease out Heligan. The house was tenanted for most of the 20th century, used by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Army" title="US Army">
       US Army
      </a>
      during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
       Second World War
      </a>
      , and then converted into flats and sold, without the gardens, in the 1970s. Against this background, the gardens fell into a serious state of neglect, and were lost to sight.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh113_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh113-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh122_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh122-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the childless death of Jack Tremayne, the Heligan estate came under the ownership of a trust to the benefit of several members of the extended Tremayne family. One of these, John Willis, lived in the area and was responsible for introducing record producer
      <a href="/wiki/Tim_Smit" title="Tim Smit">
       Tim Smit
      </a>
      to the gardens. A group of fellow enthusiasts and he decided to restore the garden to its former glory, and eventually leased them from the Tremayne family.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlgoh173_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tlgoh173-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The restoration, which was the subject of a six-part
      <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
       Channel 4
      </a>
      television series produced by Bamboo Productions and Cicada Films in 1996, proved to be an outstanding success, not only revitalising the gardens but also the local economy around Heligan by providing employment.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The gardens are now leased by a company owned by their restorers, who continue to cultivate them and operate them as a visitor attraction.
     </p>
