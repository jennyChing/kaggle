              <h2 class="heading ">
               Lacing to Prevent Heel Slippage
              </h2>

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p class="mrb">
              <span class="published">
               Updated May 26, 2015.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              Shoelacing techniques can help with shoe fit problems. Changing your lacing pattern will affect the fit of the shoe, so use caution after making a change and test it over shorter distances.
             </p>
             <p>
              To keep your heel in the heel cup of the shoe, lace your shoes to form a "lace lock" at the top of your shoe.
             </p>
             <p>
              This will allow you to tie the shoe tightly at the top without narrowing the fit for the rest of the shoe.
             </p>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Lace up from the bottom of the next to last eyelet.
              </li>
              <li>
               Then lace over and down through the top eyelet on the same side to form a "bunny ear."
              </li>
              <li>
               Do the same for the other side.
              </li>
              <li>
               Now lace through the opposite "bunny ear" you formed between those two eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Now when you tie your shoe, you can get a good tight fit at the ankle but keep it loose over the rest of the top of the foot.
              </li>
             </ul>

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p class="mrb">
              <span class="published">
               Updated May 26, 2015.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              If your shoe has two sets of eyelets on each side, lace through the ones furthest from the tongue. This will draw the two sides of the shoe together more snugly.
             </p>
             <p>
              If that is not enough, then use the "lace lock" as with the heel slippage, only between the second and third eyelet. This keeps the laces from loosening.
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Lace down through the next eyelet on the same side to form a "bunny ear."
              </li>
              <li>
               Do the same for the other side.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <ul class="cb-split">
              <li>
               Now lace through the opposite "bunny ear" you formed between those two eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Continue lacing diagonally. You will have formed a lace lock.
              </li>
             </ul>
             Changing your lacing pattern will affect the fit of the shoe, so use caution after making a change and test it over shorter distances.

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p class="mrb">
              <span class="published">
               Updated May 26, 2015.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              If the shoe has two sets of eyelets on each side, lace through the set closest to the tongue.
             </p>
             <p>
              For more room, use the over and under technique pictured. This will give space for expansion, and tightening the laces won't overly tighten the fit.
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               From the bottom, lace up through the first eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Cross the laces over and lace down through the second eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Don't cross over, lace up through the third set of eyelets.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <ul class="cb-split">
              <li>
               Cross over and lace down through the fourth set of eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Don't cross over, lace up through the fifth set of eyelets
              </li>
              <li>
               Cross over and lace up through the sixth set of eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Continue until you have used all of the eyelets you wish to use, then tie your bow.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Changing your lacing pattern will affect the fit of the shoe, so use caution after making a change and test it over shorter distances.
             </p>

              <h2 class="heading ">
               Lacing for a Narrow Heel and Wide Forefoot
              </h2>
             </div>
            </div>
            <figure class="content-img">
             <div class="sbs-img-frame">
              <div class="sbs-prev-next-top">

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p class="mrb">
              <span class="published">
               Updated May 26, 2015.
              </span>
             </p>
             <p>
              This foot type is always frustrated - if the heel fits, the forefoot is too narrow, if the forefoot fits, the heel slips around.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               Solution 1:
              </strong>
              Use two sets of laces. You should buy two shorter laces and lace the bottom 3 eyelets with one lace and the top eyelets with another lace. Then you will be able to adjust the width and tension for each set of laces. The drawback is that you have two chances now of having a "blowout" on each foot.
             </p>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <p class="cb-split">
              You can probably do a permanent knot for the lower set of laces, or use Ian's Knot or a lacekeeper solution to keep them tied:
              <br/>
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/shoecare/tp/lacelock.htm">
               11 Ways to Keep Your Shoelaces Tied
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               Solution 2:
              </strong>
              Use a combination of the
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/shoes/ss/shoelacingtofit_3.htm">
               wide forefoot over-and-under technique
              </a>
              and the
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="3" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/shoes/ss/shoelacingtofit.htm">
               narrow heel lace lock technique
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               From the bottom, lace up through the first eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Cross the laces over and lace down through the second eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Don't cross over, lace up through the third set of eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Cross over and lace down through the fourth set of eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Don't cross over, lace up through the fifth set of eyelets
              </li>
              <li>
               Cross over and lace up through the sixth set of eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Don't cross over, lace down through the seventh set of eyelets, forming a "bunny ear"
              </li>
              <li>
               Now lace through the opposite "bunny ear" you formed between those two eyelets.
              </li>
              <li>
               Now when you tie your shoe, you can get a good tight fit at the ankle but keep it loose over the rest of the top of the foot.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Changing your lacing pattern will affect the fit of the shoe, so use caution after making a change and test it over shorter distances.
             </p>
             <p>
              Sources:
             </p>

               Lacing Techniques for Proper Shoe Fit
              </a>
              .
              <br/>
              2. New Balance Shoes: Product Insert - Lacing for Better Fit.
             </p>
