          <p class="bylineBody">
           <span itemprop="author">
            by Christopher Middleton, Video by Erica Elkhershi
           </span>
          </p>

         <p class="publishedDate">
          10:15AM GMT 02 Dec 2011

         <meta content="2011-12-02" itemprop="datePublished"/>
         <p class="comments">
          <img alt="Comments" src="/template/ver1-0/i/share/comments.gif"/>
          <a dsqid="8930302" href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/finance/property/seasonal/8930302/Let-it-glow-Paul-Tooles-Christmas-lights.html#disqus_thread">

        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           Drive through any town during December, and we British are decking our halls not just with boughs of holly but with giant, luminous Santas. Our roofs are transformed into parking spaces for glowing, gift-laden reindeer and cavorting penguins.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           Its been big business in America for years, but now the trend has gone Transatlantic. And when it comes to creating a full-scale glowbiz production, no one can hold a candle to Paul Toole. The 36-year-old first went down the home-illuminating path some 13 Christmases ago. This year, its not just going to be his house thats up in lights, but his name. Hes starring in a new Channel 4 documentary entitled The King of Christmas Lights.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           The programme shows how he transforms an everyday, three-bedroom Somerset bungalow into a place of pilgrimage for the whole county. Some nights, there will be anything from 100-150 cars parked by the roadside.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           For 11 months of the year, Manning Close is just a quiet cul-de-sac on the outskirts of Wells, home to entertainer and events organiser Paul (karaoke nights a speciality), and his mum Rosemary.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           Come the start of December, though, the street changes beyond recognition. This isnt just a few twinkling stars and a random polar bear on the roof. Pauls show is a 20-minute, computer-controlled spectacular, featuring 40,000 twinkling bulbs.
          </p>
         </div>

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           There is a specially composed symphony in which music and visuals operate in intricate synchronicity. We do something different each year, says Paul, who starts work on the show several months in advance. This year, we have a Ferris wheel, giant luminous baubles and illuminations that look like fireworks. There is also a 20ft-tall tree that is made of solid lights from top to bottom.
          </p>
          <p>
           Well be covering the entire garden in artificial snow, and adding lots of polar bears and reindeer. Through the middle of it all will be a flowing river of blue and white lights.
          </p>
          <p>
           While Paul may be the King of Christmas Lights, there are plenty of other British homeowners after his crown.
          </p>
          <p>
           From the middle of November to the middle of December, its just manic with the amount of orders we receive, says Charlie Cameron, boss of online Christmas lights suppliers Xmas Direct (xmasdirect.co.uk).
          </p>
          <p>
           Covering your house with Christmas lights can cost around 2,000. Its unbelievable what people are prepared to spend. Yet there are plenty wanting to do it.
          </p>
          <p>
           Each year, weve seen an increase in turnover of around 10 per cent. We started out quite small in 2000, but now I go out twice a year to China, to buy lights from our manufacturers in Hong Kong and the Guangdong province. Every September, they ship two container-loads of illuminations over to us in the UK.
          </p>
          <p>
           As well as getting bigger, the Christmas displays are also getting more sophisticated, too, as Paul explains.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its not just a lot of flashing lights, its actually very carefully choreographed, he says.
          </p>
          <p>
           At Manning Close, I put on a Christmas concert. It even looks like Santa is standing in my bedroom window, conducting.
          </p>
          <p>
           Most of the audience like to gather outside in front of the house and watch, but if its cold, people can sit in their cars, tune to 89.2FM, and hear the music on their car radios. We dont broadcast all over Somerset, just within a 40ft radius of the house, because thats as far as the signal will reach.
          </p>
          <p>
           In America this kind of thing has been huge for years. I first got the idea of putting on a show when I visited the States in 1998.
          </p>
          <p>
           Come January, they were selling off what they call icicle lights, but we call fairy lights, for about 50 cents each. I bought a thousand on the spot, and havent looked back.
          </p>
          <p>
           Some years, I used to go out to the States to replenish my stock, and would end up with so many that Id buy an extra air ticket home, just to boost the amount of luggage I could bring back.
          </p>
          <p>
           I honestly dont know how much Ive spent. In the last few years alone, its been 15,000.
          </p>
          <p>
           Christmas spirit, certainly. But why does he do it?
          </p>
          <p>
           A lot of people ask me that, he says. I think it was because my mum and dad always gave me such a brilliant Christmas, so I wanted to share that with other people.
          </p>
          <p>
           Mums very supportive of me. She does the switching-on and off of the lights, if I cant get home in time.
          </p>
          <p>
           Not everyone thinks the same way as we do. There are some people who dont like my lights, which I think is a bit sad. I mean, it would be different if they were cheap and tacky, like some Christmas displays, but I think the show I put on is really high quality.
          </p>
          <p>
           Hes starting to win around some of the other residents in Manning Close. While the lights were very much a solo venture for the first few years, the number of participating houses in 2011 has risen from three last year  to eight this time round.
          </p>
          <p>
           I try to make it as easy as possible for everyone. I supply the lights, and all they have to do is supply the electricity, says Paul. The lights are LEDs, so they use 90 per cent less juice than traditional bulbs. We run the lights for four and a half hours each night. Yet my electricity bill is still only 12-14 higher than in a normal month.
          </p>
          <p>
           This is a figure which is hugely outweighed by the size of the charity donations made by Pauls visitors. Each year, they drop some 2,000 into his collection box. This years good cause is Make-A-Wish (make-a-wish.org.uk), which helps grant wishes for children with serious illnesses.
          </p>
          <p>
           And no sooner has Paul switched off the lights (on Twelfth Night, naturally), than he will be planning next years event, and buying in stock.
          </p>
          <p>
           Every January, I pick up new ideas at the Christmas and Gift Fair in Harrogate, and the Lighting Show at the Birmingham NEC. I buy in such bulk now that I get trade discounts from Premier Decorations (there are showrooms in Ruislip and Wrexham), who are my main suppliers.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pauls show is so famous that he gets celebrities to perform the initial turning-on ceremony.
          </p>
          <p>
           It helps that I have a friend who owns a Rolls-Royce Phantom and a Los Angeles Police Department squad car, he explains, laughing.
          </p>
          <p>
           So I can offer the celebrities a ride to and from my home.
          </p>
          <p>
           Last year, I got Martin Roberts, from the BBC One programme Homes Under the Hammer. This year it was going to be Keith Chegwin, but he hurt himself ice dancing, so his place was taken by the actor John Challis (Boycie, from Only Fools and Horses, and spin-off show The Green Green Grass).
          </p>
          <p>
           On top of which, the Grammy-nominated American singer Judy Pancoast has agreed to perform her hit The House On Christmas
          </p>
          <p>
           Street outside my bungalow.
          </p>
          <p>
           If its stars you are looking for this Christmas, you dont need to look to the sky. Just set your satnav for Number 18 Manning Close.
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>

           <li>
            Displays run nightly at Paul Tooles house at Manning Close, Wells, Somerset BA5 2XJ, from 5-9.30pm up until January 5. For more details and information, visit
            <a href="http://www. paultoole.org">

                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {

                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {

                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);

                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;

                        return true;
                    }
                });
