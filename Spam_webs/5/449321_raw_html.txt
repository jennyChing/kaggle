     <p>
      The
      <b>
       history of copyright law
      </b>
      starts with early privileges and monopolies granted to
      <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
       printers
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Books" title="Books">
       books
      </a>
      . The British
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
      1710, full title "An Act for the Encouragement of Learning, by vesting the Copies of Printed Books in the Authors or purchasers of such Copies, during the Times therein mentioned", was the first
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">
       copyright
      </a>
      statute. Initially copyright law only applied to the copying of books. Over time other uses such as translations and
      <a href="/wiki/Derivative_work" title="Derivative work">
       derivative works
      </a>
      were made subject to copyright and copyright now covers a wide range of works, including
      <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">
       maps
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Performance" title="Performance">
       performances
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">
       paintings
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
       photographs
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_recording" title="Sound recording">
       sound recordings
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
       motion pictures
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">
       computer programs
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Today national copyright laws have been standardised to some extent through international and regional agreements such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Literary_and_Artistic_Works" title="Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works">
       Berne Convention
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_European_Union" title="Copyright law of the European Union">
       European copyright directives
      </a>
      . Although there are consistencies among nations' copyright laws, each jurisdiction has separate and distinct laws and regulations about copyright. Some jurisdictions also recognize
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_rights_(copyright_law)" title="Moral rights (copyright law)">
       moral rights
      </a>
      of creators, such as the right to be credited for the work.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">
       Copyright
      </a>
      are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclusive_rights" title="Exclusive rights">
       exclusive rights
      </a>
      granted to the author or creator of an original work, including the right to copy, distribute and adapt the work. Copyright does not protect ideas, only their expression or fixation. In most jurisdictions copyright arises upon fixation and does not need to be registered. Copyright owners have the exclusive statutory right to exercise control over copying and other exploitation of the works for a specific period of time, after which the work is said to enter the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      . Uses which are covered under
      <a href="/wiki/Limitations_and_exceptions_to_copyright" title="Limitations and exceptions to copyright">
       limitations and exceptions to copyright
      </a>
      , such as
      <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">
       fair use
      </a>
      , do not require permission from the copyright owner. All other uses require permission and copyright owners can license or permanently transfer or assign their exclusive rights to others.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_developments">
       Early developments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early developments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The earliest recorded historical case-law on the right to copy comes from ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
       Ireland
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Cathach_of_St._Columba" title="Cathach of St. Columba">
       Cathach
      </a>
      is the oldest extant Irish manuscript of the
      <a href="/wiki/Psalter" title="Psalter">
       Psalter
      </a>
      and the earliest example of Irish writing. It contains a
      <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">
       Vulgate
      </a>
      version of
      <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">
       Psalms
      </a>
      XXX (30) to CV (105) with an interpretative rubric or heading before each psalm. It is traditionally ascribed to
      <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">
       Saint Columba
      </a>
      as the copy, made at night in haste by a miraculous light, of a Psalter lent to
      <a href="/wiki/Columba" title="Columba">
       Columba
      </a>
      by St. Finnian. A dispute arose about the ownership of the copy and King
      <a href="/wiki/Diarmait_mac_Cerbaill" title="Diarmait mac Cerbaill">
       Diarmait Mac Cerbhaill
      </a>
      gave the judgement "To every cow belongs her calf, therefore to every book belongs its copy."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_C%C3%BAl_Dreimhne" title="Battle of Cl Dreimhne">
       Battle of Cl Dreimhne
      </a>
      was fought over this issue.
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern copyright law has been influenced by an array of older legal rights that have been recognized throughout history, including the moral rights of the author who created a work, the economic rights of a benefactor who paid to have a copy made, the property rights of the individual owner of a copy, and a sovereign's right to censor and to regulate the printing industry. The origins of some of these rights can be traced back to ancient Greek culture, ancient Jewish law, and ancient Roman law.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Greek society, during the sixth century B.C.E., there emerged the notion of the individual self, including personal ideals, ambition, and creativity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The individual self is important in copyright because it distinguishes the creativity produced by an individual from the rest of society. In ancient Jewish Talmudic law there can be found recognition of the moral rights of the author and the economic or property rights of an author.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Prior to the invention of
      <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">
       movable type
      </a>
      in the West in the mid-15th century, texts were copied by hand and the small number of texts generated few occasions for these rights to be tested. During the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
       Roman Empire
      </a>
      , a period of prosperous book trade, no copyright or similar regulations existed,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martial.2C_1978_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Martial.2C_1978-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      copying by those other than professional booksellers was rare. This is because books were, typically, copied by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literate_slave" title="Literate slave">
       literate slaves
      </a>
      , who were expensive to buy and maintain. Thus, any copier would have had to pay much the same expense as a professional publisher. Roman book sellers would sometimes pay a well regarded author for first access to a text for copying, but they had no exclusive rights to a work and authors were not normally paid anything for their work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martial.2C_1978_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Martial.2C_1978-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_privileges_and_monopolies">
       Early privileges and monopolies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early privileges and monopolies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1170" data-file-width="750" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg/150px-Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg/225px-Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg/300px-Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum_1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Title page of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum" title="Index Librorum Prohibitorum">
          Index Librorum Prohibitorum
         </a>
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         List of Prohibited Books
        </i>
        , (Venice 1564).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The origin of copyright law in most European countries lies in efforts by the church and governments to regulate and control the output of printers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_31_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_31-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Before the invention of the
      <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">
       printing press
      </a>
      , a writing, once created, could only be physically multiplied by the highly laborious and error-prone process of manual copying by scribes. An elaborate system of censorship and control over
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scribes" title="Scribes">
       scribes
      </a>
      did not exist, as scribes were scattered and worked on single manuscripts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Sola_Pool_1983_14_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-de_Sola_Pool_1983_14-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Printing allowed for multiple exact copies of a work, leading to a more rapid and widespread circulation of ideas and information (see
      <a href="/wiki/Print_culture" title="Print culture">
       print culture
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_31_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_31-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">
       Pope Alexander VI
      </a>
      issued a
      <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">
       bull
      </a>
      in 1501 against the unlicensed printing of books and in 1559 the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_Expurgatorius" title="Index Expurgatorius">
       Index Expurgatorius
      </a>
      , or
      <i>
       List of Prohibited Books
      </i>
      , was issued for the first time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Sola_Pool_1983_14_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-de_Sola_Pool_1983_14-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Europe
      <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
       printing
      </a>
      was invented and widely established in the 15th and 16th centuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_31_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_31-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While governments and church encouraged printing in many ways, which allowed the dissemination of
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bibles
      </a>
      and government information, works of dissent and criticism could also circulate rapidly. As a consequence, governments established controls over printers across Europe, requiring them to have official licences to trade and produce books. The licenses typically gave printers the exclusive right to print particular works for a fixed period of years, and enabled the printer to prevent others from printing the same work during that period. The licenses could only grant rights to print in the territory of the state that had granted them, but they did usually prohibit the import of foreign printing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_31_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_31-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
       republic of Venice
      </a>
      granted its first privilege for a particular book in 1486. It was a special case, being the history of the city itself, the 'Rerum venetarum ab urbe condita opus' of
      <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Antonius_Coccius_Sabellicus" title="Marcus Antonius Coccius Sabellicus">
       Marcus Antonius Coccius Sabellicus
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From 1492 onwards Venice began to regularly granting privileges for books.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Republic of Venice, the dukes of
      <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
       Florence
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">
       Leo X
      </a>
      and other Popes conceded at different times to certain printers the exclusive privilege of printing for specific terms (rarely exceeding 14 years) editions of classic authors.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first copyright privilege in
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      bears date 1518 and was issued to
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pynson" title="Richard Pynson">
       Richard Pynson
      </a>
      , King's Printer, the successor to
      <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">
       William Caxton
      </a>
      . The privilege gives a monopoly for the term of two years. The date is 15 years later than that of the first privilege issued in
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      . Early copyright privileges were called
      <a href="/wiki/Government-granted_monopoly" title="Government-granted monopoly">
       "monopolies
      </a>
      ," particularly during the reign of
      <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">
       Queen Elizabeth
      </a>
      , who frequently gave grants of monopolies in articles of common use, such as salt, leather, coal, soap, cards, beer, and wine. The practice was continued until the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statute_of_Monopolies_1623" title="Statute of Monopolies 1623">
       Statute of Monopolies
      </a>
      was enacted in 1623, ending most monopolies, with certain exceptions, such as patents; after 1623, grants of
      <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">
       Letters patent
      </a>
      to publishers became common.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The earliest German privilege of which there is trustworthy record was issued in 1501 by the
      <a href="/wiki/Aulic_Council" title="Aulic Council">
       Aulic Council
      </a>
      to an association entitled the
      <i>
       Sodalitas Rhenana Celtica
      </i>
      , for the publication of an edition of the dramas of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hrosvit" title="Hrosvit">
       Hroswitha of Gandersheim
      </a>
      , which had been prepared for the press by
      <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Celtes" title="Conrad Celtes">
       Conrad Celtes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1512 an Imperial privilege was issued to the historiographer
      <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Stadius" title="Johannes Stadius">
       Johannes Stadius
      </a>
      for all that he should print, the first European privilege which was made to cover more than a single work, or undertaking to protect books not yet published. In 1794 legislation was enacted in the Prussian Parliament which was accepted by the other states of
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      (except
      <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Wrttemberg">
       Wrttemberg
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">
       Mecklenburg
      </a>
      ), under which all German authors, and foreign authors whose works were represented by publishers taking part in the book fairs in
      <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">
       Frankfurt
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">
       Leipzig
      </a>
      , were to be protected throughout the states of Germany against unauthorized reprints.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      According to historian
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eckhard_H%C3%B6ffner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eckhard Hffner (page does not exist)">
       Eckhard Hffner
      </a>
      indicated that there was no effective copyright legislation in Germany in the early 19th century leading to a proliferation of publishing as well as increased revenue for authors (compared to England).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-eh-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">
       Prussia
      </a>
      introduced a copyright law in 1837, but even then authors and publishers just had to go to another German state to circumvent its ruling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eh_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-eh-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He compared the English system where copyright ruled to the free-wheeling German situation and argues that the availability of books at low prices fostered a large educated readership in Germany laying the foundation of its industrial ascendency later in the 19th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thad_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-thad-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png/150px-Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png/225px-Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png/300px-Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Areopagitica_1644bw_gobeirne.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        First page of
        <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">
         John Milton
        </a>
        's 1644 edition of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Areopagitica" title="Areopagitica">
          Areopagitica
         </a>
        </i>
        , in it he argued forcefully against the
        <a href="/wiki/Licensing_Order_of_1643" title="Licensing Order of 1643">
         Licensing Order of 1643
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      As the "menace" of printing spread, governments established centralized control mechanisms,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Sola_Pool_1983_15_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-de_Sola_Pool_1983_15-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and in 1557 the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Crown" title="English Crown">
       English Crown
      </a>
      thought to stem the flow of seditious and heretical books by chartering the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationers%27_Company" title="Stationers' Company">
       Stationers' Company
      </a>
      . The right to print was limited to the members of that guild, and thirty years later the
      <a href="/wiki/Star_Chamber" title="Star Chamber">
       Star Chamber
      </a>
      was chartered to curtail the "greate enormities and abuses" of "dyvers contentyous and disorderlye persons professinge the arte or mystere of pryntinge or selling of books." The right to print was restricted to two universities and to the 21 existing printers in the
      <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">
       city of London
      </a>
      , which had 53
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Printing_presses" title="Printing presses">
       printing presses
      </a>
      . The French crown also repressed printing, and printer
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etienne_Dolet" title="Etienne Dolet">
       Etienne Dolet
      </a>
      was burned at the stake in 1546. As the English took control of type founding in 1637, printers fled to the Netherlands. Confrontation with authority made printers radical and rebellious, and 800 authors, printers and book dealers were incarcerated in the
      <a href="/wiki/Bastille" title="Bastille">
       Bastille
      </a>
      before it was stormed in 1789.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Sola_Pool_1983_15_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-de_Sola_Pool_1983_15-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The notion that the expression of dissent or subversive views should be tolerated, not censured or punished by law, developed alongside the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
       printing
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/News_media" title="News media">
       press
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Areopagitica" title="Areopagitica">
       Areopagitica
      </a>
      , published in 1644 under the full title
      <i>
       Areopagitica: A speech of Mr. John Milton for the liberty of unlicensed printing to the Parliament of England
      </i>
      , was
      <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">
       John Milton
      </a>
      's response to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_parliament" title="English parliament">
       English parliament
      </a>
      re-introducing government licensing of
      <a href="/wiki/Printer_(publishing)" title="Printer (publishing)">
       printers
      </a>
      , hence
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publishers" title="Publishers">
       publishers
      </a>
      . In doing so Milton articulated the main strands of future discussions about
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_of_expression" title="Freedom of expression">
       freedom of expression
      </a>
      . By defining the scope of freedom of expression and of "harmful" speech Milton argued against the principle of pre-censorship and in favour of tolerance for a wide range of views.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_British_copyright_law">
       Early British copyright law
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early British copyright law">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Statute_of_anne.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="971" data-file-width="584" height="416" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Statute_of_anne.jpg/250px-Statute_of_anne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Statute_of_anne.jpg/375px-Statute_of_anne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Statute_of_anne.jpg/500px-Statute_of_anne.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Statute_of_anne.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
         Statute of Anne
        </a>
        came into force in 1710
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In England the printers, known as stationers, formed a collective organisation, known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationers%27_Company" title="Stationers' Company">
       Stationers' Company
      </a>
      . In the 16th century the Stationers' Company was given the power to require all lawfully printed books to be entered into its register. Only members of the Stationers' Company could enter books into the register. This meant that the Stationers' Company achieved a dominant position over publishing in 17th century England (no equivalent arrangement formed in Scotland and Ireland). But the monopoly came to an end in 1694, when the English Parliament did not renew the Stationers Company's power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_31_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_31-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1707 the parliaments of England and Scotland were united as a result of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Scottish_Union" title="Anglo-Scottish Union">
       Anglo-Scottish Union
      </a>
      . The new parliament was able to change the laws in both countries and an important early piece of legislation was the Copyright Act of 1709, also known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
      , after
      <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">
       Queen Anne
      </a>
      . The act came into force in 1710 and was the first copyright statute. Its full title was "An Act for the Encouragement of Learning, by vesting the Copies of Printed Books in the Authors or purchasers of such Copies, during the Times therein mentioned".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_31_6-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_31-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The enforcement of the Statute of Anne in April of 1710 marked a historic moment in the development of copyright law. As the world's first copyright statute it granted publishers of a book legal protection of 14 years with the commencement of the statute. It also granted 21 years of protection for any book already in print.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Statute of Anne had a much broader social focus and remit than the monopoly granted to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationers%27_Company" title="Stationers' Company">
       Stationers' Company
      </a>
      . The statute was concerned with the reading public, the continued production of useful literature, and the advancement and spread of education. The central plank of the statute is a social
      <a href="/wiki/Quid_pro_quo" title="Quid pro quo">
       quid pro quo
      </a>
      ; to encourage "learned men to compose and write useful books" the statute guaranteed the finite right to print and reprint those works. It established a pragmatic bargain involving authors, the booksellers and the public.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Statute of Anne ended the old system whereby only literature that met the
      <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">
       censorship
      </a>
      standards administered by the booksellers could appear in print. The statute furthermore created a
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      for literature, as previously all literature belonged to the booksellers forever.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jonathan_1997_34_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jonathan_1997_34-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Patterson and Lindberg, the Statute of Anne:
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "...transformed the stationers' copyright - which had been used as a device of monopoly and an instrument of censorship - into a trade-regulation concept to promote learning and to curtail the monopoly of publishers... The features of the Statute of Anne that justify the epithet of trade regulation included the limited term of copyright, the availability of copyright to anyone, and the price-control provisions. Copyright, rather than being perpetual, was now limited to a term of fourteen years, with a like renewal term being available only to the author (and only if the author were living at the end of the first term)."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jonathan_1997_34_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jonathan_1997_34-18">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         18
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      When the statutory
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_term" title="Copyright term">
       copyright term
      </a>
      provided for by the Statute of Anne began to expire in 1731 London booksellers thought to defend their dominant position by seeking
      <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">
       injunctions
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">
       Court of Chancery
      </a>
      for works by authors that fell outside the statute's protection. At the same time the London booksellers lobbied parliament to extend the copyright term provided by the Statute of Anne. Eventually, in a case known as
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Midwinter_v._Hamilton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Midwinter v. Hamilton (page does not exist)">
        Midwinter v. Hamilton
       </a>
      </i>
      (17431748), the London booksellers turned to
      <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">
       common law
      </a>
      and started a 30-year period known as the
      <i>
       battle of the booksellers
      </i>
      . The battle of the booksellers saw London booksellers locking horns with the newly emerging Scottish book trade over the right to reprint works falling outside the protection of the Statute of Anne. The Scottish booksellers argued that no
      <a href="/wiki/Common_law_copyright" title="Common law copyright">
       common law copyright
      </a>
      existed in an author's work. The London booksellers argued that the Statute of Anne only supplemented and supported a pre-existing common law copyright. The dispute was argued out in a number of notable cases, including
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Millar_v._Kincaid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Millar v. Kincaid (page does not exist)">
        Millar v. Kincaid
       </a>
      </i>
      (17491751) and
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tonson_v._Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tonson v. Collins (page does not exist)">
        Tonson v. Collins
       </a>
      </i>
      (17611762).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Common_law_copyright">
       Common law copyright
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Common law copyright">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A debate raged on whether printed ideas could be owned and London booksellers and other supporters of perpetual copyright argued that without it scholarship would cease to exist and that authors would have no incentive to continue creating works of enduring value if they could not
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bequeath" title="Bequeath">
       bequeath
      </a>
      the property rights to their descendants. Opponents of perpetual copyright argued that it amounted to a
      <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">
       monopoly
      </a>
      , which inflated the price of books, making them less affordable and therefore prevented the spread of the
      <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">
       Enlightenment
      </a>
      . London booksellers were attacked for using rights of authors to mask their greed and self-interest in controlling the book trade.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Donaldson_v_Beckett" title="Donaldson v Beckett">
        Donaldson v Beckett
       </a>
      </i>
      reached the
      <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">
       House of Lords
      </a>
      in 1774
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Camden" title="Lord Camden">
       Lord Camden
      </a>
      was most strident in his rejection of the common law copyright, warning the Lords that should they vote in favour of common law copyright, effectively a
      <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_copyright" title="Perpetual copyright">
       perpetual copyright
      </a>
      , "all our learning will be locked up in the hands of the
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Tonson" title="Jacob Tonson">
       Tonsons
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Barnaby_Bernard_Lintot" title="Barnaby Bernard Lintot">
       Lintots
      </a>
      of the age". Moreover he warned that booksellers would then set upon books whatever price they pleased "till the public became as much their slaves, as their own hackney compilers are". He declared that "Knowledge and science are not things to be bound in such cobweb chains."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In its ruling the House of Lords established that copyright was a "creature of statute", and that the rights and responsibilities in copyright were determined by legislation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is however still disagreement over whether the House of Lords affirmed the existence of common law copyright before it was superseded by the Statute of Anne. The Lords had traditionally been hostile to the booksellers' monopoly and were aware of how the doctrine of
      <a href="/wiki/Common_law_copyright" title="Common law copyright">
       common law copyright
      </a>
      , promoted by the booksellers, was used to support their case for a
      <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_copyright" title="Perpetual copyright">
       perpetual copyright
      </a>
      . The Lords clearly voted against perpetual copyright
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and by confirming that the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_term" title="Copyright term">
       copyright term
      </a>
      , that is the length of time of work is in copyright, did expire according to statute the Lords also affirmed the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Donaldson_v_Beckett" title="Donaldson v Beckett">
        Donaldson v Beckett
       </a>
      </i>
      ruling confirmed that a large number of works and books first published in Britain were in the public domain, either because the copyright term granted by statute had expired, or because they were first published before the
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
      was enacted in 1709. This opened the market for cheap reprints of works from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
       Shakespeare
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">
       John Milton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">
       Geoffrey Chaucer
      </a>
      , works now considered classics. The expansion of the public domain in books broke the dominance of the London booksellers and allowed for competition, with the number of London booksellers and publishers rising threefold from 111 to 308 between 1772 and 1802.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Eventually an understanding was established whereby authors had a pre-existing common law copyright over their work, but that with the Statute of Anne parliament had limited these natural rights in order to strike a more appropriate balance between the interests of the author and the wider social good.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Patterson and Livingston there remains confusion about the nature of copyright ever since. Copyright has come to be viewed both as a natural law right of the author and as the statutory grant of a limited
      <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">
       monopoly
      </a>
      . One theory holds that copyright's origin occurs at the creation of a work, the other that its origin exists only through the copyright statute.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_French_copyright_law">
       Early French copyright law
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early French copyright law">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_copyright_law" title="French copyright law">
       French copyright law
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In pre-
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       revolutionary
      </a>
      France all books needed to be approved by official censors and authors and publishers had to obtain a royal privilege before a book could be published. Royal privileges were exclusive and usually granted for six years, with the possibility of renewal. Over time it was established that the owner of a royal privilege has the sole right to obtain a renewal indefinitely. In 1761 the Royal Council awarded a royal privilege to the heirs of an author rather than the author's publisher, sparking a national debate on the nature of literary property similar to that taking place in Britain during the battle of the booksellers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1777 a series of royal decrees reformed the royal privileges. The duration of privileges were set at a minimum duration of 10 years or the life of the author, which ever was longer. If the author obtained a privilege and did not transfer or sell it on, he could publish and sell copies of the book himself, and pass the privilege on to his heirs, who enjoyed an exclusive right into perpetuity. If the privilege was sold to a publisher, the exclusive right would only last the specified duration. The royal decrees prohibited the renewal of privileges and once the privilege had expired anyone could obtain a "permission simple" to print or sell copies of the work. Hence the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      in books whose privilege had expired was expressly recognised.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the
      <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
       French Revolution
      </a>
      a dispute over
      <a href="/wiki/Com%C3%A9die-Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Comdie-Franaise">
       Comdie-Franaise
      </a>
      being granted the exclusive right to the public performance of all dramatic works erupted and in 1791 the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(French_Revolution)" title="National Assembly (French Revolution)">
       National Assembly
      </a>
      abolished the privilege. Anyone was allowed to establish a public theatre and the National Assembly declared that the works of any author who had died more than five years ago were
      <a href="/wiki/Public_property" title="Public property">
       public property
      </a>
      . In the same degree the National Assembly granted authors the exclusive right to authorise the public performance of their works during their lifetime, and extended that right to the authors' heirs and assignees for five years after the author's death. The National Assembly took the view that a published work was by its nature a public property, and that an author's rights are recognised as an exception to this principle, to compensate an author for his work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142_28-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1793 a new law was passed giving authors, composers, and artists the exclusive right to sell and distribute their works, and the right was extended to their heirs and assigns for 10 years after the author's death. The National Assembly placed this law firmly on a natural right footing, calling the law the "Declaration of the Rights of Genius" and so evoking the famous
      <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen" title="Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen">
       Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen
      </a>
      . However, author's rights were subject to the condition of making depositing copies of the work with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_Nationale" title="Bibliothque Nationale">
       Bibliothque Nationale
      </a>
      and 19th Century commentators characterised the 1793 law as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilitarian" title="Utilitarian">
       utilitarian
      </a>
      and "a charitable grant from society".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142_28-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_141.E2.80.93142-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_United_States_copyright_law">
       Early United States copyright law
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early United States copyright law">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_States" title="Copyright law of the United States">
       Copyright law of the United States
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1316" data-file-width="864" height="381" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg/250px-Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg/375px-Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg/500px-Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Copyright_Act_of_1790_in_Colombian_Centinel.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Copyright Act of 1790 in the Columbian Centinel
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Copyright_term.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="625" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Copyright_term.svg/300px-Copyright_term.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Copyright_term.svg/450px-Copyright_term.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Copyright_term.svg/600px-Copyright_term.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Copyright_term.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Expansion of U.S. copyright law (Assuming authors create their works at age 35 and live for seventy years)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
      did not apply to the American colonies. The economy of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_(1776%E2%80%931789)" title="History of the United States (17761789)">
       early America
      </a>
      was largely
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrarian_economy" title="Agrarian economy">
       agrarian
      </a>
      and only three private copyright acts being passed in America prior to 1783. Two of the acts were limited to seven years, the other was limited to a term of five years. In 1783 several authors' petitions persuaded the
      <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">
       Continental Congress
      </a>
      "that nothing is more properly a man's own than the fruit of his study, and that the protection and security of literary property would greatly tend to encourage genius and to promote useful discoveries." But under the
      <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">
       Articles of Confederation
      </a>
      , the Continental Congress had no authority to enact copyright law. The Continental Congress passed a
      <a href="/wiki/Resolution_(law)" title="Resolution (law)">
       resolution
      </a>
      urging the States to "secure to the authors or publishers of any new book not hitherto printed... the copy right of such books for a certain time not less than fourteen years from the first publication; and to secure to the said authors, if they shall survive the term first mentioned,... the copy right of such books for another term of time no less than fourteen years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Three states had already enacted copyright statutes in 1783 prior to the Continental Congress resolution, and in the subsequent three years all of the remaining states except
      <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">
       Delaware
      </a>
      passed a copyright statute. Seven of the States followed the
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
      and the Continental Congress' resolution by providing two fourteen-year terms. The five remaining States granted copyright for single terms of fourteen, twenty and twenty one years, with no right of renewal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_143_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_143-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the
      <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_(United_States)" title="Constitutional Convention (United States)">
       Philadelphia Convention
      </a>
      in 1787, both
      <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">
       James Madison
      </a>
      of Virginia and
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cotesworth_Pinckney" title="Charles Cotesworth Pinckney">
       Charles Cotesworth Pinckney
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">
       South Carolina
      </a>
      submitted proposals that would allow Congress the power to grant copyright for a limited time. These proposals are the origin of the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Clause" title="Copyright Clause">
       Copyright Clause
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">
       United States Constitution
      </a>
      , which allows the granting of copyright and
      <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">
       patents
      </a>
      for a limited time to serve a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilitarian" title="Utilitarian">
       utilitarian
      </a>
      function, namely "to promote the progress of science and useful arts". The first federal copyright act was the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Act_of_1790" title="Copyright Act of 1790">
       Copyright Act of 1790
      </a>
      . It granted copyright for a term of 14 years "from the time of recording the title thereof" with a right of renewal for another 14 years if the author survived to the end of the first term. The act covered not only books, but also maps and charts. With exception of the provision on maps and charts the Copyright Act of 1790 is copied almost verbatim from the
      <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Anne" title="Statute of Anne">
       Statute of Anne
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_143_30-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_143-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the time works only received protection under federal statutory copyright if the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_formalities" title="Copyright formalities">
       statutory formalities
      </a>
      , such as a proper copyright notice, were satisfied. If this was not the case the work immediately entered into the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      . In 1834 the
      <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">
       Supreme Court
      </a>
      ruled in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wheaton_v._Peters" title="Wheaton v. Peters">
        Wheaton v. Peters
       </a>
      </i>
      (a case similar to the 1774 case of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Donaldson_v_Beckett" title="Donaldson v Beckett">
        Donaldson v Beckett
       </a>
      </i>
      in Britain) that although the author of an unpublished work had a
      <a href="/wiki/Common_law_copyright" title="Common law copyright">
       common law right
      </a>
      to control the first publication of that work, the author did not have a common law right to control reproduction following the first publication of the work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_K_2007_143_30-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Peter_K_2007_143-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Berne_Convention_signatories.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_party_to_the_Berne_Convention" title="List of countries party to the Berne Convention">
         Berne Convention signatory countries
        </a>
        (in blue).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Literary_and_Artistic_Works" title="Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works">
       Berne Convention
      </a>
      was first established in 1886, and was subsequently re-negotiated in 1896 (Paris), 1908 (Berlin), 1928 (Rome), 1948 (Brussels), 1967 (Stockholm) and 1971 (Paris). The convention relates to literary and artistic works, which includes films, and the convention requires its member states to provide protection for every production in the literary, scientific and artistic domain. The Berne Convention has a number of core features, including the principle of
      <i>
       national treatment
      </i>
      , which holds that each member state to the Convention would give citizens of other member states the same rights of copyright that it gave to its own citizens (Article 3-5).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_37_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_37-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another core feature is the establishment of minimum standards of national copyright legislation in that each member state agrees to certain basic rules which their national laws must contain. Though member states can if they wish increase the amount of protection given to copyright owners. One important minimum rule was that the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_term" title="Copyright term">
       term of copyright
      </a>
      was to be a minimum of the author's lifetime plus 50 years. Another important minimum rule established by the Berne Convention is that copyright arises with the creation of a work and does not depend upon any formality such as a system of public registration (Article 5(2)). At the time some countries did require registration of copyright, and when Britain implemented the Berne Convention in the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_Act_1911" title="Copyright Act 1911">
       Copyright Act 1911
      </a>
      it had to abolish its system of registration at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stationers%27_Hall" title="Stationers' Hall">
       Stationers' Hall
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_37_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_37-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Berne Convention focuses on authors as the key figure in copyright law and the stated purpose of the convention is "the protection of the rights of authors in their literary and artistic works" (Article 1), rather than the protection of publishers and other actors in the process of disseminating works to the public. In the 1928 revision the concept of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_rights_(copyright_law)" title="Moral rights (copyright law)">
       moral rights
      </a>
      was introduced (Article 6bis), giving authors the right to be identified as a such and to object to derogatory treatment of their works. These rights, unlike economic rights such as preventing reproduction, could not be transferred to others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_37_31-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_37-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Berne Convention also enshrined
      <a href="/wiki/Limitations_and_exceptions_to_copyright" title="Limitations and exceptions to copyright">
       limitations and exceptions to copyright
      </a>
      , enabling the reproduction of literary and artistic works without the copyright owners prior permission. The detail of these exceptions was left to national copyright legislation, but the guiding principle is stated in Article 9 of the convention. The so-called
      <a href="/wiki/Berne_three-step_test" title="Berne three-step test">
       three-step test
      </a>
      holds that an exception is only permitted "in certain special cases, provided that such reproduction does not conflict with a normal exploitation of the work and does not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the author". Free use of copyrighted work is expressly permitted in the case of quotations from lawfully published works, illustration for teaching purposes, and news reporting (Article 10).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacQueen_2007_37_31-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-MacQueen_2007_37-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_and_technology">
       Copyright and technology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Copyright and technology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Digital technology introduces a new level of controversy into copyright policy.
      </li>
      <li>
       Inclusion of software as copyright subject matter on the recommendation of
       <a href="/wiki/CONTU" title="CONTU">
        CONTU
       </a>
       and then later with the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EU_Computer_Programs_Directive&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EU Computer Programs Directive (page does not exist)">
        EU Computer Programs Directive
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Enactment of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agreement_on_Trade-Related_Aspects_of_Intellectual_Property_Rights" title="Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights">
        Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights
       </a>
       (TRIPS).
      </li>
      <li>
       Controversy over the copyrightability of databases (
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feist_Publications_v._Rural_Telephone_Service" title="Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone Service">
         Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone Service
        </a>
       </i>
       and contradictory cases); links to the debate over sui generis
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Database_rights" title="Database rights">
        Database rights
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       Enactment of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Intellectual_Property_Organization_Copyright_Treaty" title="World Intellectual Property Organization Copyright Treaty">
        World Intellectual Property Organization Copyright Treaty
       </a>
       ; nations begin passing anti-circumvention laws.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Commentators such as Barlow (1994) have argued that digital copyright is fundamentally different and will remain persistently difficult to enforce; others such as
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Stallman" title="Richard Stallman">
       Stallman
      </a>
      (1996)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      have argued that the Internet deeply undermines the economic rationale for copyright in the first place. These perspectives may lead to the consideration of
      <a href="/wiki/Alternative_compensation_system" title="Alternative compensation system">
       alternative compensation systems
      </a>
      in place of exclusive rights for all types of information, including software, books, movies, and music.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FMP_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FMP-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-primer_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-primer-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Expansions_in_scope_and_operation">
       Expansions in scope and operation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_copyright_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Expansions in scope and operation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Move from common law and ad-hoc grants of monopoly to copyright statutes.
      </li>
      <li>
       Expansions in subject matter (largely related to technology).
      </li>
      <li>
       Expansions on duration.
      </li>
      <li>
       Creation of new exclusive rights (such as performers' and other neighbouring rights).
      </li>
      <li>
       Creation of collecting societies.
      </li>
      <li>
       Criminalisation of copyright infringement.
      </li>
      <li>
       Creation of
       <a href="/wiki/Anti-circumvention" title="Anti-circumvention">
        anti-circumvention
       </a>
       laws.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         35
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Courts' application of
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_liability" title="Secondary liability">
        secondary liability
       </a>
       doctrines to cover file sharing networks
      </li>
     </ul>
