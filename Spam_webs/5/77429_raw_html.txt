     <div class="parsys editorsNote">
     </div>
     <div class="newbody body parsys">
      <div class="parbase image slate_image section">
       <div class=" none">
        <figure class="image inline " style="width: 590px;  display: block;margin: 0 auto;float: none;">
         <img alt="little_feat_1975_1" src="/content/dam/slate/blogs/browbeat/2014/03/05/little_feat_10_best_songs_from_all_that_you_dream_to_willin/little_feat_1975_1.jpg.CROP.promovar-mediumlarge.jpg" src_tag_name="src" title="little_feat_1975_1"/>
         <figcaption class="caption">
          <span>
           A publicity photo of Little Feat taken in 1975.
          </span>
         </figcaption>
         <p class="credit">
          Warner Bros./
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Little_Feat_1975.JPG" target="_blank">
           Wikipedia
          </a>
         </p>
        </figure>
       </div>
       <script>
        requirejs(["jquery"],function($){if($(window).width()&lt;640){$(".slate_image figure").width("100%");}});
       </script>
      </div>
      <div class="text text-1 parbase section">
       <p>
        The most underrated 70s band to come out of Los Angelesno, make that the whole countryLittle Feat never had a true hit single. In my years of listening to classic-rock radio, Ive yet to hear one of their songs in the rotation. Until former
        <em>
         Rolling Stone
        </em>
        editor Ben Fong-Torres published
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0306821311/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0306821311&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         <em>
          Willin: The Story of Little Feat
         </em>
        </a>
        in November of last year, no one had written a biography of the band. Fong-Torress book is jammed with quotes from the likes of Bonnie Raitt, Linda Ronstadt, and Rickie Lee Jones, testifying to the greatness of Little Feat during their golden age. Marshall Tucker, Eric Clapton, and Robert Plant have called Little Feat their favorite band, and the Stones and Dylan both made sure to see them live.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text-2 text parbase section">
       <p>
        They still tour, but without their founder, who died in 1979. Happily for Feats fans, though, Rhino Records has just released a 13-disc box set of the bands recorded and live songs, plus outtakes, called
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00H87JYDM/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B00H87JYDM&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         <em>
          Rad Gumbo: The Complete Warner Bros. Years 1971-1990
         </em>
        </a>
        . If youve never gotten acquainted with the band, nows the time.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="tablet-ad">
       Advertisement
       <br/>
       <div class="ad " data-ad-mode="tabletPortrait" data-ad-type="midarticleflex" id="slug_midarticleflex">
       </div>
       <div class="ad " data-ad-mode="mobile" data-ad-type="mob_bigbox" id="slug_mob_bigbox">
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="text-3 text parbase section">
       <p>
        The bands origin story begins in 1968, when guitarist and singer Lowell George joined Frank Zappas Mothers of Invention. When Zappa heard Georges song Willin,  he suggested that the guitarist break out and form his own band. You might think of Frank Zappa and the Mothers as stoner music, but Zappa wasnt into the drug scene; and, according to Lowell Georges biographer,
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0879307269/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0879307269&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         Mark Brend
        </a>
        , the references to weed, whites, and wine in Willin didnt feel like part of the Mothers catalogue. Itd be an understatement to say Little Feat violated Zappas drug-free policy, but George nonetheless learned from Zappas managerial style and became an exacting band leader himself. In 1969 he founded Lilttle Feat with keyboardist Bill Payne, combining blues, New Orleans funk, and spacey jams to create a steaming gumbo of Southern rock. (The spelling of feat, by the way, is an homage to the Beatles.)
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-4 section">
       <p>
        The band is best known for
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0000631ED/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B0000631ED&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         <em>
          Waiting for Columbus
         </em>
        </a>
        , a live album recorded over seven shows in 1977 and released in 1978. Fleshed out by a stellar horn section courtesy of
        <a href="http://www.towerofpower.com/">
         Tower of Power
        </a>
        ,
        <em>
         Waiting for Columbus
        </em>
        is widely considered one of the best live albums ever released;
        <a href="http://www.rollingstone.com/music/pictures/readers-poll-the-10-best-live-albums-of-all-time-20121121/7-little-feat-waiting-for-columbus-0351483">
         <em>
          Rolling Stone
         </em>
        </a>
        readers put it at No. 7 on their top 10 list, and, in 2010,
        <a href="http://www.spin.com/articles/phish-trick-and-treat-little-feat-led-zeppelin/">
         Phish
        </a>
        played the album in its entirety for their semi-regular 
        <a href="http://phish.net/faq/halloween">
         musical costume
        </a>
         Halloween show. For a taste of the Feats in their glory daysand a glimpse of Georges ubiquitous white overallsyou should check out the concert DVD
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B002LMOCCC/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B002LMOCCC&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         <em>
          Skin It Back
         </em>
        </a>
        , filmed in Germany just a few days before the band recorded
        <em>
         Waiting for Columbus
        </em>
        .
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-5 section">
       <p>
        Willin remains Little Feats calling card. It originally appeared on the bands self-titled debut album from 1971, and they recorded it again for 1972s
        <em>
         Sailin Shoes
        </em>
        . (Im partial to the slightly slower, less polished version of the song that appears on a
        <a href="https://archive.org/details/lf1974-09-19.shnf">
         bootleg from 1974
        </a>
        .) Its a truckers anthem, with repeated references to cities in Arizona, California, and New Mexico. (And I been from Tucson to Tucumcari/ Tehachapi and Tonopah.) Theres an echo of Willin in Rock &amp; Roll Doctor, the opening track on 1974s
        <em>
         Feats Dont Fail Me Now
        </em>
        , which name-checks MobileMoline Nacogdoches, and New Orleans. Rock &amp; Roll Doctor is a good example of Little Feats off-kilter, syncopated rhythmskept by drummer Richie Haywardwhich may have contributed to the bands lack of radio play. Thats what Feats guitarist Paul Barrere has suggested, anyway.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text text-6 parbase section">
       <blockquote>
        Lowell used to do this thing with cassette tapes where he would take the tape and cut and splice it together, not knowing what was going to happen. [On Rock &amp; Roll Doctor] there was like a couple of measures that were 3 1/2 beats instead of 4 beats and he would hand the tape to [keyboardist] Billy [Payne] and say, Normalize this. I think within the framework of the verse theres a 6/4 measure, which is probably why we didnt get a whole lot of airplay on jukeboxes. If people try to dance to it, its like theyre on the wrong foot!
       </blockquote>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-7 section">
       <p>
        <em>
         Dixie Chicken
        </em>
        , from 1973, furnished many of Little Feats best known songs: the funky/funny Fat Man in the Bathtub, penned by George; the title tune, written by George with his childhood friend Martin Kibbee; and On Your Way Down, a cover of a song released two years earlier by New Orleans composer
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B002ZJURES/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B002ZJURES&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         Allen Toussaint
        </a>
        . Little Feat moved into funkier territory with 1977s
        <em>
         Time Loves a Hero
        </em>
        , which was more of a group effort and less dominated by George himself, who at this point was working on solo material. The title track is by Barrere, Payne, and bassist Kenny Gradney, but I like to think of its lyrics as describing their band leader, who would die just two years later, of heart failure: Well they say/ Time loves a hero/ But only time will tell If hes real/ Hes a legend from heaven/ If he aint he was sent here from hell.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div class="text parbase text-8 section">
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001DP4MV4/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B001DP4MV4&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         Rock &amp; Roll Doctor
        </a>
        (
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001DPAW40/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B001DPAW40&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=slatmaga-20">
         <em>
          Feats Dont Fail Me Now
         </em>
        </a>
        , 1974)
       </p>
