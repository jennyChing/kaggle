          <h1 class="entry-title">
           Top Seven Optional Provisions to Include In Your Articles of Incorporation: Number 2
          </h1>
         </div>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <div class="meta-info-wrap">
           <span class="sep posted-on">
            Posted on
           </span>
           <a href="http://griesslaw.com/blog/top-seven-optional-provisions-to-include-in-your-articles-of-incorporation-number-2.html" rel="bookmark" title="7:00 am">
            <time class="entry-date" datetime="2014-03-30T07:00:00+00:00">
             March 30, 2014
            </time>
           </a>
           <span class="by-author">
            <span class="sep">
             by
            </span>
            <span class="author vcard">
             <a class="url fn n" href="http://griesslaw.com/blog/author/admin" rel="author" title="View all posts by Douglas Griess">

            </span>
           </span>

         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content cf">
         <p>
          The Articles should address cumulative voting. Cumulative voting is when the number of votes a shareholder has is multiplied by the number of candidates to be elected to an office, such as a director, and the shareholders are allowed to use up their votes over the course of the voting using any number of their votes for each candidate until their votes are gone. In short, the process allows a minority shareholder the ability to accumulate their total number of votes in a way so as to have a majority of the votes necessary to elect one or more of their choices for an office because the majority shareholder has to spread out his or her votes among his or her own candidates to ensure their election.
         </p>
         <p>
          Oddly enough while almost no one ever uses, or wants to use, cumulative voting for shareholders in the election of directors, it is the default process under Colorado law,
          <b>
           <i>
            UNLESS
           </i>
          </b>
          the shareholders limit cumulative voting in the Articles of Incorporation. See  7-102-102(3), 7-107-209, and 7-107-104(1.5). Sometimes corporations put this limitation in their Bylaws, but this is incorrect and probably ineffective without other mitigating factors. Accordingly, a business will want to specify in the Articles that cumulative voting in the election of directors is not permitted.
         </p>
