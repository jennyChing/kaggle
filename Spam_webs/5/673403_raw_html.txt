        <div id="article-title">
         <h1>
          7 Tips for Writers to Build Your Online Profile
         </h1>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="article-container" id="TNArticleBodySection">
        <div id="article-body">
         <p>
          Being visible on the Internet is crucial if you're pursuing a career as a writer. However, you can't always rely on links to your previously written works-- especially if they appeared on websites you don't own. Creating an online portfolio of your work (in PDFs) will get you noticed and get you more business.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          A page with hyperlinks to your articles is not an online portfolio. You might as well print them out and mail them to the person considering your work. A well-designed online portfolio will get you found, grab attention, answer questions and turn readers into paid work. In addition to
          <a href="http://www.artisancreative.com/artisan-blog/7-tips-for-your-online-writing-portfolio" target="_blank">
           organizing your work
          </a>
          , you will need to follow some of these steps.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Figure Out Your Industry and Offerings
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="man writing in notebook with pen" class="media-image media-image-left" src="http://artstg.icbdr.com/sites/all/files/3293117576_05f43d8305.jpg" style="margin: 4px 10px; float: left" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Image via
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/jjpacres/3293117576/sizes/l/in/photolist-62168f-62GZWn-63tuYR-64EXQ5-65wB4h-6ajgze-6d3oYk-6dz5kg-6dEhbu-6dKk9g-6dUvjx-6dXGqM-6h2kSQ-6int3M-6jQV5n-6keSjR-6n4cDA-6o52sJ-6q3TiK-6qNWzU-6vy1gF-6wd9Mw-6yBqPT-6yBqVi-6yFwDy-6znbYn-6BrSQ4-6Dfop4-6EMdga-6EMdkr-6EMdzn-6PqzYs-6R8GdY-6SB7EB-6SKEAY-6SN5qD-6SSrEd-6TuNgh-6TBar4-6Z3Enk-74AeT4-75qDZu-77x3Kq-781Ha5-7aN6nr-7cmC3d-7gMuVZ-7j2ubi-7nr6Wb-7trTMk-7u3DkP/">
           Flickr
          </a>
          by
          <a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/jjpacres/">
           jjpacres
          </a>
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          If you serve a limited market/industry, have minimal experience and/or are simply looking for somewhere to "hang" your work so that you can get a full-time position with a company, a single web page should suffice.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          However, if you are (or want to be) a
          <a href="http://www.clippings.me/writing-portfolio-how-to" target="_blank">
           professional journalist
          </a>
          or freelance writer who either offers several different kinds of writing, or you want to work with well-known clients you'll need an online portfolio and a larger web presence.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Another reason for creating multiple web pages is more direct targeting of the industries you want to serve. For instance, if you write both corporate communications and fiction, there is no reason for these very different clients to filter what applies to them and what does not. Creating multiple web pages to speak to each will give them the information they need to make a hiring decision, without all the rest. Keep things as simple as possible for your clients.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Create a Killer About Page
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          Yes, writing samples are important but if someone is looking to hire you, but they also want to know who you are. Creating an about section is crucial to presenting yourself professionally.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          If you're pitching two very different kinds of writing,
          <em>
           as mentioned above
          </em>
          , you may want to create an about page specific to each genre. For instance, your corporate communication writing page may contain a link to an about page that mentions you worked for a Fortune 500 company for three years in their marketing department. Your fiction page, on the other hand, may include all the networks you can leverage to market your book.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          If the markets and genres you write for do not require different skills, one about page will suit your needs. The about page should include your name, contact information, social media profiles, pertinent background, a few of your most popular pieces (these should also be listed in your online portfolio page), a picture of yourself, a link to your online portfolio and, if you don't plan on having them on a dedicated page, testimonials from clients.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Create a Contact Page
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          Another clearly marked tab on your site should be a contact tab, which should feature your contact information as well as a contact form to make it easy for people to reach out to you. People know to look for that tab on websites, so including it will help them go through the process of contacting you quicker.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           "Sell" Only What You Sell
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          Just as you would customize a rsum based on the job you were applying for, your online portfolio should work the same way. This is not an art museum. You're not showcasing everything you've ever done. You are showcasing the kind of work you want to be hired to do. Don't dump every story or article you've ever written on a web page and call it a portfolio.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          The client wants to verify you can write the kind of material they need, not read every piece you've ever written. Give them what they want and you'll get hired. Give them too much and they'll close the page.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Ask the "Why" and Sell to It
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          Why would a client hire you? Are they looking to increase web traffic? Do they need someone to tell their story? Figure out
          <em>
           what
          </em>
          your clients need and
          <em>
           why
          </em>
          they would hire you and then provide real examples of how you did that for others.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Don't Forget the Design
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          Writers are all about words, but when it comes to showcasing these words many are at a loss. The design of a page or a website will draw someone in, just as surely as a long list of hyperlinks will turn them off.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          If you don't have the knowledge yourself to build out a visually appealing page, turn to a professional web designer. If you don't have the money, find a designer just starting out who is in need of some web copy. A trade for services may suit you both.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Add an Offer
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          If you are using your online portfolio to land work and not just showcase it, you'll want to consider providing a free resource for clients. Writing a checklist of things to consider when hiring a freelancer is helpful, as is a post they can use for their industry as long as they give you credit.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          Getting your name out there and in front of people who are able to hire you is essential. Providing several free resources on your page is a good way to do this.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <p>
          A portfolio is not what it used to be. It's no longer enough to give a list of what you've written. If you're serious about writing as a career, you'll want a portfolio that represents you as a gifted writer, professional, and marketer. After all, today's writers have to be all of those things.
         </p>
         <br/>
         <p>
          An online portfolio should sell you to a client. Since you won't personally be in their office showing it to them, it will bemaking a first impression for you. Ensure it's put together professionally, is user-friendly, answers client questions, and makes it easy to reach out to you. If it doesn't do these things, then you're making a bad first impression on a client.
         </p>
