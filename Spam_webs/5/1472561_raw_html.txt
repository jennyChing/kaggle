    Sweatshop
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      <b>
       Sweatshop
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       sweat factory
      </b>
      ) is a pejorative term for a
      <a href="/wiki/Workplace" title="Workplace">
       workplace
      </a>
      that has
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socially_acceptable" title="Socially acceptable">
       socially unacceptable
      </a>
      working conditions. The work may be difficult, dangerous or underpaid. Workers in sweatshops may work long hours for low pay, regardless of laws mandating overtime pay or a
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">
       minimum wage
      </a>
      ;
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_labor" title="Child labor">
       child labor
      </a>
      laws may also be violated.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweatshop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A sweatshop is a factory or workshop, especially in the clothing industry, where manual workers are employed at very low wages for long hours and under poor conditions.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sweatshop-1890.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="323" data-file-width="400" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Sweatshop-1890.jpg/220px-Sweatshop-1890.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Sweatshop-1890.jpg/330px-Sweatshop-1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a8/Sweatshop-1890.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sweatshop-1890.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A sweatshop c.1890
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Many workplaces through history have been crowded, low-paying and without job security; but the concept of a sweatshop originated between 1830 and 1850 as a specific type of workshop in which a certain type of middleman, the
      <i>
       sweater
      </i>
      , directed others in
      <i>
       garment making
      </i>
      (the process of producing clothing) under arduous conditions. The terms
      <i>
       sweater
      </i>
      for the middleman and
      <i>
       sweat system
      </i>
      for the process of subcontracting piecework were used in early critiques like
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kingsley" title="Charles Kingsley">
       Charles Kingsley
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Cheap Clothes and Nasty
      </i>
      , written in 1850, which described conditions in London, England. The workplaces created for the
      <b>
       sweating system
      </b>
      , a system of subcontracting in the
      <a href="/wiki/Tailor" title="Tailor">
       tailoring
      </a>
      trade were called
      <i>
       sweatshops
      </i>
      and might contain only a few workers or as many as 100 and more.
     </p>
     <p>
      Between 1850 and 1900, sweatshops attracted the rural poor to rapidly growing cities, and attracted immigrants to places such as London and New York City's garment district, located near the
      <a href="/wiki/Tenement" title="Tenement">
       tenements
      </a>
      of New York's
      <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">
       Lower East Side
      </a>
      . These sweatshops incurred criticism: labour leaders cited them as crowded, poorly ventilated, and prone to fires and
      <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">
       rat
      </a>
      infestations: in many cases, there were many workers crowded into small tenement rooms.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1890s, a group calling itself the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Anti-Sweating_League" title="National Anti-Sweating League">
       National Anti-Sweating League
      </a>
      was formed in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melbourne,_Australia" title="Melbourne, Australia">
       Melbourne, Australia
      </a>
      and campaigned successfully for a
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">
       minimum wage
      </a>
      via trade boards. A group with the same name campaigned from 1906 in the UK, resulting in the
      <a href="/wiki/Trade_Boards_Act_1909" title="Trade Boards Act 1909">
       Trade Boards Act 1909
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackburn_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blackburn-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1910, the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Ladies%27_Garment_Workers%27_Union" title="International Ladies' Garment Workers' Union">
       International Ladies' Garment Workers' Union
      </a>
      was founded to try to improve the condition of these workers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Criticism of garment sweatshops became a major force behind workplace safety regulation and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_laws" title="Labor laws">
       labor laws
      </a>
      . As some journalists strove to change working conditions, the term
      <i>
       sweatshop
      </i>
      came to refer to a broader set of workplaces whose conditions were considered inferior. In the United States,
      <a href="/wiki/Investigative_journalism" title="Investigative journalism">
       investigative journalists
      </a>
      , known as
      <a href="/wiki/Muckraker" title="Muckraker">
       Muckrakers
      </a>
      , wrote exposs of business practices, and
      <a href="/wiki/Progressivism_in_the_United_States" title="Progressivism in the United States">
       progressive
      </a>
      politicians campaigned for new laws. Notable exposs of sweatshop conditions include
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Riis" title="Jacob Riis">
       Jacob Riis
      </a>
      '
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photo_journalism" title="Photo journalism">
       photo documentary
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/How_the_Other_Half_Lives" title="How the Other Half Lives">
        How the Other Half Lives
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">
       Upton Sinclair
      </a>
      's book,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">
        The Jungle
       </a>
      </i>
      about the
      <a href="/wiki/Meat_packing_industry" title="Meat packing industry">
       meat packing industry
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1911, negative public perceptions of sweatshops were galvanized by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangle_Shirtwaist_Factory_Fire" title="Triangle Shirtwaist Factory Fire">
       Triangle Shirtwaist Factory Fire
      </a>
      in New York City. The pivotal role of this time and place is chronicled at the Lower East Side Tenement Museum, part of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side_Tenement_National_Historic_Site" title="Lower East Side Tenement National Historic Site">
       Lower East Side Tenement National Historic Site
      </a>
      . While trade unions,
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">
       minimum wage
      </a>
      laws,
      <a href="/wiki/Fire_safety" title="Fire safety">
       fire safety
      </a>
      codes, and labour laws have made sweatshops (in the original sense) rarer in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_world" title="Developed world">
       developed world
      </a>
      , they did not eliminate them, and the term has come to be increasingly associated with factories in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">
       developing world
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In a report issued in 1994, the United States
      <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">
       Government Accountability Office
      </a>
      found that there were still thousands of sweatshops in the United States, using a definition of a
      <i>
       sweatshop
      </i>
      as any "employer that violates more than one federal or state labor law governing minimum wage and overtime, child labor, industrial
      <a href="/wiki/Homeworker" title="Homeworker">
       homework
      </a>
      , occupational safety and health, workers' compensation, or industry registration".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This recent definition eliminates any historical distinction about the role of a middleman or the items produced, and focuses on the legal standards of developed country workplaces. An area of controversy between supporters of outsourcing production to the
      <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">
       Third World
      </a>
      and the anti-sweatshop movement is whether such standards can or should be applied to the workplaces of the developing world.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sweatshops are also sometimes implicated in
      <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">
       human trafficking
      </a>
      when workers have been tricked into starting work without
      <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">
       informed consent
      </a>
      , or when workers are kept at work through
      <a href="/wiki/Debt_bondage" title="Debt bondage">
       debt bondage
      </a>
      or mental duress, all of which are more likely if the workforce is drawn from children or the uneducated rural poor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because they often exist in places without effective workplace safety or environmental laws, sweatshops sometimes injure their workers or the environment at greater rates than would be acceptable in developed countries.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Sometimes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penal_labor" title="Penal labor">
       penal labor
      </a>
      facilities (employing prisoners) are grouped under the sweatshop label.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Sweatshops conditions resemble prison labor in many cases, especially from a common found Western perspective. Sweatshops in question carry characteristics such as compulsory pregnancy tests for female laborers and terrorization from supervisors into submission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Workers then go into a state of forced labor, if even one day of work is not accounted for, most are immediately fired.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These working conditions have been the source of suicidal unrest within factories in the past. Chinese sweatshops known to have increased numbers of suicidal employees have suicide nets covering the whole site, in place to stop over-worked and stressed employees leaping to their deaths.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sweatshops have proved a difficult issue to resolve because their roots lie in the conceptual foundations of the world economy.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Developing countries like India, China, Vietnam, Bangladesh and Honduras encourage the outsourcing of work from the developed world to factories within their borders in order to provide employment for their people and profits for their employers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Outsourced work can at times bring some form of wealth to impoverished countries where people struggle to provide for their families, regardless of Western labor concerns, low wages are preferred to none at all in these areas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The shift of production to developing countries is part of
      <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
       globalization
      </a>
      , but may also be described as
      <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">
       neoliberal
      </a>
      globalization to emphasize the role that
      <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">
       free market
      </a>
      economics plays in outsourcing.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-sweatshop_movement">
       Anti-sweatshop movement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweatshop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anti-sweatshop movement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-sweatshop_movement" title="Anti-sweatshop movement">
       Anti-sweatshop movement
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Some of the earliest sweatshop critics were found in the 19th century
      <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism" title="Abolitionism">
       abolitionist
      </a>
      movement that had originally coalesced in opposition to chattel
      <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">
       slavery
      </a>
      , and many abolitionists saw similarities between slavery and sweatshop work. As slavery was successively outlawed in industrial countries between 1794 (in France) and 1865 (in the United States), some abolitionists sought to broaden the anti-slavery consensus to include other forms of harsh labor, including sweatshops. As it happened, the first significant law to address sweatshops (the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factory_Act" title="Factory Act">
       Factory Act of 1833
      </a>
      ) was passed in the United Kingdom at the same time that the slave trade (1807) and ownership of slaves (1833) were made illegal.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ultimately, the abolitionist movement split apart. Some advocates focused on working conditions and found common cause with trade unions and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxists" title="Marxists">
       Marxists
      </a>
      and socialist political groups, or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_movement" title="Progressive movement">
       progressive movement
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Muckraker" title="Muckraker">
       muckrakers
      </a>
      . Others focused on the continued slave trade and
      <a href="/wiki/Involuntary_servitude" title="Involuntary servitude">
       involuntary servitude
      </a>
      in the colonial world. For those groups that remained focused on slavery, sweatshops became one of the primary objects of controversy. Workplaces across multiple sectors of the economy were categorized as sweatshops. However, there were fundamental philosophical disagreements about what constituted slavery. Unable to agree on the status of sweatshops, the abolitionists working with the
      <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">
       League of Nations
      </a>
      and the United Nations ultimately backed away from efforts to define slavery, and focused instead on a common precursor of slavery 
      <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">
       human trafficking
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Those focused on working conditions included
      <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">
       Friedrich Engels
      </a>
      , whose book
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Condition_of_the_Working_Class_in_England_in_1844" title="The Condition of the Working Class in England in 1844">
        The Condition of the Working Class in England in 1844
       </a>
      </i>
      would inspire the Marxist movement named for his collaborator,
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">
       Karl Marx
      </a>
      . In the United Kingdom the Factory Act was revised six further times between 1844 and 1878 to help improve the condition of workers by limiting work hours and the use of child labor. The formation of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Labour_Organization" title="International Labour Organization">
       International Labour Organization
      </a>
      in 1919 under the League of Nations and then the United Nations sought to address the plight of workers the world over. Concern over working conditions as described by muckraker journalists during the
      <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Era" title="Progressive Era">
       Progressive Era
      </a>
      in the United States saw the passage of new workers rights laws and ultimately resulted in the
      <a href="/wiki/Fair_Labor_Standards_Act" title="Fair Labor Standards Act">
       Fair Labor Standards Act
      </a>
      of 1938, passed during the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">
       New Deal
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_anti-globalization_movement">
       Modern anti-globalization movement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sweatshop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern anti-globalization movement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StudentsMarchOnRussellHQ.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="172" data-file-width="200" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/StudentsMarchOnRussellHQ.jpg" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        Members of United Students Against Sweatshops marching in protest.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">
       anti-globalization movement
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      More recently, the
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">
       anti-globalization movement
      </a>
      has arisen in opposition to corporate
      <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
       globalization
      </a>
      , a process by which
      <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">
       multinational corporations
      </a>
      move their operations
      <a href="/wiki/Overseas" title="Overseas">
       overseas
      </a>
      in order to lower their costs and increase profits. The anti-sweatshop movement has much in common with the
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">
       anti-globalization movement
      </a>
      . Both consider sweatshops harmful, and both have accused many companies (such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Company" title="Walt Disney Company">
       Walt Disney Company
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gap_(clothing_retailer)" title="The Gap (clothing retailer)">
       The Gap
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">
       Nike
      </a>
      ) of using sweatshops. Some in these movements charge that
      <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">
       neoliberal
      </a>
      globalization is similar to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweating_system" title="Sweating system">
       sweating system
      </a>
      , arguing that there tends to be a "
      <a href="/wiki/Race_to_the_bottom" title="Race to the bottom">
       race to the bottom
      </a>
      ", as multinationals leap from one low-wage country to another searching for lower production costs, in the same way that
      <i>
       sweaters
      </i>
      would have steered production to the lowest cost sub-contractor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Various groups support or embody the anti-sweatshop movement today. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Labor_Committee" title="National Labor Committee">
       National Labor Committee
      </a>
      brought sweatshops into the mainstream media in the 1990s when it exposed the use of sweatshop and child labor to sew clothing for Kathie Lee Gifford's Wal-Mart label. United Students Against Sweatshops is active on college campuses. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Labor_Rights_Fund" title="International Labor Rights Fund">
       International Labor Rights Fund
      </a>
      filed a lawsuit
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      on behalf of workers in China, Nicaragua, Swaziland, Indonesia, and Bangladesh against Wal-Mart charging the company with knowingly developing purchasing policies particularly relating to price and delivery time that are impossible to meet while following the Wal-Mart code of conduct. Labor unions, such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFL-CIO" title="AFL-CIO">
       AFL-CIO
      </a>
      , have helped support the anti-sweatshop movement out of concern both for the welfare of workers in the developing world and those in the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Social critics complain that sweatshop workers often do not earn enough money to buy the products that they make, even though such items are often commonplace goods such as T-shirts, shoes, and toys. In 2003, Honduran garment factory workers were paid US$0.24 for each $50
      <a href="/wiki/Sean_John" title="Sean John">
       Sean John
      </a>
      sweatshirt, $0.15 for each long-sleeved T-shirt, and only five cents for each short-sleeved shirt  less than one-half of one percent of the retail price.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seanjohn_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-seanjohn-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even comparing international costs of living, the $0.15 that a Honduran worker earned for the long-sleeved T-shirt was equal in
      <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power" title="Purchasing power">
       purchasing power
      </a>
      to $0.50 in the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In countries where labor costs are low, bras that cost US$5-7 apiece retail for US$50 or more in American stores. As of 2006
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sweatshop&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      , female garment workers in Sri Lanka earned about US$2.20 per day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watson_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-watson-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anti-globalization proponents cite high savings, increased capital investment in developing nations, diversification of their exports and their status as trade ports as the reason for their economic success rather than sweatshops
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and cite the numerous cases in the East Asian "Tiger Economies" where sweatshops have reduced living standards and wages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Businessweek_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Businessweek-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They believe that better-paying jobs, increased capital investment and domestic ownership of resources will improve the economies of sub-Saharan Africa rather than sweatshops. They point to good labor standards developing strong manufacturing export sectors in wealthier sub-Saharan countries such as Mauritius
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and believe measures like these will improve economic conditions in developing nations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anti-globalization organizations argue that the minor gains made by employees of some of these institutions are outweighed by the negative costs such as lowered wages to increase profit margins and that the institutions pay less than the daily expenses of their workers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also point to the fact that sometimes local jobs offered higher wages before trade liberalization provided tax incentives to allow sweatshops to replace former local unionized jobs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They further contend that sweatshop jobs are not necessarily inevitable.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Eric Toussaint claims that quality of life in developing countries was actually higher between 1945 and 1980 before the international debt crisis of 1982 harmed economies in developing countries causing them to turn to IMF and World Bank-organized "structural adjustments"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and that unionized jobs pay more than sweatshop ones overall  "several studies of workers producing for US firms in Mexico are instructive: workers at the Aluminum Company of America's Ciudad Acuna plant earn between $21.44 and $24.60 per week, but a weekly basket of basic food items costs $26.87. Mexican GM workers earn enough to buy a pound of apples in 30 minutes of work, while GM workers in the US earn as much in 5 minutes."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      People critical of sweatshops believe that "free trade agreements" do not truly promote free trade at all but instead seek to protect multinational corporations from competition by local industries (which are sometimes unionized).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They believe free trade should only involve reducing tariffs and barriers to entry and that multinational businesses should operate within the laws in the countries they want to do business in rather than seeking immunity from obeying local environmental and labor laws. They believe these conditions are what give rise to sweatshops rather than natural industrialization or economic progression.
     </p>
     <p>
      In some countries, such as China, it is not uncommon for these institutions to withhold workers' pay.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        According to labor organizations in Hong Kong, up to $365 million is withheld by managers who restrict pay in exchange for some service, or don't pay at all.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
         <a href="#cite_note-33">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          33
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      Furthermore, anti-globalization proponents argue that those in the West who defend sweatshops show double standards by complaining about sweatshop labor conditions in countries considered enemies or hostile by Western governments, while still gladly consuming their exports but complaining about the quality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Businessweek_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Businessweek-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They contend that multinational jobs should be expected to operate according to international labor and environmental laws and minimum wage standards like businesses in the West do.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Labor historian Erik Loomis claims that the conditions faced by workers in the United States in the
      <a href="/wiki/Gilded_Age" title="Gilded Age">
       Gilded Age
      </a>
      have been replicated in developing countries where Western corporations utilize sweatshop labor. In particular, he compares the
      <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Shirtwaist_Factory_fire" title="Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire">
       Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire
      </a>
      in 1911 New York to the
      <a href="/wiki/2013_Savar_building_collapse" title="2013 Savar building collapse">
       collapse of Rana Plaza
      </a>
      in 2013 Bangladesh. He argues that the former galvanized the population to political activism which eventually pushed through reforms not only pertaining to workplace safety, but also the
      <a href="/wiki/Minimum_wage" title="Minimum wage">
       minimum wage
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Eight-hour_day" title="Eight-hour day">
       eight-hour day
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_compensation" title="Workers' compensation">
       workers' compensation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)" title="Social Security (United States)">
       Social Security
      </a>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">
       Clean Air Act
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">
       Clean Water Act
      </a>
      . American corporations responded by shifting production to developing nations where such protections did not exist. Loomis elaborates:
     </p>
     <table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
      <tr>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
       <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
        So in 2013, when over 1100 workers die at Rana Plaza in Bangladesh, it is the same industry as the Triangle Fire, with the same subcontracted system of production that allows apparel companies to avoid responsibility for work as the Triangle Fire, and with the same workforce of young and poor women, the same type of cruel bosses, and the same terrible workplace safety standards as the Triangle Fire. The difference is that most of us can't even find Bangladesh on a map, not to mention know enough about it to express the type of outrage our ancestors did after Triangle. This separation of production from consumption is an intentional move by corporations precisely to avoid being held responsible by consumers for their actions. And it is very effective.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
         <a href="#cite_note-35">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          35
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
       <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
        
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>

      and the global movement of capital cite the economic theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Comparative_advantage" title="Comparative advantage">
       comparative advantage
      </a>
      , which states that
      <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">
       international trade
      </a>
      will, in the long run, make all parties better off. The theory holds that developing countries improve their condition by doing something that they do "better" than industrialized nations (in this case, they charge less but do the same work). Developed countries will also be better off because their workers can shift to jobs that they do better. These are jobs that some economists say usually entail a level of education and training that is exceptionally difficult to obtain in the developing world. Thus, economists like Sachs say, developing countries get factories and jobs that they would not otherwise. Some
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2011)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      would say with this situation occurs when developing countries try to increase wages because sweatshops tend to just get moved on to a new state that is more welcoming. This leads to a situation where states often will not try to get increased wages for sweatshop workers for fear of losing investment and boosted GDP. However, this only means average wages around the world will increase at a steady rate. A nation only gets left behind if it demands wages higher than the current market price for that labor.
     </p>
     <p>
      When asked about the working condition in sweatshops, proponents say that although wages and working conditions may appear inferior by the standards of developed nations, they are actually improvements over what the people in developing countries had before. It is said that if jobs in such factories did not improve their workers'
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">
       standard of living
      </a>
      , those workers would not have taken the jobs when they appeared. It is also often pointed out that, unlike in the industrialized world, the sweatshops are not replacing high-paying jobs. Rather, sweatshops offer an improvement over
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence_farming" title="Subsistence farming">
       subsistence farming
      </a>
      and other back-breaking tasks, or even prostitution, trash picking, or
      <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">
       starvation
      </a>
      by unemployment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyerson_37-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-meyerson-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sweatshops, not only offer better jobs then what are available in the local communities but the wages that the workers receive lead to a better standard of living for the workers and their families. Raveena Aulkah a journalist for Mail Online News went undercover as a sweatshop worker and documented her experience. One of her main takeaways was that even though working conditions were not optimal the families could now afford "goats, schooling, and clothing for their families".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The absence of the work opportunities provided by sweatshops can quickly lead to malnourishment or starvation. After the
      <a href="/wiki/Child_Labor_Deterrence_Act" title="Child Labor Deterrence Act">
       Child Labor Deterrence Act
      </a>
      was introduced in the US, an estimated 50,000 children were dismissed from their garment industry jobs in Asia, leaving many to resort to jobs such as "stone-crushing, street hustling, and prostitution."
      <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">
       UNICEF
      </a>
      's 1997
      <i>
       State of the World's Children
      </i>
      study found these alternative jobs "more hazardous and exploitative than garment production."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unicef_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-unicef-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As Nobel prize-winning economist
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Krugman" title="Paul Krugman">
       Paul Krugman
      </a>
      states in a 1997 article for Slate, "as manufacturing grows in poor countries, it creates a ripple effect that benefits ordinary people: 'The pressure on the land becomes less intense, so rural wages rise; the pool of unemployed urban dwellers always anxious for work shrinks, so factories start to compete with each other for workers, and urban wages also begin to rise.' In time average wages creep up to a level comparable to minimum-wage jobs in the United States."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Writer
      <a href="/wiki/Johan_Norberg" title="Johan Norberg">
       Johan Norberg
      </a>
      , a proponent of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_economics" title="Market economics">
       market economics
      </a>
      , points out an irony:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       [Sweatshop critics] say that we shouldn't buy from countries like Vietnam because of its labor standards, they've got it all wrong. They're saying: "Look, you are too poor to trade with us. And that means that we won't trade with you. We won't buy your goods until you're as rich as we are." That's totally backwards. These countries won't get rich without being able to export goods.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Heavy-handed responses to reports of child labor and worker rights abuses such as widespread boycotts can be counterproductive if the net effect is simply to eliminate contracts with suppliers rather than to reform their employment practices. A 2005 article in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Science_Monitor" title="Christian Science Monitor">
        Christian Science Monitor
       </a>
      </i>
      states, "For example, in Honduras, the site of the infamous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kathy_Lee_Gifford" title="Kathy Lee Gifford">
       Kathy Lee Gifford
      </a>
      sweatshop scandal, the average apparel worker earns $13.10 per day, yet 44 percent of the country's population lives on less than $2 per day... In Cambodia, Haiti, Nicaragua, and Honduras, the average wage paid by a firm accused of being a sweatshop is more than double the average income in that country's economy."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On three documented occasions during the 1990s, anti-sweatshop activists in rich countries have apparently caused increases in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_prostitution" title="Child prostitution">
       child prostitution
      </a>
      in poor countries. In Bangladesh, there was a closure of several sweatshops which had been run by a German company, and as a result, thousands of Bangladeshi children who had been working in those sweatshops ended up working as prostitutes, turning to crime, or starving to death. In Pakistan, several sweatshops, including ones run by
      <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">
       Nike
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Reebok" title="Reebok">
       Reebok
      </a>
      , and other corporations, were closed, which caused those Pakistani children to turn to prostitution. In Nepal, a carpet manufacturing company closed several sweatshops, resulting in thousands of Nepalese girls turning to prostitution.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A 1996 study of corporate codes of conduct in the apparel industry by the U.S. Department of Labor has concluded that corporate codes of conduct that monitor labor norms in the apparel industry, rather than boycott or eliminate contracts upon the discovery of violations of internationally recognized labor norms, are a more effective way to eliminate child labor and the exploitation of children, provided they provide for effective monitoring that includes the participation of workers and their knowledge of the standards to which their employers are subject.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Arguably, the United States underwent a similar process during its own industrialization where child labor and the suppression of worker organizations were prevalent. According to an article in Gale Opposing Viewpoints in Context, sweatshops became prevalent in the United States during the Industrial Revolution. Although the working conditions and wages in these factories were very poor, as new jobs in factories began to appear, people left the hard life of farming to work in these factories, and the agricultural nature of the economy shifted into a manufacturing one because of this industrialization. However, during this new industrialized economy, the labor movement drove the rise in the average level of income as factory workers began to demand better wages and working conditions. Through much struggle, sufficient wealth was created and a large middle class began to emerge. Workers and advocates were able to achieve basic rights for workers, which included the right to form unions, and negotiate terms such as wages, overtime pay, health insurance, and retirement pensions; and eventually they were also able to attain legal protections such as minimum wage standards, and discrimination and sexual abuse protections. Furthermore, Congress set forth to ensure a minimum set of safety standards were followed in workplaces by passing the Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA) in 1970. These developments were able to improve working environments for Americans but it was through sweatshops that the economy grew and people were able to accumulate wealth and move out of poverty.
     </p>
     <p>
      In contrast, similar efforts in developing nations have not produced the same results because of corruption and a lack of democracy in communist nations such as China and Vietnam, because of worker intimidation and outright violence and murder in Latin America, and corruption throughout the developing world which prevent the creation of similar legal protections for workers in these countries, as numerous studies by the International Labor Organization have shown.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corporations_and_Worker.27s_Rights_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Corporations_and_Worker.27s_Rights-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nonetheless, a boycott approach to protesting these conditions is likely to hurt workers willing to accept employment even under poor working conditions, as a loss of employment would result in a comparatively worse level of poverty. According to a November 2001
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      article, in the previous two months, 100,000 sweatshop workers in Bangladesh had been put off work. The workers petitioned their government to lobby the U.S. government to repeal its trade barriers on their behalf to retain their jobs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Defenders of sweatshops cite Hong Kong, Singapore, South Korea, and Taiwan as recent examples of countries that benefited from having sweatshops.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It should be noted, however, that in these countries, legislative and regulatory frameworks to protect and promote labor rights and the rights of workers against unsafe and exploitative working conditions exist, and studies have shown no systematic relationship between labor rights, such as collective bargaining and the freedom of association, and national economic growth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Sweatshop-free" title="Sweatshop-free">
       Sweatshop-free
      </a>
      is a term created by the fashion brand
      <a href="/wiki/American_Apparel" title="American Apparel">
       American Apparel
      </a>
      which means coercion-free, fair-compensation for the garment workers who manufacture their products.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaworkers_51-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-aaworkers-51">
        <span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      American Apparel claims its employees earn on average double the federal minimum wage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaworkers_51-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-aaworkers-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They receive a number of employee benefits, from health insurance to subsidized transportation and meals, and have access to an onsite medical clinic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaworkers_51-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-aaworkers-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has been heavily featured in the company's advertisements for nearly a decade and become a common term in the garment industry.
