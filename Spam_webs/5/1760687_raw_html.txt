     <p>
      <b>
       Kart racing
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       karting
      </b>
      is a variant of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-wheel" title="Open-wheel">
       open-wheel
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Motorsport" title="Motorsport">
       motorsport
      </a>
      with small, open, four-wheeled vehicles called
      <b>
       karts
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       go-karts
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       gearbox/shifter karts
      </b>
      depending on the design. They are usually raced on scaled-down
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kart_circuits" title="Kart circuits">
       circuits
      </a>
      . Karting is commonly perceived as the stepping stone to the higher ranks of motorsports.
     </p>
     <p>
      Karts vary widely in speed and some (known as
      <a href="/wiki/Superkart" title="Superkart">
       Superkarts
      </a>
      ) can reach speeds exceeding 260 kilometres per hour (160mph),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superkart_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Superkart-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while amusement park
      <a href="/wiki/Go-kart" title="Go-kart">
       go-karts
      </a>
      intended for the general public may be limited to speeds of no more than 25 kilometres per hour (16mph).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001,_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="608" data-file-width="800" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001%2C_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001%2C_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001%2C_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001%2C_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001%2C_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001%2C_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-B1007-0016-001,_5._Berliner_K-Wagen-Rennen.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Kart racing in the streets of Berlin, DDR in 1963
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      American
      <a href="/wiki/Art_Ingels" title="Art Ingels">
       Art Ingels
      </a>
      is generally accepted to be the father of karting. A veteran hot rodder and a race car builder at
      <a href="/wiki/Kurtis_Kraft" title="Kurtis Kraft">
       Kurtis Kraft
      </a>
      , he built the first kart in
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">
       Southern California
      </a>
      in 1956.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Instantly popular, Karting rapidly spread to other countries,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and currently has a large following in Europe.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first kart manufacturer was an American company, Go Kart Manufacturing Co. (1958). In 1959,
      <a href="/wiki/McCulloch_Motors_Corporation" title="McCulloch Motors Corporation">
       McCulloch
      </a>
      was the first company to produce engines for karts. Its first engine, the McCulloch MC-10,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      was an adapted
      <a href="/wiki/Chainsaw" title="Chainsaw">
       chainsaw
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Two-stroke_engine" title="Two-stroke engine">
       2-stroke
      </a>
      engine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, in the 1960s,
      <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">
       motorcycle
      </a>
      engines were also adapted for kart use, before dedicated manufacturers, especially in Italy (
      <a href="/wiki/Italian_American_Motor_Engineering" title="Italian American Motor Engineering">
       IAME
      </a>
      ), started to build engines for the sport.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Components">
       Components
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chassis">
       Chassis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chassis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Chassis" title="Chassis">
       chassis
      </a>
      are made of steel tubing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chassis_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-chassis-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is no
      <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(vehicle)" title="Suspension (vehicle)">
       suspension
      </a>
      , therefore chassis have to be flexible enough to work as a suspension and stiff enough not to break or give way on a turn. Kart chassis are classified in the USA as 'Open', 'Caged', 'Straight' or 'Offset'. All
      <a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_de_Karting" title="Commission Internationale de Karting">
       Commission Internationale de Karting
      </a>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fdration Internationale de l'Automobile">
       Fdration Internationale de l'Automobile
      </a>
      or CIK-FIA approved chassis are 'Straight' and 'Open'.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Open karts have no roll cage.
      </li>
      <li>
       Caged karts have a
       <a href="/wiki/Roll_cage" title="Roll cage">
        roll cage
       </a>
       surrounding the driver; they are mostly used on dirt tracks.
      </li>
      <li>
       In Straight chassis the driver sits in the center. Straight chassis are used for
       <a href="/wiki/Kart_racing#Sprint" title="Kart racing">
        sprint racing
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       In Offset chassis the driver sits on the left side. Offset chassis are used for left-turn-only
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oval_track" title="Oval track">
        speedway
       </a>
       racing.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The stiffness of the chassis enables different handling characteristics for different circumstances. Typically, for dry conditions a stiffer chassis is preferable, while in wet or other poor traction conditions, a more flexible chassis may work better. The best chassis allow for stiffening bars at the rear, front and side to be added or removed according to race conditions.
     </p>
     <p>
      Braking is achieved by a
      <a href="/wiki/Disc_brake" title="Disc brake">
       disc brake
      </a>
      mounted on the rear axle. Front disc brakes are used in most shifter kart classes and are increasingly popular in other classes; however, certain classes do not allow them. Shifter karts have dual master cylinders, one for the front and one for the rear and are adjustable to allow for front/ rear bias changes.
     </p>
     <p>
      Professionally raced karts typically weigh 165 to 175lb (75 to 79kg), complete without driver.
      <a href="/wiki/Avanti_Kart" title="Avanti Kart">
       Avanti
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Kart" title="Tony Kart">
       Tony Kart
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jarno_Trulli" title="Jarno Trulli">
       Trulli
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Birel" title="Birel">
       Birel
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/CRG_(kart_manufacturer)" title="CRG (kart manufacturer)">
       CRG
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gillard_(kart_manufacturer)" title="Gillard (kart manufacturer)">
       Gillard
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Intrepid_Kart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Intrepid Kart (page does not exist)">
       Intrepid
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kosmic_Kart" title="Kosmic Kart">
       Kosmic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Alex_Zanardi" title="Alex Zanardi">
       Zanardi
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Alonso" title="Fernando Alonso">
       FA Kart
      </a>
      are a few well known examples of the many European manufacturers of race-quality chassis. Emmick, Coyote, Bandit, Shadow, MGM, PRC and
      <a href="/wiki/Margay_Products_Inc." title="Margay Products Inc.">
       Margay
      </a>
      are American companies producing kart chassis.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Engines">
       Engines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Engines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Amusement park
      <a href="/wiki/Go-kart" title="Go-kart">
       go-karts
      </a>
      can be powered by 4-stroke
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">
       engines
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">
       electric motors
      </a>
      , while racing karts use small 2-stroke or 4-stroke engines.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Four-stroke_engine" title="Four-stroke engine">
        4-stroke
       </a>
       engines can be standard air-cooled industrial based engines, sometimes with small modifications, developing from about 5 to 20hp.
       <a href="/wiki/Briggs_%26_Stratton" title="Briggs &amp; Stratton">
        Briggs &amp; Stratton
       </a>
       , Tecumseh, Kohler, Robin, and
       <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">
        Honda
       </a>
       are manufacturers of such engines. They are adequate for racing and fun kart applications. There are also more powerful four-stroke engines available from manufacturers like
       <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">
        Yamaha
       </a>
       , TKM,
       <a href="/wiki/Swissauto" title="Swissauto">
        Swissauto
       </a>
       or Aixro (
       <a href="/wiki/Wankel_engine" title="Wankel engine">
        Wankel engine
       </a>
       ) offering from 15hp up to 48hp. They run to and around 11,000 rpm, and are manufactured specifically for karting. Those are used in some National Championship classes like the two-strokes.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Two-stroke_engine" title="Two-stroke engine">
        2-stroke
       </a>
       kart engines are developed and built by dedicated manufacturers. WTP, Comer,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_American_Motor_Engineering" title="Italian American Motor Engineering">
        IAME
       </a>
       (Parilla, Komet), TM, Vortex, Titan, REFO, TKM,
       <a href="/wiki/PRD_Fireball" title="PRD Fireball">
        PRD
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">
        Yamaha
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rotax" title="Rotax">
        Rotax
       </a>
       are manufacturers of such engines. These can develop from about 8hp for a single-cylinder 60 cc unit (MiniROK by Vortex) to over 90hp for a twin 250 cc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superkart_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Superkart-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Today, the most popular categories worldwide are those using the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Touch-and-go_(karting)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Touch-and-go (karting) (page does not exist)">
        TaG
       </a>
       125 cc units. The recent 125 cc
       <a href="/wiki/KF1" title="KF1">
        KF1
       </a>
       engines are electronically limited at 16,000 rpm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Most are water-cooled today; however, previously air-cooled engines dominated the sport.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission">
       Transmission
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transmission">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Karts do not have a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_(mechanics)" title="Differential (mechanics)">
       differential
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chassis_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-chassis-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The lack of a differential means that one rear tire must slide while cornering; this is achieved by designing the chassis so that the inside rear tire lifts up slightly when the kart turns the corner. This allows the tire to lose some of its grip and slide or lift off the ground completely.
     </p>
     <p>
      Power is transmitted from the engine to the rear axle by a chain. Both engine and axle
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sprockets" title="Sprockets">
       sprockets
      </a>
      are removable; their ratio must be adapted to the track configuration in order to get the most from the engine.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the early days, karts were direct drive only (requiring push starts), but the inconvenience of that setup soon led to the
      <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_clutch" title="Centrifugal clutch">
       centrifugal clutch
      </a>
      for the club level classes. Dry centrifugal clutches are now used in many categories (
      <a href="/wiki/Rotax_Max" title="Rotax Max">
       Rotax Max
      </a>
      is one example) and have become the norm as the top international classes have switched to 125cc clutched engines as of January 2007.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tires">
       Tires
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tires">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Wheels and
      <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">
       tires
      </a>
      are much smaller than those used on a normal car. Rims are made of
      <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">
       magnesium
      </a>
      alloy,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">
       aluminum
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">
       composite materials
      </a>
      . Tires can support cornering forces in excess of 2 g (20m/s), depending on chassis, engine, and motor
      <a href="/wiki/Racing_setup" title="Racing setup">
       setup
      </a>
      . Some car tire manufacturers, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Bridgestone" title="Bridgestone">
       Bridgestone
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dunlop_Tyres" title="Dunlop Tyres">
       Dunlop
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxxis" title="Maxxis">
       Maxxis
      </a>
      , make tires for karts. There are also specific kart tire manufacturers, which include MG, MOJO, and Vega.
     </p>
     <p>
      Similar to other motorsports, kart tires have different types for use appropriate to track conditions:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slick_tire" title="Slick tire">
        Slicks
       </a>
       , for dry track. Slick kart tires come in many different compounds, from very soft (maximum grip) to very hard (amusement and rental karts, less grip but long life span). In international level racing, because the drivers are free to choose their tires
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       and because of the short duration of each round (10 to 20 mn maximum), these are some of the softest tires found in motorsport.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_tire" title="Rain tire">
        Rain tires
       </a>
       , or "wets", for wet weather. They are grooved, made of soft compound, and are narrower than slicks. Not all racing classes allow rain tires.
      </li>
      <li>
       Special, such as spiked tires for icy conditions, or "cuts" for high grip dirt/clay speedways. Cuts are slicks modified with a
       <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">
        lathe
       </a>
       to optimize handling.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Data_acquisition">
       Data acquisition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Data acquisition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As in other motor sports, several data acquisition systems have been developed for kart racing. These systems allow the driver to monitor from a display fixed on the steering wheel some parameters such as RPM, timing of laps (including sectors), number of laps, best lap, cooling system temperature, exhaust gas temperature and sometimes speed or even gear for shifter karts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of those systems are able to record (logging) laps data from the sensors, allowing replay of an entire running session or/and direct download to a personal computer equipped with a data analysis software. More sophisticated systems allow for more information such as lateral and longitudinal acceleration (
      <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">
       g-force
      </a>
      ), throttle position, steering wheel position and brake pressure.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Racing">
       Racing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Racing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2362" data-file-width="3543" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg/220px-Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg/330px-Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg/440px-Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Largada_Stock125_Foto_Claudio_Reis.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Sprint race at Granja Viana, Brazil
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Kart racing is generally accepted as the most economic form of
      <a href="/wiki/Motorsport" title="Motorsport">
       motorsport
      </a>
      available on four wheels. As a free-time activity, it can be performed by almost anybody, and as a motorsport in itself, it is one of the sports regulated by
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fdration Internationale de l'Automobile">
       FIA
      </a>
      (under the name of
      <a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_de_Karting" title="Commission Internationale de Karting">
       CIK
      </a>
      ), permitting licensed racing for anyone from the age of 8 onward.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the USA, there is not as much FIA involvement; instead, many organizations regulate racing, such as the IKF (
      <a href="/wiki/International_Kart_Federation" title="International Kart Federation">
       International Kart Federation
      </a>
      ), WKA (
      <a href="/wiki/World_Karting_Association" title="World Karting Association">
       World Karting Association
      </a>
      ), KART (Karters of America Racing Triad).
     </p>
     <p>
      In the UK, the MSA (
      <a href="/wiki/Motor_Sports_Association" title="Motor Sports Association">
       Motor Sports Association
      </a>
      ) regulates most 'owner driver' Karting. Some associations, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natska" title="Natska">
       NatSKA
      </a>
      (National Schools Karting Association), organize race meetings throughout the country under the authority of the MSA. Various four-stroke 'hire kart' series such as EPEC (European Prokart Endurance Championship)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.brkc.net" rel="nofollow">
       BRKC
      </a>
      (
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.brkc.net" rel="nofollow">
       British Rental Kart Championship
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      fall outside the governance of the MSA. Billed as the UK's first national karting
      <i>
       league,
      </i>
      the Elite Karting League also falls outside of MSA governance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Australia, kart racing is administered by the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Karting_Association" title="Australian Karting Association">
       Australian Karting Association
      </a>
      on the behalf of FIA and
      <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_Australian_Motor_Sport" title="Confederation of Australian Motor Sport">
       CAMS
      </a>
      . There is a manual released every year detailing the various rules and regulations that race meetings and drivers have to follow.
     </p>
     <p>
      Racing classes start at age 7 or 8 (5 in the US with "Kid Karts") and generally run in 3-year age groupings or weight divisions until "senior" status is reached at age 15 or 16, depending on the series.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Racing_formats">
       Racing formats
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Racing formats">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Typically, race formats are one of the following:
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sprint">
       Sprint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sprint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Sprint racing takes place on dedicated kart circuits resembling small road courses, with left and right turns. Tracks range from 1/4 mile (400 metres) to over 1 mile (1,600 metres) in length.
     </p>
     <p>
      The sprint format is a series of short-duration races, normally for a small number of laps, that qualify for a final, with a variety of point scoring calculations to determine the event's overall winner. Typical duration does not normally exceed 15 minutes. Here, speed and successful passing is of the most importance. It normally occurs in the format of three qualifying heats and a final race for trophy positions.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fdration Internationale de l'Automobile">
       FIA
      </a>
      championships, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Karting_World_Championship" title="Karting World Championship">
       Karting World Championship
      </a>
      , take place in this format.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Endurance">
       Endurance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Endurance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enduros.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="730" data-file-width="1041" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Enduros.jpg/220px-Enduros.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Enduros.jpg/330px-Enduros.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Enduros.jpg/440px-Enduros.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enduros.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Pack of Laydown Enduros at
        <a href="/wiki/Daytona_International_Speedway" title="Daytona International Speedway">
         Daytona
        </a>
        , USA
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Endurance races last for an extended period, ranging from 30 minutes up to 24 hours or more, for one or more drivers. In general, consistency, reliability, and pit strategy is of greater importance than all out speed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Called "Enduro" racing in the USA, most
      <a href="/wiki/World_Karting_Association" title="World Karting Association">
       WKA
      </a>
      &amp;
      <a href="/wiki/International_Kart_Federation" title="International Kart Federation">
       IKF
      </a>
      sanctioned events typically last 30 minutes (Sprint Enduro) or 45 minutes (Laydown Enduro) and are run continuous without pit stops. Enduro events are held on full-size road racing circuits that are usually between 1.5 &amp; 4 miles in length.
     </p>
     <p>
      As well as the famous
      <a href="/wiki/24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="24 Hours of Le Mans">
       24 Hours of Le Mans
      </a>
      race for automobiles there is also a 24 hours event for karts which takes place at the kart circuit Alain Prost at
      <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">
       Le Mans
      </a>
      , France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This race has taken place since 1986 and its winners list include four times
      <a href="/wiki/Champ_Car" title="Champ Car">
       Champ Car
      </a>
      champion
      <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Bourdais" title="Sbastien Bourdais">
       Sbastien Bourdais
      </a>
      (in 1996).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Speedway">
       Speedway
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Speedway">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Speedway racing takes place on
      <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">
       asphalt
      </a>
      or clay
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oval_(geometry)" title="Oval (geometry)">
       oval
      </a>
      tracks which are normally between 1/6 mile and 1/4 mile long. Tracks primarily consist of two straights and four left-turn corners, few tracks are symmetric and often the shape parallels that of an egg or a
      <a href="/wiki/Tri-oval" title="Tri-oval">
       tri-oval
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      'Offset' kart chassis have been developed for precise handling and adjustability in left-turn-only racing competition taking place on oval and tri-oval tracks.
     </p>
     <p>
      Speedway kart races range in length from 4 laps for a trophy dash, to 20 laps for a main event.
     </p>
     <p>
      The two chief racing formats used in dirt speedway karting are heat races and timed laps qualification:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Kart_Federation" title="International Kart Federation">
        International Kart Federation
       </a>
       (IKF) runs a racing format of two 10 lap heats followed by a 20 lap final. Finishing positions in the two heat races are used to calculate the starting position in the feature race.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/World_Karting_Association" title="World Karting Association">
        World Karting Association
       </a>
       (WKA) uses time qualifying. Karts equipped with transponders are sent out onto the track in groups of 5 or less to try to achieve the fastest lap time. Positions for the 20 laps feature event are determined by qualifying time.
      </li>
      <li>
       The American Kart Racing Association (AKRA)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       uses group transponder qualifying to calculate starting positions for 20-lap features.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Racing_categories">
       Racing categories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Racing categories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There are many different classes or formula in karting.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International">
       International
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: International">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">

     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Commission_Internationale_de_Karting" title="Commission Internationale de Karting">
       CIK-FIA
      </a>
      sanctions international championships in
      <a href="/wiki/KF1" title="KF1">
       KF1
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/KF2" title="KF2">
       KF2
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/KF3" title="KF3">
       KF3
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/KZ1_(karting)" title="KZ1 (karting)">
       KZ1
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/KZ2" title="KZ2">
       KZ2
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Superkart" title="Superkart">
       Superkart
      </a>
      . These are regarded as the top level classes of karting and are also raced in national championships worldwide. The
      <a href="/wiki/Karting_World_Championship" title="Karting World Championship">
       World Championship
      </a>
      is decided here. The current 2010 World Champion is
      <a href="/wiki/Nyck_de_Vries" title="Nyck de Vries">
       Nyck de Vries
      </a>
      from the Netherlands.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      CIK-FIA categories:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       KF1 (the top level), KF2, KF3, and KF4 (so-called "basic" category). All are using the same water-cooled no-gearbox 125 cc "long life" two-stroke engines with starter and clutch, each with different technical specifications (mufflers, air boxes, carburetor, rev limit etc.).
      </li>
      <li>
       KZ1 and KZ2, both 125 cc gearbox categories.
      </li>
      <li>
       Superkart, a 250 cc gearbox category.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Non CIK-FIA categories: The '
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.kartworldchampionship.com" rel="nofollow">
       Kart World Championship
      </a>
      ' (or KWC) as opposed to the FIA's 'Kart
      <b>
       ing
      </b>
      World Championship' uses 4-stroke rental karts and travels to a different country each year.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="National">
       National
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: National">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In the
      <b>
       UK
      </b>
      , the most celebrated karting series is the National karting series, also known as
      <a href="/wiki/Super_1_National_Kart_Championships" title="Super 1 National Kart Championships">
       Super One
      </a>
      . There are three types of
      <a href="/wiki/Super_1_National_Kart_Championships" title="Super 1 National Kart Championships">
       Super One
      </a>
      championships:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       MSA series:
       <a href="/wiki/KF2" title="KF2">
        KF2
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/KF3" title="KF3">
        KF3
       </a>
       , Formula KGP, Super Cadet and Cadet
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Rotax_Max" title="Rotax Max">
        Rotax
       </a>
       series: Minimax, Junior Max, Senior Max, Senior Max 177
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/TKM_(Karting)" title="TKM (Karting)">
        TKM
       </a>
       series: Formula Junior TKM, Formula TKM Extreme, TKM Senior 4-stroke and since 2006, Honda Cadet
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Other UK National Championships include:
      <a href="/wiki/Birel" title="Birel">
       Birel
      </a>
      Easykart (MSA Approved) series: Cadet 60cc, Junior 100cc, Senior Light 125cc, Senior Heavy 125cc
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.brkc.net" rel="nofollow">
       BRKC
      </a>
      is the UK's rental karting National championship, and the UK's official feeder series to the rental
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.kartworldchampionship.com" rel="nofollow">
       Kart World Championship
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natska" title="Natska">
       NatSKA
      </a>
      is a budget karting association set up for schools and youth groups in the UK, with 13 classes.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <b>
       United States
      </b>
      , Dirt oval classes
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      (which often use
      <a href="/wiki/Briggs_%26_Stratton" title="Briggs &amp; Stratton">
       Briggs &amp; Stratton
      </a>
      industrial engines) are prominent in the Southeast and Midwest.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In the West, European style sprint racing is much more common.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In particular, 125cc shifter karts using Honda CR125 power units have gained tremendous popularity in recent years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <b>
       Australia
      </b>
      , classes include Cadet (previously called Midget), Rookie, Junior National Pro, Junior National, Junior Clubman, Junior Rotax (Jmax), KF3, Senior National, Senior Rotax, Senior Clubman, Senior TAG (Restricted and Unrestricted). Most classes run a light and heavy category (with some running super heavy).
     </p>
     <p>
      Many people race worldwide in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spec_series" title="Spec series">
       Spec series
      </a>
      such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rotax_Max" title="Rotax Max">
       Rotax Max
      </a>
      (a
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Touch-and-go_(karting)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Touch-and-go (karting) (page does not exist)">
       TaG
      </a>
      class) or those using the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KT100" title="KT100">
       Yamaha KT100
      </a>
      engine.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Racing_licenses">
       Racing licenses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Racing licenses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As in other disciplines in motorsports, a license is required to drive a racing kart on a circuit or enter competitions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The licenses, issued by governing bodies, are provided by clubs or associations. Most of the time, but not always, a basic insurance coverage is included in the licence annual fee. In some countries, such as
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      , regulations require the drivers to pass a medical exam each year.
     </p>
     <p>
      License classes differ between countries according to age groups or levels. Most of the time a Practice License can be easily obtained, while a Racing License might require a capability assessment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Driver_equipment">
       Driver equipment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Driver equipment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      For their safety, kart drivers are required to wear proper equipment:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Full-face
       <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">
        helmet
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Snell_Memorial_Foundation" title="Snell Memorial Foundation">
        Snell
       </a>
       SA2005 or K2005 certification is required for racing, K2005 is same as SA2005 without fire resistance)
      </li>
      <li>
       Driving suit (abrasion resistant overalls with at least one
       <a href="/wiki/Cordura" title="Cordura">
        Cordura
       </a>
       external layer, CIK-FIA Level 2 homologation for top level racing)
      </li>
      <li>
       Gloves
      </li>
      <li>
       Driving boots (ankles must be covered)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Rib protector and neck brace, although highly recommended, are optional in most countries. None of the above need to be made of fire retardant material. Superkart drivers are required to wear leather overalls, similar to those used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorcycle_safety_clothing" title="Motorcycle safety clothing">
       motorcycling
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Karting_as_a_learning_tool">
       Karting as a learning tool
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kart_racing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Karting as a learning tool">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Kart racing is usually used as a low-cost and relatively safe way to introduce drivers to motor racing. Many
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">
       Formula One
      </a>
      drivers grew up racing karts, most prominent among them,
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Drivers' Champions">
       World Champions
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">
       Ayrton Senna
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">
       Michael Schumacher
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">
       Alain Prost
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Alonso" title="Fernando Alonso">
       Fernando Alonso
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kimi_R%C3%A4ikk%C3%B6nen" title="Kimi Rikknen">
       Kimi Rikknen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jenson_Button" title="Jenson Button">
       Jenson Button
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Hamilton" title="Lewis Hamilton">
       Lewis Hamilton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Vettel" title="Sebastian Vettel">
       Sebastian Vettel
      </a>
      . Many
      <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">
       NASCAR
      </a>
      drivers also got their start in racing from karts, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Darrell_Waltrip" title="Darrell Waltrip">
       Darrell Waltrip
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Danica_Patrick" title="Danica Patrick">
       Danica Patrick
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Speed" title="Lake Speed">
       Lake Speed
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Rudd" title="Ricky Rudd">
       Ricky Rudd
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Juan_Pablo_Montoya" title="Juan Pablo Montoya">
       Juan Pablo Montoya
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Stewart" title="Tony Stewart">
       Tony Stewart
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Gordon" title="Jeff Gordon">
       Jeff Gordon
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2009, in anticipation to a possible return to F1 with
      <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">
       Ferrari
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">
       Formula One
      </a>
      world champion
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">
       Michael Schumacher
      </a>
      did some preparation driving a kart in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lonato" title="Lonato">
       Lonato
      </a>
      , Italy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Schumacher also raced at the SKUSA SuperNationals, an event taking place each year in
      <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Valley" title="Las Vegas Valley">
       Las Vegas
      </a>
      , along with F1 drivers
      <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Buemi" title="Sbastien Buemi">
       Sbastien Buemi
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet,_Jr." title="Nelson Piquet, Jr.">
       Nelson Piquet, Jr.
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Massa" title="Felipe Massa">
       Felipe Massa
      </a>
      also used karting in September 2009 to test his condition in Brazil, two months after his
      <a href="/wiki/2009_Hungarian_Grand_Prix" title="2009 Hungarian Grand Prix">
       Hungarian Grand Prix
      </a>
      accident during qualifying.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
