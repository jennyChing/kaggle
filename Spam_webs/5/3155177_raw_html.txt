    <h1>
     Liberal candidate muzzled after calling for abortion laws to be rolled back
    </h1>
    <p class="publishing">
     Alison Caldwell reported this story on
     <a href="/pm/archives.html">
      Tuesday, November 18, 2014
     </a>
     18:42:00
    </p>
    <div class="storyplayerpage">
     <a href="http://mpegmedia.abc.net.au/news/audio/pm/201411/20141118-pm09-vicpolcandidates.mp3" id="fullstoryplayer" title="click to play MP3 or right click to save it to your desktop">
      Listen to MP3 of this story (
      <em>
      </em>
      minutes)
     </a>
     <p class="alternateaudio">
      |
      <a href="http://mpegmedia.abc.net.au/news/audio/pm/201411/20141118-pm09-vicpolcandidates.mp3" title="click to play MP3 or right click to save it to your desktop">
       MP3 download
      </a>
     </p>
    </div>
    <div id="article">
     <!-- &lt;p class="intro"&gt;In Victoria, the Liberal candidate in a key marginal seat in the state's west has been muzzled by his party after saying abortion laws should be rolled back. The new seat of Buninyong is held by Labor by just 1.6 per cent and the Coalition is determined to win it back. The pro-choice Nationals candidate is now refusing to preference her Liberal counterpart. The Premier denies there's any tension between the Coalition partners.&lt;/p&gt; -->
     <p class="body">
      MARK COLVIN: The Liberal Party in Victoria has muzzled its candidate in a key marginal seat after he said abortion laws should be rolled back.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Labor theoretically holds the new seat of Buninyong by just 1.6 per cent and the Coalition's determined to get it back.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      But it's a three cornered contest, and the Nationals candidate has reacted to the comments by refusing to preference her Liberal counterpart.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      The Premier Denis Napthine is calling it a storm in a teacup.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Alison Caldwell reports.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: Pre-poll voting has already begun with the Victorian state election is just 11 days away, and now a split has appeared in the Liberal/National Coalition between the candidates in the new seat of Buninyong, north-west of Melbourne.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      A difference of opinion over abortion laws emerged last Friday at a forum organised by the Australian Christian Lobby in Ballarat.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      The Liberal candidate Ben Taylor had this to say.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      BEN TAYLOR: I think in Victoria we've got the, I think you could say for abortion laws, we've probably gone a little bit too far when you start talking about post-20-week abortions through to birth, and that late term abortion is quite, when you hear statistics and see what it is, it's pretty horrific and what can happen.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      I look at a moral issue, when you look at a room in Ballarat that you're saving a baby's life that's premature and then another room in Ballarat you're terminating that life, I think that's very questionable.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: The seat is held by Labor's Geoff Howard with a margin of just 1.6 per cent. He supports Victoria's abortion laws as they are and was heckled for saying so.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      GEOFF HOWARD: I would want to see that mothers are always given the best support they can be. If they find that they are pregnant
      <br/>
      <br/>
      AUDIENCE MEMBER: What about the child?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      (Audience member is shushed by crowd)
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: Yesterday Sonia Smith announced she'd be running an open ticket, leaving the preferences up to voters.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      Speaking to ABC Local Radio's Prue Bentley in Ballarat this morning, the Liberal's Ben Taylor was asked to clarify his views on abortion.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      BEN TAYLOR: My concern is around close to birth, from actually 20 weeks to birth. It's late term abortion is the concern.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      PRUE BENTLEY: Did you have any idea that you had such a different perspective on abortion views?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      BEN TAYLOR: Ah, no I didn't. For me, I'm not worried about what effect this has on this campaign. For me, it's about being in front of as many people as I can.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: The Nationals' Sonia Smith maintains she wont be preferencing her Coalition partner, the Liberals Ben Taylor, at next week's election.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      SONIA SMITH: I encourage people to vote for me; I also encourage people to preference who you would like to preference. I think that's really important.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      On issues such as this, and there are other issues - rights for same sex couples, or the climate - these are issues that are really important and it's critical that people know the views of the candidates. You may start to see now other candidates whose views aren't in step with the Coalition start to drop off a little bit and you probably won't hear too much from them now.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: We won't hear too much from Ben Taylor?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      SONIA SMITH: Well, it's a pity because people have a right to know.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: And the Nationals? I mean as I understand it, the Nationals state president, the Nationals state director, are the ones who decide where preferences go.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      SONIA SMITH: The reality is all politics is local. So I have a volunteer campaign, I have around 105 volunteers who will be helping me staff the booths on election day. These people are part of a grass roots community campaign that I've been running full time since the 19th of January. These are not paid members.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: Are you concerned a bit that the party maybe hasn't learnt from what happened in Indi, hasn't learnt from what's happened at Frankston with the maverick Geoff Shaw?
      <br/>
      <br/>
      SONIA SMITH: I am. For me, I've been running a grass roots campaign.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: Speaking in Ballarat this morning, Premier Denis Napthine denied the abortion issue is causing problems between the two parties.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      DENIS NAPTHINE: There is no underlying tension. I think it's a bit of a storm in a tea cup. The National Party will be preferencing the Liberal Party and the Liberal Party will be preferencing the National Party in all three-cornered contests.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      ALISON CALDWELL: The Liberal Party has spent a huge amount of time and money on the seat, hoping to counteract possible losses in Melbourne.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      It's understood Ben Taylor has been advised not to do any more interviews.
      <br/>
      <br/>
      MARK COLVIN: Alison Caldwell.
     </p>
    </div>
