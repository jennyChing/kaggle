         <a href="http://www.dailygalaxy.com/my_weblog/2008/01/lost-egyptian-c.html">
          Lost Egyptian City Reveals Horrors of Lives of Pharaoh's Builders
         </a>
        </h3>
        <div class="entry-content">
         <div class="entry-body">
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.dailygalaxy.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/01/28/egy005_2.jpg">
            <img alt="Egy005_2" border="0" height="260" src="http://www.dailygalaxy.com/my_weblog/images/2008/01/28/egy005_2.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="Egy005_2" width="245"/>
           </a>
           Evidence of the brutal lives endured by some ancient Egyptians to build the monuments of the Pharaohs has been uncovered by archaeologists at Amarna, a new capital built on the orders of the Pharaoh Akhenaten, 3,500 years ago.
          </p>
          <p>
           Archaeologists from a British-based team made a breakthrough when they
found human bones in the desert, which had been washed out by floods.
          </p>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </div>
         <a id="more">
         </a>
         <div class="entry-more" style="float:left">
          <p>
           Skeletal remains from a lost city in the middle of Egypt suggest many
ordinary people died in their teenage years and lived a punishing
lifestyle with common spinal injuries, poor nutrition and stunted
growth. The temples and palaces required thousands of large stone
blocks.
Working in summer temperatures of 40C (104F), the workers would have
had to chisel these out of the rock and transport them 1.5 miles (2.5
km) from the quarries to the city.
          </p>
          <p>
           The bone remains show many workers suffered spinal and other injuries.
"These people were working very hard at very young ages, carrying heavy
loads," says Professor Jerry Rose, of the University of Arkansas,
whose anthropological team has been analysing the Amarna bones. "The
incidence of youthful death amongst the Amarna population was
shockingly high by any standard." Not many lived beyond 35. Two-thirds
were dead by 20.
          </p>
          <p>
           But even this backbreaking schedule may not be enough to explain the extreme death pattern at Amarna.
          </p>
          <p>
           Even Akhanaten's son, Tutankhamen, died aged just 20; and
archaeologists are now beginning to believe that there might also have
been an epidemic here.
          </p>
          <p>
           This corroborates the historical records of Egypt's principal enemy,
the Hittites, which tell of the devastation of an epidemic caught from
Egyptians captured in battle around the time of Tutankhamen's reign. It
appears this epidemic may also have been the final blow to the people
of Amarna.
          </p>
          <p>
           Hieroglyphs written at the time record that the Pharaoh, who was father
of Tutankhamun, was driven to create a new city in honor of the god,
the Aten, with elaborate temples, palaces and tombs. Along with his
wife Nefertiti, he abandoned the capital Thebes, leaving the old gods
and their priests behind and marched his people 200 miles (320km) north
to an inhospitable desert plain beside the River Nile.
          </p>
          <p>
           Painted murals found in the tombs of high officials from the time show
offering-tables piled high with food. But the bones of the ordinary
people who lived in the city reveal a different picture."The skeletons that we see are certainly not participating in that form
of life," says Rose.
          </p>
          <p>
           The city, housing up to 50,000 people, was built in 15 years; but
within a few years of the Pharaoh's death, the city was abandoned, left
to the winds and sands of the Egyptian deserts.
          </p>
          <p>
           Posted by Casey Kazan.
          </p>
