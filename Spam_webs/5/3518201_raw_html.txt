     <h2 class="blog-name text-center">
      <a href="/articles?blog=strange-maps">
       Strange Maps
      </a>
     </h2>
     <h1 class="text-center">
      607 - That Monkey Don't Swim: Maps, Sex and Violence
     </h1>
     <p class="text-center byline">
      by
      <span class="author">
       <a href="/users/frankjacobs">
        Frank Jacobs
       </a>
      </span>
     </p>
     <ul class="side-info">
      <li>
       <ul class="meta-data horizontal">
        <li class="since">
         about 2 years ago
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <ul class="social-sharing vertical">
        <li>

         <a onclick="share.twitter('http%3A%2F%2Fbigthink.com%2Fstrange-maps%2F607-that-monkey-dont-swim-maps-sex-and-violence', '607%20-%20That%20Monkey%20Don%27t%20Swim%3A%20Maps%2C%20Sex%20and%20Violence', '');">
          <i class="fa fa-twitter">

         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a onclick="share.gplus('http%3A%2F%2Fbigthink.com%2Fstrange-maps%2F607-that-monkey-dont-swim-maps-sex-and-violence');">

         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a onclick="share.linkedin('http%3A%2F%2Fbigthink.com%2Fstrange-maps%2F607-that-monkey-dont-swim-maps-sex-and-violence', '607%20-%20That%20Monkey%20Don%27t%20Swim%3A%20Maps%2C%20Sex%20and%20Violence', '');">

     <p class="p1">
      I had you at
      <em>
       maps
      </em>
      . Obviously. Because you too are puzzled by this peculiar cartographic specimen, showing the spatial distribution of certain social traits across Central Africa.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      The purple blob in the middle is a matriarchal zone, where females rule, and social stress tends to be relieved through sex. The orange, green and yellow areas arching around the blob are patriarchies. Here, men have the upper hand, and conflicts are settled mainly by violent means.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      We're not talking humans here, but if both behavioural patterns sound all too familiar - the former as a naive, outdated hippie utopia [1], the latter as the cynical, and all too current underpinning to much of what ails the world - it's because they are exhibited by humanity's two closest living relatives.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      Each colour on the map denotes the habitat of a particular type of hominid [2]. The purple zone is the domain of the bonobo (
      <em>
       Pan paniscus
      </em>
      ), the others are inhabited by varieties of chimpanzee (
      <em>
       Pan troglodytes
      </em>
      ) [3]. Both ape species are closely related - so closely, in fact, that the bonobo was only recognised as a separate species in 1928 [4].
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/123522713@N06/14710954257" title="bonobo-distribution by Frank Jacobs, on Flickr">
       <img alt="bonobo-distribution" src="https://farm6.staticflickr.com/5572/14710954257_da13268136.jpg" width="580"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      <span style="font-size: 10px;">
       Which is not surprising. Bonobos and chimps look very similar, the former being slightly smaller versions of the latter. Still sharing 99.6% of their DNA, bonobos and chimps started developing along separate evolutionary paths about 1 million years ago, when the Congo River divided
      </span>
      <em style="font-size: 10px;">
       Pan
      </em>
      <span style="font-size: 10px;">
       's ancestral homeland.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="p2">
      As great apes are notoriously reluctant swimmers [5], all contact between the populations on either side of the river ceased. Over time, separate geographies led to different societies. The
      <em>
       Pan
      </em>
      population south of the river developed into the bonobo [6], those on the Congo's north bank became chimpanzees. The southern border of the bonobo habitat is formed by the Kasai and Sankuru rivers.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      Some researchers speculate that bonobos developed their more consensual approach to conflict resolution because their habitat had better food resources. Plus, they didn't have to compete for them with gorillas, who live only on the right bank of the Congo. Both factors would explain why bonobos experience less need for confrontation.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      But others wonder whether the reverse might not have been the case. Perhaps it was the chimpanzee who adopted a more violence-based resource management policy, in reaction to scarcer food supplies. Necessity being the mother of invention, that scarcity might also explain why chimps adopted tools, which bonobos as yet haven't done.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      Did chimpanzees become more violent, or bonobos more promiscuous than their
      <em>
       Pan
      </em>
      ancestor? That might sound like a simian variant of the chicken-and-egg question. But it is more relevant to who we are as humans, because of our close relation to the Cain and Abel of the ape world.
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      <em>
       Homo sapiens
      </em>
      and
      <em>
       Pan
      </em>
      shared an ancestor until about 5 million years ago, and humans still share about 98,7% of their DNA with both bonobos and chimpanzees. However, these percentages don't entirely overlap: about 1,6% of our DNA we share only with bonobos, not with chimps. And a similar percentage that we share only with chimps, not with bonobos [7].
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      These genetic differences could eventually help explain why bonobos are more playful than chimpanzees, and why humans are more cerebral than either. And perhaps even help answer the ultimate question, relevant to the deep origins of human nature: Did our common ancestor behave more like a bonobo, like a chimp or like yet something else?
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      This map doesn't answer that question, but it does offer us a tantalising glimpse in the social structure of our closest cousins - and our own: divergent behaviour can be determined by sheer geographic accident.
     </p>
     <p class="p2">
      In ape society, those cultural differences are neatly defined by Central Africa's big rivers. Human society is more complex - or at least more difficult. Even though we are a single species, we often find a Congo River to divide us from our fellow humans.
     </p>
     <p class="p2">
      And if we don't we go look for one.
      <span style="font-size: 10px;">
       Geography is one of the firm foundations of our everyday human prejudices:
       <em>
        Northerners are stern, hardworking folk, Southerners are jovial layabouts; Western society is decadent and atomised, Eastern society is callously collectivist.
       </em>
       And so on.
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="p2">
      But we don't have to stare at each other across the fast-flowing water of our mutual incomprehension until a million years turn us into separate species. Fortunately, we are the one ape that can teach itself to swim. If we can overcome our fear of water, why not our fear of each other?
     </p>
     <p class="p1">
      <em>
       This map found
       <a href="http://atheistuniverse.net/group/avm/forum/topics/bonobo-genome-bonobos-as-close-to-humans-as-chimpanzees?commentId=6381005%3AComment%3A272269&amp;groupId=6381005%3AGroup%3A2001">
        here
       </a>
       on
      </em>
      <a href="http://atheistuniverse.net/">
       Atheist Universe
      </a>
      <em>
       .
      </em>
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      <em>
       ______
      </em>
      <span style="font-size: 10px;">
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [1] The phrase 'Make love, not war' gained currency in the mid-1960s as a countercultural slogan against the Vietnam War. The quote is sometimes attributed to John Lennon, who wrote a song with that title during the
      <em>
       Let It Be
      </em>
      sessions (1968-'70), recycling it as
      <em>
       Mind Games
      </em>
      for his eponymous 1973 solo album. A chronologically more apposite claim is that of the American folklorist, and chroniqueur of the bawdy, Gershon Legman (1917-'99), who theorised that sadism and violence were so deeply ingrained in American culture because of a strong taboo on sex. Legman claimed to have invented 'Make love, not war' for a 1963 lecture at the University of Ohio. Other candidates include Louis Abolafia, who ran for president in 1968 on the Nudist Party ticket (his campaign slogan was 'I have nothing to hide'), the German-American philosopher Herbert Marcuse, a University of Oregon student named Diane Newell Meyer, and Franklin and Penelope Rosemont, who turned the slogan into a popular button. The French version of the slogan is sometimes completed with:
      <em>
       faites les deux, mariez-vous!
      </em>
      ('make both, get married!')
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [2]
      <span class="s1">
       <em>
        Hominidae
       </em>
      </span>
      refers to a taxonomic family of the primate order, consisting of four genera:
      <em>
       Pan
      </em>
      (chimps and bonobos),
      <em>
       Pongo
      </em>
      (orang-utans),
      <em>
       Gorilla
      </em>
      (gorillas), and
      <em>
       Homo
      </em>
      (humans). These four hominids are also referred to as 'great apes', although humans are sometimes excluded. The term hominid should not be confused with
      <em>
       hominin
      </em>
      (describing humans and their closer-than-chimp, extinct relatives, like Neanderthals, or the recently discovered 'Flores Man'),
      <em>
       hominine
      </em>
      (any member of the Homininae subfamily, which includes all hominids except orang-utans), and
      <em>
       hominoids
      </em>
      (great apes and lesser apes - i.e. gibbons).
      <span style="font-size: 10px;">
      </span>
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [3] the Nigerian-Cameroonian chimpanzee (
      <em>
       Pan troglodytes ellioti
      </em>
      ) [in orange on the map], the central chimpanzee (
      <em>
       Pan troglodytes troglodytes
      </em>
      ) [in yellow] and the eastern chimpanzee (
      <em>
       Pan troglodytes schweinfurthii
      </em>
      ) [in green]. Not shown on this map is the distribution of the western chimpanzee (
      <em>
       Pan troglodytes verus
      </em>
      ), in an arc from Senegal to Ghana.
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [4] By the German biologist Ernest Schwarz, after the analysis of a skull initially believed to be a chimpanzee's, in the Africa Museum at Tervuren, near Brussels. Making the bonobo the only species of ape discovered in Belgium.
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [5] Ernest Schwarz's paper on the discovery of the bonobo was called 'Le chimpanz de la Rive Gauche du Congo', which could be interpreted as a tongue-in-cheek reference to the bohemian lifestyles on the
      <em>
       Rive Gauche
      </em>
      in Paris (i.e. the Left Bank of the Seine). The term 'bonobo', incidentally, didn't come into use until the 1950s, and may derive from a Bantu word for ancestor, or from a mis-read place of origin on one of the crates destined for the Tervuren museum (i.e. the Congo river town of Bolobo).
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [6] Most animals instinctively know how to swim, but none of the great apes (including humans) are natural swimmers; what aquatic skills they have, need to be taught actively. Nobody really knows why this is so, but some theories include the relatively high body mass ratio, or the fact that apes are sentient enough to panic when in water. The latter theory could help explain why monkeys can swim. Monkeys are distinct from apes in that they have tails, live in trees, and generally have significantly smaller bodies and brains.
     </p>
     <p class="p3">
      [7] Scientists from the Max Planck Institute in Germany have calculated that this implies that the group of common ancestors from which humans, bonobos and chimpanzees eventually evolved, was quite large, numbering about 27.000 breeding individuals.
     </p>
