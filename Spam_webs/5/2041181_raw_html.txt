       <h1 class="article-title">
        Don't make a beaver problem worse
       </h1>
       <div id="article-essentials">
        <div class="user-profile">
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="deck">
       </div>
       <div id="article-abstract">
        <p>
         SIR, - Through discussion with others, it has become apparent that some farmers within Tayside might be creating problems for themselves with regards to the beaver population here, which is currently being monitored by SNH via the Tayside Beaver Study Group.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div class="article-share-box">
        <div class="fb-like" data-action="recommend" data-layout="button_count" data-send="false" data-show-faces="false" data-width="100">
        </div>

         Tweet
        </a>

       </div>
       <div class="body-content">
        <div class="main-image" style="left: 30px;">
         <img alt="" class="imagecache imagecache-400xY" height="312" src="http://www.thescottishfarmer.co.uk/sites/default/files/imagecache/400xY/2014/2/23446031.jpg" title="" width="400"/>
         <div class="main-image-desc image-desc">
          <span class="main-image-credit image-credit">
          </span>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="slider-wrapper">
         <div class="nivoSlider" id="slider">

        <div id="videoHolder">
        </div>
        <div id="article-content" property="dc:description">
         <p>
          There have been instances of individuals clearing away food caches. Unfortunately this will only encourage the animals to fell more trees.
         </p>
         <p>
          Removal of bushes and trees as a food source results in sacrificing the integrity of the river banks and can/will lead to long term issues such as erosion and scouring of the banks.
         </p>
         <div id="loading-spinner">
          <img src="/sites/all/themes/custom/sf/images/misc/loading-spinner.gif"/>
          <p>
           Loading article content
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="roa">
          <p>
           Digging out dams and clearing out burrows will force them to create more burrows elsewhere, perhaps in a worse location. Lethal removal of the animals will often just encourage other beavers to take up the same location.
          </p>
          <p>
           A point to note is that beaver burrows and structures are often used by otters and water voles, both of which are protected, the former especially.
          </p>
          <p>
           Although the law relating to beaver protection is grey and muddy in Scotland (EU legislation considers them protected but SNH think otherwise), it is, therefore, more than possible to unwittingly harm another protected species and cause an offence to be committed through which some-one might be prosecuted. So, a more structured, robust and long term solution must be sought on problem sites.
          </p>
          <p>
           Far from being a nuisance, the beavers will and do benefit farm lands.
          </p>
          <p>
           The coppicing of trees encourages new growth of various other plants, which in turn provides habitat and feeding for pollinators such as bees which can only benefit all concerned with crop growing.
          </p>
          <p>
           The habitat that the beaver bring with them can also provide water retention in burns etc through the drier months of summer. The slowing down of the main water source also increases the chances of soil retention and reduces the risks of flooding further downstream.
          </p>
          <p>
           There was a recent article, written by George Monbiot, for The Guardian, that, though not mentioning beavers does, however, highlight the need for appropriate riparian edge management (
           <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2014/jan/13/flooding-public-spending-britain-europe-policies-homes" title="http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2014/jan/13/flooding-public-spending-britain-europe-policies-homes">
            http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2014/jan/13/flooding-public-spe...
           </a>
           )
          </p>
          <p>
           There are many pros regarding the beaver and some cons, most of which can be overcome, but giving nature a chance through this trial period is surely an option worth trying?
          </p>
          <p>
           This current period of monitoring is actually useful for getting a proper feel for what these animals are capable of doing in Scotland, both good and bad.
          </p>
          <p>
           It is, therefore, highly important to document everything that is happening and any mitigating action taken.
          </p>
          <p>
           The above study group can advise on the best action to take in particular circumstances, and a number of mitigation techniques have been developed.
          </p>
          <p>
           If you want some more discreet help without going through the study group, there are a number of people around who can quietly advise.
          </p>
          <p>
           It is important to learn from this so that when the Scottish Government is deciding whether to formally introduce beavers or not, that it has some evidence to fall back on.
          </p>
          <p>
           If it ise not aware off the range of situations that might be occurring, it will assume that no such issues exist.
          </p>
          <p>
           Other farmers will thank you for coming forwards with your experiences now, as lessons learned may well help them in the future.
          </p>
          <p>
           Bob Smith
          </p>
          <p>
           Treetops, Victoria St,
          </p>
          <p>
           Rattray, Blairgowrie,
          </p>
          <p>
           Perthshire.
          </p>
