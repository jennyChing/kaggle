             <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
              <a href="http://emilyardoin.blogspot.com/2015/06/ernest-gaines-southern-literature.html">
               Ernest Gaines and Southern Literature
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header">
              <div class="post-header-line-1">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2946514360396265882" itemprop="articleBody">
              So sorry guys. It looks like I fell off the map a bit. Life is busy and really hard sometimes, but I'm finally seeing the light (and feeling more inspired than ever)!
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I'm currently spending the week at my alma mater, University of Louisiana at Lafayette, studying at the Ernest J. Gaines Center for the 2nd annual Ernest J. Gaines Teaching Institute. Ten teachers from all over the south were chosen to come together this week and talk about how to better incorporate Ernest Gaines's literature into our curriculums.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Most of the teachers in the Institute are college professors of Southern Literature or African American Literature. Needless to say, I'm a bit out of my league in this room, but I'm learning SO MUCH. It's like a crash course in teaching novels in an effective way. Plus, I'm feeling so inspired by our book discussions, and it's only the second day.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              In preparation for this week, I read four of Ernest Gaines's novels. (In fact, those are the only books I've read in the last two months. Sigh.) These novels are so interesting to me, partly because I'm from rural Louisiana, so I can totally connect to the setting, but also because these novels tell a story that the history books skip--Jim Crow Louisiana from the African American point of view.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-Bj36GGSzeuA/VXhEVluarjI/AAAAAAAABuA/c6q4I0C3jR0/s1600/1.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="320" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-Bj36GGSzeuA/VXhEVluarjI/AAAAAAAABuA/c6q4I0C3jR0/s320/1.png" width="213"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <h4 style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <i>
                A Lesson Before Dying
               </i>
               by Ernest J. Gaines
              </h4>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Publication Year:
               </b>
               1997
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Genre:
               </b>
               Adult Fiction, Southern Lit
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                My Rating:
               </b>
               3 Stars
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                From the Publisher:
               </b>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <i>
                A Lesson Before Dying
               </i>
               , is set in a small Cajun community in the late 1940s. Jefferson, a young black man, is an unwitting party to a liquor store shoot out in which three men are killed; the only survivor, he is convicted of murder and sentenced to death. Grant Wiggins, who left his hometown for the university, has returned to the plantation school to teach. As he struggles with his decision whether to stay or escape to another state, his aunt and Jefferson's godmother persuade him to visit Jefferson in his cell and impart his learning and his pride to Jefferson before his death. In the end, the two men forge a bond as they both come to understand the simple heroism of resistingand defyingthe expected.
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               Ernest J. Gaines brings to this novel the same rich sense of place, the same deep understanding of the human psyche, and the same compassion for a people and their struggle that have informed his previous, highly praised works of fiction.
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <br/>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-TyYuEG6jHw4/VXhEVvBBVdI/AAAAAAAABuE/tOQgxHUhnTs/s1600/2.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="320" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-TyYuEG6jHw4/VXhEVvBBVdI/AAAAAAAABuE/tOQgxHUhnTs/s320/2.png" width="213"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <h4 style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <i>
                A Gathering of Old Men
               </i>
               by Ernest J. Gaines
              </h4>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Publication Year:
               </b>
               1992
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Genre:
               </b>
               Adult Fiction, Southern Lit
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                My Rating:
               </b>
               2.5 Stars
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                From the Publisher:
               </b>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               Set on a Louisiana sugarcane plantation in the 1970s,
               <i>
                A Gathering of Old Men
               </i>
               is a powerful depiction of racial tensions arising over the death of a Cajun farmer at the hands of a black man.
              </div>
              <br/>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-OuFGWsGT6BU/VXhEVvnzd3I/AAAAAAAABuU/yP5EY0GfPf0/s1600/3.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="320" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-OuFGWsGT6BU/VXhEVvnzd3I/AAAAAAAABuU/yP5EY0GfPf0/s320/3.png" width="213"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <h4 style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <i>
                The Autobiography of Miss Jane Pittman
               </i>
               by Ernest J. Gaines
              </h4>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Publication Year:
               </b>
               1982
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Genre:
               </b>
               Adult Fiction, Southern Lit
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                My Rating:
               </b>
               3.5 Stars
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                From the Publisher:
               </b>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               This is a novel in the guise of the tape-recorded recollections of a black woman who has lived 110 years, who has been both a slave and a witness to the black militancy of the 1960's. In this woman Ernest Gaines has created a legendary figure, a woman equipped to stand beside William Faulkner's Dilsey in
               <i>
                The Sound And The Fury
               </i>
               . Miss Jane Pittman, like Dilsey, has 'endured,' has seen almost everything and foretold the rest. Gaines' novel brings to mind other great works
               <i>
                The Odyssey
               </i>
               for the way his heroine's travels manage to summarize the American history of her race, and Huckleberry Finn for the clarity of her voice, for her rare capacity to sort through the mess of years and things to find the one true story in it all.
              </div>
              <br/>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-JdbOPfE2xuY/VXhEWfycH3I/AAAAAAAABuI/si4epJsrz_s/s1600/4.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="320" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-JdbOPfE2xuY/VXhEWfycH3I/AAAAAAAABuI/si4epJsrz_s/s320/4.png" width="213"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <h4 style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <i>
                Bloodline: Five Stories
               </i>
               by Ernest J. Gaines
              </h4>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Publication Year:
               </b>
               1997
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>
                Genre:
               </b>

              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <b>

               <b>
                From the Publisher:
               </b>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               In these five stories, Gaines returns to the cane fields, sharecroppers' shacks, and decaying plantation houses of Louisiana, the terrain of his great novels
               <i>
                A Gathering of Old Men
               </i>
               and
               <i>
                A Lesson Before Dying
               </i>
               . As rendered by Gaines, this country becomes as familiar, and as haunted by cruelty, suffering, and courage, as Ralph Ellison's Harlem or Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha County.

          <div class="date-posts">
           <div class="post-outer">

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
              <a href="http://emilyardoin.blogspot.com/2015/04/exciting-opportunities-abound.html">
               Exciting Opportunities Abound
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header">
              <div class="post-header-line-1">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-9072342274058562704" itemprop="articleBody">
              Beginning in August, I will be using my MBA to teach college-level Economics classes to juniors and seniors in high school. I will also be using my education degree to teach English to some unsuspecting tenth graders.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              This week, I received an email saying I had been chosen for a very special professional development opportunity. My Alma Mater, The University of Louisiana at Lafayette, hosts a teaching institute every year, where ten teachers are selected to work with author Ernest Gaines and curriculum specialists to plan lessons for high school english classes that center around Gaines's novels. Gaines has many awards to his name, including recipient of the MacArthur Foundation's Genius Grant and a Guggenheim Foundation Fellowship. His novels are central to Louisiana culture, but are award winning and critically acclaimed. (Plus, almost every film/TV adaptation of his novels have won Emmys. NBD.)
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I get the opportunity to work closely with this author and other English teachers from the area to develop a curriculum that incorporates these novels into student lives. I'm so excited! It also means that between now and June 8th, I need to read four novels by Ernest Gaines.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Then (because I'm crazy) I signed up for a 10-week course through
              <a href="https://www.coursera.org/" target="_blank">
               Coursera
              </a>
              , which is an organization that partners with many top-notch universities to provide free access to classes on a variety of subjects. I'll be taking a 10-week course on Greek and Roman mythology through the University of Pennsylvania. We will be reading some of the epics, like the Odyssey, Oedipus the King, and Metamorphoses. That course starts at the end of April and runs until July.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              For those of you keeping count, that's a whole lot of reading for academic/professional purposes, and not a whole lot of fun reading. (I'll be having fun... I just don't know if you want me reviewing the Iliad on this blog!)
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Jeromy made fun of me last night, saying that somehow, I always manage to land myself back in college, no matter how hard I try to graduate.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              What can I say? I love being a student.

             <meta content="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-MwZfze655RY/VS1lPMFzUCI/AAAAAAAABtc/09dr5OQkzNg/s1600/Overcoming%2BReader's%2BSlump.png" itemprop="image_url"/>

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
              <a href="http://emilyardoin.blogspot.com/2015/04/what-slump.html">
               What a Slump.
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header">
              <div class="post-header-line-1">
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4093220273285835313" itemprop="articleBody">
              Some of you may have noticed (at least the 15 people that still visit this blog might have noticed) that I've been in a slump. Not a life slump, per se, but a reading and blogging slump. I haven't read a book since our book club book back in March, even though I have 10 checked out from the library, 5 borrowed from other teachers, and a $25 Barnes and Noble gift card burning a hole in my pocket.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-MwZfze655RY/VS1lPMFzUCI/AAAAAAAABtc/09dr5OQkzNg/s1600/Overcoming%2BReader's%2BSlump.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="268" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-MwZfze655RY/VS1lPMFzUCI/AAAAAAAABtc/09dr5OQkzNg/s1600/Overcoming%2BReader's%2BSlump.png" width="320"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <br/>
              <h3>
               Why do we get in slumps?
              </h3>
              Honestly, it happens to me for a variety of reasons. My house is dirty. There are new things on Netflix. Life is busy. I have a million other things I
              <i>
               SHOULD
              </i>
              be doing.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              But last week, I had the whole week off, and still didn't pick up one book. (I did, however, eat a lot of
              <a href="http://emilyardoin.blogspot.com/2015/04/happy-birthday-to-me.html" target="_blank">
               birthday cake
              </a>
              and jelly beans.) I'm dying to get out of this slump, but when I look at the books on my shelf (books I was previously dying to read), nothing catches my attention.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I've been in a reading slump in that I haven't read anything since March, but I also haven't really found a book I've loved in a few months. I started my year off so strong with
              <i>
               <a href="http://emilyardoin.blogspot.com/2015/01/review-ill-give-you-sun.html" target="_blank">
                I'll Give You the Sun
               </a>
              </i>
              and the
              <a href="http://emilyardoin.blogspot.com/2015/02/book-review-discovery-of-witches-by.html" target="_blank">
               All Souls' Trilogy
              </a>
              , but alas, I've hit an roadblock.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <h3>
               What am I going to do about it?
              </h3>
              I have a few options.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <ol>
               <li>
                <b>
                 Read a best seller.
                </b>
                At least I have concrete proof that
                <i>
                 The Girl on the Train
                </i>
                by Paula Hawkins is good. Or at least not bad. Plus, it's our book club book this month, so I kinda have to read it before April 27th.
               </li>
               <li>
                <b>
                 Reread a favorite.
                </b>
                Both my mother and cousin are rereading the Harry Potter series, and I'm quite jealous. It's always so hard to reread books as a blogger, because I want to be reading books that I can review, but hell, I need to get out of this book funk and Harry never steers me wrong.
               </li>
               <li>
                <b>
                 Latch onto a series.
                </b>
                Normally, if the first book in a series is awesome, I'm dying to get my hands on the next few. The few last series that I attempted have been busts, but I'm going to get down to researching to find a new series I might love.
               </li>
              </ol>
              <div>
               So give me some recommendations, folks. I need some good love stories, some suspense, some nitty gritty characters that I can fall in love with. Do you have any suggestions?
              </div>
