     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Birman
      </b>
      , also called the "Sacred Cat of Burma",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFA_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CFA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a domestic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cat_breed" title="Cat breed">
       cat breed
      </a>
      . The Birman is a long-haired,
      <a href="/wiki/Point_coloration" title="Point coloration">
       colorpointed
      </a>
      cat distinguished by a silky coat, deep blue eyes and contrasting white "gloves" on each paw.
     </p>
     <p>
      The breed name is derived from
      <i>
       Birmanie
      </i>
      , the French form of
      <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">
       Burma
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       Birman
      </i>
      breed was first recognized in France by the Cat Club de France in 1925, then in England by the
      <a href="/wiki/Governing_Council_of_the_Cat_Fancy" title="Governing Council of the Cat Fancy">
       Governing Council of the Cat Fancy
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (GCCF) in 1966 and in United States by the
      <a href="/wiki/Cat_Fanciers%27_Association" title="Cat Fanciers' Association">
       Cat Fanciers' Association
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFA_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CFA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (CFA) in 1967. It is also recognized by the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Cat_Association" title="Canadian Cat Association">
       Canadian Cat Association
      </a>
      (CCA), and by
      <a href="/wiki/The_International_Cat_Association" title="The International Cat Association">
       The International Cat Association
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TICA_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TICA-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (TICA) in 1979.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Birman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There is no clear record of the breed's origin. They are most often claimed to have originated as the companions of temple priests in Northern Burma in the Mount of Lugh.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFA_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-CFA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are many stories extant of how the cats first came to France, including pairs of cats being given as a reward for helping defend a temple, or being smuggled out of Burma by a
      <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_(surname)" title="Vanderbilt (surname)">
       Vanderbilt
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFA_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-CFA-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another pair of Birmans (or a pregnant female called
      <i>
       Poupe de Maldapour
      </i>
      ) were said to have been stolen and later imported to France by Thadde Haddisch.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first traces of historical Birmans go back to a Mme Leotardi
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the city of Nice in France.
     </p>
     <p>
      Birmans were almost wiped out as a breed during World War II. Only two cats were alive in Europe at the end of the war, a pair named Orloff and Xenia de Kaabaa, both belonging to Baudoin-Crevoisier. The foundation of the breed in postwar France were offspring of this pair. They had to be heavily outcrossed with long-hair breeds such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_(cat)" title="Persian (cat)">
       Persian
      </a>
      and Siamese to rebuild the Birman breed. By the early 1950s, pure Birman litters were once again being produced. The restored breed was recognized in Britain in 1965 and by the CFA in 1966
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first Birman cats were seal point. The blue point colour was introduced in 1959 using blue Persian lines. New colours were later added by English breeders including chocolate, red, and lynx (tabby) points.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Birmans have also been used in the development of new breeds, notably including the
      <a href="/wiki/Ragdoll" title="Ragdoll">
       Ragdoll
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">
       Appearance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Birman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appearance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Birmans have a medium-sized, rectangular body with a broad face and distinct Roman nose. Their ears are ideally as wide on the base as they are tall and should be set as much on top of the head as on the side. The eyes are rounded and should be a deep sapphire blue.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Birman's fur is medium-long and should have a silky texture. Unlike a Persian or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalayan_(cat)" title="Himalayan (cat)">
       Himalayan
      </a>
      , they have no undercoat, and are thus much less prone to matting. Coat colour is always
      <a href="/wiki/Point_coloration" title="Point coloration">
       pointed
      </a>
      , save for the contrasting pure white, symmetrical "gloves" on each paw that are the trademark of the breed. The white must involve all toes and in front must stop at the articulation or at the transition of toes to metacarpals. These gloves should extend noticeably further up the back of the leg (referred to as the "laces"), finishing with an inverted V extended 1/2 to 3/4 up the hock. Any other spot of white on the points is considered a serious fault. The base body colour is white to cream, with a wash of color that corresponds to the points but is much paler.
     </p>
     <p>
      Recognized point colours are seal, chocolate, blue, lilac (a softer silver-grey), red or cream.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabby" title="Tabby">
       Tabby
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tortoiseshell_cat" title="Tortoiseshell cat">
       tortie
      </a>
      variations in seal, chocolate, blue or lilac are also allowed; other colours are in development.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colinelavocat.blogspot.com_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-colinelavocat.blogspot.com-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Like all colourpoint breeds, Birman kittens are born white and begin developing their points after one week if a dark color (i.e. seal point) and 14 days or more if the points are "clear" or lighter-colored (i.e. lilac point). Their coat does not reach full development until the cat is two years old.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_diversity">
       Genetic diversity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Birman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genetic diversity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The 2008 study The Ascent of Cat Breeds: Genetic Evaluations of Breeds and Worldwide Random-bred Populations by Lipinski et al. conducted at UC Davis by the team led by leading feline geneticist Dr Leslie Lyons found that the Birman has one of the lowest levels of genetic diversity of all the breeds studied.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colinelavocat.blogspot.com_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-colinelavocat.blogspot.com-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Health">
       Health
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Birman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Health">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The most severe threat is feline hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), the most common heart disease seen in cats. In Birman cats, it is thought to be inherited as an autosomal dominant trait. HCM is a progressive disease and can result in heart failure, paralysis of the hind legs due to clot embolization originating in the heart, and sudden death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Birman_naming_conventions">
       Birman naming conventions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Birman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Birman naming conventions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many Birman breeders follow the French tradition of assigning all kittens born in a particular year given names that begin with the same letter of the alphabet. Countries with breeders using this convention include Canada, France, UK, US, Australia, and New Zealand.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kittens born in 2014 would be K, and in 2015 L, and so on.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
