     <p>
      <b>
       Ruth Stout
      </b>
      (June 14, 1884 - August 22, 1980) was an American author best known for her "No-Work"
      <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">
       gardening
      </a>
      books and techniques.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_mid-life">
       Early and mid-life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Stout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early and mid-life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ruth Stout was born June 14, 1884,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Girard,_Kansas" title="Girard, Kansas">
       Girard, Kansas
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">
       Quaker
      </a>
      parents John Wallace Stout and Lucetta Elizabeth Todhunter Stout.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Her brother
      <a href="/wiki/Rex_Stout" title="Rex Stout">
       Rex Stout
      </a>
      , also an author, was famous for the
      <a href="/wiki/Nero_Wolfe" title="Nero Wolfe">
       Nero Wolfe
      </a>
      detective stories.
     </p>
     <p>
      Stout moved to
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York
      </a>
      when she was 18 and was employed at various times as baby nurse,
      <a href="/wiki/Bookkeeping" title="Bookkeeping">
       bookkeeper
      </a>
      , secretary, business manager, and factory worker. She was a lecturer and coordinated lectures and debates, and she owned a small tea shop in
      <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">
       Greenwich Village
      </a>
      and worked for a fake
      <a href="/wiki/Mentalism" title="Mentalism">
       mind-reading act
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSP_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BSP-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masters_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Masters-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1923, she accompanied fellow
      <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">
       Quakers
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
       Russia
      </a>
      to assist in famine relief.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSP_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BSP-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She met and married Alfred Rossiter in June,1929.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masters_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Masters-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rossiter, the son of an American businessman, was born in Germany in 1882. His family relocated to New York City in 1894.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BP-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In March 1930, the couple moved to Poverty Hollow, Redding Ridge, on the outskirts of
      <a href="/wiki/Redding,_Connecticut" title="Redding, Connecticut">
       Redding
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">
       Connecticut
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-BP-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ruth continued to use her maiden name as her pen name and Rossiter as her official name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty_Hollow">
       Poverty Hollow
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Stout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Poverty Hollow">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Rossiters retired to country living when they moved to the 55-acre (220,000m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      ) farm in Poverty Hollow. Fred, a
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
       Columbia
      </a>
      -trained
      <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">
       psychologist
      </a>
      , followed his passion for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_turning" title="Wood turning">
       wood turning
      </a>
      and subsequently became known for his wooden bowls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-BP-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ruth decided to try her luck at gardening, and in the spring of 1930, she planted her first garden.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunotts_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brunotts-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roots_of_the_no-work_method">
       Roots of the no-work method
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Stout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roots of the no-work method">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      During her first year of gardening and for many after, Ruth employed conventional techniques and practices in her garden with mixed results. She had to wait for someone else to come and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plow" title="Plow">
       plow
      </a>
      the fields before she could start. This gentleman was frequently late or delays would occur due to mechanical failures. Wasted time lessened the already short
      <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">
       growing season
      </a>
      and tried her patience. Furthermore, the manual labor involved in planting a traditional garden became more than she could handle by herself. In the Spring of 1944, Stout decided that she wasn't going to wait for the plowman, nor was she going to plow on her own. Instead she planted the seeds and covered them, waiting to see what would happen, and discovered surprising success.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunotts_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brunotts-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">
       Later life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Stout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As the years progressed, Stout refined her techniques, eventually adopting a year-round
      <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">
       mulch
      </a>
      which virtually eliminated the labor associated with traditional gardening. Her minimalist approach spawned a long-running series of articles in
      <i>
       Organic Gardening and Farming
      </i>
      magazine as well as several books.
     </p>
     <p>
      Her husband, Fred, died on November 24, 1960, after an extended illness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BP_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-BP-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Her sister, Mary, who also lived at Poverty Hollow for over 40 years, died on August 20, 1977, at 88.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
