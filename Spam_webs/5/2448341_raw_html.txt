        <header class="entry-header">
         <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
          Robert Rose on content marketing and storytelling
         </h1>
         <p class="entry-meta">
          By
          <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
           <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/author/iscoop/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
            <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
             J-P De Clerck
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="entry-comments-link">
           <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/robert-rose-content-marketing-storytelling/#respond">
            <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="1150 http://www.i-scoop.eu/?p=1150">
             Leave a Comment
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
        </header>
        <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
         <div class="shareaholic-canvas" data-app="share_buttons" data-app-id="16232743" data-link="http://www.i-scoop.eu/robert-rose-content-marketing-storytelling/" data-summary="" data-title="Robert Rose on content marketing and storytelling">
         </div>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_1575" style="width: 160px">
          <img alt="Robert Rose" class="size-full wp-image-1575" height="173" src="http://www.i-scoop.eu/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Rober-Rose.jpg" width="150"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Robert Rose
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Robert Rose is the Chief Strategist at the Content Marketing Institute. In this interview with Robert we take a look the evolutions in
           <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/content-marketing/" title="The online guide to Content Marketing">
            content marketing
           </a>
           and in related areas such as content management,
           <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/customer-experience/customer-experience-management/" title="Customer experience management  putting the customer first">
            customer experience management
           </a>
           and storytelling.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Robert is known as one of the key driving forces of the Content Marketing Institute, with Joe Pulizzi. He is also the co-author of 
          <em>
           Managing Content Marketing: The Real-World Guide for Creating Passionate Subscribers to Your Brand
          </em>
          .
          <strong>
           <br/>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          When we first started talking about content marketing in 2009, Robert and Joe already had joined forces. And, while we interviewed Joe several times and organized
          <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/content-marketing-context-lessons-roundtable/">
           a round table
          </a>
          with him, prior to helping launch the
          <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/digital-magazine-content-marketing-professionals-europe/">
           European version of the Chief Content Officer magazine
          </a>
          , we never interviewed Robert. If youre familiar with Roberts work, youll know its more than time to make that up! So, here goes.
          <span id="more-1150">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Youre the Chief Strategist at the Content Marketing Institute. When did you meet
           <a href="http://joepulizzi.com/" target="_blank" title="Joe Pulizzi">
            Joe Pulizzi
           </a>
           and started working with him?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          I met Joe way back in 2008, and he and I hit it off immediately. We were both talking about content marketing  and specifically the approach of it  but from two different lenses. He was talking from the publishers point of view  and I from the brand marketers point of view.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_1577" style="width: 190px">
          <img alt="Managing Content Marketing: The Real-World Guide for Creating Passionate Subscribers to Your Brand" class="size-full wp-image-1577" height="270" src="http://www.i-scoop.eu/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Managing-Content-Marketing.jpg" width="180"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Managing-Content-Marketing-Real-World-Subscribers/dp/0983330719/ref=pd_sim_b_6" target="_blank" title="Managing Content Marketing: The Real-World Guide for Creating Passionate Subscribers to Your Brand">
            Managing Content Marketing: The Real-World Guide for Creating Passionate Subscribers to Your Brand
           </a>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          We decided that there was no owners manual for the content marketing process, and thats when we decided to write a book together. During the writing of that book, CMI (
          <a href="http://contentmarketinginstitute.com/" target="_blank" title="Content Marketing Institute">
           Content Marketing Institute
          </a>
          ) was really expanding quickly and Joe asked me if Id like to help him work with companies to deliver content marketing planning services. As I got more involved with CMI, I was then blessed to meet all the amazing people that make up this team  and Im so glad to be on this path with all of them.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Did you expect, when you started at the CMI, that content marketing would become so big and are there areas where you believe the term might better not be used but is anyway?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          I did expect it; though perhaps not this quickly. After I had initial success with the approach,
          <strong>
           I knew that this would change marketing fundamentally  but I had no idea how quickly it would become such an important piece of marketing strategy
          </strong>
          . Its because of this growth that, quite frankly, there is still a lot of confusion about the term more broadly.
         </p>
         <p>
          Of course thats our mission at CMI  to help advance the practice and assist marketers with getting their arms around it. There are definitely A LOT of different types of companies that are adopting the term just to ride the buzz. But this is the case with any popular new idea. In the end, its really the work that matters.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Talking about terms: in virtually every content marketing predictions list you read nowadays, I notice content marketers are saying its going to be just marketing. Same question as I asked one of the keynote speakers at our Content Marketing Conference Europe speakers,
           <a href="http://contentmarketingexperience.i-scoop.eu/content-marketing-conference-europe/content-marketing-conference-europe-2014-antwerp-belgium/doug-kessler-taking-stand-content-marketing-evangelism/" title="Doug Kessler: taking a stand  content marketing as evangelism">
            Doug Kessler
           </a>
           in an interview: of course it is marketing and content is essential everywhere but how do you see the evolutions of content marketing yourself?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          I dont actually agree that it will be just marketing. I believe that marketing and advertising that persuades and sells will always have a place in the business strategy.
          <strong>
           When content marketing is leveraged well  it is a differentiated approach that is infused and well integrated into the broader practice of marketing and communications.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          So, I actually think that the evolution of content marketing becomes a practice within the business to handle this approach as a separate (but integrated) function. Businesses will become skilled at the creation, management and general use of content in the same way that they are becoming skilled at technology.
          <strong>
           Content is an integrated practice within the business  not a replacement for the practice of marketing.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <h2>
          On silos and the customer experience
         </h2>
         <p>
          <em>
           Enough about terminology and predictions. We have something in common: we both have  among others  a content management and marketing technology background. You work at
           <a href="http://www.digitalclaritygroup.com/" target="_blank" title="Digital Clarity Group">
            Digital Clarity Group
           </a>
           , a research-based advisory firm focused on content technologies,
           <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/marketing-automation/" title="Marketing automation: strategy, practice, evolutions and vendors">
            marketing automation
           </a>
           , etc. In content management, which is traditionally still more IT-related, and in marketing automation (and many other technologies and areas) the core message is its about providing the right information/content at the right time to the right people across the right channels and so on. This is also a message in content marketing. Yet, each time Im at an IT conference where this message is loud and clear, I never see marketers. And, each time, Im at a marketing conference, hearing that same message, I see no IT people. While CIOs and CMOs are quick to say they work well together, there is clearly an issue. I wonder what your take on that is and how do you see all those technologies, platforms and processes integrating and connecting?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          Indeed, its a real challenge. I actually just wrote a blog post on this topic where I like to bust some of the myths around CMO and CIO alignment.
          <strong>
           Right now, for most businesses, they look at CMO/CIO alignment as making sure that the CMO understands the IT strategy and that the CIO understands the marketing strategy. This is a fools errand.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Its like that classic Homer Simpson quote just because I understand doesnt mean I care.
          <strong>
           Marketing and IT have to move beyond just understanding each others acronyms and actually develop a cohesive strategy that actually DOES facilitate the buyers journey with great content-driven experiences. Sadly, this is mostly still done in silos.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          To be fair though  at least here in the states I am actually seeing a lot more marketers at traditionally content technology events and vice versa. I think were getting there  but its still early.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_1576" style="width: 610px">
          <img alt="Robert Rose on the customer journey and silos" class="size-full wp-image-1576" height="291" src="http://www.i-scoop.eu/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Robert-Rose-on-the-customer-journey-and-silos.jpg" width="600"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Robert Rose on the customer journey and silos
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <em>
           The narrative in the content management industry is shifting from a more technical and systems of records narrative towards a focus on customer experiences and so-called systems of engagement. At Digital Clarity Group, customer experience management is a core topic too. And, again, I see IT execs, marketers and managers increasingly  and finally  putting customer experiences first. At the same time, different vendors of different solutions claim the market for customer experience management and optimization: some CMS vendors, some marketing automation vendors, some CRM vendors, some content marketing software vendors, you name it. In which of all these markets do you see a true focus on customer experience management? Are there any vendors that do better and how do you see this evolving?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          Youre so right.
          <strong>
           Every CMS vendor has latched on to some form of the Customer Experience buzz
          </strong>
          . And, the answer is not really. But, interestingly I dont think this is (necessarily) all the software providers fault. The challenge is that organizations have spent the last 10 years siloing themselves across the buyers journey.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Weve literally spent the last 15 years (since the growth of digital) siloing ourselves for slivers of the customer journey
          </strong>
          . We have a brand team, and a lead gen team, and a sales team, and a customer loyalty team, and a social crm team, and a measurement and data team. Is it any wonder that vendors have sliced themselves into optimizing for one of those waypoints on the customer journey? They had to do it.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now, of course, were starting to see the pendulum swing back here  with the large companies offering up
          <a href="http://www.i-scoop.eu/customer-experience/" title="The customer experience: connecting business and customer value">
           customer experience
          </a>
          stacks (e.g Salesforce, Adobe, Oracle et. al). They are now recognizing that its actually a holistic process that needs to be addressed. But, the question remains 
          <strong>
           is it better to have one solution for the entire stack  or integrated best-of-breed solutions that hand off seamlessly between waypoints of the customer journey
          </strong>
          ? I, honestly, think the answer could be either depending on the situation.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           I asked quite some question on technology and systems. But they are important, even if they depend on the context of the business and customer goals. Another thing we have in common is the love of testing content marketing software. You tested a whole bunch of players and wrote reports about them. How do you see the content marketing software landscape and what have you learned from all those tests?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          Yeah, Im not sure I LOVE it as much as I see it as necessary for me to stay current. Ha! Anyway, indeed, I have seen some very interesting technology. Ive also seen some very boring un-inventive technology.
         </p>
         <p>
          Mostly what I see is that content marketing software vendors are emerging in the gaps that the traditional companies in all those spaces you mentioned before (CMS, CRM, Marketing Automation) not covering. So,
          <strong>
           why CMS systems arent yet offering editorial calendaring in a meaningful way baffles me
          </strong>
          .
          <strong>
           Why marketing automation vendors havent moved into content scoring instead of (or in addition to) lead scoring is a mystery
          </strong>
          .
          <strong>
           Why measurement companies havent developed analytics on teams, and freelancers and helped to monetize the content creation process is just odd.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Anyway  there are some very interesting technologies being developed (especially on the search and normalization technologies inherent to great curation software).
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           I looked at some of your blog posts before this interview and seem to discover a passion that is far away from the whole technology and strategy dimension you know so well: storytelling. What makes it so appealing to you and can you share some lessons?
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <span style="color: #ff6600;">
           <strong>
            Robert Rose:
           </strong>
          </span>
          Well its in my DNA for sure. I came out to Hollywood to be a songwriter and screenwriter  and in my fantasy I still have some of that in me. But truly  and this goes back to the question earlier 
          <strong>
           I believe that storytellers will be an inherent skill position in businesses moving forward.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           This is a new era for marketing communications  and I think storytelling (and the skills that are needed for that) will become an increasingly important skill for businesses
          </strong>
          . If I can help advance that  it feeds my passion and helps me realize my boyhood dreams of being a storyteller for a living.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           To conclude, some fun (I hope, at least). Please complete following sentences:
          </em>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Content marketing is.
           <strong>
            a practice that not every business will be good at  but every successful business will.
           </strong>
          </li>
          <li>
           Robert Roses favorite hobby is playing and composing music.
          </li>
          <li>
           If I would ask your friends they would tell you arehopefully they would say a nice guy.
          </li>
          <li>
           <strong>
            The largest misconceptions about content marketing are that its only for the top of the funnel.
           </strong>
          </li>
          <li>
           Joe Pulizzi is obsessed with orange and Robert Roses favorite color is bluish-gray like the color of rainy skies.
          </li>
          <li>
           In five years from now, I see myself
           <strong>
            helping businesses build content storytelling as a structured part of their business.
           </strong>
          </li>
          <li>
           The buzzword in content marketing or marketing as a whole Robert Rose absolutely despises is. Native Advertising.
           <a href="http://contentmarketinginstitute.com/2013/08/native-advertising-neither/" target="_blank" title="Why Native Advertising Is Neither">
            <strong>
             Native advertising is neither.
            </strong>
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           Your biggest sources of inspiration in work and life are Joseph Campbell, Theodore Levitt, My Wife Elizabeth.
          </li>
          <li>
           If theres one thing Robert Rose would like his partner Joe Pulizzi to change it is his disdain for the Dallas Cowboys.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          Thats it folks. You can follow Robert on
          <a href="https://twitter.com/Robert_Rose" target="_blank" title="Robert Rose on Twitter">
           Twitter
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/robrose" target="_blank" title="Robert Rose on LinkedIn">
           LinkedIn
          </a>
          and of course the
          <a href="http://contentmarketinginstitute.com/blog/" target="_blank" title="CMI blog">
           blog of the CMI
          </a>
          .
         </p>
