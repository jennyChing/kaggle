      <b>
       Halicarnassus
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">

       </span>
      </span>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
       AncientGreek
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">
       <i>
        Halikarnasss
       </i>
      </span>
      or
      <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       Alikarnasss
      </i>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
       Turkish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">
       <i>
        Halikarnas
       </i>
      </span>
      ) was an
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       ancient Greek
      </a>
      city at the site of modern
      <a href="/wiki/Bodrum" title="Bodrum">
       Bodrum
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
       Turkey
      </a>
      . It was located in southwest
      <a href="/wiki/Caria" title="Caria">
       Caria
      </a>
      on a picturesque, advantageous site on the
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_G%C3%B6kova" title="Gulf of Gkova">
       Ceramic Gulf
      </a>
      . The city was famous for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Halicarnassus" title="Mausoleum of Halicarnassus">
       tomb
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Mausolus" title="Mausolus">
       Mausolus
      </a>
      , the origin of the word mausoleum, built between 353BC and 350BC, and one of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_wonders_of_the_ancient_world" title="Seven wonders of the ancient world">
       seven wonders of the ancient world
      </a>
      . It was part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">
       Persian Empire
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
       Achaemenid Empire
      </a>
      ) until captured by
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
       Alexander the Great
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Halicarnassus" title="Siege of Halicarnassus">
       siege of Halicarnassus
      </a>
      in 334BC.
     </p>
     <p>
      Halicarnassus originally occupied only a small island near to the shore called Zephyria, which was the original name of the settlement and the present site of the great
      <a href="/wiki/Bodrum_Castle" title="Bodrum Castle">
       Castle of St. Peter
      </a>
      built by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_Rhodes" title="Knights of Rhodes">
       Knights of Rhodes
      </a>
      in 1404; but in course of time, the island united with the mainland and the city extended to incorporate
      <a href="/wiki/Salmacis" title="Salmacis">
       Salmacis
      </a>
      , an older town of the
      <a href="/wiki/Leleges" title="Leleges">
       Leleges
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Carians" title="Carians">
       Carians
      </a>
      and site of the later citadel.
     </p>

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Herodotusstatue.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Herodotusstatue.JPG/200px-Herodotusstatue.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Herodotusstatue.JPG/300px-Herodotusstatue.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Herodotusstatue.JPG/400px-Herodotusstatue.JPG 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Herodotusstatue.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
         Herodotus
        </a>
        (Greek: ) is honored with a statue in his home of Halicarnassus (modern
        <a href="/wiki/Bodrum" title="Bodrum">
         Bodrum
        </a>
        ).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The founding of Halicarnassus is debated among various traditions; but they agree in the main point as to its being a
      <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">
       Dorian
      </a>
      colony, and the figures on its coins, such as the head of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medusa_(mythology)" title="Medusa (mythology)">
       Medusa
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">
       Athena
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">
       Poseidon
      </a>
      , or the trident, support the statement that the mother cities were
      <a href="/wiki/Troezen" title="Troezen">
       Troezen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">
       Argos
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The inhabitants appear to have accepted Anthes, a son of Poseidon, as their legendary founder, as mentioned by
      <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
       Strabo
      </a>
      , and were proud of the title of
      <i>
       Antheadae
      </i>
      . The Carian name for Halicarnassus has been tentatively identified with Aloskarnos in inscriptions.
     </p>
     <p>
      At an early period Halicarnassus was a member of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doric_Hexapolis" title="Doric Hexapolis">
       Doric Hexapolis
      </a>
      , which included
      <a href="/wiki/Kos" title="Kos">
       Kos
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cnidus" title="Cnidus">
       Cnidus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lindos" title="Lindos">
       Lindos
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kameiros" title="Kameiros">
       Kameiros
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ialysus" title="Ialysus">
       Ialysus
      </a>
      ; but it was expelled from the league when one of its citizens, Agasicles, took home the prize tripod which he had won in the Triopian games, instead of dedicating it according to custom to the Triopian Apollo. In the early 5th century Halicarnassus was under the sway of
      <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_I_of_Caria" title="Artemisia I of Caria">
       Artemisia I of Caria
      </a>
      (also known as Artemesia of Halicarnassus
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), who made herself famous as a naval commander at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Salamis" title="Battle of Salamis">
       battle of Salamis
      </a>
      . Of Pisindalis, her son and successor, little is known; but
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Lygdamis" title="Lygdamis">
       Lygdamis
      </a>
      , who next attained power, is notorious for having put to death the poet
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panyasis" title="Panyasis">
       Panyasis
      </a>
      and causing
      <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
       Herodotus
      </a>
      , possibly the best known Halicarnassian, to leave his native city (c. 457BC).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hekatomnid_dynasty">
       The Hekatomnid dynasty
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Halicarnassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Hekatomnid dynasty">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Hecatomnus" title="Hecatomnus">
       Hecatomnus
      </a>
      became king of Caria, at that time part of the Persian Empire, ruling from 404BC to 358BC and establishing the Hekatomnid dynasty. He left three sons, Mausolus,
      <a href="/wiki/Idrieus" title="Idrieus">
       Idrieus
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pixodarus_of_Caria" title="Pixodarus of Caria">
       Pixodarus
      </a>
       all of whom  in their turn, succeeded him in the sovereignty; and two daughters,
      <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_II_of_Caria" title="Artemisia II of Caria">
       Artemisia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ada_of_Caria" title="Ada of Caria">
       Ada
      </a>
      , who were married to their brothers Mausolus and Idrieus.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mausolus moved his capital from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mylasa" title="Mylasa">
       Mylasa
      </a>
      to Halicarnassus. His workmen deepened the city's harbor and used the dragged sand to make protecting
      <a href="/wiki/Breakwater_(structure)" title="Breakwater (structure)">
       breakwaters
      </a>
      in front of the channel
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . On land they paved streets and
      <a href="/wiki/Town_square" title="Town square">
       squares
      </a>
      , and built houses for ordinary citizens. And on one side of the harbor they built a massive fortified palace for Mausolus, positioned to have clear views out to sea and inland to the hills  places from where enemies could attack. On land, the workmen also built walls and
      <a href="/wiki/Watchtower" title="Watchtower">
       watchtowers
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architecture_of_ancient_Greece" title="Architecture of ancient Greece">
       Greek
      </a>
      style theatre and a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_temple" title="Greek temple">
       temple
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">
       Ares
      </a>
       the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
       Greek
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/God_(male_deity)" title="God (male deity)">
       god
      </a>
      of war.
     </p>
     <p>
      Artemisia and Mausolus spent huge amounts of tax money to embellish the city. They commissioned statues, temples and buildings of gleaming
      <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">
       marble
      </a>
      . When he died in 353BC, his wife, sister and successor,
      <a href="/wiki/Artemisia_II_of_Caria" title="Artemisia II of Caria">
       Artemisia II of Caria
      </a>
      , began construction of a magnificent tomb for him and herself on a hill overlooking the city. She died in 351 BC (of grief, according to Cicero;
      <i>
       Tusculan Disputations
      </i>
      3.31). According to
      <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
       Pliny the Elder
      </a>
      the craftsmen continued to work on the tomb after the death of their patron, "considering that it was at once a memorial of his own fame and of the sculptor's art," finishing it in 350BC. This tomb of Mausolus came to be known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Halicarnassus" title="Mausoleum of Halicarnassus">
       Mausoleum
      </a>
      , one of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_wonders_of_the_ancient_world" title="Seven wonders of the ancient world">
       seven wonders of the ancient world
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Artemisia was succeeded by her brother Idrieus, who, in turn, was succeeded by his wife and sister Ada when he died in 344BC. However, Ada was usurped by her brother Pixodarus in 340BC. On the death of Pixodarus in 335BC his son-in-law, a Persian named
      <a href="/wiki/Orontobates" title="Orontobates">
       Orontobates
      </a>
      , received the satrapy of Caria from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_III_of_Persia" title="Darius III of Persia">
       Darius III of Persia
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Alexander_the_Great_and_Ada_of_Caria">
       Alexander the Great and Ada of Caria
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Halicarnassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alexander the Great and Ada of Caria">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Halicarnassus" title="Siege of Halicarnassus">
       Siege of Halicarnassus
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      When
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
       Alexander the Great
      </a>
      entered Caria in 334BC,
      <a href="/wiki/Ada_of_Caria" title="Ada of Caria">
       Ada
      </a>
      , who was in possession of the fortress of
      <a href="/wiki/Alinda" title="Alinda">
       Alinda
      </a>
      , surrendered the fortress to him. After taking Halicarnassus, Alexander handed back the government of Caria to her; she, in turn, formally adopted Alexander as her son, ensuring that the rule of Caria passed unconditionally to him upon her eventual death. During the
      <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Halicarnassus" title="Siege of Halicarnassus">
       siege of Halicarnassus
      </a>
      the city was fired by the retreating Persians. As he was not able to reduce the citadel, Alexander was forced to leave it blockaded. The ruins of this citadel and moat are now a tourist attraction in Bodrum.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">
       Later history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Halicarnassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Not long afterwards the citizens received the present of a gymnasium from
      <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">
       Ptolemy
      </a>
      and built in his honour a
      <i>
       stoa
      </i>
      or portico. Under Egyptian hegemony, around 268 BC, a citizen named
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hermias_of_Halicarnassus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hermias of Halicarnassus (page does not exist)">
       Hermias
      </a>
      became
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nesiarch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nesiarch (page does not exist)">
       Nesiarch
      </a>
      of the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nesiotic_League&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nesiotic League (page does not exist)">
       Nesiotic League
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cyclades" title="Cyclades">
       Cyclades
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Halicarnassus never recovered altogether from the disasters of the siege, and
      <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
       Cicero
      </a>
      describes it as almost deserted.
     </p>
     <p>
      Baroque artist
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Elias_Ridinger" title="Johann Elias Ridinger">
       Johann Elias Ridinger
      </a>
      depicted the several stages of siege and taking of the place in a huge copper engraving as one of only two known today from his Alexander set.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Christian and later history of the site is continued at
      <a href="/wiki/Bodrum" title="Bodrum">
       Bodrum
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Archeological_notes_and_restorations">
       Archeological notes and restorations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Halicarnassus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Archeological notes and restorations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The site is now occupied in part by the town of Bodrum; but the ancient walls can still be traced round nearly all their circuit, and the position of several of the temples, the theatre, and other public buildings can be fixed with certainty.
     </p>
     <p>
      The ruins of the Mausoleum have been recovered sufficiently by the 1857 excavations of
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Thomas_Newton" title="Charles Thomas Newton">
       Charles Newton
      </a>
      to enable a fairly complete restoration of its design to be made. The building consisted of five partsa basement or podium, a pteron or enclosure of columns, a pyramid, a pedestal and a chariot group. The basement, covering an area of 114 feet by 92, was built of blocks of greenstone, cased with marble and covered in carvings of cows. Round the base of it were probably disposed groups of statuary. The pteron consisted (according to
      <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
       Pliny
      </a>
      ) of thirty-six columns of the Ionic order, enclosing a square cella. Between the columns probably stood single statues. From the portions that have been recovered, it appears that the principal frieze of the pteron represented combats of Greeks and
      <a href="/wiki/Amazons" title="Amazons">
       Amazons
      </a>
      . In addition, there are also many life-size fragments of animals, horsemen, etc., belonging probably to pedimental sculptures, but formerly supposed to be parts of minor friezes. Above the pteron rose the pyramid, mounting by 24 steps to an apex or pedestal.
     </p>
     <p>
      On this apex stood the chariot with the figure of Mausolus himself and an attendant. The height of the statue of Mausolus in the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">
       British Museum
      </a>
      is 9'9" without the plinth. The hair falls from the forehead in thick waves on each side of the face and descends nearly to the shoulder; the beard is short and close, the face square and massive, the eyes deep set under overhanging brows, the mouth well formed with settled calm about the lips. The drapery is grandly composed. All sorts of restorations of this famous monument have been proposed. The original one, made by Newton and Pullan, is obviously in error in many respects; and that of Oldfield, though to be preferred for its lightness (the Mausoleum was said anciently to be "suspended in mid-air"), does not satisfy the conditions postulated by the remains. The best on the whole is that of the veteran German architect, F. Adler, published in 1900; but fresh studies have since been made (see below).
     </p>
