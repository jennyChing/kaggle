       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Kindle Unlimited
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="sep">
          Posted on
         </span>
         <a href="http://www.indiesunlimited.com/2014/07/24/kindle-unlimited/" rel="bookmark" title="9:00 am">
          <time class="entry-date" datetime="2014-07-24T09:00:00+00:00">
           July 24, 2014
          </time>
         </a>
         <span class="by-author">
          <span class="sep">
           by
          </span>
          <span class="author vcard">
           <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.indiesunlimited.com/author/lynne-cantwell/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Lynne Cantwell">
            Lynne Cantwell
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!-- .entry-meta -->
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.indiesunlimited.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/KindleUnlimited.jpg">
          <img alt="KindleUnlimited" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-56539" height="295" src="http://www.indiesunlimited.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/KindleUnlimited.jpg" title="" width="228"/>
         </a>
         Youd have to have been living under a rock for the past week (or on vacation somewhere where theres no internet) to have missed Amazons big news about its newest feature, Kindle Unlimited.
        </p>
        <p>
         For the low, low price of $9.99 a month, Amazon.com will let readers download an unlimited number of ebooks and audiobooks from its special Kindle Unlimited store. (Those of you who are forced, by geographical happenstance, into shopping from Amazons other storefronts are out of luck. Sorry about that.) And this month, Amazons making the service free, so everyone can try it out.
        </p>
        <p>
         This would seem to be a boon for the voracious reader. One trad-pubbed novel can set you back $9.99 or more, and a single audiobook costs easily twice that. The drawback for readers, though, is that Big 5 books are severely under-represented in the KU store. So people who pick books based on the bestseller lists are going to be disappointed. There are other factors limiting reader interest in KU, but Id rather talk today about what this means for indie authors.
         <span id="more-56206">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         The good news for indies is that we have an easy way into the KU store: all you have to do is enroll your book(s) in KDP Select. Which is also, of course, the bad news, as all of the usual complaints about participating in Select apply here. (Amazon opted in every title in Select to start with, but you can opt out.)
        </p>
        <p>
         Moreover, Amazon hasnt been terribly explicit about how well be paid, other than to say that KU purchases will be paid out of the same fund Amazon uses for Kindle Owner Lending Library borrows. But author payments on KU purchases will be treated differently. For KU, authors will be paid only when the reader gets to ten percent in the book. Amazon beefed up the fund for July due to the KU rollout, and I think its reasonable to expect the Zon will increase the funding level every month, going forward. In his article about KU,
         <a href="http://davidgaughran.wordpress.com/2014/07/20/kindle-unlimited-the-key-questions/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          David Gaughran
         </a>
         says the KOLL fund has historically averaged about $2 per borrow. So assuming you can hook readers into getting to the magic ten percent mark, you can count on about that much per KU purchase.
        </p>
        <p>
         Indies love to talk strategy, of course, and a number of folks are already trying to make this new feature work to their advantage. If you write shorter works, the reasoning goes, and throw them up on KDP Select with a list price of 99 cents, youd have a good chance of a reader getting to ten percent very fast  and you would earn twice as much on each book as you would for a purchase at the list price. Moreover, it appears that for now, at least, Amazon is treating KU purchases the same as full-price paid sales for ranking purposes. This could be a great way to boost your visibility and maybe bring in sales for your other books.
        </p>
        <p>
         By happenstance, Im bucking the trend to a degree. I only have a couple of things in Select right now  a short story called Lulie that never sells, and the Pipe Woman Chronicles Omnibus. KU downloads of the omnibus will cost me money, as Ill earn less on them than I would on a regular purchase. But if people read the omnibus and like it, then maybe theyll shell out for the Land, Sea, Sky books, which are not in Select. And if anybody ever bought Lulie, Id make money on the sale.
        </p>
        <p>
         Theres a very real chance that reader interest in KU will drop off next month, when people have to start paying for it. And theres also the problem of promotion for KU titles. Its unclear as I write this how Amazon will treat affiliate earnings on these books; that may limit advertising opportunities on the big promotional sites, at least in the short term.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the meantime, though, Amazon has given us one more tool to reach new readers. How is it working for you?
        </p>

           </p>

The other benefit for indies is  theres no price barrier to try your book. Just click the Read for Free button. Its like offering your book for free every daybut, you get paid!

             <p>
              Pedro, Ive found theres more similarities than differences, though the latter can be significant, just depending on your needs.
             </p>
             <p>
              Amazon of course has an ease of use and reach that makes it the gorilla everyone either hates or loves or is unsure about
              <img alt=":-)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://www.indiesunlimited.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/simple-smile.png" style="height: 1em; max-height: 1em;"/>
             </p>
             <p>
              I use Amazon and Scribd daily, both their apps and differing titles.
             </p>
             <p>
              Apps are continually being upgraded, so I havent found enough that Scribds or Oysters or Amazons reading apps have that would make me dump either of them.
             </p>
             <p>
              Scribd lets you keep in your library to read, I believe  unless its changed, up to 20 titles vs 10 from Amazon. I dont find that a useful difference at my level either.
             </p>
             <p>
              Prices are also too close to matter for me.
             </p>
             <p>
              That leaves title selection.
             </p>
             <p>
              KU is exclusive, though I hope that changes.
             </p>
             <p>
              But while it is, one can legitimately say there is creative work in KU thats not in Scribd.
             </p>
             <p>
              Unfortunately, the inverse is also true  all the titles in Scribd, though not exclusive to them, are excluded from Kindle Unlimited.
             </p>
             <p>
              Where the problem between the two has been for me, and why, though I have all my titles with Amazons regular store, I dont have any in Select or Kindle Unlimited, is 
             </p>
             <p>
              If I like a title on one, and want to recommend it to a friend, I have a different experience with one platform over the other.
             </p>
             <p>
              In Kindle Unlimited, not all my friends buy from Amazon, and even less are in Kindle Unlimited. A recommended title within that subscription program, would mean my friend would have to join KU to enjoy that title, or buy it.
             </p>
             <p>
              More and more of the people I know, are preferring and enjoying the ease and benefits of being in a subscription program.
             </p>
             <p>
              If I recommend a title from Scribd, and they dont want to join Scribd, they at least have quite a few options for finding and reading it elsewhere : iTunes, Barnes &amp; Noble, Kobo, Smashwords, Oyster, Amazons regular Kindle store (though not KU)  and maybe most importantly  libraries.
             </p>
             <p>
              Ive read that titles in Select and KU are excluded even from libraries.
             </p>
             <p>
              Amazon, in my opinion, simply doesnt need exclusivity anymore to attract business.
             </p>
             <p>
              Even if KU gets bundled, or a combo bundle is optionally offered, with Prime  readers will want the spectrum of available titles.
             </p>
             <p>
              The only things I pay attention to as being exclusive, is a handful of currently wildly popular titles or movies or TV shows. And sometimes that desired handful shrinks to just a couple of fingers
              <img alt=":-)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://www.indiesunlimited.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/simple-smile.png" style="height: 1em; max-height: 1em;"/>
             </p>
             <p>
              Hope that helps, Pedro. Best wishes deciding or experimenting which way to go.
             </p>
