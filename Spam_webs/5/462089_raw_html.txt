       <div class="l-meta-container meta--has-author-image">
        <h1 class="page-title" id="page-title" property="dc:title">
         It's time to scrap negative gearing
        </h1>
        <div class="meta--author-image" property="dc:image">
         <img alt="" height="128" src="http://www.businessspectator.com.au/sites/default/files/styles/media-scalecrop-128x128/public/headshots/bs/callam-pickering.jpg?itok=ttgMTwvq" typeof="foaf:Image" width="128"/>
        </div>
        <ul class="l-meta l-row-1">
         <li>
          <span class="meta--author" property="dc:creator">
           <a href="/contributor/callam-pickering" title="Callam Pickering">
            Callam Pickering
           </a>
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <time class="meta--published-at" datetime="2015-04-17" title="Friday, 17th April 2015 - 7:21am [Melbourne]">
           17 Apr, 7:21 AM
          </time>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a class="meta--comment-count livefyre-commentcount-wrapper active" href="/article/2015/4/17/national-affairs/its-time-scrap-negative-gearing#comments">
           <span class="livefyre-commentcount" data-lf-article-id="business_spectator_conv_955486" data-lf-site-id="366253">
            0 Comments
           </span>
          </a>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <ul class="meta--category-list l-meta l-row-2">
         <li>
          <a href="/politics">

     <div class="block block--aibm-ads block--aibm-ads-btyb-pos1" id="block-aibm-ads-btyb-pos1">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="ad-block ad-custom" data-ad-size="300x50" data-ad-tar="adposition=btyb_pos1&amp;pos=1">
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div about="/article/2015/4/17/national-affairs/its-time-scrap-negative-gearing" class="ds-1col node node--commentary view-mode-bs_full node--bs-full node--commentary--bs-full clearfix" typeof="sioc:Item foaf:Document">
      <div class="field--body">
       <p>
        By any metric, negative gearing on Australian residential property is one of the least successful economic policies in our countrys history. It was designed decades ago to boost the housing supply by encouraging greater investment in housing construction. Unfortunately it has achieved nothing of the sort.
       </p>
       <p>
        The policy received some well-deserved criticism this week from the Australian Council of Social Service, whichpublished a
        <a href="http://www.acoss.org.au/images/uploads/Fuel_on_the_fire.pdf">
         <span style="color:#287BCE;">
          report
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color:#287BCE;">
        </span>
        recommending that Australians should no longer be able to claim losses on investment properties against their wages.
       </p>
       <p>
        Its a sensible policy recommendation but Prime Minister Tony Abbott immediately rejected the idea. Its no surprise that there is little desire in Canberra to reform housing policy; back in 2013, Australian parliamentarians owned 563 properties estimated to be worth over $300 million (
        <em>
         <a href="http://www.businessspectator.com.au/article/2014/8/6/politics/conflict-interest-killing-housing-reform">
          <span style="color:#287BCE;">
           The conflict of interest killing housing reform
          </span>
         </a>
        </em>
        , August 6 2014).
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <img alt="" class="media-element file-media-original" height="320" src="http://www.businessspectator.com.au/sites/default/files/callam1704a.png" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="491"/>
       </p>
       <p align="center">
       </p>
       <p>
        Its difficult to mount a defence of negative gearing using logic or sound economic principles. Its a policy that is almost entirely directed towards property speculation rather than productive investment. It costs Australian households and businesses billions of dollars -- and is a major cause of rising household debt and property prices -- but has led to no discernible increase in living standards.
       </p>
       <p>
        Based on the latest data, new construction accounts for just 6.5 per cent of the total value of investor loan approvals. The long-term trend could not be clearer: Australian investors don't wantnewly constructed properties.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <img alt="" class="media-element file-media-original" height="320" src="http://www.businessspectator.com.au/sites/default/files/callam1704b.png" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="492"/>
       </p>
       <p align="center">
       </p>
       <p>
        Rather than boosting the housing supply and increasing employment within the construction sector, negative gearing has instead encouraged speculation, boosted house prices and undermined home ownership.
       </p>
       <p>
        Negative gearing isnt solely to blame -- the capital gains discount also has a lot to answer for -- but its hard to frame an argument in which negative gearing isnt a refuge for tax avoidance rather than productive investment.
       </p>
       <p>
        According to the ATO, Australiahad 1.9 million property investors in the 2011-12 financial year. Most of those investors failed to cover their costs, suffering a $7.9 billion net rental loss. Losses of this magnitude have become exceedingly common in Australia over the past decade.
        <br/>
        <br/>
        <img alt="" class="media-element file-media-original" height="320" src="http://www.businessspectator.com.au/sites/default/files/callam1704c.png" title="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="492"/>
       </p>
       <p align="center">
       </p>
       <p>
        According to ACOSS, over half of individual taxpayers taking advantage of negative gearing are in the top 10 per cent of income earners (earning over $100,000 in 2011). Around 30 per cent earned over $500,000.
       </p>
       <p>
        That busts the myth -- usually perpetrated by the Housing Industry Association-- that property investors are generally middle-income mum and dad investors. Sure, those investors exist, but the vast majority of tax concessions from negative gearing accrue to wealthy Australians. A point so obvious that its hard to believe that HIA ever pretended otherwise.
       </p>
       <p>
        HIA also claims that removing or quarantining negative gearing would cause rents to skyrocket. Its a bogus claim since the last time that negative gearing was restricted -- by the Hawke government from 1985 to 1987 -- there was no identifiable surge in rental growth.
       </p>
       <p>
        Another common argument for maintaining the existing frameworkis that negative gearing creates a stock of cheap rental property. The facts, however, speak for themselves: investors were more active in building new properties during the brief restriction on negative gearing than in any time since then. These days, negative gearing does not contribute materially to the housing stock, instead it simply replaces homeowners with landlords.
       </p>
       <p>
        I dont blame property investors for taking advantage of the benefits of negative gearing -- theyd be crazy not to -- but its clear that its a policy that serves no purpose aside from reducing the tax burden of Australias wealthiest citizens.
       </p>
       <p>
        If thats the case, why doesnt the government simply remove negative gearing and simultaneously cut taxes? The end result would be revenue neutral but would also remove the capital market distortions that have made negative gearing so destructive.
       </p>
       <p>
        Alternatively, why doesnt the government restrict negative gearing to newly constructed properties? At least then the policy would actually achieve its original purpose of increasing the housing supply and boosting employment.
       </p>
       <p>
        Unfortunately, the reality is that reforming the rules surrounding negative gearing is plagued with difficulties. Our politicians clearly lack the political courage to tackle housing affordability, while many investors would be left vulnerable to any change in the tax code.
       </p>
       <p>
        In practice, any change would have to be grandfathered in, which would limit the exposure of current investors and allow prices to adjust more gradually. The US approach, which only allows losses on property investment to be offset against property income, appears to be a sound model.
       </p>
       <p>
        Its time that our politicians, at both the state and federal level, begin to take housing affordability seriously. Unfortunately the government, through inaction, is actively supporting excessive speculation, capital market distortions and rising indebtedness.
       </p>
       <p>
        Had they actually pursued housing reforms over the past decade, rather than cheering on their own property portfolios, then perhaps the economy would be in a much stronger state and better placed to manage the end of the mining boom. Unfortunately, reform remains as distant as ever. Sound policy is no match for political greed.
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>

scrapped. Negative gearing has caused a large shift to investor purchases and
away from owner occupied especially first home buyers. Investors mainly go for
cheap existing housing, pushing prices way up. First home buyers have been
priced out of the market and are forced to pay high rent to support well-off
folk reducing their tax. Investors also grab the gains of higher prices, pushed
up by these same investors.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <span>
                   Keating
got rid of negative gearing in July 1985. The real estate industry jumped up
and down of course but rents rose mainly in Sydney. Negative gearing came back
in September 1987. There was an economic downturn in 1986 due to a balance of
payments problem, falling dollar and low commodity prices. The presence or
otherwise of negative gearing didnt seem to be an issue. GDP growth fell
steadily in 1985-86, bottoming out at 0.7% annual growth in September quarter
1986. It then rose steadily to annual growth of 4.2% in June quarter 1987 and
5.1% in September quarter. Housing prices werent moving much back then and
were a fraction of prices today.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <span>
                   Negative gearing has had a major downward
impact on government revenue.
                  </span>
                  <span>

and in repairs and maintenance is a tax deduction for investors, but if this
money is spent by owner occupiers, it wouldnt be a deduction. Agents,
insurers, repairers, etc. would still be paying tax just the same. Further, negative
gearers are probably on a higher marginal tax rate than the average agent, insurer,
repairer, with a good sprinkling of investors probably at each of 32.5, 37 and
45 cents in the dollar. Repairers would be concentrated in the 32.5 cent
bracket, others at 32.5 and 37. Companies of course pay 30 cents in the dollar.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <span>
                   In 1985, investment
housing finance was $150 million a month (and 12% of the market). Its now
climbed to $12 billion a month (and 40% of the market) and growing rapidly.
Its one reason federal government revenue is only 23% of GDP these days.
Pre-GFC, it was 25-26%. One per cent of GDP is about $16 billion. The 2014-15
deficit is estimated at $40 billion (at MYEFO) or 2.5% of GDP.
                  </span>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <span>
                   Negative
gearing also takes funds away from more productive investments.
                  </span>

is a very low tax country by OECD standards. Our tax concessions are worth
about $120 billion a year or 8% of GDP, far more generous than comparable
countries, and this needs to be looked at.

                 <p>
                  I've been a chartered accountant in public practice for over 30 years. The abolition of negative gearing in the 80's was a dismal failure, delivering absolutely none of the expected benefits it was supposed to deliver. It did nothing whatsoever to arrest the growth in property prices, did nothing to curb property speculation and resulted in higher rents as landlords tried to recoup non-deductible losses or sold their holdings. The arguments to abolish it were the same then as they are now.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Look at the real facts. Those of us that work in the field can tell you the vast majority of these investors are not wealthy. They are people looking to achieve investment returns above the paltry cash rate. Negative gearing is exactly that - negative. Even after tax deductions, the investor is out of pocket. Therefore, these investments are necessarily medium to long term holds.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  So what do we do if an investor has very little debt on a property but loses a tenant? The asset would be negatively geared. Do we punish them for losing a tenant by not allowing tax deductions? What about commercial landlords? If interest rates rise, some of their investments will be negatively geared? What. No deductions for the loss?
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Negative gearing has been around for DECADES. It follows normal and long-accepted taxation equity principles. Learn from the lessons of the past and leave it alone.
