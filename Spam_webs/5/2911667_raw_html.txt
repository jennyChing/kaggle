      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Seeds Of Alibaba's IPO Planted In 2004 Battle With eBay
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">
        Comment Now
       </a>
       <div class="comments_follow followable_block">
        <div class="followable_block initializing" data-object-json="{&quot;title&quot;:&quot;Seeds Of Alibaba's IPO Planted In 2004 Battle With eBay&quot;,&quot;avatar_src&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;follow_count&quot;:&quot;&quot;}" data-target-id="blogAndPostId/blog/post/1146-833" data-target-name="" data-target-type="article" data-tracking-link-name="articleLink_ArticleCommentsFollow" data-type-advoice="false">
         <div class="follow_unireg">
          <a class="follow_unireg_link" href="javascript://follow">

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         In light of Alibabas upcoming IPO, I thought it would be appropriate to run a series of excerpts from my book
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1452898049" target="_blank">
          <em>
           The Chinese Dream
          </em>
         </a>
         , which documents Alibabas humble beginning, its triumph over
         <a href="/companies/ebay/">
          eBay
         </a>
         <span class="quotecard_hook" data-exchange="NASDAQ" data-naturalid="fred/company/1387" data-quotes-closing="62.36" data-quotes-now="63.4799" data-ticker="EBAY" data-type="organization">
          <span class="wrapper">
           <a href="/companies/ebay/">
            eBay
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         , and
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/jack-ma/">
          Jack Mas
         </a>
         legendary story.
        </p>
        <p>
         When I first visited Alibabas headquarters in my hometown Hangzhou in 2004, few people in the West had heard of Alibaba. Now, the Chinese e-commerce giant could raise as much as $26 billion in its initial public offering, making it the biggest IPO in history. Jack Ma once said his dream was that Alibaba would become the largest publicly listed Chinese non-state-owned company. It seems that his wildest dream is coming true!
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          In 2004, it was an exciting time for China. Technology start-ups were sprouting up to highlight the already fascinating landscape of the Chinese economy. They ranged from e-commerce sites to video-sharing portals, from Internet search engines to social networking sites. Almost every coastal city has a technology park which is modeled after Silicon Valley and incubates new technology ventures. Venture capital firms from the United States and other countries have been racing into China to look for the next new, new thingalthough they may soon find out that there are totally different kinds of new, new things in China.
         </p>
         <p>
          While visiting Alibabas headquarters in Hangzhou, I felt the same insanely great energy of entrepreneurship as I felt in Silicon Valley. In 2004, eBay had just entered China and was planning to dominate the China market. Alibaba was a local Chinese company that helped small- and medium-sized enterprises conducting
          <a href="http://www.forbes.com/business/">
           business
          </a>
          online. Most people in the West had barely heard about it. When I asked a senior manager at Alibaba whether the company was worried that it would be bought by eBay, I was blown away by the answer: We will buy eBay!
         </p>
         <p>
          Alibabas Jack Ma represents a new generation of savvy competitors and technocrats in China. They study their markets and bring to bear their local knowledge. They learn from their competition and from their own mistakes as they move up the competitive landscape. The case of Alibaba provides an invaluable lesson for multinationals to succeed in the China market.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Jack Ma: See the World Upside Down
         </h3>
         <p>
          In May 2003, the board room at Alibabas headquarters in Hangzhou was filled with smoke from cigarettes. The top management team was intensely debating about a life-and-death decision the company was about to make. A young company just four years old, Alibaba was an e-commerce site for small- and medium-sized enterprises conducting business online. Its charismatic chairman and CEO, Jack Ma, proposed to start a sister site, Taobao, to compete with eBay, which had just entered China.
         </p>
         <p>
          The team had always believed in their leader, who was known as an oddity with an unconventional vision. But this time, they were not sure. eBay had everything that Alibaba could not match: it was a multinational with billions of dollars in revenue, whereas Alibaba was a small local company, still struggling with growing pains and barely making any profit. Moreover, eBay had leading-edge technology and a strong international brand with a record of success, not to mention its top-notch management team, who were mostly graduates from
          <a href="http://www.forbes.com/colleges/harvard-university/harvard-business-school/">
           Harvard Business School
          </a>
          . Alibaba had none of those. It was laughable to even think of the idea. Was Jack Ma really insane?
         </p>
         <p>
          Okay, everyone, Jack Ma said, clapping his hands and rolling up his sleeves. Lets all get up and stand against the wall.
         </p>
         <p>
          Everyone stood up from their chairs, wondering what sleight of hand their boss would show.
         </p>
         <p>
          Now, I want you all to stand on your heads, Jack Ma said.
         </p>
         <p>
          What? You want us to stand upside down? one member of the executive team asked in disbelief.
         </p>
         <p>
          Yes, stand upside down on your heads, Jack Ma said.
         </p>
         <p>
          Oh, no, another member murmured. I have never done that in my life, and I am too old for that.
         </p>
         <p>
          No excuse, Jack Ma said. I am the oldest among this group. Well all stand upside down on our heads.
         </p>
         <p>
          So, everyone in the room, one by one, managed to stand upside down on his or her head like the headstand pose in yoga practice.
         </p>
         <p>
          After they finished the exercise, Jack Ma said, When you stand upside down, youll see the world from a new perspective. Then he turned to the person who said he had never stood on his head. You see, you can do things that you have never done before.
          <a href="/Users/Helen/Documents/Book%20-%20The%20Chinese%20Dream/2nd%20Edition/Wang_Chinese_Dream_2nd_edition_2012_06_08_FINAL.doc#_edn1" title="">
           <br/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Jack Ma continued: EBay may be famous in the United States, but in China, if you ask one hundred people whether theyve heard about eBay, I believe that less than 10 percent have heard of it. But if you ask one hundred people whether theyve heard about Alibaba, 90 percent know about us. Although eBay has acquired four million users (via EachNet, the company eBay acquired to enter China), compared with eighty million Internet users in China (at the time), it was still a small penetration. I believe we have a chance.
          <a href="/Users/Helen/Documents/Book%20-%20The%20Chinese%20Dream/2nd%20Edition/Wang_Chinese_Dream_2nd_edition_2012_06_08_FINAL.doc#_edn2" title="">
           <br/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          Thus began one of the most startling competitions between a global giant and a small maverick company.
         </p>
        </blockquote>
