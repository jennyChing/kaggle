              <h1>
               8 Ways to Ease Psoriatic Arthritis Pain
              </h1>
              <h2>
               Living with chronic pain can make your days challenging, but it shouldn't keep you from living your life. Try these expert tips to relieve psoriatic arthritis pain.
              </h2>
             </div>

               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Managing Joint Pain
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  1
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  Most
forms of arthritis have one thing in common: chronic pain. When you have
                  <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment.aspx">
                   psoriatic
arthritis
                  </a>
                  , that pain can involve any of the joints and range from mild to
severe. Although there's no cure for psoriatic arthritis, you can control the
inflammation that causes pain and can lead to permanent joint damage. Learn
tips from rheumatology and physical therapy experts for managing the chronic
pain associated with psoriatic arthritis.
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="printslide galleria-container">
               <div class="galleria-stage">
                <img alt="pain medications for psoriatic arthritis" class="slideshow-image" data-src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_01_full.jpg" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/blank.gif"/>
                <img alt="pain medications for psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_01_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Pain Medications for Psoriatic Arthritis
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  2
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  <p>
                   Over-the-counter
pain medications such as ibuprofen and naproxen are often used to manage pain
in psoriatic arthritis. If those don't provide enough relief for you, Eric L.
Matteson, MD, chairman of the division of rheumatology at the Mayo Clinic in
Rochester, Minn., also recommends trying non-narcotic prescription medications.
According to Dr. Matteson, the prescription medications change the way the brain's
pain center processes certain proteins that trigger pain. "The idea is to push
down the pain, to change the pain threshold," he says. Although the
prescription drugs aren't narcotics, side effects can include stomach upset and
bad dreams.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Medications
to treat psoriatic arthritis include traditional
                   <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment/faqs-psoriatic-arthritis-dmards-biologics.aspx">
                    disease-modifying
anti-rheumatic drugs
                   </a>
                   (DMARDs) and biologic therapies. Always follow your
treatment plan as prescribed and talk to your doctor before taking a pain
medication.
                  </p>
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="printslide galleria-container">
               <div class="galleria-stage">
                <img alt="stretching and strengthening with psoriatic arthritis" class="slideshow-image" data-src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_02_full.jpg" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/blank.gif"/>
                <img alt="stretching and strengthening with psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_02_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Gentle Stretching and Strengthening
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  3
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  If
you're less active because of psoriatic arthritis pain, your muscles can start
to weaken. And tight, weak muscles can put extra
                  <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment/protect-your-joints-from-psoriatic-arthritis.aspx">
                   pressure on your joints
                  </a>
                  , which
can make pain worse, says Maribeth Gibbon, a physical therapist at the
Cleveland Clinic in Ohio. Stretching and strengthening can increase your range
of motion and build muscle to help take pressure off of your joints. But,
Gibbon says, the key is to determine which muscles to work and how much to work
them. With chronic pain, she recommends stretching to a tolerable limit and
then gradually increasing the stretch over time. Likewise, strength training with
free weights, resistance bands, or your own body weight can help build muscle,
but only do as much as your body allows without leading to more pain and swelling.
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="printslide galleria-container">
               <div class="galleria-stage">
                <img alt="physical activity with psoriatic arthritis" class="slideshow-image" data-src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_03_full.jpg" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/blank.gif"/>
                <img alt="physical activity with psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_03_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Physical Activity
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  4
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  Exercise
is important for maintaining a healthy weight and strong muscles, both of which
can reduce
                  <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment/protect-your-joints-from-psoriatic-arthritis.aspx">
                   pressure on your joints
                  </a>
                  . The National Psoriasis Foundation
recommends getting 30 minutes of moderate exercise at least five times a week. If
joint pain makes it too hard to walk, bike, or run, Gibbon recommends trying
swimming or water aerobics. Several organizations, including the Arthritis
Foundation, offer exercise programs geared toward people with arthritis. Call 800-283-7800
for more information or check out the organization's
                  <a href="http://www.arthritis.org/resources/community-programs/excercise/" target="_new">
                   website
                  </a>
                  .
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="printslide galleria-container">
               <div class="galleria-stage">
                <img alt="heat or cold therapy for psoriatic arthritis" class="slideshow-image" data-src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_04_full.jpg" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/blank.gif"/>
                <img alt="heat or cold therapy for psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_04_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Heat or Cold Therapy
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  5
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  People
who suffer from
                  <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment/different-forms-psoriatic-arthritis-may-take.aspx">
                   chronic pain
                  </a>
                  often find relief through temperature. Most people
respond best to heat, but others might find relief from cold, Gibbon says. For
example, a hot shower or bath in the morning can relax joints. Otherwise, try
using a hot pad or cold pack for up to 20 minutes several times a day. However,
Gibbon cautions against placing heat or ice directly on the skin, especially open
sores, and recommends using a towel or other surface barrier.
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="printslide galleria-container">
               <div class="galleria-stage">
                <img alt="electrical stimulation for psoriatic arthritis" class="slideshow-image" data-src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_05_full.jpg" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/blank.gif"/>
                <img alt="electrical stimulation for psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_05_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Electrical Stimulation
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  6
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  Electrical
stimulation of the nerves is another therapy that can be used to relieve pain.
This is done using a device called a transcutaneous electrical nerve
stimulation (TENS) unit. To operate a TENS unit, the electrodes are placed at
the location of the pain to gently stimulate the nerve cells. The device is often
used in 12-hour intervals, Matteson says. However, skin irritation is a
possible side effect.
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="printslide galleria-container">
               <div class="galleria-stage">
                <img alt="adaptive devices for psoriatic arthritis" class="slideshow-image" data-src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_06_full.jpg" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/blank.gif"/>
                <img alt="adaptive devices for psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_06_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Adaptive Devices
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  7
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  There
are many ways to make it easier and less painful to do everyday tasks. Crutches
and walkers can help some people get around better, Matteson says. In addition,
Gibbon recommends using devices to help with fine motor tasks like buttoning your
shirt and pulling on your socks. She also suggests eating with large-handled
utensils that are easier to hold, trying extended-length shoehorns to put on
your shoes, and switching your shoelaces to elastic ones that stay tied.
                  <div class="galleria-credits">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>

               <div class="galleria-stage">

                <img alt="shoes and arch support for psoriatic arthritis" class="gallery-thumbnail" src="http://images.agoramedia.com/cs/eh/photogallery_psoriatic_arthritis_ways_to_ease_pain_07_full.jpg" style="display:none;"/>
               </div>
               <div class="galleria-info">
                <div class="galleria-info-text">
                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Good Shoes and Arch Support
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">

                  People
with
                  <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment.aspx">
                   psoriatic arthritis
                  </a>
                  often experience pain in the toes, which can make it
difficult to walk or exercise. Wearing supportive shoes with plenty of toe room
and using orthotics, either off the shelf or custom-made, can relieve foot
pain. Orthotics redistribute the pressure on your foot, Gibbon says, and
supportive or cushioned shoes can provide comfort and better skeletal
alignment. She also recommends seeing an arthritis specialist (rheumatologist)
or foot specialist (podiatrist) if foot pain related to psoriatic arthritis is
an issue.

                 <h3 class="galleria-info-title">
                  Stress Reduction and Pacing Yourself
                 </h3>
                 <div class="galleria-info-id">
                  9
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-type">
                  image
                 </div>
                 <div class="galleria-info-description">
                  Relaxing
your muscles is important to
                  <a href="/health-report/psoriatic-arthritis-treatment/how-psoriatic-arthritis-is-treated.aspx">
                   easing the pain of psoriatic arthritis
                  </a>
                  . People
with chronic pain tend to tense their muscles, which actually tightens the
joints and makes the pain worse, Gibbon says. Deep, abdominal breathing and
guided imagery  like closing your eyes and imagining a relaxing
place  can be an effective way to help quiet your muscles. Balancing
your life from day to day is also important, Gibbon says, explaining that rushing
to get as much done as possible on a "good" day may only cause you more pain
later on.
