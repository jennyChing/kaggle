        <h1 class="entry-title">
         11 Tips for Growing a Vegetable Garden on a Deck
        </h1>
        <h2 class="entry-meta">
        </h2>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
        <p>
         This post brought to you by
         <a href="http://izea.it/l3s9t" rel="nofollow">
          Gilmour
         </a>
         . The content and opinions expressed below are that of The Gardening Cook.
        </p>
        <h2 style="text-align: center;">
         Growing a Vegetable Garden on a Deck is easy with these 11 tips.
        </h2>
        <p>
         Growing fresh vegetables is one of the highlights of my summer gardening experience. To me, there is nothing quite like taking out an empty basket and coming back into the house with a basket full of fresh veggie harvest. But if you dont have much space, vegetable gardening might not seem possible. It is though. You can grow an entire Vegetable Garden on a Deck and get a great harvest for your efforts.
         <img alt="11 tips to Grow a Vegetable Garden on a deck from thegardeningcook.com/vegetable-garden-on-a-deck" class="aligncenter wp-image-18830 size-full" height="532" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/vegetable-garden-on-a-deck.png" width="483"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         I have gone back and forth in my mind about my vegetable garden. Two years ago, I had a 1000 square foot garden filled with vegetables. Alas, the squirrels made it their mission to eat the entire crop almost down to the last vegetable. (read about
         <a href="http://thegardeningcook.com/the-squirrels-and-my-vegetable-garden/">
          that disaster here
         </a>
         .) Last year, I transformed that garden into a
         <a href="http://thegardeningcook.com/combining-perennials-and-vegetables/">
          perennial vegetable garden.
         </a>
         None of the vegetables produced much and the tomatoes were a disaster. Not being a person to be defeated by critters or diseased plants, I persevered! This year I am trying to grow an entire vegetable garden on a deck just outside my back door.
        </p>
        <p>
         I have teamed up with a new sponsor,
         <a href="http://izea.it/l3s9r" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Gilmour
         </a>
         . This wonderful company has a wide range of products for your gardening needs, including pruning tools, watering products and a great variety of garden hoses. They are a trusted and reliable brand with decades of American heritage. One of their products which is of special interest to me is their Flexogen hose.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://izea.it/l3s9r" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          <img alt="Gilmour Flexogen" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-19212" height="368" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/flexogen-hose-glimour.png" width="500"/>
         </a>
         You simply cannot beat their
         <a href="http://izea.it/l3s9s" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Flexogen hose
         </a>
         . It has so many wonderful features:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          It has been proudly made in the USA for over 40 years and has a lifetime warranty.
         </li>
         <li>
          It is perfect for any type of homemaker, especially those, like me, who put their tools to the ultimate test and expect them to really last.
         </li>
         <li>
          The Flexogen hose is an unbeatable combination of quality, flexibility and durability.
         </li>
         <li>
          And it is the only 8 layer hose on the market with a one-of-a-kind patented construction (many other hoses are made with only six layers.) With these features, it is easy to see why the Flexogen hose is the longest-lasting and most durable hose in its class.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <a href="http://izea.it/l3s9r" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          Gilmour
         </a>
         provided me with both their Flexogen hose and a wonderful durable metal spray nozzle to test for them this summer and tell all of my readers about the quality of the tools and how well they work for me. I am just delighted to team up with them to test this and other products for them and to give my readers an opportunity to see the garden tools in action.
         <img alt="durable hose nozzle" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-18855" height="509" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/garden-nozzle.jpg" width="427"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         I decided to put the hose and nozzle to use in my latest vegetable garden project. I plan to grow my vegetable garden (as well as lots of flowers) completely on my deck. This summer the squirrels and rabbits will not defeat me! (that is my new mantra  I repeat it daily!) I am bringing the garden up close and personal as they say. It resides on my deck in very large pots. I figure that I will be able to keep an eye on the critters and keep the plants in tip top condition. So far, it is working pretty well. Keep tuned over the summer to see how it does!
         <img alt="11 tips for growing a vegetable garden on a deck" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-19025" height="1009" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/veggie-garden-deck.jpg" width="650"/>
        </p>
        <h2>
         11 Tips for Growing Vegetables on a Deck.
        </h2>
        <p>
         Do you wish you had a vegetable garden but dont think you have the room? Think again! The following tips and photos show just what you can grow successfully in a very small space. Just follow these few tips and then start planting:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Watering
          </strong>
          . Be sure your pots are within easy reach of your watering system. (this is where my Flexogen hose and nozzle are perfectly placed!)
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Size of pots
          </strong>
          . Have pots that will accommodate the size of the plant you want to grow. (there is no way a 5+ foot tomato plant will grow in a 5 gallon pot. It needs room for the roots!) Think of the final size of the plant when you choose the pot.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Think Big
          </strong>
          . Err on the large size for pots. You wont go wrong, I promise. The plants will need less water and get larger too. Think 12 inches at the smallest and up to 20 inches or larger for the biggest ones, such as tomato plants.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Work area
          </strong>
          . Have a small potting area nearby. I take
          <a href="http://thegardeningcook.com/propagating-tomato-plants-with-cuttings/">
           cuttings of my tomato plants
          </a>
          for new plants later in the year and start my seedlings right on my deck.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Room to Tend.
          </strong>
          Be sure you have room, either outside the deck or near the pots, for plant maintenance. You will want to inspect, water and tend to them easily without moving furniture.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Supports
          </strong>
          . Have stakes and climbing support ready. I put my stakes in with the seedlings so as not to disturb the roots later. There are many things to use for support for cucumbers and beans. (I used pieces of old garden wire fencing. I just poked them into the earth and they allow beans to climb and supports for cucumbers too!)
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Plastic vs Terra cotta?
          </strong>
          I have both, but be aware that terra cotta pots need more watering because of their porous nature so plastic may be a better choice if water conservation is important to you.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Color of pots.
          </strong>
          Very dark or black pots will absorb the heat, so lighter colors work better and will need less watering and be gentler on the roots.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Soil for the vegetables
          </strong>
          . The soil needs to be well draining, and soil made especially for containers will give the best results, so no just digging up garden soil!
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           How to get started.
          </strong>
          It doesnt matter whether you plant seeds or seedlings. Water first before you plant. The seeds will get a better start and the seedlings wont be stressed when they are transplanted.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Good tools make for a better garden.
          </strong>
          Having the right tools on hand can make all the difference. For this project, the Gilmour Flexogen hose and their durable hose nozzle made watering my vegetable garden a breeze. There is no substitute for good garden tools. They last so much longer than cheap imitations and save you money in the long run by providing excellent use for years to come!
         </li>
        </ul>

         The Deck Garden in Photos.
        </h2>
        <p>

        </p>
        <p>
         I have a very large deck which measure about 14 x 25 feet. It has ample room for seating, dining, a potting area and a BBQ area, PLUS the vegetable garden and several areas for flowers. Is is amazing, what can be grown on a deck, isnt it?
        </p>
        <p>

         <a href="http://thegardeningcook.com/my-test-garden-in-july/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
          test garden
         </a>
         and we spend a lot of time sitting there.
         <img alt="cozy seating area" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-18817" height="481" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/cozy-seating-area.jpg" width="500"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         The other area has a large table and umbrella set up for home barbeques and guests. Even with those two areas, there is still a ton of space left for containers.
         <img alt="dining area" class="aligncenter wp-image-18818" height="377" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/dining-area.jpg" width="501"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         I also have a tiered plant stand which holds my seedlings and cuttings  a great way to increase my harvest and have plants ready for succession planting when the time is right. It holds lots of pots and some of my tools as well. It is just a few steps from my table that doubles as a work station when I am potting.
         <img alt="Potting area" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-18819" height="676" src="http://thegardeningcook.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/potting-area.jpg" width="500"/>
