         <p>
          The black market for illicit substances occupies a significant portion ofthe global economy. Although measuring its socioeconomic impact has itspitfalls due to the inherently clandestine nature of the industry, economistsand researchers have compiled an impressive inventory of studies, evidence, andconjecture about the international drug trade. Individuals with severe, chronicdrug abuse problems have a negative impact on the economy, as well. Explore the number ofintriguing facts about drugs and drug abuse.
         </p>
         <h2>
          The Global IllicitDrug Industry is Larger Than Several World Markets
         </h2>
         <p>
          The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime aggregates volumes of data fromvarious law enforcement agencies around the globe to determine the capitalturnover linked to global drug markets. They once estimated the global illicitdrug market was around
          <a href="http://www.unodc.org/pdf/technical_series_1998-01-01_1.pdf" target="_blank" title="$400 billion">
           $400 billion
          </a>
          and larger than markets for iron/steel, motor vehicles,textiles/clothing, and tourism. To put that in perspective, that was about fivepercent of the United States GDP in 1995, and almost six times more thanofficial development aid worldwide.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Drug Abusers Createa Void in Labor Productivity
         </h2>
         <p>
          The Office of National Drug Control Policy estimated the United Stateseconomy
          <a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/ondcp/ondcp-fact-sheets/how-illicit-drug-use-affects-business-and-the-economy" target="_blank" title="lost $120 billion in 2007">
           lost $120 billion in 2007
          </a>
          due to costs associated with lostworkplace productivity, participation in drug abuse treatment, incarceration,and premature death linked to chronic drug abusers. The problem continues togrow; In 2009, the ONDCP also estimated that around 34 percent of drug usersover the age of 18 were either unemployed (13 percent) or not in the laborforce (21 percent). Those that did work full-time were twice as likely toreport working for three or more employers in a single year and were about fivepercent more likely to report missing days from work the month beforeresearchers conducted the study.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Drug Abuse Coststhe Health Industry Billions Every Year
         </h2>
         <p>
          According to the National Drug Intelligence Center in Washington, D.C.,illicit drug use
          <a href="http://www.drugwarfacts.org/cms/Economics#sthash.nHUoPI35.dpbs" target="_blank" title="chalked up an $11,416,232,000 bill">
           chalked up an $11,416,232,000 bill
          </a>
          with the American healthcare industry in 2011 alone. Of that figure, doctors spent $3.7billion onspecialty treatment costs, $5.6 billion for non-homicide emergency care, $12.9million for homicide-related emergency care, $544,000 on administrativeinsurance costs, and almost $2 billion in miscellaneous expenses. Watching this
          <a href="http://www.12keysrehab.com/blog/economics-of-drug-abuse-video" target="_blank" title="economics of drug abuse video">
           economics of drug abuse video
          </a>
          will make you think twicebefore you turn to the pipe or needle for a rush.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Two-Thirds ofCountries Have GDPs Less Than What the U.S. Spends on Drug-Related LawEnforcement Expenses
         </h2>
         <p>
          Numbers from the National Drug Intelligence Center show that the UnitedStates spent $61,376,694,000 as a result of drug-related crime costs in 2007:$56 billion spent on the criminal justice system, $1.4 billion on crimevictims, and just over $3.5 billion in other miscellaneous drug-related crimecosts. To help you wrap your mind around that figure, only 63 countries hadGDPs (gross domestic products) over $61 billion that same year. You could alsobuild around 8,714 elementary schools for that same price.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Drug Purity and Drug Price are Moving in Opposite Directions
         </h2>
         <p>
          For the past three decades, the Office of National Drug Control Policytracked the relative purity and price of drugs such as cocaine and heroinacross five different major metropolitan hubs. They discovered the price ofthose substance gradually decreased while purity has gradually increased.Canadas International Centre for Science in Drug Policy
          <a href="http://www.havocscope.com/price-of-illegal-drugs-goes-down-in-the-us-while-purity-goes-up/" target="_blank" title="reported similar findings">
           reported similar findings
          </a>
          : Since 1990, the price of heroinand cocaine dropped around 80 percent each. However, cocainepurityincreased by 11 percent and heroins by 60 percent. What makesthese figures so alarming is their residual effectsif drugs and stronger andcheaper than ever before, then cases of drug abuse and overdose are sure toskyrocket.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.drugfree.org/untaxed/new-data-show-millions-of" target="_blank" title="Studies show">
           Studies show
          </a>
          some 23 million Americans are addicted toalcohol or illicit substances. However, nine in 10 of those people arentreceiving treatment for their addictions. If youre suffering from the sideeffects of drug abuse, it may be time to seek help and avoid becoming anotherstatistic.
