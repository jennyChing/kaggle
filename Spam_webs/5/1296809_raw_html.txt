       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         Claiming dependents on your tax return can make a big difference in what you pay in taxes (or how big a refund you get). Each child you can claim as a dependent on your 2014 tax return knocks $3,950 off your taxable income, saving $988 in the 25 percent bracket. But children aren't the only ones you can claim as dependents.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="" height="424" src="https://images.turbotax.intuit.com/iqcms/marketing/lib/articlepics2/12trickydependentquestions_INF12074.jpg" width="640"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <meta content="https://images.turbotax.intuit.com/iqcms/marketing/lib/articlepics2/12trickydependentquestions_INF12074.jpg" property="og:image"/>
         <meta content="https://images.turbotax.intuit.com/iqcms/marketing/lib/articlepics2/12trickydependentquestions_INF12074.jpg" name="twitter:image:src"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Despite attempts by the IRS to clarify whether or not someone qualifies as a dependent, today's complex living arrangements often raise questions as to just whom can be claimed on your tax return. There are now two classes of people who can qualify as dependents:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Qualifying children
         </li>
         <li>
          Qualifying relatives
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         See how the IRS defines dependents
         <a href="http://www.irs.gov/publications/p501/ar02.html#en_US_publink100041829" target="_blank">
          here
         </a>
         . The following Q&amp;As can also help clear up the confusion about who can and cannot be claimed as a dependent.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Birth of a Child
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          The rules say a qualifying child must live with you for more than half the year. My daughter was born in October. Does that mean I have to wait until next year to claim her?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. No. Even a child born on December 31 qualifies as a dependent and earns the full $3,900 exemption. The same rule applies if a child dies during the year. A child who is born or dies during the year is treated as having lived with you all year long. Check out
         <a href="https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tools/tax-tips/Family/Birth-of-a-Child/INF12019.html">
          this article
         </a>
         for more tips.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Living Together I
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My girlfriend and I live together. She doesnt have a job, so I pay for the rent and all the groceries. Can I claim her as my dependent?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Perhaps, if she meets the requirements for a qualifying relative. That means you must have lived together all year long, her gross income must be less than $3,900, and you must have provided more than half of her support. One other test: Your living arrangements must not violate local law. The IRS notes, for example, that some states prohibit couples from living together if one party is married to someone else. In such a case, the IRS says, a dependency exemption would be disallowed even if the other requirements are met.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Living Together II
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My girlfriend and her two-year-old son live with me and I basically pay all the expenses. Can I claim both of them as my dependents?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Yes, if they meet all the IRS requirements for dependents. Did they live with you all year long? Did you provide more than half of their support? Did either of them have gross income of less than $3,900? If you can answer YES to all three questions, then you may claim both
         <a href="http://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tools/tax-tips/Family/Can-I-Claim-a-Boyfriend-Girlfriend-As-a-Dependent-on-Income-Taxes--/INF14242.html">
          your girlfriend
         </a>
         and her son as your dependents. (This assumes your living arrangements dont violate local law. See above.) Until recently, in this situation the boyfriend could not claim the child as a qualifying relative because the child was considered a qualifying child of the mother. However, the IRS now says if the parents income is so low that he or she doesnt have to file a tax return, then the boyfriend who lives with the mother and child all year long can claim the child as a dependent.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Boomerang Children I
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. After our 28-year-old daughters divorce, she and her two young children moved back in with me and my wife. Can we claim all three of them as dependents on our tax return?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. The answer depends on how much money your daughter made in 2014. If she made less than $3,950 and you provided more than half of her support for the year, then she can be claimed as your dependent as a qualifying relative. The same rules apply to your grandchildren. If your daughter made more than $3,950, she doesnt qualify as your dependent, but the grandkids might because they can be qualifying children for both you and your daughter. If your daughter agrees to let you claim the children as your dependents, and her Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) is less than yours assuming doing so will save the family money if youre in a higher tax bracketthen you may claim them. In that case, of course, your daughter could not claim them. If your daughter's AGIis greater than yours then she can claim the grandchildren but you cannot.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Boomerang Children II
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. Our 25-year-old son is back home after completing his college degree. He has a pretty good job, so he makes too much for us to claim him as a dependent. However, weve heard that theres a way he can claim his 16-year-old sister as a dependent, since we all live together in the same house. Is that really possible?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. The answer is maybe  depending on your income and your son's. In order for your son to claim his sister as a dependent, his adjusted gross income (AGI) must be higher than any parent who could also claim her as a dependent, regardless of whether you choose to claim her or not. This is called a "tiebreaker" rule, which helps determine who, if anyone, can claim a dependent. Remember, only one person can claim the dependent in any given tax year.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         In addition, your 16-year-old daughter would also have to meet the all of the other qualifications for being her brother's dependent (living with her brother more than half the year, not providing more than half of her own support, etc.). For the full list of qualifications, see
         <a href="http://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tools/tax-tips/Family/Rules-for-Claiming-a-Dependent-on-Your-Tax-Return/INF12139.html" target="_self">
          Rules for Claiming a Dependent
         </a>
         .
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Children of Divorced Parents
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My divorce was final last year, and the three kids live with me. Now my ex says that since hes paying child support, hes going to claim them as dependents on his return. He says that means I cant claim them on mine. Is that true?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Not unless your divorce decree gives him that right. Since the general rule for qualifying children demands that the child live with you more than half the year, children of divorced parents are usually dependents of the custodial parent. There are exceptions. The custodial parent can release the exemption to his or her ex-spouse by signing a written declaration (
         <a href="https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tools/tax-tips/IRS-Tax-Forms/What-is-Form-8332--Release-Revocation-of-Release-of-Claim-to-Exemption-for-Child-by-Custodial-Parent/INF26050.html" target="_blank" title="IRS Form 8332">
          Form 8332
         </a>
         ) that the noncustodial spouse must attach to the tax return each year he or she claims the children as dependents. Or if your divorce decree gives your ex-spouse the right to claim the children, he can do so if he attaches key pages of that document to his tax return. Otherwise, you get to claim the children as your dependents. If your ex-spouse claims them, too, the IRS will step in and likely deny his claim. Also read
         <a href="https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tools/tax-tips/Family/What-Happens-When-Both-Parents-Claim-a-Child-on-a-Tax-Return-/INF14352.html">
          What Happens When Both Parents Claim a Child on a Tax Return
         </a>
         for more information on this topic.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Adult Child in Need
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. Our 30-year-old son has fallen on hard times. Because he lost his job, my spouse and I are basically supporting him, paying rent on his apartment and sending him money for food. Can we claim him as a dependent?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Although hes too old to be your qualifying child, he may qualify as a qualifying relative if he earned less than $3,950 in 2014. If thats the case and you provided more than half of his support during the year, you may claim him as a dependent.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Elderly Parent
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My 83-year-old mother moved in with me when she could no longer live alone. Her only income is her Social Security, so she doesnt have to file a tax return. Can I claim her as my dependent?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Yes, assuming you provide more than half of her support, she can pass the test as a qualifying relative. Tax-free Social Security benefits dont count as gross income for the $3,900 test. When figuring that portion of her support you provide, include a value for the housing you provide. If someone else helps support your motherone of your brothers or sisters, for exampleand your combined support passes the 50 percent threshold, you may claim your mother as a dependent if you file a
         <a href="http://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/f2120.pdf" target="_blank" title="IRS Form 2120, Multiple Support Agreement">
          Form 2120: Multiple Support Agreement
         </a>
         . Also read
         <a href="https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tools/tax-tips/Family/Steps-to-Claiming-an-Elderly-Parent-as-a-Dependent/INF19455.html">
          Steps to Claiming an Elderly Parent as a Dependent
         </a>
         for more tips.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Child with Scholarship
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My daughter won a full-ride scholarship to an expensive college. Im thrilled, but I think the value of the tuition is more than what it costs me to feed and clothe the young scholar. If I dont provide more than half of support, do I lose her as a dependent?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Dont worry. First, scholarships are specifically excluded when figuring support. And remember, the test for a qualifying child is no longer that you provide more than half the support, but that she does not provide more than half of her own support. You can still claim her, assuming shes under age 24.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Child of Separated Parents
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My wife and I separated at the end of September. Our 15-year-old son lived with me in October and with his mother the rest of the year. Well be filing married-filing-separate returns this year. Who gets to claim our son as a dependent?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Its up to you and your wife. You might decide that the parent who gets the biggest tax benefit (the one in the higher tax bracket) should claim the exemption. If you cant agree, however, the exemption goes to your wife because your son lived with her for more of the year than he lived with you.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Unmarried Parents
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. My boyfriend and I live together with our 3-year-old son. Since were not married, we cant file a joint return. Which one of us gets to claim our son as a dependent
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Its up to you. Since he qualifies as a qualifying child for each of you, either parent may claim the child as a dependent. If you cant decide, the exemption goes to whichever of you reports the higher Adjusted Gross Income on your separate tax return.
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <!--index returns 0 for the first paragraph all will be ignored -->
      <div class="article-content">
       <h3 class="article-subtitle">
        Child Receives Inheritance
       </h3>
       <div class="article-subtext">
        <p>
         <b>
          Q. Our 17-year-old received a $100,000 inheritance from his uncle last year. Does that mean we cant claim him as a dependent on our 2014 return?
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         A. Not unless he splurged on an expensive car, a lavish trip, or otherwise spent a lot of the money on his own behalf. When it comes to the qualifying child tests, it doesnt matter how much money a child receives during the year (from work, a gift or inheritance). What matters is if he provides more than half of his own support. Any money he saved doesnt count toward going toward his support, so you can probably still claim him as your dependent.
         <i>
          <br/>
         </i>
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
