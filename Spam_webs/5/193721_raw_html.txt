     <section id="article-section-1">
      <p>
       You may not realize this, but we can see you. Yes,
       <em>
        you
       </em>
       . The human reading this article. We have analytics that tells us roughly where you are, what site you've just arrived from, how long you stay, how far you read, where you hop to next. We've got eyeballs on your eyeballs.
      </p>
      <p>
       Why is it so important that digital news organizations track which articles you're reading on our websites? The obvious answer is that it teaches us what you're interested in. The less-obvious, but equally true, answer is that it teaches
       <em>
        you
       </em>
       what
       <em>
        you're
       </em>
       interested in.
      </p>
      <p>
       If we merely asked what you wanted, without
       <em>
        measuring
       </em>
       what you wanted, you'd just keep lying to usand to yourself.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here's what I mean by lying. This year, the Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism asked thousands of people around the world what sort of news was most important to them. The graph below shows the responses from Americans. International news crushed celebrity and "fun" news by a margin of two-to-one. Economic and political news finished even higher.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_business&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_business&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector1&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=16"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       <figure class="" style="max-width: 570.0px;">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:67.7192982456%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/news1.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </figure>
      </p>
      <p>
       But what happens when we stop asking readers what's important and start looking at what they actually read?
      </p>
      <p>
       Let's start with today. The most important story in the world, according to every major American newspaper this morning, is the violent splintering of Iraq. It was the front-page and top-of-the-homepage story in the
       <em>
        Washington Post
       </em>
       , the
       <em>
        Wall Street Journal
       </em>
       , the
       <em>
        New York Times
       </em>
       , and more.
      </p>
      <p>
       Surely, there are millions of people who are reading about Iraq, because they're fascinated in the Middle East, in foreign policy, or in the general news cycle. But despite Iraq's prominent location on every major newspaper, the
       <em>
        most
       </em>
       -
       <em>
        read
       </em>
       stories on those papers' websites aren't about Iraq, at all.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <em>
        Post,
       </em>
       the top stories included an op-ed about
       <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/dana-milbank-heritages-ugly-benghazi-panel/2014/06/16/b8bd423c-f5a3-11e3-a606-946fd632f9f1_story.html?tid=pm_pop">
        Benghazi
       </a>
       , and updates about the World Cup and a midwest tornado.
       <em>
        WSJ
       </em>
       's most-read box led off with two stories about YouTube games and taxes. The
       <em>
        Times
       </em>
       ' most-emailed stories included two pieces about gluten and p
       <span>
        ostpartum depression.
       </span>
       Not one of the most-read or most-emailed boxes on three papers' websites included the words
       <em>
        Iraq
       </em>
       ,
       <em>
        Sunni
       </em>
       , or
       <em>
        Maliki
       </em>
       when I looked this morning.
      </p>
      <p>
       Iraq is a uniquely difficult news story. But there's nothing unique about U.S. readers side-stepping the news cycle. Last year,
       <span>
        BuzzFeed released a
        <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2014/02/the-facebook-effect-on-the-news/283746/">
         review of traffic to sites within its partner network
        </a>
        , including the
       </span>
       <em>
        New York Times
       </em>
       and
       <em>
        The Atlantic
       </em>
       . Of the 20 most viral stories across those sites, just three dealt with recent news eventsthe Miss America Pageant, a Netflix announcement, and the Video Music Awards but the vast majority weren't news. They were quizzes, lists, and emotional poppers.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span tabindex="-1">
        <a href="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/Screen%20Shot%202014-02-11%20at%205.26.01%20PM.png">
         <figure class="" style="max-width: 570.0px;">
          <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:112.105263158%;">
           <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/Screen%20Shot%202014-02-11%20at%205.26.01%20PM.png" data-widget="cropduster" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
          </picture>
         </figure>
         <figure class="" style="max-width: 14.0px;">
          <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:100.0%;">
           <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAPABAP///wAAACH5BAEKAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAICRAEAOw%3D%3D" draggable="true" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
          </picture>
         </figure>
        </a>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ask audiences what they want, and they'll tell you vegetables. Watch them quietly, and they'll mostly eat candy.
      </p>
      <p>
       Audiences are liars, and the media organizations who listen to them without measuring them are dupes. At the Aspen Ideas Festival last year, Ehab Al Shihabi,
       <span class="st">
        executive director of international operations for
       </span>
       Al Jazeera America, shared survey data suggesting that
       <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/business/archive/2013/06/whats-the-matter-with-tv-news/277316/">
        40 to 50 million people were desperate for in-depth and original TV journalism
       </a>
       . Nine months later, it averaged
       <a href="http://Al%20Jazeera%20America%20was%20averaging%20about%2010,000%20viewers%20at%20any%20given%20time%20of%20the%20day,%20while%20CNN%20had%20272,000,%20MSNBC%20349,000%20and%20Fox%20News%20924,000.%20But%20it%20is%20important%20to%20understand%20that%20Al%20Jazeera%20America%20is%20only%20in%2050%20million%20to%2055%20million%20of%20the%20nation's%20110%20million%20homes">
        10,000 viewers per hour
       </a>
       1.08 percent of Fox News' audience and 3.7 percent of CNN. AJAM, built for an audience of vegetarians, is stuck with a broccoli stand in a candy shop.
      </p>
     </section>
     <div class="ad-boxinjector-wrapper">
      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector2">
       <noscript>
        <div>
         <a href="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/jump?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_business&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=15" title="">
          <img alt="" src="http://pubads.g.doubleclick.net/gampad/ad?iu=%2F4624%2FTheAtlanticOnline%2Fchannel_business&amp;t=src%3Darticle%26pos%3Dboxinjector2&amp;sz=300x250%7C320x350%7C728x90%7C768x350%7C970x250%7C1024x350&amp;c=857697270&amp;tile=15"/>
         </a>
        </div>
       </noscript>
      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-3">
      <p>
       The culprit isn't Millennials, or Facebook, or analytics software like Chartbeat. The problem is our brains. The more attention-starved we feel, the more we thirst for stimuli that are familiar. We like ice cream when we're sad, old songs when we're tired, and easy listicles when we're busy and ego-depleted. The Internet shorthand for this fact is "cat pictures." Psychologists prefer the term
       <em>
        fluency.
       </em>
       Fluency isn't how we think: It's how we feel while we're thinking. We prefer thoughts that come easily: Faces that are symmetrical, colors that are clear, and sentences with parallelisms. In this light, there are two problems with hard news: It's
       <em>
        hard
       </em>
       and it's
       <em>
        new
       </em>
       . (Parallelism!)
      </p>
      <p>
       Fluency also explains one of the truisms of political news: That most liberals prefer to read and watch liberals (because it feels easy), while conservatives prefer to read and watch conservatives (because it feels easy). It's a not-even-industry-secret that down-the-middle political reporting that doesn't massage old biases is a hard sell for TV audiences. Fox News has monopolized the market of 60-and-overs watching cable news, predominantly because that group watches the most cable news and naturally skews conservative. Grappling with new information is exhausting, so we prefer to consume it in
       <a href="http://www.vox.com/">
        explicitly digestible lists
       </a>
       or wrapped in old viewpoints we already have.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before there were eyeballs on your eyeballs, it was difficult to know exactly what you were reading. The
       <em>
        Times
       </em>
       knew you subscribed to the
       <em>
        Times
       </em>
       , for example, but we couldn't see exactly what pages you were reading. We could survey readers to learn what they wanted, but readers lie.
      </p>
      <p>
       The analytic age of journalism has its detractors, but with regard to serving our audience, it gets us closer than ever to that highest purpose of journalism: learning the ugly truth.
      </p>
     </section>

        <h3 class="hed">
         Donald Trump Is No Conservative
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/conor-friedersdorf/" title="Conor Friedersdorf">
          Conor Friedersdorf
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         His early support in the GOP reflects an inability to distinguish between plausible champions and charlatans.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          There is an Ivy League grad who has spent most of his life in Manhattan, where he is chauffeured around in limousines. He frequently brags to strangers about his massive personal wealth. In public statements, he has advocated government healthcare, a womans right to an abortion, an assault weapons ban,
          <span>
           and
           <a href="http://www.cnn.com/ALLPOLITICS/stories/1999/11/09/trump.rich/index.html?_s=PM:ALLPOLITICS">
            paying off the national debt
           </a>
           by forcing rich people
          </span>
          <span>
           <em>
           </em>
           to forfeit 14.25 percent of their total wealth.

       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Arguments for and against demoting Pluto
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p icap="on">
          Pluto has been shrinking at an alarming rate
for some time now. Well, not actually shrinkingrather, our awareness of how
small Pluto is has been growing. Upon its discovery, in 1930, scientists
trumpeted that Pluto was about as large as Earth. By the 1960s textbooks were
listing it as having a diameter about half that of Earth. In 1978 astronomers
discovered that Pluto has a relatively large moon, whose brightness had been
mistakenly lumped in with the planet's; when this was taken into account, Pluto
was left with a diameter about a sixth that of Earth, or less than half that of
Mercurylong considered the runt of the solar system. Seven moons in the solar
system are bigger than Pluto.
         </p>
         <p>
          In addition to being out of place among the planets in terms of size, Pluto has
always seemed conceptually lost as well. The four innermost planets are rocky
and of modest size; the next four are gas giants. What was a lone, tiny ice
ball doing way out at the edge of the solar system? A surprising answer has
emerged over the past few years. Pluto, it turns out, is one of at least sixty,
and possibly hundreds of thousands of, small, cometlike objects in a belt that
extends far beyond the confines of the planets.
         </p>

       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Lincoln Heights, Ohio, once had the potential to become a thriving community, but today its in crisis. This is no accident.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            LINCOLN HEIGHTS, OhioAfrican Americans started coming to Cincinnati more than a century ago, fleeing the violence and economic constraints of the South for jobs and homes.
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            But redlining and other restrictive zoning laws prohibited black families from buying homes in many of the citys neighborhoods. So when developers started selling off lots of unincorporated land north of Cincinnati to black buyers, it seemed like a good opportunity, one of the few paths to homeownership in the segregated North.
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            The land had no paved roads and no streetlights. Few homes had running water and there was no police or fire protection. Carl Westmoreland, who grew up in this village in the 1940s, remembers watching black men rush over a hill toward a burning home with a small fire cart theyd bought. They didnt save it in time, but the neighborhood banded together and rebuilt the house together. He refers to the community at the time as Americas Soweto for the primitive living conditions there.
           </span>
          </span>
