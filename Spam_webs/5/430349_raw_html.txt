     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Paranoia
       </b>
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">
       dystopian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
       science-fiction
      </a>
      tabletop
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role-playing_game_(pen_and_paper)" title="Role-playing game (pen and paper)">
       role-playing game
      </a>
      originally designed and written by
      <a href="/wiki/Greg_Costikyan" title="Greg Costikyan">
       Greg Costikyan
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dan_Gelber_(game_designer)" title="Dan Gelber (game designer)">
       Dan Gelber
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Goldberg_(game_designer)" title="Eric Goldberg (game designer)">
       Eric Goldberg
      </a>
      , and first published in 1984 by
      <a href="/wiki/West_End_Games" title="West End Games">
       West End Games
      </a>
      . Since 2004 the game has been published under licence by
      <a href="/wiki/Mongoose_Publishing" title="Mongoose Publishing">
       Mongoose Publishing
      </a>
      . The game won the
      <a href="/wiki/Origins_Award" title="Origins Award">
       Origins Award
      </a>
      for Best Roleplaying Rules of 1984
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_1984_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-origins_1984-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was inducted into the Origins Awards Hall of Fame in 2007.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origins_2007_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-origins_2007-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      is notable among tabletop games for being more competitive than co-operative, with players encouraged to betray one another for their own interests, as well as for keeping a lighthearted, tongue in cheek tone despite its dystopian setting.
     </p>
     <p>
      The game is set in a dystopian future city which is controlled by an artificial intelligence construct called 'The Computer', and where information (including the game rules) are restricted by color-coded 'security clearance'. Players are initially enforcers of The Computer's authority, and will be given missions to seek out and eliminate threats to The Computer's control. The players are also part of prohibited underground movements, and will have secret objectives including theft from and murder of other players.
     </p>
     <p>
      Several editions of the game have been published since the original version, and the franchise has spawned several spin-offs, novels and comic books based on the game.
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a class="external text" href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1990654819/paranoia-rpg" rel="nofollow">
       Kickstarter
      </a>
      has successfully been funded for a new edition.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Paranoia
       </b>
      </i>
      is a humorous
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Role-playing_game_(pen_and_paper)" title="Role-playing game (pen and paper)">
       role-playing game
      </a>
      set in a
      <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">
       dystopian future
      </a>
      similar to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four" title="Nineteen Eighty-Four">
        Nineteen Eighty-Four
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Brave_New_World" title="Brave New World">
        Brave New World
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Logan%27s_Run" title="Logan's Run">
        Logan's Run
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/THX_1138" title="THX 1138">
        THX 1138
       </a>
      </i>
      among others; however, the tone of the game is rife with
      <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">
       black humor
      </a>
      , frequently
      <a href="/wiki/Tongue-in-cheek" title="Tongue-in-cheek">
       tongue-in-cheek
      </a>
      rather than dark and heavy.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">
       Setting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Setting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The game's main setting is an immense and futuristic city called Alpha Complex, which is controlled by The Computer, a
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">
       civil service
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">
       AI
      </a>
      construct (a literal realization of the "
      <a href="/wiki/On_the_Origin_of_the_%22Influencing_Machine%22_in_Schizophrenia" title='On the Origin of the "Influencing Machine" in Schizophrenia'>
       Influencing Machine
      </a>
      " that some schizophrenics fear). The Computer serves as the game's principal
      <a href="/wiki/Antagonist" title="Antagonist">
       antagonist
      </a>
      , and fears a number of threats to its 'perfect' society, such as The Outdoors,
      <a href="/wiki/Mutant_(fictional)" title="Mutant (fictional)">
       mutants
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">
       secret societies
      </a>
      (especially
      <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">
       Communists
      </a>
      ). To deal with these threats, The Computer employs
      <i>
       Troubleshooters
      </i>
      , whose job is to go out, find trouble, and shoot it.
      <a href="/wiki/Player_character" title="Player character">
       Player characters
      </a>
      are usually Troubleshooters, although later game supplements have allowed the players to take on other roles.
     </p>
     <p>
      The player characters frequently receive mission instructions from the Computer that are incomprehensible, self-contradictory, or obviously fatal if adhered to, and side-missions which conflict the main mission. They are issued equipment that is uniformly dangerous, faulty or "experimental" (i.e. almost certainly dangerous
      <i>
       and
      </i>
      faulty). Additionally, each player character is generally an unregistered mutant and a secret society member, and has a hidden agenda separate from the group's goals, often involving stealing from or killing teammates. Thus, missions often turn into a comedy of errors, as everyone on the team seeks to double-cross everyone else while keeping their own secrets. The game's manual encourages suspicion between players, offering several tips on how to make the gameplay as paranoid as possible.
     </p>
     <p>
      Every player's character is assigned six clones, known as a "six-pack," which are used to replace the preceding clone upon his or her death. The game lacks a conventional health system; most wounds the player characters can suffer are assumed to be fatal. As a result,
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      allows characters to be routinely killed, yet the player can continue instead of leaving the game. This easy spending of clones tends to lead to frequent firefights, gruesome slapstick, and the horrible yet humorous demise of most if not all of the player character's clone family. Additional clones can be purchased if one gains sufficient favour with the Computer.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      rulebook is unusual in a number of ways; demonstrating any knowledge of the rules is forbidden, and most of the rulebook is written in an easy, conversational tone that often makes fun of the players and their characters, while occasionally taking digs at other notable role-playing games.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Security_clearances">
       Security clearances
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Security clearances">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      features a security clearance system based on colors of the
      <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
       visible spectrum
      </a>
      which heavily restricts what the players can and cannot legally do; everything from corridors to food and equipment have security restrictions. The lowest rating is Infrared, but the lowest playable security clearance is Red; the game usually begins with the characters having just been promoted to Red grade. Interfering with anything which is above that player's clearance carries significant risk.
     </p>
     <p>
      The full order of clearances from lowest to highest is Infrared (visually represented by Black), Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet, and Ultraviolet (visually represented by White). Within the game, Infrared-clearance citizens live dull lives of mindless drudgery and are heavily medicated, while higher clearance characters may be allowed to demote or even summarily execute those of a lower rank and those with Ultraviolet clearance are almost completely unrestricted and have a great deal of access to The Computer; they are the only citizens that may (legally) access and modify the Computer's programming, and thus Ultraviolet citizens are also referred to as "High Programmers". Security clearance is not related to competence or even authority: clearance is instead a measure of The Computer's trust in a citizen.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Secret_Societies">
       Secret Societies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Secret Societies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the game,
      <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">
       secret societies
      </a>
      tend to be based on sketchy and spurious knowledge of historical matters. For example, previous editions included societies such as the "Seal Club" that idolizes the Outdoors but is unsure what plants and animals actually look like. Even earlier editions, published by West End Games, had it named as the "Sierra Club". Other societies include the Knights of the Circular Object (based on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Round_Table" title="Knights of the Round Table">
       Knights of the Round Table
      </a>
      ), the Trekkies, and the First Church of Christ Computer Programmer. In keeping with the theme of paranoia, many secret societies have spies or double agents in each other's organizations. The 1st edition also included secret societies such as Programs Groups (the personal agents and spies of the High Programmers at the apex of Alpha Complex society) and Spy For Another Alpha Complex.
     </p>
     <p>
      Of special notice is the secret society known as the Wobblies. The game's backstory indicates that the Computer was worried about this society, and sent a pack of Troubleshooters to investigate. Since the society didn't actually exist, the Troubleshooters found nothing to report, and were terminated for laziness and insubordination. After a couple of Troubleshooter groups were thus disposed of, a newly sent group founded the society themselves in order to have something to report on. By the time the game takes place, a number of other secret societies have sent spies to join the Wobblies, resulting in a group that consists entirely of spies for other groups. The in-joke for the Communist-hating computer is that this group is loosely modeled on the
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Workers_of_the_World" title="Industrial Workers of the World">
       Industrial Workers of the World
      </a>
      (IWW), the original "wobbly" trade union movement that attempted to stand up to the robber barons at the start of the 20th century.
     </p>
     <p>
      The actual societies which would be encountered in a game depends on the play style; some societies are more suited for more light-hearted games (Zap-style, or the lighter end of Classic), whereas others represent a more serious threat to Alpha Complex and are therefore more suitable for Straight or the more dark sort of Classic games.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Five editions have been published. Three of these were published by West End Games - the 1st, 2nd, and "Fifth" Editions - whereas the later two editions (
      <i>
       Paranoia XP
      </i>
      and the 25th Anniversary editions) were published by Mongoose Publishing. In addition to these five published editions, it is known that West End Games were working on a "Third Edition" - to replace the poorly received Fifth Edition - in the late 1990s, but their financial issues would prevent this edition from seeing the light of day.
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Metaplot_and_the_Second_Edition">
       Metaplot and the Second Edition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Metaplot and the Second Edition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Many of the supplements released for the Second edition fall into a story arc set up by new writers and line editors that was intended to freshen up the game and broaden roleplay possibilities. While they undoubtedly did so, giving roleplayers the opportunity to transcend time and space, play in a post-apocalyptic Computerless Alpha Complex, or play in a post-post-apocalyptic Alpha Complex in which the Computer battled for control with other factions, many fans felt these new settings ran counter to the spirit of the game. In particular, many
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2014)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      felt the removal of the Computer - and thus the power structure associated with it - and the greater freedom given to player characters effectively wrecked the very premise of the game, which required that player characters had little freedom, had to appease the demands of the Computer, and were presented with insane situations that arose from the ludicrous rules of Alpha Complex society. Second edition supplements can generally be divided into four eras:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Classic: No metaplot.
      </li>
      <li>
       Secret Society Wars: Introduced in
       <i>
        The DOA Sector Travelogue
       </i>
       , and supported by a series of
       <i>
        Secret Society Wars
       </i>
       modules. Individual missions can be run in the Classic format, but running themes and conspiracies persist from book to book.
      </li>
      <li>
       The Crash: Detailed in
       <i>
        The Crash Course Manual
       </i>
       , and supported by the
       <i>
        Vulture Warriors of Dimension X
       </i>
       series of time-travelling modules. Adventures occur in a fractured Complex in which there is no Computer, possibly as a result of the Secret Society Wars, possibly not.
      </li>
      <li>
       Reboot: Detailed in
       <i>
        The Paranoia Sourcebook
       </i>
       , and supported by a few modules and supplements. The Computer returns, but does not control all of Alpha Complex - plays as a hybrid of the other eras, with players free to choose sides.
      </li>
     </ol>

      </b>

       </a>
      </i>
      parody. As well as the lighter, sillier atmosphere, fans also disliked the lower production values of the new edition, in which most of the internal art consisted of extremely cartoonish and sketchy illustrations as opposed to the more detailed and thematically-appropriate Jim Holloway illustrations of previous editions.

      <a href="/wiki/Gen_Con" title="Gen Con">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Paranoia_XP">
       <i>
        Paranoia XP
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Paranoia XP">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="floatright">
      <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paranoia_XP.png" title="Cover of XP Edition">
       <img alt="Cover of XP Edition" data-file-height="426" data-file-width="325" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/ba/Paranoia_XP.png/150px-Paranoia_XP.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/ba/Paranoia_XP.png/225px-Paranoia_XP.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/ba/Paranoia_XP.png/300px-Paranoia_XP.png 2x" width="150"/>
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Following the bankruptcy of West End Games, the original designers of
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      banded together and purchased the rights to the game from West End in order to regain control of the line. The designers in turn granted a license to
      <a href="/wiki/Mongoose_Publishing" title="Mongoose Publishing">
       Mongoose Publishing
      </a>
      to produce a new version of the game, with the result that
      <i>
       <b>
        Paranoia XP
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9781904854265">
       ISBN 978-1-904854-26-5
      </a>
      ), written by
      <a href="/wiki/Allen_Varney" title="Allen Varney">
       Allen Varney
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Allston" title="Aaron Allston">
       Aaron Allston
      </a>
      , Paul Baldowski, Beth Fischi, Dan Curtis Johnson and
      <a href="/wiki/Greg_Costikyan" title="Greg Costikyan">
       Greg Costikyan
      </a>
      , was published in 2004. In 2005,
      <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">
       Microsoft
      </a>
      requested that the XP be removed. As such, the name was shortened to just
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      . This edition of the game has received a much warmer critical reception, as well as selling well.
     </p>
     <p>
      This edition also introduced three different styles of play, with some game mechanics differing between the various modes to support the specific tone being sought-after:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Zap
       </b>
       is anarchic slapstick with no claims to making sense and little effort at satire. Zap represents
       <i>
        Paranoia
       </i>
       as popularly understood: troubleshooters who open fire on each other with little to no provocation. It is often associated with the "Fifth Edition". The symbol of this game style is two smoking boots, much like the front cover.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Classic
       </b>
       is the atmosphere associated with the 2nd edition. While conflict inside of troubleshooter teams is common, it is less common and less frequently lethal. The symbol of this game style is a computer (representing The Computer).
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Straight
       </b>
       represents a relatively new style for
       <i>
        Paranoia
       </i>
       , although it is not entirely without precedent in the darker portions of the original 1st edition rules. Straight
       <i>
        Paranoia
       </i>
       is more serious and focuses more on dark, complex satire. In Straight
       <i>
        Paranoia
       </i>
       , players are punished for executing other characters without first filing evidence of the other character's treason; this encourages slower, more careful gameplay and discourages random firefights and horseplay. The symbol of this game style is an enormous eye, much like the eye on the front cover.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Primary designer Allen Varney, in the designer's notes, explained that his aim with the new edition was to return to the game's roots whilst updating both the game system and the satirical setting to take account of twenty years of game design progress. In both the core rulebook and the
      <i>
       Flashbacks
      </i>
      supplement - a reprint of classic adventures originally published by West End Games - Varney was highly critical of West End Games' handling of the product line in its latter days. In a posting on RPG.net he explained that the point of including the three playstyles in
      <i>
       Paranoia XP
      </i>
      was to counteract the impression that "Zap"-styled play was the default for
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      , an impression which had in part been created by the more cartoonish later supplements in the West End Games line (as well as "Fifth Edition").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Allen-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In order to distance the new edition from the less commercially and critically successful aspects of the West End Game line, and to discourage new players from wasting time and money on what he considered to be inferior products, Varney additionally used the designer's notes to declare many West End products, including the "Fifth Edition" and everything published for the 2nd Edition after
      <i>
       The People's Glorious Revolutionary Adventure
      </i>
      , to be "unproducts" - no longer part of the game's continuity, and not recommended for use with the new edition. An upshot of this is that much of the poorly received metaplot established late in the West End Games line, from the Secret Society Wars to the Reboot and beyond, was disposed of. Varney has explained that this is due mainly to his distaste for the direction the metaplot took the game line in, a distaste he asserts is shared by the game's fan community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Allen-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He has also stated that he personally has little affection for the "Zap" style,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and therefore may have given it short shrift in the main rulebook, although later supplements for
      <i>
       Paranoia XP
      </i>
      did provide more support for Zap play.
     </p>
     <p>
      Long-time
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      artist
      <a href="/wiki/Jim_Holloway_(artist)" title="Jim Holloway (artist)">
       Jim Holloway
      </a>
      , called "the master of the fun-filled illustration",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dragon132_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-dragon132-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      drew the cover art and much of the internal art for the game until 1986. His art for the series generally portray comedic scenarios that capture the essential "deathtrap" feeling of Alpha Complex.
      <i>
       Paranoia XP
      </i>
      marked his return to the line as well; he has designed every cover of the XP edition, and many books contain both his classic and new
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      art.
     </p>
     <p>
      While
      <i>
       Paranoia XP
      </i>
      kept Communists as the big bad scapegoat in spite of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
       Cold War
      </a>
      being long over, the updated edition integrates several 21st century themes into its satire. Troubleshooters carry PDCs (Personal Digital Companion) that are reminiscent of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PDA" title="PDA">
       PDAs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">
       smartphones
      </a>
      and can try to acquire gear by bidding on CBay (obvious pun on
      <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">
       eBay
      </a>
      ). New threats to Alpha Complex include
      <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">
       file sharing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">
       phishing
      </a>
      scams,
      <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">
       identity theft
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">
       WMDs
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">
       Consumerism
      </a>
      in Alpha Complex has been tooled into its economy and has taken on an element of patriotism, echoing sentiments expressed after
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9/11" title="9/11">
       9/11
      </a>
      along similar trends. A mission pack released in 2009 titled
      <i>
       War On (Insert Noun)
      </i>
      lampoons government initiatives like the
      <a href="/wiki/War_on_Drugs" title="War on Drugs">
       War on Drugs
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">
       War on Terror
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In writing the new edition, Varney, Goldberg and Costikyan reached out to and actively collaborated with
      <i>
       Paranoia'
      </i>
      s online fan community through an
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.costik.com/paranoia" rel="nofollow">
       official blog
      </a>
      and through
      <a class="external text" href="http://paranoia-live.net/" rel="nofollow">
       Paranoia-Live.net
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, Varney ran an online game, the
      <a class="external text" href="http://paranoia.allenvarney.com/" rel="nofollow">
       Toothpaste Disaster
      </a>
      , where players took the role of High Programmers documenting the titular disaster in a
      <a href="/wiki/Lexicon_(game)" title="Lexicon (game)">
       Lexicon
      </a>
      format. Many ideas established in the
      <i>
       Lexicon
      </i>
      game were written into the rulebook. Later, some of the best players and writers from the game and a few other places were formally integrated as the Traitor Recycling Studio to write official
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      material; their first credited work was the mission supplement
      <i>
       Crash Priority
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2006, Varney's fellow
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      writer, Mongoose Publishing employee Gareth Hanrahan, took over as primary writer for the
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      line. During the lifetime of the XP line Mongoose released numerous supplements and adventures for the game. Notable amongst the supplements was
      <i>
       Extreme Paranoia
      </i>
      , which provided ideas for scenarios based around characters of security clearances Orange to Violet, with premises differing greatly from the standard Red-clearance Troubleshooter concept but remaining thematically appropriate to the game's setting at atmosphere. (This included an updated reprint of the 1st Edition supplement
      <i>
       HIL Sector Blues
      </i>
      , which focused on playing Blue-clearance IntSec agents.) The idea of devising new and varied concepts to base
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      adventures and campaigns around would be revisited for the next edition of the game.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="25th_Anniversary_Editions">
       25th Anniversary Editions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 25th Anniversary Editions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In June 2009, Mongoose Publishing announced that they would be retiring the books in the XP line to clear the way for the 25th Anniversary Edition line - revealing a new edition of the rulebook as well as two new rulebooks, one casting the players as higher-clearance Internal Security investigators and one as Ultraviolet High Programmers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They stated that the
      <i>
       XP
      </i>
      material would "maintain a 90% compatibility rating with the new
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      books".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Each of the three books is an entirely self-contained and playable game:
      <i>
       Paranoia: Troubleshooters
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Paranoia: Internal Security
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       Paranoia: High Programmers
      </i>
      . The
      <i>
       Troubleshooters
      </i>
      volume presents a slimmed-down version of the
      <i>
       XP
      </i>
      rules, the most notable difference being the removal of the Service Firms and the advanced economy of the
      <i>
       XP
      </i>
      edition, with the focus firmly on the game's traditional premise of casting the player characters as Red-clearance Troubleshooters. The
      <i>
       Internal Security
      </i>
      volume casts the player characters as Blue-clearance Internal Security agents, a refinement of the premise of the 1st edition supplement
      <i>
       HIL Sector Blues
      </i>
      (reprinted in the
      <i>
       XP
      </i>
      line as part of
      <i>
       Extreme Paranoia
      </i>
      ). The third game,
      <i>
       Paranoia: High Programmers
      </i>
      , casts the player characters as the Ultraviolet-clearance elite of Alpha Complex society and focuses on the political plotting and infighting that dominates the High Programmers' lives, a premise not dissimilar to the Violet-level campaign ideas presented in
      <i>
       Extreme Paranoia
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Troubleshooters
      </i>
      volume retains the play styles of the
      <i>
       XP
      </i>
      rulebook; however, the "Classic" playstyle is assumed by default, with "Zap" and "Straight" relegated to an appendix. Allen Varney, designer of the
      <i>
       XP
      </i>
      edition, explained in a posting on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RPG.net" title="RPG.net">
       RPG.net
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that this decision came about as a result of the XP edition successfully convincing the wider gaming public that "Zap" was not the default playstyle for the game; since it was now generally accepted that
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      could have a variety of playstyles and each GM would interpret it somewhat differently, it was considered no longer necessary to emphasise the different playstyles in the main text. The
      <i>
       Internal Security
      </i>
      volume includes an appendix listing three new styles tailored for the game - "Heist", "Overkill" and "Horror".
      <i>
       High Programmers
      </i>
      does not specify playstyles.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
       Reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jackson_(US_game_designer)" title="Steve Jackson (US game designer)">
       Steve Jackson
      </a>
      described
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      as "the first sophisticated parody of the basic tropes of roleplaying. Paranoia didn't offer dungeons full of monsters with sillier names than those in
      <i>
       D&amp;D
      </i>
      . It introduced something scarier... the futuristic tunnels of Alpha Complex, in which all the monsters were human and nobody ever got out.
      <i>
       Paranoia
      </i>
      held all of roleplaying, as it was then practiced, to a dark and twisted mirror. Then it threw cream pies."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HG_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HG-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Paranoia-related_software">
       Paranoia-related software
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paranoia_(role-playing_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Paranoia-related software">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       JParanoia
      </i>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">
       freeware
      </a>
      fan-made
      <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">
       software
      </a>
      specifically created for playing Paranoia over the Internet and can be
      <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">
       downloaded
      </a>
      from the fansite
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.paranoia-live.net/" rel="nofollow">
       Paranoia Live
      </a>
      . It runs on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_Virtual_Machine" title="Java Virtual Machine">
       Java Virtual Machine
      </a>
      and consists of a client and a server with built-in features for character and gameplay management. In September 2004, both attracted some mainstream attention when the UK edition of
      <a href="/wiki/PC_Gamer" title="PC Gamer">
       PC Gamer
      </a>
      magazine ran an article about Paranoia as one of their "Extra Life" columns and showcased
      <i>
       JParanoia
      </i>
      and Paranoia Live; coincidentally the publicity came right before the site was poised to celebrate the launch of the new Paranoia edition from Mongoose.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jparanoia_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-jparanoia-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Paranoia was also made into a
      <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">
       video game
      </a>
      called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Paranoia_Complex" title="The Paranoia Complex">
        The Paranoia Complex
       </a>
      </i>
      released in 1989 by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magic_Bytes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magic Bytes (page does not exist)">
       Magic Bytes
      </a>
      . It was available for
      <a href="/wiki/Amiga" title="Amiga">
       Amiga
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Amstrad_CPC" title="Amstrad CPC">
       Amstrad CPC
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">
       Commodore 64
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/ZX_Spectrum" title="ZX Spectrum">
       ZX Spectrum
      </a>
      . It took the form of a top-down maze shooter dressed in a Paranoia plot and trappings; reviews of the game from hobby magazines of the period pegged it as mediocre to poor.
     </p>
     <p>
      Finally, a Paranoia-themed piece of "choose-your-own-adventure" hyperfiction (or
      <a href="/wiki/Gamebook" title="Gamebook">
       gamebook
      </a>
      ) was published in issue #77 of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Space_Gamer" title="The Space Gamer">
       SpaceGamer/FantasyGamer
      </a>
      magazine in the late '80s. Since then, various unauthorised automated versions of the story (a Troubleshooter's assignment to undermine the subversive activity known as Christmas) have been circulating through mainframes and PCs, with machine-independent ports to
      <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">
       C
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">
       Python
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Inform" title="Inform">
       Inform
      </a>
      as well as to
      <a href="/wiki/Adventure_Game_Toolkit" title="Adventure Game Toolkit">
       Adventure Game Toolkit
      </a>
      and for
      <a href="/wiki/Applix" title="Applix">
       Applix
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">
       CP/M
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Cybiko" title="Cybiko">
       Cybiko
      </a>
      .
     </p>
