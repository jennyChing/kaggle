          <span title="'n' in 'no'">

       </span>
      </span>
      ;
      <small>
       German:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">
        [e fn dnkn]
       </a>
      </span>
      ; born 14 April 1935) is a Swiss author of several books which make
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudoscientific" title="Pseudoscientific">
       pseudoscientific
      </a>
      claims about
      <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
       extraterrestrial
      </a>
      influences on early human culture, including the best-selling
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_the_Gods%3F" title="Chariots of the Gods?">
        Chariots of the Gods?
       </a>
      </i>
      , published in 1968. Dniken is one of the main figures responsible for popularizing the "paleo-contact" and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_astronauts" title="Ancient astronauts">
       ancient astronauts
      </a>
      hypotheses. The ideas put forth in his books are rejected by scientists and academics, who categorize his work as
      <a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">
       pseudohistory
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pseudoarchaeology" title="Pseudoarchaeology">
       pseudoarchaeology
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dniken later became a co-founder of the Archaeology, Astronautics and SETI Research Association (AAS RA). He designed Mystery Park (now known as
      <a href="/wiki/Jungfrau_Park" title="Jungfrau Park">
       Jungfrau Park
      </a>
      ), a
      <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">
       theme park
      </a>
      located in
      <a href="/wiki/Interlaken" title="Interlaken">
       Interlaken
      </a>
      , Switzerland, that opened in May 2003.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erich_von_D%C3%A4niken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dniken was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Zofingen" title="Zofingen">
       Zofingen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Aargau" title="Aargau">
       Aargau
      </a>
      . Brought up as a Catholic, he attended the Saint-Michel International Catholic School in
      <a href="/wiki/Fribourg" title="Fribourg">
       Fribourg
      </a>
      , Switzerland. During his time at the school he rejected the church's interpretations of the Bible and developed an interest in astronomy and the phenomenon of
      <a href="/wiki/Flying_saucer" title="Flying saucer">
       flying saucers
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playboy_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-playboy-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At the age of 19, Dniken was given a four-month suspended sentence for theft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playboy_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-playboy-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He left the school and became apprenticed to a Swiss hotelier for a time,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      before moving to Egypt. While in Egypt, he was involved in a jewelry deal which resulted in a nine-month conviction for fraud and embezzlement upon his return to Switzerland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playboy_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-playboy-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following his release, he became manager of the Hotel Rosenhgel in
      <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">
       Davos
      </a>
      , Switzerland, during which time he wrote
      <i>
       Chariots of the Gods?
      </i>
      , working on the manuscript late at night after the hotel's guests had retired.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revealed_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-revealed-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The draft of
      <i>
       Chariots of the Gods?
      </i>
      (German
      <i>
       Erinnerungen an die Zukunft
      </i>
      ) had been rejected by a variety of publishers.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Econ_Verlag&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Econ Verlag (page does not exist)">
       Econ Verlag
      </a>
      (now part of
      <a href="/wiki/Ullstein_Verlag" title="Ullstein Verlag">
       Ullstein Verlag
      </a>
      ) was willing to publish the book after a complete reworking by a professional author, Wilhelm Roggersdorf. (Roggersdorf was a pseudonym used by
      <a class="extiw" href="//de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utz_Utermann" title="de:Utz Utermann">
       de:Utz Utermann
      </a>
      , a former Schriftleiter of
      <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkischer_Beobachter" title="Vlkischer Beobachter">
       Vlkischer Beobachter
      </a>
      and Nazi bestseller author.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) Against all expectations, the book gained widespread interest and became a bestseller. In 1970, the Spiegel stated a hype about Dniken and called it
      <i>
       Dnikitis
      </i>
      , a pun on
      <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">
       Meningitis
      </a>
      and other infections.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_40.2F70_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Spiegel_40.2F70-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dniken gained 7 percent of the books turnover, 3 percent went to Utermann.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In December 1964, Dniken wrote
      <i>
       Hatten unsere Vorfahren Besuch aus dem Weltraum?
      </i>
      ("Did our Ancestors have a Visit from Space?") for the German-Canadian periodical
      <i>
       Der Nordwesten
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Chariots of the Gods?
      </i>
      was accepted by a publisher in early 1967, and printed in March 1968.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revealed_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-revealed-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 1968 Dniken was arrested for fraud, after falsifying hotel records and credit references in order to take out loans
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revealed_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-revealed-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for $130,000 over a period of twelve years. He used the money for foreign travel to research his book.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-playboy_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-playboy-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two years later,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revealed_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-revealed-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dniken was convicted for "repeated and sustained" embezzlement, fraud and forgery, with the court ruling that the writer had been living a "
      <a href="/wiki/Playboy_(lifestyle)" title="Playboy (lifestyle)">
       playboy
      </a>
      " lifestyle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1_12-0">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Claims_of_alien_influence_on_Earth">
       Claims of alien influence on Earth
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erich_von_D%C3%A4niken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Claims of alien influence on Earth">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The general claim of Dniken over several published books, starting with
      <i>
       Chariots of the Gods?
      </i>
      in 1968, is that extraterrestrials or "ancient astronauts" visited Earth and influenced early human culture. Dniken writes about his belief that structures such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids" title="Egyptian pyramids">
       Egyptian pyramids
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">
       Stonehenge
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Moai" title="Moai">
       Moai
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">
       Easter Island
      </a>
      and artifacts from that period represent higher technological knowledge than is presumed to have existed at the times they were manufactured. He also describes ancient artwork throughout the world as containing depictions of astronauts, air and space vehicles, extraterrestrials, and complex technology. Dniken explains the origins of religions as reactions to contact with an alien race, and offers interpretations of sections of the Old Testament of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
       Criticism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erich_von_D%C3%A4niken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1966, when Dniken was writing his first book, scientists
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">
       Carl Sagan
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iosif_Samuilovich_Shklovskii" title="Iosif Samuilovich Shklovskii">
       I.S. Shklovskii
      </a>
      wrote about the possibility of paleocontact and extraterrestrial visitation claims in one chapter of their book
      <i>
       Intelligent Life in the Universe
      </i>
      , leading author Ronald Story to speculate in his book
      <i>
       The Space-gods Revealed
      </i>
      that this may have been the genesis of Dniken's ideas.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Many ideas from this book appeared in different form in Dniken's books.
     </p>
     <p>
      Prior to Dniken's work, other authors had presented ideas of extraterrestrial contacts. He has failed to credit these authors properly or at all, even when making the same claims using similar or identical evidence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first edition of Dniken's
      <i>
       Erinnerungen an die Zukunft
      </i>
      failed to cite
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Charroux" title="Robert Charroux">
       Robert Charroux
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       One Hundred Thousand Years of Man's Unknown History
      </i>
      despite making very similar claims, and publisher Econ-Verlag were forced to add Charroux in the bibliography in later editions, to avoid a possible lawsuit for plagiarism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Piri_Reis" title="Piri Reis">

      The
      <i>

        Nova
       </a>
      </i>
      documentary
      <i>
       The Case of the Ancient Astronauts
      </i>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyramid_of_Cheops" title="Pyramid of Cheops">
       Pyramid of Cheops
      </a>
      were wrong in all accounts. The technique of construction is well understood, scholars know perfectly what tools were used, the marks of those tools in the quarries are still visible, and there are many tools preserved in museums. Dniken claims that it would have taken them too long to cut all the blocks necessary and drag them to the construction site in time to build the Great Pyramid in only 20 years, but Nova shows how easy and fast it is to cut a block of stone, and shows the rollers used in transportation. He also claims that Egyptians suddenly started making pyramids out of nowhere, but there are several pyramids that show the progress made by Egyptian architects while they were perfecting the technique from simple
      <a href="/wiki/Mastaba" title="Mastaba">
       mastabas
      </a>
      to later pyramids. Dniken claims that the height of the pyramid multiplied by one million was the distance to the Sun, but the number falls too short. If it were true, it would make the pyramid 93 miles high... He also claims that Egyptians could not align the edges so perfectly to true North without advanced technology that only aliens could give them, but Egyptians knew of very simple methods to find North via star observation, and it is trivial to make straight edges.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dniken claimed that the
      <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_the_Inscriptions#Sarcophagus" title="Temple of the Inscriptions">
       Sarcophagus of Palenque
      </a>
      depicted a spaceman sitting on a rocket-powered spaceship, wearing a spacesuit. However, archaeologists see nothing special about the figure, a dead Mayan monarch wearing traditional Mayan hairdo and jewellery, surrounded by Mayan symbols that can be observed in other Mayan drawings. The right hand is not handling any rocket controls, but simply making a traditional Mayan gesture, that other figures in the sides of the lid also make, and is not holding anything. The rocket shape is actually two serpents joining their heads at the bottom, with the rocket "flames" being the beards of the serpents. The rocket motor under the figure is the face of a monster, symbol of the underworld.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Dniken put forward photographs of ancient stones in Peru, with carvings of men using telescopes, detailed world maps, and advanced medical operations, all beyond the knowledge of ancient Peruvians. But the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">
       PBS
      </a>
      television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">
        Nova
       </a>
      </i>
      determined that the stones were modern, and located the potter who made them. This potter makes stones daily and sells them to tourists. Dniken had visited the potter and examined the stones himself, but he didn't mention this in his book. He says that he didn't believe the potter when he said that he had made the stones. Dniken says that he asked Doctor Cabrera, a local surgeon who owns the museum, and Cabrera had told him that the potter's claims were a lie and that the stones were ancient. But the potter had proof that Cabrera had thanked him for providing the stones for the museum. Dniken claimed that the stones at the museum were very different from those made by the potter, but the Nova reporters oversaw the manufacturing of one stone and confirmed that it was very similar to those in the museum.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

      <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">
