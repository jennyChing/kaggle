     <div class="articleBody" itemprop="articleBody">
      <p>
       We learnt last week that Julia Gillard has for years dyed her hair red in order to be "more noticeable". It would be a rational tactic, since red hair is one of the most recessive, hence rarest, of physical attributes. Throughout history and still today, red hair is also one of the most divisive and controversial bits of physiognomy. But it may become more acceptable as scientists close in on the solution to a genetic puzzle redefining what it means to be human.
      </p>
      <p>
       Of the many unsolved questions of prehistory, one of the most intriguing is why homo sapiens prevailed over the 18 or so other upright apes who cohabited the Earth from 300,000 to 12,000 BC. While it has been politic to disparage Neanderthals as a byword for primitive, these bipedal silver medallists were for a long time more numerous, were always stronger and had a bigger brain than homo sapiens. And it is now emerging that they left more to us than fossils.
      </p>
      <p>
       The early orthodoxy had it that we "tool makers" simply wiped out Neanderthals with a combination of spear and fire-making, domestication of dogs and a more sophisticated sexual contract.
      </p>
      <p>
       But the emerging school argues that human and Neanderthal ancestors interbred, producing a transfer of genes in a species blend, rather than clean-cut extinction.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       By 2003, the Human Genome Project had largely mapped the role of each of the 20,000 to 25,000-odd genes in our double helix.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2008, researchers at the Max Planck Institute of Evolutionary Anthropology extracted and identified 60 per cent of the Neanderthal genome from bone fragments preserved in the Croatian Vindija Cave.
      </p>
      <p>
       The researchers took a big step towards proving "species merger" by demonstrating that 1 per cent to 4 per cent of (non-African) human DNA comes from Neanderthals. Interestingly, there appears to be no traffic in the other direction.
      </p>
      <p>
       Current redheaded glamours include Lindsay Lohan, Nicole Kidman and Isla Fisher. Among the blokes, Robert Redford was for a decade Hollywood's hottest lead. After his brother's wedding last month, the world's most eligible bachelor is a ''ranga''. It appears the singular allure of the redhead to some humans may have ancient roots.
      </p>
      <p>
       A tantalising question is which human traits do we owe to the legacy of that primaeval co-mingling? Neanderthals were concentrated in northern Europe and appear to have been largely, or exclusively, redheads. Two copies of the recessive gene on human chromosome 16 yields a classic helpful mutation. A defective protein (MC1R) on the plasma membrane allows a pale-skinned redhead to process more vitamin D with less sunlight - giving an evolutionary edge in cold climes to sun-starved Vikings, Celts and their northern European cousins. It also delivers higher pain thresholds.
      </p>
      <p>
       The highest concentration of the gene combo is found in Scotland where fully 40 per cent of the population are carriers (of the Irish, 10 per cent). Is it drawing too long a bow to wonder if that explains why the Scots are massively over-represented in the annals of human achievement and innovation (see Arthur Herman's
       <em>
        How the Scots Invented the Modern World . . . and Everything in It
       </em>
       ")?
      </p>
      <p>
       The common association between redheads and hotheads may hark back to Scotland, too. Sir Walter Scott remarked "I am a Scotsman . . . therefore I had to fight my way into the world".
      </p>
      <p>
       The redhead persecution/achievement complex also features in the Jewish story. The Ashkenazi Jews are blessed with a titian abundance, echoing some of their great forebears - Esau was "red all over" and King David was "handsome and ruddy".
      </p>
      <p>
       Playground teasing of redheads continues, as if children were sensing the Neanderthal link. A recent
       <em>
        South Park
       </em>
       episode asked whether gingers have souls. The YouTube response - Gingers do have souls - has garnered 18 million hits.
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        The Courier-Mail
       </em>
       and
       <em>
        National Geographic
       </em>
       both fell for a hoax that the key gene was not only rare but headed for oblivion. Turns out the source was unnamed "geneticists" at a foundation funded by the hair dye maker Procter &amp; Gamble. They need not have worried - the queue of faux reds is long and illustrious: Lucille Ball, Sophia Loren, Julia Roberts and Cynthia Nixon are all in the Australian Prime Minister's camp.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the product may lie, the genes never do. Their power to lock in identity and destroy conspiracy theories was shown last week with the demise of Osama bin Laden. The US Navy SEAL who fired the fatal shot also extracted a "DNA sample" which was compared to another from bin Laden's sister, who died of brain cancer in Boston last year. The match allowed President Barack Obama to announce, with 99.9 per cent certainty, "justice has been done".
      </p>
      <p>
       We should not fear our own story - which is what our genes are. Knowledge is the antidote to fear. DNA matching has seen the release of 17 innocent men on death row in the US. Most couples will soon have access to a useful genetic prenuptial compatibility test. Future generations will never be uncertain about something as basic as paternity or ancestry.
      </p>
      <p>
       Let's begin by recognising that most of us are part-Neanderthal. That 1 per cent to 4 per cent gift may help to explain the stellar northern European achievement of which modern Australia is one befreckled example.
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Ross Cameron was the federal Liberal MP for Parramatta from 1996 to 2004.
       </strong>
      </p>
