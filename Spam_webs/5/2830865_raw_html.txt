    <h1>
     Extracting colours with alpha
    </h1>
    <h2>
     Sampling a colour from an image is easy  open it in Photoshop, choose the Eyedropper tool, and click the target pixel. Sampling a non-opaque colour thats been flattened onto a background is harder, but not impossible.
    </h2>
    <p>
     Before we begin, lets discuss tools. Many designers will be familiar with the Eyedropper tool in Photoshop, but may not be familiar with the Color Sampler tool. In this scenario, the Color Sampler tool is far more valuable  it can sample four points and once, and the location of the points are locked, so its possible to tweak values and observe the results at the same time.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/color-sampler.png"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     Having four samplers means you can watch the original and the new version together as they converge. To place a sampler, select the Color Sampler tool and click on the canvas. Turning caps lock on (or changing your settings) will give you a more precise cursor while doing so.
    </p>
    <p>
     Lets work out the colour and alpha of the highlight used in OS Xs menubar icons. It looks like pure white, so well start with that and lower the opacity to see if we can get a match.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/osx-menubar-icon.gif"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     The #2 and #4 values in the info panel change as the opacity is altered. White at 35% opacity matches in two places in the Notification Center icon, so that looks like a good fit.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/map.png"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     Now for something a little harder. Assume were trying to extract the red colour and opacity from the image above, and we only have a flattened file to work with.
    </p>
    <p>
     Theres quite a few places on the map where the background looks identical under and beside the overlay. Placing some colour samplers and drawing a small shape layer gets us started.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/map-zoomed.png"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     When tweaking the colour, the fastest way to zero in is one channel at a time.
    </p>
    <p>
     Try to guess the alpha  my attempt here is 70%. Now open up the shapes colour, altering red only for now (or a prominent channel, if not much red is used). An easy way to alter just the red while looking at the info panel is to click inside the text field, then use the up and down arrow keys (or shift-up/down for steps of 10). If youre able to get the red to match, try the same technique for green and blue. If you cant find a match, adjust the alpha and try again.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/map-guessalpha.png"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     Eventually everything will line up. This one turned out to be 80% opacity. Remember that a designer or a developer probably chose the opacity, so theres a very good chance itll land on a nice, even number.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/map-perfect.png"/>
    </p>
    <h3>
     Gradients, shadows and glows
    </h3>
    <p>
     The same technique works well for linear gradients  just place samplers at the start and end of the original and your copy, and adjust each colour to match.
    </p>
    <p>
     Recreating shadows and glows is harder, but possible. Start with a couple of points and match as per the instructions above. The image below shows some initial work recreating the OS X in-focus window shadow, using a drop shadow and stroke.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="" src="/images/articles/extractingcolours/osx-window-shadow.png"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     Good artists use the Eyedropper tool. Great artists use the Color Sampler tool.
    </p>
