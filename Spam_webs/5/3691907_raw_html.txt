     <p>
      <b>
       Francis Julius Bellamy
      </b>
      (May 18, 1855  August 28, 1931) was a
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">
       Christian socialist
      </a>
      minister and author,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mason_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mason-1">

       <a href="#cite_note-OldTimeIsland-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      best known for writing the American
      <a href="/wiki/Pledge_of_Allegiance" title="Pledge of Allegiance">
       Pledge of Allegiance
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Bellamy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Francis Julius Bellamy was born in Mount Morris, NY. His family was deeply involved in the Baptist church and they moved to Rome, NY when Bellamy was only 5. Here, Bellamy became an active member of the First Baptist Church; which his father was minister of until his death in 1864. He attended college at the University of Rochester, in Rochester, NY and studied theology and was part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Delta_Phi" title="Alpha Delta Phi">
       Alpha Delta Phi
      </a>
      Fraternity.
     </p>
     <p>
      As a young man, he became a minister and, influenced by the vestiges of the Second Great Awakening, began to travel to promote his faith and help his community. Bellamy's travels brought him to Massachusetts. It was there that he penned the "Pledge of Allegiance" for a campaign by the "Youth's Companion;" a patriotic circular of the day.
     </p>
     <p>
      Bellamy married Harriet Benton in Newark, NY in 1881. They had two sons: John, who lived in California, and David, who lived in Rochester, New York. His first wife died in 1918, and he later married Marie Morin (1920).
     </p>
     <p>
      Bellamy ran for Governor of New York, but lost. His daughter-in-law Rachael (David's wife) lived in Rochester until Feb/Mar of 1989 when she died at the age of 93. No information is readily available on son John or his family in California or of any other relatives. David and Rachael had two children, David Jr. and Peter.
     </p>
     <p>
      Francis Bellamy spent most of the last years of his life living and working in Tampa, FL. He died there on August 28, 1931 at the age of 76. His cremated remains were brought back to New York where they were buried in a family plot in a cemetery in Rome.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Pledge">
       The Pledge
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Bellamy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Pledge">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Pledge_of_Allegiance" title="Pledge of Allegiance">
       Pledge of Allegiance
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1891, Daniel Sharp Ford, the owner of the
      <i>
       Youth's Companion
      </i>
      , hired Bellamy to work with Ford's nephew James B. Upham in the magazine's premium department. In 1888, the
      <i>
       Youth's Companion
      </i>
      had begun a campaign to sell
      <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the United States">
       American flags
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" title="Public school (government funded)">
       public schools
      </a>
      as a premium to solicit subscriptions. For Upham and Bellamy, the flag promotion was more than merely a business move; under their influence, the
      <i>
       Youth's Companion
      </i>
      became a fervent supporter of the schoolhouse flag movement, which aimed to place a flag above every school in the nation. Four years later, by 1892, the magazine had sold American flags to approximately 26,000 schools. By this time the market was slowing for flags, but was not yet saturated.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1892, Upham had the idea of using the 400th anniversary of
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
       Christopher Columbus
      </a>
      reaching the
      <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
       Americas
      </a>
      to further bolster the schoolhouse flag movement. The magazine called for a national Columbian Public School Celebration to coincide with the
      <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">
       World's Columbian Exposition
      </a>
      . A flag salute was to be part of the official program for the
      <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">
       Columbus Day
      </a>
      celebration to be held in schools all over America.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pledge was published in the September 8, 1892, issue of the magazine, and immediately put to use in the campaign. Bellamy went to speak to a national meeting of
      <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_(education)" title="Superintendent (education)">
       school superintendents
      </a>
      to promote the celebration; the convention liked the idea and selected a committee of leading educators to implement the program, including the immediate past president of the National Education Association. Bellamy was selected as the chair. Having received the official blessing of educators, Bellamy's committee now had the task of spreading the word across the nation and of designing an official program for schools to follow on the day of national celebration. He structured the program around a flag-raising ceremony and his pledge.
     </p>
     <p>
      His original Pledge read as follows:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       "
       <i>
        I pledge allegiance to my Flag and to* the Republic for which it stands, one nation, indivisible, with liberty and justice for all
       </i>
       "
       <dl>
        <dd>
         (* 'to' added in October 1892).
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The recital was accompanied with a salute to the flag known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Bellamy_salute" title="Bellamy salute">
       Bellamy salute
      </a>
      , described in detail by Bellamy. During
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , the salute was replaced with a hand-over-heart gesture because the original form involved stretching the arm out towards the flag in a manner that resembled the later
      <a href="/wiki/Nazi_salute" title="Nazi salute">
       Nazi salute
      </a>
      . (
      <i>
       For a history of the pledge, see
       <a href="/wiki/Pledge_of_Allegiance" title="Pledge of Allegiance">
        Pledge of Allegiance
       </a>
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1954, in response to the perceived threat of secular Communism, President Eisenhower encouraged Congress to add the words "under God," creating the 31-word pledge that is recited today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Bellamy commented on his thoughts as he created the pledge, and his reasons for choosing the careful wording:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       "
       <i>
        It began as an intensive communing with salient points of our national history, from the
        <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">
         Declaration of Independence
        </a>
        onwards; with the makings of the Constitution... with the meaning of the
        <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
         Civil War
        </a>
        ; with the aspiration of the people...
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       "
       <i>
        The true reason for allegiance to the Flag is the '
        <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">
         republic
        </a>
        for which it stands'. ...And what does that last thing, the Republic mean? It is the concise political word for the Nation  the One Nation which the Civil War was fought to prove. To make that One Nation idea clear, we must specify that it is indivisible, as
        <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Webster" title="Daniel Webster">
         Webster
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">
         Lincoln
        </a>
        used to repeat in their great speeches. And its future?
       </i>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       "
       <i>
        Just here arose the temptation of the historic slogan of the
        <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
         French Revolution
        </a>
        which meant so much to
        <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">
         Jefferson
        </a>
        and his friends, '
        <a href="/wiki/Libert%C3%A9,_%C3%A9galit%C3%A9,_fraternit%C3%A9" title="Libert, galit, fraternit">
         Liberty, equality, fraternity
        </a>
        '. No, that would be too fanciful, too many thousands of years off in realization. But we as a nation do stand square on the doctrine of liberty and justice for all..."
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Bellamy "viewed his Pledge as an '
      <a href="/wiki/Inoculation" title="Inoculation">
       inoculation
      </a>
      ' that would protect
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant">
       immigrants
      </a>
      and native-born but insufficiently
      <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">
       patriotic
      </a>
      Americans from the '
      <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">
       virus
      </a>
      ' of radicalism and subversion."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reason_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reason-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Bellamy was a
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">
       Christian socialist
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mason_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mason-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who "championed 'the rights of working people and the equal distribution of economic resources, which he believed was inherent in the teachings of
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      .'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reason_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reason-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1891, Bellamy was forced from his Boston pulpit for his socialist sermons, and eventually stopped attending church altogether after moving to Florida, reportedly because of the racism he witnessed there.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In "Cultural Movements and Collective Memory: Christopher Columbus and the Rewriting of the National Origin Myth," Timothy Kubal explains:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Francis Bellamy was a leader in three related movement groups  the public education movement, which sought to celebrate and expand public schools, the nationalist movement, which sought to nationalize public services and protect them from privatization, and the Christian socialist movement, which sought to promote an economy based on justice and equality. Bellamy had preexisting relationships with grassroots groups representing each movement and he united his diverse network for collective memory activism in 1892.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Francis Bellamy fused his participation in the Christian socialist movement and the nationalist movement. Francis began his career as a preacher, but after several years of service, he was ousted by a congregation that disliked his tendency to describe Jesus as a socialist. Bellamy was no simple preacher-turned-journalist, but the cousin of the most famous nationalist icon of the era.
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Bellamy" title="Edward Bellamy">
        Edward Bellamy
       </a>
       was the symbolic leader of the nationalist movement, which sought to nationalize public services and the economy to meet the needs of the masses rather than the few. Francis's cousin book  a utopian tale of an advanced socialist society  sparked more than one hundred grassroots nationalist organizations around the country called Bellamy Clubs. Edward protested against the name for his local group, and the others agreed to change the name to the Nationalist Club.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Francis Bellamy also drew from networks established by the Christian socialist movement. Early in the nineteenth century, the French philosopher Henri de Sain-Simon expounded a "new Christianity" that sought to use scientific principles to help solve problems with the poor. Religion, science, and community were to be used to end the exploitation of the masses. By the end of the century, Bellamy and many of the "new St. Simonians," as they sometimes called themselves, saw nationalism (de-privatization) and public education as the policy solutions to meet the goals established by the Christian socialists, thus leading to a natural connection among the nationalist and Christian Socialist movements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The Society of Christian Socialists, a grassroots organization, was founded in Boston 1889. Francis Bellamy served as founding vice president and wrote several articles for the Society of Christian Socialists' newspaper, the Dawn. The newspaper was run by his cousin Edward and Frances Willard, president of the Women's Christian Temperance Union. In one article, Francis Bellamy wrote that Christian socialists had the obligation to live out the golden rule, to act toward contemporary society as did Jesus. He quoted Bible passages that revered Moses and Jesus as denouncing the evils of greed and lust for money (ibid). Francis Bellamy (hereafter Bellamy) was also chairman of the Boston chapter of the Society of Christian Socialists' education committee.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       To raise the stature of the organizations, bring in funds, and educate the public about Christian Socialism, Bellamy offered public education classes; for a fee participants joined courses with topics such as "looking Backward," Jesus the socialist," "What is Christian Socialism?" "The Subject of Labor in Light of the Bible," and "Socialism versus anarchy." This last lecture became particularly popular and Bellamy in 1891 was asked to turn it into a written piece for the Nationalist Club's newspaper, the Arena. Bellamy's essay distinguished anarchy from nationalism and socialism, and it declared the need for a strong government to protect the weak masses from the powerful corporations. He argued that the only socialist economy could produce work environments where both the worker and the owner could practice the golden rule (ibid). Through these experiences, Bellamy gained increasing experience with the media and with public relations. He used these tools to coordinate a massive Columbus Day campaign.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      On
      <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">
       immigration
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">
       universal suffrage
      </a>
      , Bellamy wrote: "[a]
      <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
       democracy
      </a>
      like ours cannot afford to throw itself open to the world where every man is a lawmaker, every dull-witted or fanatical
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant">
       immigrant
      </a>
      admitted to our
      <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">
       citizenship
      </a>
      is a bane to the commonwealth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reason_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Reason-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      And further: "Where all classes of society merge insensibly into one another every alien immigrant of inferior race may bring corruption to the stock. There are races more or less akin to our own whom we may admit freely and get nothing but advantage by the infusion of their wholesome blood. But there are other races, which we cannot assimilate without lowering our racial standard, which we should be as sacred to us as the sanctity of our homes."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trofrelig_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-trofrelig-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
