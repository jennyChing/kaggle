          </span>
         </h1>
         <div class="submitted">

         <h2 class="strapline">
          A cult hero and an inspiration, it's time to drool over the sketchbook of Ray Harryhausen
         </h2>
         <div class="copy">
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/UNI55_sbook_rh_hs.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
           <figcaption>
            The stop motion animating legend with Medusa
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p>
           If you wanted to bring a skeleton army to life or have a Greek colossus stomp your heroes into mush in the days before digital, you called the one man who could make it happen:
           <a href="http://www.rayharryhausen.com/" target="_blank">
            Ray Harryhausen
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           As one of the most highly acclaimed stop-motion animators of the pre-digital era, Ray worked on some all-time classic movies, including War of the Worlds, Sinbad and the Eye of the Tiger, and the epic Clash of the Titans.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.creativebloq.com/freelance-handbook" target="_blank">
            Win clients &amp; work smarter with our FREE ebook: get it now!
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           The creatures Ray animated would always steal the scenes from the human actors  he managed to make them gruesome but also emotive. To achieve this Ray would immerse himself in the movie project, creating rough sketches before drawing a scene and then preparing the sequence's storyboards.
          </p>
          <p>
           Only then would he construct the models and begin the task of animating the titanic battle that would ensue. Working in clay to create his models, Ray would then painstakingly design the creature's moveable skeleton before covering the armature in latex and the days of animation could begin.
          </p>
          <div class="future-inline-cta">
          </div>
          <p>
           Inspired by traditional painters John Martin, Gustave Dor, Charles Knight, and Joseph Michael Gandy, whose painting Jupiter Pluvius depicted an enormous statue of Jupiter, that statue would form the inspiration for Ray's version of Olympus as seen in Clash of the Titans.
          </p>
          <h3 class="crosshead">
           Medusa
          </h3>
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/UNI55_sbook_medusa_02.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
          </figure>
          <p>
           Ray has been an inspiration for an army of filmmakers from George Lucas to Peter Jackson, who all credit him as an influence.
          </p>
          <p>
           Two charcoal and pencil sketches for the Medusa sequence in Clash of the Titans, these were created in 1977 and were used as a presentation piece to sell the film to MGM.
          </p>
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/UNI55_sbook_medusa_01.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
          </figure>
          <h3 class="crosshead">
           The Kraken
          </h3>
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/UNI55_sbook_kraken.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
          </figure>
          <p>
           This 1978 sketch for Clash of the Titans was created as a comparison piece to demonstrate the sheer size of the Kraken.
          </p>
          <h3 class="crosshead">
           Tiger, tiger
          </h3>
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/tiger.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
          </figure>
          <p>
           The end sequence of Sinbad and the Eye of the Tiger, sketched in 1974. Here a frozen sabretooth tiger is defrosted to kill Sinbad.
          </p>
          <h3 class="crosshead">
           The cut
          </h3>
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/the cut.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
          </figure>
          <p>
           This sketch shows the sabre-tooth tiger battling Sinbad's companion, a troglodyte man. The original 'Trog' was a Neanderthal man, but was removed from the final cut, as were a valley of vipers, a prehistoric rhino and a giant worm.
          </p>
          <h3 class="crosshead">
           Skeleton hordes
          </h3>
          <figure class="captioned-image" style="width:535px">
           <img alt="Ray Harryhausen's sketchbook unearthed" src="http://media.creativebloq.futurecdn.net/sites/creativebloq.com/files/images/2015/03/13/UNI55_sbook_skeletons.jpg" style="width:535px"/>
          </figure>
          <p>
           Skeleton's feature heavily in Ray's work. These scenes are from The 7th Voyage of Sinbad and were sketched in 1962 to illustrate the film's finale.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Next page:
           </strong>
           <strong>
            more of Ray's cult classic sketches
           </strong>
           ...
          </p>
