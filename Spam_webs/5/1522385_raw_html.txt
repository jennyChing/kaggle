          <h1 class="post-title">
           <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/2015/01/15/after-65-surgeries-boy-that-swallowed-a-button-battery-is-doing-well/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to After 65 Surgeries, Boy that Swallowed a Button Battery is Doing Well">
            After 65 Surgeries, Boy that Swallowed a Button Battery is Doing Well
           </a>
           <div class="comments">
            <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/2015/01/15/after-65-surgeries-boy-that-swallowed-a-button-battery-is-doing-well/#comments">
             0
            </a>
           </div>
          </h1>
          <small>
           by
           <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/author/cathy/" rel="author" title="Posts by Kate">
            Kate
           </a>
           <a href="" plugin="Google Plus Authorship" rel="author" title="Google Plus Profile for +">
            +
           </a>
           in
           <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/category/child-safety/" rel="category tag">
            Child Safety
           </a>
           <br/>
          </small>
          <br style="clear:both;"/>
          <hr/>
          <div id="getsocialmain">
           <p>
            Button batteries can be found in everything from childrens toys to remote controls to greeting cards. Potentially lethal, they have put many children in danger. Emmett Rauch was one of those children.
           </p>
           <p>
            Having been born a preemie, Emmett had already shown his fighting spirit. Then, at just over a year old, he swallowed a button battery. The result would be a fight for survival that would rival the one hed fought at birth. In fact,
            <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/2011/11/14/toddler-is-recovering-in-hospital-after-swallowing-a-button-battery/">
             when we shared his story in 2011, he was two and still fighting for his life.
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/2011/11/14/toddler-is-recovering-in-hospital-after-swallowing-a-button-battery/emmett-rauch-2/" rel="attachment wp-att-52110">
             <img alt="Emmett Rauch " class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-52110" height="270" src="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/11/Emmett-Rauch-2-500x270.jpg" width="500"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            Emmett had just had his first birthday. It was a Saturday, and we noticed he had a fever and was coughing, but there had been no choking episode. The doctor said it was just a cold and had to run its course. But he was lethargic and crying every time he tried to eat, Emmetts mother, Karla Rauch, told TODAY. On the following Tuesday, when Emmett coughed and blood came up, we called the pediatrician. I was freaking out. She said it sounded like croup and sent us home. But as I was walking to the car, the pediatrician came out and said, I have this feelingsend him to the ER.
           </p>
           <p>
            It was in the emergency room that a radiologist found the button battery lodged in Emmetts esophagus. He was then rushed by ambulance to Phoenix Childrens Hospital where doctors conducted a three-hour surgery to remove the battery from his throat.
           </p>
           <p>
            The surgeon said it looked like a firecracker had gone off in Emmetts esophagus, Karla said. It was lodged a centimeter above the aorta and they couldnt tell if hed survive. At that moment, we fell apart. How did we not know? And where did he get the battery?
           </p>
           <p>
            That same night, Emmetts heart stopped. Doctors were able to revive him, but he still had a long and difficult fight ahead of him. He went through 13 major surgeries that year, including a surgery in which half of his stomach was used to recreate his esophagus. After that surgery failed, doctors stapled the bottom half of his esophagus to the outside of his neck for three months, and then they put in a feeding tube so that he could receive food and water.
           </p>
           <p>
            Emmett lived in the ICU for eight months in 2011, and there were times when we thought he would pass away, Karla said. It was very humbling to watch him, because he has this fighting spirit. He stole the hearts of all the nurses and doctors with his beautiful smile.
           </p>
           <p>
            Then, in 2012, doctors at Cincinnati Childrens Hospital Medical Center performed a very rare surgerythey removed his entire esophagus and replaced it with a part of his colon. Emmett also had two inches of his rib cage removed and then used to help open up his paralyzed vocal chords.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its one of the hardest things Ive ever been through, Karla said. Ever day, even when he was in a medical coma, we would talk positively to him: Emmett, you can do this.
           </p>
           <p>
            Now, at five-years-old, Emmett has endured 65 surgeries, and there are some life-long complications. His stomach is tiny, so he has a feeding tube in his small intestine to ensure he receives enough nutrients, and his vocal chords will never be the same. But he is alive, and for that, his parents are eternally grateful.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/?attachment_id=96264" rel="attachment wp-att-96264">
             <img alt="Emmett Rauch  6 years old" class="aligncenter wp-image-96264" height="646" src="http://www.growingyourbaby.com/wp-content/uploads/Emmett-Rauch-6-years-old.jpg" width="484"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            Emmett is doing well. He loves wrestling with his older brother and hes playing with his friends on the trampoline and the swing set. He had his first soccer practice, which hes been waiting to do, Karla said, adding that she looks at life differently now.
           </p>
           <p>
            We didnt know how long Emmett would be with us, she said. Every kiss, every hug, every I love you is so important. We realize what is important in life . . . you treasure moments.
           </p>
           <p>
            But the family is doing more than just treasuring their moments; due to the lack of information that was available when Emmett started his struggle, they started up a non-profit organization to raise awareness and hopefully help prevent other children from having to endure the same struggle as their son.
           </p>
           <p>
            We went online to find support and there was very little, only a paragraph by the National Poison Control Center, Karla said. [Then], while we were in the hospital in Phoenix, a Hispanic boy was flown in from Yuma, and he had the exact same thing, but there was a language barrier involving his family and the staff. The social worker asked if I would be comfortable talking to his mother. I broke down in tears because, I realized, this had become our mission. So we started an awareness campaign coordinating with Safe Kids and National Poison Control. That campaign has spread awareness about button batteries in more than just the statesin fact, parents as far away as South Africa and India have written letters to the family, sharing their experience with tiny and dangerous batteries.
           </p>
           <p>
            Every story is the same: Parents dont see their kids choking, and it gets misdiagnosed. We didnt know, and our pediatrician never said anything, Karla said. I am heartbroken that Emmett has had to suffer, and I would have taken his place in a heartbeat. But I am grateful that there is some sort of silver lining that is helping to save other children from suffering and possibly losing their lives.
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             Tips for Button Battery Safety from Safe Kids and National Poison Control:
            </em>
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <em>
              Know where battery buttons can be found: car key fobs, mini remotes that control MP3 speakers, calculators, bathroom scales, reading lights, flameless candles, talking books, singing greeting cards, watches, thermometers, hearing aids, flashing jewelry, ornaments, games, and toys.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Keep all loose batteries out of reach and sight of children. Store them like medication, in a locked cabinet, if at all possible.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Try to purchase products with battery compartments that require a tool to open.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              If a tool is not required, ensure battery compartments to toys and other household products are secure.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Secure the back of remote controls, toys, household products, and other devices that can be opened with strong tape.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              If battery compartments to devices are loose or broken, keep the device out of reach of children.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Do not allow children to play with batteries.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Do not charge or change batteries in front of children.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              A button battery can begin to burn within two hours of swallowing. If you suspect your child has swallowed one,, contact poison control and seek medical care immediately.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              If you suspect a button battery has been swallowed, do not allow a child to eat or drink anything until an X-ray can be performed.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Do not induce vomiting if you suspect a battery button has been swallowed.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              Tell doctors and nurses that you suspect a button battery was swallowed so that they can quickly and effectively check.
             </em>
            </li>
            <li>
             <em>
              If possible, provide the identification number found on the batterys package.
             </em>
            </li>
           </ul>
