     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <b>
        saeta
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <small>
       Spanish pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">
        [saeta]
       </a>
      </span>
      ) is a revered form of Spanish religious song, whose form and style has evolved over many centuries. Saetas evoke strong emotion and are sung most often during public processions.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">
       Performance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saeta_(flamenco)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Performance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Saeta_by_Julio_Romero_de_Torres_(part).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="377" data-file-width="354" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/La_Saeta_by_Julio_Romero_de_Torres_%28part%29.jpg/300px-La_Saeta_by_Julio_Romero_de_Torres_%28part%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/La_Saeta_by_Julio_Romero_de_Torres_%28part%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/La_Saeta_by_Julio_Romero_de_Torres_%28part%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Saeta_by_Julio_Romero_de_Torres_(part).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         The saeta
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/Julio_Romero_de_Torres" title="Julio Romero de Torres">
         Julio Romero de Torres
        </a>
        (1918).
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The saeta is a song of Catholic Spain dating back many centuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       saeta antigua
      </i>
      [old saeta] probably arose from the recitation of
      <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">
       psalms
      </a>
      under the influence of
      <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_music" title="Liturgical music">
       liturgical music
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "Saetas vary greatly in form and style, ranging from simple syllabic melodies to highly ornamented ones."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willi_Apel_1944_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Willi_Apel_1944-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the older tradition, solemn drums and horns might accompany the singer, or the saetero sang alone. Since the nineteenth century, however, the more favored saetas have incorporated distinct elements associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">
       Flamenco
      </a>
      music, particularly the
      <a href="/wiki/Siguiriyas" title="Siguiriyas">
       siguiriyas
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The saeta is best known for its mournful power during
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_in_Spain" title="Holy Week in Spain">
       Holy Week in Spain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The song is performed during the
      <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">
       processions
      </a>
      by religious
      <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">
       confraternities
      </a>
      that move slowly through the streets of cities and towns in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalucia" title="Andalucia">
       southern Spain
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caballero-127_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Caballero-127-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Possessing a plaintive emotional intensity, and dramatic charge, the saeta is sung by the
      <i>
       saetero
      </i>
      , often from a balcony, and may be addressed to the statue of
      <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">
       Jesus
      </a>
      below, in his agony on the
      <a href="/wiki/Via_Dolorosa" title="Via Dolorosa">
       Via Dolorosa
      </a>
      , or to that of his suffering mother
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">
       Mary
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These and other crafted statues are mounted on platforms and carried along the streets on the shoulders of several
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penitent" title="Penitent">
       penitents
      </a>
      who pass among the assembled public.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The immediate emotional response to the saeta, often of intense sorrow, may be the reason for its name, as the Spanish word
      <i>
       saeta
      </i>
      can mean "arrow or dart" (also "bud of a vine" or "hand of a clock" or "magnetic needle").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batista_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Batista-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Saetas are also sung at outdoor devotions throughout
      <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">
       Lent
      </a>
      , and may be sung during the
      <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">
       Christmas
      </a>
      season as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willi_Apel_1944_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Willi_Apel_1944-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A special form of the saeta (the
      <i>
       saeta carcelera
      </i>
      ) is also sung at prisons during visits there by the confraternities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caballero-127_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Caballero-127-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several of the cities in
      <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
       Andalusia
      </a>
      have their own peculiar styles of the saeta.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Four intentions or themes have been distinguished from the religious lyrics, which mark the music of Saeta: descriptive, praising, prayerful, and exhortative.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_music">
       The music
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saeta_(flamenco)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The music">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       saeta
      </b>
      is frequently sung
      <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">
       a cappella
      </a>
      . Taken from
      <a href="/wiki/Cante_flamenco#Types_of_Cante" title="Cante flamenco">
       flamenco
      </a>
      music are the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melismas" title="Melismas">
       melismas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tuplet" title="Tuplet">
       tercios
      </a>
      , and other flourishes. Although the saeta is one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cantes_a_palo_seco" title="Cantes a palo seco">
       cantes a palo seco
      </a>
      , the singer may also be accompanied, e.g., by beating drums, and horns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batista_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Batista-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Arabic and Hebraic origins have been proposed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Palo_(flamenco)" title="Palo (flamenco)">
       Palos
      </a>
      of flamenco adopted by the saeta include especially the
      <a href="/wiki/Siguiriyas" title="Siguiriyas">
       siguiriyas
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Martinetes" title="Martinetes">
       martinetes
      </a>
      . Other forms include the
      <i>
       saeta por
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soleares" title="Soleares">
        soleares
       </a>
      </i>
      , the
      <i>
       por polos
      </i>
      , the
      <i>
       por caas
      </i>
      , and the
      <i>
       por
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fandangos" title="Fandangos">
        fandangos
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Flamenco guitar accompaniment may be in 2/4 or 6/8
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_(music)" title="Time (music)">
       time
      </a>
      , or may interchange regularly between 3/4 and 6/8 .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Commonly the
      <i>
       saetero
      </i>
      sings in a minor key finishing on the dominant; the
      <a href="/wiki/Meter_(music)" title="Meter (music)">
       meter
      </a>
      of different verses will often be variable depending on the interpretation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batista_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Batista-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The cantaor
      <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Torre" title="Manuel Torre">
       Manuel Torre
      </a>
      (18781933) was well regarded for his Saeta.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Its_passion">
       Its passion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saeta_(flamenco)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Its passion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Of a diverse heritage, the
      <b>
       Saeta
      </b>
      has become the emotional artistic fruit of several cultures. The Gypsies "se sienten identificados con los episodios de la
      <a href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" title="Passion (Christianity)">
       Pasin
      </a>
      y consideran a Jess como un hermano en desgracia que sufre persecusin y
      <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">
       muerte
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nothing of course substitutes for hearing and bearing witness to the Saeta.
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       "La saeta, pues, costituye la sntesis antropolgica del andaluz (hondura, plstica, seoro, dolor metafsico) coronada en santidad. La saeta exige un mximo de veracidad pasional, por lo mismo que a nadie le es dado encaramarse en la audacia de sus ayes sin la potencia y la certeza que brinda la posesin heroica del dolor."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         21
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      It is said that Andalusians must talk to God during
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_in_Spain" title="Holy Week in Spain">
       Holy Week
      </a>
      , singing the Saeta during a
      <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">
       cofrada
      </a>
      procession being a mode of choice.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
