         <div class="entry-header">
          <div class="entry-header-wrap">
           <h1 class="entry-title">
            Why Did America Drop The U In British Spellings?
           </h1>
           <div class="entry-meta">
            <span class="meta-prep meta-prep-author">
             By
            </span>
            <a class="url fn" href="http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/author/frasermcalpine/" rel="author" title="Posts by Fraser McAlpine">
             Fraser McAlpine
            </a>
            <span class="meta-sep meta-sep-date">
             |
            </span>
            <span class="posted-on">
             Posted on May 13th, 2014
            </span>
           </div>
           <div class="share-bar">
            <span class="share-copy fancy-font">
            </span>
            <span class="share gplus">
             <div class="g-plusone" data-annotation="none" data-href="http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/2014/05/america-drop-u-british-spellings/" data-size="medium">
             </div>
            </span>
            <span class="share twitter">
             <a class="twitter-share-button" data-count="horizontal" data-related="bbcamerica" data-text="Why Did America Drop The U In British Spellings?" data-url="http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/2014/05/america-drop-u-british-spellings/" data-via="anglophenia" href="http://twitter.com/share?url=http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/2014/05/america-drop-u-british-spellings/">
             </a>
            </span>
            <span class="share facebook">
             <div class="fb-like" data-action="like" data-href="http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/2014/05/america-drop-u-british-spellings/" data-layout="button_count" data-share="false" data-show-faces="false" data-width="84">
             </div>
            </span>
            <span class="share tumblr">
             <a href="http://www.tumblr.com/share" style="display:inline-block; text-indent:-9999px; overflow:hidden; width:62px; height:20px; margin-left:10px; background:url('http://platform.tumblr.com/v1/share_2.png') top left no-repeat transparent;" title="Share on Tumblr">
              Share on Tumblr
             </a>
             <script src="http://platform.tumblr.com/v1/share.js" type="text/javascript">
             </script>
            </span>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="entry-content">
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_119978" style="width: 622px">
           <a class="thickbox no_icon" href="http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/files/2014/05/armour.jpg" rel="gallery-119976" title="A medieval knight in his armour, sorry, armor. (Pic: AP Images)">
            <img alt="A medieval knight in his armour, sorry, armor. (Pic: AP Images)" class="size-full wp-image-119978" height="344" src="http://www.bbcamerica.com/anglophenia/files/2014/05/armour.jpg" width="612"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            A medieval knight in his armour, sorry, armor. (Pic: AP Images)
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           Whichever branch of the English language you are brought up with, you have to admit parts of it are an inconsistent mess. There are words that are spelled broadly the same but pronounced entirely differently
           <em>
            cough
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            bough
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            borough
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            through
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            brought
           </em>
           words that are spelled entirely differently but pronounced the same
           <em>
            write
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            right
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            two
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            too
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            to
           </em>
           and words that do the same job and have only slightly different spellings
           <em>
            obfuscate
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            obfusticate
           </em>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           And of course, as with most things that are old and full of character, theres a lot of beauty in that mess, but also a lot of room for people to argue over which aspect of the mess best exemplifies the true spirit of this living, breathing, evolving thing we all use every day.
          </p>
          <p>
           One particularly vexatious argument concerns the lack of uniform spellings between British and American English. The simple reason for this is that England and America went their separate ways before anyone became unduly rigorous about spelling words the same way every time. The firm nailing down of language happened in earnest during the 1800s, on both sides of the Atlantic, and thanks largely to the reforming zeal of American lexicographer
           <strong>
            Noah Webster
           </strong>
           , it was with markedly different results in the U.S. than in Victorian Britain.
          </p>
          <p>
           Seeking to wrest control of the language from the British ruling classes, Noah wrote three books that aimed to make a tidy pile of that mess we were talking about. One on grammar, one on reading, and one on spelling. His firstoriginally titled
           <em>
            The First Part of the Grammatical Institute of the English Language
           </em>
           , then
           <em>
            The American Spelling Book
           </em>
           , then
           <em>
            The Elementary Spelling Book
           </em>
           became the standard text book from which American teachers taught spelling for 100 years, and it was from reprints and reissues of that original text that Noah began to subtly refine words, spelling them according to how they sound.
          </p>
          <p>
           So while British English still insists on a
           <em>
            c
           </em>
           in the word
           <em>
            defense
           </em>
           , Webster changed it to an
           <em>
            s
           </em>
           .
           <em>
            Theatre
           </em>
           and
           <em>
            centre
           </em>
           were simplified into
           <em>
            theater
           </em>
           and
           <em>
            center
           </em>
           .
           <em>
            Plough
           </em>
           became
           <em>
            plow
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            axe
           </em>
           became
           <em>
            ax
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            catalogue
           </em>
           became
           <em>
            catalog
           </em>
           , and
           <em>
            flavour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            honour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            savour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            saviour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            candour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            behaviour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            colour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            armour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            demeanour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            glamour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            harbour
           </em>
           and all the rest lost their
           <em>
            u.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           This was largely to differentiate those words from the ones that end in 
           <em>
            our
           </em>
           and sound like 
           <em>
            ower
           </em>
           . As in
           <em>
            hour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            flour
           </em>
           ,
           <em>
            sour
           </em>
           and so on. Some words still enjoy a dual existence, in that the U.S. Space Shuttle
           <em>
            Endeavour
           </em>
           kept its u, as it was named after Lieutenant* James Cooks ship, HMS Endeavour.
           <em>
            Glamour
           </em>
           , being a Scots word, often keeps its
           <em>
            u
           </em>
           as well.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ironically, the one word that Noah Webster failed in his attempts to get it spelled exactly as it rolls off the tongue is
           <em>
            tongue
           </em>
           itself, which he arguedshould be
           <em>
            tung
           </em>
           , but somehow this was a step too far, despite the loss of the concluding
           <em>
            ue
           </em>
           in words like
           <em>
            catalog
           </em>
           and
           <em>
            analog
           </em>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Id have stopped before the
           <em>
            e
           </em>
           was lopped off
           <em>
            ax
           </em>
           too, but its too late to try and graft it back on now.
          </p>
          <p>
           *The reverse approach to Websterssaying things as they are spelledcan be found in this word, which the British pronounce leftenant.
          </p>
