       <header>
        <h4 class="entry-title">
         Roku 2 INXS: How the Phantom Got His Pop Culture Back (Sort Of)
        </h4>
        <span class="entry-date">
          Feb 28, 2012
        </span>
        <a class="entry-comments" href="http://capcy.com/2012/02/roku-2-inxs-phantom-pop-culture-sort-of/#comments">
         4
        </a>
        <span class="entry-view">
         4
        </span>
       </header>
       <div class="elements-box">
        <!-- Start Shareaholic LikeButtonSetTop Automatic -->
        <!-- End Shareaholic LikeButtonSetTop Automatic -->
        <p>
         Well, I promised this article in my last post, and now that Ive had the Roku 2 XS for about two weeks, I feel Ive had enough experience with it to do a decent writeup. Also, because I am an electrical engineer, I cannot escape the harsh reality of talking about technology even on weekends.
        </p>
        <p>
         First off, some of you may be asking What is a Roku?. It is a small device dedicated wholly to the streaming of music and/or video content. My first experience with them was in college, where we bought one to set up in the campus cafeteria so that the radio station could be heard by a bigger audience than just the ten closest friends of the current DJ (
         <a href="http://wcpr.org" target="_blank">
          sidenote, here is a shameless plug for WCPR
         </a>
         ). This device was called the SoundBridge, and it is made to play streaming Internet music as well as AM/FM radio. It was just discontinued this month, but the forums are still very active with users.
        </p>
        <p>
         Moving onRoku in recent years has expanded to, and decided to focus squarely on, streaming video content. They were the first to market with a device made for streaming Netflix onto your TV. Currently, they are on their second series of video streaming boxes, which
         <a href="http://www.roku.com/roku-products" target="_blank">
          come in several models
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         So whats the point of buying one of these? There are several:
         <br/>
         1) Cable is a bit expensive.
         <br/>
         2) Its hard to watch things on your own schedule without buying a TiVo or something similar.
         <br/>
         3) Cable doesnt let you buy channels a la carte.
         <br/>
         4) You may have your own collection of music, photos, and video files you want to watch in addition.
        </p>
        <p>
         There are several similar devices on the market such as Boxee and Western Digital TV. There is also the expensive and more time-consuming option of building a home theater PC. Dont get me wrong, these are all also solid options, and if I had the time or money I would have built a HTPC.
        </p>
        <p>
         I went for the Roku 2XS. This is the highest end model. It comes with a wired ethernet port (for some reason the lower models in the 2 series do not  but no one in their right mind should stream over wifi unless they have a blazing fast connection), a USB port, a microSD card port, and a controller that somewhat resembles that of the Wii. The Roku 2XS costs around a hundred bucks. I cashed in some Amazon points, so I got the Roku, 2 microSD cards, and an HDMI cable for about 50 bucks total. Buying a microSD card is absolutely necessary as it increases the memory capacity and shortens loading time for channels.
        </p>
        <p>
         Okay, so after enough tech mumbo-jumbo, is this thing worth it? Absolutely.
        </p>
        <p>
         There are many channels here. But dont think of them as channels in the cable sense. They are more like apps on a smartphone or tablet (surprise surprise, the Roku runs Linux). They are really just aggregating various Internet streams for you to access easily. For example, the Revision3 channel, airs Epic Meal Time (probably my favorite Youtube series), Diggs video podcasts, and more. There are free channels and paid channels (subscription or one-time fee). There are also private channels. These are where a developer is working on a channel but hasnt got enough confidence to release it for use in the Roku Channel Store. So, you know, if you want to watch the Shamu The Whale cameras at SeaWorld, there is one for that (not a joke).
        </p>
        <p>
         The Roku also has games. The controller has a motion sensor, so you can play Angry Birds on your glorious big-screen TV in high definition. Most of the games are paid (Angry Birds is free with the XS), although some are free. Dont expect Call of Duty or something like that. They are mainly arcade, number, or casino games. Not a bad thing, and I think there is a definite market for expansion here for casual gaming.
        </p>
        <p>
         So far as playing your own media, this is really my only griping point. There is still no support for AVI files, which a lot of my collection is. It plays many other types of photo, music, and video files, though, so I suppose this is just a matter of waiting for some a thousand Linux geeks typing furiously at a thousand computers to produce the right codec.
        </p>
        <p>
         All in all is the Roku worth it? Definitely.
         <br/>
         1) I like watching whatever I want on my own time without overwriting TiVo or DVR hard drive space. Now that I have Netflix, I can actually understand references to Mad Men and other shows that my friends watch. To be fair, they dont get all my sci-fi references, but still.
         <br/>
         2) Theres stuff similar to what youll find on cable, but without ads or snotty celebrities. Programs are also shorter  there are a lot of them that are less than 5 minutes if you just want to watch some quick videos.
         <br/>
         3) Its good to support smaller, independent media with original content. I mean, really, how many reality shows do you need to watch?
         <br/>
         4) Theres a very dedicated and friendly user base on the forums.
         <br/>
         5) Angry Birds on a 46-inch screen in 1080p is freaking BALLER. go back to playing on your smartphone, you primitives!
        </p>
