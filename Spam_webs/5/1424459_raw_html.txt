      <h1 class="omc-post-heading-standard">
       How your brain prevents your business from evolving
      </h1>
      <p>
       <a id="dd_start">
       </a>
       <br/>
      </p>
      <h5>
       Change management initiative stalled? Blame neuroscience (and yourself), not your team
      </h5>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Its easy to blame a failed initiative on stubbornness and resistance, but the more likely culprit is lack of empathy and poor management.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Ask anyone who has ever tried to push through significant change at an organization and theyll tell you its hard.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Buy them a beer after a long week and ask them why, and theyll probably give you the same basic answer: people.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Individuals complain, teams resist, initiatives crawl and managers get frustrated. Its natural enough when these all-too-common issues arise to conclude that humans are simply contrary creatures, some folks are ornery and complacent, and why cant everyone just get with the program already?!
      </p>
      <h5 style="color: #393939;">
       The neuroscience of cranky
      </h5>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       The passage above presents a common view of what holds back big change, but its not a view shared by Sylvia Doane Stephenson, VP of client engagement and strategy at change management consultancy
       <a href="http://www.futurestate.com/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.futurestate.com/', 'Future State']);" target="_blank">
        Future State
       </a>
       , which works with clients like the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation and Facebook. Where others see cranky or clueless individuals, Doane Stephenson sees basic biology.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Essentially whats happening is the old flight or fight syndrome. If your manager comes to you and says we have to make some really significant changes around here, the reaction is fear. Immediately the tiger is at the door, and whats literally happening in your body is the release of the stress hormone cortisol, which causes that same chemical reaction as something like a tiger, she explained to Central Desktop in an interview.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Of course, your employees arent literally bolting for the door when you inform them of a reorg or merger, but their fear will nonetheless manifest in destructive ways, according to Doane Stephenson. Your brain sort of shuts down and you start either putting yourself down emotionally  Im not ready. I cant do this.  or mentally tearing down whoever is delivering the feedback  They dont know what theyre talking about. Im not going to buy into this, she says. Both of those are not great because you are not engaged and youre not actually hearing the important message, much less getting on board with it.
      </p>
      <h5 style="color: #393939;">
       <strong>
        Whos to blame?
       </strong>
      </h5>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       When your team resists change, in other words, they arent being difficult; theyre being human. And if that reality has stalled your initiative, the fault probably lies with your management approach, not their bad attitudes. We want change to be about numbers and rational policies, but if you want to get anywhere, Doane Stephenson believes, change also has to be about human psychology.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       The culture of the corporation is not one that is set up for human interaction and designed for things like empathy. Its more about data and analytics, she says. But this cut-and-dried approach, devoid of any appreciation for how the brains of humans actually function, leaves many organizations poorly prepared to implement significant change.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Everybody loves to talk about Lets collaborate! Lets innovate! There are a lot of barriers to that happening when people feel theyre not really seen and heard or its not safe to put forth their ideas. Organizations are not set up to align to the natural brain wiring of human beings, she explains.
      </p>
      <h5 style="color: #393939;">
       <strong>
        Abetter way
       </strong>
      </h5>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Successfully implementing change requires companies to take human psychology into consideration from the start. Which, Doane Stephenson concedes, can be time consuming. It is an investment of time, most especially at the beginning of an initiative. Ideally, bake in all along the roadmap some opportunities for people to be engaged, she says.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       What form should those opportunities to talk through fears, understand objectives, and build consensus take? Things that sound really sort of trite like simply making time for people to go take happy hour together, suggests Doane Stephenson, who rattles off a long list of best practices that managers can use to lessen their teams innate response to threatening change, including town halls, hearing from executives, management by walking around (even virtual walking around) and various other ways for employees to ask questions and give feedback.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       People dont want to make that investment. Its gosh, we already told those people where were going. Cant they just get on board?! she says, but putting in that initial effort can pay big dividends. Take a tech initiative, where resistance and fear can often result in underutilization and missed ROI. If you bake this stuff into your project, the project actually goes live on time, and the return on investment is achieved because theres much more significant adoption, claims Doane Stephenson.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       Her bottom line recommendation is to stop thinking of resistance as exceptional and individual and start planning to cope with it from the word go.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       If we can remember that this is actually a very normal human response, that our brains are actually wired to function this way and that its not somebody just intentionally being contrary, we can design things to engage them from the beginning, concludes Doane Stephenson.
      </p>
      <p style="color: #393939;">
       The end result? Projects go better. They get delivered on time.
      </p>
