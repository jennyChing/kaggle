        <h1 class="title entry-title">
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/2010/09/10/q-a-how-can-anything-come-from/" rel="bookmark">
          Q &amp; A: How can anything
          <i>
           come from
          </i>
          a black hole?
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="byline vcard author">
         <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-58 photo" height="58" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2013/10/SWAB_logo-58x58.jpg" srcset="http://0.gravatar.com/avatar/6e260b0d51ec7a86c48b3360c8682a32?s=116&amp;d=mm&amp;r=pg 2x" width="58"/>
         Posted by
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/author/esiegel/" title="Posts by Ethan">
           Ethan
          </a>
         </span>
         on
         <abbr class="published" title="2010-09-10T19:50:43+00:00">
          September 10, 2010
         </abbr>
        </div>
        <!--/byline-->
       </div>
       <!--/header-->
       <div class="box-share">
        <div class="comments">
         <a class="to-comments" href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/2010/09/10/q-a-how-can-anything-come-from/#comments" rev="post-505">

        <div addthis:title="Q &amp; A: How can anything &lt;i&gt;come from&lt;/i&gt; a black hole?" addthis:url="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/2010/09/10/q-a-how-can-anything-come-from/" class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style">
         <a class="addthis_button_facebook_like" fb:like:layout="button_count" style="opacity:1;">
         </a>
         <a class="addthis_button_tweet" tw:via="Scienceblogs">
         </a>
         <a class="addthis_button_google_plusone" g:plusone:size="medium">
         </a>

       <div class="main">
        <div class="content entry-content">
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           Consideration of particle emission from black holes would seem to suggest that God not only plays dice, but also sometimes throws them where they cannot be seen. 
           <em>
            Stephen Hawking
           </em>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Last week, I wrote about how high-energy cosmic rays
          <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/2010/09/particle_physics_relativity_ma.php">
           strike Earths atmosphere
          </a>
          and wind up bombarding everything on Earths surface with super-fast moving particles, some of which are even
          <em>
           unstable
          </em>
          !
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/cosmic-rays.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-22180" height="911" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/cosmic-rays-600x911.jpg" title="cosmic-rays" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          In passing, I mentioned that these cosmic rays (mostly protons) come from a variety of sources, such as the Sun, neutron stars, supernova remnants, the centers of galaxies, and (everyones favorite)
          <strong>
           black holes
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/cosmic-rays-galaxies-picture.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22181" height="332" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/cosmic-rays-galaxies-picture.jpeg" title="cosmic rays galaxies picture" width="461"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          (Above is a starburst galaxy, with light from cosmic rays colliding with the interstellar/intergalactic medium shown in false color.)
         </p>
         <p>
          As always, there were some very good comments on the post, including
          <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/2010/09/particle_physics_relativity_ma.php#comment-2777203">
           one
          </a>
          that took me to task for the black holes statement:
         </p>
         <blockquote>
          <p>
           How does anything come from a black hole?
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          Aaah, black holes, quite possibly the
          <a href="http://www.abc.net.au/science/articles/2008/07/23/2312355.htm">
           suckiest
          </a>
          objects in the Universe.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/Black-hole-Reuters.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-22182" height="776" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/Black-hole-Reuters-600x776.jpg" title="Undated illustration shows artist's impression of black hole engine" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          So, how about it? Everyone knows that the definition of a black hole is that its something so dense and so massive that nothing  not matter, not anti-matter, not even light  can escape from behind the
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon">
           event horizon
          </a>
          of a black hole. And this is true!
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/BlackHole.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22183" height="294" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/BlackHole.jpeg" title="BlackHole" width="368"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          But
          <em>
           near
          </em>
          a black hole, just outside the event horizon, theres some incredible stuff going on. First off, the matter. Every black hole has a flat disc of matter orbiting it at an incredibly high speed. Due to forces like friction and gravitational tides, the matter gets ripped apart into individual molecules, atoms, and subatomic particles, and eventually spirals in towards the center.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_22184" style="width: 610px">
          <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/blackhole_hobart_big.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="size-medium wp-image-22184" height="400" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/blackhole_hobart_big-600x400.jpg" title="blackhole_hobart_big" width="600"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Image credit: April Hobart.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          So we wind up with  somewhat close to the event horizon  a bunch of small, fast-moving, and
          <em>
           charged
          </em>
          particles. But the black hole, like all star-type objects, has a magnetic field. Unlike the Earth, which has a magnetic field of about 0.6 Gauss at the surface, or the Sun, which can reach a field strength of up to
          <a href="http://www.windows2universe.org/sun/sun_magnetic_field.html">
           4,000 Gauss
          </a>
          on a Sunspot, a black hole can have magnetic fields in excess of
          <strong>
           1,000,000,000,000 Gauss
          </strong>
          !!
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/event_horizon.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22185" height="404" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/event_horizon.jpeg" title="event_horizon" width="485"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          And while these charged, fast moving particles spiral in toward the black holes center, they feel the effects of the magnetic field. What effects are those?
         </p>
         <p>
          It turns out that a
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force">
           basic law of physics
          </a>
          is that, if you hold your right hand in an L shape (fingers together, thumb out), and point your fingers in the direction of the magnetic field and thumb in the direction the particle is moving, your palm pushes the particle perpendicular to both directions!
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/Right_Hand_Rule_vBF2.png">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-22186" height="260" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/Right_Hand_Rule_vBF2.png" title="Right_Hand_Rule_vBF2" width="285"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          This causes black holes to suck these particles up and down, perpendicular to the disk, and shoot them out at ultra-high speeds in two jets!
         </p>
         <p>
          And if one of those jets happens to be pointed towards you, are you ever in for a treat!
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <center>
         </center>
         <a href="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/black-hole-3.jpeg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-22187" height="450" src="http://scienceblogs.com/startswithabang/files/2010/09/black-hole-3-600x450.jpg" title="black-hole-3" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          Its a cosmic ray feast! Courtesy of your friendly neighborhood black hole, of course.
         </p>
         <p>
          And thats how a whole slew of ultra-high energy particles can come, without any problems at all, from a black hole! So enjoy the cosmic ray showers, and I hope this takes you into a great weekend!
         </p>

            <img alt="" class="avatar avatar-50 photo" height="50" src="http://2.gravatar.com/avatar/51f31b22a7064a0b3d865fe8391795bf?s=50&amp;d=mm&amp;r=pg" srcset="http://2.gravatar.com/avatar/51f31b22a7064a0b3d865fe8391795bf?s=100&amp;d=mm&amp;r=pg 2x" width="50"/>

           <div class="content">
            <p>
             Concerning black hole dynamics;
             <br/>
             On the surface, data from Hubble seems to indicate that galaxies in a local cluster collide in any number of super positions though there is one that has not been observed (AFAIK),
             <br/>
             and that is the stable jet to jet configuration of the opposing black holes with their corresponding galactic constituents. The spin and gravitational attraction may precursory account for the collision/merger, yet within closer parameters we may see a magnetic alignment that may govern the actual merger dynamics.
            </p>
            <p>
             We see M87 shining its light/jet in our direction from 55 Mly away,M87/NGC 4486 in the Virgo cluster being the largest cluster of our local super-cluster in which we reside in a separate local group which leads to the question of how close does a neighboring galaxy have to be to significantly magnetically influence another galaxy?
            </p>
            <p>
             I find it highly unlikely that any major galactic body contained within a local group to have a stable jet to jet configuration with another attracted galactic body within the same group, the science for this realm of magnetic fields is in its infancy.
            </p>
            <p>
             Further a field still, when the combined influence of local groups act upon other local groups should indicate the over all absence of jet to jet alignment of galaxies compared to galaxies in opposing local cluster within a super-cluster.
            </p>
            <p>
             So it would seem the statement:
            </p>
            <p>
             And if one of those jets happens to be pointed towards you, are you ever in for a treat!
            </p>

             Stands
            </p>

            </p>
            <p>
             If a black holes jet is pointed right at you, youll die. Read Eater by Gregory Benford for examples.
            </p>
            <p>
             In my opinion it would be very unfortunate and highly unlikely in an evolved local group such as ours.

             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             An ideal, theoretical, black hole is a purely gravitational phenomenon.  It is a feature of curved spacetime described by Einsteins theory; it involves no matter (or anti-matter) at all.
            </p>
            <p>
             In the real world, black holes are formed by the collapse of stuff out in space, and that stuff is almost all matter (as opposed to anti-matter).
