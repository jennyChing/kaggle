    Olga Taratuta

     <p>
      <b>
       Olga Taratuta
      </b>
      (January 21, 1876 [or possibly 1874 or 1878]  February 8, 1938) was a
      <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">
       Ukrainian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_communism" title="Anarchist communism">
       anarcho-communist
      </a>
      . She was the founder of the Ukrainian
      <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_Black_Cross" title="Anarchist Black Cross">
       Anarchist Black Cross
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_activism">
       Early life and activism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Olga_Taratuta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and activism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Taratuta was born Elka Ruvinskaia in the village of Novodmitrovka, near
      <a href="/wiki/Kherson" title="Kherson">
       Kherson
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
       Ukraine
      </a>
      , then part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">
       Russian Empire
      </a>
      . Her family was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">
       Jewish
      </a>
      and her father ran a small shop. Taratuta worked as a teacher after completing her studies.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taratuta was arrested on "political suspicions" in 1895. In 1897 she joined a
      <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">
       social democratic
      </a>
      group associated with Abram and Iuda Grossman in
      <a href="/wiki/Dnipropetrovsk" title="Dnipropetrovsk">
       Ekaterinoslav
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avrich69_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Avrich69-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Taratuta was a member of the South Russian Union of Workers and the Elizavetgrad committee of the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Social_Democratic_Labour_Party" title="Russian Social Democratic Labour Party">
       Social-Democratic Party
      </a>
      from 1898 and 1901. In 1901 she moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      and then to
      <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
       Switzerland
      </a>
      ; during this period she worked for the Party organ
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Iskra" title="Iskra">
        Iskra
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Spark") and met
      <a href="/wiki/Georgi_Plekhanov" title="Georgi Plekhanov">
       Georgi Plekhanov
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">
       Vladimir Lenin
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1903, while in Switzerland, Taratuta became an anarcho-communist. She returned to
      <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">
       Odessa
      </a>
      in 1904 and joined
      <i>
       Neprimirimye
      </i>
      ("The Intransigents"), which was made up of anarchists and other followers of
      <a href="/wiki/Jan_Wac%C5%82aw_Machajski" title="Jan Wacaw Machajski">
       Jan Wacaw Machajski
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Taratuta was arrested in April 1904, but she was freed for lack of evidence several months later. After her release she joined the Odessa Workers Group of Anarcho-Communists. She began to acquire a reputation as one of the foremost anarchists in Russia.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taratuta was arrested again in October 1905, but she was released during the political amnesty that resulted from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_in_1905" title="Russian Revolution in 1905">
       that year's Revolution
      </a>
      . She joined the militant wing of the South Russian Group of Anarcho-Communists, which used "motiveless terror"attacks against institutions and representatives of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">
       bourgeoisie
      </a>
      , rather than specific individuals. Taratuta was involved in the bomb attack on Odessa's Caf Libman in December 1905, for which she was sentenced to 17 years in prison.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Taratuta escaped from prison in December 1906 and fled to Geneva, where she joined
      <i>
       Buntar
      </i>
      ("The Mutineer") and edited its newspaper, also called
      <i>
       Buntar
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avrich69_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Avrich69-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In late 1907, she returned to Odessa, where she helped plan
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_of_the_deed" title="Propaganda of the deed">
        attentats
       </a>
      </i>
      against General Aleksandr Kaulbars, the commander of the Odessa military region, and against General Tolmachev, governor of Odessa, and an explosion at the Odessa tribunal.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taratuta was arrested in 1908 in Ekaterinoslav and given a 21-year prison sentence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avrich69_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Avrich69-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She was released in March 1917, following the
      <a href="/wiki/February_Revolution" title="February Revolution">
       February Revolution
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In May 1918, she joined the Political Red Cross, which help imprisoned revolutionaries of all political affiliations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avrich207_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Avrich207-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although she initially kept her distance from the anarchist movement, the growing persecution of anarchists by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">
       Bolshevik
      </a>
      government inspired Taratuta to join
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Golos_Truda" title="Golos Truda">
        Golos Truda
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Voice of Labor") and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nabat" title="Nabat">
        Nabat
       </a>
      </i>
      confederation in June 1920.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Avrich207_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Avrich207-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She returned to Ukraine in September 1920, after the
      <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Insurrectionary_Army_of_Ukraine" title="Revolutionary Insurrectionary Army of Ukraine">
       Makhnovists
      </a>
      signed a truce with the
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
       Soviet
      </a>
      government. The Makhnovist commanders gave her 5 million
      <a href="/wiki/Soviet_ruble" title="Soviet ruble">
       rubles
      </a>
      ; Taratuta went to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kharkov" title="Kharkov">
       Kharkov
      </a>
      and used the money to establish the
      <a href="/wiki/Anarchist_Black_Cross" title="Anarchist Black Cross">
       Anarchist Black Cross
      </a>
      to provide aid to imprisoned and other persecuted anarchists.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_repression">
       Soviet repression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Olga_Taratuta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soviet repression">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In November 1920, Taratuta was arrested during a Soviet sweep of anarchists and Makhnovists in Ukraine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Soviets shut down the Anarchist Black Cross. Taratuta was transferred to
      <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">
       Moscow
      </a>
      in January 1921 and to
      <a href="/wiki/Orlov,_Russia" title="Orlov, Russia">
       Orlov
      </a>
      in April 1921. The following month, she was offered a release on the condition that she publicly denounce her anarchist beliefs. Instead, she joined her fellow anarchist prisoners in an 11-day
      <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">
       hunger strike
      </a>
      . In March 1922 she was exiled to
      <a href="/wiki/Veliky_Ustyug" title="Veliky Ustyug">
       Veliky Ustyug
      </a>
      for two years.
     </p>
     <p>
      After her release in 1924, Taratuta moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">
       Kiev
      </a>
      . She was arrested in the middle of that year for publishing anarchist propaganda, but she was soon released. Later that year she moved to Moscow. In 1927, she joined the international campaign to support
      <a href="/wiki/Sacco_and_Vanzetti" title="Sacco and Vanzetti">
       Sacco and Vanzetti
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During 1928 and 1929, Taratuta wrote many letters trying to organize an international organization to support anarchists in Soviet prisons.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She moved to Odessa in 1929, and she was arrested for trying to organize an anarchist organization among the rail workers. She was sentenced to two years in prison.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taratuta returned to Moscow after her release. She joined the Society of Political Prisoners and Exiles, which tried without success to obtain pensions for old, impoverished, and sick revolutionaries. She was arrested and sentenced in 1933, but little is known about this arrest.
     </p>
     <p>
      Taratuta was arrested on November 27, 1937, and accused of anarchist and anti-Soviet activity. She was condemned to death on February 8, 1938, and executed the same day.
     </p>
