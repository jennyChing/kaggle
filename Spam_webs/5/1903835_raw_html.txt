        DJing in Nightclubs with Controllers and Laptops
       </div>
       <div class="post-521 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry tag-club-djing tag-digital-djing-in-clubs tag-djing-in-clubs entry">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         DJing in Nightclubs with Controllers and Laptops
        </h1>
        <div class="post-info">
         <span class="date published time" title="2010-07-13T17:55:59+00:00">
          July 13, 2010
         </span>
         by
         <span class="author vcard">
          <span class="fn">
           <a href="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/author/pbmorse/" rel="author">
            Phil Morse
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="post-comments">
          <a href="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/2010/07/djing-in-nightclubs-with-controllers-and-laptops/#comments">
           61 Comments
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <div class="entry-content">
         <div class="post-views post-521 entry-meta">
          <span class="post-views-icon dashicons dashicons-visibility">
          </span>
          <span class="post-views-label">
           Post Views:
          </span>
          <span class="post-views-count">
           749
          </span>
         </div>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_522" style="width: 310px">
          <a href="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/miketruman.png">
           <img alt="Mike Truman" class="size-medium wp-image-522" height="188" src="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/miketruman-300x188.png" title="miketruman" width="300"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Digital kit has never stopped Ritchie Hawtin rocking clubs worldwide...
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Got a club gig coming up and scared about the practicalities of setting up in the DJ box when you get there? Worried about buying digital DJ kit because you want to play in clubs and feel you should learn CDJs instead?
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Calling time on timecode
          </strong>
          <br/>
          While many DJs have DVS systems so they can use a pair of Technics in a club environment, there's only one way all that malarkey is going, especially as most clubs don't even have Technics any more. (Mind you, at least with Technics you know what you're getting. Trying to use timecode CDs in a club opens you up to worrying about what type of CDJs you're going to find there too...)
         </p>
         <p>
          And as you've still got to take a laptop, what are you really gaining over the laptop/controller route by using such a system?
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           CDs still rule, but they're a cop-out
           <br/>
          </strong>
          This is why even today many DJs still choose to organise their music on their laptops, but then either burn it to CD or - for the few lucky enough to play in clubs with compatible equipment - put their sets onto USB drives to use with installation club players like the Pioneer CDJ-2000s.
         </p>
         <p>
          Which is a real shame, because such DJs are missing out on all the advantages of DJing with a laptop and a controller, such as familiarity with your own equipment, lightning-fast search of your complete collection, access to previous playlists, the ability to throw everything in your bag and head off to DJ at after-parties wherever you wish, and so on.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_526" style="width: 310px">
          <a href="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/amnesiadjbooth.jpg">
           <img alt="Amnesia DJ booth" class="size-medium wp-image-526" height="247" src="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/amnesiadjbooth-300x247.jpg" title="amnesiadjbooth" width="300"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           That litttle glowing Apple has become as common as the Technics and Pioneer logos on DJ kit.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Things are changing all the time
          </strong>
          <br/>
          When I started DJing in clubs, it was vinyl all the way. Then we started booking acts who mixed live and sequenced music with their vinyl or CD sets. (I'm thinking the likes of Hybrid with their acid groove box, or Groove Armada's Tom Cato marching round our dancefloor with a miked-up trombone...) Later still, we started seeing DJs turning up with Ableton. I knew the game was lost and got a laptop myself. That was 2004, and things have got MUCH better for digital DJs since then.
         </p>
         <p>
          So how did we do it? How did we squeeze all those increasingly weird and wonderful combinations of acts, gear and DJ systems into our DJ booth?
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Playing it by ear
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Truth is, just like the acts and DJs we booked, you'll never know exactly how to set up in a new place until you get there (unless it's local and you can check the DJ booth out beforehand, of course. It is? Great! Do it.)
         </p>
         <p>
          So here's some of the things DJs often do.
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           They put the lids onto the Technics record decks and set their equipment up on the top of those
          </li>
          <li>
           They carry fold-up stands (such as these sold by the ever-excellent DJ Tech Tools boys).
          </li>
          <li>
           They unplug and move CDJs (Alright, so it's probably good manners to plug then in again if then next DJ needs them, but that's life nowadays in DJ boxes. It's no biggie.)
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          But you can think more laterally too. Why not set up away from the normal DJ box? We used to set up on a club stage in a great position way away from the DJ box. We'd Duct-taped long phono-to-phono or XLR leads connecting our digital kit to the "main " DJ booth. After all, no amount of jumping around will make digital DJ sets skip and jump like records or CDs, so why not get out into the crowd instead?
         </p>
         <div class="ad-zone ad-zone-468" id="acm-ad-tag-1368693551520" style="width:468px; height:60px;">
         </div>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_523" style="width: 310px">
          <a href="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/djbooth.jpg">
           <img alt="DJ booth" class="size-medium wp-image-523 " height="225" src="http://www.digitaldjtips.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/djbooth-300x225.jpg" title="djbooth" width="300"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Laptops, controllers, records and CDs can co-exist happily in today's DJ booth.
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Smile and be bold - it's all been done before...
          </strong>
          <br/>
          The lesson is to try things. Investigate. Be prepared. Be bold. Digital is fun, so use its flexibility. With USB controllers and long-life laptop batteries, you don't even need a mains socket to pay for a couple of hours nowadays, as long as theres a PA for you to plug into!
         </p>
         <p>
          Another thing is, the venue manager has probably seen it all before. So just make sure you explain how you work, and ask beforehand (when you're booked preferably).
         </p>
         <p>
          But the most important thing is that you shouldn't ever feel shy or feel inferior about using controllers or laptops to DJ from in clubs - DJing is about music, not kit. You could play off an iPod if you loved it enough and your tunes were hyper-cool. I'm being serious.
         </p>
         <p>
          There's a lot of rubbish spoken by blinkered people about "you have to do it this way, or that way", and they're mainly just scared of new (probably younger) DJs. Telling you that you "can't play in a club with that kit" is just another barrier such people try to put up, a barrier that you're going to have to break down if you want to make it as a modern digital DJ.
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           Have you got a story about getting your digital DJing kit set up in a nightclub? Are you going to be playing in a nightclub and feel worried that you'll have problems with getting set up when you get there? We'd love to know your thoughts below.
          </em>
         </p>

           <p>
            Yes, its about music. And sound quality is directly related to what music is.
            <br/>
            Dont get lost in useless talks about vinyl v/s CDs or 320 kbps vs lower rates. Digital music could be every bit as good as vinyl and even better, but that is if you play WAVs, not mp3s. Not ANY mp3 does it. An all 320 kbps set Dj doesnt actually exist, so get over this and play a 100% uncompressed WAV set and feel the difference. Yes, its about the music.
           </p>
           <p>
            Try telling virtual Djs that mp3s harsh treble tire peoples ears, its similar to telling newbie Djs to keep green volumen levels.
           </p>
           <p>
            Mp3s could get away with it at home, car &amp; headphones and possibly at very noisy small pubs or with noisy music styles(indie, punk) but any medium to big installed amplification with mostly any other music style(even Metallica and 70s funk fits this category) will sound completely different when sourced from WAVs instead.
           </p>
           <p>
            Party and Local owners are starting to realize that attendance early-leaving is not because of bad quality booze, but sound systems and bad quality sourced music inficting on the listeners ears. There are studies about this, even with people who couldt tell the difference, but reacted after some time of exposure
           </p>
           <p>
            That is the remaining barrier for Digital Djs with controllers &amp; laptops at Nightclubs. Its not the gear, its not some kind of anti-controllerism or a condescendent attitude from due past turnablists or beatmatchers; its the much spreaded own Digital Djs unprofesionalism when comes to gathering their music.
           </p>
           <p>
            And sites like this one contribute to this effect by not providing visible enough correct advice in this sensitive matter.
           </p>

          <div class="comment-content">
           <p>
            On the WAV vs MP3 issue
           </p>
           <p>
            I do think DJs should play uncompressed audio if they can, but I dont have any time for people that claim _all_ MP3s sound terrible and shouldnt ever be used on a big sound system.
           </p>
           <p>
            I think Jeff (two posts up) makes some excellent points. The weakest point in the audio chain is where the sound enters the air, i.e. the speakers, and the way it interacts with the room and other speakers in the system.
           </p>
           <p>
            Clubs and Bars almost always have poor acoustics. Id be very surprised if anyone could tell the difference between a first generation 192k MP3 and WAV on most club systems. Most people cant tell the difference on top notch studio gear.
           </p>
           <p>
            I think the reason MP3 djs get a bad rap is that some of them get all their music from illegal download sites or Youtube. Files that have been re-compressed several times, start out at very low bitrates, or that have just been badly produced in the first place really will sound terrible on any sound system.
           </p>
           <p>
            Digital music has made it a lot easier to get into DJing, which is great but
           </p>
           <p>
            When many of us old timers started, getting music was very time consuming and expensive. I used to travel for hours on the train to get to a decent record shop, and pay 7 or more (sometimes much more) for each record. When your time and money is limited (as it is for most people) it forces you to REALLY LISTEN to what you are buying, and be very critical about what you choose.
           </p>
           <p>
            This is the skill that the new generation of digital DJs need to learn. Get a good pair of headphones and REALLY LISTEN to your tracks, be ruthless and ditch the ones that sound muddy, flat or lifeless.
           </p>
           <p>
            Learning to spot great production will make you a better DJ. As always, the best advice is to use your ears!
           </p>

            <a href="http://www.idontpayformusic.com" rel="nofollow">
             http://www.idontpayformusic.com
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            I believe in the right gear for the job and I also beg anyone to notice the difference between 320kb mp3 and 256kb AAC. Ive played 256kb AAC files from 22:00hrs  07:00hrs in 200sqm marque on a 3000watt system running at nearly 100dB for over 6 hours and I just couldnt tire the guests out. I basically ended the party on a mutual basis but Im sure Id still be there now if I hadnt said 5 times Ok, this is the last song.
            <br/>
            I didnt have any solid walls keeping the bass in and perfectly placed speakers for the best acoustics.
           </p>
           <p>
            So from 1st hand experience I know that WAV vs. Digital isnt the issue. A lot of top DJs are producers and push 24-bit files through their PA systems which just makes a laughing stock WAV anyway, so should we be demanding .aif from record companies to play out in clubs. What does surprise me is all bitching about HQ audio but theres enough video evidence on YouTube showing so called top DJs having their mixers lit up like July 4th. So in that respect they could well be playing 24-bit .aif audio but all thats coming out those metal bins is crap. Mind you at over 100dB the crowd cant tell either.
           </p>
           <p>
            So really this is about the lazy vs. the professionals. The evolutionists vs. those who cant comprehend. When I see a turntablist I dont think Dinasour. If i see someone using a little midi-controller I dont think cheat. If theyre rockin the crowd thats all that counts. However when I see some gimp playing a YouTube stream from his iPhone and the crowd are holding their ears in pain then I feel like stamping on his iPhone and smashing it into as many pieces possible and then say to the DJ. Thatll be you next time I see you ever do that again.
           </p>
           <p>
            Please dont class all digital DJs as the same. were not; just like not all turntablists can scratch.
           </p>
