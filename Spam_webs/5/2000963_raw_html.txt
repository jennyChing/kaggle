     <p>
      <b>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="w:Groucho Marx">
        Julius Henry Marx
       </a>
      </b>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2_October" title="2 October">
       2 October
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/1890" title="1890">
       1890
      </a>
      
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/19_August" title="19 August">
       19 August
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/1977" title="1977">
       1977
      </a>
      ), primarily known as
      <b>
       Groucho Marx,
      </b>
      was an American
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/comedian" title="w:comedian">
       comedian
      </a>
      and
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/actor" title="w:actor">
       actor
      </a>
      , famous for his work in the
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="w:Marx Brothers">
       Marx Brothers
      </a>
      comedy team, and his solo film and television career.
     </p>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:146px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:You_bet_your_life_postcard_1953.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="222" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/You_bet_your_life_postcard_1953.JPG/144px-You_bet_your_life_postcard_1953.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/You_bet_your_life_postcard_1953.JPG/216px-You_bet_your_life_postcard_1953.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6e/You_bet_your_life_postcard_1953.JPG 2x" width="144"/>

        I get credit all the time for things I never said.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       A likely story  and probably true.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <i>
          The Al Jolson Show
         </i>
         repartee following a trite, scripted
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Jolson" title="w:Al Jolson">
          Al Jolson
         </a>
         joke. (1949)
         <sup title="Inadequate citation">
          [
          <a href="/wiki/Wikiquote:Citing_sources" title="Wikiquote:Citing sources">
           <i>
            specificcitationneeded
           </i>
          </a>
          ]
         </sup>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Although it is generally known, I think it's about time to announce that I was born at a very early age.
       <ul>
        <li>
         From his autobiography
         <i>
          Groucho and Me
         </i>
         (1959)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       I sent the club a wire stating, "PLEASE ACCEPT MY RESIGNATION. I DON'T WANT TO BELONG TO ANY CLUB THAT WILL ACCEPT PEOPLE LIKE ME AS A MEMBER".
       <ul>
        <li>
         Telegram to the
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friars_Club_of_Beverly_Hills" title="w:Friars Club of Beverly Hills">
          Friar's Club of Beverly Hills
         </a>
         to which he belonged, as recounted in
         <i>
          Groucho and Me
         </i>
         (1959), p. 321
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        [Variant:]
       </i>
       "Please accept my resignation. I dont care to belong to any club that will have me as a member".
       <ul>
        <li>
         As quoted in
         <i>
          The Groucho Letters
         </i>
         (1967) by Arthur Sheekman. The sentiment predates Marx by 61 years, however; it likely originated with John Galsworthy in The Forsyte Saga.
         <a class="external text" href="http://www.gutenberg.org/etext/2559" rel="nofollow">
          In Part I, Chapter II, "Old Jolyon Goes to the Opera"
         </a>
         , it's said of Old Jolyon that, "He naturally despised the Club that
         <i>
          did
         </i>
         take him." after another refused him because he was in a trade.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       No one is completely unhappy at the failure of his best friend.
       <ul>
        <li>
         From his book
         <i>
          Groucho and Me
         </i>
         . It is a variation of a maxim by 17th-century French nobleman
         <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_de_La_Rochefoucauld" title="Franois de La Rochefoucauld">
          Franois de La Rochefoucauld
         </a>
         : "In the adversity of our best friends, we often find something that is not displeasing." (Maxim 99 from
         <i>
          Reflections; or Sentences and Moral Maxims
         </i>
         , 1665 edition.)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Here's to our wives and girlfriends... may they never meet!
       <sup title="Needs citation">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Wikiquote:Citing_sources" title="Wikiquote:Citing sources">
         <i>
          citationneeded
         </i>
        </a>
        ]
       </sup>
       (Variation on an old Royal Navy wardroom toast: "Wives and Sweethearts! May they never meet!"
       <sup title="Needs citation">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Wikiquote:Citing_sources" title="Wikiquote:Citing sources">
         <i>
          citationneeded
         </i>
        </a>
        ]
       </sup>
       )
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       From the moment I picked your book up until I laid it down I was convulsed with laughter. Someday I intend on reading it.
       <ul>
        <li>
         To
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S_J_Perelman" title="w:S J Perelman">
          S J Perelman
         </a>
         about his book
         <i>
          Dawn Ginsberghs Revenge
         </i>
         (1929), as quoted in
         <i>
          LIFE
         </i>
         (9 February 1962)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       I never forget a face, but in your case I'll be glad to make an exception.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Quote by
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Rosten" title="w:Leo Rosten">
          Leo Rosten
         </a>
         in
         <a class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=8FkwAAAAIAAJ&amp;q=%22i+never+forget+a+face+but+in+your+case+i'll+be+glad+to+make+an+exception%22&amp;pg=PA17#v=onepage" rel="nofollow">
          <i>
           The Many Worlds of Leo Rosten
          </i>
         </a>
         (1964)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       They say Allen got something from the Marx Brothers. He got nothing. Maybe twenty years ago, he might have been inspired. Today he's an original. The best, the funniest.
       <ul>
        <li>
         On
         <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">
          Woody Allen
         </a>
         , in an interview with
         <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
          Roger Ebert
         </a>
         in
         <a class="external text" href="http://rogerebert.suntimes.com/apps/pbcs.dll/article?AID=/19720307/PEOPLE/41116001" rel="nofollow">
          <i>
           Esquire
          </i>
          magazine (7 March 1972)
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       I got $25 from
       <i>
        Reader's Digest
       </i>
       last week for something I never said. I get credit all the time for things I never said. You know that line in
       <i>
        You Bet Your Life
       </i>
       ? The guy says he has seventeen kids and I say: "I smoke a cigar, but I take it out of my mouth occasionally"? I never said that.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Interview with
         <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
          Roger Ebert
         </a>
         in
         <i>
          Esquire
         </i>
         magazine (7 March 1972); more on this at
         <a class="external text" href="http://www.snopes.com/radiotv/tv/grouchocigar.asp" rel="nofollow">
          Snopes.com: "I Love My Cigar"
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       I did a bond tour during the Second World War... We were raising money, and we played Boston and Philadelphia and most of the big cities. And we got to Minneapolis. There wasn't any big theater to play there, so we did our show in a railroad station. Then I told the audience that I knew a girl in Minneapolis. She was also known in St.Paul, she used to come over to visit me. She was known as "The Tail Of Two Cities." I didn't sell any more bonds, but eh... they didn't allow me to appear anymore.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Recounting a War Bonds tour in his Carnegie Hall appearance (6 May 1972)
         <sup title="Inadequate citation">
          [
          <a href="/wiki/Wikiquote:Citing_sources" title="Wikiquote:Citing sources">
           <i>
            specificcitationneeded
           </i>
          </a>
          ]
         </sup>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       My experience is that people are most likely to listen to reason when in bed.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Liner notes of
         <i>
          An Evening With Groucho
         </i>
         (1972) the recording of his appearance at Carnegie Hall.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       I don't have a photograph. I'd give you my footprints, but they're upstairs in my socks.
       <ul>
        <li>
         When asked for a photograph for identification
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          The Groucho Phile
         </i>
         (1976)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       I find television very educational. Every time someone switches it on I go into another room and read a good book.
       <ul>
        <li>
         As quoted in
         <i>
          Halliwells Filmgoers Companion
         </i>
         (1984) by Leslie Halliwell
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       To write an autobiography of Groucho Marx would be as asinine as to read an autobiography of Groucho Marx.
       <ul>
        <li>
         Just after completing his second autobiography, as quoted in
         <i>
          The Marx Brothers: A Bio-bibliography
         </i>
         (1987) by Wes D. Gehring, p. 137
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Years ago, I tried to top everybody, but I don't anymore. I realized it was killing conversation. When you're always trying for a topper you aren't really listening. It ruins communication.
       <ul>
        <li>
         As quoted in
         <i>
          What Color is Your Paradigm: Thinking for Shaping Life and Results
         </i>
         (2003) by Howard Edson, p. 184
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Die, my dear? Why that's the last thing I'll do!
       <ul>
        <li>
         Last words
         <sup title="Needs citation">
          [
          <a href="/wiki/Wikiquote:Citing_sources" title="Wikiquote:Citing sources">
           <i>
            citationneeded
           </i>
          </a>
          ]
         </sup>
        </li>
        <li>
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Temple,_3rd_Viscount_Palmerston" title="w:Henry Temple, 3rd Viscount Palmerston">
          Lord Palmerston
         </a>
         had similar last words in 1865: "Die, my dear doctor! That's the last thing I shall do!"
         <sup title="Needs citation">
          [
          <a href="/wiki/Wikiquote:Citing_sources" title="Wikiquote:Citing sources">
           <i>
            citationneeded
           </i>
          </a>
          ]
         </sup>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>

       <li>
        I've been around so long, I knew
        <a href="/wiki/Doris_Day" title="Doris Day">
         Doris Day
        </a>
        before she was a virgin.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Apparently said by
          <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Levant" title="Oscar Levant">
           Oscar Levant
          </a>
          : "I knew Doris Day before she was a virgin" (as quoted in
          <i>
           The Wit and Wisdom of Hollywood
          </i>
          (1972) by Max Wilk).
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Time flies like an arrow. Fruit flies like a banana.
        <ul>
         <li>
          No known citation to Marx. First appears unattributed in mid-1960s logic/computing texts as an example of the difficulty of machine parsing of ambiguous statements.
          <a class="external text" href="http://books.google.co.uk/books?client=firefox-a&amp;lr=&amp;as_brr=0&amp;q=%22fruit-flies%22+%22time+flies%22+banana&amp;btnG=Search+Books&amp;as_drrb_is=b&amp;as_minm_is=0&amp;as_miny_is=1900&amp;as_maxm_is=0&amp;as_maxy_is=1970" rel="nofollow">
           Google Books
          </a>
          . The
          <i>
           Yale Book of Quotations
          </i>
          dates the attribution to Marx to a 9 July 1982 net.jokes post on
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usenet" title="w:Usenet">
           Usenet
          </a>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Outside of a dog, a book is man's best friend. Inside of a dog, it's too dark to read.
        <ul>
         <li>
          This may be original with Groucho, but the
          <a class="external text" href="http://quoteinvestigator.com/category/jim-brewer/" rel="nofollow">
           Quote Investigator
          </a>
          mentions the earliest report found in a 1958 issue of
          <i>
           Boy's Life
          </i>
          magazine where it is attributed to Jim Brewer.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Politics is the art of looking for trouble, finding it everywhere, diagnosing it incorrectly, and applying the wrong remedies.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Apparently attributed to Marx in
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bennett_Cerf" title="w:Bennett Cerf">
           Bennett Cerf
          </a>
          's
          <i>
           <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Try_and_Stop_Me_(book)" title="w:Try and Stop Me (book)">
            Try and Stop Me
           </a>
          </i>
          , first published in 1944. A citation of this can been seen in the
          <a class="external text" href="http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=X-orAAAAIBAJ&amp;sjid=ZWcFAAAAIBAJ&amp;pg=4581,3323702&amp;dq=art-of-looking-for-trouble&amp;hl=en" rel="nofollow">
           <i>
            Kentucky New Era
           </i>
           on November 9, 1964
          </a>
          . Also attributed to Marx by
          <a href="/wiki/Rand_Paul" title="Rand Paul">
           Rand Paul
          </a>
          in "The Long Stand," ch. 1 of
          <i>
           Taking a Stand: Moving Beyond Partisan Politics to Unite America
          </i>
          (New York, N.Y.: Center Street, 26 May 2015), p. 5.
         </li>
         <li>
          The original quotation ("Politics is the art of looking for trouble, finding it
          <i>
           whether it exists or not
          </i>
          , diagnosing it incorrectly and applying the wrong
          <i>
           remedy
          </i>
          .") belongs to
          <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Benn" title="w:Ernest Benn">
           Sir Ernest Benn
          </a>
          ; a first known citation reportedly appears in the
          <i>
           Springfield (MA) Republican
          </i>
          on July 27, 1930.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes_about_Marx">
       Quotes about Marx
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Groucho_Marx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quotes about Marx">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Some years back, after a childhood of preoccupation with comedy that led me to observing the styles of all the great comedians, I came to the conclusion that Groucho Marx was the best comedian this country ever produced. Now I am more convinced than ever that I was right. I can't think of a comedian who combined a totally original physical conception that was hilarious with a matchless verbal delivery. I believe there is a natural inborn greatness in Groucho that defies close analysis as it does with any genuine artist. He is simply unique in the same way that
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">
        Picasso
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">
        Stravinsky
       </a>
       are, and I believe his outrageous unsentimental disregard for order will be equally as funny a thousand years from now.
       <br/>
       In addition to all this, he makes me laugh.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">
          Woody Allen
         </a>
         on the liner notes of
         <i>
          An Evening With Groucho
         </i>
         (1972)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Groucho appeals on so many levels at once that you could go nuts trying to figure out whether it's the funny movement, the incomparable tone of voice, what he is saying, or that keenly witty face that hits you the hardest. I swear that if he never existed, we would sense a lack in the world of comedy, like that planet in the solar system that astronomers say Ought to be there.
       <br/>
       For me he is The Master.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cavett" title="Dick Cavett">
          Dick Cavett
         </a>
         on the liner notes of
         <i>
          An Evening With Groucho
         </i>
         (1972)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Groucho Marx is the only actor I ever allowed to ad-lib in a show I wrote. That was because I couldn't stop him.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_S._Kaufman" title="w:George S. Kaufman">
          George S. Kaufman
         </a>
         on the liner notes of
         <i>
          An Evening With Groucho
         </i>
         (1972)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       George Kaufman and I wrote a lot of funny lines for Groucho, some of which he occasionally used... but it was Groucho who by his innate sense of timing and his inimitable delivery, added the ingredient that brought the house down.
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morrie_Ryskind" title="w:Morrie Ryskind">
          Morrie Ryskind
         </a>
         on the liner notes of
         <i>
          An Evening With Groucho
         </i>
         (1972)
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
