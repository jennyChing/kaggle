             <h1>
              Clostridium difficile Infections Are More Common In States With Fewer Hospitals Per Person
             </h1>
             <div class="field-subtitle">
              <p>
               If they don't improve protocols, big hospitals could be putting thousands of lives at risk
              </p>
             </div>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">
              <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
              <span class="author">
               <span class="label">

            <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/clostridium_difficile_01.png?itok=Kr4zG1hg" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/clostridium_difficile_01.png?itok=7XIUKek4" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/clostridium_difficile_01.png?itok=zIrwldII" height="668" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="401" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/clostridium_difficile_01.png?itok=7XIUKek4" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Clostridium difficile colonies after 48 hours' growth
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clostridium_difficile_colitis#/media/File:Clostridium_difficile_01.png">
                 Credit: CDC via Wikimedia Commons
                </a>
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Every day, one in 25 hospital patients gets an infection just because they are in a hospital. One particularly nasty kind of bacteria is called
              <em>
               Clostridium difficile
              </em>
              , or
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              . In some cases, it causes nothing more than a bad case of diarrhea. But for some, it can be deadly; 20 percent of the deaths caused by hospital infections can be directly traced to
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              According to a report from the Centers of Disease Control and Prevention, the number of bacterial infections across the country decreased between 2011 and 2013, thanks to hospitals efforts to control
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              and other bacteria. California, Maryland, Rhode Island and Nevada are the
              <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/hai/pdfs/state-progress-landscape.pdf">
               only states
              </a>
              where the infection rate was higher than the national average. These four states have some of the
              <a href="http://www.statemaster.com/graph/hea_tot_hos_percap-health-total-hospitals-per-capita">
               lowest ratios
              </a>
              of hospitals to people, which means they are putting more people into fewer hospitals. An outbreak of a particularly strong strain of
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              or lax hygiene regulations at even just a few of these hospitals could endanger thousands of lives.
             </p>
             <p>
              Hospital patients are particularly vulnerable to infections; they often have weakened immune systems, and invasive procedures open the body up to microbes that can cause disease. Healthcare workers may transfer microbes between patients if they are not extremely diligent. So to combat the spread of disease, hospitals have adopted policies that enforce diligent hygiene habits for workers and tools alikeand for the most part, they've worked.
             </p>
             <p>
              But
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              is different because it is so opportunisticand stubborn, says Melissa Brower, a spokesperson for the CDC. When a patient takes an antibiotic, the pill wipes out much of the bacteria in his intestines. That leaves space for
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              to move in. Healthcare workers are around all this stuff most of the time and it doesnt bother them, because they are not taking antibiotics, Brower says. Alcohol-based antibiotic hand sanitizers kill most bacteria, but
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              is so hardy that
              <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/HAI/pdfs/cdiff/Cdiff_tagged.pdf">
               only soap-and-water hand washing
              </a>
              will do.
             </p>
             <p>
              The bacteria spread through spores, and are constantly evolving into stronger strains, some of which are already resistant to antibiotics. Thats probably why these states are having trouble, according to the California Department of Public Health. Morbidity and mortality due to
              <em>
               C. difficile
              </em>
              infection have increased over the past several years due to the emergence of more infectious and more virulent
              <em>
               C. difficile
              </em>
              strains, spokesperson Corey Egel wrote in an email. If even a small step in the hospital's hygiene protocol isn't performed by all members of the hospital staff, the bacteria could persist there
              <a href="http://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/c-difficile/basics/causes/con-20029664">
               for years
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              California is investigating why its infection rates are so high, and plans to implement new hygiene strategies to try to bring the numbers down. Emerging technologies may help these hospitals cut down on bacterial transmission by designing
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/slippery-coating-help-bacteria-slide-medical-materials">
               easy-clean medical tools
              </a>
              or more effective
              <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/handwashing/show-me-the-science-hand-sanitizer.html">
               hand sanitizers
              </a>
              . But these technologies aren't ready for immediate use, so for now hospitals with high infection rates just have to pay more attention to hygiene protocols. Hopefully with more concerted efforts, healthcare professionals can keep
              <em>
               C. diff
              </em>
              at bay--and keep more people alive.
             </p>
            </div>
