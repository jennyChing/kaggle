			&lt;h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"&gt;Japanese verb conjugation&lt;/h1&gt;
									&lt;div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"&gt;
									&lt;div id="siteSub"&gt;From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia&lt;/div&gt;
								&lt;div id="contentSub"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-redirectedfrom"&gt;(Redirected from &lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugations_and_adjective_declensions&amp;redirect=no" title="Japanese verb conjugations and adjective declensions"&gt;Japanese verb conjugations and adjective declensions&lt;/a&gt;)&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/div&gt;
												&lt;div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump"&gt;
					Jump to:					&lt;a href="#mw-head"&gt;navigation&lt;/a&gt;, 					&lt;a href="#p-search"&gt;search&lt;/a&gt;
				&lt;/div&gt;
				&lt;div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"&gt;&lt;p&gt;This is a list of &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language"&gt;Japanese&lt;/a&gt; &lt;a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb"&gt;verb&lt;/a&gt; &lt;a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation"&gt;conjugations&lt;/a&gt;. Almost all of these are regular, but there are a few &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_irregular_verbs" title="Japanese irregular verbs"&gt;Japanese irregular verbs&lt;/a&gt;, and the conjugations of the very few &lt;a href="/wiki/Irregular_verb" title="Irregular verb" class="mw-redirect"&gt;irregular verbs&lt;/a&gt; are also listed. &lt;b&gt;Japanese verb conjugation&lt;/b&gt; is the same for all subjects, first &lt;a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person"&gt;person&lt;/a&gt; ("I", "we"), second &lt;a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person"&gt;person&lt;/a&gt; ("you") and third &lt;a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person"&gt;person&lt;/a&gt; ("he/she/it" and "they"), singular and plural. The plain form of all verbs ends in &lt;i&gt;u&lt;/i&gt;. In modern Japanese, there are no verbs, at least in the plain form, ending in &lt;i&gt;fu&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;pu&lt;/i&gt;, or &lt;i&gt;yu&lt;/i&gt;, no verbs ending in &lt;i&gt;zu&lt;/i&gt; other than certain  forms (such as  &lt;i&gt;kin-zu&lt;/i&gt;), and &lt;a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E6%AD%BB%E3%81%AC#Japanese" class="extiw" title="wikt:en:"&gt;&lt;/a&gt; (&lt;a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:%E3%81%97%E3%81%AC#Japanese" class="extiw" title="wikt:en:"&gt;&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;i&gt;&lt;a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:shinu#Japanese" class="extiw" title="wikt:en:shinu"&gt;shinu&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/i&gt;; to die) is the only one ending in &lt;i&gt;nu&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;This article describes a set of conjugation rules widely used in order to teach Japanese as a foreign language. However, Japanese linguists have been proposing various grammatical theories for over a hundred years and there is still no consensus about the conjugations. Japanese people learn the more traditional "school grammar" in their schools, which explains the same grammatical phenomena in a different way with different terminology (see the corresponding Japanese article).&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_verb_conjugations"&gt;Summary of verb conjugations&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary of verb conjugations"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Verb conjugates are often grouped into consonant-stems &lt;span style="font-weight: normal"&gt;(&lt;span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none"&gt;,&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;godandoushi&lt;/i&gt;&lt;sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"&gt;&lt;a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"&gt;&lt;span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;"&gt;?&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;)&lt;/span&gt;, vowel-stems &lt;span style="font-weight: normal"&gt;(&lt;span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none"&gt;,&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;ichidandoushi&lt;/i&gt;&lt;sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"&gt;&lt;a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"&gt;&lt;span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;"&gt;?&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;, &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;iru&lt;/i&gt; and &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;eru&lt;/i&gt; forms)&lt;/span&gt;, and action verbs. The plain form of a type I verb has an &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;u&lt;/i&gt; sound (&lt;i&gt;&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;ts&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;r&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;k&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;g&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;b&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;m&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;s&lt;u&gt;u&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;), the &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;-masu&lt;/i&gt; form has an &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; sound (&lt;i&gt;&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;ch&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;r&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;k&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;g&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;b&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;m&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;sh&lt;u&gt;i&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;), and the negative form has an &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;a&lt;/i&gt; sound (&lt;i&gt;w&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;t&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;r&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;k&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;g&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;b&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;m&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;s&lt;u&gt;a&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;). The potential form has an &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;e&lt;/i&gt; sound (&lt;i&gt;&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;t&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;r&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;k&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;g&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;b&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;m&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;s&lt;u&gt;e&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;) and the volitional form has an &lt;span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;i&gt;&lt;/i&gt; sound (&lt;i&gt;&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;t&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;r&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;k&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;g&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;b&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;m&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;, &lt;i&gt;s&lt;u&gt;&lt;/u&gt;&lt;/i&gt;), so putting these together with the sounds above shows that verb conjugations follow the vowel syllabary of the Japanese character set: &lt;i&gt;a&lt;/i&gt;,  &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt;,  &lt;i&gt;u&lt;/i&gt;,  &lt;i&gt;e&lt;/i&gt; and  &lt;i&gt;o&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;li id="cite_note-13"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-cite-backlink"&gt;&lt;b&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_ref-13"&gt;^&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;span class="reference-text"&gt;With all verbs, the prohibitive form is simply obtained by adding - to the dictionary form. It is used to command someone not to do something. An example is  &lt;i&gt;hairu na&lt;/i&gt; "Do not enter."&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li id="cite_note-14"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-cite-backlink"&gt;&lt;b&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_ref-14"&gt;^&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/span&gt; &lt;span class="reference-text"&gt;The imperative form can be used as a command, e.g.  &lt;i&gt;damare&lt;/i&gt; "shut up!",  &lt;i&gt;yame&lt;/i&gt; "stop!" or  &lt;i&gt;tomare&lt;/i&gt; "Stop (sign)". Non-volitional verbs (e.g.  &lt;i&gt;aru&lt;/i&gt;,  &lt;i&gt;wakaru&lt;/i&gt;,  &lt;i&gt;dekiru&lt;/i&gt;) have no imperative form and  &lt;i&gt;kureru&lt;/i&gt; "to give" is an exception that conjugates to  &lt;i&gt;kure&lt;/i&gt; (the plain form of  &lt;i&gt;-te kudasai&lt;/i&gt; "Please (do)...").&lt;br /&gt;
A politer way of telling someone to do something is to use (&lt;i&gt;masu&lt;/i&gt; stem) &lt;i&gt;-nasai&lt;/i&gt; instead (e.g.  &lt;i&gt;nominasai&lt;/i&gt; "Drink up.",  &lt;i&gt;shinasai&lt;/i&gt; "Do (what was said)."), or more informally, (&lt;i&gt;masu&lt;/i&gt; stem) &lt;i&gt;-na&lt;/i&gt;. Imperative form:  &lt;i&gt;takusan tabena&lt;/i&gt; "Eat a lot." Prohibitive form:  &lt;i&gt;takusan taberu na&lt;/i&gt; "Don't pig out!"&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/li&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Imperfective"&gt;Imperfective&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Imperfective"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;In Japanese, the basic verb form is an &lt;a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect"&gt;imperfective aspect&lt;/a&gt;. It is broadly equivalent to the present and future tenses of English, and is sometimes called the "non-past tense". The imperfective form of a verb is the same as its dictionary formit is used as the &lt;a href="/wiki/Headword" title="Headword"&gt;headword&lt;/a&gt;, or &lt;a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)"&gt;lemma&lt;/a&gt;and no conjugation needs to be done. For example, using the verb  ("do"):&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;() &lt;i&gt;(watashi wa) kaimono suru&lt;/i&gt;: "(I) shop", or "(I) will shop". (&lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_pronouns" title="Japanese pronouns"&gt;Japanese pronouns&lt;/a&gt; are usually omitted when it is clear about whom the speaker is talking.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;() &lt;i&gt;(watashi wa) ashita benky suru&lt;/i&gt;: "Tomorrow, (I) will study".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;In most cases, the base form of the imperfective aspect cannot be used to make a progressive statement, such as in the English sentence "I am shopping". Rather, it can only be used to express habit or other actions that are expected to continue into the future, such as in "I shop". To convey the former, the &lt;i&gt;te&lt;/i&gt; form with &lt;i&gt;iru&lt;/i&gt; must be used.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Perfective"&gt;Perfective&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Perfective"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect"&gt;perfective aspect&lt;/a&gt;, on the other hand, has a specific suffix. The basic pattern is the &lt;i&gt;-ta&lt;/i&gt; (or &lt;i&gt;-da&lt;/i&gt;) ending, but various phonetic changes are made, depending on the verb's last syllable. These phonetic changes are known as  &lt;i&gt;onbin&lt;/i&gt; "&lt;a href="/wiki/Euphony" title="Euphony" class="mw-redirect"&gt;euphony&lt;/a&gt;", and the resulting form as  &lt;i&gt;onbinkei&lt;/i&gt; "euphonic form"  see &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_grammar#Euphonic_changes_.28.E9.9F.B3.E4.BE.BF_onbin.29" title="Japanese grammar"&gt;Euphonic changes ( onbin)&lt;/a&gt;. The perfective is broadly equivalent to English past tense, and is often called past tense in treatments of Japanese grammar, but it is not restricted to any single tense.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;li&gt;Non-exhaustive list of actions (like AB is used for non-exhaustive lists of objects):  &lt;i&gt;hon o yondari, terebi o mitari shita&lt;/i&gt; (I read a book, watched TV, etc.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Note that the perfective conjugation for verbs ending in - more commonly follows the v5u-s pattern listed above for speakers of &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_dialects#Western_Japanese" title="Japanese dialects"&gt;Western Japanese&lt;/a&gt;. The  in the perfective ending - may be pronounced either as an &lt;i&gt;u&lt;/i&gt; or as an &lt;i&gt;o&lt;/i&gt; depending on the preceding vowel, according to regular Japanese phonological rules. Consequently, in &lt;a href="/wiki/Kansai" title="Kansai" class="mw-redirect"&gt;Kansai&lt;/a&gt;, one may hear forms such as  &lt;i&gt;tsukau&lt;/i&gt;   &lt;i&gt;tsukta&lt;/i&gt;, or  &lt;i&gt;iu&lt;/i&gt;   &lt;i&gt;iuta&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"&gt;&lt;a href="#cite_note-17"&gt;&lt;span&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;4&lt;span&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/sup&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Usage of the perfective aspect follows the same pattern as the imperfective aspect. For example,  &lt;i&gt;nihon ni iku&lt;/i&gt; (I go to Japan) becomes  &lt;i&gt;nihon ni itta&lt;/i&gt; (I went to Japan).&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;The  &lt;i&gt;nai&lt;/i&gt; ending conjugates in two ways.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ol&gt;
&lt;li&gt;As an &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; adjective. For example the past tense of  &lt;i&gt;tabenai&lt;/i&gt; is  &lt;i&gt;tabenakatta&lt;/i&gt; and the &lt;i&gt;te&lt;/i&gt; form is  &lt;i&gt;tabenakute&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;There is a special te/naide form made by adding  &lt;i&gt;de&lt;/i&gt; which yields  &lt;i&gt;naide&lt;/i&gt;  this can be replaced with  &lt;i&gt;zu&lt;/i&gt; in formal usage.
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Requesting someone to cease/desist:  &lt;i&gt;tabenaide kudasai&lt;/i&gt; "Please don't eat (this)"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Joining a subordinate clause:  &lt;i&gt;tabenaide, neta&lt;/i&gt; "Without eating, I went to bed."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ol&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form has many uses, typically as a prefix. These include:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;form polite verbs&lt;/b&gt; when followed by the - &lt;i&gt;-masu&lt;/i&gt; ending:  &lt;i&gt;iku&lt;/i&gt;  &lt;i&gt;ikimasu&lt;/i&gt;,  &lt;i&gt;tsukau&lt;/i&gt;   &lt;i&gt;tsukaimasu&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;express a wish&lt;/b&gt; when followed by the ending - &lt;i&gt;-tai&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;tabetai&lt;/i&gt;: "I want to eat it",  &lt;i&gt;ikitai&lt;/i&gt;: "I want to go". (The &lt;i&gt;-tai&lt;/i&gt; ending conjugates as an &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_adjectives" title="Japanese adjectives" class="mw-redirect"&gt;- &lt;i&gt;-i&lt;/i&gt; adjective&lt;/a&gt;.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;express a strong negative intention&lt;/b&gt; when followed by - &lt;i&gt;-wa shinai&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;iki wa shinai yo, anna tokoro&lt;/i&gt; "no way I'm going someplace like that".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;form a command&lt;/b&gt; when followed by
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;i&gt;-nasai&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;kore o tabenasai&lt;/i&gt;: "eat this",  &lt;i&gt;asoko e ikinasai&lt;/i&gt;: "go over there".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;i&gt;-na&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;massugu kaerina&lt;/i&gt; "go straight home":  &lt;i&gt;nakayoku asobina&lt;/i&gt; "play nice". (Used with children, etc.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To express that something is &lt;b&gt;easy&lt;/b&gt; or &lt;b&gt;hard&lt;/b&gt; when followed by - &lt;i&gt;-yasui&lt;/i&gt; or - &lt;i&gt;-nikui&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;shitashimiyasui&lt;/i&gt;: "easy to befriend":  &lt;i&gt;wakarinikui&lt;/i&gt;: "hard to understand".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;express excessiveness&lt;/b&gt; when followed by the verb - &lt;i&gt;-sugiru&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;nomisugiru&lt;/i&gt;: "to drink too much". (&lt;i&gt;sugiru&lt;/i&gt; can also be used with the stems of &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_adjectives" title="Japanese adjectives" class="mw-redirect"&gt;adjectives&lt;/a&gt;.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;express doing something in conjunction with something else&lt;/b&gt;. When followed by the suffix - -&lt;i&gt;nagara&lt;/i&gt;, the verb becomes an adverb that means doing something while doing something else.
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;arukinagara hon o yonda&lt;/i&gt;: "I read a book as I walked."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;When followed by the verb - -&lt;i&gt;yagaru&lt;/i&gt; in &lt;a href="/wiki/Yakuza" title="Yakuza"&gt;yakuza&lt;/a&gt; speech, to &lt;b&gt;express affronted contempt&lt;/b&gt; (a conjugation of opposite polarity to the honorifics) showing disrespect in the form of hatred combined with haughty/macho disdain for the doer/subject of the action/verb:  &lt;i&gt;koroshiyagaru&lt;/i&gt;: "to have the &lt;expletive&gt; gall to kill ___" (e.g. without my permission). (The &lt;i&gt;te&lt;/i&gt; form can be substituted for the &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form also has some uses on its own, such as:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To &lt;b&gt;express purpose&lt;/b&gt;, with  &lt;i&gt;ni&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;tabe ni ikimashita&lt;/i&gt;: "I went there to eat". This is called the &lt;i&gt;infinitive of purpose&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In &lt;b&gt;formal &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_honorifics" title="Japanese honorifics"&gt;honorifics&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/b&gt; such as  &lt;i&gt;o tsukai kudasai&lt;/i&gt;: "Please use this".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In &lt;b&gt;conjunctions&lt;/b&gt; in formal writing.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;For some verbs, the &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form also forms part of related words in ways that are not governed by any general rules. For example:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form of  &lt;i&gt;taberu&lt;/i&gt; (to eat) can prefix  &lt;i&gt;mono&lt;/i&gt; to form  &lt;i&gt;tabemono&lt;/i&gt; (food). Similarly with  &lt;i&gt;nomu&lt;/i&gt; (to drink) and  &lt;i&gt;kau&lt;/i&gt; (to buy).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form of  &lt;i&gt;kakeru&lt;/i&gt; (to bet) is a word on its own:  &lt;i&gt;kake&lt;/i&gt;, which means "a bet".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;hanasu&lt;/i&gt; (to separate) can be suffixed to the &lt;i&gt;i&lt;/i&gt; form of &lt;i&gt;kiru&lt;/i&gt; (to cut) to form  &lt;i&gt;kirihanasu&lt;/i&gt; (to cut off).&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="te_form"&gt;&lt;i&gt;te&lt;/i&gt; form&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: te form"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;i&gt;te&lt;/i&gt; form of a Japanese verb (sometimes called the "participle") is used when the verb has some kind of connection to the following words. This originally came from the combination of the "i" form described above plus the particle "te". For all verbs, it is formed by changing the -&lt;i&gt;a&lt;/i&gt; of the perfective aspect form to -&lt;i&gt;e&lt;/i&gt;. Adjectives behave slightly differently.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;As a simple command:
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;Tabete&lt;/i&gt;: "Eat."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;Yonde&lt;/i&gt;: "Read."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In &lt;a href="/wiki/Honorific_speech_in_Japanese#Requests" title="Honorific speech in Japanese"&gt;requests&lt;/a&gt; with  &lt;i&gt;kureru&lt;/i&gt; and  &lt;i&gt;kudasai&lt;/i&gt;.
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;: "Please read the book."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;With the verbs&#160;:
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;iru&lt;/b&gt;: Forms a &lt;a href="/wiki/Progressive_tense#Japanese" title="Progressive tense" class="mw-redirect"&gt;progressive or continuous tense&lt;/a&gt;. For example:  &lt;i&gt;matte iru&lt;/i&gt;: "I am waiting",  &lt;i&gt;shitte iru&lt;/i&gt;: "I know",  &lt;i&gt;motte iru&lt;/i&gt;: "I have",  &lt;i&gt;koko ni sunde iru&lt;/i&gt;: "I live here". Colloquially, in this form, the "i" often disappears (also in the past tense), so  &lt;i&gt;matte iru&lt;/i&gt; becomes  &lt;i&gt;matteru&lt;/i&gt; and  &lt;i&gt;shitte iru&lt;/i&gt; becomes  &lt;i&gt;shitteru&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;oku&lt;/b&gt;: To indicate a preparatory action in advance.  &lt;i&gt;obent o tsukutte oita&lt;/i&gt;: "I have made a boxed lunch (for later)". Colloquially, in this form, the "e" often disappears, so  &lt;i&gt;tsukutte oita&lt;/i&gt; becomes  &lt;i&gt;tsukuttoita&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;aru&lt;/b&gt;: This forms a kind of passive when used with a transitive verb.  &lt;i&gt;koko ni moji ga kaite aru&lt;/i&gt;: "There are some characters written here". It shows that something was left in a certain state. Contrast to  "kaite iru", "I am writing", which applies to the person doing the writing rather than what is written.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;shimau&lt;/b&gt;: This implies something is completed or done, usually unintentionally or accidentally or unexpectedly and sometimes expressing that the action is contrary to right or correct action:  &lt;i&gt;katazukete shimatta&lt;/i&gt;: "I have finished tidying". It can also suggest a regrettable situation:  &lt;i&gt;watashi no kagi ga kiete shimatta&lt;/i&gt;: "My keys have disappeared".
&lt;dl&gt;
&lt;dd&gt;The form - &lt;i&gt;-te shimau&lt;/i&gt; is shortened to the very commonly used and casual - &lt;i&gt;-chimau&lt;/i&gt; or - &lt;i&gt;-chau&lt;/i&gt; with the same consonant doubling as the te form. For example, "I forgot my mobile phone!": "keitai wasurechatta!" "" The &lt;i&gt;-de shimau&lt;/i&gt; form is shortened to - &lt;i&gt;-jau&lt;/i&gt; or - &lt;i&gt;-jimau&lt;/i&gt; in colloquial speech.&lt;/dd&gt;
&lt;/dl&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;oru&lt;/b&gt;: Can express a continuing situation. It is the humble form of  &lt;i&gt;iru&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;miru&lt;/b&gt;: It means "to try doing".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;iku&lt;/b&gt;: Can express continuous action or a change of state in the future.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;b&gt;kuru&lt;/b&gt;: Can express continuous action or a change of state in the past.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To combine clauses or adjectives, as if by the English conjunction "and". For example:
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;yakkyoku e itte, kusuri o kau&lt;/i&gt;: "(I am going to) go to the pharmacy and buy medicine."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;ano hito wa shinsetsu de, atama ga yokute, wakariyasui&lt;/i&gt;: "That person is kind, smart, and easy to understand."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt; &lt;i&gt;yasukute ii ne&lt;/i&gt;: "It's good that it's cheap." (lit. "Being cheap, it is good.")&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;With particles in formations such as
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te wa ikenai&lt;/b&gt;: "You must not ...". For example,  &lt;i&gt;tabete wa ikenai&lt;/i&gt;: "You must not eat this". (Other words of prohibition, such as  &lt;b&gt;dame&lt;/b&gt;, can be substituted for &lt;b&gt;ikenai&lt;/b&gt;.)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te mo ii&lt;/b&gt;: "You may do/It's ok if you do". For example,  &lt;i&gt;tabete mo ii&lt;/i&gt;: "You may eat it".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te mo kamawanai&lt;/b&gt;: "You may do/I don't mind if you do"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te hoshii&lt;/b&gt;: "I want you to do (for me)"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te sumimasen&lt;/b&gt;: "Sorry for making you go through the trouble of"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;-&lt;b&gt;-te kurete arigatou&lt;/b&gt;: "Thank you for". For example,  &lt;i&gt;mite kurete arigatou&lt;/i&gt;: "Thank you for watching".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te kureru&lt;/b&gt;: Used when somebody does you a favor.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;- &lt;b&gt;-te ageru&lt;/b&gt;: Used when you do someone a favor.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The potential is used to express that one has the &lt;b&gt;ability&lt;/b&gt; to do something. Direct objects are marked with the particle  &lt;i&gt;ga&lt;/i&gt; instead of  &lt;i&gt;o&lt;/i&gt;. For example  &lt;i&gt;nihongo ga yomeru&lt;/i&gt;: "I can read Japanese".&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;It is also used to request some action from someone, in the exact sense of the English "Can you ...&#160;?" For example  &lt;i&gt;kh kaeru?&lt;/i&gt;: "Can (you) buy (some) coffee?" However, sometimes in English "Will you...?" and "Can you ...&#160;?" is used interchangeably to make requests. Though it is possible in Japanese,  &lt;i&gt;kh kau?&lt;/i&gt;, it is very casual and might also mean simply "Are you buying/Will you buy coffee?" in a very dry factual sense.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Unlike in English, the potential is not often used to express permission (as in the sentence "Can I eat this apple?") as it is almost always understood to mean "Do I have the ability to eat this apple?":  &lt;i&gt;kono ringo ga taberareru?&lt;/i&gt;. And since the &lt;i&gt;-reru&lt;/i&gt; form is more often used in speech than the more correct passive potential form &lt;i&gt;-rareru&lt;/i&gt;, and subjects are often implied in Japanese, it may implicitly be asking (in this case) if the apple is edible. So, to seek permission, a more polite form is used, such as the - &lt;b&gt;-te mo ii&lt;/b&gt; or more casual - "-te ii"" usage of the - &lt;i&gt;-te&lt;/i&gt; form, resulting in something literally more like "Is eating this apple OK?"  &lt;i&gt;Kono ringo o tabete mo ii desu ka?&lt;/i&gt; or  &lt;i&gt;Kono ringo o tabete ii?&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The potential &lt;i&gt;-ru&lt;/i&gt; ending conjugates as a vowel stem verb.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Consonants and vowels conjugate differently, see &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_consonant_and_vowel_verbs#Conjugation" title="Japanese consonant and vowel verbs"&gt;consonant and vowel conjugation&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;There is no potential equivalent for ; other constructions for expressing may-be situations are used:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Using  expression. For verbs:  &lt;i&gt;ashita furu kamo shirenai&lt;/i&gt; "It may rain tomorrow.", i-adj:  &lt;i&gt;ryokou wa takai kamo shirenai&lt;/i&gt; "The journey is perhaps expensive.", na-adj:  &lt;i&gt;taisetsu kamo shirenai&lt;/i&gt; "(This thing is) probably important."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Using adverbs.  &lt;i&gt;osoraku furu&lt;/i&gt; "It probably will rain",  &lt;i&gt;tabun furu&lt;/i&gt; "Perhaps it will rain"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;(A rather strange and archaic-literary-sounding possibility is by transforming  to  and then constructing the potential, .)&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;

&lt;li&gt;as a form of &lt;b&gt;&lt;a href="/wiki/Honorific_speech_in_Japanese" title="Honorific speech in Japanese"&gt;respectful language&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/b&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;dochira e ikaremasu ka&lt;/i&gt;: "Where are you going?"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;

&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The &lt;i&gt;-ru&lt;/i&gt; ending of the causative form becomes the new verb ending. This conjugates as a &lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_consonant_and_vowel_verbs" title="Japanese consonant and vowel verbs"&gt;vowel stem&lt;/a&gt; verb.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Negatives are not normally made into causatives. Instead, a negative ending is added to the causative of the verb. Thus, for example, &lt;i&gt;Tabesasenai&lt;/i&gt;: "Do not let eat".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;&lt;a href="/wiki/Japanese_adjectives" title="Japanese adjectives" class="mw-redirect"&gt;Adjectives&lt;/a&gt; are made causative by using the adverb form plus &lt;i&gt;saseru&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;A shortened causative form exists where the final &lt;i&gt;-u&lt;/i&gt; becoming &lt;i&gt;-asu&lt;/i&gt; for consonant stem verbs, and &lt;i&gt;-ru&lt;/i&gt; becoming &lt;i&gt;-sasu&lt;/i&gt; for vowel stem verbs.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The causative is used for:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;&lt;b&gt;Making&lt;/b&gt; someone do something:  &lt;i&gt;shukudai o saseru&lt;/i&gt;: "(I) make (him) do homework".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;&lt;b&gt;Letting&lt;/b&gt; someone do something:  &lt;i&gt;soto de asobaseru&lt;/i&gt;: "(I) let (him) play outside".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;With explicit actors:  &lt;i&gt;sensei ga kodomo ni benky o saseta&lt;/i&gt;: "The teacher made the children study."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;The &lt;a href="/wiki/Honorific_speech_in_Japanese" title="Honorific speech in Japanese"&gt;honorific&lt;/a&gt; forms  &lt;i&gt;sasete morau&lt;/i&gt; or  &lt;i&gt;sasete itadaku&lt;/i&gt; using the verbs  &lt;i&gt;morau&lt;/i&gt; or its humble equivalent  &lt;i&gt;itadaku&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Causative_passive"&gt;Causative passive&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Causative passive"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The causative passive form is obtained by first conjugating in the causative form and then conjugating the result in the passive form.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;As its rule suggests, the causative passive is used to express causation passively:  &lt;i&gt;ryshin ni benky saserareru&lt;/i&gt;: "(I) am made to study by (my) parents".&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Because words such as  &lt;i&gt;mataserareru&lt;/i&gt; are considered to be difficult to pronounce, frequently in colloquial speech, the middle part of the causative passive would contract. That is,  &lt;i&gt;mataserareru&lt;/i&gt; (I was made to wait), would become  &lt;i&gt;matasareru&lt;/i&gt;. Another example such as "(I) was made to buy (something)" would formally be  &lt;i&gt;kawaserareta&lt;/i&gt; from the verb  &lt;i&gt;kau&lt;/i&gt;, but colloquially, it is frequently contracted to  &lt;i&gt;kawasareta&lt;/i&gt;. This abbreviation is not used for vowel-stem verbs, nor for the irregular  &lt;i&gt;suru&lt;/i&gt; and  &lt;i&gt;kuru&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Provisional_Conditional_eba_form"&gt;Provisional Conditional &lt;i&gt;eba&lt;/i&gt; form&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Provisional Conditional eba form"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;i&gt;eba&lt;/i&gt; provisional conditional form is characterized by the final &lt;i&gt;-u&lt;/i&gt; becoming &lt;i&gt;-eba&lt;/i&gt; for all verbs (with the semi-exception of &lt;i&gt;-tsu&lt;/i&gt; verbs becoming &lt;i&gt;-teba&lt;/i&gt;).&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;li&gt;The  &lt;i&gt;nakereba&lt;/i&gt; form used for the negative form can be colloquially contracted to  &lt;i&gt;nakya&lt;/i&gt; or  &lt;i&gt;nakucha&lt;/i&gt; (this has roots in ). Thus  &lt;i&gt;ikanakereba&lt;/i&gt; can become  &lt;i&gt;ikanakya&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;i&gt;-eba&lt;/i&gt; provisional conditional form is used:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In &lt;b&gt;conditionals&lt;/b&gt; where the emphasis rests more on the condition than the result. For example:  &lt;i&gt;nani o sureba ii ka&lt;/i&gt; - "What should I do?" (lit. "It would be good if I did what?")&#160;;  &lt;i&gt;wakareba ii&lt;/i&gt; - "As long as you understand" (lit. "If you understand, it is good.")&#160;;  &lt;i&gt;jikan ga areba, kaimono wo shiy&lt;/i&gt; - "If there's time, let's go shopping."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;Expressing &lt;b&gt;obligations&lt;/b&gt;: The expression  &lt;i&gt;nakereba naranai&lt;/i&gt; (or in a more formal manner  &lt;i&gt;nakereba narimasen&lt;/i&gt;), where  &lt;i&gt;naru&lt;/i&gt; is the verb to become, means literally if you dont, its no good or in other words you must or you have to. The negative dont have to  is expressed with  - nakute mo ii. For example:  &lt;i&gt;Jiko shoukai wa Nihongo de nakereba narimasen&lt;/i&gt; - Your self-introduction must be in Japanese.&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The &lt;i&gt;nakereba&lt;/i&gt; negative conditional form means "if not X" or also "unless X". It is obtained by replacing the final &lt;i&gt;-i&lt;/i&gt; of the plain negative form with &lt;i&gt;-kereba&lt;/i&gt;. (&lt;i&gt;tabenakereba&lt;/i&gt;: "if I don't eat" or "unless I eat")&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The conditional is also called the "provisional form" in some grammars, because the implied condition is "provided that X happens" (&lt;i&gt;mireba shiru&lt;/i&gt;: "provided that you see, you'll know" = "if you see, you'll know").&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;&lt;span class="mw-headline" id="Conditional_ra_form"&gt;Conditional &lt;i&gt;ra&lt;/i&gt; form&lt;/span&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection"&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;[&lt;/span&gt;&lt;a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_verb_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Conditional ra form"&gt;edit&lt;/a&gt;&lt;span class="mw-editsection-bracket"&gt;]&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The conditional &lt;i&gt;ra&lt;/i&gt; form (also called the &lt;i&gt;past&lt;/i&gt; conditional) is formed from the &lt;a href="#Perfective"&gt;past tense&lt;/a&gt; (TA form) by simply adding &lt;i&gt;ra&lt;/i&gt;. &lt;i&gt;ba&lt;/i&gt; can be further added to that, which makes it more formal.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The conditional &lt;i&gt;ra&lt;/i&gt; form can be used in the same way as the provisional &lt;i&gt;eba&lt;/i&gt; form. However, it implies more certainty about the condition, and therefore places more emphasis on the result than the condition. It can be used to mean more like "if and when", and is typically preferred over the &lt;i&gt;eba&lt;/i&gt; form when this meaning is more accurate. For example:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;&lt;i&gt;nihon ni ittara, kamera wo kaitai&lt;/i&gt;: "If (when) I go to Japan, then (when that has happened) I want to buy a camera."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The conditional &lt;i&gt;ra&lt;/i&gt; form can also be used when the main clause is in the past tense. In such situations, it means "when", and carries the additional implication that the result was unexpected. For example:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;&lt;i&gt;kissaten ni ittara, Suzuki-san ni deatta&lt;/i&gt;: "When I went to the cafe, I came across Suzuki.".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The imperative form is used&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;in &lt;i&gt;orders&lt;/i&gt;, such as in the military, or to inferiors, or to very intimate friends or family depending on the nature of the relationship (e.g., among very close male friends), or in textbook exercises&#160; highly risky for use by learners until cultural nuances have been well and truly mastered&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;in &lt;a href="/wiki/Set_phrase" title="Set phrase"&gt;set phrases&lt;/a&gt; such as &lt;i&gt;nani shiro&lt;/i&gt;: "no matter what".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;in &lt;i&gt;reported speech&lt;/i&gt;, where a polite request may be reported using a plain imperative: &lt;i&gt;kashite kudasai&lt;/i&gt; (direct) &lt;i&gt;kase to iwareta&lt;/i&gt; (he told me to lend it to him).&lt;/li&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;b&gt;Usage&lt;/b&gt;&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;In general, the volitional form expresses intention, such as in these cases:&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;ul&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In &lt;b&gt;volitional&lt;/b&gt; ("let's" or "I shall") statements:  &lt;i&gt;benky shiy&lt;/i&gt;: "Let's study" or "I shall study".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To ask &lt;b&gt;volitional&lt;/b&gt; ("shall we") questions:  &lt;i&gt;ik ka&lt;/i&gt;: "Shall (we) go?"&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To express a conjecture with &lt;i&gt;desh&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;ashita hareru desh&lt;/i&gt;: "Tomorrow will probably be sunny."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;To express what one is thinking of doing, via  &lt;i&gt;omou&lt;/i&gt;:  &lt;i&gt;ka to omou&lt;/i&gt;: "(I) am thinking of buying (it)".&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;li&gt;In the form  &lt;i&gt;shiy to suru&lt;/i&gt;: &lt;b&gt;be about to&lt;/b&gt; or &lt;b&gt;be trying to&lt;/b&gt;.  &lt;i&gt;Inu ga hoey to shite iru&lt;/i&gt;: "The dog is about to bark."&lt;/li&gt;
&lt;/ul&gt;
