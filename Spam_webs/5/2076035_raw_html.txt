    <h1>
     <i>
      Talos
     </i>
    </h1>
    <img border="" src="../../images/species/t/talos-size.jpg"/>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Name:
     </b>
     <i>
      Talos
     </i>
     (After a giant bronze automaton from Greek Mythology).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Phonetic:
     </b>
     Tay-los.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Named By:
     </b>
     Lindsay E. Zanno, David J.
Varricchio, Patrick M. O'Connor, Alan L. Titus &amp;
Michael J. Knell - 2011.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Classification:
     </b>
     Chordata, Reptilia, Dinosauria,
Saurischia, Theropoda, Troodontidae.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Species:
     </b>
     <i>
      T. sampsoni
     </i>
     (type).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Diet:
     </b>
     Carnivore.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Size:
     </b>
     Roughly estimated at about 2 meters long.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Known locations:
     </b>
     USA, Utah - Kaiparowits
Formation.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Time period:
     </b>
     Campanian of the Cretaceous.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Fossil representation:
     </b>
     Partial post cranial remains
including rear limbs, partial pelvis, vertebrae and a forelimb bone.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     <i>
      Talos
     </i>
     is so far only known from a partial post cranial skeleton that is
mostly represented by the rear limbs. However when these remains are
scaled to the same proportions as other more complete troodontid
genera, a rough estimate of around two meters in length can be
attained for the animal. Like other
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/troodonts.html">
      troodonts
     </a>
     and earlier
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/dromaeosaurs.html">
      dromaeosaurs
     </a>
     ,
     <i>
      Talos
     </i>
     had an enlarged sickle shaped
killing claw on
each foot.
     <br/>
     <i>
      Talos
     </i>
     is the first genus of troodontid dinosaur recovered from the
Kaiparowits Formation of Utah. Other theropods known from this
formation at this time include
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/oviraptorosaurs.html">
      oviraptosaurs
     </a>
     and probable
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/ornithomimids-ornithomimosaurs.html">
      ornithomimosaurs
     </a>
     ,
though the principal predatory threat would have
still belonged to
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/articles/tyrannosaurs.html">
      tyrannosaurs
     </a>
     ,
with the mid-sized genus
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/t/teratophoneus.html">
      <i>
       Teratophoneus
      </i>
     </a>
     also known, as well as isolated but not so well
preserved remains of even larger tyrannosaurs.
     <br/>
     A
little bit further South in the Campanian aged Wahweap Formation,
another tyrannosaur named
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/l/lythronax.html">
      <i>
       Lythronax
      </i>
     </a>
     was roaming around at the same
time as
     <i>
      Talos
     </i>
     , and its not wholly
inconceivable that the two may
have crossed paths since living animals would not have been confined to
current fossil locations.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Further reading
     </b>
     <br/>
     - A new troodontid theropod,
     <i>
      Talos sampsoni
     </i>
     gen. et sp. nov.,
from the Upper Cretaceous Western Interior Basin of North America.
- PLoS ONE 9 (6): e24487 - Lindsay E. Zanno, David
J. Varricchio, Patrick M. O'Connor, Alan L. Titus &amp;
Michael J. Knell - 2011.
    </p>
