         <h1 class="title entry-title">
          The Domhnach Airgid, an Early Irish Book Shrine
         </h1>
        </header>
        <div class="post-meta">
         <span class="small">
          By
         </span>
         <span class="author vcard">
          <span class="fn">
           <a href="http://irisharchaeology.ie/author/admin/" rel="author" title="Posts by Colm">
            Colm
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="small">
          on
         </span>
         <abbr class="date time published updated" title="2015-02-20T18:16:05+0100">
          February 20, 2015
         </abbr>
         <span class="small">
          in
         </span>
         <span class="categories">
          <a href="http://irisharchaeology.ie/category/archaeology-blogs/" title="View all items in Archaeology blogs">
           Archaeology blogs
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://irisharchaeology.ie/category/irish-treasures/" title="View all items in Irish treasures">
           Irish treasures
          </a>
         </span>
        </div>
        <section class="entry">
         <p>
          <a href="http://irisharchaeology.ie/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Domhnach-airgid.jpg?5a7560">
           <img alt="Domhnach airgid" class="aligncenter wp-image-6942" height="445" src="http://irisharchaeology.ie/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Domhnach-airgid.jpg?5a7560" width="580"/>
          </a>
          The
          <em>
           Domhnach Airgid
          </em>
          (silver church) is an ornate shrine, fashioned out of silver and bronze that was intended to hold a sacred text. When opened in the 19th century it was found to contain an ancient copy of the gospels and according to tradition this book was a gift from Saint Patrick to his disciple MacCartan. An important early church figure, Saint MacCartan founded Clogher church in Co. Tyrone and was also associated with Clones in Co. Monaghan.
         </p>
         <p>
          The shrine consists of a simple wooden box, encased in bronze panels, the earliest of which date from the 8th century AD. The reliquary was substantially remodelled in the 14th century by John OBardan, a craftsman working at the behest of John OCarbri, abbot of Clones.Most of the decorative panels visible today are associated with this later embellishment. These gilt silver pieces depict the apostles, the Virgin and Child and a number of Irish saints, including Patrick, MacCartan, Columba and Brigit (see images below).
         </p>
         <p>
          A large crucifix, which dominates the front of the
          <em>
           Domhnach Airgid
          </em>
          , is noticeably worn, possibly from devotional rubbing/touching. Above the crucifix is a rock crystal and this originally held fragments of wood, thought to be pieces of the True Cross.
         </p>
         <p>
          Today the
          <em>
           Domhnach Airgid
          </em>
          can be viewed at the National Museum of Ireland, Kildare Street, Dublin.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://irisharchaeology.ie/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/DSC_0110-002.jpg?5a7560">

          </a>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <strong>
           St. Patrick presenting St. MacCartan with the Gospel Book.
          </strong>
         </p>

           <img alt="DSC_0110-001" class="aligncenter wp-image-6943" height="419" src="http://irisharchaeology.ie/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/DSC_0110-001.jpg?5a7560" width="580"/>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <strong>
           Three of the apostles
          </strong>
         </p>

          </a>
         </p>

          </strong>

           Three figures most likely representing Saints Columba/Colmcille, Brigid and Patrick
