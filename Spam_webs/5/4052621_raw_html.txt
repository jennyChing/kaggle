  <p align="CENTER">
   <font size="+3">
    LICHENS AND PEOPLE
   </font>
  </p>
  <p align="CENTER">
   <b>
    <font size="+1">
     For a
    </font>
    BIBLIOGRAPHICAL DATABASE OF
THE HUMAN USES OF LICHENS
   </b>
  </p>
  <p align="CENTER">
   <b>
    <font size="+1">
     compiled by Sylvia Duran Sharnoff
    </font>
   </b>
  </p>
  <blockquote>
   <h4 align="CENTER">
    <font size="+1">
     Go to
     <a href="usetaxon.html">
      Database sorted
  by lichen name
     </a>
     or
     <a href="usetype.html">
      Database sorted by type of
  use
     </a>
    </font>
   </h4>
  </blockquote>
  <blockquote>
   <p>
    <font size="+1">
     My attention was on the ground in front of me as we ambled
  through the New Mexico sagebrush, but Ramah Navajo weaver Jaymes Henio
  spotted a distant coyote. He explained that we should not hold the intention
  of finding "ground lichen" but rather we should be open to finding
  it. It moved from place to place, he said.
    </font>
   </p>
  </blockquote>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    (
    <b>
     Click on photos
    </b>
    for enlarged views.)
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Xchlorochroa.html">
    <img align="LEFT" height="128" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Xchlorochroa.jpeg" width="192"/>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    Known as a vagrant lichen because it grows loose on the
ground, "ground lichen" (
    <b>
     Xanthoparmelia chlorochroa
    </b>
    ) is
free to wander on the wind.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Navajo.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="192" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Navajo.jpeg" width="128"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    Like the other members of the Ramah Navajo Weavers Association,
Jaymes Henio raises and shears his own sheep and spins his own yarns, which
he dyes with vegetal dyes. The warm brown in these weavings and in James's
skein of yarn come from "ground lichen."
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    For information on the Ramah Navajo Weavers Association,
write to P.O. Box 153, Pine Hill, NM 87357 .
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <blockquote>
   <p>
    <font size="+1">
     The unique biochemical compounds
  produced by lichens (see
    </font>
    <a href="biology.html">
     <b>
      <font size="+1">
       Lichen
  Biology and the Environment
      </font>
     </b>
    </a>
    <font size="+1">
     ) have
  made them useful to people in traditional cultures as dyes and medicines.
    </font>
   </p>
  </blockquote>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/dyes.html">
    <img align="BOTTOM" height="128" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/dyes.jpeg" width="192"/>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    A sampling of lichen-dyed yarns by textile artists Glenna
Dean and Karen Casselman. Some dyes are extracted by boiling the lichens
in water; others by fermenting the lichens in ammonia. Karen has developed
lichen-conserving methods that greatly reduce the quantity needed.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Chilkat.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="128" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Chilkat.jpeg" width="192"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    The Chilkat Tlingit traditionally dyed their prized dancing
blankets with "wolf lichen" (
    <b>
     Letharia vulpina
    </b>
    ). They traded
valuable coastal commodities such as fish grease to groups in the interior
in exchange for the lichens.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Lvulpina.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="192" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Lvulpina.jpeg" width="128"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    "Wolf lichen" (
    <b>
     Letharia vulpina
    </b>
    ) was the
most widely used dye lichen for native peoples in North America, from what
is now the Yukon, down the west coast, and through the mountain states into
Arizona.
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    The Apache painted wolf-lichen crosses on their feet so
they could pass their enemies unseen. In another example of ritualistic
use of lichens, the Gitksan in British Columbia associated the lichen
   </font>
   <a href="bigpix/Lpulmonaria.html">
    <b>
     <font size="+1">
      Lobaria pulmonaria
     </font>
    </b>
    <font size="+1">
    </font>
   </a>
   <font size="+1">
    with frogs and used it in a spring bathing
ritual to bring health and long life.
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    Wolf lichen is poisonous. Its name reflects its traditional
use in northern Europe as a poison for wolves, and the Achomawi used it
(sometimes with rattlesnake venom added) to make poison arrowheads. Nonetheless,
the Blackfoot and the Okanagan-Colville took Letharia as a medicinal tea.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Ufillipendula.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="192" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Ufilipendula.jpeg" width="128"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    In the Russian Far East,
    <b>
     Usnea filipendula
    </b>
    was used as a powder to treat wounds. When it was tested for antibacterial
activitity, the results were quite positive. The lichen compound usnic acid
(in extracts of Usnea species) has been used recently in antiboitic salves,
deodorants, and herbal tinctures.
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    Species of Usnea are used in Chinese medicine, contemporary
homeopathic medicine, and traditional medicine in the Pacific Islands, New
Zealand, and every continent except Australia.
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    Many other lichens have been used medicinally, and it is
estimated that about 50% of all lichen species have antibiotic properities.
Research to develop pharmaceuticals from lichens continues, especially in
Japan.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <blockquote>
   <p>
    <font size="+1">
     Explorer Gabriel Franchere tasted pit-cooked
     <b>
      Bryoria
  fremontii
     </b>
     in 1814 and recorded, "I thought I had put a piece of
  soap in my mouth." Some of the many Native American groups in the
  Northwest who ate this lichen savored it as a delicacy, though; others
  used it only as a famine food.
    </font>
   </p>
   <p>
   </p>
  </blockquote>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Bfremontii.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="192" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Bfremontii.jpeg" width="128"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <b>
    <font size="+1">
     Bryoria fremontii
    </font>
   </b>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    David Friesan, who lives with the Ulkatcho Indian Band
in western British Columbia, recently decided to try Bryoria. He put some
to soak in a lake overnight, and in the morning it was covered with freshwater
shrimp. So he boiled the whole thing into a tasty soup.
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    The insulating qualites of Bryoria as fiber made it somewhat
useful to the Lillooet and the Nlaka'pamux (Thompson Indians) in British
Columbia, who twined it together into clothing when they couldn't get animal
skins. It must have been scratchy when dry and soggy when wet.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Bryoriacooking.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="164" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Bryoriacooking.jpg" width="122"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    A Secwempec (Shuswap) elder from Kamloops, British Columbia,
places
    <b>
     Bryoria fremontii
    </b>
    in a pit oven to cook it. Photograph copyright
Sandra Peacock 1992.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <a href="bigpix/Eprunastri.html">
    <font size="+1">
     <img align="LEFT" height="192" naturalsizeflag="3" src="smallphotos/Eprunastri.jpeg" width="128"/>
    </font>
   </a>
  </p>
  <p>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    At one time, "Oakmoss lichen" (
    <b>
     Evernia prunastri
    </b>
    )
was made into jelly in Turkey , and it was also imported into Egypt to be
baked in bread.
   </font>
  </p>
  <p>
   <font size="+1">
    Oakmoss lichen is an important ingredient in fine perfumes.
It is harvested commercially in large quantities in south-central Europe.
    <br clear="ALL"/>
   </font>
  </p>
