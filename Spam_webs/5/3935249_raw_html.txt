             <h2 class="post-title">
              In love with Rat Girl
             </h2>
            </header>
            <!-- Post Content -->
            <div class="post_content">
             <div style="float: left; margin-right: 12px; margin-bottom: 0px;">
              <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0143117394?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0143117394">
               <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6021" height="280" src="http://www.minnesotareads.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2010/10/ratgirl.jpg" title="ratgirl" width="183"/>
              </a>
             </div>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.minnesotareads.com/author/christa/">
               Christa
              </a>
              once said, and I can never find the original post, that she was incapable of talking about Haruki Murakamis writing without breaking into an interpretive dance. Thats kind of how I feel about Kristin Hershs memoir
              <em>
               <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0143117394?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0143117394">
                Rat Girl
               </a>
              </em>
              . Its kind of fitting that I need to quote Christa, since shes the entire reason I read this book.
             </p>
             <p>
              I was feeling a little guilty for abandoning
              <em>
               Anthropology of an American Girl
              </em>
              , which
              <a href="http://www.minnesotareads.com/2010/07/an-epic-anthropology/">
               Christa loved and recommended
              </a>
              to me. I did not love it. Not at all. So when
              <a href="http://www.minnesotareads.com/2010/10/lovable-blue-hairs/">
               she started raving about
               <em>
                Rat Girl
               </em>
              </a>
              , I saw my chance at redemption.
             </p>
             <p>
              Redemption is mine.
             </p>
             <p>
              <em>
               Rat Girl
              </em>
              is the kind of good that gives you nextbookaphobia, where you dont want to start another book because you know it will not be as good as the one you just finished (youve been put on notice Mr. Franzen and
              <em>
               Freedom
              </em>
              ).  In her memoir Hersh chronicles 1985-1986 the year she was diagnosed with bipolar disorder, her band Throwing Muses landed a record deal, and she became a single mother at the age of nineteen.
             </p>
             <p>
              Its a lot, and the way Hersh tells her story is beautiful. Shes written the memoir based on the diary she kept that tumultuous year and she keeps the story in eighteen-year-old Kristins point of view the entire time. A lot of memoir is rife with the me of today reflecting on the me of yesteryear, making excuses, justifying behavior, and reflecting on the significance of bygone events. Its this kind of wankery that makes a lot of memoir insufferable. Its as though the memoirist doesnt trust the reader enough to figure out the importance of the moments he/she chose to share.
             </p>
             <p>
              Hersh does none of that, and its so damn refreshing I want to kiss her. I also want to kiss her for not
              <a href="http://www.iwilldare.com/2008/06/22/an-open-plea-to-all-rockstarauthors/">
               mentioning recoupable debt once
              </a>
              . Actually, she kind of had me at page two of the introduction when she wrote, And it all happened twenty-five years ago, so it cant really count as a story about
              <em>
               me
              </em>
               that girl isnt me anymore. Now its just a story.
             </p>
             <p>
              What a story it is. From squatting in a dead guys abandoned apartment to suffering a concussion after being hit by a car (Hersh thinks shes been hit by a wicked witch) to the slow spiral into mania, the story is engrossing and engaging. Then theres the music, the music that Hersh sees as colors. Music that plagues her and makes her feel tattooed by the songs. Ive read a lot of books about Rock &amp; Roll and nobody has been able to put it into words quite the way Kristin Hersh has. It makes me wish I had been a Throwing Muses fan back in the day (talk about missing the boat).
             </p>
             <p>
              If youve ever felt music was your religion or your life or had a deep, confusing, inexplicable relationship with Rock &amp; Roll you should read this book (you can get a teeny, tiny taste by reading
              <a href="http://www.largeheartedboy.com/blog/archive/2010/08/book_notes_kris_2.html">
               Hershs Largeheartedboy Book Notes essay
              </a>
              ). While I believe Hershs relationship with music is wholly unique to her, she writes about it in such a way that it gives those of us who have yet to find the words hope that those words are still out there.
             </p>
