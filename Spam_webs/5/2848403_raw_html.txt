     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="NOAA_.28unidentified.29">
       NOAA (unidentified)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

     <p>

      .

     <p>
      <b>
       Upsweep
      </b>
      is an unidentified sound detected on the American
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">
       National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_Pacific_Ocean_autonomous_hydrophone_array" title="Equatorial Pacific Ocean autonomous hydrophone array">
       equatorial autonomous hydrophone arrays
      </a>
      . This sound was present when the
      <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Marine_Environmental_Laboratory" title="Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory">
       Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory
      </a>
      began recording its sound surveillance system
      <a href="/wiki/SOSUS" title="SOSUS">
       SOSUS
      </a>
      in August, 1991. It consists of a long train of narrow-band upsweeping sounds of several seconds in duration each. The source level is high enough to be recorded throughout the Pacific.
     </p>
     <p>
      The sound appears to be seasonal, generally reaching peaks in spring and autumn, but it is unclear whether this is due to changes in the source or seasonal changes in the propagation environment. The source can be roughly located at

     <p>
      According to the NOAA description, it "rises rapidly in frequency over about one minute and was of sufficient amplitude to be heard on multiple sensors, at a range of over 5,000 km." The NOAA's Dr. Christopher Fox did not believe its origin was man-made, such as a submarine or bomb, nor familiar geological events such as volcanoes or earthquakes. While the audio profile of Bloop does resemble that of a living creature,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the source was a mystery both because it was different from known sounds and because it was several times louder than the loudest recorded animal, the
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_whale" title="Blue whale">
       blue whale
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The NOAA Vents Program has attributed the sound to that of a large icequake. Numerous icequakes share similar spectrograms with Bloop, as well as the amplitude necessary to spot them despite ranges exceeding 5000km. This was found during the tracking of iceberg A53a as it disintegrated near South Georgia island in early 2008. If this is indeed the origin of Bloop, the iceberg(s) involved in generating the sound were most likely between Bransfield Straits and the Ross Sea; or possibly at Cape Adare, a well-known source of cryogenic signals.

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Train
      </b>
      is the name given to an unidentified sound recorded on March 5, 1997 on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_Pacific_Ocean_autonomous_hydrophone_array" title="Equatorial Pacific Ocean autonomous hydrophone array">
       Equatorial Pacific Ocean autonomous hydrophone array
      </a>
      . The sound rises to a quasi-steady frequency. According to the NOAA, the origin of the sound is most likely generated by a very large iceberg grounded in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ross_Sea" title="Ross Sea">
       Ross Sea
      </a>
      , near
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_Adare" title="Cape Adare">
       Cape Adare
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
