&lt;div class="post-top"&gt;
&lt;h1 class="post-title entry-title" &gt;5 Ways You May Be Unwittingly Inviting Rejection&lt;/h1&gt;




&lt;div class="post-meta the-icons pmeta-alt"&gt;
&lt;span class="post-author vcard"&gt;
By &lt;a rel="author" style="text-decoration:none;"  
        href="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/nlp/author/mbundrant/"&gt;Mike Bundrant&lt;/a&gt;
 &lt;br /&gt;
~ 3 min read
&lt;/span&gt;
&lt;/div&gt;
&lt;br /&gt;





&lt;/div&gt;



&lt;div class="post-content"&gt;

  &lt;div class="entry-content" &gt;




&lt;p&gt;&lt;a href="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/nlp/files/2013/02/worrieds.jpg"&gt;&lt;img class="alignright size-full wp-image-125" src="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/nlp/files/2013/02/worrieds.jpg" alt="rejection" width="275" height="183" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;Rejection. Its the greatest social fear. Who enjoys being hurt and rejected?&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Yet, so many of us unwittingly walking into rejection again and again. We feel consistently disregarded, hurt, isolated and betrayed in our personal and professional relationships.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;div align="center"&gt;&lt;div id="mj_adblock"&gt;&lt;script type="text/javascript"&gt;
//&lt;![CDATA[ 
 OAS_RICH('Right2'); 
 //]]&gt;
          </script>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         A pattern of feeling rejected leads to resentment and even depression. In fact, a
         <a href="http://www.naturalnews.com/040370_relationships_depression_mental_health.html" target="_blank">
          ten-year study
         </a>
         recently confirmed that such stressful relationships are directly linked to clinical depression.
        </p>
        <p>
         Amazingly, behaviors that flow from our conscious intention to be accepted are often the culprit. Here are five examples of how we may unwittingly invite rejection while banking on acceptance.
        </p>
        <h2>
         Do you see yourself in any of the following 5 scenarios?
        </h2>
        <p>
         <span id="more-1163">
         </span>
        </p>
        <h3>
         1. Not Speaking Up
        </h3>
        <p>
         You need something, but dont ask for it. Perhaps youve worked hard all day and would like your partner to help with the dishes. You think, I wish he would just help. If he really cared about me, hed know that I want help and just offer. So, just forget it. I guess Im stuck with an uncaring mate.
         <br/>
         In this scenario, you probably feel taken advantage of, unappreciated and resentful.
        </p>
        <p>
         The alternative is to simply ask for what you need. Your partner will either agree to help or refuse to help in one way or another. If he helps, then you have much less reason to feel rejected. If he doesnt help even though he easily could, then you experience a real world rejection, which is very different from the self-induced rejection. This is a problem to solve  and one you can begin to address immediately.
        </p>
        <h3>
         2. Pressuring
        </h3>
        <p>
         You want something really badly, so you keep asking for itover and over. Your partner feels pressured because she needs more time to decide or doesnt really want to come out and say no.
        </p>
        <div align="center">
         <div id="mj_adblock">
          <script type="text/javascript">
           //&lt;![CDATA[ 
 OAS_RICH('x02'); 
 //]]&gt;
          </script>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         You sense the hesitation, which compels you pressure her more. You know what happens next. Bam! She blows up and tells you off.
        </p>
        <h3>
         3. Avoiding Conflict
        </h3>
        <p>
         Something difficult yet important needs to be discussed. You know this is a hot button topic and the last thing you want is another argument. So, with looming images of verbal battles in your mind, you avoid the subject entirely.
        </p>
        <p>
         This only makes arguing inevitable. For example. You told your friends that you could go out with them Friday night. You know your partner will be put off, so you dont tell him. Friday night comes and you spring the news on him at the last minute, or worse, just leave without telling him.
        </p>
        <p>
         Hes upset and loses his cool in your direction.
        </p>
        <h3>
         4. Masking the Truth
        </h3>
        <p>
         Lets just call it lying.
        </p>
        <p>
         So, you spilled milk on the counter and neglected to clean it up. Your partner hates sloppiness. Who made a mess on the counter? she demands.
        </p>
        <p>
         You and the kids look around suspiciously. Apparently, nobody did it. Thirty minutes later, she discovers the truth and you are not only busted, but suffer through another rejection  and for more than sloppiness. Now, your trustworthiness is in question.
        </p>
        <h3>
         5. Being Too Agreeable
        </h3>
        <p>
         Agreeing with something that you dont really agree with leads to passive aggressive behavior.
        </p>
        <p>
          You agree to go to a play, but sulk your way through the evening.
         <br/>
          You agree to mow the yard on Sunday, but somehow forget.
         <br/>
          You agree to save more money, but spend it like always.
         <br/>
          You agree to do more around the house, but continue your couch potato ways.
         <br/>
          You agree to spend more time together, but continue as a busybody.
        </p>
        <p>
         All of these passive aggressive patterns ultimately lead to arguments and rejection. Its much better to disagree up front and negotiate something that you know you can willingly do.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Why oh why do we set ourselves up for rejection?
        </h3>
        <p>
         Often times we know the ultimate outcome of our behaviors, even as we do them. You know where the above behaviors are likely to take you. Yet, in the moment, you stick with old habits.
        </p>
        <p>
         The set up for almost certain rejections comes from a little known phenomenon called a
         <a href="http://inlpcenter.org/psychological-attachments" target="_blank">
          negative psychological attachment
         </a>
         . With psychological attachments, behaviors that cause us pain tend to happen on autopilot, as if some part of us were actually seeking unfavorable outcomes. Even though we consciously would not choose rejection, for example, somehow we end up there. It can be confusing!
        </p>
        <p>
         In the end, such attachments create
         <strong>
          self-sabotage
         </strong>
         , which is perhaps the most important issue to understand and resolve.
        </p>
        <p>
         To learn more about how negative psychological attachments create self-sabotaging behaviors,
         <a href="http://inlpcenter.org/aha-process-self-sabotage" target="_blank">
          watch this enlightening free video.
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          If you like this article, then

          </a>
          to keep up with all my writing.
         </em>
        </p>
