        </span>
        <a href="http://maps.nationalgeographic.com/map-machine#s=r&amp;c=-20.31598169294921,%2057.665321350097656&amp;z=8">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          (map)
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         is located about 1,200 miles (2,000 kilometers) off the coast of Africa, east of
        </span>
        <a href="http://travel.nationalgeographic.com/travel/countries/madagascar-guide/">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          Madagascar
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         . Scientists think the tiny island formed some nine million years ago from cooling lava spewed by undersea
        </span>
        <a href="http://environment.nationalgeographic.com/environment/natural-disasters/volcano-profile/">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          volcanoes
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ffffff;">
         .
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         But recently, researchers have found sand grains on Mauritius that contain fragments of the mineral zircon that are far older than the island, between 660 million and about 2 billion years old.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         In a new study, detailed in the current issue of the journal
        </span>
        <a href="http://www.nature.com/ngeo/index.html">
         <em>
          <span style="color: #ffffff;">
           Nature Geoscience
          </span>
         </em>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         , scientists concluded that the older minerals once belonged to a now vanished landmass, tiny bits of which were dragged up to the surface during the formation of Mauritius. (Also see
        </span>
        <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2005/11/1128_021128_earth_crust.html">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          Worlds Oldest Rocks Suggest Early Earth Was Habitable.
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         )
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         When lavas moved through continental material on the way towards the surface, they picked up a few rocks containing zircon, study co-author
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
         </span>
        </span>
        <a href="http://pgp.uio.no/index.php/people/management/8-about-pgp/people/97-bjorn-jamtveit">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          Bjrn Jamtveit
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         , a geologist at the University of Oslo in Norway, explained in an email.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Most of these rocks probably disintegrated and melted due to the high temperatures of the lavas, but some grains of zircons survived and were frozen into the lavas [during the eruption] and rolled down to form rocks on the Mauritian surface.
        </span>
       </p>
       <h2 style="text-align: center;">
        Prehistoric Atlantis
       </h2>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Jamtveit and his colleagues estimate that the lost microcontinent, which they have dubbed Mauritia, was about a quarter of the size of
        </span>
        <a href="http://maps.nationalgeographic.com/map-machine#s=r&amp;c=-18.90040681629307,%2046.84123611450195&amp;z=5">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          Madagascar (map)
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ffffff;">
         .
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Furthermore, based on a recalculation of how the ancient continents drifted apart, the scientists concluded that Mauritia was once a tiny part of a much larger supercontinent that included
        </span>
        <a href="http://travel.nationalgeographic.com/travel/countries/india-guide/">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          India
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         and Madagascar, called Rodinia.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         The three landmasses were tucked together in one big continent prior to the formation of the Indian Ocean, Jamtveit said.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         But like a prehistoric Atlantis, Mauritia was eventually drowned beneath the waves when India broke apart from Madagascar about 85 million years ago. (Also see
        </span>
        <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2007/10/071017-india-tectonics.html">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          
          <span style="color: #ffffff;">
           Slimmer Indian Continent Drifted Ten Times Faster
          </span>
          .
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         )
        </span>
       </p>
       <h2 style="text-align: center;">
        Ancient Rocks
       </h2>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         <a href="http://www.thetruthseeker.co.uk/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/02/Lion-Mountain-in-southeastern-Mauritius.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-66063" height="225" src="http://www.thetruthseeker.co.uk/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2013/02/Lion-Mountain-in-southeastern-Mauritius-300x225.jpg" title="Lion Mountain in southeastern Mauritius. Click to enlarge" width="300"/>
         </a>
         Scientists have long suspected that volcanic islands might contain evidence of lost continents, and Jamtveit and his team decided to test this hypothesis during a layover in Mauritius as part of a longer research trip in 1999. (See
        </span>
        <a href="http://environment.nationalgeographic.com/environment/photos/volcano-general/">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          volcano pictures
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         .)
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         The stop in tropical Mauritius was a very tempting thing to do for a Norwegian in the cold month of January, Jamtveit said.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Mauritius was a good test site because it was a relatively young island and, being formed from ocean lava, would not naturally contain zircon, a tough
        </span>
        <a href="http://science.nationalgeographic.com/science/earth/inside-the-earth/minerals-gems/">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          mineral
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         that doesnt weather easily.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         If zircon older than 9 million years was found on Mauritius, it would be good evidence of the presence of buried continental material, Jamtveit explained. (
        </span>
        <a href="http://photography.nationalgeographic.com/photography/photos/patterns-rocks-lava/">
         <span style="color: #ffffff;">
          See lava and rock pictures
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         .)
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         At first, the scientists crushed rocks from Mauritius to extract the zircon crystals, but this proved difficult because the crushing equipment contained zircon from other sites, raising the issue of contamination.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         That was a show stopper for a while, Jamtveit said.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         A few years later, however, some members of the team returned to Mauritius and this time brought back sand from two different beaches for sampling.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         The scientists extracted 20 zircon samples and successfully dated 8 of them by calculating the rate that the elements uranium and thorium inside of the samples slowly break down into lead.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         They all provided much older ages than the age of the Mauritius lavas, Jamtveit said. In fact they gave ages consistent with the ages of known continental rocks in Madagascar, Seychelles, and India.
        </span>
       </p>
       <h2 style="text-align: center;">
        Missing Evidence?
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.ipgp.fr/~jdy/#JD">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          Jrme Dyment
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         , a geologist at the Paris Institute of Earth Physics in France, said hes unconvinced by the work because its possible that the ancient zircons found their way to the island by other means, for example as part of ship ballast or modern construction material.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, which are not given by the authors so far, said Dyment, who did not participate in the research.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Finding zircons in sand is one thing, finding them within a rock is another one  Finding the enclave of deep rocks that, according to the authors inference, bring them to the surface during an eruption would be much more convincing evidence.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Dyment added that if Mauritia was real, evidence for its existence should be found as part of a joint French and German experiment that installed deep-sea seismometers to investigate Earths mantle around Runion Island, which is situated about 120 miles (200 kilometers) from Mauritius. (
        </span>
        <a href="http://science.nationalgeographic.com/science/earth/inside-the-earth/">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          Learn whats inside the Earth
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         .)
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         If a microcontinent lies under Runion, it should be depicted by this experiment, said Dyment, who is part of the project, dubbed
        </span>
        <a href="http://www.rhum-rum.net/en/">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          RHUM-RUM
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         .
        </span>
       </p>
       <h2 style="text-align: center;">
        More Dismembered Continents to Be Found?
       </h2>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         But
        </span>
        <a href="http://www.earth.ox.ac.uk/people/profiles/academic/conallm">
         <span style="color: #ccffff;">
          Conall Mac Niocaill
         </span>
        </a>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         , a geologist at the University of Oxford in the U.K. who was also not involved in the study, said the lines of evidence are, individually, only suggestive, but collectively they add up to a compelling story.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         The zircons produce a range of ages, but all yield ages older than 660 million years, and one is almost 2 billion years old, he added.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         There is no obvious source for them in Mauritius, and they are unlikely to have been blown in by the wind, or carried in by human activity, so the obvious conclusion is that the young volcanic lava sampled some older material on their way through the crust.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         Based on the new findings, Mac Niocaill and others think other vanished microcontinents could be lurking beneath the Indian Ocean.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         In fact, analyses of Earths gravitational field have revealed other areas in the worlds oceans where the rock appears to be thicker than normal and could be a sign of continental crusts.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="color: #ccffff;">
         We know more about the topography of Mars than we do about the [topography] of the worlds ocean floor, so there may well be other dismembered continents out there waiting to be discovered.
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2013/02/130225-microcontinent-earth-mauritius-geology-science/">
         Source
        </a>
       </p>
      </div>

          Satanic Temple still plans to unveil devil statue in Detroit, despite protests
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p class="byline">
         By
         <span class="author vcard">
