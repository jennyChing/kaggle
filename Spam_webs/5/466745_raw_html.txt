     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Hotel Chevalier
       </b>
      </i>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/2007_in_film" title="2007 in film">
       2007
      </a>
      American-French
      <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">
       short film
      </a>
      written and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Wes_Anderson" title="Wes Anderson">
       Wes Anderson
      </a>
      and released in 2007. Starring
      <a href="/wiki/Jason_Schwartzman" title="Jason Schwartzman">
       Jason Schwartzman
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Portman" title="Natalie Portman">
       Natalie Portman
      </a>
      as former lovers who reunite in a Paris hotel room, the 13-minute film acts as a
      <a href="/wiki/Prologue" title="Prologue">
       prologue
      </a>
      to Anderson's 2007 feature
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Darjeeling_Limited" title="The Darjeeling Limited">
        The Darjeeling Limited
       </a>
      </i>
      . It was shot on location in a Parisian hotel by a small crew and self-financed by Anderson, who initially intended it to be a stand-alone work. Its first showing was at the
      <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">
       Venice Film Festival
      </a>
      premire of the feature film on September 2, 2007, and it made its own debut later that month at
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Store" title="Apple Store">
       Apple Stores
      </a>
      in four American cities. The day after its premire, it was made available for free from the
      <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">
       iTunes Store
      </a>
      for one month, during which it was downloaded more than 500,000 times. The film garnered near-universal critical acclaim from reviewers who compared it favorably with
      <i>
       The Darjeeling Limited
      </i>
      and praised its richness, poignancy, and careful construction.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hotel_Chevalier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In a hotel lobby, the
      <a href="/wiki/Concierge" title="Concierge">
       concierge
      </a>
      answers a phone call from one of the guests' rooms. A man (
      <a href="/wiki/Jason_Schwartzman" title="Jason Schwartzman">
       Jason Schwartzman
      </a>
      ) lies on a hotel bed in a yellow bathrobe, watching the black-and-white American war film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Stalag_17" title="Stalag 17">
        Stalag 17
       </a>
      </i>
      and reading the newspaper. After ordering room service from the concierge in broken
      <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
       French
      </a>
      , he receives a call from a woman whose voice he recognizes. She tells him she is on her way from the airport and asks for his room number. Despite objecting that he did not tell her she could come, the man consents nevertheless. He then hurriedly attempts to tidy the room  pausing to play the opening bars of the song "
      <a href="/wiki/Where_Do_You_Go_To_(My_Lovely)%3F" title="Where Do You Go To (My Lovely)?">
       Where Do You Go To (My Lovely)?
      </a>
      " by
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sarstedt" title="Peter Sarstedt">
       Peter Sarstedt
      </a>
      on his stereo system  and runs a bath.
     </p>
     <p>
      The man is again lying on the bed, now in a gray suit. Hearing a knock, he starts the song playing again before opening the door to a woman (
      <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Portman" title="Natalie Portman">
       Natalie Portman
      </a>
      ). After staring at him for several seconds, the woman breaks the silence by asking what music is playing. Receiving no response, she steps into the room and presents the man with a bouquet of flowers. When she moves to kiss him on the mouth, he turns his head away and they embrace instead. He closes the door and asks how she found him; she replies that it "wasn't actually that hard". She moves around the room browsing through his possessions, brushes her teeth with his toothbrush and declines to take the bath he had run for her.
     </p>
     <p>
      Stepping back into the bedroom, the woman turns to face the man and confronts him, asking slowly "what the fuck is going on?" He motions to her to join him on the bed and at her prompting, he reveals in the ensuing conversation that he has been living in the hotel room for "more than a month", and that he had left to escape their relationship. They lie back on the bed looking at one another before being interrupted by the arrival of room service. Once alone again, the two kiss and the man begins to undress the woman. They have an uncomfortable exchange about not having slept with other people and when he notices bruises on her arm after undressing her further, the woman chooses not to comment on them. Lying on top of him, she tells the man that she does not want to lose his friendship, that she loves him and never meant to hurt him. He responds coldly that he "will never be [her] friend", but holds her when she embraces him. "Where Do You Go To (My Lovely)?" starts again and the man offers to show the woman his view of Paris.
     </p>
     <p>
      The woman is perched against an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armoire" title="Armoire">
       armoire
      </a>
      , the man approaching and covering her naked body with the yellow bathrobe, and the two moving towards the window. After they step out on the balcony, the man draws a toothpick from his pocket and hands it to her with an upwards nod, which she reciprocates. After looking out for another few seconds she clasps his neck lightly and they step back inside.
     </p>

     <p>
      Director
      <a href="/wiki/Wes_Anderson" title="Wes Anderson">
       Wes Anderson
      </a>
      first approached actors Schwartzman and Portman about
      <i>
       Hotel Chevalier
      </i>
      in 2005.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Schwartzman and Anderson had previously worked on
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rushmore_(film)" title="Rushmore (film)">
        Rushmore
       </a>
      </i>
      (1998), Anderson's cult second feature, and had been living together in Schwartzman's Paris apartment in the months leading up to the shoot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newyorker_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newyorker-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Portman was approached after the director obtained her email address from
      <a href="/wiki/Scott_Rudin" title="Scott Rudin">
       Scott Rudin
      </a>
      , producer of 2004's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closer_(film)" title="Closer (film)">
        Closer
       </a>
      </i>
      in which she starred.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-latimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The actors appeared for free,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-variety-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Anderson financed the remainder of the production himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hindu_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hindu-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was filmed at the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%B4tel_Rapha%C3%ABl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Htel Raphal (page does not exist)">
       Htel Raphal
      </a>
      in Paris, which had previously been used as a setting for the films
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Love_in_Paris" title="Love in Paris">
        Love in Paris
       </a>
      </i>
      (1996) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Place_Vend%C3%B4me_(film)" title="Place Vendme (film)">
        Place Vendme
       </a>
      </i>
      (1998).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raphael_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-raphael-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was shot by a crew of 15 in Panavision and used props from Anderson's apartment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-latimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hindu_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-hindu-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Filming took two and a half days, and editing (done on Anderson's computer) another week.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Despite his use of a wardrobe from prestigious fashion designer
      <a href="/wiki/Marc_Jacobs" title="Marc Jacobs">
       Marc Jacobs
      </a>
      and a handmade suitcase from
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vuitton" title="Louis Vuitton">
       Louis Vuitton
      </a>
      , the director described the production as "like making a student film".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-latimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anderson initially intended it to be a stand-alone short film,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifc_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ifc-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but shortly before filming commenced, he realized that Schwartzman's character bore a close resemblance to one of the protagonists of a feature film he was writing at the time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifc_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ifc-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      That film would begin production a year later as
      <i>
       The Darjeeling Limited
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Chevalier
      </i>
      takes place two weeks before Schwartzman's character (named Jack Whitman in the feature) joins his two older brothers on a journey in India in
      <i>
       Darjeeling
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-latimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The dialogue between the characters at the end of
      <i>
       Chevalier
      </i>
      is recounted by Schwartzman's character to his brothers at the close of the feature film, in the form of an excerpt from a short story he has composed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corliss_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-corliss-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Portman's character has a brief cameo in the feature.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Fox_Searchlight_Pictures" title="Fox Searchlight Pictures">
       Fox Searchlight Pictures
      </a>
      , the studio that backed
      <i>
       Darjeeling
      </i>
      , was unaware of the short until the feature had been made and claimed to have no financial interest in it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div style="clear:both;">
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Release">
       Release
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hotel_Chevalier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Release">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
      <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
       <div>
        <p>
         "When it was all done, I didn't want to incorporate the short into the movie. But I couldn't decide how I wanted it to go. I wanted to play the short in front of the movie, but not always. Sometimes I preferred to watch the movie without the short. It became a puzzle to me. So in the end I decided that I would like to have the movie open in America without the short, but I would like people to have access to it if they want to see it first."
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="text-align: left;">
       Wes Anderson, October 2007
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phoenix_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-phoenix-10">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         10
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Hotel Chevalier
      </i>
      was screened as part of the program at the world premire of
      <i>
       The Darjeeling Limited
      </i>
      at the 64th Annual
      <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">
       Venice Film Festival
      </a>
      on September 2, 2007.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corliss_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-corliss-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The short had its own premire at
      <a href="/wiki/Apple_Store" title="Apple Store">
       Apple Stores
      </a>
      in New York City, Chicago, San Francisco and Santa Monica, California, on September 25.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-variety-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Anderson, Schwartzman and Portman appeared at the New York screening in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SoHo" title="SoHo">
       SoHo
      </a>
      , after which they held a questions-and-answers session with the audience.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-variety-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The short was made available the following day as a free download from Apple's
      <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">
       iTunes Store
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On September 28, it preceded the showing of the feature on the opening night of the 2007
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Film_Festival" title="New York Film Festival">
       New York Film Festival
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyff_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyff-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A press release posted before the premire described the short as "the brief coda to a doomed romance and the prologue to
      <i>
       The Darjeeling Limited
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slashfilm_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-slashfilm-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The film was withdrawn from iTunes after having been available for download for a month.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although it had been dismissed by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">
        USA Today
       </a>
      </i>
      columnist Susan Wloszczyna before its iTunes release as an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Amuse-bouche" title="Amuse-bouche">
        amuse-bouche
       </a>
      </i>
      that was "destined to be a mere footnote",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-usatoday-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it was downloaded nearly 500,000 times during its run, and received wide acclaim.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Following its withdrawal from iTunes, the short was distributed in theaters as the prologue to the feature-length film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although
      <i>
       The New York Times
      </i>
      reported in October 2007 that distributor
      <a href="/wiki/Fox_Searchlight_Pictures" title="Fox Searchlight Pictures">
       Fox Searchlight Pictures
      </a>
      intended to promote
      <i>
       Chevalier
      </i>
      as a competitor for the
      <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Live_Action_Short_Film" title="Academy Award for Best Live Action Short Film">
       Best Live Action Short Film
      </a>
      of the Academy Awards,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_13-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyt-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it was not among that year's nominations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oscars_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oscars-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was included as an extra feature on the
      <i>
       Darjeeling
      </i>
      2008 DVD release,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyff_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyff-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Anderson's screenplay for the short was published in the Winter 2007 issue of the literary magazine
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zoetrope:_All-Story" title="Zoetrope: All-Story">
        Zoetrope: All-Story
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zoetrope_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zoetrope-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
       Critical reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hotel_Chevalier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Apart from being one of the year's most discussed short films,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-latimes-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Hotel Chevalier
      </i>
      attracted considerable critical praise, with reviewers comparing it favorably to
      <i>
       The Darjeeling Limited
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">
        Entertainment Weekly
       </a>
      </i>
      's Gary Susman described it as "an exquisite short story where we learn not much but exactly enough about these two characters", adding that "
      <i>
       Chevalier
      </i>
      sees Anderson working in his customary jewel-box/dollhouse mode, but the form and length really suit each other here."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-susman_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-susman-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_Press" title="New York Press">
        New York Press
       </a>
      </i>
      , Armand White judged the short "moving and genuinely contemporary", citing its "lost-girl poignancy".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyp_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyp-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film drew some attention for co-star Natalie Portman's extended nude scene, and for her character's bruised body.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_2-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-breznican_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-breznican-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sequels_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sequels-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Portman expressed disappointment at this focus, saying "It really depressed me that half of every review ... was about the nudity". The episode made the actress reconsider the wisdom of this aspect of her performance,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbsnews_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cbsnews-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and she subsequently swore off nude appearances in film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbsnews_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cbsnews-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Portman's performance was praised by
      <i>
       TIME
      </i>
      reviewer
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Corliss" title="Richard Corliss">
       Richard Corliss
      </a>
      , who declared her to be "a comic actress in fresh bloom" in the "beguiling vignette", and expressed his wish that her role in
      <i>
       Darjeeling
      </i>
      had been greater.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corliss_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-corliss-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The feature, he felt, lacked "the feeling and wit of the short film".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corliss_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-corliss-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">
       Salon.com
      </a>
      's Stephanie Zacharek concurred, stating that "the untold story of
      <i>
       Hotel Chevalier
      </i>
      is 10 times more interesting, and infinitely richer, than the one told outright in
      <i>
       The Darjeeling Limited
      </i>
      ", and calling the short "very close to perfect".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-salon-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       The Guardian
      </i>
      columnist Danny Leigh contrasted the lukewarm reception of the feature among bloggers and critics with the "genuine ardour" that greeted the "perfectly measured narrative" of
      <i>
       Chevalier
      </i>
      . He proposed that the constraints of the short-film format suited Anderson, whose trademark deadpan humor, idiosyncratic set designs and choice of soundtrack inclined to exhaust the viewers' patience in a feature-length work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grauniad_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-grauniad-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/A._O._Scott" title="A. O. Scott">
       A. O. Scott
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       The New York Times
      </i>
      hailed
      <i>
       Chevalier
      </i>
      as "a small gem" in comparison to the "overstuffed suitcase" of the feature, and wrote that "It is worth seeking out, not only because it fleshes out part of the story of the Whitman brothers but also because, on its own, it is an almost perfect distillation of Mr. Anderson's vexing and intriguing talents, enigmatic, affecting and wry."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyff_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nyff-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
