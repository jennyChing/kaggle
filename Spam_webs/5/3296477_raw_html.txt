       <h1>
        Phone app allows US users to film police activity
       </h1>
       <h5 class="data">
        July 7, 2012
       </h5>
      </header>
      <section class="content-holder content-table content-story">
       <!--begin:content-->
       <section class="news-content">
        <!--begin:article-block-->
        <section class="content article-block">
         <article>
          <div class="first-block">
           <figure class="image-block">
            <div class="image-block-ins">
             <a href="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/gfx/news/hires/2012/auscivilrigh.jpg" rel="lightbox" title="">
              <img alt="A US civil rights advocacy group has launched a free mobile phone app that allows users to record police activity" src="http://cdn.phys.org/newman/csz/news/800/2012/auscivilrigh.jpg"/>
             </a>
            </div>
            <figcaption class="image-block-caption">
             A US civil rights advocacy group has launched a free mobile phone application that allows users to record police activity discreetly, saying it will help boost police force accountability.
            </figcaption>
           </figure>
           <p>
            A US civil rights advocacy group has launched a free mobile phone application that allows users to record police activity discreetly, saying it will help boost police force accountability.
           </p>
          </div>
          <!-- 1st block -->
          <div class="article-banner first-banner gtext-ad">
           <script language="JavaScript">
            &lt;!-- 
            var google_adnum = 0;
            var ad_channel_cmn = '+0559369967', google_hints="";

          <p>
           The New Jersey branch of the
           <a class="textTag" href="http://phys.org/tags/american+civil+liberties+union/" rel="tag">
            American Civil Liberties Union
           </a>
           (ACLU) explains that "Police Tape," available to the public since Tuesday, "allows people to securely and discreetly record and store interactions with police."
          </p>
          <p>
           The Android
           <a class="textTag" href="http://phys.org/tags/phone+application/" rel="tag">
            phone application
           </a>
           , available for download on the website of the ACLU of New Jersey, is "an essential tool for police accountability," said the office's executive director Deborah Jacobs.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Too often, incidents of serious misconduct go unreported because citizens don't feel that they will be believed."
          </p>
          <p>
           Unlike traditional smartphone recording apps, "Police Tape" disappears from the screen once it is launched, minimizing the possibility that police will notice the recorder.
          </p>
          <p>
           The application allows users to send the file to the ACLU for safe-keeping and analysis.
          </p>
          <p>
           "Police Tape" also contains legal advice on the rights of citizens confronted by police.
          </p>
          <p>
           The ACLU of New Jersey website cites several court rulings that recognized citizens' rights to film police activity.
          </p>
          <p>
           A similar application for
           <a class="textTag" href="http://phys.org/tags/iphone/" rel="tag">
            iPhone
           </a>
           will be available later this summer, the site says.
          </p>

            But on the other, it's extremely alarming that many jurisdictions outlaw capturing the police on video.  That's a gestapo-esque law that doesn't belong in American.  Good cops can also be vindicated by video and shouldn't fight them.  But there's no doubt that some officers think they're free to beat anyone they want.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            I think the ACLU is doing a good thing in this case, which isn't something I often say.
           </div>
