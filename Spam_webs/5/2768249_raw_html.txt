     <p>
      <b>
       Diaspora
      </b>
      (currently styled
      <b>
       diaspora*
      </b>
      and formerly styled
      <b>
       DIASPORA*
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonprofit" title="Nonprofit">
       nonprofit
      </a>
      , user-owned,
      <a href="/wiki/Distributed_social_network" title="Distributed social network">
       distributed social network
      </a>
      that is based upon the
      <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">
       free
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Diaspora_(software)" title="Diaspora (software)">
       Diaspora software
      </a>
      . Diaspora consists of a group of independently owned nodes (called
      <i>
       pods
      </i>
      ) which interoperate to form the network. As of March 2014, there are more than
      <span class="nowrap">
       1 million
      </span>
      Diaspora accounts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The social network is not owned by any one person or entity, keeping it from being subject to corporate take-overs or advertising. In September 2011 the developers stated, "...our distributed design means no big corporation will ever control Diaspora. Diaspora* will never sell your social life to advertisers, and you wont have to conform to someones arbitrary rules or look over your shoulder before you speak."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora21sep11_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora21sep11-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Diaspora software development is managed by the Diaspora Foundation, which is part of the Free Software Support Network (FSSN). The FSSN is in turn run by
      <a href="/wiki/Eben_Moglen" title="Eben Moglen">
       Eben Moglen
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Software_Freedom_Law_Center" title="Software Freedom Law Center">
       Software Freedom Law Center
      </a>
      . The FSSN acts as an umbrella organization to Diaspora development and manages Diaspora's branding, finances and legal assets.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora27Aug13_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora27Aug13-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">
       Technology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora_(social_network)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Diaspora_(software)" title="Diaspora (software)">
       Diaspora (software)
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Diaspora social network is constructed of a network of nodes, or
      <i>
       pods
      </i>
      , hosted by many different individuals and institutions. Each node operates a copy of the Diaspora software acting as a
      <a href="/wiki/Personal_web_server" title="Personal web server">
       personal web server
      </a>
      . Users of the network can host a pod on their own server or create an account on any existing pod of their choice, and from that pod can interact with other users on all other pods.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaste28Sep11_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kaste28Sep11-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hancock14Sep11_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hancock14Sep11-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwyer11May11_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dwyer11May11-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Friendica" title="Friendica">
       Friendica
      </a>
      instances are also a part of the Diaspora social network through the Friendica Diaspora connector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friendica_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Friendica-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
       Features
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora_(social_network)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Features">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Diaspora users retain ownership of their data and do not assign ownership rights. The software is specifically designed to allow users to download all their images and text that has been uploaded at any time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora21sep11_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora21sep11-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Diaspora software allows user posts to be designated as either "public" or "limited". In the latter case, posts may only be read by people assigned to one of the groups, termed
      <i>
       aspects
      </i>
      , which the user has approved to view the post. Each new account is assigned several default aspects  friends, family, work and acquaintances  and the user can add as many custom aspects as they like.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiasporaTutorial3_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiasporaTutorial3-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is possible to follow another user's public posts without the mutual
      <a href="/wiki/Friending" title="Friending">
       friending
      </a>
      required by other social networks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiasporaTutorial4_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiasporaTutorial4-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Users can also send private messages, called conversations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiasporaTutorial6_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiasporaTutorial6-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A user can filter their news stream by aspect.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiasporaTutorial3_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiasporaTutorial3-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The developers consider the distributed nature of the network crucial to its design and success:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora21sep11_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora21sep11-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Diasporas distributed design is a huge part of it. Like the Internet itself, Diaspora* isnt housed in any one place, and its not controlled by any one entity (including us). Weve created software that lets you set up and run your own social network on your own pod (or server) and connect your network to the larger Diaspora* ecosystem. You can have a pod all to yourself, or one for just you and your friends, or your family, giving you complete ownership and control over your personal social information (including your identity, your posts, and your photos) and how its all stored and shared. Or you can simply ... sign up at one of [the] open pods.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora21sep11_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora21sep11-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      The distributed design attracted members of the militant Islamist extremist group
      <a href="/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant" title="Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant">
       ISIS
      </a>
      after their propaganda campaigns were censored by Twitter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Diaspora developers issued a statement urging users to report offensive content, but due to the design of the service were unable to block content without the approval of pod admins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Diaspora has been specifically noted by U.S.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">
       National Public Radio
      </a>
      for its policy that allows the use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudonyms" title="Pseudonyms">
       pseudonyms
      </a>
      , in contrast to competitor
      <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">
       Facebook
      </a>
      , which does not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaste28Sep11_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kaste28Sep11-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Posts in Diaspora can include
      <a href="/wiki/Hashtag" title="Hashtag">
       hashtags
      </a>
      and 'mentions' (a username preceded by a @ symbol). Users can upload photos to posts, and can format text and links using
      <a href="/wiki/Markdown" title="Markdown">
       Markdown
      </a>
      . Posts can be propagated to connected accounts on Facebook,
      <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">
       Twitter
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tumblr" title="Tumblr">
       Tumblr
      </a>
      . Diaspora supports embedding of media from YouTube, Vimeo and a number of other sites, and also supports OpenGraph previews.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiasporaTutorial6_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiasporaTutorial6-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora_(social_network)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inception">
       Inception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora_(social_network)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Diaspora project was founded in 2010 by four students at
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">
       New York University
      </a>
      s
      <a href="/wiki/Courant_Institute_of_Mathematical_Sciences" title="Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences">
       Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Zhitomirskiy" title="Ilya Zhitomirskiy">
       Ilya Zhitomirskiy
      </a>
      , Dan Grippi, Max Salzberg, and Raphael Sofaer. The word
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">
        diaspora
       </a>
      </i>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      in origin and refers to a scattered or dispersed population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dict_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dict-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="1024" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg/220px-Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg/330px-Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg/440px-Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ilya_Zhitomirskiy_and_Daniel_Grippi.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Zhitomirskiy" title="Ilya Zhitomirskiy">
         Ilya Zhitomirskiy
        </a>
        and Daniel Grippi (2011)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Grippi, Salzberg, Sofaer, and Zhitomirskiy started the Diaspora project after being motivated by a February 5, 2010 speech by
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Law_School" title="Columbia Law School">
       Columbia University law
      </a>
      professor
      <a href="/wiki/Eben_Moglen" title="Eben Moglen">
       Eben Moglen
      </a>
      . In his speech, delivered to the
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_Society" title="Internet Society">
       Internet Society
      </a>
      's New York Chapter, "Freedom in the Cloud", Moglen described
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_network_service" title="Social network service">
       centralized social networks
      </a>
      as "spying for free."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwyer11May11_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dwyer11May11-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moglen-speech_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-moglen-speech-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">
        New York Times
       </a>
      </i>
      interview, Salzberg said "When you give up that data, youre giving it up forever ... The value they give us is negligible in the scale of what they are doing, and what we are giving up is all of our privacy." Sofaer said, "We don't need to hand our messages to a hub. What Facebook gives you as a user isn't all that hard to do. All the little games, the little walls, the little chat, aren't really rare things. The technology already exists".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwyer11May11_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dwyer11May11-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Salzberg has said that "Facebook is not what we are going after".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BBC-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The group decided to address this problem by creating a distributed social network. To obtain the necessary funds the project was launched on April 24, 2010 on
      <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">
       Kickstarter
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crowd_funding" title="Crowd funding">
       crowd funding
      </a>
      website. The first 39 days were assigned to raise the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">
       US$
      </a>
      10,000 that they estimated would be needed to get started. However, the initial funding goal was met in just 12 days and the project eventually raised more than US$200,000 from more than 6000 backers (making it the second most successful Kickstarter project of its time).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Grippi said, "We were shocked. For some strange reason, everyone just agreed with this whole privacy thing." Among the donors was Facebook CEO
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Zuckerberg" title="Mark Zuckerberg">
       Mark Zuckerberg
      </a>
      who contributed an undisclosed amount, saying "I donated. I think it is a cool idea."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwyer11May11_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dwyer11May11-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weise10May12_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weise10May12-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watters27Dec10_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Watters27Dec10-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanGrove28May10_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-VanGrove28May10-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      "Diaspora is trying to destroy the idea that one network can be totally dominant," stated Sofaer in laying down the aim of Diaspora.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weise10May12_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weise10May12-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>

      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora_(social_network)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Work on the Diaspora software began in May 2010. Finn Brunton, a teacher and digital media researcher at New York University, described their method as "a return of the classic geek means of production: pizza and ramen and guys sleeping under the desks because it is something that it is really exciting and challenging."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwyer11May11_6-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dwyer11May11-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A developer preview was released on September 15 and received criticism for various security bugs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first Diaspora "pod" was launched by the development team on November 23, 2010; as a private, invitation-only alpha.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation23Nov10_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Foundation23Nov10-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 2010
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ReadWriteWeb" title="ReadWriteWeb">
       ReadWriteWeb
      </a>
      named the project as one of its
      <i>
       Top 10 Start-Ups of 2010
      </i>
      , saying "Diaspora certainly represents the power of crowd funding, as well as an interest in making sure the social Web is not centralized in one company".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watters27Dec10_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Watters27Dec10-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On 7 January 2011
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Duck_Software" title="Black Duck Software">
       Black Duck Software
      </a>
      named the project one of its Open Source Rookies of 2010, for being "the privacy aware, personally controlled, do-it-all, open source social network. ",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlackDuck_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BlackDuck-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In September 2011, although the network and its software was still in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_software" title="Alpha software">
       alpha
      </a>
      , Terry Hancock of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Free_Software_Magazine" title="Free Software Magazine">
        Free Software Magazine
       </a>
      </i>
      described it as "already quite usable for some purposes". While it supported text, photographs, and links, it still lacked some features, including link preview, the ability to upload or embed videos (although videos could be linked to on other services) and chat. Animated
      <a href="/wiki/GIF" title="GIF">
       GIFs
      </a>
      were supported, however.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hancock14Sep11_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hancock14Sep11-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>

     <p>
      Since its release, features of Diaspora have appeared in similar forms in other social networks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In a September 2011 message the developers noted similarities such as
      <a href="/wiki/Google%2B" title="Google+">
       Google+
      </a>
      's "circles" (a version of Diaspora's aspects) and new sets of user privacy controls implemented by Facebook. They said "we cant help but be pleased with the impact our work has had". That Google borrowed heavily from Diaspora was a particular point of pride for Zhitomirskiy, although Google denied that Diaspora had influenced their designs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weise10May12_20-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weise10May12-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation08Sep11_27-0">

         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In October 2011, Diaspora announced that it was starting a fundraising campaign. Maxwell Salzberg explained, "The key right now is to build something that our community wants to use and that makes a difference in our users' lives. In the future, we will work with our community to determine with them how we could best turn Diaspora* into a self-sustaining operation." Within days of commencing the campaign over US$45,000 had been raised when
      <a href="/wiki/PayPal" title="PayPal">
       PayPal
      </a>
      froze Diaspora's account without explanation. After a large number of complaints to PayPal from Diaspora users and the threat of legal action, the account was unfrozen with an apology from a PayPal executive, but still without explanation. This incident prompted the acceptance of other payment processors, including
      <a href="/wiki/Stripe_(company)" title="Stripe (company)">
       Stripe
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">
       Bitcoin
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brockmeier12Oct11_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brockmeier12Oct11-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation12Oct11_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Foundation12Oct11-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kobie19Oct11_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kobie19Oct11-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Companys20Oct11_31-0">

         [

        31

     <p>
      The Diaspora Project website was started on September 29, 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foundation29Sep11_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Foundation29Sep11-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its declared mission is "to build a new and better social web, one thats 100% owned and controlled by you and other Diasporans."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora21sep11_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora21sep11-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On November 12, 2011 co-founder Zhitomirskiy committed suicide, at the age of twenty-two. Reports linked pressures related to Diaspora to his death.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weise10May12_20-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Weise10May12-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webpro15nov11_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-webpro15nov11-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

       </a>
      </sup>
      Zhitomirskiys mother, Inna Zhitomirskiy, said, "I strongly believe that if Ilya did not start this project and stayed in school, he would be well and alive today."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weise10May12_20-4">

     <p>
      In February 2012 the developers wrote that their own research indicated a change in the focus for the project. They stated that, unlike other social networking websites, on which users mostly interact with people they know in real life, on Diaspora users mostly interact with people from all over the world whom they do not know. Whereas traditional social media mostly deals with user's trivial daily details, much of the traffic on Diaspora deals with ideas and social causes. As a result the developers decided to make changes to the interface to better facilitate more lengthy and detailed conversations on complex subjects as the project progresses towards
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betaware" title="Betaware">
       beta status
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiasporaBlog03Feb12_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DiasporaBlog03Feb12-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In August 2012, the remaining founders formally handed the project over to its community.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora27Aug12_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora27Aug12-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since that date, Diaspora has been fully developed and managed by its community members. The focus of the community development team has been on creating stable software releases to act as a basis for further development, which included adopting a semantic versioning system for releases, improving the performance of data federation between pods, and enabling as many volunteers as possible to write code for the project. The project has also adopted the
      <a href="/wiki/Loomio" title="Loomio">
       Loomio
      </a>
      platform to enable democratic group decision-making.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaspora27Aug13_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Diaspora27Aug13-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
       Reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diaspora_(social_network)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      On September 14, 2011 Terry Hancock of
      <i>
       Free Software Magazine
      </i>
      endorsed the Diaspora network in an article entitled,
      <i>
       Why You Should Join Diaspora Now, Like Your Freedom Depends On It
      </i>
      , calling it "good enough" for mainstream use. In explaining his reasoning for encouraging people to sign up he stated:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hancock14Sep11_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hancock14Sep11-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       With all of the concerns over who controls the Social Web (Weve addressed some of these problems before in Free Software Magazine)  regarding the Google+ name policy and other privacy issues, Facebooks questionable ethics, and the overall danger of controlled networks. I think it is extremely important for a more decentralized, more democratic, more open, and more free solution to succeed in the interest of personal freedom on the internet. And it looks to me like Diaspora is an essential part of that solution, so Im endorsing it now, even though its not entirely ready.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hancock14Sep11_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hancock14Sep11-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      On November 14, 2011 Suw Charman-Anderson wrote in firstpost.com, in connection to Zhitomirskiy's death, about why Diaspora's slower growth can be an advantage:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CharmanAnderson14Nov11_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CharmanAnderson14Nov11-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       One key difference, however, is in number of users. Google+ has 40 million, whereas Diaspora has just 180,000 users, in part because the service is still in alpha testing. This might actually work to Diasporas advantage in the long run as it will have more time to build a sense of community. Experience shows us that online communities that grow too fast fragment and can become fractious as different groups clash over what kind of behaviour they think should be allowed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CharmanAnderson14Nov11_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CharmanAnderson14Nov11-38">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         38
         <span>
          ]
         </span>

       </sup>
      </p>
