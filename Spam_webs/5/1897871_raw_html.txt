     <p>
      The
      <b>
       North American Free Trade Agreement
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       NAFTA
      </b>
      ; Spanish:
      <i>
       Tratado de Libre Comercio de Amrica del Norte
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       TLCAN
      </i>
      ; French:
      <i>
       Accord de libre-change nord-amricain
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       ALNA
      </i>
      ) is an agreement signed by
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , creating a trilateral
      <a class="external text" href="https://www.nafta-sec-alena.org/Default.aspx?tabid=87&amp;language=en-US" rel="nofollow">
       rules-based
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Trade_bloc" title="Trade bloc">
       trade bloc
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
       North America
      </a>
      . The agreement came into force on January 1, 1994.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It superseded the
      <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="CanadaUnited States Free Trade Agreement">
       CanadaUnited States Free Trade Agreement
      </a>
      between the U.S. and Canada.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      NAFTA has two supplements: the
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_Agreement_on_Environmental_Cooperation" title="North American Agreement on Environmental Cooperation">
       North American Agreement on Environmental Cooperation
      </a>
      (NAAEC) and the North American Agreement on Labor Cooperation (NAALC).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiation_and_U.S._ratification">
       Negotiation and U.S. ratification
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nafta.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="481" data-file-width="715" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Nafta.jpg/270px-Nafta.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Nafta.jpg/405px-Nafta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Nafta.jpg/540px-Nafta.jpg 2x" width="270"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nafta.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Back row, left to right: Mexican President
        <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Salinas_de_Gortari" title="Carlos Salinas de Gortari">
         Carlos Salinas de Gortari
        </a>
        , U.S. President
        <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
         George H. W. Bush
        </a>
        , and Canadian Prime Minister
        <a href="/wiki/Brian_Mulroney" title="Brian Mulroney">
         Brian Mulroney
        </a>
        , at the initialing of the draft North American Free Trade Agreement in October 1992. In front are Mexican Secretary of Commerce and Industrial Development
        <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Serra_Puche" title="Jaime Serra Puche">
         Jaime Serra Puche
        </a>
        , United States Trade Representative
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carla_Hills" title="Carla Hills">
         Carla Hills
        </a>
        , and Canadian Minister of International Trade
        <a href="/wiki/Michael_Wilson_(Canadian_politician)" title="Michael Wilson (Canadian politician)">
         Michael Wilson
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Following diplomatic negotiations dating back to 1990 among the three nations, U.S. President
      <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
       George H. W. Bush
      </a>
      , Canadian Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Brian_Mulroney" title="Brian Mulroney">
       Brian Mulroney
      </a>
      and Mexican President
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlos_Salinas" title="Carlos Salinas">
       Carlos Salinas
      </a>
      , each responsible for spearheading and promoting the agreement, ceremonially signed the agreement in their respective capitals on December 17, 1992.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The signed agreement then needed to be
      <a href="/wiki/Ratification" title="Ratification">
       ratified
      </a>
      by each nation's legislative or parliamentary branch.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="CanadaUnited States Free Trade Agreement">
       CanadaUnited States Free Trade Agreement
      </a>
      had been very controversial and divisive in Canada, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1988" title="Canadian federal election, 1988">
       1988 Canadian election
      </a>
      was fought almost exclusively on that issue. In that election, more Canadians voted for anti-free trade parties (the
      <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">
       Liberals
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_(Canada)" title="New Democratic Party (Canada)">
       New Democrats
      </a>
      ) but the split caused more seats in parliament to be won by the pro-free trade
      <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Canada">
       Progressive Conservatives
      </a>
      (PCs). Mulroney and the PCs had a
      <a href="/wiki/Majority_government" title="Majority government">
       parliamentary majority
      </a>
      and were easily able to pass the 1987 Canada-U.S. FTA and NAFTA bills. However, he was replaced as Conservative leader and prime minister by
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Campbell" title="Kim Campbell">
       Kim Campbell
      </a>
      . Campbell led the PC party into the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1993_Canadian_election" title="1993 Canadian election">
       1993 election
      </a>
      where they were decimated by the Liberal Party under
      <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chr%C3%A9tien" title="Jean Chrtien">
       Jean Chrtien
      </a>
      , who had campaigned on a promise to renegotiate or abrogate NAFTA; however, Chrtien subsequently negotiated two supplemental agreements with the new U.S. president. In the US, Bush, who had worked to "fast track" the signing prior to the end of his term, ran out of time and had to pass the required ratification and signing of the implementation law to incoming president
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">
       Bill Clinton
      </a>
      . Prior to sending it to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">
       United States Senate
      </a>
      Clinton added two side agreements, The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Agreement_on_Labor_Cooperation" title="North American Agreement on Labor Cooperation">
       North American Agreement on Labor Cooperation
      </a>
      (NAALC) and the
      <a href="/wiki/North_American_Agreement_on_Environmental_Cooperation" title="North American Agreement on Environmental Cooperation">
       North American Agreement on Environmental Cooperation
      </a>
      (NAAEC), to protect workers and the environment, plus allay the concerns of many House members. It also required U.S. partners to adhere to environmental practices and regulations similar to its own.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After much consideration and emotional discussion, the House of Representatives passed the North American Free Trade Agreement Implementation Act on November 17, 1993, 234-200. The agreement's supporters included 132 Republicans and 102 Democrats. The bill passed the Senate on November 20, 1993, 61-38.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Senate supporters were 34 Republicans and 27 Democrats. Clinton signed it into law on December 8, 1993; the agreement went into effect on January 1, 1994.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Clinton, while signing the NAFTA bill, stated that "NAFTA means jobs. American jobs, and good-paying American jobs. If I didn't believe that, I wouldn't support this agreement."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Provisions">
       Provisions
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The goal of NAFTA was to eliminate barriers to trade and investment between the U.S., Canada and Mexico. The implementation of NAFTA on January 1, 1994 brought the immediate elimination of
      <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">
       tariffs
      </a>
      on more than one-half of Mexico's exports to the U.S. and more than one-third of U.S. exports to Mexico. Within 10 years of the implementation of the agreement, all U.S.-Mexico tariffs would be eliminated except for some U.S. agricultural exports to Mexico that were to be phased out within 15 years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most U.S.-Canada trade was already duty-free. NAFTA also seeks to eliminate non-tariff trade barriers and to protect the intellectual property right of the products.
     </p>
     <p>
      Chapter 52 provides a procedure for the interstate resolution of disputes over the application and interpretation of NAFTA. It was modeled after Chapter 69 of the Canada-United States Free Trade Agreement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Intellectual_Property">

      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The North American Free Trade Agreement Implementation Act made some changes to the
      <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_States" title="Copyright law of the United States">
       Copyright law of the United States
      </a>
      , foreshadowing the
      <a href="/wiki/Uruguay_Round_Agreements_Act" title="Uruguay Round Agreements Act">
       Uruguay Round Agreements Act
      </a>
      of 1994 by restoring copyright (within NAFTA) on certain motion pictures which had entered the public domain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">
       Environment
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Securing U.S. congressional approval for NAFTA would have been impossible without addressing public concerns about NAFTAs environmental impact. The Clinton administration negotiated a side agreement on the environment with Canada and Mexico, the North American Agreement on Environmental Cooperation (NAAEC), which led to the creation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Environmental_Cooperation" title="Commission for Environmental Cooperation">
       Commission for Environmental Cooperation
      </a>
      (CEC) in 1994. To alleviate concerns that NAFTA, the first regional trade agreement between a developing country and two developed countries, would have negative environmental impacts, the CEC was given a mandate to conduct ongoing
      <i>
       ex post
      </i>
      environmental assessment of NAFTA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In response to this mandate, the CEC created a framework for conducting environmental analysis of NAFTA, one of the first
      <i>
       ex post
      </i>
      frameworks for the environmental assessment of trade liberalization. The framework was designed to produce a focused and systematic body of evidence with respect to the initial hypotheses about NAFTA and the environment, such as the concern that NAFTA would create a "race to the bottom" in environmental regulation among the three countries, or the hope that NAFTA would pressure governments to increase their environmental protection mechanisms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The CEC has held four symposia using this framework to evaluate the environmental impacts of NAFTA and has commissioned 47 papers on this subject. In keeping with the CECs overall strategy of transparency and public involvement, the CEC commissioned these papers from leading independent experts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">
       Agriculture
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      From the earliest negotiation,
      <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
       agriculture
      </a>
      was (and still remains) a controversial topic within NAFTA, as it has been with almost all
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_trade_agreement" title="Free trade agreement">
       free trade agreements
      </a>
      that have been signed within the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">
       WTO
      </a>
      framework. Agriculture is the only section that was not negotiated trilaterally; instead, three separate agreements were signed between each pair of parties. The CanadaU.S. agreement contains significant restrictions and tariff quotas on agricultural products (mainly sugar, dairy, and poultry products), whereas the MexicoU.S. pact allows for a wider
      <a href="/wiki/Liberalization" title="Liberalization">
       liberalization
      </a>
      within a framework of phase-out periods (it was the first
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North-South_divide" title="North-South divide">
       NorthSouth
      </a>
      FTA on agriculture to be signed).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_infrastructure">
       Transportation infrastructure
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      NAFTA established the
      <a href="/wiki/CANAMEX_Corridor" title="CANAMEX Corridor">
       CANAMEX Corridor
      </a>
      for road transport between Canada and Mexico, also proposed for use by rail, pipeline, and fiber optic telecommunications infrastructure. This became a High Priority Corridor under the U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_Surface_Transportation_Efficiency_Act" title="Intermodal Surface Transportation Efficiency Act">
       Intermodal Surface Transportation Efficiency Act
      </a>
      of 1991.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">
       Canada
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Like Mexico and the U.S., Canada received a modest positive economic benefit as measured by GDP. Many feared declines failed to materialize, and some industries, like the furniture industry, were expected to suffer but grew instead. Canadian manufacturing employment held steady despite an international downward trend in developed countries. One of NAFTA's biggest economic effects on U.S.-Canada trade has been to boost bilateral agricultural flows.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the year 2008 alone, Canada exports to the United States and Mexico were at $381.3 billion, and imports from NAFTA were at $245.1 billion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A book written by
      <a href="/wiki/Mel_Hurtig" title="Mel Hurtig">
       Mel Hurtig
      </a>
      published in 2002 called
      <i>
       The Vanishing Country
      </i>
      charged that since NAFTA's ratification more than 10,000 Canadian companies had been taken over by foreigners, and that 98% of all foreign direct investments in Canada were for foreign takeovers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">
       Mexico
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Maquiladora" title="Maquiladora">
       Maquiladoras
      </a>
      (Mexican factories that take in imported raw materials and produce goods for export) have become the landmark of trade in Mexico. These are plants that moved to this region from the United States, hence the debate over the loss of American jobs. Hufbauer's (2005) book shows that income in the maquiladora sector has increased 15.5% since the implementation of NAFTA in 1994. Other sectors now benefit from the free trade agreement, and the share of
      <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">
       exports
      </a>
      from non-border states has increased in the last five years while the share of exports from maquiladora-border states has decreased. This has allowed for the rapid growth of non-border metropolitan areas, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Toluca" title="Toluca">
       Toluca
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n,_Guanajuato" title="Len, Guanajuato">
       Len
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Puebla,_Puebla" title="Puebla, Puebla">
       Puebla
      </a>
      ; all three larger in population than
      <a href="/wiki/Tijuana" title="Tijuana">
       Tijuana
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Ciudad Jurez">
       Ciudad Jurez
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Reynosa" title="Reynosa">
       Reynosa
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The overall effect of the MexicoU.S. agricultural agreement is a matter of dispute. Mexico did not invest in the infrastructure necessary for competition, such as efficient railroads and highways, which resulted in more difficult living conditions for the country's poor. Mexico's agricultural exports increased 9.4 percent annually between 1994 and 2001, while imports increased by only 6.9 percent a year during the same period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the most affected agricultural sectors is the meat industry. Mexico has gone from a small player in the pre-1994 U.S. export market to the 2nd largest importer of U.S. agricultural products in 2004, and NAFTA may be credited as a major catalyst for this change. The allowance of free trade removed the hurdles that impeded business between the two countries. As a result, Mexico has provided a growing market for meat for the U.S., leading to an increase in sales and profits for the U.S. meat industry. This coincides with a noticeable increase in Mexican per capita GDP that has created large changes in meat consumption patterns, implying that Mexicans can now afford to buy more meat and thus per capita meat consumption has grown.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Production of
      <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
       corn
      </a>
      in Mexico has increased since NAFTA's implementation. However, internal corn demand has increased beyond Mexico's sufficiency, and imports have become necessary, far beyond the quotas Mexico had originally negotiated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Zahniser &amp; Coyle have also pointed out that corn prices in Mexico, adjusted for international prices, have drastically decreased, yet through a program of subsidies expanded by former president
      <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Fox" title="Vicente Fox">
       Vicente Fox
      </a>
      , production has remained stable since 2000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
       United States
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The U.S. Chamber of Commerce credits NAFTA with increasing U.S. trade in goods and services with Canada and Mexico from $337 billion in 1993 to $1.2 trillion in 2011, while the AFL-CIO blames the agreement for sending 700,000 American manufacturing jobs to Mexico over that time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_balances">
       Trade balances
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The U.S. goods trade deficit with NAFTA was $94.6 billion in 2010, a 36.4% increase ($25 billion) over 2009.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The U.S. goods trade deficit with NAFTA accounted for 26.8% of the overall U.S. goods trade deficit in 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The U.S. had a services trade surplus of $28.3 billion with NAFTA countries in 2009 (the latest data available).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a study published in the August 2008 issue of the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/American_Journal_of_Agricultural_Economics" title="American Journal of Agricultural Economics">
        American Journal of Agricultural Economics
       </a>
      </i>
      , NAFTA has increased U.S. agricultural exports to Mexico and Canada even though most of this increase occurred a decade after its ratification. The study focused on the effects that gradual "phase-in" periods in regional trade agreements, including NAFTA, have on trade flows. Most of the increase in members agricultural trade, which was only recently brought under the purview of the World Trade Organization, was due to very high trade barriers before NAFTA or other regional trade agreements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Investment">
       Investment
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The U.S. foreign direct investment (FDI) in NAFTA countries (stock) was $327.5 billion in 2009 (latest data available), up 8.8% from 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The U.S. direct investment in NAFTA countries is in nonbank holding companies, and in the manufacturing, finance/insurance, and mining sectors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Foreign_direct_investment" title="Foreign direct investment">
       foreign direct investment
      </a>
      of Canada and Mexico in the United States (stock) was $237.2 billion in 2009 (the latest data available), up 16.5% from 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustrstats_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-ustrstats-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.fas.usda.gov/itp/policy/nafta/nafta.asp" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jobs">
       Jobs
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Many American small businesses depend on exporting their products to Canada or Mexico under NAFTA. According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_United_States_Trade_Representative" title="Office of the United States Trade Representative">
       U.S. Trade Representative
      </a>
      , this trade supports over 140,000 small and medium-sized businesses in the US.
      <a class="external autonumber" href="https://ustr.gov/trade-agreements/free-trade-agreements/north-american-free-trade-agreement-nafta" rel="nofollow">
       [2]
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_Policy_Institute" title="Economic Policy Institute">
       Economic Policy Institute
      </a>
      , some 700,000 jobs were lost as production was shifted to Mexico.
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
       Texas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">
       Michigan
      </a>
      and other states with high concentrations of manufacturing jobs were most affected by job loss due to NAFTA.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environment_2">
       Environment
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      For more details on this topic, see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAFTA%27s_Impact_on_the_Environment" title="NAFTA's Impact on the Environment">
       NAFTA's Impact on the Environment
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      Overall, none of the initial hypotheses were confirmed.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      NAFTA did not inherently present a systemic threat to the North American environment, as was originally feared. NAFTA-related environmental threats instead occurred in specific areas where government environmental policy, infrastructure, or mechanisms were unprepared for the increasing scale of production under trade liberalization.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In some cases, environmental policy was neglected in the wake of trade liberalization; in other cases, NAFTA's measures for investment protection, such as Chapter 11, and measures against non-tariff trade barriers threatened to discourage more vigorous environmental policy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The most serious overall increases in pollution due to NAFTA were found in the base metals sector, the Mexican petroleum sector, and the transportation equipment sector in the United States and Mexico, but not in Canada.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mobility_of_persons">
       Mobility of persons
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="Department of Homeland Security">
       Department of Homeland Security
      </a>
      Yearbook of Immigration Statistics, during fiscal year 2006 (i.e., October 2005 through September 2006), 73,880 foreign professionals (64,633 Canadians and 9,247 Mexicans) were admitted into the United States for temporary employment under NAFTA (i.e., in the
      <a href="/wiki/TN_status" title="TN status">
       TN status
      </a>
      ). Additionally, 17,321 of their family members (13,136 Canadians, 2,904 Mexicans, as well as a number of third-country nationals married to Canadians and Mexicans) entered the U.S. in the treaty national's dependent (TD) status.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because DHS counts the number of the new
      <a href="/wiki/Form_I-94" title="Form I-94">
       I-94 arrival records
      </a>
      filled at the border, and the TN-1 admission is valid for three years, the number of non-immigrants in TN status present in the U.S. at the end of the fiscal year is approximately equal to the number of admissions during the year. (A discrepancy may be caused by some TN entrants leaving the country or changing status before their three-year admission period has expired, while other immigrants admitted earlier may change their status
      <i>
       to
      </i>
      TN or TD, or extend TN status granted earlier).
     </p>
     <p>
      Canadian authorities estimated that, as of December 1, 2006, a total of 24,830 U.S. citizens and 15,219 Mexican citizens were present in Canada as "foreign workers". These numbers include both entrants under the NAFTA agreement and those who have entered under other provisions of the Canadian immigration law.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_Mexican_farmers">
       Impact on Mexican farmers
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 2000, U.S. government subsidies to the corn sector totalled $10.1 billion. These subsidies have led to charges of
      <a href="/wiki/Dumping_(pricing_policy)" title="Dumping (pricing policy)">
       dumping
      </a>
      , which jeopardizes Mexican farms and the country's food self-sufficiency.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other studies reject NAFTA as the force responsible for depressing the incomes of poor corn farmers, citing the trend's existence more than a decade before NAFTA's existence, an increase in maize production after NAFTA went into effect in 1994, and the lack of a measurable impact on the price of Mexican corn due to subsidized corn coming into Mexico from the United States, though they agree that the abolition of U.S. agricultural subsidies would benefit Mexican farmers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Graham Purchase in
      <i>
       Anarchism and Environmental Survival
      </i>
      , NAFTA could cause "the destruction of the ejidos (peasant cooperative village holdings) by corporate interests, and threatens to completely reverse the gains made by rural peoples in the Mexican Revolution."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>

        </span>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Zapatista_Uprising_in_response_to_NAFTA_in_Chiapas">
       Zapatista Uprising in response to NAFTA in Chiapas
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      For more details on this topic, see
      <a href="/wiki/Zapatista_Army_of_National_Liberation" title="Zapatista Army of National Liberation">
       Zapatista Army of National Liberation
      </a>

     <p>
      The preparations for NAFTA included cancellation of Article 27 of Mexico's constitution, the cornerstone of
      <a href="/wiki/Emiliano_Zapata" title="Emiliano Zapata">
       Emiliano Zapata
      </a>
      's revolution of 19101919. Under the historic Article 27, Indian communal landholdings were protected from sale or privatization. However, this barrier to investment was incompatible with NAFTA. With the removal of Article 27, Indian farmers feared the loss of their remaining lands, and also feared cheap imports (substitutes) from the US. Thus, the Zapatistas labeled NAFTA as a "death sentence" to Indian communities all over Mexico. Then
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EZLN" title="EZLN">
       EZLN
      </a>
      declared war on the Mexican state on January 1, 1994, the day NAFTA came into force.

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Investor_state_dispute_settlement" title="Investor state dispute settlement">

The tribunal based its decision namely on following reasoning: But as a matter of general international law, a non-discriminatory regulation for a public purpose, which is enacted in accordance with due process and, which affects, inter alios, a foreign investor or investment is not deemed expropriatory and compensable unless specific commitments had been given by the regulating government to the then putative foreign investor contemplating investment that the government would refrain from such regulation.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chapter_19">
       Chapter 19
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Also contentious is NAFTA's Chapter 19, which subjects
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antidumping" title="Antidumping">
       antidumping
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countervailing_duty" title="Countervailing duty">
       countervailing duty
      </a>
      (AD/CVD) determinations to binational panel review instead of, or in addition to, conventional judicial review. For example, in the United States, review of agency decisions imposing antidumping and countervailing duties are normally heard before the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_International_Trade" title="United States Court of International Trade">
       U.S. Court of International Trade
      </a>
      , an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Article_I_and_Article_III_tribunals" title="Article I and Article III tribunals">
       Article III court
      </a>
      . NAFTA parties, however, have the option of appealing the decisions to binational panels composed of five citizens from the two relevant NAFTA countries. The panelists are generally lawyers experienced in international trade law. Since the NAFTA does not include substantive provisions concerning AD/CVD, the panel is charged with determining whether final agency determinations involving AD/CVD conform with the country's domestic law. Chapter 19 can be considered as somewhat of an anomaly in international dispute settlement since it does not apply international law, but requires a panel composed of individuals from many countries to reexamine the application of one country's domestic law.
     </p>
     <p>
      A Chapter 19 panel is expected to examine whether the agency's determination is supported by "substantial evidence." This standard assumes significant deference to the domestic agency. Some of the most controversial trade disputes in recent years, such as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_%E2%80%93_Canada_softwood_lumber_dispute" title="United States  Canada softwood lumber dispute">
       U.S.-Canada softwood lumber dispute
      </a>
      , have been litigated before Chapter 19 panels.
     </p>
     <p>
      Decisions by Chapter 19 panels can be challenged before a NAFTA extraordinary challenge committee. However, an extraordinary challenge committee does not function as an ordinary appeal. Under the NAFTA, it will only vacate or remand a decision if the decision involves a significant and material error that threatens the integrity of the NAFTA dispute settlement system. Since January 2006, no NAFTA party has successfully challenged a Chapter 19 panel's decision before an extraordinary challenge committee.
     </p>
