     <p>
      A
      <b>
       valley
      </b>
      is a low area between hills, often with a river running through it.
     </p>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
       geology
      </a>
      , a
      <b>
       valley
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dale_(origin)" title="Dale (origin)">
        dale
       </a>
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">
       depression
      </a>
      that is longer than it is wide. The terms
      <a href="/wiki/U-shaped_valley" title="U-shaped valley">
       U-shaped
      </a>
      and V-shaped are descriptive terms of
      <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
       geography
      </a>
      to characterize the form of valleys. Most valleys belong to one of these two main types or a mixture of them, (at least) with respect of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">
       cross section
      </a>
      of the slopes or hillsides.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Valley_terminology">
       Valley terminology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Valley terminology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A valley in its broadest geographic sense is also known as a
      <i>
       dale
      </i>
      . A valley through which a river runs may also be referred to as a
      <b>
       vale
      </b>
      . A small, secluded, and often wooded valley is known as a
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Dell_(landform)" title="Dell (landform)">
        dell
       </a>
      </b>
      or in Scotland as a
      <b>
       glen
      </b>
      . A wide, flat valley through which a river runs is known in Scotland as a
      <b>
       strath
      </b>
      . A
      <a href="/wiki/Cove_(Appalachian_Mountains)" title="Cove (Appalachian Mountains)">
       mountain
       <b>
        cove
       </b>
      </a>
      is a small valley, closed at one or both ends, in the central or southern
      <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">
       Appalachian Mountains
      </a>
      which sometimes results from the erosion of a
      <a href="/wiki/Cove_(Appalachian_Mountains)" title="Cove (Appalachian Mountains)">
       geologic window
      </a>
      . A small valley surrounded by mountains or
      <a href="/wiki/Ridge" title="Ridge">
       ridges
      </a>
      is sometimes known as a
      <b>
       hollow
      </b>
      . A deep, narrow valley is known as a
      <b>
       cwm
      </b>
      (also spelled
      <i>
       combe
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       coombe
      </i>
      ). Similar geological structures, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">
       canyons
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ravine" title="Ravine">
       ravines
      </a>
      , gorges,
      <a href="/wiki/Gully" title="Gully">
       gullies
      </a>
      , and kloofs, are not usually referred to as valleys. See also: "
      <a href="/wiki/Chine" title="Chine">
       chine
      </a>
      ". A valley formed by
      <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
       erosion
      </a>
      is called an erosional valley; a valley formed by geologic events such as drop faults or the rise of highlands is called a structural valley.

      <a href="/wiki/River_phenomenon" title="River phenomenon">
       River phenomenon
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A valley formed by
      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       flowing water
      </a>
      , or
      <i>
       river valley
      </i>
      , is usually
      <b>
       V-shaped
      </b>
      . The exact shape will depend on the characteristics of the stream flowing through it. Rivers with steep gradients, as in
      <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">
       mountain ranges
      </a>
      , produce steep walls and a bottom. Shallower slopes may produce broader and gentler valleys. However, in the lowest stretch of a river, where it approaches its
      <a href="/wiki/Base_level" title="Base level">
       base level
      </a>
      , it begins to deposit sediment and the valley bottom becomes a
      <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">
       floodplain
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Some broad
      <i>
       V
      </i>
      examples are:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Canyon_of_the_Gunnison_National_Park" title="Black Canyon of the Gunnison National Park">
        Black Canyon of the Gunnison National Park
       </a>
       , and others in
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_National_Park" title="Grand Canyon National Park">
        Grand Canyon NP
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       :
       <ul>
        <li>
         Austria: narrow passages of upper
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inn_valley" title="Inn valley">
          Inn valley
         </a>
         (Inntal), affluents of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enns_River" title="Enns River">
          Enns
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         Switzerland:
         <a href="/wiki/Napf" title="Napf">
          Napf
         </a>
         region, Zurich Oberland,
         <a href="/wiki/Engadin" title="Engadin">
          Engadin
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         Germany: affluents to the middle reaches of
         <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">
          Rhine
         </a>
         and
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moselle_River" title="Moselle River">
          Mosel
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Some of the first human
      <a href="/wiki/Complex_society" title="Complex society">
       complex societies
      </a>
      originated in river valleys, such as that of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">
       Nile
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigris-Euphrates" title="Tigris-Euphrates">
       Tigris-Euphrates
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">
       Indus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">
       Ganges
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">
       Yangtze
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huang_He" title="Huang He">
       Huang He
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">
       Mississippi
      </a>
      , and arguably
      <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">
       Amazon
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">
       prehistory
      </a>
      , the rivers were used as a source of
      <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">
       fresh water
      </a>
      and food (fish and game), as well as a place to wash and a sewer. The proximity of water moderated
      <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
       temperature
      </a>
      extremes and provided a source for
      <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">
       irrigation
      </a>
      , stimulating the development of
      <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
       agriculture
      </a>
      . Most of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_civilization" title="Cradle of civilization">
       first civilizations
      </a>
      developed from these river valley communities. Another word for river valley is River phenomenon.
     </p>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
       geography
      </a>
      , a
      <i>
       vale
      </i>
      is a wide river valley, usually with a particularly wide
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_plain" title="Flood plain">
       flood plain
      </a>
      or flat valley bottom.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Southern England, vales commonly occur between the
      <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">
       escarpment
      </a>
      slopes of pairs of
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_England_Chalk_Formation" title="Southern England Chalk Formation">
       chalk formations
      </a>
      , where the chalk dome has been
      <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
       eroded
      </a>
      , exposing less
      <a href="/wiki/Geological_resistance" title="Geological resistance">
       resistant
      </a>
      underlying rock, usually
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claystone" title="Claystone">
       claystone
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      There are various forms of valley associated with glaciation that may be referred to as glacial valleys.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="U-shaped_valley">
       U-shaped valley
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: U-shaped valley">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/U-shaped_valley" title="U-shaped valley">
       U-shaped valley
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A valley carved by
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
       glaciers
      </a>
      is normally U-shaped. The valley becomes visible upon the recession of the glacier that forms it. When the ice recedes or thaws, the valley remains, often littered with small boulders that were transported within the ice. Floor gradient does not affect the valley's shape, it is the glacier's size that does. Continuously flowing glaciers  especially in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
       ice age
      </a>
       and large-sized glaciers carve wide, deep incised valleys.
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples of U-shaped valleys are found in every mountainous region that has experienced
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">
       glaciation
      </a>
      , usually during the
      <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">
       Pleistocene
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">
       ice ages
      </a>
      . Most present U-shaped valleys started as V-shaped before glaciation. The glaciers carved it out wider and deeper, simultaneously changing the shape. This proceeds through the glacial erosion processes of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">
       glaciation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(geology)" title="Abrasion (geology)">
       abrasion
      </a>
      , which results in large rocky material (glacial till) being carried in the glacier. A material called boulder clay is deposited on the floor of the valley. As the ice melts and retreats, the valley is left with very steep sides and a wide, flat floor. A
      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       river
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">
       stream
      </a>
      may remain in the valley. This replaces the original stream or river and is known as a
      <a href="/wiki/Misfit_stream" title="Misfit stream">
       misfit stream
      </a>
      because it is smaller than one would expect given the size of its valley.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other interesting glacially carved valleys include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Valley" title="Yosemite Valley">
        Yosemite Valley
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA" title="USA">
        USA
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Side_valley" title="Side valley">
        Side valleys
       </a>
       of the Austrian river
       <a href="/wiki/Salzach" title="Salzach">
        Salzach
       </a>
       for their parallel directions and hanging mouths.
      </li>
      <li>
       Some Scottish glens full with bushes and flowers.
      </li>
      <li>
       That of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Mary_River_(Montana-Alberta)" title="St. Mary River (Montana-Alberta)">
        St. Mary River
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(United_States)" title="Glacier National Park (United States)">
        Glacier National Park
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">
        Montana
       </a>
       , USA.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tunnel_valley">
       Tunnel valley
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tunnel valley">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_valley" title="Tunnel valley">
       Tunnel valley
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A tunnel valley is a large, long, U-shaped valley originally cut under the glacial ice near the margin of continental ice sheets such as that now covering Antarctica and formerly covering portions of all continents during past glacial ages.[1]
     </p>
     <p>
      A tunnel valley can be up to 100km (62mi) long, 4km (2.5mi) wide, and 400 m (1,300ft) deep (its depth may vary along its length).
     </p>
     <p>
      Tunnel valleys were formed by subglacial erosion by water. They served as subglacial drainage pathways carrying large volumes of melt water. Their cross-sections exhibit steep-sided flanks similar to fjord walls, and their flat bottoms are typical of subglacial glacial erosion.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meltwater_valley">
       Meltwater valley
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Meltwater valley">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Urstromtal" title="Urstromtal">
       Urstromtal
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      In northern Central Europe, the Scandinavian ice sheet during the various ice ages advanced slightly uphill against the lie of the land. As a result, its meltwaters flowed parallel to the ice margin to reach the North Sea basin, forming huge, flat valleys known as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urstromt%C3%A4ler" title="Urstromtler">
        Urstromtler
       </a>
      </i>
      . Unlike the other forms of glacial valley, these were formed by glacial meltwaters.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_forms_and_valley_shoulders">
       Transition forms and valley shoulders
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transition forms and valley shoulders">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="607" data-file-width="2000" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg/300px-Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg/450px-Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg/600px-Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paria_View_at_Bryce_Canyon_NP.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Look from Paria View to a valley in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryce_Canyon" title="Bryce Canyon">
         Bryce Canyon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">
         Utah
        </a>
        , with very striking shoulders
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Depending on the
      <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">
       topography
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_type" title="Rock type">
       rock types
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
       climate
      </a>
      , a lot of transitional forms between V-, U- and plain valleys exist. Their bottoms can be broad or narrow, but characteristic is also the type of valley shoulder. The broader a mountain valley, the lower its shoulders are located in most cases. An important exception are canyons where the shoulder almost is near the top of the valley's slope. In the Alps  e.g. the
      <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">
       Tyrolean
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inn_River" title="Inn River">
       Inn
      </a>
      valley  the shoulders are quite low (100200 meters above the bottom). Many
      <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">
       villages
      </a>
      are located here (esp. at the sunny side) because the
      <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
       climate
      </a>
      is very mild: even in winter when the valley's floor is completely filled with fog, these villages are in
      <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
       sunshine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In some stress-tectonic regions of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocky_mountains" title="Rocky mountains">
       Rockies
      </a>
      or the Alps (e.g.
      <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">
       Salzburg
      </a>
      ) the
      <a href="/wiki/Side_valley" title="Side valley">
       side valleys
      </a>
      are parallel to each other, and additionally they are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanging_valleys" title="Hanging valleys">
       hanging
      </a>
      . The brooks flow into the
      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       river
      </a>
      in form of deep canyons or
      <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">
       waterfalls
      </a>
      . Usually this fact is the result of a violent erosion of the former valley shoulders. A special genesis we find also at
      <a href="/wiki/Ar%C3%AAte" title="Arte">
       artes
      </a>
      and glacial
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cirque_(landform)" title="Cirque (landform)">
       cirques
      </a>
      , at every Scottish glen, or a northern
      <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">
       fjord
      </a>
      .
     </p>

        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Closeup_of_Bridalveil_Fall_seen_from_Tunnel_View_in_Yosemite_NP.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Bridal Veil Falls in
        <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">
         Yosemite National Park
        </a>
        flowing from a hanging valley.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       hanging valley
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">
       tributary
      </a>
      valley that is higher than the main valley. They are most commonly associated with U-shaped valleys when a tributary
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
       glacier
      </a>
      flows into a glacier of larger volume. The main glacier erodes a deep U-shaped valley with nearly vertical sides while the tributary glacier, with a smaller volume of ice, makes a shallower U-shaped valley. Since the surfaces of the glaciers were originally at the same
      <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
       elevation
      </a>
      , the shallower valley appears to be 'hanging' above the main valley. Often,
      <a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">
       waterfalls
      </a>
      form at or near the outlet of the upper valley.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Glaciated terrain is not the only site of hanging streams and valleys. Hanging valleys are also simply the product of varying rates of erosion of the main valley and the tributary valleys. The varying rates of erosion are associated with the composition of the adjacent rocks in the different valley locations. The tributary valleys are eroded and deepened by glaciers or erosion at a slower rate than that of the main valley floor, thus the difference in the two valleys' depth increases over time. The tributary valley composed of more resistant rock then hangs over the main valley.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Valley_floors">
       Valley floors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Valley floors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Usually the bottom of a main valley is broad  independent of the U or V shape. It typically ranges from about one to ten kilometers in width and is commonly filled with mountain
      <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">
       sediments
      </a>
      . The shape of the floor can be rather
      <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_plane" title="Horizontal plane">
       horizontal
      </a>
      , similar to a flat
      <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)" title="Cylinder (geometry)">
       cylinder
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Fluvial_terrace" title="Fluvial terrace">
       terraced
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Side_valley" title="Side valley">
       Side valleys
      </a>
      are rather V than U-shaped; near the mouth waterfalls are possible if it is a hanging valley. The location of the
      <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">
       villages
      </a>
      depends on the across-valley profile, on
      <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
       climate
      </a>
      and local traditions, and on the danger of
      <a href="/wiki/Avalanche" title="Avalanche">
       avalanches
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">
       landslides
      </a>
      . Predominant are places on terraces or
      <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">
       alluvial fans
      </a>
      if they exist.
     </p>
     <p>
      Historic siting of villages within the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainstem_(hydrology)" title="Mainstem (hydrology)">
       mainstem
      </a>
      valleys, however, have chiefly considered the potential of
      <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">
       flooding
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">
       stream bed
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Ozarks" title="Ozarks">
       Ozarks
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">
       Arkansas
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">
       Missouri
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">
       river
      </a>
      valleys, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mansfield_Hollow" title="Mansfield Hollow">
       Mansfield Hollow
      </a>
      , or they may be relatively dry clefts with a notch-like characteristic in that they have a height of land and consequent
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_divide" title="Water divide">
       water divide
      </a>
      in their bases.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rift_valleys">
       Rift valleys
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rift valleys">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">
       Rift valley
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Rift valleys, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Albertine_Rift" title="Albertine Rift">
       Albertine Rift
      </a>
      , are formed by the expansion of the
      <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
       Earth
      </a>
      's crust due to
      <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
       tectonic
      </a>
      activity beneath the Earth's surface.
     </p>
