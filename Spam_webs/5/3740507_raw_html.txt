      <h2 class="page-name">
       San Diegans Petition to Free Orca Corky at SeaWorld
      </h2>
      <div class="blog-meta">
       January 9, 2015 by Mark Palmer, Save Japan Dolphins
      </div>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:16px;">
        <strong>
         By Mark J. Palmer
         <br/>
         Associate Director
         <br/>
         International Marine Mammal Project
         <br/>
         Earth Island Institute
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        Even SeaWorlds hometown of San Diego, one of Californias most conservative cities, is showing concern about SeaWorlds treatment of their captive orcas. The Blackfish effect, named after the shocking documentary
        <em>
         Blackfish
        </em>
        , is causing many citizens to question the unquestioning support of SeaWorld that has dominated local government.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        On December 9th, a group of citizens led by Martha Sullivan presented 1,600 signatures to the office of Mayor Kevin Faulconer of San Diego, urging the Mayor and the city of San Diego to support retiring the captive orca Corky at SeaWorld San Diego into a sea pen.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        Sullivan told the local media: "It is time to retire her, just as other performing animals are retired to a sanctuary to live out the rest of their lives in some semblance of their natural (habitat), and without having to perform, without having to be subjected to loud noise and constant stress of living in a complete unnatural environment."
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        Corky has been in captivity for 45 years, and like all of SeaWorlds orcas, she must perform the same tricks daily, over and over again, for food rewards. Activists have hopes that Corky could be restored to her home waters in British Columbia, where she was captured.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        This past year has seen a resurgence of energy directed towards giving Corky a chance to retire to an ocean sanctuary, where she would continue to be cared for by humans and also be able to meet and interact with her kin and community, writes our colleague, Paul Spong of Orcalab in British Columbia, who has long sought to free Corky and return her to her home pod. The location is Blackfish Sound in British Columbia, Canada. Corkys family visits regularly each summer, and even occasionally during the winter. Its a place where SeaWorld can establish a facility to care for Corky, bringing staff who know her well, and incorporating a live video/audio link to their home base in San Diego. Though still confined in her new home, Corky could meet her kin again. It would require SeaWorld to come clean about who Corky is and where she comes from, that she has family, even a brother and sister though she has never met them, and become involved in the future of the marine park industry. That future does not include performing prisoners.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        SeaWorld, unfortunately, continues to contend that Corky is happy and that the delegation of citizens is an extreme fringe of people. SeaWorld continues a PR blitz in denial of their own problems.
        <em>
         Blackfish
        </em>
        was not based on commentary from extreme animal rights groups, but on statements from former SeaWorld trainers who question the treatment of orcas in captivity.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">
        SeaWorld overall value has plummeted on the stock market in response to the documentarys scathing expose of SeaWorlds orca husbandry. Attendance is down at all three parks, with attendant decline in revenues. Yet still SeaWorld is lashing out at critics with a shoot the messenger mentality that only serves to underline their fanatic clinging to their circus business model.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span style="font-size:14px;">

          Sign the petition!
         </a>
        </strong>
       </span>
      </p>
