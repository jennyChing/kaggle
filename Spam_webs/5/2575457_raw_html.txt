Chicago\u003C\/p\u003E\u003C\/div\u003E\u003Cdiv class=\u0022field-credit\u0022\u003E\u003Cp\u003E\u003Ca week saw two nearly simultaneous infrastructure failures in major industries: finance and transportation.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EOn Wednesday, July 8th, the New York Stock Exchange abruptly went down for a big chunk of the trading day. Suspicions of a cyber attack erupted almost immediately after the exchange went dark, but the NYSE denied this, and later clarified that the problem had resulted from \u003Ca href=\u0022https:\/\/www.nyse.com\/market-status\/history\u0022\u003Egateway software compatibility\u003C\/a\u003E.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe same day \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/abcnews.go.com\/Travel\/united-airlines-flights-grounded-nationwide-automation-issues-faa\/story?id=32297190\u0022\u003EUnited Airlines experienced a network crash\u003C\/a\u003E due to what they say was a \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/mashable.com\/2015\/07\/08\/united-computer-problems-flights-grounded\/\u0022\u003Efaulty router connection\u003C\/a\u003E that degraded network connectivity. After 59 cancelled flights, the network was \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/money.cnn.com\/2015\/07\/08\/news\/companies\/united-flights-grounded-computer\/\u0022\u003Emostly back online\u003C\/a\u003E.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EBoth were back online within a matter of hours, and while some damage was done the majority of people went on about their lives without problem. But the frequency of these episodes is increasing as networks become more complicated and as we rely more on them for day-to-day life.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Ch2\u003EA New Type of Natural Disaster?\u003C\/h2\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThere\u2019s an argument to be made that network outages are becoming the world\u2019s most frequent natural disaster: while the results are more often inconvenience than destruction, they\u2019re complicated to fix, and affect telecommunications, service providers, transportation, finance, and sometimes even medical devices.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003ESo what\u2019s causing the problems?\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EDavid Erickson of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.forwardnetworks.com\/\u0022\u003EForward Networks\u003C\/a\u003E, a startup focused on bringing more computer science practices into networking, says the problem is more than just human error: it\u2019s an increasingly complex and uncoordinated system of hardware and languages. \u201cYou\u2019ve now got organizations that have thousands or tens of thousands of devices that are moving packets: routers, switchers, firewalls--you name it,\u201d he tells \u003Cem\u003EPopular Science,\u003C\/em\u003E \u201cand each of these things has upwards of between 1,000 and 1,000,000 or more rules that actually define the behavior of how what it does with packets as they come in and out.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThose things can be taught to play nice together, but Erickson says it\u2019s a steep learning curve. \u201cThe net problem is that it\u2019s primarily humans that are having to install, roll these things forward, fix them, evolve them, everything. and it\u2019s no surprise at all that one misconfiguration can pretty easily bring down major critical systems, which is what you saw with United.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe problem increases with each passing year. Erickson says that over time, these devices \u201chave become more and more complex, and you have more and more of them, they\u2019re individually more and more complex, and there\u2019s continually new software demands being placed on them.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EErickson explains that many companies don\u2019t realize that major chunks of their company and their operating systems are \u201cjust a couple of software misconfigurations away from being turned off or unavailable.\u201d And it\u2019s not just a time-and-money quandary when things go down. He pointed to a \u003Ca href=\u0022https:\/\/www.reddit.com\/r\/networking\/comments\/3cme3b\/a_route_leaking_misconfig_took_united_airlines\/\u0022\u003Esubreddit about network issues\u003C\/a\u003E where a user offered an anecdote of neonatal heart monitors being configured on a network that was not functioning.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Ch2\u003EMo Complexity, Mo Problems\u003C\/h2\u003E\u003Cp\u003EPart of the problem comes from the fact that networks are a relatively new infrastructure. There are more safeguards for utilities like power and water, but they don\u2019t exist for networking.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAnd they get worse with age not just because of new complexities, but the sudden appearance of old problems.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EEric Hunsader, of \u003Ca href=\u0022http:\/\/www.nanex.net\/\u0022\u003ENanex, LLC\u003C\/a\u003E, a company that makes stock market information software. Hunsader has been in software development for the financial industry since 1986, and Nanex processes market data of stocks, options, futures and everything trading in the U.S. and worldwide. He explains that problems present from the start can take time to make themselves known. \u201cAs your product matures, after a while the only bugs left are the ones that nobody foresaw, and they tend to be the real difficult ones to figure out. So if technology is more complex with fewer errors, the few errors are significant.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EFor the NYSE, shutdowns aren\u2019t necessarily a problem, but bad timing can create a nightmare. \u201cThe nightmare is it happening one second before close,\u201d says Hunsader. \u201cThe best time for something to cause trouble is 15:59:59. The problem is that so much of the system depends on those closing prices. You would back up everything.\u201d He says that trades and options would have to be rolled back, and an error in the last few minutes of the day could cause the market not to open the next morning.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe good news is that things like the stock market, which are frequently affected by panic, aren\u2019t affected by network issues most of the time. Hunsader says it doesn\u2019t have any psychological impact on the market. If it had been an attack, well, \u201cI think it would be all the difference, because we\u2019d all be thinking if they can take a server out, maybe they can do it again. Or even worse, to be able to do it without being detected or... the smart thing would have been to rake the system for money.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAirlines are a little more sensitive: a down network means down planes, which can ruin everyone\u2019s holiday weekends and cost millions in a matter of hours. But these problems aren\u2019t so simple as someone kicking a power cord out of the back of a Netgear product.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Ch2\u003ETesting, Testing...\u003C\/h2\u003E\u003Cp\u003ERight now there\u2019s not really a technology in place that lets network experts test configurations in a vacuum. Erickson says changes are planned ahead of time, with group consensus being the only reliable estimate of what\u2019s going to happen. Once it goes live, testing new system arrangements (like with the NYSE or United) is a race against time. \u201cIf you mis-configure a device that happens to be the core device at that moment in time, it doesn\u2019t matter how much redundancy you have.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe lack of a standardized language or set of practices means that experts are in demand. Erickson says to be a network\u2019s perfect manager, \u201cyou\u2019d need to be able to understand all of the devices you\u2019ve got in your network. There are tons of vendors, hundreds if not thousands of devices.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EIs there someone trying to standardize these tools? Not really. competitors have no impetus to collaborate when they can earn more from beating one another on innovation. But the reality of the market is that companies aren\u2019t often replacing every unit every time there\u2019s an upgrade, so legacy systems and legacy software will always be a problem.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EErickson says that in the absence of customers saying, \u201cwe need some sort of unifying standard here so we can have confidence our network is doing what we expect it to do all the time,\u201d then it\u2019s just not going to happen.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAnd even if there was desire, it\u2019s a huge undertaking. \u201cFor a company to solve this,\u201d Erickson explains, \u201cthey have to then go out and talk to every one of these devices and understand them extremely well,\u201d he says. \u201cAnd that\u2019s just really               <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2012/02/20111122_337_LOWLINE-slide-1LYQ-articleLarge.jpg?itok=ClJGPhpk" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2012/02/20111122_337_LOWLINE-slide-1LYQ-articleLarge.jpg?itok=jvgMUabB" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2012/02/20111122_337_LOWLINE-slide-1LYQ-articleLarge.jpg?itok=elvRot3G" height="583" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>

              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Delancey Underground
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                Raad Studio
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              We wrote about the LowLine park concept
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/science/gallery/2011-12/images-week-december-5-9-2011">
               a couple months back
              </a>
              , but at the time it was just a crazy design for a futuristic underground public space in Manhattan's Lower East Side. Now,
              <a href="http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/855802805/lowline-an-underground-park-on-nycs-lower-east-sid">
               it's on Kickstarter
              </a>
              , and the project's creators are making some serious headway in actually getting construction moving. They're asking for $100,000 to build and display a working demo to show that their blend of solar tech and natural sunlight could actually make an appealing park below street level. Check out the project over at
              <a href="http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/855802805/lowline-an-underground-park-on-nycs-lower-east-sid">
               Kickstarter
              </a>
              .
