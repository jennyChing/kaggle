        <h1>
         Making a Sacrifice in a Relationship Isn't a Bad Thing. Unless You Do This...
        </h1>
        <span class="g-byline">
         By
         <a href="">
          Eliza Krpoyan
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="g-timestamp">
         July 17, 2014
        </span>
        <div class="g-social-container">
         <div class="g-sharrre-tools">
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="g-article-body">
        <p>
         Have you ever had to quit your job, move far away, or make some similar sacrifice to be with a boyfriend? Maybe you gave your cat away because your guy had terrible allergies? Well, if you have ever had to make a sacrifice like that, you know it comes with lots of mixed feelings. And how you handle those feelings is apparently what can make or break your relationship.
         <br/>
         <img alt="couple-packing-moving" data-dam-id="2000000002680769" src="http://www.glamour.com/images/sex-love-life/2014/03/couple-packing-moving-w352.jpg"/>
         <br/>
         Think about what happened after the last time you did something you didn't really want to do for a guy. Did you suppress your emotions for the good of the relationship? Did you downplay how upset the sacrifice really made you? In
         <a href="http://wjh.harvard.edu/~oveis/publications/impett.2014.pdf">
          this new study
         </a>
         , researchers examined what really happens to relationships when one partner sacrifices something. And the results are worth knowing:
        </p>
        <p>
         First, if you're the type of person who's OK with making sacrifices, you are probably happy in your relationship: People who are more willing to sacrifice for an intimate partner are more satisfied with their relationships," the researchers confirmed. In addition, if you're the partner of that person, and you notice that person happily sacrificing for you, it makes you more committed: "Perceiving a romantic partner [to] engage in costly actions for the relationship increases peoples own commitment. But! Here's where it can go wrong...
        </p>
        <p>
         In the cases where people grudgingly made sacrifices and then suppressed how upset they felt about it, relationships didn't really benefit from the sacrifices made. Why? Because their partner sensed they were silently stewing about it and in turn felt like the effort was less authentic. Translation: The deed isn't worth it if he can tell you're bitter about doing it.
        </p>
        <p>
         OK, lets examine. Say you move to another state to be with your sweetie (yay!), but you still have feelings of regret (why couldn't he come
         <em>
          here
         </em>
         instead?). Youre left with the decision to either tell your guy how you feel or suppress these feelings in an attempt to not ruin the experience. Well, while the intentions to suppress these feelings
         <em>
          are
         </em>
         good, by doing so youre not being honest with your partneror fair to yourself. So the moral of
         <a href="http://wjh.harvard.edu/~oveis/publications/impett.2014.pdf">
          the study
         </a>
         is that if you really aren't happy with a sacrifice, either don't make it to begin with or be open about it from the start.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Does that make sense to you? And have you ever made a sacrifice for a relationship? How did it turn out?
         </strong>
         <br/>
        </p>
