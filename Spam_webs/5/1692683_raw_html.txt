       <h1 itemprop="headline name">
        Discover what makes Ireland so special
       </h1>
       <h2 itemprop="alternativeHeadline description">
        Autumn is the perfect time of year to take your car to Irelands east coast 
  and discover a region thats rich in heritage
       </h2>

          <p class="bylineBody">
           <span itemprop="author">
            By Kevin Coyd
           </span>
          </p>
         </div>
         <p class="publishedDate">
          4:11PM BST 11 Aug 2014
         </p>
         <meta content="2014-08-11" itemprop="datePublished"/>
         <div class="cl">
         </div>
        </div>
        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           One of the most evocative Irish proverbs tells us that autumn days come quickly, like the running of a hound on the moor. Some may see a hint of melancholy in this but as far as Im concerned, autumn in
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/sponsored/travel/visit-ireland/">
            Ireland
           </a>
           cant come quickly enough.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           Its a season for scuffing through fallen leaves on long woodland walks, watching the shadows lengthen over mountains and meadows and enjoying cosy twilight dinners before a turf fire.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           Its a great time of year to take a motoring tour of Irelands heritage-rich east.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           Thanks to ferry connections at Dublin, Dun Laoghaire and Rosslare, its easy to bring your own car over from Great Britain. Ive always felt that my holiday begins the moment I engage the handbrake and go up on deck to look out over the Irish Sea.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           Ive a real soft spot for Wexford. The countys north coast has become famous for its place in a fictionalised history. Its beaches, many of which have been awarded Blue Flag status for their cleanliness, doubled for those of Normandy in Steven Spielbergs
           <i>
            Saving Private Ryan
           </i>
           . The seven-mile-long sands of Curracloe Strand, which represented Omaha Beach, is arguably the most picturesque.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class=" related_links_inline" id="tmg-related-links">
          <div class="headerOne styleTwo">

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           In the autumn, Ive ambled along the beach and dunes with hardly another soul in sight, watching the birds on the breeze and stopping to swap tales with the occasional dog-walker.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img align="center" alttext="Kilkenny Castle, Ireland" caption="Medieval marvel: Kilkenny Castle" declared-caption="" height="287" name="Kilkenny-Castle" photographer="" refid="3002777" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03002/Kilkenny-Castle_3002777c.jpg" version="c" width="460"/>
           <sup>
            <strong>
             Medieval marvel: Kilkenny Castle
            </strong>
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           The town of Wexford is an ancient county seat with an eye to the future. Its striking Opera House, completed in 2008, is the envy of Ireland. Whats more, its construction has delivered a boost to the Wexford Festival Opera, which has been taking place each autumn since 1951.
          </p>
          <p>
           Highlights of this years programme are the rarely performed opera based on Oscar Wildes
           <i>
            Salom
           </i>
           , composed by Antoine Mariotte (October 22, 25, 28 and 31); and the European premiere of
           <i>
            Silent Night
           </i>
           by Kevin Puts (October 24, 27, 30 and November 2). Its a timely choice, being based on the Christmas truce in the First World War trenches almost 100 years ago.
          </p>
          <p>
           Wexfords own heritage is a particularly rich one. The town has been taken by force of arms many times and has been controlled by Viking raiders, Normans and Oliver Cromwells forces. Theres a lot of history to cram into a single visit but Id recommend a look around the 12th-century Selskar Abbey, abutting a part of the old city walls. Tradition holds that this was where King Henry II came to do penance for the murder of Thomas  Becket, though the legend has proved impossible to confirm.
          </p>
          <p>
           The south of the county is not short of fine beaches but before making for the coast, the Irish National Heritage Park warrants a detour.
          </p>
          <p>
           Irelands largest open-air museum brings 9,000 years of history to life with sectors devoted to prehistoric Ireland, early Christian Ireland and the Age of Invasion. Reconstructions range from a Stone Age campsite and megalithic tomb to a Viking harbour and Celtic monastery. Not all the exhibits are modern: the Norman ditch fortifications date back more than 800 years.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Heritage Park really helps to set in context some of the historic sites you will see as you travel about.
          </p>
          <p>
           Kilmore Quay is delightful. Though its marina is a magnet for shiny pleasure craft, I found that the village still has the feel of an authentic fishing port. Its well worth staying overnight  I enjoyed talking to yachters and fishermen in its pubs who were keen to explain why they loved this stretch of coast where the Irish Sea meets the Atlantic.
          </p>
          <p>
           A small group of birdwatchers were bound for the Saltee Islands, just three miles offshore yet as wild and remote as you could wish for. They told me they were hoping to see not just the migratory flocks on what is Irelands largest seabird colony, but also the grey seals that number more than 100 in autumn. An excellent reason to return
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Panoramas and potters
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           A scenic drive westward brings you to the magnificent Hook Peninsula, a place of desolate beauty and also one of Irelands sunniest spots. The 12th-century lighthouse at Hook Head feels as though it sits at the end of the Earth rather than being an aid to navigation.
          </p>
          <p>
           Though its the oldest working lighthouse in Ireland (and one of the oldest in the world), its open to visitors. I puffed up the 115 steps with a guide who told me the local history and was rewarded with a wraparound panorama. The peninsula is sprinkled with castles, abbeys and other old stone buildings which makes following the various walking and cycling trails even more tempting.
          </p>
          <p>
           I couldnt help but keep an eye on the sea for whales, which are occasionally spotted from this vantage point. They proved elusive but, for me, the view was more than enough. Inland to the north, the rolling countryside of Kilkenny is ideal driving terrain and there are many different tangents to follow.
          </p>
          <p>
           You can eat and drink your way around the area on a food trail, for instance, dropping in on organic orchards, farmhouse cheese producers and old breweries (such as St Francis Abbey, which has been producing beer for three centuries).
          </p>
          <p>
           The much-anticipated Smithwicks Experience Kilkenny has now opened in the old Victorian brewing building on Parliament Street. Dating from the 1700s, Smithwicks is Irelands oldest beer brand and visitors can enjoy an interactive tour, from its medieval origins to the present day, assisted by a life-size holographic monk.
          </p>
          <p>
           As well as a great brewing heritage, its good to know, too, that Kilkenny boasts a wealth of Michelin-starred restaurants: both Campagne (Kilkenny town) and Lady Helen Restaurant (Thomastown) have been honoured in the latest edition of the Michelin Green Guide.
          </p>
          <p>
           Gastronomy apart, I found Kilkenny to have much to excite my inner historian and the city has neatly summed up its heritage attractions with its Medieval Mile.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <img align="center" alttext="Wexford Harbour, Ireland" caption="Bay of tranquility: Wexford Harbour" declared-caption="" height="287" name="Wexford-Harbour" photographer="" refid="3002771" src="http://i.telegraph.co.uk/multimedia/archive/03002/Wexford-Harbour_3002771c.jpg" version="c" width="460"/>
            <sup>
             Bay of tranquility: Wexford Harbour
            </sup>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Following the trail really helped me to gain a deeper understanding of the citys passage through time. The route includes the magnificent castle with its round towers, a cathedral, an abbey, a theatre, historic houses the scope could fill a whole page.
          </p>
          <p>
           I especially enjoyed the National Craft Gallery that is part of the Mile  theres a separate Made in Kilkenny Craft Trail for those who wish to concentrate on the proud local traditions of jewellery and pottery. Its worth looking online for the latest details before planning a trip: offerings change as more young producers start their own cottage industries in the area.
          </p>
          <p>
           At the Gallery, which is housed in a stable block of Kilkenny Castle, I watched potters in action at the wheel  a hypnotic sight.
          </p>
          <p>
           The nearby Kilkenny Design Centre is the place to buy jewellery, glassware, knitwear and ceramics but one masterpiece you cant take away is the amazing stained-glass Rosary Window at the 13th-century Black Abbey, which depicts the life of Christ in around 10,000 different shards of ruby and sapphire glass.
          </p>
          <p>
           A short distance away is Rothe House and Garden, a meticulously restored 17th-century Tudor townhouse. It grants an insight into the comfortable life of a wealthy merchant and I was fascinated to discover that the swan bones that have been found on site were probably the remains of the merchants dinner!
          </p>
          <p>
           To the west is Waterford and here I was drawn to the sea again, especially as the slanting autumn sunlight was illuminating one of Irelands most beautiful patches of coastline, the Copper Coast European Geopark, named after the mining that once drove the areas economy.
          </p>
          <p>
           The landscape is deeply etched with centuries of human activity, but that pales into insignificance beside the millions of years of geological activity that carved out the precipitous cliffs. I found the best way to get a handle on all this was to pull up at the Geopark Centre in Bunmahon, which has information on a number of walking trails.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Cascades and crystal
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Behind the cliffs of the Copper Coast are the imposing Comeragh Mountains, drawing walkers from afar. The next day, as the weather was still benign, I headed up there with my own walking boots, discovering sparkling streams, little lakes and slopes of scree at the foot of mighty crags.
          </p>
          <p>
           The lure of the Mahon Falls, a 250ft waterfall, proved irresistible and although a little lane leads to this spectacular sight, I preferred to walk through the silent valley. Close by is the quite beautiful glacial lough of Coumshingaun. Peering into its deep blue depths, I could easily see why locals believed this lake to be bottomless and home to a demonic serpent.
          </p>
          <p>
           The city of Waterford, with its Viking Triangle, is Irelands oldest. Its most famous export has carried its name throughout the world and the Waterford Crystal Visitor Centre is a must-see.
          </p>
          <p>
           When I saw the flawless end products sparkling in the light, I began to appreciate how much hard labour goes into each piece. The tour takes you through every stage of manufacture and perhaps most impressive of all is the work of the craftspeople who produce intricate sculptures and trophies. I found myself contemplating how one false move could lead to hundreds of wasted man-hours.
          </p>
          <p>
           But not all of the citys claims to fame are so transparent. Elsewhere in Ireland, Waterford is just as strongly associated with the blaa  a soft, floury white roll that serves equally well for breakfast, lunch or an afternoon snack. Packed with corned beef and munched on a wall near Reginalds Tower, its close to perfection.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>

             ireland.com
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>

                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'click_headline', 'version_1']);

                        getNavPos($(this).attr("href"), "wayfarerReadNext");
                    });

                    bindUIActions();

                    var displayed = false;

                    setTimeout(function () {
                        if (!displayed) {
                            displayed == true;
                            readNextWidget.display();
                        }

                    }, 10000);

                    $(window).scroll(function () {
                        if (isScrolledIntoView("#tmg-related-links")) {
                            if (!displayed) {
                                displayed == true;
                                readNextWidget.display();
                            }
                        }

                        if(isScrolledIntoView("#shareBottom") || isScrolledIntoView("#newGigyaShareBottom")) {
                            if(settings.autoOpen) {
                                openArticleContainer();
                            }
                        }

                        if($(window).scrollTop() + $(window).height() == $(document).height()) {
                            if(settings.autoOpen) {
                                openArticleContainer();
                            }
                        }
                    });
                }
            };

            var display = function () {
                $(settings.widgetContainer).fadeIn(2000, function () {
                    //animation complete
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_closed', 'version_1']);
                });
            };

            var bindUIActions = function () {
                $(settings.openButton).mouseenter(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'hover_closed', 'version_1']);
                    openArticleContainer();
                });
                $(settings.widgetContainer).mouseleave(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    closeArticleContainer();
                });
                $(settings.closeButton).click(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();
                    _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'click_close', 'version_1']);
                    updateCookie(settings.articleId, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    closeArticleContainer();
                    settings.autoOpen = false;
                });
            };

            var openArticleContainer = function () {
                if (!settings.panelOpen) {
                	$("#readNextArticleContainer").show();//show/hide because height is calculated incorrectly for elements with display:none
                    $("#readNextArticleContainer").hide();
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                        _gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'wayfarer_read_next', 'display_open', 'version_1']);
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                    settings.autoOpen = false; //set auto open to false once user has viewed this once
                }
                return false;
            };

            var closeArticleContainer = function () {
                if (settings.panelOpen) {
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                }
                return false;
            };


            var isScrolledIntoView = function(elem) {
                if($(elem).length) { //check if element exists
                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));
                } else {
                    return false;
                }
            };

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {
                    return index == arr.indexOf(el);
                });
            }

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
        })();

    };
  </script>
