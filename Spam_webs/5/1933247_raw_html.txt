       <div class="fdVideoWof">
        <h4 itemprop="name">
         Tony's tradies bunch
        </h4>
        <p itemprop="description">
         Here's the story of families and small businesses and how the budget made them all the tradie bunch. Rocco Fazzari and Denis Carnahan with apologies to The Brady Bunch.
        </p>
        <span class="gone" itemprop="duration">
         PT1M3S
        </span>
        <span class="gone" itemprop="embedUrl">
        </span>
        <span class="gone" itemprop="width">
         620
        </span>
        <span class="gone" itemprop="height">

         <li class="autoplay">

      <!-- id:video-player-content -->
      <p>
       Tony Abbott loves a fight so it shouldn't come as a surprise that he's itching to get back in the election ring.
      </p>
      <p>
       Governing is just so dreary. Making hard choices and keeping everyone happy. Copping constant criticism no matter what you do. Worst of all: negotiating with those bloody ferals in the Senate.
      </p>
      <p>
       That's no fun at all.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="There are plenty of reasons for Tony Abbott to hold fire and play a longer game." src="/content/dam/images/g/h/2/k/r/f/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.gh28z7.png/1431829386135.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        There are plenty of reasons for Tony Abbott to hold fire and play a longer game.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Brendan Esposito
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       But beating up on the Labor Party? Now that's a good time. And beating them at the ballot box? That's even better.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       So armed with a cash splash budget and a new three-word slogan  "Have a go"  there's no doubt the Prime Minister is at least entertaining the idea of an early poll.
      </p>
      <p>
       But will he really pull the trigger? And if he does, will he stand a chance of winning?
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imagePortrait">
       <img alt="Illustration: Matt Davidson." itemprop="image" src="/content/dam/images/g/h/3/c/4/h/image.related.articleLeadNarrow.300x0.gh28z7.png/1431811722386.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Illustration: Matt Davidson.
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       A few months ago, when the government's poll numbers languished and Abbott was just barely clinging to the leadership, the thought of an election this year seemed thoroughly fanciful.
      </p>
      <p>
       But almost imperceptibly, things have changed. The polls have narrowed to within a couple of points. Support for Bill Shorten has softened. Unpopular polices have been jettisoned. The backbench has been mollified. And Abbott's confidence has returned after his "humbling" start to the year.
      </p>
      <p>
       That's not all. The Tories defied the opinion polls to easily triumph in Britain, buoying the spirits of conservatives everywhere; and here at home Labor is about to descend into another round of infighting and navel-gazing ahead of the ALP national conference.
      </p>
      <p>
       What's more, Abbott's a great campaigner and he knows it. He believes he can win an election against Shorten even if he starts a few points behind. He might be right: Shorten still has a lot of work to do to persuade people that he's ready to be prime minister.
      </p>
      <p>
       And then there's this budget.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sure, it goes against everything the government told us during its first year about the dire state of our national finances. But ultimately, it aims to put more money into the pockets of parents and small business owners. Surely that will win back a few votes.
      </p>
      <p>
       And why not go to the polls before the economy tanks and we realise just how hopeful many of the budget's projections were and why the path to surplus maybe isn't so credible after all?
      </p>
      <p>
       Oh, and just think of the rewards.
      </p>
      <p>
       An election win would not only give Abbott three more years in power, it would restore his wounded authority with the electorate and his own party. It would galvanise his team behind him, all but eliminating the prospect of another leadership challenge.
      </p>
      <p>
       And perhaps most tantalisingly of all: it would go down in history as one of the greatest comebacks in political history.
      </p>
      <p>
       So clearly the stars have aligned and it's on, right?
      </p>
      <p>
       Well, not so fast. It's easy to get carried away with election speculation  after all, the only thing we Canberra press gallery types love more is leadership speculation. But there are some equally compelling reasons why Tony Abbott will think twice about rushing to the polls.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first and most obvious: as things stand, there's a good chance he'd lose. It might be close but Abbott remains a very unpopular figure. Many Australians who voted for him in 2013 did so because they were fed up with Labor's psychodrama, not because they liked him.
      </p>
      <p>
       Abbott may have great faith in his campaigning abilities but it would still be an incredibly high-stakes gamble.
      </p>
      <p>
       And unless you're certain of victory, why give up power a year earlier than you have to? Why spend five years as opposition leader only to throw away the top job after less than two?
      </p>
      <p>
       "You don't give up government easily. I mean, by god you don't," Treasurer Joe Hockey said last week.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are other considerations that may stay the Prime Minister's hand, too.
      </p>
      <p>
       The budget bounce is likely to be modest  if it materialises at all  and it probably won't last long. And for many people, this year's budget won't fully wash away the bitter taste of last year's.
      </p>
      <p>
       And while federal governments have often benefited from going early in the past, recent state examples offer very different lessons. In Western Australia, voters tossed out Alan Carpenter's government amid fury over his decision to bring on an early poll. Campbell Newman had his record majority smashed  and his seat taken  after going early just a few months ago.
      </p>
      <p>
       Australians are smart enough to see early elections for what they usually are: cynical bids to cling to power.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the moment, the government also lacks a credible double dissolution trigger. While the Senate has twice rejected the government's bill to abolish the Clean Energy Finance Corporation, Abbott may have a tricky time explaining why that's a good enough reason to force Australians back to the polling booth.
      </p>
      <p>
       There's also the small matter of that "feral" Senate. Under current electoral laws, a double dissolution would open the door to even more independents and micro-parties, potentially making the job for the government  if it did prevail on election day  even harder.
      </p>
      <p>
       So there are plenty of reasons for Abbott to hold fire and play a longer game. At the moment, it's more likely he'll go full-term.
      </p>
      <p>
       But with this crazy brave Prime Minister, you never know. He might just have a go.
      </p>
