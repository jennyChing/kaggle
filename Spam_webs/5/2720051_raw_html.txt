       <p>
        Our sharks are in a bit of a pickle. Yes, we are amazed at them when we go
        <a href="http://www.sharkbookings.com/shark-trips/great-white-shark-cage-diving/">
         <strong>
          shark cage diving
         </strong>
        </a>
        , or spend hours watching documentaries on oceanic activity on the Discovery Channel. But the sad reality is that every year, the US fishing industry alone exports over 226 000 kilograms of shark fins to Hong Kong  this statistic is just the tip of the iceberg. Millions of sharks are killed all over the world for their fins, meat and other by-products. The global shark population is not in a good place. The numbers are dwindling significantly, and unbelievably quickly. Thankfully, shark conservation efforts are in full effect globally, with hundreds of thousands of people doing little bits here and there to help preserve these precious predators. We take a look at the worlds top five shark conservation efforts from all over the world:
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         Exposing the culprits
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        For years now, shark meat and fins have been considered a delicacy in numerous parts of the world, particularly Asia. For this reason, sharks are highly sought-after and can fetch quite a pretty penny. However, recent studies have begun to expose the countries who buy and sell shark meat to and from each other, resulting in global pressure being focussed on the guilty economies. Slowly but surely, the trade is decreasing. While there is still a long way to go, the already dwindling numbers show a positive impact has taken place.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         Getting their feet wet
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        Most shark conservationists do the best that they can to protect sharks from land. However, there are volunteers who actually go out into the sea to help sharks stuck in treacherous fishing nets. These nets are mainly there to trap fish  a common fishing practice. However, sharks are very often caught in them too, and volunteers work for hours at a time to free the sharks caught in these nets, and its an amazing feat, too. Youd be surprised at how many sharks are accidentally caught in them every year.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         <a href="http://www.wellaware.co.za/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/sb7.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-2056" height="200" src="http://www.wellaware.co.za/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/sb7-300x200.jpg" title="Shark Bookings" width="300"/>
         </a>
         Recording shark attacks
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        This is not nearly as counter-productive as it sounds. Keeping a record of the global number of shark attacks over a certain period of time has really shed some light on the plight of the shark conservationists, as it has proven that the aggression of sharks is nothing but a myth and the fear that many people have is merely just perpetuated by the media. For example, in a ten year period, between 1999 and 2009, only 51 fatal shark attacks were reported globally. This, compared to the millions of sharks killed by mankind each year, proves that sharks should really be more afraid of us than we are of them.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         Collecting evidence
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        Through research and tracking, it has become easy to collect concrete evidence to support shark conservation projects. As soon as people are presented with numbers and cold, hard facts, they start to take matters seriously. The data pertaining to roughly how many sharks are left has shocked many into doing whatever they can to preserve sharks, whether it be donating money or going out into the ocean and helping trapped sharks.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         Boycotts
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        Even the smallest actions can make the biggest differences. Public calls to boycott restaurants that serve shark fin soup, or any shark by-product, have been in effect for a number of years. By simple skipping over to the next shark fin-free restaurant, consumers have contributed in a significant number of restaurants throughout Asia and Australasia no longer serving an meal that contains shark. The ripple effect of this extended to the imports and exports of shark meat, leading to an ever-decreasing number of global shark meat trades.
       </p>
       <p>
        The smallest action can make the biggest difference. Sharks are an extremely important part of the oceanic ecosystem. Do whatever you can, no matter how big or small, to help conserve the sharks in our oceans before its too late.
       </p>
       <p>
