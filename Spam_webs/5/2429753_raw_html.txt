              <h1 class="story-title entry-title" itemprop="name headline">
               UFC Broomfield  By the Numbers [with INFOGRAPHIC]
              </h1>
              <div class="relative" id="right-content">
               <div class="left relative" id="content-area" itemprop="articleBody">
                <div class="relative" id="feat-img-reg">
                 <img itemprop="image" src="http://www.todaysknockout.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Nate-Diaz-punched-by-Benson-Henderson-Associated-Press.jpg"/>

                <div class="hide-title">
                 <span class="entry-title" itemprop="name headline">
                  UFC Broomfield  By the Numbers [with INFOGRAPHIC]
                 </span>
                </div>
                <!--hide-title-->
                <div id="left-content-mobi">
                 <div class="author-img left">
                  <img alt="Dane Curley" class="avatar avatar-120 wp-user-avatar wp-user-avatar-120 alignnone photo" height="120" src="http://www.todaysknockout.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Dane-TK-Default-150x150.png" width="120"/>

                  <span class="vcard author">
                   <span class="author-name left fn" itemprop="author">

                <p>
                 <!-- BEGIN THEIA POST SLIDER -->
                </p>
                <p>
                 The UFC has made a habit of
                 <a href="http://www.ufc.com/news/by-the-numbers-broomfield">
                  sharing
                 </a>
                 awesome infographics like this one, just before each event.
                 <em>
                  <a href="http://www.todaysknockout.com/tag/ufc-broomfield/">
                   UFC Broomfields
                  </a>
                 </em>
                 has some interesting digits in particular.
                </p>
                <p>
                 <a href="http://www.todaysknockout.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/UFCBroomfield-Infographic-By-the-Numbers.jpg">
                  <img alt="UFC Broomfield Infographic - By the Numbers (UFC)" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-3875" height="958" src="http://www.todaysknockout.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/UFCBroomfield-Infographic-By-the-Numbers-663x1024.jpg" width="620"/>
                 </a>
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: center;">
                 <span style="text-decoration: underline; font-size: 14pt;">
                  <span style="font-family: verdana, geneva;">
                   <strong>
                    Bendo vs. Thatch
                   </strong>
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p>
                 The first number is an eye-opener: 27-days is not a lot of time in between fights. But Benson Henderson is a storied professional fighter, among the elite, and hes trying to take his career out of a sticky situation by betting on himself in another one.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The number 5 doesnt really speak for itself. Bendos title reign was mired with two controversial (but arguable) decisions over
                 <a href="http://www.todaysknockout.com/?s=frankie+edgar">
                  Frankie Edgar
                 </a>
                 and one more against
                 <a href="http://www.todaysknockout.com/?s=gilbert+melendeZ">
                  Gilbert Melendez
                 </a>
                 . That doesnt contradict his elite status, however. Hes still the bees knees in the lightweight division, and getting to tough, arguable decisions with the likes of Edgar and Melendez is no small matter.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Hendersons takedown defense percentage is kind of an unnecessary plug. Its up there simply because its the third highest among active lightweights. Hes up against a striker in Brandon Thatch.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Thats why Thatchs first notable number is 7  number of strikes absorbed in the UFC. Hes only been hit seven times in two fights, represented by the number 2 above. He TKOd Justin Edwards in 1-minute 23-seconds. He TKOd Paulo Thiago in 2-minutes 10-seconds. Finally, 6 sub-minute finishes to his credit. And if youre reading between the lines  his quickest was 15-seconds.
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: center;">
                 <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
                  <span style="font-family: verdana, geneva;">
                   <strong>
                    Numbers Off the Chart
                   </strong>
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p>
                 A number they dont provide, but one Id like to see, is what Thatch weighs outside of competition. I think Bendo is 180ish in the off-season, which is why he said this move to the 170lb welterweight division is temporary, after he took this fight.Ashe never got out of fighting-shape from his match against Cowboy Cerrone, he entered the weigh-ins without need for cutting. He didnt have to dehydrate himself, and he was able to eat a healthy breakfast the morning of  weighing in at 169lbs.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Thatch has the benefit of sizing down for the weigh-in. Or, maybe its a con  depends how you look at it. Hell have to rehydrate, which will shoot him up in weight, while his opponent remains at 170.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Im also surprised that the infographic did not contain the height and reach advantage Brandon Thatch has in significance.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Hes 62.
                 <br/>
                 Bendo is 59.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Hes got a 74.5 reach.
                 <br/>
                 Bendos is 70.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Thats an aggregate 9.5-inch advantage in distance.
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: center;">
                 <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
                  <span style="font-family: verdana, geneva; font-size: 14pt;">
                   <strong>
                    Holloway vs. Miller
                   </strong>
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: left;">
                 They start Max Holloway off with a conservative number 3. But once you read between the lines, you discover its more boastful than first impressions suggest. That number three represents how many UFC fights Max has landed over 100 significant strikes in. 124, 120, 118, are the real numbers. And hes a three-round fighter.
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: left;">
                 The 23-year-old Holloway is indeed on a 4 fight winning streak  all finishes. Though the UFC tries to bolster that claim with the subtext that it ties UFC Womens Bantamweight Champion Ronda Rousey and UFC Lightweight Champion Anthony Pettis for the longest active streak of stoppages, you have to consider the competition-level. Holloway is down at a respectable #11 on our 145lb
                 <a href="http://www.todaysknockout.com/ufc-rankings/mma-rankings/featherweight-mma-rankings/">
                  Featherweight Rankings
                 </a>
                 . Hes been crushing guys that arent on the Top-15. In contrast, Rousey and Pettis have done it all within the Top-5 or 10. So, its definitely an impressive accolade for Max, just not the sort of awe-inspiring kind the UFCwould have youbelieve.
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: left;">
                 Cole Millers numbers do well to promote this fight. I didnt realize before this infographic that he was in the top three for most submission wins in the promotion, at 7. His striking rate at 4.36 per minute, is a good counter-point to his opponents striking counts.
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: center;">
                 <span style="text-decoration: underline; font-family: verdana, geneva;">
                  <strong>
                   Numbers Off the Chart
                  </strong>
                 </span>
                </p>
                <p style="text-align: left;">
                 Unfortunately, Cole Millers numbers dont highlight his inconsistency, and most of his successes were in the heavier 155lb division. There, he earned four
                 <em>
                  Performanceof the Night
                 </em>
                 awards, three for submissions. He had an admirable record of 7-3 at lightweight. Since entering the UFC featherweight scene, however, hes 3-3, moving his overall promotionalrecord to a much less flattering 10-6. Hell have to make his current state as good as his former best to beat the Top-15 ranked Max Holloway.
                </p>
                <p>
