     <p>
      <b>
       Natural Sequence Farming
      </b>
      is a method of landscape regeneration devised by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian" title="Australian">
       Australian
      </a>
      agricultural pioneer,
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Andrews_(agricultural_pioneer)" title="Peter Andrews (agricultural pioneer)">
       Peter Andrews
      </a>
      , in the 1970s. From the 1970s till the mid-2000s, Natural Sequence Farming was ignored and rejected by many farmers, the agriculture industry and local, state and federal governments, but these bodies have since come full circle and today NSF is recognised as a viable solution to environmental regeneration.
     </p>
     <p>
      The method involves implementing major earthworks on a given area of land that has been devastated by
      <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">
       deforestation
      </a>
      and general agricultural activities, to emulate the role of natural watercourses in an effort to reverse salinity, slow erosion and increase soil and water quality to enable native vegetation to regenerate and restore the
      <a href="/wiki/Riparian_zone" title="Riparian zone">
       riparian zone
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The method does not require the use of artificial fertilisers or herbicides.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_control">
       Flood control
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_Sequence_Farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Flood control">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Flood events may occur only once every 1 or 2 years, however they have the ability to enhance or destroy a property. If the waters move too fast over bare land, then they can strip away topsoil and nutrients, leaving behind barren sands. By inserting barriers across creeks and encouraging water to spread outwards, the energy of a flood is reduced, waters tend to deposit soil from upstream, and the water can soak into the land. This encourages grasses and fast growing plants to take root.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_role_of_weeds">
       The role of weeds
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_Sequence_Farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The role of weeds">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ground cover protects the land from drying and baking, and also stabilises the land in future flood events. While rye and other grasses have long been seen as beneficial crops for grazing, other plants that have been considered weeds can improve the soil, augment the growth of other crops, and assist the diet of grazing animals. These 'weeds' should not be removed. When they die naturally they should be cut for feed or used as
      <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">
       green manure
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Many 'weeds' only thrive in the regenerative cycle. Once the land recovers fertility the weeds tend to be replaced naturally by trees.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Case_studies">
       Case studies
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_Sequence_Farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Case studies">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first case study in which Natural Sequence Farming was successfully implemented was the 3,000 hectare Tarwyn Park in the Upper
      <a href="/wiki/Hunter_River_(New_South_Wales)" title="Hunter River (New South Wales)">
       Hunter River
      </a>
      catchment, formerly affected by deforestation and high salinity levels, amongst much else. Other case studies include successful retailer
      <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Harvey" title="Gerry Harvey">
       Gerry Harvey
      </a>
      's nearby property,
      <a href="/wiki/Baramul_Stud" title="Baramul Stud">
       Baramul Stud
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fraudulent_NSF">
       Fraudulent NSF
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_Sequence_Farming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fraudulent NSF">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Since the 1990s, there have been a number of cases where individuals or groups have sought financial gain through offering fraudulent versions of NSF.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many of these cases are discussed and exposed on the NSF
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.naturalsequencefarming.com/truthfile/index.htm" rel="nofollow">
       here
      </a>
      .
     </p>
