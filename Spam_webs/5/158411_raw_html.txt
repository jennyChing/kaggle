         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.352media.com/">
          352
         </a>
         tries the opposite approach, suggesting 11 things SEOs should never ever do for link building again. The article might be slightly on a rant side, still it's a good one to give ones' own link building activities a hard look.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Local SEO
        </h1>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://dejanseo.com.au/feature-request-for-google-places-reviews/">
          31. Feature request for Google Places reviews
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         It's widely known that user reviews are of utter importance for local SEO. Given that, there are cases of manipulative use of user reviews in pursuit of high rankings. To make matters worse, Google's current ways of detecting fake reviews are far from perfect. In his article,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+DanPetrovic/posts">
          Dan Petrovic
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://dejanseo.com.au/index/">
          Dejan SEO
         </a>
         suggests a way out and illustrates three simple elements which would make a world of difference to business owners. The article can be summarised in one simple statement:Google should allow managers of verified business listings to view all reviews including suspended ones and include a reconsideration function to restore falsely suspended listings.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://searchenginewatch.com/article/2293053/Google-Places-for-Business-vs.-Google-Local">
          32. Google Places for Business vs. Google+ Local
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Many small local businesses are puzzled about how Google Places is different from Google+ Local. Google hasn't made it easy for small local businesses to understand if they need one, both or neither, and it's left many confused. In her article at Search Engine watch,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+AmandaDiSilvestro/posts">
          Amanda DiSilvestro
         </a>
         of Higher Visibility clears up the confusion. According to the author, it is crucial for local businesses to be involved in both to be in control of your brand and gain increased visibility.
         <strong>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.whitespark.ca/blog/post/12-how-to-identify-quality-citation-sources">
          33. How to identify quality citation sources
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         As many of you know, lots of web promotion experts are involved into citation building as a standalone activity compared to link building. One of these experts is
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+DarrenShaw/posts">
          Darren Shaw
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.whitespark.ca/">
          WhiteSpark
         </a>
         . According to Darren, not all citation sources are created equal, i.e. some are going to help your rankings, while others could hurt them. To help readers differentiate between the two, the author provides a 9-point checklist, and though there's no simple formula to determine the citation source quality for sure, these tips will let you get a better judgment.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.smallbusinessonlinecoach.com/blog/seo/onpage-optimization-local-seo-perfectly-optimized-local-page/">
          34. How to do onpage optimization for local SEO  the "perfectly optimized local page" example
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         When figuring out the structure of their pages, many site owners working on the local market resort to general tips on building landing pages. Yet onpage optimization for local SEO has rules of its own, and we're lucky to have
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+MatthewHuntmatthewhunt/posts">
          Matthew Hunt
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.smallbusinessonlinecoach.com/">
          Small Business Online Coach
         </a>
         to have them summarized for us. In his article Matthew shares an infographic, which goes into many details and includes a well-thought-out structure of an ideally optimized local page.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://niftymarketing.com/10-random-peoples-reactions-to-google-local-carousel/">
          35. 10 random people's reactions to Google local carousel
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Google has been generous in changing SERPS structure in 2013 (
         <a href="http://www.link-assistant.com/blog/google-serp-changes-2013/">
          check out a detailed recap here
         </a>
         ), one of the most notable definitely being the local carousel. How about you? Do you personally feel the carousel has improved your experience as a user of Google search? If you're curious to check out what others think, turn to this article written by
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+MikeRamsey/posts">
          Mike Ramsey
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://niftymarketing.com/">
          Nifty Marketing
         </a>
         . What's most valuable, Mike's article includes videos, capturing reactions of people viewing carousel results. Its a powerful insight indeed into how average non-technical searchers interact with Google results.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Penguin, Hummingbird and Not Provided
        </h1>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.iacquire.com/blog/surviving-the-not-provided-apocalypse/">
          36. Surviving the (Not Provided) Apocalypse
         </a>
         <strong>
         </strong>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In September 2013, Google announced they're encrypting even more users' searches, meaning that SEOs will find it even harder to understand whether a particular keyword resulted in search traffic. One of the best articles that explains the ins and outs of the Not Provided problem is the one by
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+JoelKlettke/posts">
          Joel Klettke
         </a>
         written for the iAcquire blog. The author explains how you can still get vital data. For example, while we no longer see which phrases brought people in, we can still mash up data to get an idea.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.searchenginejournal.com/how-to-remove-unnatural-links-to-your-site-choosing-the-best-solution-after-penguin-2-0/64115/">
          37. How to remove unnatural links to your site: choosing the best solution after Penguin 2.0
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         When Penguin 2.0 was announced in May 2013, many SEOs became aware they should get rif of unnatural links. However it turned out to be easier said than done because it was not quite clear what steps in particular one had to take to get rid of unnatural links. In her article at Search Engine Journal,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+AlesiaKrush/posts">
          Alesia Krush
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.link-assistant.com/">
          Link-Assistant.Com
         </a>
         provides insights into spotting low-quality backlinks and investigates the possible actions to take depending on the link warnings received.
         <strong>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.johnfdoherty.com/freak-hummingbird/">
          38. Let's not freak out about Hummingbird and Not Provided
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In October 2013 when the SEO world was all ears about the ''not provided'' and Hummingbird,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+JohnDoherty/">
          John Doherty
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.johnfdoherty.com/">
          JohnDoherty.Com
         </a>
         was one of SEO experts to share his viewpoint on the state of SEO things. John explains why Hummingbird's effect should be relatively small and the types of searches it has influence on. As for not provided, the author's experience as an SEO consultant suggests that most clients (decision-makers in particular) don't care about individual keywords. They look at the overall revenue coming from the organic traffic. Thus, the future is bright!
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.seo-writer.com/blog/2013/10/04/five-backlink-metrics-to-to-elude-the-dreaded-penguin/">
          39. Five backlink metrics to elude the dreaded Penguin
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In this article
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/u/0/112928640804164819202">
          David Leonhardt
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://thehappyguy.com/">
          The Happy Guy Marketing
         </a>
         describes five metrics you can use to identify the worthwhile backlinks and the dangerous backlinks you might have or might have the opportunity to acquire. While there are other guides to help you avoid a questionable backlink profile, I like this one in particular for its simplicity  and the fairy tale inside.
        </p>

          40. The PR strategies SEOs haven't learned

        <p>
         Most web promoters these days think content marketing, social media marketing  anything that helps creating linkable asserts and make people actually link to those. But how about good old PR methods
         <em>
          ?
         </em>

The author provides a breakdown of what exactly these PR-savvy SEOs do to get attention of journalists, thus securing press mentions and links.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Round-ups
        </h1>

         <a href="http://digitalphilippines.net/link-building/">
          41. 37 experts share their most actionable link building tips
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         I love crowdsourced content. There are easy to browse through and it's like a stroke of luck that instead of having to read tons of posts on different blogs you get the answer to a very specific question from multiple experts in one place. In my opinion, one of the best round-ups of 2013 is the one on link building tips by
         <a href="https://twitter.com/venchito14">
          Venchito Tampon

          42. The most actionable SEO tips ever

        <p>
         This article by
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/115014686407570806260/posts">
          Steve Webb
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.webgnomes.org/">
          WebGnomes
         </a>

The author categorizes the responses into 8 areas of SEO: content marketing, keyword research, link building, community building, technical SEO, strategic SEO, user experience and conversion rate optimization and local SEO. Definitely a must-read!
        </p>
        <h1>
         SEO as a career
        </h1>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.cucumbernebula.com/blog/personalities-that-make-great-seos/">
          43. Personalities that make great SEOs
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         SEO is an interesting industry where one rarely becomes an SEO
         <em>
          on purpose
         </em>
         . Search marketers are composed of several different interests and come from a multitude of backgrounds. That's one of the things that makes the community so lively. In his article,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+PeterAttiaA/posts">
          Peter Attia
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.cucumbernebula.com/">
          Cucumber Nebula
         </a>
         portrays 8 popular personalities that can be found within the industry. The gamer, the artist, the nerd, the chef, the bartender, the musician, the geek, the math punk  what kind of personality are you so that you're so comfortable with being an SEO?
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.paddymoogan.com/2013/03/07/blackhat-vs-whitehat-seo-my-opinion/">
          44. Blackhat vs. Whitehat SEO  My Opinion
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In this article at his blog,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/104334957300160196129/posts">
          Paddy Moogan
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.paddymoogan.com/">
          PaddyMoogan.Com
         </a>
         provides a fresh perspective on blackhat vs. whitehat problem. Surprising as it may sound to many, Paddy makes a confession that he learnt SEO through black / greyhat tactics, which was ok since he was taking risks with his own websites. According to Paddy, here's where comes the borderline between the blackhat and whitehat: SEOs can apply questionable tactics only on behalf of themselves, not on behalf of their clients. Still, SEO is to a great extent about experimenting and testing stuff, so SEOs should test for themselves (on their sites!) and don't take the word of others.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.greenlaneseo.com/blog/2013/10/the-kind-of-seo-i-want-to-be/">
          45. The kind of SEO I want to be
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In this engaging and highly personal article
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+BillSebald/posts">
          Bill Sebald
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.greenlaneseo.com/">
          Greenlane SEO
         </a>
         walks us thorugh the way his SEO outlook matured during his 13 years in the industry. Bill confesses in morphing from doing strictly keyword focused stuff to promoting bigger picture ideas, SEO marketing as he puts it. The author touches upon how this approach relates to the recent Hummingbird update and the ''not provided'' issue. And what kind of SEO do you want to be?
        </p>
        <h1>
         SEO and Social
        </h1>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://ghergich.com/blog/seo-and-social-media/">
          46. 3 social media tips to win friends and influence rankings
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Though social shares don't improve rankings directly, social media still actively impacts SEO. According to
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+AJGhergich">
          A.J. Ghergich
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://ghergich.com">
          Chergich and Co.
         </a>
         , both SEO and social are all about relationships. To get links, you need quality content and robust outreach practices. In his article, A.J. shows 3 ways of using social media to assist SEO. In particular, the author shows how to target the right press on Facebook, how to choose best headlines with Twitter adds and nails the point about the importance of Google authorship.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.aimclearblog.com/2013/10/21/easily-buy-seo-lift-disrupt-googles-vaunted-search-algorithm/">
          47. Easy buy SEO lift &amp; disrupt Google's vaunted search algorithms
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         If you're unfamiliar with the concept of paid-organic amplification, be sure to read this article by
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+MartyWeintraub/posts">
          Marty Weintraub
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.aimclearblog.com/">
          aimClear
         </a>
         . The concept is about buying organic looking ads in social media to drive traffic, increase shares and get natural links. In this particular article, Marty looks into the possibilities of blending networks, i.e. his team is curious if there are social networks that would let you buy ads pointing at other social networks and organic search results. The findings are fascinating!
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://seogadget.com/data-entry-is-a-waste-of-time/">
          48. Creating efficient data collection systems for SEO and social
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Web scraping is a vast part of every SEO's life. For a lot of new SEOs web scraping and data collection can be complicated and intimidating. In this article,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/110079414003081332522/posts">
          John-Henry Scherck
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://seogadget.com/">
          SEOgadget
         </a>
         shows how managers can create efficient systems and names four types of waste that cause inefficiency. Beyond that, the author shares some ready-made excel templates, case studies and directions on using various API data.
        </p>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://www.stonetemple.com/does-facebook-activity-impact-seo/">
          49. Does Facebook activity impact SEO?
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In this post
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+EricEnge">
          Eric Enge
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.stonetemple.com/">
          Stone Sample Consulting
         </a>
         reviews the data his team has assembled to see if Facebook activity impacts rankings. They ran two tests. One drove a large number of likes to brand new pages on established domains. The other shared brand new pages on established domains via Facebook. In both cases, the liked and shared pages had no links to them, so there was no way other than Facebook for Google to discover them. Eric's net conclusion based on multiple data sets is that Google doesn't use Facebook as a discovery, indexing or ranking factor.
        </p>
        <h1>
         Structured data
        </h1>
        <h2>
         <a href="http://searchengineland.com/measuring-the-effect-of-semantic-markup-on-your-search-traffic-164736">
          50. Measuring the effect of semantic markup on your search traffic
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         We all hear about the importance of rdfa, schema.org and other types of semantic markup. Still, this stuff is a bit on the techy SEO side, and many site promoters are reluctant to apply it. And even if they do apply semantic markup there are not sure if it's to improve their online presence. In her article at Search Engine Land,
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/+CarrieHill/posts">
          Carrie Hill
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://ignitordigital.com/">
          Ignitor Digital
         </a>
         shows how you can measure if semantic markup resulted in traffic, revenue and ranking gains and if there were any changes in mobile/tablet vs. desktop traffic.
        </p>
