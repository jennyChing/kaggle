     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=in_for_a_penny,_in_for_a_pound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Originally with reference to the fact that if one owed a penny, one might as well owe a pound (
      <a href="/wiki/pound_sterling" title="pound sterling">
       pound sterling
      </a>
      , UK currency) as the penalties for non-payment were virtually identical in severity.
     </p>

     <ol>
      <li>
       Having started something, one must see it through to its end, rather than stopping short; one must 
       <a href="/wiki/go_the_whole_hog" title="go the whole hog">
        go the whole hog
       </a>
       .
       <ul>
        <li>
         <b>
          1964
         </b>
         , J. F. Holleman,
         <i>
          Experiment in Swaziland: report of the Swaziland sample survey, 1960
         </i>
         , p. 9:
         <dl>
          <dd>
           Under the circumstances it seemed to be a case of
           <b>
            in for a penny, in for a pound
           </b>
           . If the Institutes team were still prepared to accept the challenge, the Administration was willing to do likewise
          </dd>
         </dl>
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          1964
         </b>
         , Sanki Ichikawa,
         <i>
          The Kenkyusha Dictionary of Current English Idioms
         </i>
         , p. 509:
         <dl>
          <dd>
           
           <b>
            in for a penny, in for a pound
           </b>
           : if one undertakes something, it must be carried through at whatever cost.
          </dd>
         </dl>
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          2001
         </b>
         , B. J. James,
         <i>
          A Lady for Lincoln Cade
         </i>
         , p. 159:
         <dl>
          <dd>
           Turning before the mirror, she studied the gown shed spent much of her savings on in Belle Terre. Okay, but not great.
           <b>
            In for a penny, in for a pound
           </b>
           . Soon I have to get a job.
          </dd>
         </dl>
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          2002
         </b>
         , Kathryn Wall,
         <i>
          In for a Penny: A Bay Tanner Mystery
         </i>
         , p. 123:
         <dl>
          <dd>
           I rummaged in my bag for Miss Addies keys, turned off the car, and marched purposefully toward the building.
          </dd>
          <dd>
           <b>
            In for a penny, in for a pound
           </b>
           , I mumbled under my breath as I pushed open the door and headed for the elevator.
          </dd>
         </dl>
        </li>
        <li>
         <b>
          2004
         </b>
         , M. Mihkel Mathiesen,
         <i>
          Global Warming in a Politically Correct Climate: How Truth Became Controversial
         </i>
         , p. 133:
         <dl>
          <dd>
           It appears to be a situation where the greenhouse proponents are
           <b>
            in for a penny, in for a pound
           </b>
           . As long as the myth needs to be kept alive, this is the inescapable conclusion.
          </dd>
         </dl>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </li>
     </ol>
