       <h1 class="article-title">
        Graphene Converts Single Particles of Light Into Many Electrons
       </h1>
       <section class="motherboard-portlet motherboard-portlet-about" id="yw0">
        <div class="author-info image">
         <a href="/author/MichaelByrne">
          <div class="article-media-overlay">
          </div>
          <img src="//motherboard-images.vice.com/authors/no-id/1427595469491811.jpg?resize=300:*&amp;output-format=jpeg&amp;output-quality=90"/>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="author-info hasImage">
         <span>
          Written by
         </span>
         <h3>
          <a href="/author/MichaelByrne">
           Michael  Byrne
          </a>
         </h3>

          Editor
         </h4>

         <a class="social-facebook sharing-item" data-share-type="facebook" href="#">

        <div class="article-content rich-text">
         <p>
          It sounds like free energy: A single incoming photon meets a sheet of graphene and, upon collision, produces multiple electrons. From this single particle of light, one of the infinitesimal points of energy behind the electromagnetic force, comes electric current.
         </p>
         <p>
          This feat was
          <a href="http://pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/nl503614v">
           recently demonstrated
          </a>
          by researchers at the Swiss
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           cole Polytechnique Fdrale de Lausanne (EPFL) and, while not really offering free energy, it demonstrates the potential of graphene as a high-efficiency photovoltaic (solar energy-producing) material. The phenomenon, known properly as carrier multiplication, has the potential to nearly double the theoretical upper limit of solar energy conversion from 32 to 60 percent,
          </inline>
          <a href="http://spectrum.ieee.org/nanoclast/green-tech/solar/graphene-gets-another-boost-in-high-conversion-efficiency-photovoltaics">
           reports IEEE Spectrum
          </a>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           .
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           The efficiency boost comes from a simple enough idea. Typically, when a photon smashes into an electron in a photovoltaic material the result is an excited electron or an electron at a higher energy state than before. This electron might just pop loose from its atomic home and become freely moving electric current, which is then conducted away and harvested for power. The amount of energy needed to pop an electron loose varies by material, and is known as the band gap.
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p class="has-image">
          <img alt="" class="vmp-image" data-original-height="720" data-original-width="969" src="//motherboard-images.vice.com/content-images/contentimage/no-id/1422223051836734.png"/>
         </p>
         <p class="photo-credit">
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           Image: Wiki
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           Where the inefficiency comes in is if a photon arrives with too much energy (above the gap), leaving some leftover to be dissipated as heat.
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           In a carrier multiplication scheme, that excess goes instead
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           toward popping out another electron. So: less waste.
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           Graphene, single atom-thick two-dimensional sheets of graphite, has all kinds of neat and highly desirable properties for the technology near-future, including very high thermal and electrical conductivity, incredible strength, and, indeed, an extremely strong interaction with light. Potential graphene applications include but are hardly limited to fuel cells, industrial lubricants, novel drug delivery devices, integrated circuits, desalinization filters, environmental sensing, and synthetic bone tissue.
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           The multiplicative properties of graphene were demonstrated first indirectly by researchers at the
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           Barcelona-based Institute of Photonic Science last year. Here, the EPFL researchers go a step further, using a new technique called
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           ultrafast time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy to observe photoconversion in action, a feat that until recently was impossible just given that photoconversion occurs on timescales in the femto-neighborhood of
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           10^-15 seconds.
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           The general idea behind the technique is that the energized
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           material to be observed is hit with successive "probe"
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           pulses of light, each one illuminating the evolving
          </inline>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           energy states of the electrons within the material. It's sort of like using a flash to photograph a dark room, only this particular room isn't dark so much as fast.
          </inline>
         </p>
         <p>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           Graphene still isn't quite ready for photovoltaic prime-time, however. It
          </inline>
          <a href="http://spectrum.ieee.org/nanoclast/green-tech/solar/graphene-can-create-hot-carrier-cells-for-photovoltaics">
           turns out
          </a>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           that the stuff doesn't absorb light very well in the first place. Being able to convert sunlight to electricity at very high efficiencies doesn't much matter if the material wants to ignore the light in the first place. And there is also the question of how exactly to harvest current from graphene, the
          </inline>
          <a href="http://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2014/08/140815102320.htm">
           edges of which
          </a>
          <inline style="background-color: initial;">
           tend to have different conductivity properties than the central zones and are also quite reactive when exposed.
          </inline>
         </p>
        </div>
