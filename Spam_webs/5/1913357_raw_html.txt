      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="For Redan resident Lou Ridsdale, green anarchy is all about sharing." src="/content/dam/images/g/h/4/9/9/x/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.gh0rfg.png/1431930944754.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        For Redan resident Lou Ridsdale, green anarchy is all about sharing.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Justin Whitelock
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       At some point last week  unseen, unheard  someone deposited a pile of quinces in a puddle-pocked lane in Ballarat. It was a political act.
      </p>
      <p>
       The lane in question  Warrior Placein the suburb of Redan  is the modest ground zero of Food Is Free, a volunteer movement that is, by turns, challenging and charming.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since it began in October last year, it has spread rapidly to other locations, including Ballarat, Bendigo, Smythes Creek, Daylesford, Castlemaine, Gippslandand Sunshine. The newest Food Is Free site is scheduled to start up in Ararat on May 21.
      </p>
      <div class="cT-imagePortrait">
       <img alt="Lou Ridsdale has planted an innovative idea and set up a Food Is Free site in a laneway near her home in Redan." itemprop="image" src="/content/dam/images/g/h/0/t/d/u/image.related.articleLeadNarrow.300x0.gh0rfg.png/1431930944754.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Lou Ridsdale has planted an innovative idea and set up a Food Is Free site in a laneway near her home in Redan.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Andrew Masterson
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       The driving idea behind Food Is Free is simple. People leave their surplus fruit, vegetables, herbs, seedlingsand seeds at a particular spot. Then other people come along and take them, at no cost. There is no fee, no membership, no paperwork, no opening hours, no obligation  and no lawful premises.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       "It's very, very naughty," said Lou Ridsdale, 42, who set up the Redan site. "That's why I love it. It breaks a lot of taboos. We don't own the land it's on, for instance. The community has just taken it over. I'm a great believer in green anarchy."
      </p>
      <p>
       Each Victorian Food Is Free site in some way reflects the needs of its users.
      </p>
      <p>
       However,Ridsdale's initial action was promptedby two American movements:one rather utopian, and the other more hard-edged.
      </p>
      <p>
       The utopian influence came from the US version of Food Is Free, a non-profit organisation that startedin Austin, Texas, in January 2012. Volunteers build large planter boxes out of recycled materials, which are given free to communities and schools.
      </p>
      <p>
       Founder John VanDeusen Edwards estimates there are Food Is Free schemes in 190 cities around the world.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ridsdale's grittier inspiration came from another movement, Guerilla Gardening. Using a more confrontational model, guerilla gardening groups appropriate unused innercity land  vacant lots, weed-strewn pocket parks, the middle of roundabouts  and plant food crops. Run-ins with authority are common.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Texas organisation tends to attract free-spirited folk. Guerilla gardeners, on the other hand, are motivated by the brute fact that people who live in poor neighbourhoods often have little access to fresh fruit and vegetables. For both groups, food is a matter of social justice.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There are some homeless people who come here at night to get some food," Ridsdale said. "We've installed some solar-powered lights so they can see what's available.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I like the fact that this can be all anonymous if someone wants it to be. It's available to everyone. People can come here to pick up some fruit or veg if the family budget is falling a few dollars short,or they can come here to pick up some fresh produce just because they want to.
      </p>
      <p>
       "There's no stigma about coming here. No one's judged in the laneway."
      </p>
      <p>
       Some 98 kilometres to the north-west of Warrior Place, the soontobelaunched Ararat Food Is Free site will be focused more sharply, although not exclusively, onsupplementing straitened budgets. The project has been set up by a non-profit group,Grampians Food Alliance, and the members represent local restaurants, farmers and welfare groups.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Our organisation is working towards increasing the level of food security in the Grampians region," co-ordinator Melissa O'Loughlan said. "Food Is Free is one aspect of that. We know that access to fresh fruit and vegetables is difficult for many people."
      </p>
      <p>
       O'Loughlan said the site,initially,would be stocked with donations from farmers and backyard growers.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Perhaps, people, too, if they've found they've bought too much at the shops, could come down and drop off what they don't need. Perhaps, too, we could even get the retailers donating their surplus stock.
      </p>
      <p>
       "We'll see what happens. We want this to be community-driven initiative."
      </p>
      <p>
       It is the bottom-up, organic nature of Food Is Free projects that makesthem unpredictable and curious things. They are anarchic exercises, essentially without direction or agenda beyond securing a space for unwanted fruit and vegetables.
      </p>
      <p>
       "I started this one by just putting a single small table in the laneway with some fruit on it and a hand-drawn sign saying 'Food Is Free'," Lou Ridsdale said.
      </p>
      <p>
       "At first, I didn't think anyone was going to reciprocate, then a few days later I found a small bunch of parsley on the table. It was going to seed but there was a note attached that said, 'I thought you might need this'. That was the start."
      </p>
      <p>
       After that, things moved quickly. More tables turned up. Seedlings were planted in polystyrene boxes. A seed bank was set up in an old toolbox. Backyard growers started bringing in their harvests. This week, there were figs, cauliflowers, feijoas, carrots, garlicand beetroots.
      </p>
      <p>
       One old bloke on a mobility scooter plundered a wild apple tree growing in a nearby cemetery and brought back the crop. Last week, someone dropped off a compost tumbler.
      </p>
      <p>
       "On sunny days, it's not unusual now to have 100 people coming through the lane," Ridsdale said. "All the neighbours have got involved. I lived here for four years and never really knew anybody. Now I know them all;we all hang out together around the food tables."
      </p>
      <p>
       However,just as a collection of rickety furniture covered in fruit and veg serves as a comfort to some people, it makes others uneasy. The nature of Food Is Free is fundamentally subversive. There is no need to pay, or to swap, or to reciprocate, or even let anyone know you're there. In a society built on the bedrock notion of commodity exchange, genuinely free stuff provided by random strangers can be anunsettling idea.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Some people when they come here for the first time are so suspicious," Ridsdale laughed.
      </p>
      <p>
       "They think there must be a catch. They look around for CCTV. They can't get their heads around the concept. I've been asked if it's a Christian charity thing,which it definitely isn't. Some people try to offer money, which we refuse. It's really interesting hearing a mother explain to her child that taking food from here isn't stealing;it's sharing."
      </p>
      <p>
       Religious it may not be, but Food Is Free, at least in its Ballarat incarnation, has a couple of outreach programs. Through a very active social media presence, volunteers are often called on to advise food activists in other areas about setting up a project.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, more unusualhas been the recent instigation of regular Food Is Free Days. On the first Sunday of every month, city residents are encouraged to put free food outside their houses for anyone to take.
      </p>
      <p>
       "It might just be a single zucchini on the mailbox," Ridsdale said. "Or perhaps a basket of fruit from a backyard tree, put outside the front fence. Some people put food at busstops, or in their workplaces. The first time we tried this it was Easter Sunday;that was a total fizzer, but it's really taken off since then."
      </p>
      <p>
       Indeed, the Warrior Place project has grown so quickly in its seven months of operation,it's about to go legit. Sort of.
      </p>
      <p>
       After discussions with the local council, Food Is Free has been told it can set up a permanent presence in a section of park about 30 metres from its current location. Over the next few weeks, the fruit and veg tables will migrate.
      </p>
      <p>
       "We'll keep the seedling trays and the seed bank where they are, though," Ridsdale said. "After all, no one wants to leave the lane completely."
      </p>

      </p>
