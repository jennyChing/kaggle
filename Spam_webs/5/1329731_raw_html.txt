  <meta content="The Neurotropic Parasite Toxoplasma Gondii Increases Dopamine Metabolism" property="og:title"/>
  <meta content="
         The highly prevalent parasite  Toxoplasma gondii  manipulates its host's behavior. In infected rodents, the behavioral changes increase the likelihood that the parasite will be transmitted back to its definitive cat host, an essential step in completion of the parasite's life cycle. The mechanism(s) responsible for behavioral changes in the host is unknown but two lines of published evidence suggest that the parasite alters neurotransmitter signal transduction: the disruption of the parasite-induced behavioral changes with medications used to treat psychiatric disease (specifically dopamine antagonists) and identification of a tyrosine hydroxylase encoded in the parasite genome. In this study, infection of mammalian dopaminergic cells with  T. gondii  enhanced the levels of K+-induced release of dopamine several-fold, with a direct correlation between the number of infected cells and the quantity of dopamine released. Immunostaining brain sections of infected mice with dopamine antibody showed intense staining of encysted parasites. Based on these analyses,  T. gondii  orchestrates a significant increase in dopamine metabolism in neural cells. Tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme for dopamine synthesis, was also found in intracellular tissue cysts in brain tissue with antibodies specific for the parasite-encoded tyrosine hydroxylase. These observations provide a mechanism for parasite-induced behavioral changes. The observed effects on dopamine metabolism could also be relevant in interpreting reports of psychobehavioral changes in toxoplasmosis-infected humans.

         <h2>
          Abstract
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1" name="article1.front1.article-meta1.abstract1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          The highly prevalent parasite
          <em>
           Toxoplasma gondii
          </em>
          manipulates its host's behavior. In infected rodents, the behavioral changes increase the likelihood that the parasite will be transmitted back to its definitive cat host, an essential step in completion of the parasite's life cycle. The mechanism(s) responsible for behavioral changes in the host is unknown but two lines of published evidence suggest that the parasite alters neurotransmitter signal transduction: the disruption of the parasite-induced behavioral changes with medications used to treat psychiatric disease (specifically dopamine antagonists) and identification of a tyrosine hydroxylase encoded in the parasite genome. In this study, infection of mammalian dopaminergic cells with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          enhanced the levels of K+-induced release of dopamine several-fold, with a direct correlation between the number of infected cells and the quantity of dopamine released. Immunostaining brain sections of infected mice with dopamine antibody showed intense staining of encysted parasites. Based on these analyses,
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          orchestrates a significant increase in dopamine metabolism in neural cells. Tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme for dopamine synthesis, was also found in intracellular tissue cysts in brain tissue with antibodies specific for the parasite-encoded tyrosine hydroxylase. These observations provide a mechanism for parasite-induced behavioral changes. The observed effects on dopamine metabolism could also be relevant in interpreting reports of psychobehavioral changes in toxoplasmosis-infected humans.
         </p>

         <h2>
          Introduction
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          A complex range of interactions exist between a pathogen with its host, which may include manipulation of the host for the pathogen's own advantage. There are several examples of viruses, such as rabies virus
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Lagrue1">
           [1]
          </a>
          , and parasites, including
          <em>
           Acanthocephala
          </em>
          spp.
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Lefevre1">
           [2]
          </a>
          and
          <em>
           Toxoplasma gondii
          </em>
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Berdoy1">
           [3]
          </a>
          , that influence host behavior to increase their transmission efficiency. For years, scientists have been intrigued by the association between
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection and altered aversive behavior. The underlying mechanism(s) responsible for this behavior change are presently unknown. The aim of our study was to identify a possible explanation for this phenomenon.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p2" name="article1.body1.sec1.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          is a common, global protozoan parasite, which requires both a definitive host and an intermediate host to complete its life cycle. Although felines are the only definitive host of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          , any warm-blooded animal, including humans, can be infected
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Dubey1">
           [4]
          </a>
          . It is estimated that one quarter of the population (over 12 years of age) in the United States is positive for
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection (Center for Disease Control, USA, 2008). Prevalence in some areas can be as high as 95% in older populations. Latent, chronic infection, which is characterized by parasite encystment in the host muscle and brain cells (particularly neurons and glial cells), persists following the resolution of acute infection and continues with seropositivity throughout the host's lifetime
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Dubey1">
           [4]
          </a>
          . Due to its high prevalence in the human population, it is critical to better understand the effects of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection in the brain.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p3" name="article1.body1.sec1.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          During the chronic stage of infection, infected rodents, which are a key intermediate host for
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          , exhibit a distinct repertoire of specific behavioral changes, including a loss of aversion to cat odors
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Berdoy1">
           [3]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Vyas1">
           [5]
          </a>
          . Infected rodents are, conversely, attracted to these odors, and this may be responsible for increased predation and for an increase in successful transmission of the parasite to the feline host; as cats are the only animal that can shed the environmentally-resistant stage of the parasite known as oocysts. This behavior change in infected rodents during the chronic stage of infection appears highly specific to feline odor, as a similar change is not evoked by other predators and has no effect on conditioned fear and anxiety
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Lamberton1">
           [6]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Webster1">
           [7]
          </a>
          . The underlying mechanism(s) responsible for this behavior change still remain unclear, however, it has been revealed that anti-psychotic (haloperidol) and mood-stabilizing medication (valproic acid) can prevent the development of these behavior changes
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Webster1">
           [7]
          </a>
          in addition the dopamine uptake inhibitor GBR12909 modifies behavioral responses associated with latent toxoplasmosis in infected rodents
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Skallova1">
           [8]
          </a>
          . Furthermore, we have recently identified an enzyme with tyrosine hydroxylase activity encoded in the
          <em>
           Toxoplasma
          </em>
          genome whose expression is induced during differentiation to tissue cyst stages
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Gaskell1">
           [9]
          </a>
          . Several studies have suggested that
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection in humans can have serious neurological effects
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Brown1">
           [10]
          </a>
          . Associations have been identified between
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          seroprevalence and schizophrenia
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Brown2">
           [11]
          </a>
          
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Torrey1">
           [13]
          </a>
          . The schizophrenia-associated risk factors of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection have been found to be greater than the risk factors associated with an individual's genes and with other environmental factors
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Torrey1">
           [13]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Purcell1">
           [14]
          </a>
          . Schizophrenia affects approximately 1% of the adult population and in most cases is a lifelong disease with exacerbations. Although schizophrenia is a multifactorial disease, pharmacological and genetic evidence suggest that dysregulation of dopamine metabolism is involved in schizophrenia
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Howes1">
           [15]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Seeman1">
           [16]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec1.p4" name="article1.body1.sec1.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          Thus, it is crucial to examine whether dopamine metabolism is affected by
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection, particularly based on evidence of a tyrosine hydroxylase encoded by
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          . To address these questions, dopamine metabolism was monitored
          <em>
           in vivo
          </em>
          in the brains of chronically infected mammals and monitored
          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          during infection of neural cells.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="section toc-section" id="section2">
         <a class="link-target" data-toc="s2" id="s2" name="s2" title="Methods">
         </a>
         <h2>
          Methods
         </h2>
         <h3>
          Ethics statement
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          All animal work was performed according to national and international guidelines following approved animal procedures by the Beltsville Area Animal Care Committee, United States Department of Agriculture (Protocol no. 09-010Toxoplasmosis in mice; approved June 4, 2009). This protocol is reviewed annually, and any amendments are approved separately.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Growth of parasites and host cells
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec2.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          strains were maintained in human foreskin fibroblasts (HFFs) as previously described
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Gaskell1">
           [9]
          </a>
          . PC-12 cells obtained from ECACC (Salisbury) were maintained as described by the supplier.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Mouse strains
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec3.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Female Swiss Webster mice infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          VEG strain were used for histology.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Immunofluorescence assay of brain sections
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec2.sec4.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Immunofluorescence against multiple targets was performed on paraformaldehyde-fixed, paraffin-embedded mouse brain sections. Female Swiss Webster mice were infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          VEG strain oocysts 68 weeks prior to processing. Tissues were collected, formalin-fixed and paraffin-embedded using standard protocols and following approved guidelines. Slides were deparaffinized and rehydrated with an alcohol descending row, which was then followed by epitope retrieval in 10 mM sodium citrate buffer (pH 6.0) overnight at 60C following sectioning. Slides were blocked with 2% normal goat sera for 1 h at room temperature. TRITC-conjugated lectin from
          <em>
           Dolichos biflorus
          </em>
          (Cat # L9658, Sigma, St. Louis) was introduced to the slides for 4 h at room temperature, diluted 1:200 in primary staining solution (1% BSA, 0.1% cold fish skin gelatine, 0.5% Triton X-100 in 0.1 M PBS pH 7.2). Next, samples were washed (310 min) in wash buffer (TBS pH 8.4 with 0.1% Triton X-100 and 1% fish skin gelatin) and blocked using a biotin-streptavidin blocking kit (Cat # SP-2002, Vector Labs, Peterborough) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Samples were incubated with primary antibody (raised in rabbit) against dopamine (Cat # ab8888, Abcam, Cambridge, MA) (diluted 1:200) or tyrosine hydroxylase (Cat # ab112, Abcam) (diluted 1:500) overnight at 4C. Samples were rinsed with wash buffer and incubated for 1 h with biotinylated anti-rabbit IgG secondary antibody (Cat # B-1000, Vector Labs) diluted 1:500 in secondary antibody solution (0.05% Tween in 0.1 M PBS pH 7.2). Sections were rinsed and incubated with FITC-conjugated streptavidin (Cat # SA-5001, Vector Labs) diluted 1:100 according to the manufacturer's guidelines in secondary antibody buffer at room temperature. Finally, slides were rinsed in wash buffer containing DAPI and double-distilled water prior to mounting in Fluoromount G (Southern Biotech, Birmingham). All incubation and blocking steps were carried out in a wet chamber. All slides were kept at 4C in the dark before imaging using a Zeiss LSM510 META laser scanning inverted AxioVert 200M confocal microscope with DIC optics. 3D reconstructions of serial sections were generated with the same equipment using the LSM imaging software for the 3D deconvolution. To assess the specificity of dopamine staining, sections were incubated either without primary antibody or with primary anti-dopamine antibody in the presence of freshly prepared dopamine or serotonin for 30 min prior to and for overnight following addition to the sections.

          <em>

          </em>

          <em>

          </a>

          Results
         </h2>
         <h3>
          Dopamine metabolism in infected neural cells in brain tissue
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec1.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          A previous study found that the global content of dopamine in the brains of mice chronically infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          was increased by 14% (114% of uninfected (P&lt;0.01)), whereas other neurotransmitters were unchanged
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Stibbs1">
           [19]
          </a>
          . The localized effects of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection on dopamine metabolism in tissue cysts have not been examined.
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          forms intracellular tissue cysts in neurons with each tissue cyst containing hundreds of bradyzoites (slowly dividing stage) that may remain
          <em>
           in situ
          </em>
          through the host's lifetime
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Dubey1">
           [4]
          </a>
          . Formaldehyde-fixed brain sections from mice chronically infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          were probed with dopamine antibody (Abcam). Dopamine antibody staining was readily apparent in infected cells (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g001">
           Fig. 1
          </a>
          ). Surprisingly, the localization was primarily within the
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cysts containing the parasites visualized as intensely stained cysts (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g001">
           Figs. 1
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g002">
           2
          </a>
          ), rather than the host neural cell. The dopamine antibody staining in tissue cysts was punctate. Image rotation illuminated staining throughout the tissue cyst with most concentrated staining near the periphery. The antibody was raised against dopamine glutaraldehyde conjugated to bovine serum albumin (BSA). The antibody also labelled neurons in the amygdala and hippocampus in uninfected and infected mice (data not shown); areas with a high concentration of dopaminergic neurons. Intracellular tissue cysts were identified based on morphology (for immunohistochemistry) and by labeling the periphery of the tissue cysts with fluorescently-tagged lectin (for immunofluorescence)
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Coppin1">
           [20]
          </a>
          . DAPI counterstaining of nuclei visualized individual parasites in the tissue cysts, highlighting the hundreds of bradyzoites within each tissue cyst.
         </p>

         <p>
          The presence of dopamine in tissue cyst-containing neural cells was confirmed by cytochemical staining and competition assays. Glyoxylic acid staining, a classic method for detection of dopamine-containing cells by chemical reaction of glyoxylic acid with dopamine to produce a fluorescent product
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Lent1">
           [17]
          </a>
          , was applied. Interestingly, the tissue cyst infected cells fluoresced blue and white, with the entire cell body of the infected cell displaying fluorescent staining (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g002">
           Fig. 2A
          </a>
          ). Staining of the encysted parasites within cells and neural cell nuclei are black due to the presence of parasite and host nuclear chromatin. The lack of cytosolic staining in the immunofluorescent images with dopamine antibody are likely to be due to saturation of the image with the very intense cyst staining. The specificity of the dopamine antibody was confirmed by competition assays. Primary dopamine antibody staining of tissue sections was performed in the presence of exogenous dopamine followed by secondary staining with fluorescein labelled antibody. This eliminated staining as visualized by loss of fluorescence (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g002">
           Fig. 2B
          </a>
          ). In contrast, addition of exogenous serotonin (another catecholamine neurotransmitter) did not disrupt staining with the dopamine antibody. This verifies that the dopamine antibody is detecting dopamine. It remains possible that the dopamine antibody is also detecting the metabolic precursor to dopamine, L-DOPA, although manufacturer (Abcam) tests show a &gt;400-fold higher affinity for dopamine compared to L-DOPA using conjugates to BSA. Competition assays exhibited some decrease in staining with exogenous L-DOPA although this was not quantifiable (data not shown).
         </p>
         <h3>
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infected cells release high amounts of dopamine
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          To assess whether the dopamine detected in
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cysts could affect neurotransmission, the effect of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection on dopamine release from dopaminergic neural cells
          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          was determined. PC12 cells were utilized as this cell line is the most commonly used
          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          model of dopaminergic neurons. Dopaminergic PC12 cells were infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          parasites incubated under conditions that induce differentiation, and dopamine content and release were monitored by HPLC-ED. Conditions were used (as described in the Materials and
          <a href="#s2">
           Methods
          </a>
          ) for stage conversion of tachyzoites (the rapidly dividing stage of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          ) into the tissue cyst stages (ie. bradyzoites) with alkaline pH and decreased CO
          <sub>
           2
          </sub>
          content as described by others
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Dubey1">
           [4]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p2" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          The total dopamine in infected PC12 cultures was measured to determine whether infection increases the amount of dopamine synthesized in dopaminergic cells. Cultures were infected with different numbers of alkaline-induced
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          and total dopamine was quantitated by HPLC-ED following washes with low KCl buffer. We found that infected cultures accumulated significantly greater levels of dopamine and the increase correlated with infection rate (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g003">
           Fig. 3
          </a>
          ). Infection led to greater than three-fold increase in total dopamine content compared to mock-treated, uninfected cells.
         </p>

           <em>

           </em>
           infected dopaminergic cells.
          </div>

         <p>
          Dopamine release assays were performed with cultures of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          -infected PC12 cells to assess effects of infection on dopamine signalling. The cultures infected with different numbers of alkaline-induced
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          were induced to release dopamine with potassium as K+ causes release of dopamine in vesicles following methods in other studies
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Yamboliev1">
           [18]
          </a>
          . As a result of infection, dopamine release increased in infected cultures in a dose-dependent manner with the number of parasites in the culture correlating with the amount of dopamine released (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g003">
           Fig. 3
          </a>
          ). Dopamine release in infected cells was up to 350% greater compared to dopamine release in uninfected cells. Dopamine release was specific for high KCl induction since wash buffer (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g003">
           Fig. 3B
          </a>
          ) and media alone (data not shown) did not induce the release of detectable amounts of dopamine in infected or uninfected cultures. The low KCl wash ensures that the dopamine released is induced by potassium and not due to dopamine released by cell lysis of infected cells. The dopamine release reported here is the minimum amount increased by
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection as less than or equal to half of the cells in the cultures were infected. Normalizing for the infection rate results in a seven-fold increase in dopamine release in infected cells relative to uninfected PC12 cells.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p4" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec2.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          Taken together, an increase in dopamine content and an increase in dopamine release were observed in neural cells as a direct response to
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection. The enhanced dopamine release observed in infected cells in this study is likely to be an underestimate of the effect on dopamine release
          <em>
           in vivo
          </em>
          , cultured parasites contain few bradyzoites per vacuole compared to brain tissue cysts that contain hundreds of bradyzoites.
         </p>
         <h3>
          Tyrosine hydroxylase is expressed in bradyzoites
         </h3>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p1" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Tyrosine hydroxylase is the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine biosynthesis. Tyrosine hydroxylase localization in the brain sections of mice chronically infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          was determined to examine the expression of this crucial enzyme in infected neural cells. Significant levels of tyrosine hydroxylase were localized
          <em>
           within T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cysts in the brain sections of infected mice (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g004">
           Fig. 4
          </a>
          ). As expected, tyrosine hydroxylase was also found in the cytosol of neurons in the expected areas of the brain in both infected and uninfected mice (data not shown). Staining was not apparent in control sections that were treated with only secondary antibody. It is intriguing that both tyrosine hydroxylase and dopamine staining were localized in the tissue cysts of infected mouse brains, displaying similar staining patterns (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g001">
           Figs. 1
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g004">
           4
          </a>
          ). Thus, the rate limiting enzyme for dopamine synthesis and the product itself were both found
          <em>
           in T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cysts in the brain.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="pone-0023866-g004" name="pone-0023866-g004">
         </a>

         <p>
          It is possible that the tyrosine hydroxylase expression observed within the tissue cyst could be either the
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          -encoded tyrosine hydroxylase or neuronal tyrosine hydroxylase that has been imported from the host.
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          has complex interactions with its host cell and co-ops several host proteins (e.g. calpains), and hence could potentially import neuronal tyrosine hydroxylase into the tissue cyst
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Chandramohanadas1">
           [21]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p3" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          Alternatively,
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          could provide an enzyme with tyrosine hydroxylase activity. We previously described a
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          encoded tyrosine hydroxylase that could be expressed in the brain tissue cysts
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Gaskell1">
           [9]
          </a>
          .
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          has two copies of the tyrosine hydroxylase gene encoding nearly identical proteins (97.5%) with one gene induced in bradyzoite-stage parasites. The parasite tyrosine hydroxylase has a high degree of homology (53% identity) with mammalian tyrosine hydroxylases. Unique for tyrosine hydroxylases, the parasite orthologue enzyme contains a putative signal sequence that could permit the enzyme to be trafficked to an organelle or secreted by
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          . Additionally, it was observed that the commercial tyrosine hydroxylase antibody used in these studies recognizes the
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          encoded orthologue, as well as the mammalian tyrosine hydroxylases (unpublished observations). Indeed,
          <em>
           in vitro
          </em>
          cultivated parasites under alkaline conditions that induce formation of bradyzoites bind commercial tyrosine hydroxylase antibody (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g004">
           Fig. 4B
          </a>
          ). The tyrosine hydroxylase antibody stains the parasitophorous vacuole and also stains the periphery of parasites.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p4" name="article1.body1.sec3.sec3.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          To specifically identify parasite-encoded tyrosine hydroxylase within brain tissue, a custom antibody for
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tyrosine hydroxylase (TgTH) was developed. The target sequence of this custom antibody is located in the amino-terminal domain of TgTH, which is unique and divergent from mammalian tyrosine hydroxylases.
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tyrosine hydroxylase was localized within tissue cysts in neural cells in chronically-infected mouse brains (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g005">
           Fig. 5A
          </a>
          ). Staining was only detectable in tissue cysts in infected neurons. Western analysis was performed to validate the specificity of the TgTH antibody. The antibody recognized recombinant TgTH but did not bind mouse brain proteins, confirming the specificity of this antibody for
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tyrosine hydroxylases (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g005">
           Fig. 5B
          </a>
          ). Hence the intense dopamine antibody staining and TgTH are both found in
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          brain tissue cysts.
         </p>

         <a class="link-target" data-toc="s4" id="s4" name="s4" title="Discussion">
         </a>
         <h2>
          Discussion
         </h2>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p1" name="article1.body1.sec4.p1">
         </a>
         <p>
          Changes in behavior of the intermediate host that could lead to increased transmission of a parasite to its definitive host are likely to be positively selected as these changes would provide a significant benefit in completion of the parasite's life cycle.
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          induces behavioral alterations in infected rodents that would facilitate the transmission of the parasite to its definitive feline host, however, the mechanism responsible for these changes remains unclear. Our study provides a mechanism for these changes. Previous studies showing that anti-dopaminergic drugs can prevent the development of the behavior changes in rodents suggest that dopamine regulation altered by
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection of mammals
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Webster1">
           [7]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p2" name="article1.body1.sec4.p2">
         </a>
         <p>
          The altered behavior may be a direct effect or an indirect effect of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection. In this study, significant levels of dopamine was detected by immunohistochemistry in
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cysts in the brain (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g001">
           Fig. 1
          </a>
          ), as well as, increased dopamine release from dopaminergic cells infected with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g003">
           Fig. 3
          </a>
          ). Based on these novel findings, this is the first study to suggest that a parasite can directly alter dopamine signalling to mediate host behavior changes. These results provide a potential mechanism for
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          -induced host behavioural changes.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p3" name="article1.body1.sec4.p3">
         </a>
         <p>
          In our study, localizing the changes in dopamine metabolism during infection was crucial, as the location of dopamine metabolic changes in the brain is likely to be a critical factor for its effect on host behavior. Encysted
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          have been observed in functional neurons with intact synapses
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Melzer1">
           [22]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Ferguson1">
           [23]
          </a>
          . Tissue cysts have been detected throughout the brain, although higher percentages of cysts were reported in the amygdala and nucleus accumbens
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Vyas1">
           [5]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Gonzalez1">
           [24]
          </a>
          . These limbic brain regions are well known to contain dopamine that plays important functions in the control of movements (basal ganglia), reward to stimuli, pleasure, dependency (nucleus accumbens and hippocampus), motivation and cognition, and species and stimuli specific fear (amygdala). Altered dopamine levels induced by
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          in tissue cysts in these regions of the brain could have significant harmful consequences on a variety of brain functions, possibly leading to an array of behavioral changes and possible neurological malfunctions.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p4" name="article1.body1.sec4.p4">
         </a>
         <p>
          The observed intense dopamine staining within the
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cysts in brains was unexpected. Dopamine in neurons is synthesized in the cytosol, packaged into vesicles, and transported along axons
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Cartier1">
           [25]
          </a>
          . Thus, dopamine staining in neurons is primarily detected within vesicles. Indeed, cytosolic dopamine can induce cell apoptosis if it is not properly packaged into vesicles
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Ogawa1">
           [26]
          </a>
          . Packaged dopamine in neurons is rapidly transported away from the cell body to the axon terminal. In our brain sections, any dopamine released from the cyst into the cell body of the neuron would be packaged and transported by the efficient dopaminergic vesicle transport along axons. This may explain the apparent lower level of dopamine in the host cell body compared to the tissue cyst (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g001">
           Figs. 1
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g002">
           2
          </a>
          ). Alternatively, the observed staining by the dopamine antibody could be due to detection of L-DOPA within the
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tissue cyst that escapes the cyst and is metabolised into dopamine in the host cytosol. This interpretation is coherent with the observed cytosolic staining of infected neurons using glycoxylic acid that yields a specific product with dopamine (
          <a href="#pone-0023866-g002">
           Fig. 2
          </a>
          ). The parasite provides tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine synthesis but the source of DOPA decarboxylase required for conversion of L-DOPA to dopamine needs further investigation. DOPA decarboxylase present in the cytosol of dopaminergic neurons could provide this enzyme. A hypothetical nutrient pore expressed in the parasitophorous vacuole membrane of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          tachyzoites that permits the passage of metabolites (&lt;1300 Da) from the host cell cytosol into the parasitophorous vacuole could allow passage of small compounds from the vacuole into the host cytosol
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Schwab1">
           [27]
          </a>
          . If the pore is expressed in bradyzoites then it could provide a means for dopaminergic metabolites (L-DOPA, dopamine) to exit the vacuole and enter the host cytosol where L-DOPA would be converted to dopamine by cytosolic DOPA decarboxylase and dopamine would be packaged into secretory vesicles. The generation of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          mutants that will provide a conclusive dissection of the role of the parasite's tyrosine hydroxylase in the dopamine synthesis and release are in progress, but with either site of dopa decarboxylase action, the increased dopamine metabolism has important implications on the host neurochemistry.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p5" name="article1.body1.sec4.p5">
         </a>
         <p>
          In addition to dopamine, neurotransmitters such as serotonin and glutamate need to be considered in
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          -induced behavioral changes. Prior studies have proposed that the host immune response to
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection may lead to altered neurotransmitter levels
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Webster2">
           [28]
          </a>
          . Immunocompetent hosts control chronic
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection with a T-lymphocytedriven defense
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Denkers1">
           [29]
          </a>
          . Infection of mice with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          elicits a dominant Th1 response involving interferon-gamma (IFN-), interleukin-12 (IL-12), IL-18, and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-). TNF- induction has a serious impact on
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          induced pathology at early stages of infection. Th2-associated cytokines, such as IL-4 and IL-10, appear relatively late after infection and may limit immune pathology. To resolve acute infection, IFN- induces indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) release, resulting in tryptophan degradation and kynurenic acid accumulation
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Silva1">
           [30]
          </a>
          . Tryptophan depletion is thought to be responsible for suppression of the growth of the acute stage tachyzoites. Changes in serotonin levels were not observed in mice with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          chronic infections although there may be localized undetected changes
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Stibbs1">
           [19]
          </a>
          . Kynurenic acid accumulation in the CNS could potentially alter dopamine metabolism due to its NMDA antagonistic properties
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Mortensen1">
           [12]
          </a>
          . Thus, the host immune response to
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection could contribute to alterations in neurotransmitter levels that could affect behaviour in conjunction with the increased dopamine mediated by the parasite. Further studies are essential to investigate these possibilities.
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p6" name="article1.body1.sec4.p6">
         </a>
         <p>
          Behavioral changes associated with
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection may contribute to serious neurological disorders in humans. Several studies have observed an association between
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          seroprevalence with schizophrenia
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Brown1">
           [10]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Torrey1">
           [13]
          </a>
          . Since
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection has been found to last throughout the lifetime of the host, seroprevalence is likely to reflect chronic infection
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Dubey1">
           [4]
          </a>
          . Dopamine dysregulation is proposed to play a central role in schizophrenia, potentially in combination with glutamate metabolism. How dopamine dysregulation plays a role in schizophrenia, however, is still unknown. The principal antipsychotic drug that has been used to treat schizophrenia, dopamine antagonist haloperidol, can also block the development of behavior changes in
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infected rodents. It is possible that the increased dopamine accumulation and release observed during
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection may contribute to
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          associated schizophrenia. Dopamine metabolite concentrations have been inversely correlated with gray matter volume in schizophrenia patients, and recent MRI evidence found that the majority of volume reduction is in those patients seropositive for
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          , suggesting that
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection leads to an increase in dopamine metabolite concentrations
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Breier1">
           [31]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Horacek1">
           [32]
          </a>
          . It would be of interest to analyze the ability of other pathogens associated with schizophrenia, and other neurological disorders, to directly alter dopamine metabolism to see if other pathogens have this ability or if this phenomena is unique to
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          .
         </p>
         <a class="link-target" id="article1.body1.sec4.p7" name="article1.body1.sec4.p7">
         </a>
         <p>
          Malfunctions of dopamine metabolism have a serious impact on human behavior. Dopamine dysfunction has been associated with a variety of neurological disorders including schizophrenia, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, tic disorders, Tourette's syndrome, and dyskinesias. The novel findings of this study, that demonstrate
          <em>
           T. gondii's
          </em>
          ability to directly alter dopamine levels will not only help to better understand the relationship between schizophrenia and
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          seroprevalence, but these findings may be critical for understanding the mechanism(s) involved in a variety of pathogen-associated neurological disorders
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Brown1">
           [10]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Torrey1">
           [13]
          </a>
          . Thus, it is crucial to determine if other pathogens associated with neurological disorders also have the ability to directly alter dopamine levels. It is also critical to determine the possible contributions of
          <em>
           T. gondii
          </em>
          infection to other dopamine-related diseases
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Brynska1">
           [33]
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="ref-tip" href="#pone.0023866-Miman1">
           [34]
          </a>
          .
         </p>
        </div>
