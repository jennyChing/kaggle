          <div class="singlepost">
           <div class="post" id="post-main-14647">
            <div class="entry">
             <div class="navigation">
              <span class="prev">
               
               <a href="http://blogs.discovermagazine.com/collideascape/2015/03/14/climate-change-and-the-power-of-narrative/" rel="prev">

             <h1 class="post-title single">
              <a href="http://blogs.discovermagazine.com/collideascape/2015/03/18/a-badly-flawed-nyt-story-trumpets-cell-phone-health-dangers/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to A Badly Flawed NYT Story Trumpets Cell Phone Health Dangers">
               A Badly Flawed NYT Story Trumpets Cell Phone Health Dangers
              </a>
             </h1>
             <div class="meta">
              <span class="meta-date">
               By
               <a href="http://discovermagazine.com/authors?name=Keith+Kloor" style="color:#555">
                Keith Kloor
               </a>
               | 
		March 18, 2015 12:43 pm
              </span>
             </div>
             <div class="shareIcons" style="border-top:1px solid #999;border-bottom:1px solid #999;padding:7px 0 7px 0;margin-bottom:20px;">
              <div class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style addthis_32x32_style">
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_1">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_2">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_3">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_4">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_compact">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_counter addthis_bubble_style">
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              [
              <strong>
               UPDATE:
              </strong>
              <em>
               See media reaction at bottom of this post. Also, be sure to read the
               <a href="http://mobile.nytimes.com/2015/03/19/style/could-wearable-computers-be-as-harmful-as-cigarettes.html?ref=topics&amp;_r=1&amp;referrer=" target="_blank">
                correction at end of the NYT article
               </a>
               and the
               <a href="http://publiceditor.blogs.nytimes.com/2015/03/19/a-tech-column-on-wearable-gadgets-draws-fire-as-pseudoscience/?module=BlogPost-Title&amp;version=Blog%20Main&amp;contentCollection=Opinion&amp;action=Click&amp;pgtype=Blogs&amp;region=Body" target="_blank">
                response
               </a>
               fromthe NYT public editor
              </em>
              <strong>
               <em>
                .
               </em>
              </strong>
              ]
             </p>
             <p>
              Im racing to meet a deadline,but this
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/03/19/style/could-wearable-computers-be-as-harmful-as-cigarettes.html" target="_blank">
               story
              </a>
              in the
              <em>
               New York Time
              </em>
              s is so dismaying I had to take a few minutes to call attention to it. The headline alone is a red flag: Could Wearable Computers Be as Harmful as Cigarettes?
             </p>
             <p>
              It gets worse:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               We have long suspected that cellphones, which give off low levels of radiation, could lead to brain tumors, cancer, disturbed blood rhythms and other health problems, if held too close to the body for extended periods.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Who is we? The reporter, Nick Bilton (who covers technology), goes on to mention numerousstudies, some which are ambiguous, but one that
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               concluded that talking on a mobile or cordless phone for extended periods could triple the risk of a certain kind of brain cancer.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              The thrust of the discussion in the piece gives theimpression that
              <del>
               heavy
              </del>
              frequent cell phone users are atrisk of developing brain tumors. That would be mistaken, as the National Cancer Institute
              <a href="http://www.cancer.gov/cancertopics/causes-prevention/risk/radiation/cell-phones-fact-sheet" target="_blank">
               says
              </a>
              on its website. Another expert source apparently missed by Bilton isthe Mayo Clinic, which
              <a href="http://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-living/adult-health/expert-answers/cell-phones-and-cancer/faq-20057798" target="_blank">
               says
              </a>
              :
              <span id="more-14647">
              </span>
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               Currently, theres no consensus about the degree of cancer risk  if any  posed by cellphone use.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Instead, for a story on the potential health risks of wearable electronic gadgets (such as the new Apple watch), the reporter turns to
              <a href="http://twitter.com/mercola" target="_blank">
               Joseph Mercola
              </a>
              , an osteopath who is
              <a href="http://skepdic.com/mercola.html" target="_blank">
               notorious
              </a>
              for his
              <a href="http://www.quackwatch.com/11Ind/mercola.html" target="_blank">
               manyunsubstantiated medicalclaims
              </a>
              , some of which have drawn a
              <a href="http://www.fda.gov/ICECI/EnforcementActions/WarningLetters/2011/ucm250701.htm" target="_blank">
               warning
              </a>
              from the U.S. Food &amp; Drug Administration (FDA). The reader is not given this background information on Mercola, who is an integral part of the story after being introduced this way:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               Joseph Mercola, a physician who focuses on alternative medicine and has written extensively about the potential harmful effects of cellphones on the human body, said that as long as a wearable does not have a 3G connection built into it, the harmful effects are minimal, if any.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Hows that for unintended irony? And from a dubious source who has no authoritative standing whatsoever to comment on anyhealth-related matters.
             </p>
             <p>
              The NYT reporter concludes:
             </p>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               After researching this column, talking to experts and poring over dozens of scientific papers, I have realized the dangers of cellphones when used for extended periods, and as a result I have stopped holding my phone next to my head and instead use a headset during phone calls.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Can somebody at the
              <em>
               New York Times
              </em>
              please give this technology reporter a refresher course on how to sniff out pseudo expert sources, how to assess the merits of a given study, and lastly, how to weigh scientific evidence? Theres already enough
              <a href="http://neuroecology.wordpress.com/2014/12/13/why-is-reporting-on-health-and-science-so-bad-because-the-reporters-cant-do-their-jobs/" target="_blank">
               problematic journalism
              </a>
              on medical and health issues. Readers deserve better from an illustrious newspaper.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               UPDATE:
              </strong>
              Some folks have pointed out that the
              <em>
               Times
              </em>
              story appeared in the Styles section. My response:
             </p>
             <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
              <p>
               Where a story gets slotted in a newspaper (or a website) is irrelevant in the digital age. The internet doesnt care.
               <a href="http://t.co/VqPxdSGkYE">
                http://t.co/VqPxdSGkYE
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
                keith kloor (@keithkloor)
               <a href="https://twitter.com/keithkloor/status/578257829573865472">
                March 18, 2015
               </a>
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              <strong>
               UPDATE:
              </strong>
              The headline for the
              <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/03/19/style/could-wearable-computers-be-as-harmful-as-cigarettes.html?_r=0" target="_blank">
               online version
              </a>
              of the story now reads: The Health Concerns in Wearable Tech
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               UPDATE
              </strong>
              : Nice takedowns of the story
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/if-were-going-demonize-wearable-electronics-lets-be-rational-about-it" target="_blank">
               here
              </a>
              (
              <em>
               Popular Science)
              </em>
              and
              <a href="http://www.wired.com/2015/03/times-attack-wearables-really-attack-science/" target="_blank">
               here
              </a>
              (
              <em>
               Wired)
              </em>
              , though the headline in the latter is off-putting.
              <em>
               Gawker,
              </em>
              like everyone, is
              <a href="http://tktk.gawker.com/why-is-the-times-quoting-this-quack-about-the-danger-of-1692188966#" target="_blank">
               dumfounded
              </a>
              that Mercola is a primary source.
              <em>
               The Verge
              </em>
              , too, is
              <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2015/3/18/8252087/cell-phones-cancer-risk-tumor-bilton-new-york-times" target="_blank">
               astounded
              </a>
              . Phil Plaits
              <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/bad_astronomy/2015/03/19/wearable_tech_and_health_nyt_fear_mongering.html" target="_blank">
               jaw dropped
              </a>
              and Business Insider
              <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/smartwatch-cancer-apple-watch-nick-bilton-new-york-times-wrong-2015-3" target="_blank">
               asks
              </a>
              why the NYT is relying on alternative-health practitioners?
             </p>

             <p>
              Keith Kloor is a NYC-based journalist, and an adjunct professor of journalism at New York University. His work has appeared in Slate, Science, Discover, and the Washington Post magazine, among other outlets.   

From 2000 to 2008, he was a senior editor at Audubon Magazine.

In 2008-2009, he was a Fellow at the University of Colorados Center for Environmental Journalism, in Boulder, where he studied how a changing environment (including climate change) influenced prehistoric societies in the U.S. Southwest.
