      <h2>
       <a href="/technology/?type=opinion">
        Opinion
       </a>
      </h2>
      <h1>
       Premium smartphones: How expensive can they get?
      </h1>
      <cite>
       <span class="author">
        Alex Kidman
       </span>
       <span class="source">
        ABC Technology and Games
       </span>
       <span class="time">
        <!-- HTML5 equivalent: &lt;time&gt; -->
        24Sep2014
       </span>
      </cite>
     </div>
     <!-- end .header -->
     <div class="aside grid-6">
      <!-- HTML5 equivalent: &lt;aside&gt; -->
      <div class="figure">
       <figure>
        <a class="media" href="http://www.abc.net.au/reslib/201409/r1333429_18558327.jpg">
         <img alt="note edge" src="http://www.abc.net.au/reslib/201409/r1333429_18558275.jpg" title="note edge"/>
        </a>
        <figcaption>
         <p class="figcaption">
          With phones becoming more powerful than tablets and laptops, is it any wonder that they're becoming more expensive?
         </p>
        </figcaption>
       </figure>
      </div>
     </div>
     <!-- end .aside -->
     <a class="comment" href="#comments">
      Comment
     </a>
     <!-- id is for skiplink target -->
     <div class="text" id="content">
      <p class="first">
       Samsung's announcement of pricing for its Galaxy Note 4 and Galaxy Note Edge smartphones reveals a widening gap between "affordable" smartphones and premium handsets
      </p>
      <p>
       You could, if you like (and many do) blame Apple. It's always played in the premium smartphone space, and it makes no secret of the fact that it sees itself as a premium brand, priced accordingly. When the
       <a href="http://www.abc.net.au/technology/articles/2014/09/10/4085063.htm">
        iPhone 6 Plus was announced a couple of weeks ago
       </a>
       the headline price of $1249 for the 128GB model made more than a few heads turn, and not without reason. To give that figure some perspective, an entry level 11-inch Macbook Air (remembering again that Apple sells itself as a "premium" brand) only costs $1099.
      </p>
      <p>
       Am I the only one who finds it somewhat astonishing that a full function premium laptop can be had for less than the cost of a mobile phone?
      </p>
      <p>
       The thing is, Apple's not alone in pushing the price of premium handsets up quite markedly, as evidenced by Samsung's push for its own large-screen Note phones, the prices for which was announced in Sydney overnight. Samsung has got a lead on Apple in the large screen phone space, having kickstarted the whole idea with the original Note smartphone, but this year it's opted for two models; the baseline Galaxy Note 4 and the slightly unusual Galaxy Note Edge.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Note 4 is (more or less) the logical progression of the Note brand, with a higher-resolution screen, more sensitive "S-Pen" and faster processor. Not a bad recipe for a smartphone - I've not had anywhere near enough time with a Note 4 to generate much more of a considered opinion than that - and one with an interesting hook by way of the optional Gear VR headset that only works with the Galaxy Note 4.
      </p>
      <p>
       Developed in conjunction with Oculus, the Gear VR headset uses the Note 4 as the virtual "screen" for virtual reality experiences. The effect is astonishingly immersive, with Samsung showing off game and video demonstrations last night, albeit not without a few software quirks. Those can presumably be ironed out, although the Gear VR still won't suit everybody. You can't wear glasses while wearing the Gear VR, for a start. Instead, a small focus wheel on the top of the headset allows you to adjust focus to find a comfortable viewing spot that comes as close to your eyesight as possible. That won't suit every glasses wearer. Purely anecdotally, I was able to find a reasonable but not pin-sharp position in a few minutes of testing, but equally found that while it was genuinely immersive, it was also somewhat dizzying when emerging from my VR cocoon. There are places for the Gear VR without a doubt. It would make an excellent in-flight entertainment system, for example.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Galaxy Note Edge, on the other hand, marks an interesting innovation point for Samsung. It's much the same size as the Galaxy Note 4, but its big selling point is the inclusion of a secondary curved "edge" screen along the side, which can be used for additional controls such as camera or web browsing buttons. Again, I can't give any kind of real verdict on the Galaxy Note Edge, as half an hour in a noisy launch room is no place for considered reasoning, although I do wonder if anyone except Samsung will ever develop apps that seriously push the potential of a secondary smartphone screen.
      </p>
      <p>
       Features aside, the other headline aspect of the Galaxy Note 4 and Galaxy Note Edge is the pricing. The Note 4 will sell outright for $949 when it goes on sale on 29 October, while the Galaxy Note Edge will sell for $1249 when it goes on sale on 5 November. That's the same headline price as the top end Apple iPhone 6 Plus, and there's little doubt that the two titanic phones are intended to go head to head.
      </p>
      <p>
       The issue is that the gap between premium phones and the experiences they offer is widening quite a lot with relation to mid-to-entry-level phones. It's still true that there's a big difference between, say, the $100 Android phone you can pick up in a supermarket and a $1249 Android or iOS powerhouse, but when you start shifting more towards the mid range ground where companies like Motorola, LG and Oppo are pushing heavily, the differences lessen rapidly.
      </p>
      <p>
       Google has been a significant disruptor in this space with its low-cost, high end Nexus devices, and that trend appears to be continuing with reports that the next in the Nexus phone lineup will be manufactured by Motorola, while the accompanying Nexus tablet may well wear an HTC badge. Nexus devices have won plaudits amongst the Android faithful not only for their vanilla approach to Android - meaning that they're incredibly customisable and more often updated than any other Android device - but also because they've genuinely pushed against the usual "premium" pricing of high-end handsets. But with premium pricing set north of $1000, where will Google actually go with its next Nexus?
      </p>
      <p>
       Then again, having witnessed the lineup for Apple's iPhone at launch in Sydney, with queues stretching for a good 500m and a lineup of more than 1000, perhaps there's plenty of life in selling handsets with premium price points even if - as some estimates suggest - around half of the line were overseas resellers from where territories where the iPhone 6/Plus hasn't launched yet.
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        Alex Kidman is a freelance technology writer; follow him on
        <a href="https://twitter.com/alexkidman">
         Twitter @alexkidman
        </a>
        .
       </em>
      </p>
