     <p>
      <b>
       Felix Graf von Luckner
      </b>
      (9 June 1881  13 April 1966), sometimes called in English
      <b>
       Count Luckner
      </b>
      , was a German nobleman, naval officer, author, and sailor who earned the epithet
      <i>
       Der Seeteufel
      </i>
      (the Sea-Devil), and his crew that of
      <i>
       Die Piraten des Kaisers
      </i>
      (the Emperor's Pirates), for his exploits in command of the sailing
      <a href="/wiki/Commerce_raiding" title="Commerce raiding">
       commerce raider
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMS_Seeadler_(Windjammer)" title="SMS Seeadler (Windjammer)">
       SMS
       <i>
        Seeadler
       </i>
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Sea Eagle
      </i>
      ) between 1916 and 1917.
     </p>
     <p>
      It was Luckner's habit of successfully waging war without casualties which made him a hero and a legend on both sides.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Luckner was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">
       Dresden
      </a>
      , Germany, the great-grandson of
      <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Luckner" title="Nicolas Luckner">
       Nicolas Luckner
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marshal_of_France" title="Marshal of France">
       Marshal of France
      </a>
      and commander-in-chief of the French Army of the Rhine, who in the 18th century was elevated to the rank of Count (
      <i>
       Graf
      </i>
      ) by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Denmark" title="King of Denmark">
       King of Denmark
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_journey">
       First journey
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First journey">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The young Luckner had dreams of being a sailor, but his father was determined that he should follow the family tradition and go into the
      <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">
       cavalry
      </a>
      . After failing his exams at various private schools, at the age of thirteen Luckner ran away to sea, with the promise in his mind that he would not return until he was wearing "the Emperor's naval uniform, and with honour". He signed up, under the assumed name of "Phylax Ldecke", as an unpaid cabin boy on the Russian sailing ship
      <i>
       Niobe
      </i>
      travelling between
      <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">
       Hamburg
      </a>
      (Germany) and Australia. His story might have ended there, because the Russian captain, fearing that the lives of other crew members would be endangered, refused to allow a lifeboat to be launched in order to pick Luckner up when he fell overboard in the middle of the ocean. The
      <a href="/wiki/Chief_mate" title="Chief mate">
       chief mate
      </a>
      defied the captain (who had threatened him with a harpoon), and launched a lifeboat with the help of volunteers. As a number of albatrosses circled over Luckner, one swooped down and seized his outstretched hand in its beak, but Luckner grabbed the bird in desperation. Although severely pecked, he hung on for his life. The flapping of the bird's huge wings and the circling of the other albatrosses gave the crew of the lifeboat a point to aim at in his rescue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jack-of-all-trades">
       Jack-of-all-trades
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jack-of-all-trades">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Arriving at
      <a href="/wiki/Fremantle" title="Fremantle">
       Fremantle
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">
       Western Australia
      </a>
      , Luckner jumped ship and for seven years worked in a bewildering array of occupations: he was a seller of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" title="Salvation Army">
       Salvation Army
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_War_Cry" title="The War Cry">
        The War Cry
       </a>
      </i>
      ; an assistant lighthouse keeper at the
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_Leeuwin_Lighthouse" title="Cape Leeuwin Lighthouse">
       Cape Leeuwin Lighthouse
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Western_Australia" title="Augusta, Western Australia">
       Augusta, Western Australia
      </a>
      , a job he abandoned when he was discovered with his hotelkeeper's daughter by her father; a kangaroo hunter; a circus worker; a professional
      <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">
       boxer
      </a>
      (due to his exceptional strength); a fisherman; a seaman; a guard in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Army" title="Mexican Army">
       Mexican Army
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Porfirio_D%C3%ADaz" title="Porfirio Daz">
       President Daz
      </a>
      , a railway construction worker, a barman, and a tavern keeper. He was incarcerated for a short time in a
      <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
       Chilean
      </a>
      jail accused of stealing pigs, he twice suffered broken legs, and he was thrown out of a hospital in
      <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">
       Jamaica
      </a>
      for lack of money.
     </p>
     <p>
      Luckner was also an accomplished magician.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_II_of_Germany" title="Wilhelm II of Germany">
       Kaiser Wilhelm
      </a>
      was fascinated by his tricks and frequently invited Luckner on board his yacht to entertain important dignitaries.

          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Back_in_Germany">
       Back in Germany
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Back in Germany">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      At the age of twenty, Luckner entered a German navigation training school, where he passed the examinations for his mate's ticket. By 1908 he had joined the
      <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_S%C3%BCd" title="Hamburg Sd">
       Hamburg-Sdamerikanische Line
      </a>
      steamer
      <i>
       Petropolis
      </i>
      , intending to serve for nine months before volunteering to serve in the
      <a href="/wiki/Imperial_German_Navy" title="Imperial German Navy">
       Imperial German Navy
      </a>
      for a year, to obtain a naval commission. He had vowed not to return to his family except in uniform and was eventually welcomed back by his family, who had given him up for lost. In February 1912 Luckner was finally called up by the Navy and served on the gunboat
      <a href="/wiki/SMS_Panther" title="SMS Panther">
       SMS
       <i>
        Panther
       </i>
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="First_World_War">
       First World War
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First World War">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the early months of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
       First World War
      </a>
      Felix von Luckner saw action at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Heligoland_(1914)" title="Battle of Heligoland (1914)">
       Battle of Heligoland Bight
      </a>
      (1914). At the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jutland" title="Battle of Jutland">
       Battle of Jutland
      </a>
      he commanded a gun turret on board the battleship
      <a href="/wiki/SMS_Kronprinz_Wilhelm" title="SMS Kronprinz Wilhelm">
       <i>
        Kronprinz Wilhelm
       </i>
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      At the beginning of the War, Germany converted a considerable number of merchant ships into
      <a href="/wiki/Merchant_raider" title="Merchant raider">
       merchant raiders
      </a>
      by equipping them with guns and sending them in search of Allied merchant shipping. Most of the armed raiders were not particularly successful, but they did tie up considerable Allied forces in hunting them. By early 1915, most of the armed raiders had either been hunted down and sunk or else had run out of fuel and been interned in neutral ports.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1452" data-file-width="1146" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG/220px-SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG/330px-SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG/440px-SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SMSSeeadlerFront.PNG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        SMS
        <i>
         Seeadler
        </i>
        , the three-masted windjammer that raided the Atlantic and Pacific in an age of dreadnoughts (painting by
        <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Rave" title="Christopher Rave">
         Christopher Rave
        </a>
        )
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Hoping to revive commerce raiding, the Imperial Navy equipped the impounded three-masted sailing ship
      <i>
       Pass of Balmaha
      </i>
      (1,571 tons) with two 105mm guns hidden behind hinged gunwales, several machine guns, and two carefully hidden 500 HP auxiliary engines. She was commissioned as the auxiliary cruiser
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMS_Seeadler_(Windjammer)" title="SMS Seeadler (Windjammer)">
        Seeadler
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Sea Eagle"). As he was almost the only officer in the German Navy with extensive experience of large sailing ships, Luckner was appointed to command her.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Voyage_of_Seeadler">
       The Voyage of
       <i>
        Seeadler
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Voyage of Seeadler">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Raider">
       Raider
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Raider">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      left port on 21 December 1916 and managed to slip through the British blockade disguised as a Norwegian ship. Many of the crew of six officers and 57 men, including Luckner himself, had been selected for their ability to speak Norwegian, in case they were intercepted by the British. By Christmas Day,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      was southeast of Iceland, where she encountered the British armed merchant cruiser
      <i>
       Avenger
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       Avenger
      </i>
      put an inspection party aboard, but failed to detect the German deception.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 9 January 1917,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      came upon a single-funneled steamer, raised a signal requesting a time signal (not an uncommon thing for a sailing ship long out of contact with land to do), and raised the German ensign too late for the target ship to take any evasive action. Three shots were needed to persuade the 3,268 ton
      <i>
       Gladys Royle
      </i>
      , carrying coal from
      <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">
       Cardiff
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
       Buenos Aires
      </a>
      , to heave to. Her crew was taken off unharmed, and she was
      <a href="/wiki/Scuttling" title="Scuttling">
       scuttled
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The following day,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      encountered another steamship, which refused to identify itself. The German ensign was raised and a shot fired across the bow of the
      <i>
       Lundy Island
      </i>
      , which was carrying sugar from
      <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">
       Madagascar
      </a>
      . The steamer still refused to heave to, and Luckner fired four rounds directly at her. The steamer then hove to and lowered her boats, but her captain ignored an order to come to
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      . A German boarding party was sent over and discovered that the crew had abandoned ship when the first shots were fired, leaving the captain alone on board. Captain Bannister later told Luckner that he had previously been captured by a German raider and had given his parole, which he had broken; thus, he was not anxious to be a
      <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">
       prisoner of war
      </a>
      again. Luckner continued his voyage southwards, and by 21 January he was in mid-
      <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
       Atlantic
      </a>
      between
      <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
       Brazil
      </a>
      and West Africa when he found the 2,199 ton French three-masted barque
      <i>
       Charles Gounod
      </i>
      , which was loaded with corn. She was quickly dispatched, but her log book recorded information about other ships she had met and their intended route.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 24 January, the small 364-ton Canadian schooner
      <i>
       Perce
      </i>
      was met and sunk by machine gun fire, after taking off her crew and her captain's new bride. The 3,071 ton French four-master
      <i>
       Antonin
      </i>
      , which was loaded with Chilean
      <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">
       saltpetre
      </a>
      , was overhauled on 3 February and soon scuttled. On 9 February, the 1,811 ton Italian
      <i>
       Buenos Aires
      </i>
      , also carrying saltpetre, was sunk. On 19 February, a four-masted barque was spotted, which immediately piled on sail in an effort to get away; however,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      's engines allowed her to overhaul the 2,431 ton British steamer
      <i>
       Pinmore
      </i>
      , which was carrying a cargo of grain. By coincidence, Luckner had himself sailed in
      <i>
       Pinmore
      </i>
      in his civilian sailing days, back in 1902. He took
      <i>
       Pinmore
      </i>
      into
      <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">
       Rio de Janeiro
      </a>
      in order to get more supplies, before eventually scuttling her.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next ship to be stopped was the
      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Danish
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">
       barque
      </a>
      <i>
       Viking
      </i>
      , but as there was nothing unusual about her cargo the neutral ship was allowed to proceed unmolested.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg/290px-Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg/435px-Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg/580px-Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg 2x" width="290"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Felixvonluckergermanensign.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The ensign which Luckner would raise on the
        <i>
         Seeadler
        </i>
        to convey hostile intent is now on display at the
        <a href="/wiki/Auckland_War_Memorial_Museum" title="Auckland War Memorial Museum">
         Auckland War Memorial Museum
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="More_victims">
       More victims
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: More victims">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      On the morning of 26 February, the 1,953 ton British barque
      <i>
       British Yeoman
      </i>
      , carrying a welcome cargo including chickens and pigs, was stopped and sunk; the same evening the French four-master
      <i>
       Le Rochefoucauld
      </i>
      fell victim to the
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      . The boarding party discovered
      <i>
       Le Rochefoucauld
      </i>
      had only recently been stopped by a British cruiser which was looking for
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      On the evening of 5 March,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      discovered a four-masted barque in the moonlight and signalled "Stop immediately! German Cruiser". Bizarrely, the captain of the 2,206 ton French ship
      <i>
       Dupleix
      </i>
      rowed across to
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      , convinced another French captain was playing a practical joke on him. He was soon disabused of the idea when his ship was scuttled.
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      's next victim on 10 March was asked for the time, but ignored the signal. Luckner ordered a smoke generator to be lit, and the 3,609 ton
      <i>
       Horngarth
      </i>
      turned back to render assistance to the 'burning' sailing ship. A single shot put the British ship's radio out of action; this resulted in the only loss of life in the
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      's voyage. A British sailor, Douglas Page, was killed when a steam pipe was ruptured by the shot.
      <i>
       Horngarth
      </i>
      was soon scuttled by
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      's now experienced crew.
     </p>
     <p>
      By this time, Luckner had the problem of feeding and keeping safe nearly 300 prisoners, in addition to his own crew. Consequently, when on 20 March, the French four-masted barque
      <i>
       Cambronne
      </i>
      was captured, Luckner arranged for the ship's
      <a href="/wiki/Topgallant_sail" title="Topgallant sail">
       topgallant
      </a>
      mast and additional spars and sails to be removed, before putting his prisoners aboard
      <i>
       Cambronne
      </i>
      under the command of Captain Mullen of
      <i>
       Pinmore
      </i>
      . The much-reduced rigging on
      <i>
       Cambronne
      </i>
      ensured
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      would be able to escape before her location could be reported to the hunting ships.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">
       Royal Navy
      </a>
      was well aware of
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      's general location and set a trap consisting of the armed merchant cruisers
      <i>
       Otranto
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Orbita
      </i>
      and the armoured cruiser
      <a href="/wiki/HMS_Lancaster_(1902)" title="HMS Lancaster (1902)">
       HMS
       <i>
        Lancaster
       </i>
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">
       Cape Horn
      </a>
      . However, a severe storm blew
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      considerably further south, before she entered the Pacific Ocean on 18 April and sailed north along the Chilean coast. By early June,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      was east of
      <a href="/wiki/Kiritimati" title="Kiritimati">
       Christmas Island
      </a>
      and learned that the United States had entered the war.
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      therefore turned her attention to American shipping, sinking the 529-ton
      <i>
       A. B. Johnson
      </i>
      of San Francisco on 14 June, the 673 ton
      <i>
       R. C. Slade
      </i>
      the next day, and the schooner
      <i>
       Manila
      </i>
      on 8 July. By this time,
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      needed to be laid up so that her hull could be scraped clean. She put into the small island of Mopelia, also known as
      <a href="/wiki/Maupihaa" title="Maupihaa">
       Maupihaa
      </a>
      , a coral atoll some 10km (6mi) in diameter in the
      <a href="/wiki/Society_Islands" title="Society Islands">
       Society Islands
      </a>
      , some 450km (280mi) from Tahiti.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wrecking_and_stranding">
       Wrecking and stranding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wrecking and stranding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      was too large to enter the sheltered lagoon of Mopelia, and consequently had to anchor outside the reef. On 24 August, disaster struck. According to Luckner, the ship was struck by a
      <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">
       tsunami
      </a>
      which wrecked her on the reef. However, some American prisoners alleged that the ship drifted aground while the prisoners and most of the crew were having a picnic on the island.
     </p>
     <p>
      The crew and their 46 prisoners were now stranded on Mopelia, but they managed to salvage provisions, firearms, and two of the ship's boats.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hide_and_seek">
       Hide and seek
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hide and seek">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Luckner decided to sail with five of his men in one of the 10m (33ft) long open boats, rigged as a
      <a href="/wiki/Sloop" title="Sloop">
       sloop
      </a>
      and named
      <i>
       Kronprinzessin Cecilie
      </i>
      . Ever the optimist, he intended to sail to
      <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">
       Fiji
      </a>
      by way of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">
       Cook Islands
      </a>
      , capture a sailing ship, return to Mopelia for his crew and prisoners, and resume his raiding career.
     </p>
     <p>
      Three days after leaving Mopelia, the seamen reached
      <a href="/wiki/Atiu" title="Atiu">
       Atiu
      </a>
      Island, where they pretended to be Dutch-American mariners crossing the Pacific for a bet. The New Zealand
      <a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner" title="Resident Commissioner">
       Resident
      </a>
      , the administrator of the island, gave them enough supplies to reach another island in the group,
      <a href="/wiki/Aitutaki" title="Aitutaki">
       Aitutaki
      </a>
      , where they posed as Norwegians. The New Zealand Resident in Aitutaki was suspicious but had no means of detaining the group, and Luckner quickly took his party to the island of
      <a href="/wiki/Rarotonga" title="Rarotonga">
       Rarotonga
      </a>
      . Approaching Rarotonga in the dark, Luckner saw a dark ship which he thought was an auxiliary cruiser, but in fact it was a beached ship.
     </p>
     <p>
      Luckner pressed on to the Fijian
      <a href="/wiki/Wakaya_Island" title="Wakaya Island">
       Wakaya Island
      </a>
      , arriving after a voyage of 3,700km (2,300mi) in an open boat. Most people on Wakaya accepted the Germans' story of being shipwrecked Norwegians, but one sceptic called a party of police from the old Fijian capital of
      <a href="/wiki/Levuka" title="Levuka">
       Levuka
      </a>
      . On 21 September, the police threatened that a non-existent gun on the inter-island ferry
      <i>
       Amra
      </i>
      would blow Luckner out of the water. Not wishing to cause bloodshed, and not realizing the police were unarmed, Luckner and his party surrendered
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and were confined in a prisoner-of-war camp on
      <a href="/wiki/Motuihe_Island" title="Motuihe Island">
       Motuihe Island
      </a>
      , off
      <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">
       Auckland
      </a>
      , New Zealand.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile, back on Mopelia, a small French trading ship, the
      <i>
       Lutece
      </i>
      , anchored outside the reef. Leutnant Kling of
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      , having heard on the radio of his captain's capture, sailed out to
      <i>
       Lutece
      </i>
      and captured her at gunpoint. The French crew was put ashore with the other prisoners, and all the Germans embarked on the ship, which they renamed the
      <i>
       Fortuna
      </i>
      , and set course for South America. The master of
      <i>
       A. B. Johnson
      </i>
      , Captain Smith, then took the remaining open boat from Mopelia with three other American seamen, and sailed 1,600km (990mi) to
      <a href="/wiki/Pago_Pago" title="Pago Pago">
       Pago Pago
      </a>
      , arriving on 4 October, where they were finally able to inform the authorities of the activities of
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      and arrange for the rescue of the other 44 sailors left stranded on Mopelia.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Fortuna
      </i>
      , meanwhile, came to grief when she struck uncharted rocks off
      <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">
       Easter Island
      </a>
      . The crew scrambled ashore, where they were interned by the Chileans for the remainder of the war.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Escape">
       Escape
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Escape">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Luckner still refused to accept that the war was over for him. The commander of the
      <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">
       prisoner of war
      </a>
      camp at Motuihe had a fast motor boat, the
      <i>
       Pearl
      </i>
      , at his disposal, and on 13 December 1917, Luckner faked setting up a play for Christmas with his men and used his provisions for the play to plan his escape. He and other prisoners seized the
      <i>
       Pearl
      </i>
      and made for the
      <a href="/wiki/Coromandel_Peninsula" title="Coromandel Peninsula">
       Coromandel Peninsula
      </a>
      . Using a machine gun, Luckner then seized the 90-ton scow
      <i>
       Moa
      </i>
      and, with the help of a handmade sextant and a map copied from a school atlas,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      he sailed for the
      <a href="/wiki/Kermadec_Islands" title="Kermadec Islands">
       Kermadec Islands
      </a>
      , which was a New Zealand provisioning station, with larger ships anchored there. A pursuing auxiliary ship, the
      <i>
       Iris
      </i>
      , had guessed Luckner's probable destination and caught up with him on 21 December. A year after his mission began, the war finally ended for Felix von Luckner. He spent the remainder of the war in various prisoner of war camps in New Zealand, including
      <a href="/wiki/Ripapa_Island" title="Ripapa Island">
       Ripapa Island
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Lyttelton_Harbour" title="Lyttelton Harbour">
       Lyttelton Harbour
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hero_or_braggart_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hero_or_braggart-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      before being repatriated to Germany in 1919.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Luckner was married twice. He married Petra Schultz from
      <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">
       Hamburg
      </a>
      , with whom he had a daughter, Inge-Maria, born in 1913. They were divorced in 1914. On 24 September 1924 he married Ingeborg Engestrm in
      <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malm">
       Malm
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_life">
       Postwar life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Postwar life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On 12 May 1921, Luckner became a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freemason" title="Freemason">
       Freemason
      </a>
      of the Lodge
      <i>
       Zur goldenen Kugel
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       Groe Landesloge von Deutschland
      </i>
      ) in Hamburg. He wrote a book about his wartime adventures which became a bestseller in Germany, and a book about him by
      <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Thomas" title="Lowell Thomas">
       Lowell Thomas
      </a>
      spread his fame more widely.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1926 Luckner raised funds to buy a sailing ship which he called the
      <i>
       Vaterland
      </i>
      and he set out on a goodwill mission around the world, leaving
      <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">
       Bremen
      </a>
      on 19 September and arriving in New York on 22 October 1926. An entertaining speaker, he was widely admired for his seamanship and for having fought his war with such a minimal loss of life. This opened many doors for him in the United States, where he spoke on hundreds of occasions across the country, both in German and later, increasingly, in English. He won the support of many notable people, including diplomats, politicians, and even some of the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">
       American Legion
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">
       Henry Ford
      </a>
      presented Luckner with a motor car, and the city of San Francisco made him an honorary citizen. US President
      <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">
       Calvin Coolidge
      </a>
      wanted to meet him, but Luckner declined at the request of his government. Feeling that his "goodwill mission", as he called it in his travelogue,
      <i>
       Seeteufel erobert Amerika
      </i>
      ("Sea-devil conquers America"), could neither have greater success elsewhere, nor could he be financially sustained by the income as a speaker, however popular and successful; he returned to Germany, where he arrived on 19 April 1928.
     </p>
     <p>
      Luckner was a frequent visitor to the Heydrich house in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halle,_Saxony-Anhalt" title="Halle, Saxony-Anhalt">
       Halle
      </a>
      , where his stories of the adventures of
      <i>
       Seeadler
      </i>
      inspired a young
      <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Heydrich" title="Reinhard Heydrich">
       Reinhard Heydrich
      </a>
      to join the inter-war
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Reichsmarine" title="Reichsmarine">
        Reichsmarine
       </a>
      </i>
      . In 1937 and 1938, Luckner and his wife undertook a round-the-world voyage in his yacht
      <i>
       Seeteufel
      </i>
      , and they were welcomed in New Zealand and Australia, although some
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
         <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2012)">
          who?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      viewed him as an apologist for the
      <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">
       Nazi
      </a>
      regime. During their visit
      <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">
       Queensland
      </a>
      , Australia, the Luckners were feted by the press and public. The
      <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">
       Brisbane
      </a>
      office of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Investigation_Branch" title="Commonwealth Investigation Branch">
       Commonwealth Investigation Branch
      </a>
      (CIB), maintained a surveillance of the Luckners during their visit, with the inspector in charge of the CIB in Brisbane, Bob Wake, attending a gala function held in honour of the German couple. The gala menu was decorated with a
      <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">
       swastika
      </a>
      . The CIB kept detailed records of all of their contacts, and when
      <a href="/wiki/Military_history_of_Australia_during_World_War_II" title="Military history of Australia during World War II">
       Australia declared war on Germany
      </a>
      many of these contacts were rounded up and interned.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
       Second World War
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">
       Adolf Hitler
      </a>
      tried to use Luckner for propaganda purposes, although as a Freemason Luckner was not in one of the Nazis' favoured groups of people. In 1939 he was implicated in a scandal and put on trial before a
      <i>
       Sonderehrengericht
      </i>
      (Special Court of Honour) for
      <a href="/wiki/Incest" title="Incest">
       incest
      </a>
      and for having sexual congress with a minor, but he was never convicted (it is said that his retirement from public life was a condition for the discontinuation of the trial.)
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Luckner refused to renounce his membership of the Freemasons and the various honorary citizenships granted in the US, and consequently he suffered by having his bank account frozen. In 1943, he saved the life of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">
       Jewish
      </a>
      woman, Rose Janson, whom he provided with a passport he found on a bombsite, and who subsequently managed to escape to the United States via a neutral country. At the end of the war, the mayor of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halle,_Saxony-Anhalt" title="Halle, Saxony-Anhalt">
       Halle
      </a>
      , where he was living, asked Luckner to negotiate the town's surrender to the approaching American forces, which he did, although he did not return to the town after hearing that the Nazis had condemned him to death.
     </p>
     <p>
      Luckner had great physical strength and was noted for his ability to bend coins between the thumb, index, and middle finger of his right hand and to tear up telephone directories (the thickest being that of New York City), with his bare hands. On the occasion of his visit to Australia in 1938, the Sydney
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Labor_Daily" title="Labor Daily">
        Labor Daily
       </a>
      </i>
      published a cartoon showing Kaiser Wilhelm tearing up the
      <a href="/wiki/German_invasion_of_Belgium" title="German invasion of Belgium">
       Belgian Neutrality Pact
      </a>
      , Adolf Hitler tearing up another agreement, and Luckner tearing up a directory, with the caption "They All Have the Habit".
     </p>
     <p>
      Luckner was a prolific autograph signer, and his original autographs turn up frequently at auctions and estate sales.
     </p>
     <p>
      After the Second World War, Luckner moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      , where he lived in
      <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malm">
       Malm
      </a>
      with his Swedish second wife Ingeborg Engestrm until his death in Malm at the age of 84 in 1966. However, his body was returned to Germany and was buried in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_Cemetery_Ohlsdorf" title="Main Cemetery Ohlsdorf">
       Main Cemetery Ohlsdorf
      </a>
      , Hamburg.
     </p>
     <h2>

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_von_Niezychowski" title="Alfred von Niezychowski">

      's book
      <i>
       The Cruise of the Kronprinz Wilhelm
      </i>

      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_liner_Kronprinz_Wilhelm" title="German liner Kronprinz Wilhelm">
       <i>
        Kronprinz Wilhelm
       </i>
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="TV_series">
       TV series
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

     <p>
      Between 1973 and 1975, a Franco-German Co-production company produced a 39-episode adventure series entitled
      <i>
       "Graf Luckner"
      </i>
      for the German
      <a href="/wiki/ARD_(broadcaster)" title="ARD (broadcaster)">
       ARD
      </a>
      television network, featuring Luckner as the hero. The French sub-title was
      <i>
       "Les Aventures du Capitaine Luckner"
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Episode 26 of the television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tales_from_Te_Papa" title="Tales from Te Papa">
        Tales from Te Papa
       </a>
      </i>
      featured the sextant used by Luckner in his attempt to escape from captivity in New Zealand.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Count_Felix_von_Luckner_Society">
       The Count Felix von Luckner Society
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_von_Luckner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The Count Felix von Luckner Society">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On 29 March 2004 the society "Felix Graf von Luckner Gesellschaft" was founded in Halle, with the objective of commemorating Luckner's life and work, especially his role in safeguarding the city of Halle in April 1945. The Society also wishes to create a memorial and museum for Luckner in Halle and to restore his yacht
      <i>
       Seeteufel
      </i>
      , which is currently in poor condition and lying in Russia. Within a few months of its creation, the Society had over 100 members in 14 countries.
     </p>
