      <h1 class="name post-title entry-title" itemprop="itemReviewed" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Thing">
       <span itemprop="name">
        5 of the Best Regional Desserts America Has to Offer
       </span>
      </h1>
      <p class="post-meta">
       <span>
        September 24, 2013
       </span>
      </p>
      <div class="clear">
      </div>
      <div class="entry">
       <h1>
        <a href="http://blog.foodydirect.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/images-1.jpeg">
         <img alt="images-1" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2093" height="183" src="http://blog.foodydirect.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/images-1.jpeg" width="275"/>
        </a>
       </h1>
       <p>
        If youre like many of todays travelers, you know that one of the best and most enjoyable ways to get the true flavor of a destination is to try foods and restaurants unique to your destination. Even better, our nation has so many distinctive and delicious regional cooking styles and specialties that its nearly impossible not to find something uniquely wonderful wherever you travel. While looking to websites and mobile apps like Google, Yelp, Zagat, and Pinterest will help you learn about best restaurants, cafes, wine bars, and ice cream shops worth seeking on your next trip, we also recommend you keep your eyes and taste buds on alert for the following five regional desserts. Should you head to any of the regions that feature them, you can taste for yourself why we consider them among the nations very best!
       </p>
       <h1>
        Hawaiis Shave Ice
       </h1>
       <p>
        As if you need another reason to plan a trip to Hawaii, the islands are known for shave ice. Although not actually invented in the region, the people of Hawaii have mastered the art of the frozen dessert to the point that foodies literally wait in line for an hour to get their hands on a paper cone overflowing with the finest, fluffy powdery-snow-like flavored ice money can buy. Sweetened with exceptional syrups ranging in flavor from coconut to lychee to passion fruit to tamarindplus all the usual fruit flavorsand often accompanied by optional additions such as sweet adzuki beans and vanilla ice cream, this frozen treat is so much better than words can describe, you really have to try it for yourself. But dont expect the same experience at many places outside of the islands. The machines that make this particularly fluffy and fine ice cost upward of $15,000, making it less approachable to many establishments not specializing in the dessert. Plus, at the better Hawaiian shave ice stands the ice is flavored with locally produced juices and syrups made with Hawaiis own cane sugar. For your own taste of paradise, try any of the famous shave ice shops on the islands, such as Ululanis on Maui, or
        <a href="http://www.islandsnow.com/news/President-Obama-at-Island-Snow-Kailua" target="_blank">
         Island Snow in Oahu
        </a>
        . The latter is a favorite stop of President Barack Obama
       </p>
       <h1>
        New York Citys Black-and-White Cookies
       </h1>
       <p>
        Before Jerry Seinfeld famously utilized one to espouse his views on racial harmony to Elaine, black-and-white cookies were already long being eaten and enjoyed by New Yorkers in cafes all across the state. Made of circular sponge cake-like shortbread topped with half vanilla and half chocolate icing, the black and white cookie is similar to many German-style iced treats, such as the Amish sugar cookie
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        In the early 20
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century, these cookies were called Half-Moons and first sold at Utilca, New Yorks Hemstroughts Bakery. Their base cookie flavors varied from the classic lemony-vanilla often seen today to chocolate and coconut. Hemstroughts no longer has a storefront, but they still bake their classic cookies for vendors. If youre not a New Yorker and want to try one, check out your local NYC-style delitheyre a countertop staple.
       </p>
       <h1>
        South Dakotas Kuchen
       </h1>
       <p>
        If you ever wanted to travel to South Dakota, heres one good reason to make the trip: kuchen. A dessert rooted in German tradition, Kuchen is the German word for cake, and predictably came to the states from German immigrants in the 19
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century. Similar to a coffeecake, it is the official state dessert of South Dakota, and a symbol of the states heritage. But dont expect one type of kuchen. The term covers a large variety of desserts, including rolled log-style cakes with filling, fruit-topped cheesecakes, and a type of frosted pie. With so many options, its good to pinpoint the basics of a good kuchencakes raised with yeast. The allowance of yeast enables kuchen to bake up light and fluffy. Although kuchen is whipped up in kitchens all over the Midwest, for the ultimate in kuchen perfection, we recommend trying Grandmas Kuchen in Pollock, South Dakota.
       </p>
       <h1>
        Key Wests Key Lime Pie
       </h1>
       <p>
        Florida Key West is practically as famous for its Key lime pie as it is for its Jimmy Buffet lifestyle. Not named for Key West, but rather for the Key lime, a small, sweet, pale cousin of the limes we see in supermarkets today, this pie is made from Key lime juice mixed with canned condensed milk and whole eggs. This thickening process helps the pie to take on its custard-like consistency and distinctive tangy flavor. Although the original Key lime pie was usually not baked (which made it the perfect treat for sailors at sea), todays pies are typically cooked, as well as covered with a meringue topping. Looking for some of the best? Try Key West
        <a href="http://www.keywestkeylimepieco.com" target="_blank">
         Key Lime Pie Company
        </a>
        . Their authentic pie beat one baked by celebrity chef Bobby Flay on an episode of his Food Network TV show Throwdown!
       </p>
       <h1>
        The American Souths The Fried and The Pied
       </h1>
       <p>
        In the heart of the American South there are two distinctive desserts worth seeking outthe fried and the pied. In recent years, Fried Oreos, Fried Twinkies, and even Fried Coke have been steadily increasing in popularity at trendy cafes and local carnivals across the South. But few Southern-fried bona fide chefs would allow their laurels to rest on a fried treat. Pie, in all of its great and bountiful forms, is the true gem of the South. Pecan is the quintessential Southern pie. A high-calorie, corn syrupy bomb of nuts and sugar, pecan pie is a blue-ribbon winner at fairs all over the region. The pie itself traces back to the late 19
        <sup>
         th
        </sup>
        century, but became a real star in the 1930s when it became the preferred vehicle for Karo Syrup. For another Southern classic, try Chess pie. In the same vein as pecan pie, Chess pie is considered a pantry pie, in that the ingredients are not supposed to be hard to come by; its made from standard ingredients that are most likely already in the pantry. One of the suggestions as to Chess pies etymology says the name is a mispronunciation of Chest pie, as in where the pies ingredientsflour, sugar, butter and eggswould be kept. A uniquely North Carolina twist to southern pie is vinegar pie, which has a sweet-tangy flavor thanks to the inclusion of vinegar. The Angus Barn in Raleigh, North Carolina makes one of the Souths best, and they even add chocolate.
       </p>
