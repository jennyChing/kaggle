     <p>
      A great hall in
      <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">
       Eleusis
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       Telesterion
      </b>
      ("Initiation Hall" from Gr. , to complete, to fulfill, to consecrate, to initiate) was one of the primary centers of the
      <a href="/wiki/Eleusinian_Mysteries" title="Eleusinian Mysteries">
       Eleusinian Mysteries
      </a>
      . Devoted to
      <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">
       Demeter
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">
       Persephone
      </a>
      , these initiation ceremonies were the most sacred and ancient of all the religious rites celebrated in Greece.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antiq_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Antiq-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At some point in the 5th century BC,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iktinos" title="Iktinos">
       Iktinos
      </a>
      , the great architect of the
      <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">
       Parthenon
      </a>
      , built the Telesterion big enough to hold thousands of people. In about 318 BC,
      <a href="/wiki/Philon" title="Philon">
       Philon
      </a>
      added a portico with twelve Doric columns. The site of the Telesterion is believed to have had some temple since the 7th century BC, or the time of the
      <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymns" title="Homeric Hymns">
       Homeric Hymns to Demeter
      </a>
      (650-550 BC); the Telesterion had ten different building phases.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Athenians used several calendars, each for different purposes. The festival of Eleusinia was celebrated each year in Eleusis and Athens for nine days from the 15th to the 23rd of the month of
      <a href="/wiki/Attic_calendar" title="Attic calendar">
       Boedromion
      </a>
      (in September or October of the Gregorian calendar); because the festival calendar had 12 lunar months, the celebrations were not strictly calibrated to a year of 365 days. During the festival, Athens was crowded with visitors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antiq_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Antiq-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As the climax of the ceremonies at Eleusis, the initiates entered the Telesterion where they were shown the sacred relics of
      <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">
       Demeter
      </a>
      and the priestesses revealed their visions of the holy night (probably a fire that represented the possibility of life after death). This was the most secretive part of the Mysteries and those who had been initiated were forbidden to ever speak of the events that took place in the Telesterion.
     </p>
     <p>
      It was destroyed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">
       Persians
      </a>
      after the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thermopylae" title="Battle of Thermopylae">
       Battle of Thermopylae
      </a>
      , when the Athenians withdrew to
      <a href="/wiki/Salamis_Island" title="Salamis Island">
       Salamis
      </a>
      in 480 BC and all of
      <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">
       Boeotia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Attica" title="Attica">
       Attica
      </a>
      fell to the Persian army, who captured and burnt Athens. After the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Persian_invasion_of_Greece" title="Second Persian invasion of Greece">
       defeat of the Persians
      </a>
      , the Telesterion was rebuilt some time later by
      <a href="/wiki/Pericles" title="Pericles">
       Pericles
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In AD 170, during the rule of Roman emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">
       Marcus Aurelius
      </a>
      , an ancient tribe called the
      <a href="/wiki/Costoboci" title="Costoboci">
       Costoboci
      </a>
      launched an invasion of Roman territory south of the Danube, entering
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thracia_(Roman_province)" title="Thracia (Roman province)">
       Thracia
      </a>
      and ravaging the provinces of
      <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(Roman_province)" title="Macedonia (Roman province)">
       Macedonia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Achaea_(Roman_province)" title="Achaea (Roman province)">
       Achaea
      </a>
      (Greece). The Costoboci reached as far south as Eleusis, where they destroyed the Telesterion. The emperor responded by despatching general Vehilius Gratus Iulianus to Greece with emergency reinforcements, who eventually defeated the Costoboci. Marcus Aurelius then had the Telesterion rebuilt.
     </p>
     <p>
      In AD 396, the forces of
      <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">
       Alaric the Visigoth
      </a>
      invaded the Eastern Roman Empire and
      <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I#In_Greece" title="Alaric I">
       ravaged Attica, destroying the Telesterion
      </a>
      , which was never to be rebuilt.
     </p>
