          <figure aria-labelledby="figcaption_attachment_132567" class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_132567" style="width: 610px">
           <a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/05/illustris_box_dmdens_gasdens.jpg">
            <img alt="An early universe simulation" class="size-medium wp-image-132567" height="600" src="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/05/illustris_box_dmdens_gasdens-600x600.jpg" width="600"/>
           </a>
           <figcaption class="wp-caption-text" id="figcaption_attachment_132567">
            An early universe simulation shows dark matter (left) transitioning to gas (right).
            <br/>
            Credit: Illustris Collaboration
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Everyone loves cartoons, even cosmologists. They just call them simulations.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Now, one of the great triumphs of modern cosmologyour understanding of how the universe evolved
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            its kaleidoscope of stars, worlds, and galaxieshas been visualized in its first realistic simulation:
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="301" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/SY0bKE10ZDM" width="535">
           </iframe>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            Until now, no single simulation was able to reproduce the universe on both large and small scales simultaneously,
            <a href="http://web.mit.edu/physics/people/faculty/vogelsberger_mark.html">
             Mark Vogelsberger
            </a>
            of MIT and the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics, who led the simulation team,
            <a href="http://www.cfa.harvard.edu/news/2014-10">
             says in a statement
            </a>
            . Using a computer program, t
           </span>
           he team grew the virtual galaxies in simulated time, starting from 12 million years after the big bang (which kicked off about 13.8 billion years ago), they report in a
           <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/nature13316">
            study released this week by the journal
            <em>
             Nature
            </em>
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            Named
            <a href="http://www.illustris-project.org/">
             Illustris
            </a>
            , the simulation built its
           </span>
           universe
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            out of 10 billion tiny cubes stuffed inside a virtual box measuring some 350 million light-years on each side.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           Stuffed inside those cubes was
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            simulated cosmological
           </span>
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            gas, which cooled, compressed, and formed filaments of galaxies that resemble ones seen today. As it evolved, its
           </span>
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            early
           </span>
           cosmos
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
           </span>
           resembled
           <span style="font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.5em;">
            the real one, which is filled with small, energetic galaxies revealed by Hubble Space Telescope observations.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           If this all sounds somewhat complicated,do not be fooled: It is extremely complicated, University of Maryland astronomer
           <a href="http://www.astro.umd.edu/~mbk/">
            Michael Boylan-Kolchin
           </a>
           , says in a commentary accompanying the study.
          </p>
          <p>
           The simulation still has a way to go,Boylan-Kolchin adds, since it does not produce the biggest of black holes seen in the early universe or the dwarf galaxies that circle the Milky Way today. But thanks to the simulation, he says in the commentary, creating arealistic virtual Universe of gas, stars, black holesand dark matter is already a reality. 
          </p>
