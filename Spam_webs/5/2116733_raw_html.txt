    Modern Fantasy: The 100 Best Novels
   </h1>

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Modern Fantasy: The 100 Best Novels, An English-Language Selection, 1946-1987
       </b>
      </i>
      is a nonfiction book written by
      <a href="/wiki/David_Pringle" title="David Pringle">
       David Pringle
      </a>
      , published by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grafton_Books" title="Grafton Books">
       Grafton Books
      </a>
      in 1988 (UK); next year by Peter Bedrick Books (US). The foreword is by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brian_W._Aldiss" title="Brian W. Aldiss">
       Brian W. Aldiss
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Primarily the book comprises 100 short essays on the selected works, covered in order of publication, without any ranking. It is considered an important critical summary of the field of modern fantasy literature.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Modern Fantasy
      </i>
      followed Pringle's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction:_The_100_Best_Novels" title="Science Fiction: The 100 Best Novels">
        Science Fiction: The 100 Best Novels
       </a>
      </i>
      , published by Xanadu in 1985. In the introduction he commends the nearly simultaneous "rival" followup by Xanadu:
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jones_(author)" title="Stephen Jones (author)">
       Stephen Jones
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Newman" title="Kim Newman">
       Kim Newman
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       Horror: The 100 Best Books
      </i>
      (Xanadu, 1988).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In fact Xanadu had followed with at least three more books in its
      <a href="/wiki/Science_Fiction:_The_100_Best_Novels#100_Best_series" title="Science Fiction: The 100 Best Novels">
       100 Best series
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       Crime and Mystery
      </i>
      in 1987, both
      <i>
       Horror
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Fantasy
      </i>
      in 1988. Xanadu had commissioned
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Moorcock" title="Michael Moorcock">
       Michael Moorcock
      </a>
      to write
      <i>
       Fantasy
      </i>
      but the project had been turned over to
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Cawthorn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Cawthorn (page does not exist)">
       James Cawthorn
      </a>
      when it became "clear that I would not be able to deliver it for a long time".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to ISFDB, Pringle's
      <i>
       Modern Fantasy
      </i>
      was released in October 1988, September 1989 in the U.S.; Xanadu's
      <i>
       Fantasy
      </i>
      was released in November and published almost simultaneously by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carroll_and_Graf" title="Carroll and Graf">
       Carroll and Graf
      </a>
      in the U.S.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Fantasy">
       Modern Fantasy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_Fantasy:_The_100_Best_Novels&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Modern Fantasy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Pringle says in the introduction, rebutting one academic complaint about the sprawling scope of fantasy, "But it seems to me that fantasy
      <i>
       is
      </i>
      an indiscriminate form. ... We may even view it as the primal genre, essentially formless, a swamp which has served as the breeding ground for all other popular fiction genres ... Up to the eighteenth century, almost all narrative fictions, both verse and prose, were fantastic to a greater or lesser degree."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the introduction to the earlier book, he had distinguished "Supernatural Horror" and "Heroic Fantasy" as the other important divisions of fantastic fiction beside science fiction. They may be represented by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Dracula" title="Dracula">
        Dracula
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">
        The Lord of the Rings
       </a>
      </i>
      , featuring "the irruption of some supernatural force into the everyday world" and "set in completely imaginary worlds" respectively. Here he adds "the Fabulation, or absurdist metafictionstories which are set in the real world, but which distort that world in ways other than the supernaturally horrific." He names Thomas Pynchon, Angela Carter, John Crowley, and Geoff Ryman as authors of fabulations included in his hundred.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      While covering no foreign-language fantasies and few children's or 'light' fantasies, "I have tried to make a balanced list, and in so doing I have included some books which are not really to my tastethey may well be other people's favourites, though. In truth, there are not a hundred masterpieces of modern fantasy, any more than there a hundred masterpieces of science fiction."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "[A]t least some of the novels I have selected are masterpieces of modern literature, full of beauty and wonder. The others are craftsmanlike entertainments which I happily commend to you for your enjoyment."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
