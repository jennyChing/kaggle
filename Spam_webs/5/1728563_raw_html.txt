        <h2>
         Rosa's Fresh Pizza offers a taste of New York-style pizza to the people of Philadelphia.
        </h2>
        <p>
         But that's not all they serve. Rosa's and their customers have teamed up to feed the city a daily taste of hope and kindness.
        </p>
        <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/brzjeICcIt0?wmode=transparent&amp;showinfo=0&amp;controls=1&amp;enablejsapi=1&amp;rel=0&amp;version=3&amp;color=white" width="560">
        </iframe>
        <h2>
         Philadelphia is the poorest large city in the country.
        </h2>
        <p>
         And it's the city with the worst
         <a href="http://www.urban.org/deeppoverty/" target="_blank">
          deep poverty
         </a>
         rate in the U.S., with roughly
         <a href="http://articles.philly.com/2014-09-26/news/54322611_1_deep-poverty-poverty-line-south-philadelphia" target="_blank">
          185,000 people
         </a>
         (including 60,000 kids) living on incomes below half of the federal poverty line.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img src="//upw-prod-images.global.ssl.fastly.net/nugget/54f5e22732356100197a0000/attachments/phillyhomelessness-8e75bcf1ddb7a1132ecfc154e0682cd5.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         With that,
         <a href="http://www.nbcphiladelphia.com/news/local/No-shelter-for-nearly-half-of-homeless-238584651.html" target="_blank">
          homelessness
         </a>
         <a href="http://www.nbcphiladelphia.com/news/local/No-shelter-for-nearly-half-of-homeless-238584651.html">
          is on the rise
         </a>
         , and the city doesn't have enough resources to be there for everyone.
        </p>
        <p>
         Heavy, right? Well, this particular story takes to take a happy turn.
        </p>
        <h2>
         At Rosa's, customers can "pay it forward" by pre-purchasing slices for people in need.
        </h2>
        <p>
         Owner Mason Wartman, who left his Wall Street desk job to open the shop, says pay-it-forward pizza started with one customer, one dollar, and one Post-it note.
        </p>
        <p class="image-caption">
         <img src="//upw-prod-images.global.ssl.fastly.net/nugget/54f61a3e323561000ce10000/attachments/mason-wartman-45afc7c80f29e040c2f4591108968ce7.png"/>
         <br/>
         Mason Wartman stands at the helm of Rosa's Fresh Pizza.
        </p>
        <p>
         The customer was inspired by an Italian coffee house practice called
         <a href="https://www.upworthy.com/if-youre-as-addicted-to-coffee-as-i-am-youll-understand-why-this-is-important" target="_blank">
          caff sospeso
         </a>
         (suspended coffee), by which customers can pre-purchase cups of coffee for less fortunate customers.
        </p>
        <p>
         Wartman wrote the purchase on a Post-it and slapped it on the wall behind the register to be redeemed by the next homeless patron to enter the store.
        </p>
        <h2>
         As word spread, more and more customers participated.
        </h2>
        <p>
         And Rosa's wall blossomed with colorful notes signifying acts of kindness and a guaranteed slice for everyone who walked in, regardless of their ability to pay.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img src="//upw-prod-images.global.ssl.fastly.net/nugget/54f5fce7323561000c8a0000/attachments/post-it-pizza2-822a0581457bc92965fad515a4da039b.png"/>
        </p>
        <h2>
         Since that first pay-it-forward slice, Rosa's has provided nearly 10,000 pizza slices to needy Philadelphians.
        </h2>
        <p>
         Pre-purchased slices now represent a whopping
         <a href="http://www.post-gazette.com/news/portfolio/2015/01/17/Portfolio-Philadelphia-pizza-shop-clients-pay-it-forward-buy-slices-for-homeless/stories/201501170028" target="_blank">
          10%
         </a>
         of Rosa's business. And it's having a remarkable impact on the community, showing not only that acts of kindness can be contagious, but also how a small gesture of support can have a ripple effect of positivity.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img src="//upw-prod-images.global.ssl.fastly.net/nugget/54f61801323561000cdc0000/attachments/payitforwardpizza1-83720371ac374e7e1084490d0ba74509.gif"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         In the video, Wartman tells the story of a homeless regular who disappeared for a while only to return having found a new job and wanting to pay it forward as others had done for him.
        </p>
        <p>
         And in an interview with the
         <a href="http://www.post-gazette.com/news/portfolio/2015/01/17/Portfolio-Philadelphia-pizza-shop-clients-pay-it-forward-buy-slices-for-homeless/stories/201501170028" target="_blank">
          Pittsburgh Post-Gazette
         </a>
         , Wartman notes that some have even said the program has helped to keep them out of trouble with the law:
         <span>
         </span>
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         "[Wartman] said people who receive the slices have told him the generosity helps them avoid committing petty crime to get money for food. 'I knew it saved people money,' Mr. Wartman said. 'I hadn't considered that it stopped people from committing crime.'"
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         If one small business acting as a hub of kindness can have this kind of effect,
         <strong>
          can you imagine the possibilities of entire communities of consumers and businesses doing the same thing?
         </strong>
        </p>

            <p>
             I've been homeless on and off for the past five years so it really sucks but at least I know I can look forward to 11 o'clock when I know I can get some fresh, hot pizza.
            </p>
            <p>
             Rosa's Fresh Pizza in Philadelphia has a "pay it forward" system. Customers can pre-purchase $1 slices for those in need. Which are redeemed with a sticky note on the wall.
            </p>
            <p>
             The first day the "pay it forward" program kind of started was when a gentleman walked in and he asked if a homeless person ever came in short and he offered to pre-purchase a slice of pizza for the next homeless person. So he gave me a dollar, I ran out, got Post-it notes, put one up on a wall behind the register, told more people about it. Eventually a couple of days later a homeless person came in, he had like 65 cents on him and we told him to just keep the change, it was already paid for. He redeemed one of the pre-purchased slices. So now we've given away almost 10,000 slices of pizza.
            </p>
            <p>
             I feel welcome. I feel like I almost bought the slice but I know I didn't.
            </p>
            <p>
             I try to donate at least $5 a week and that's so funny because today I left my wallet and everything home and I had 78 cents in my pocket and I just came in. I was like, "I'm a little short" and they were like, "okay, don't worry about it" and that's a really great feeling so I can imagine how the homeless people feel. And it's just a great thing. And the pizza is good. I'm from New York so you know I know what pizza is.
            </p>
            <p>
             Before I opened up Rosa's, I just worked at a desk job in New York. I showed up every morning, did some computer work and then went home, pretty regular stuff. I wanted to do something new. I saw the success of the dollar pizza stores throughout New York so I thought I'd open this place up and try my hand at it.
            </p>
            <p>
             I've been three years off the streets. I was seven years homeless myself as a veteran and I finally got myself out of the rut, got myself an income and now I want to pay it forward.
            </p>
            <p>
             Oh, just yesterday there was a guy that came in. He'd been homeless for like eight months and I hadn't seem him for like three months. And I'm like, "Yo, where you been at?" He's like, "Yeah, yeah, yeah, I'm working now. I figured I'd buy a slice for someone else." And I was like, that was awesome. Just a couple days prior I had been like, "Man, I hope that guy's okay" and it turns out he's killing it.
            </p>
            <p>
             We don't always have a chance to come in and get fresh, hot food whenever we can and for people to donate money towards slices of pizza for us really made a change in Philadelphia.
            </p>
            <p>
             In a city with a poverty rate that is higher than all other big cities comparable to it, to see such a show of compassion and brotherly love, it's inspiring.
            </p>
            <p>
             I think it's pretty cool how powerful something this small and simple can be.
            </p>
