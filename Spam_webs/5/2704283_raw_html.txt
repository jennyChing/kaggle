       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         Interview with Gone Home developers, The Fullbright Company
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         By
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://www.macgamerhq.com/author/rdomolina/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Ric Molina
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         on
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2013-09-25T21:23:46+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          September 25, 2013
         </time>
         <span class="entry-comments-link">
          <a href="http://www.macgamerhq.com/opinion/interview-gone-home-developers-fullbright-company/#comments">
           <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="5440 http://www.macgamerhq.com/?p=5440">
            1 Comment
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <div style="float:left;margin:5px 5px 5px 0;">
         <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
         </script>
         <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7064415235808881" data-ad-slot="4508437458" style="display:inline-block;width:336px;height:280px">
         </ins>
         <script>
          (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
         </script>
        </div>
        <p>
         Indie gaming is such a great opportunity for people to get together and just make games. No need of big publishers or lots of money to create a game worth playing, a game that doesnt have to meet market desirability criteria or high ROI. The problem is, there are so many Indie developers these days, pumping new games each day, its hard to get people to pay attention. Well,
         <a href="http://thefullbrightcompany.com/about/" target="_blank">
          The Fullbright Company
         </a>
         is one of those studios that certainly managed to get into everybodys radar and all thanks to the very well-received adventure game,
         <a href="http://www.gonehomegame.com/" target="_blank">
          Gone Home
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Well, I got a chance to sit together with Steve Gaynor, one of the 4 members of the team. We discussed about Gone Home, the studio and even the Mac platform. Keep reading for all the juicy details!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h4>
         About The Game
        </h4>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <em>
           Gone Home is an original take, how did you come up with that idea?
          </em>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         For us, the small team was integral to coming up with the idea for the game. The scale of Gone Home  just one house to explore, full of details but with no people in it  was based on the fact that we only had 4 team members (and none of us are character artists!) So really, the game idea was born out of the team as much as our creative desires.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h4>
         About The Fullbright Company
        </h4>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Can you tell us a little bit about yourselves? How many are you at The Fullbright Company?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Were four developers working on the game full-time. Three of us share a house in Portland, Oregon, and our fourth (Kate Craig) is up in Vancouver, BC. The three of us who cofounded the company all worked on the BioShock series together, then left large-scale game development to start our own small indie team. Gone Home is our first game as The Fullbright Company.
        </p>
        <pre>
        </pre>
        <h4>
         About indie development
        </h4>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Indie gaming is getting more and more popular with hundreds of games released each year. How did you stand out? How of you make people pay attention to YOUR game?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Its true, promoting the game is one of the hardest parts. Getting the word out and getting people to pay attention is always a challenge. We had some advantages  from developers who worked on BioShock doesnt hurt, and we knew a lot of journalists from our time in AAA. But it was definitely a learning experience, figuring out how to do press releases, schedule preview sessions, and so on. But were glad to have had to learned it, instead of the normal experience from bigger organizations of that somebody elses job.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Your advice for a couple of guys dreaming on developing a game?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Get started now! Build stuff, make stuff, FINISH IT, start small, and get it out into the world for people to play and react to. Youll spend a lot of time making things here and there before you get to your magnum opus. So download free tools for making mods and games, and get going!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h4>
         The Mac platform
        </h4>
        <p>
         <strong>
          What made you decide to develop a version for the Mac too?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         For us, we used the Unity engine, which exports to Mac natively. So really there wasnt a whole lot of extra work to do to support Mac. We did need to package it for distribution on Mac hardware, and we did some extra work to support gamepad on Mac, but it was mostly a no-brainer to do so.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.macgamerhq.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/Gone-Home-gameplay.jpg">
          <img alt="Gone Home Mac" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5447" src="http://www.macgamerhq.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/Gone-Home-gameplay.jpg"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <h4>
         The future
        </h4>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Where do you see The Fullbright Company in a couple of years?
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In a couple years, we hope well be in the midst of working on our next game. For now were taking some time to support Gone Home on more platforms and with a little more content, and then will be taking a break for a bit. But hopefully well take what weve made with Gone Home, and extend it in smart ways to explore new experiences that dont just feel like a rehash. We shall see!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         I would like to thank Steve and the whole team for their time and attention. Gone Home is brilliantly put together and I bet most Indie developers would give a kidney to have such a strong debut! Our official review for Gone Home for Mac is currently underway. You can expect it early next week!
        </p>
        <p>
         Regarding Mac support from Indie developers, I cant help wondering why more tools arent as multi-platform ready as the Unity engine. Basically, The Fullbright Company had the same answer as
         <a href="http://www.macgamerhq.com/opinion/interview-with-cargo-commander-developers/" target="_blank" title="Great interview with Cargo Commander developers">
          Cargo Commander developers
         </a>
         : 
         <em>
          We did a Mac (and Linux) version because Unity does it natively!
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         If you have any questions that you would like to ask Steve, please share them in the comments and I promise you Steve will get them
         <img alt=":)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://www.macgamerhq.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/simple-smile.png" style="height: 1em; max-height: 1em;"/>
         .
        </p>
