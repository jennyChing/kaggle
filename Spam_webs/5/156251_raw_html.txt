      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Looking To Transform Healthcare? - Ask A Nurse
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         Traditionally, nurses have been the face of healthcare to the patient. According to the American Nurses Association (
         <a href="http://www.nursingworld.org" target="_blank">
          ANA
         </a>
         ), the nursing profession involves the protection, promotion, and optimization of health and abilities, prevention of illness and injury, alleviation of suffering, and advocacy in the care of individuals, families, communities, and populations.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unfortunately, due to the culture of the healthcare industry, nurses have usually taken a back seat to physicians and administrators when it comes to changing the policies and practices of optimizing care. However, there is a wealth of evidence that points to the vital and increasing leadership role nurses are taking in healthcare practices around the country. According to Teri Lynn Kiss, President of the American Association of Critical-Care Nurses (
         <a href="http://www.aacn.org/" target="_blank">
          AACN
         </a>
         ), Studies confirm that empowered nurses provide the best patient care.
        </p>
        <p>
         Bolstering the case for that claim, in 2012, AACN began a national rollout of their
         <a href="http://www.aacn.org/wd/csi/content/csi-landing.pcms?sidebar=none&amp;menu=csi" target="_blank">
          Clinical Scene Investigator (CSI) Academy
         </a>
         , a 16-month, hospital-based nurse leadership and innovation training program with teams of staff nurses from 42 hospitals in six regions. The programs goal is to empower bedside nurses as clinician leaders and change agents whose initiatives quantifiably improve the quality of patient care and hospital bottom lines. Teams in Texas, North Carolina, Indiana, and Massachusetts have completed the program, and initial results point to anticipated savings of $21 million. (Teams in Pennsylvania and New York will finish by the end of 2014.)
        </p>
        <p>
         Kiss summarized results from the four regions so far, saying that on average, their nurse-driven initiatives decreased patient length of stay in ICUs and progressive care units by up to a full day. They decreased by one day the time patients needed support from a mechanical ventilator. And they cut in half ICU complications and infections. To achieve these improvements, the CSI teams have taken on three challenges that often elude hospital staff: reducing skin pressure ulcers, preventing falls, and helping patients become mobile sooner.
        </p>
        <div class="zemanta-img">
         <div class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 680px">
          <img alt="English: ACAJUTLA, El Salvador (July 31, 2007)..." class="zemanta-img-configured" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/robertszczerba/files/2014/09/670px-us_navy_070731-n-4238b-007_lt-_megan_zeller_an_intensive_care_unit_nurse_checks_the_vital_signs_of_patient_while_he_recovers_after_surgery_aboard_the_hospital_ship_usns_comfort_t-ah_20.jpg" title="English: ACAJUTLA, El Salvador (July 31, 2007)..."/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Being on the front lines of patient care gives nurses a unique perspective on how to improve the quality and efficiency of the healthcare system. (Photo credit: Wikipedia)
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         The CSI Academy team at Brigham and Womens Hospital in Boston implemented an evidence-based, pressure ulcer prevention program, adding a two-person, head-to-toe skin assessment when a patient is admitted, and testing new products for dressings and incontinence. They reduced pressure ulcers at the lower back by 63% and anticipate hospital savings of nearly $2 million a year.
        </p>
        <p>
         The team at St. Francis Health in Indianapolis developed a No Fall Zone initiative that combined staff education about the how tos of fall prevention with a patient-family fall prevention contract. They decreased patient falls by 35%, with anticipated annual savings of more than $120,000.
        </p>
        <p>
         Seven of the CSI Academy teams have taken on the challenge of helping ICU patients become mobile sooner. The projected annual hospital savings from these initiatives is more than $6 million. Early mobility is important because it reduces the risk of unintended and costly consequences of severe illness such as hospital acquired infections (HAI).
        </p>
        <p>
         Some of the most impressive and comprehensive
         <a href="http://www.wral.com/icu-program-puts-patients-back-on-their-feet/13459626/" target="_blank">
          results
         </a>
         so far are from the team at Duke Raleigh Hospital. They engaged the entire patient care team  doctors, nurses, administrators, and respiratory, physical, and occupational therapists  to reframe how they approached mobilizing severely ill patients. They developed a protocol that decreased by one day the average length of stay for ICU postoperative patients and those requiring mechanical ventilation. The results had a favorable impact on patient satisfaction scores and saved the hospital more than $1.2 million a year.
        </p>
        <p>
         Each teams project materials are available via a free, searchable online
         <a href="http://www.aacn.org/wd/csi/content/csi-landing.pcms?sidebar=none&amp;menu=csi" target="_blank">
          database
         </a>
         thats already being used as a resource by non-participating hospitals developing their own initiatives. For example, nurses at Inova Fairfax Medical Campus in Falls Church, Virginia, applied project plans from the database to achieve an 80 percent reduction in central line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSIs) in ICUs, and $962,000 in savings.
         <br/>
         <!--donotpaginate-->
         <br/>
         The message to hospital administrators should be clear  if youre looking to improve the quality of care and reduce costs, try talking to the people working on the front lines every day  talk to a nurse.
        </p>

         <div class="comment_content">
          <p>
           First Id like to thank the author of this article for stating what nurses have always known. Yes nurses, like soldiers, are at the front line of healthcare, and like soldiers, must follow the orders of those above them without a lot of power to change policy. Administrators are more concerned with their bottom line, and despite studies that show the correlation between adequate nurse to patient ratios effecting patient mortality (http://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMsa1001025?viewType=Print), hospitals continue to understaff units, and tighten budgets in all areas of the hospital.
          </p>
          <p>
           Nurses are highly educated, professionals whose priorities are towards: patient care, safety, treatment, and satisfaction. Nurses are advocates for their patients first and foremost. They learn to work around poorly run hospital systems to go the extra mile to care for their patients. Nurses wear many hats, are expected to perform multiple duties, and no where does it say they should work out of the goodness of their hearts for little pay while mutely standing by as policy makers make poor decisions that effect how their next twelve hour shift is going to go.
          </p>
          <p>
           Even though Pauls comment above did not quite fit with subject matter of this article, hes not totally wrong.
           <br/>
           Gruber, Jonathan, and Samuel A. Kleiner. 2012. Do Strikes Kill? Evidence from New York State. American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, 4(1): 127-57.
          </p>
          <p>
           But the real tragedy effecting patient mortality is better tackled by the IOMs report, To Err is Human, which cites approximately 98,000 American deaths in hosptials due to medical errors (Doctors do not need to be holding picket signs to cause harm to their patients). These errors are a combined result of mistakes made by all members of the healthcare team, and this is where nurses and all members of the healthcare team need to come together to change policy and radically reduce these unacceptable figures.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, in the land of cuts, and more cuts to Medicare funding and reimbursement, and Medicares policies on quality measures, it will take some very ingenious and dedicated people at the front lines, to do more with less and protect our fragile patients from the follies of inefficient health care reform.
          </p>
