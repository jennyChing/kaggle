     <h2 id="article-title">
      How on Earth Do You Make a Tank Stealthy?
     </h2>
     <h3 class="subtitle">
      DOD wants a smaller, lighter, elusive armored vehicle
     </h3>
     <strong class="author_n_date">
      By
     </strong>
     <a href="/authors/michael_peck/">
      <b>
       Michael Peck
      </b>
     </a>
     <br/>
     <div class="article_body" id="article_body">
      <div class="picture" style="">
       <img src="http://images.realclear.com/256616_5_.jpg" style=""/>
      </div>
      <div class="art_syn_block_content" style="float:right; margin-right:0px; width:220px;">
       <div align="center">
        <a href="http://bit.ly/1ovr8FB">
        </a>
        <img src="http://images.realclear.com/253054_1_.jpg"/>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The Pentagons future tank could resemble a stealth fighter jet more than an armored behemoth like the current M-1 Abrams.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The military wants a vehicle that can survive lethal anti-tank missiles, but without having to haul around so much armor plate that it can barely move. It should also have stealth capabilities to avoid detection.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       What might this future tank look like? A conceptual drawing of the
       <a href="http://www.darpa.mil/uploadedImages/Content/NewsEvents/Releases/2014/GXV-T%20Concept%20Image%207.jpg" target="_blank">
        Ground X-Vehicle
       </a>
       looks like something out of
       <em>
        Star Wars
       </em>
       four wheels, no turret, a rotating gun and a chassis so small that it couldnt accommodate more than one or two crewmen.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agencythe Pentagons future technology labhas stated
       <a href="http://www.darpa.mil/NewsEvents/Releases/2014/08/18.aspx" target="_blank">
        some ambitious goals
       </a>
       for the Ground X-Vehicle. It should be half the size and weight of todays tanks, but with twice the speed.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       It should also have half the crew, be able to traverse 95 percent of the terrain it encounters
       <em>
        and
       </em>
       have reduced sensor signatures.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The latest M-1A2 weighs 60 tons, is 26 feet long, moves at 25 miles per hour and has a crew of four. If DARPA gets its way, the new vehicle would weigh 30 tons, be 13 feet longsmaller than a Humveezoom at 50 miles per hour and have a crew of two.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The Pentagon has some radical suggestions for achieving this seemingly impossible dream. To increase mobility, prospective tank designers should consider rapid omnidirectional movement changes in three dimensions. This sounds like a vehicle that can raise or lower its chassis like the famously oddball
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stridsvagn_103" target="_blank">
        Swedish S-Tank
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       For added protection
       <em>
        without
       </em>
       heavy armor plate, the GXV might automatically dodge incoming threats, which suggests a computer-controlled system that takes evasive action. DARPA also mentions active repositioning of armor, which could mean the tank automatically reorienting itself so its thickest armor is facing enemy fire  or some kind of flexible armor plate that rearranges itself to deflect enemy projectiles.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Most interesting is DARPAs desire for signature management to reduce the enemys ability to detect the tank through visible light, infrared radiation, sound, or electromagnetic sensors such as radar.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       If this sounds familiar, its because the description also matches stealth aircraft such as the F-35.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Indeed, DARPA says the GXV project draws inspiration from the militarys long line of experimental X-planes. The X-planes pioneered a host of innovations, such as the X-1's breaking of the sound barrier. Most X-planes never went into production. One exception is the X-35, which evolved into the F-35.
      </p>
      <div align="center">
       <table border="1" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="MsoTableGrid" style="border-collapse: collapse; border: none; mso-border-alt: solid windowtext .5pt; mso-yfti-tbllook: 1184; mso-padding-alt: 0in 5.4pt 0in 5.4pt;">

      <p class="MsoNormal">
       DARPA is attempting to solve a dilemma that has confounded tank designers since the first clumsy armored vehicles trundled into history a century ago.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Designing a tank means juggling three factorsfirepower, armor protection and mobility. The problem is that emphasizing one factor comes only at the expense of the other two.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Add a bigger gun or more armor and the tank become slower and more expensive. Reduce armor protection and the tank becomes easier prey for enemy weapons.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The historical trend has always been toward
       <em>
        more
       </em>
       armor and
       <em>
        bigger
       </em>
       tanks. Thus the French FT-17 of 1918 was seven tons, while the M-1 weighs 60 tons. Sometimes the extra protection works. Nazi Germanys Tiger tank could deflect Allied anti-tank shells like ping-pong balls.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       But as DARPA points out, in the eternal race between tanks and anti-tank weapons, todays tank-killers are winning. In the 2006 Lebanon war, Russian Kornet missiles destroyed thickly-armored Israeli Merkavas.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Merely adding extra armor wont work anymore. What makes a tank such a powerful weapon is that its a mobile armored gun platform. DARPA believes that tank design has reached a dead-end where more armor transforms tanks into ponderous dinosaurs.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       In fact, the M-1 tanks the Army dispatched from Germany to Bosnia during the 1996 peacekeeping mission had difficulty moving over many European bridges without engineer support. Heavier tanks are also harder for the Pentagon to ship to overseas theaters like the Middle East.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       DARPA says the situation has become so bad that clever innovations are needed to ensure the operational viability of the next generation of armored fighting vehicles.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       In other words, unless tanks benefit from some bright ideas, they will either be too weakly armored to survive on the battlefield or too heavy to even get there.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Will technology save the tank? Its too early to say. What we
       <em>
        can
       </em>
       say is that every weapon, like every machine, is a compromise. The F-35 sacrifices payload and performance to avoid detectionor so the Pentagon hopes.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       A two-man crew means a smaller tank, but it could also mean an overworked creware two people enough to fix a broken track or stay up all night on radio watch? Dodging enemy missiles like a comic book superhero may
       <em>
        sound
       </em>
       great, but if it doesnt work, that tank better have some thick armor as a backup.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       The F-35 comparison also raises a more fundamental question for the future of tanks. Some critics warn that the hundreds of billions of dollars the Pentagon is spending on the F-35 better would be spent on developing drones.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Even if that belief paints a rosy picture of drone capabilities, one wonders whether the Pentagons vision of a smaller, faster tank should be an unmanned one.
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal">
       Either way, the tank as we know it must change  or die.
      </p>
