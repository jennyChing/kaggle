     <p>
      <b>
       Christopher Poole
      </b>
      (born c. 1988)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chris_Poole_part_1.2F3-_ROFLCON_2012_-_Solo_Panel_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chris_Poole_part_1.2F3-_ROFLCON_2012_-_Solo_Panel-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is an American entrepreneur. He is best known for founding two web sites,
      <a href="/wiki/4chan" title="4chan">
       4chan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Canvas_Networks" title="Canvas Networks">
       Canvas
      </a>
      . He originally started 4chan pseudonymously, under the screen name
      <b>
       moot
      </b>
      (always written entirely in lower case).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_and_activity">
       Impact and activity
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2008, Leopoldo Godoy of Brazilian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TV_Globo" title="TV Globo">
       TV Globo
      </a>
      called Poole's 4chan "the ground zero of Western web culture."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In April 2009, Poole was voted the world's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marblecake_also_the_game" title="Marblecake also the game">
       most influential person of 2008
      </a>
      by an open Internet poll conducted by
      <i>
       Time
      </i>
      magazine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The results were questioned even before the poll completed, however, as automated voting programs and manual
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballot_stuffing" title="Ballot stuffing">
       ballot stuffing
      </a>
      were used to influence the vote.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCMag-moot_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PCMag-moot-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechCrunch-moot_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TechCrunch-moot-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      4chan's interference with the vote seemed increasingly likely, when it was found that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrostics" title="Acrostics">
       reading the first letter
      </a>
      of the first 21 candidates in the poll spelled out a phrase containing two 4chan memes: "mARBLECAKE. ALSO,
      <a href="/wiki/The_Game_(mind_game)" title="The Game (mind game)">
       THE GAME
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On September 12, 2009, Poole gave a talk on why 4chan has a reputation as a "
      <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">
       Meme
      </a>
      Factory" at the Paraflows Symposium in Vienna, Austria, which was part of the Paraflows 09 festival, themed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reality_Hacking" title="Reality Hacking">
       Urban Hacking
      </a>
      . In this talk, Poole mainly attributed this to the anonymous system, and to the lack of data retention on the site ("The site has no memory").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On February 10, 2010, Poole spoke at the
      <a href="/wiki/TED_(conference)" title="TED (conference)">
       TED2010 conference
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">
       Long Beach
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
       California
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He spoke about the increasing prevalence of persistent user identities and the sharing of personal information on sites such as
      <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">
       Facebook
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">
       Twitter
      </a>
      and he also spoke about the value of anonymous posting on sites such as 4chan.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fred Leal of the Brazilian newspaper
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/O_Estado_de_S._Paulo" title="O Estado de S. Paulo">
        O Estado de S. Paulo
       </a>
      </i>
      said his inclusion in the conference "indicates that something extraordinary is happening... [4chan] challenges every Internet convention: it is, alone, the antithesis of Google, social networking sites, and blogs."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2010, Poole was reported to have raised $625,000 to create a new online enterprise,
      <a href="/wiki/Canvas_Networks" title="Canvas Networks">
       Canvas
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cha_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cha-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechRev_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-TechRev-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The web site opened on January 31, 2011, and features digitally modified images uploaded by users who are required to self-identify using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facebook_Connect" title="Facebook Connect">
       Facebook Connect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeffries_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jeffries-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2010, Poole gave evidence in the trial,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Palin_email_hack" title="Sarah Palin email hack">
        United States of America v. David Kernell
       </a>
      </i>
      , as a government witness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As a witness, Poole explained the terminology used on 4chan to the prosecutor, ranging from "OP" to "lurker." He also explained to the court the nature of the data given to the FBI as part of the search warrant, including how users may be identified uniquely from site audit logs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In a 2010 interview, Poole discussed his belief in the value of multiple identities, including anonymity, in contrast to the merger of online and real-world identities occurring on
      <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">
       Facebook
      </a>
      and many other social networking sites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechRev_15-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-TechRev-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2012, Poole sent a
      <a href="/wiki/Cease_and_desist" title="Cease and desist">
       cease and desist
      </a>
      letter to Moot.It, an internet startup.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Betabeat_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Betabeat-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tripfags.com_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tripfags.com-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In January 2014, Poole announced that Canvas, and its DrawQuest feature, would be going out of business.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-close_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-close-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BI_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-BI-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-verge-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In January 2015, Poole announced that he would be stepping down as the 4chan administrator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On January 23, moot hosted a final Q&amp;A with site users using the /qa/ board and YouTube to livestream. This marked the beginning of his "retirement" from being an administrator and owner of the web site after eleven-and-a-half years.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He began a process of turning control of the site over to three anonymous 4chan moderators while searching for a buyer for the website.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">
       Identity
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Previously known only as moot, Poole's name was revealed on July 9, 2008, in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">
        The Wall Street Journal
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wsj-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The same day,
      <a href="/wiki/Lev_Grossman" title="Lev Grossman">
       Lev Grossman
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
        Time
       </a>
      </i>
      published an interview describing the influence of Poole as a non-visible administrator, as "one of the most [significant]" on the evolution of content collaboration. Although Grossman's article began with the confession that "I don't even know his real name," he claimed to identify moot as Christopher Poole.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-time-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later, on July 10, Grossman admitted that there was an outside chance that Christopher Poole was not the real name of moot, rather an obscure reference to a 4chan inside joke.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blog_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-blog-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">
        Washington Post
       </a>
      </i>
      concurred that "Christopher Poole" could be "a big hoax, a 'gotcha.' It would be just what you'd expect from the creator of 4chan."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP-17Feb09_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WP-17Feb09-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In March 2009,
      <i>
       Time
      </i>
      backpedaled somewhat on the identity issue by placing the moot persona on the 2009
      <a href="/wiki/Time_100" title="Time 100">
       Time 100
      </a>
      finalists list.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time1000nom_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Time1000nom-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Prior to the
      <i>
       Wall Street Journal
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Time
      </i>
      interviews, moot deliberately kept his real identity separate from 4chan. He told Grossman, "my personal private life is very separate from my Internet life... There's a
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/firewall" title="wikt:firewall">
       firewall
      </a>
      in between."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_28-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-time-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As moot, he has spoken at conferences at
      <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">
       Yale University
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
       Massachusetts Institute of Technology
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_28-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-time-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A 2008 article in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">
        The Observer
       </a>
      </i>
      had him down as "the most influential web entrepreneur you've never heard of," although he has been described since that time in more limited terms such as a "benefactor."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ObserverGoogle_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ObserverGoogle-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In February 2009,
      <i>
       The Washington Post
      </i>
      reported that Poole had attended
      <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Commonwealth_University" title="Virginia Commonwealth University">
       Virginia Commonwealth University
      </a>
      for a few semesters before dropping out. It reported that Poole was living with his mother while looking for a way to make money from owning 4chan.
