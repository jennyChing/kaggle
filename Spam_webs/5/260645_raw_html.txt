     <p>
      The
      <b>
       cockatiel
      </b>
      (
      <i>
       Nymphicus hollandicus
      </i>
      ), also known as the
      <b>
       quarrion
      </b>
      and the
      <b>
       weiro
      </b>
      , is a member of the cockatoo family
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemism_in_birds" title="Endemism in birds">
       endemic
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      . They are prized as household
      <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">
       pets
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Companion_parrot" title="Companion parrot">
       companion parrots
      </a>
      throughout the world and are relatively easy to breed. As a caged bird, cockatiels are second in popularity only to the
      <a href="/wiki/Budgerigar" title="Budgerigar">
       budgerigar
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ausmus_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ausmus-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cockatiel is the only member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">
       genus
      </a>
      <i>
       <b>
        Nymphicus
       </b>
      </i>
      . It was previously considered a crested parrot or small cockatoo; however, more recent molecular studies have assigned it to its own subfamily,
      <i>
       Nymphicinae
      </i>
      . It is, therefore, now classified as the smallest of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cacatuidae" title="Cacatuidae">
       Cacatuidae
      </a>
      (cockatoo family). Cockatiels are native to Australia, and favour the Australian wetlands, scrublands, and bush lands.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_etymology">
       Taxonomy and etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cockatiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Originally described by Scottish writer and naturalist
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kerr_(writer)" title="Robert Kerr (writer)">
       Robert Kerr
      </a>
      in 1793 as
      <i>
       Psittacus hollandicus
      </i>
      , the cockatiel (or cockateel) was moved to its own genus,
      <i>
       Nymphicus
      </i>
      , by
      <a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Wagler" title="Johann Georg Wagler">
       Wagler
      </a>
      in 1832. Its genus name reflects the experience of one of the earliest groups of
      <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
       Europeans
      </a>
      to see the birds in their native
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">
       habitat
      </a>
      ; the travellers thought the birds were so beautiful that they named them after mythical
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nymphs" title="Nymphs">
       nymphs
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Specific_name_(zoology)" title="Specific name (zoology)">
       specific name
      </a>
      <i>
       hollandicus
      </i>
      refers to
      <a href="/wiki/New_Holland_(Australia)" title="New Holland (Australia)">
       New Holland
      </a>
      , a historic name for Australia.
     </p>
     <p>
      Its biological relationship had long been argued; it is now classified into a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotypic" title="Monotypic">
       monotypic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">
       subfamily
      </a>
      Nymphicinae but had sometimes in the past been misclassified among the
      <a href="/wiki/Platycercinae" title="Platycercinae">
       Platycercinae
      </a>
      , the broad-tailed parrots. This issue has now been settled with molecular studies. A 1984 study of protein allozymes signalled its closer relationship to cockatoos than to parrots,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams1984_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-adams1984-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA">
       Mitochondrial
      </a>
      12S
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RRNA" title="RRNA">
       rRNA
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">
       sequence
      </a>
      data
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      places it amongst the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calyptorhynchinae" title="Calyptorhynchinae">
       Calyptorhynchinae
      </a>
      (dark cockatoos)
      <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">
       subfamily
      </a>
      . The unique,
      <a href="/wiki/Parakeet" title="Parakeet">
       parakeet
      </a>
      (meaning long-tailed parrot) morphological feature is a consequence of the decrease in size and accompanying change of
      <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">
       ecological niche
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Sequence analysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Intron" title="Intron">
       intron
      </a>
      7 of the
      <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_DNA" title="Nuclear DNA">
       nuclear
      </a>
      <i>
       ?
      </i>
      -
      <a href="/wiki/Fibrinogen" title="Fibrinogen">
       fibrinogen
      </a>
      gene, on the other hand, indicates that it may yet be distinct enough as to warrant recognition of the Nymphicinae rather than inclusion of the genus in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calyptorhynchinae" title="Calyptorhynchinae">
       Calyptorhynchinae
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cockatiel is now biologically classified as a genuine member of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cacatuidae" title="Cacatuidae">
       Cacatuidae
      </a>
      on account of sharing all of the cockatoo family's biological features, namely, the erectile crest, a
      <a href="/wiki/Gallbladder" title="Gallbladder">
       gallbladder
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powder_down" title="Powder down">
       powder down
      </a>
      , suppressed cloudy-layer (which precludes the display of blue and green structural colours), and facial feathers covering the sides of the beak, all of which are rarely found outside the Cacatuidae family.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_dimorphism">
       Sexual dimorphism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cockatiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sexual dimorphism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      All wild cockatiel chicks and juveniles look female, and are virtually indistinguishable from the time of hatching until their first
      <a href="/wiki/Moulting" title="Moulting">
       moulting
      </a>
      . They display horizontal yellow stripes or bars on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">
       ventral
      </a>
      surface of their tail feathers, yellow spots on the ventral surface of the primary flight feathers of their wings, a grey coloured crest and face, and a dull orange patch on each of their cheeks.
     </p>
     <p>
      Adult cockatiels are
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">
       sexually dimorphic
      </a>
      , though to a lesser degree than many other avian species. This is only evident after the first moulting, typically occurring about six to nine months after hatching: the male loses the white or yellow barring and spots on the underside of his tail feathers and wings. The grey feathers on his cheeks and crest are replaced by bright yellow feathers, while the orange cheek patch becomes brighter and more distinct. The face and crest of the female will typically remain mostly grey, though also with an orange cheek patch. Additionally, the female commonly retains the horizontal barring on the underside of her tail feathers.
     </p>
     <p>
      The colour in cockatiels is derived from two
      <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">
       pigments
      </a>
      :
      <a href="/wiki/Melanin" title="Melanin">
       melanin
      </a>
      (which provides the grey colour in the feathers, eyes, beak, and feet), and
      <a href="/wiki/Lipochrome" title="Lipochrome">
       lipochromes
      </a>
      (which provide the yellow colour on the face and tail and the orange colour of the cheek patch). The grey colour of the melanin overrides the yellow and orange of the lipochromes when both are present.
     </p>
     <p>
      The melanin content decreases in the face of the males as they mature, allowing the yellow and orange lipochromes to be more visible, while an increase in melanin content in the tail causes the disappearance of the horizontal yellow tail bars.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to these visible characteristics, the vocalization of adult males is typically louder and more complex than that of females.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayal">
       Portrayal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cockatiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Portrayal">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brehms_Tierleben_Cockatiels.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brehms_Tierleben_Cockatiels.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        1927

     <p>
      The cockatiel's distinctive erectile
      <a href="/wiki/Crest_(feathers)" title="Crest (feathers)">
       crest
      </a>
      expresses the animal's emotional state. The crest is dramatically vertical when the cockatiel is startled or excited, gently oblique in its neutral or relaxed state, and flattened close to the head when the animal is angry or defensive. The crest is also held flat but protrudes outward in the back when the cockatiel is trying to appear alluring or flirtatious. In contrast to most cockatoos, the cockatiel has long tail feathers roughly making up half of its total length. At 30 to 33cm (1213in), the cockatiel is the smallest of the cockatoos which are generally larger at between 30 and 60cm (1224in).
     </p>
     <p>
      The "normal grey" or "wild-type" cockatiel's plumage is primarily grey with prominent white flashes on the outer edges of each wing. The face of the male is yellow or white, while the face of the female is primarily grey or light grey, and both sexes feature a round orange area on both ears, often referred to as "cheddar cheeks". This orange colouration is generally vibrant in adult males, and often quite muted in females. Visual sexing is often possible with this variant of the bird.
     </p>

      Cockatiels are native to Australia, where they are found largely in

      <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">
       Western Australian
      </a>
      deserts, and
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">

      </a>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_span">
       Life span
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cockatiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Life span">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The cockatiel's lifespan in captivity is generally given as 16 to 25 years,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though it is sometimes given as short as 10 to 15 years, and there are reports of cockatiels living as long as 32 years, the oldest confirmed specimen reported being 36 years old.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Diet and exercise are major determining factors.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Colour_mutations">
       Colour mutations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cockatiel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Colour mutations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Cockatiel_colour_genetics" title="Cockatiel colour genetics">
       Cockatiel colour genetics
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fifteen different cockatiel colour
      <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">
       mutations
      </a>
      are currently established in
      <a href="/wiki/Aviculture" title="Aviculture">
       aviculture
      </a>
      , including grey, pied, pearled, cinnamon, whitefaced, lutino, albino (a.k.a. whitefaced lutino) and yellowcheeked cockatiels. Mutations in captivity have emerged in various colours, some quite different from those observed in nature. In 1949 the species began to spread throughout the world, with the creation of "wild", and then "pied" mutation developed in California in the United States. There are many mutations of cockatiels with varied colours, they are: silvestre, harlequin, lutino, cinnamon, opaline (pearl), cara black, silver, fawn, albino (there is a pattern and not just albino genetic mutations), pastel, silver and recessive silver dominant.
     </p>
