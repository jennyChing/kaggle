     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Gianduiotto
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       IPA:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">
        [dandujto]
       </a>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
       Piedmontese
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       Giandojt
      </b>
      ) is a
      <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
       Piedmontese
      </a>
      chocolate whose shape is similar to an upturned boat. Gianduiotti are individually wrapped in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tinfoil" title="Tinfoil">
       tinfoil
      </a>
      cover, usually gold or silver-colored. It is the speciality of Turin, and takes its name from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gianduja_(chocolate)" title="Gianduja (chocolate)">
        gianduja
       </a>
      </i>
      , the preparation of chocolate that is used for gianduiotti and other sweets (including
      <a href="/wiki/Nutella" title="Nutella">
       Nutella
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bicerin_Di_Gianduiotto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bicerin Di Gianduiotto (page does not exist)">
       Bicerin Di Gianduiotto
      </a>
      ); in turn, this preparation is named after
      <a href="/wiki/Gianduja_(commedia_dell%27arte)" title="Gianduja (commedia dell'arte)">
       Gianduja
      </a>
      , a mask in
      <a href="/wiki/Commedia_dell%27arte" title="Commedia dell'arte">
       commedia dell'arte
      </a>
      that represents the archetypal Piedmontese. Indeed, Gianduja's hat inspired the shape of the Gianduiotto.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gianduiotti are produced from a paste of
      <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">
       sugar
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_solids" title="Cocoa solids">
       cocoa
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">
       hazelnut
      </a>
      <i>
       Tonda Gentile delle
       <a href="/wiki/Langhe" title="Langhe">
        Langhe
       </a>
      </i>
      . The official birth of gianduiotti is set at 1865 in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torino" title="Torino">
       Turin
      </a>
      , by Paul Caffarel and Michele Prochet, the first to completely grind hazelnuts to a paste before adding them to the cocoa and sugar mix.
     </p>
     <p>
      Apparently, the idea of mixing hazelnut pieces to standard chocolates was born during
      <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">
       Napoleon
      </a>
      s reign, when importing cocoa from
      <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
       South America
      </a>
      became extremely difficult. Raw cocoa was extremely expensive, so local producers started incorporating bits of roasted hazelnuts (hazelnuts are locally grown and were easy to come by in Piedmont) to make the final product more affordable.
     </p>
     <p>
      The chocolates are particularly popular among vegans and those allergic to dairy products due to their similarity to milk chocolate.
     </p>
