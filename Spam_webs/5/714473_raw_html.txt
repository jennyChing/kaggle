         <div class="article-body" dir="ltr" itemprop="articleBody">
          <p>
           In a world where the
           <a href="http://mashable.com/2012/11/27/email-stats-infographic/" target="_blank">
            average person spends 11.2 hours every week
           </a>
           answering and monitoring email, you can imagine how the quest for inbox zero can almost feel like searching for the Holy Grail. This is why it is crucial that you save your email recipients (and yourself) time and energy by practicing good email etiquette. Below are some tips for managing and answering email:
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            1. Include as much information in the subject line as possible
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           The subject line is the first thing people see in the inbox; it makes sense to keep this as detailed as possible. A good subject line will tell the recipient
           <em>
            exactly
           </em>
           what the email is about at a glance. This way, the recipient can decide to ignore or answer the email as she sees fit.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Bad subject line:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           Meeting?
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Good subject line:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           Can we schedule a meeting at 10:30 tomorrow?
          </blockquote>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            2. Make sure to introduce yourself
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           You cant always be sure that the recipient will recognize you by name or email address. Its good practice to briefly introduce yourself at the beginning of an email to people you don't know well. This keeps confusion to a minimum and helps the recipient remember the context in which he or she met you.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Bad way to start an email:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           Dear Sam,
           <br/>
           Are you available for a meeting tomorrow?
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Good way to start an email:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <br/>
           Dear Sam,
           <br/>
           This is Mark from Salesforce. We spoke briefly about CRM integrations yesterday.
           <br/>
           Are you available for a meeting tomorrow?
          </blockquote>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            3. Ask specific questions
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Emails that ask generic questions are difficult to answer. The recipient has to think about what exactly you want to hear, and then frame a response accordingly. This is why your generic what do you think? emails usually get dropped down to the bottom of the priority list.
          </p>
          <p>
           A much better way to ensure a response is to ask pointed, specific questions.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Dont ask:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <br/>
           What do you think about using a CRM?.
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Instead, ask:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           What CRM do you recommend  Salesforce or SugarCRM?
          </blockquote>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            4. Keep chatter to a minimum
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           While its great that you care about your email recipients and want to know whats up in their lives, email isnt the tool to get it done. Business email should be about business. Those how are you doing? do nothing but waste time.
          </p>
          <p>
           Before sending an email, ask yourself: Is email the best medium for this message, or can I take this conversation to a more casual platform?
          </p>
          <p>
           If the answer is yes, then go ahead and press send. Otherwise, take the conversation to Facebook, Twitter or the phone.
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            5. Be careful with Reply All
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Reply All is the bane of business email. No one needs to see an email that has nothing to do with them pop in and out of their inbox 10 times a day. Unless you are sure your email offers value to everyone in the CC list, dont hit Reply All.
          </p>
          <h3>
           <strong>
            6. Create a follow-up schedule
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Effective salespeople have known this for years: to close a deal, you need to follow-up. In fact, research shows that on average, it
           <a href="http://www.marketingdonut.co.uk/marketing/sales/sales-techniques-and-negotiations/why-8-of-sales-people-get-80-of-the-sales" target="_blank">
            takes five follow-ups to close a deal
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Following-up ensures that your prospects remember you and continue to consider what you have to offer. It doesnt matter if you are selling a product or setting up a meeting; a strong follow-up schedule will drastically improve the success of your email prospecting efforts.
          </p>
          <p>
           To make following-up easier, use a tool like FollowUp.cc to schedule your emails.
