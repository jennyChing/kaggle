         <h2 class="blog__title">
          Academic Cyberbully Is Sentenced to Jail in Dead Sea Scrolls Case
         </h2>
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="blog__content clearfix">
         <p>
          The Dead Sea Scrolls cyberbully is being sent to jail. A judge in New York States main trial court sentenced Raphael Golb, a lawyer, to six months in prison for
          <a href="http://chronicle.com/article/Academic-Cyberbully-Is/1583/">
           using false online identities to harass and discredit academics
          </a>
          in a debate over the origin of the Dead Sea Scrolls, the
          <a href="http://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5gL88oB3kUUGltGmffjx3s-uqN4JQ?docId=bfb7c00fbb6549d5bf9345889c0a49e6">
           Associated Press
          </a>
          reported.
         </p>
         <p>
          At his trial in September, Mr. Golb was
          <a href="http://chronicle.com/article/Cyberbully-Is-Found-Guilty-on/124762/">
           found guilty
          </a>
          of 30 criminal charges, including identify theft, forgery, and aggravated harassment. He also was sentenced to five years probation, during which he cannot enter any online discussion about the Dead Sea Scrolls using a name other than his own or anonymous. And he has been disbarred.
         </p>
         <p>
          Mr. Golb, who is the son of Norman Golb, a prominent Dead Sea Scrolls scholar at the University of Chicago, plans to appeal. During the past several years, according to court documents, Raphael Golb used several Internet aliases to claim that a scholar he perceived as his fathers rival, Lawrence H. Schiffman of New York University, was a plagiarist. Raphael Golb also impersonated Mr. Schiffman by opening an e-mail account in his name and using the account to send messages admitting to the plagiarism charge. During the trial, he
          <a href="http://chronicle.com/blogs/ticker/accused-cyberbully-says-he-used-professors-name-but-did-nothing-illegal/27235">
           testified
          </a>
          that his actions were intended as satire.
         </p>
         <p>
          Robert R. Cargill, an adjunct assistant professor of Near Eastern languages and cultures at the University of California at Los Angeles, was also attacked by Raphael Golb online. Mr. Cargill told
          <em>
           The Chronicle
          </em>
          that he had not expected Mr. Golb to get jail time, but that he believes the sentence fits the crime.
         </p>
         <p>
          Mr. Cargill added, however, that the sentence added to the list of victims. Its a good old-fashioned tragedy where no one wins, he said. Its ironic that [Mr. Golb] set out to rewrite the legacy of his father, but because he employed unprofessional and illegal messages to do so, he ended up damaging his fathers legacy beyond repair. This is the greatest sentence of all.
         </p>
