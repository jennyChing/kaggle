         <p>
          <strong>
           "Broadband for America" gets most of it's budget from ISP trade group.
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.vice.com/read/cables-companies-are-astroturfing-fake-consumer-support-to-end-net-neutrality" target="_blank" title="">
           VICE
          </a>
          just published a report that calls out cable company representation for funding "cable company-created front groups," who are unabashedly trying to strike down net neutrality.
         </p>
         <p>
          The controversy starts with a
          <a href="http://www.sfgate.com/default/article/Don-t-make-the-Internet-a-public-utility-5478946.php" target="_blank" title="">
           San Francisco Chronicle
          </a>
          piece by former Senator John Sununu (R-NH), and former Congressman Harold Ford (D-TN). The opinion argues against net neutrality, reasoning that net neutrality could lead to decreased investment in broadband infrastructure.
         </p>
         <p>
          A politician arguing for or against a possible policy change? Inconceivable! The problem is that Sununu and Ford are "honorary co-chair's" of
          <a href="http://www.broadbandforamerica.com/about/mission" target="_blank" title="">
           Broadband for America
          </a>
          , a group "dedicated to ensuring every American citizen has high quality access to the Internet." So why would such a group be against something like net neutrality? It might have something to do with 57 percent of the groups budget being funded by the National Cable and Telecom Association (NCTA), a trade group operated by ISPs.
         </p>
         <p>
          It's one thing to come out against net neutrality -- no one was seriously expecting ISPs to embrace the concept -- but it's a wholly different practice to manipulate pro-consumer groups to your own end, instead of letting them actually represent consumer interests.
         </p>
         <p>
          There's other mischief afoot, too, including the like of shady lobbying groups, and other "pro-consumer" advocacy groups that have monetary ties to the cable industry. Go check out the VICE piece for a full rundown.
         </p>
         <p>
          And while you're at it? Maybe
          <a href="http://www.escapistmagazine.com/news/view/135058-FCC-Open-Comments-on-Net-Neutrality-72-000-Posts-and-Counting" target="_blank" title="">
           file an opinion with the FCC
          </a>
          , whether you're for or against net neutrality.
         </p>

         </p>
