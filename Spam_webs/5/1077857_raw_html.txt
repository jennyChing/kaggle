                The project presents a mixed programme to build a school, a gymnasium, but also incorporate a third element: encouraging biodiversity. It has been designed as part of a particularly innovative programme, environmentally speaking. The concept of the building relies on the development of a primary landscape which would draw its textures and components from the wider landscape in which it is set.
               </div>
               <a name="more">
               </a>
               <br/>
               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">
                <img alt="copyright david foessel" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-167294" height="840" src="http://www.arthitectural.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/ChartierDalix_BLG_vue_de_la_fa+%C2%BAade_rideau-%C2%ACdavid_Foessel.jpg" style="border: none; box-sizing: border-box; display: block; height: auto; margin: 7px auto; max-width: 100%; vertical-align: middle; width: 810px;" width="1120"/>
               </div>
               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">
                This project may well signal the start of a new trend: striving to return biodiversity to the heart of urban areas. More than any other project, building a school is an opportunity to re-think the fundamental conceptual connections between poetry, education and nature, drawing inspiration from new aesthetic impulses. Thus, the building takes up the challenge of recreating a fully functional eco-system as a place of learning, a space where local children will go to fulfil their potential, but also a social hub for local residents.
               </div>
               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">

                The project involves two structures: a school with eighteen classrooms (seven pre-school, eleven primary school) and a gymnasium which will be open to local residents. It is located in Boulogne-Billancourt on the old Renault grounds, now a densely built area. The two structures are united in a single volume, bounded by a same skin: the mineral wall.
               </div>
               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">
                <a href="http://www.arthitectural.com/chartier-dalix-architectes-primary-school-for-sciences-and-biodiversity/%c2%accyrille_weiner_wec1410_cda_0543/" rel="attachment wp-att-167272" style="background: transparent; box-sizing: border-box; color: #eb268f; line-height: inherit; max-width: 100%; text-decoration: none;">

                </a>

               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">
                There are two distinct parts to the building: a mineral section  the facades  and a section made of plants  the roof. This envelope wraps itself around the school, a general volume with smooth contours and supple lines, revealing fluid interior spaces and elastic exterior ones, avoiding ruptures between volumes. The highly compact building opens onto the neighbourhood, offering a multitude of perspectives.
               </div>
               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">

                 <img alt="copyright david foessel" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-167293" height="1493" src="http://www.arthitectural.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/ChartierDalix_BLG_vue_de_la_fa+%C2%BAade_ouest-%C2%ACdavid_Foessel.jpg" style="border: none; box-sizing: border-box; display: block; height: auto; margin: 7px auto; max-width: 100%; vertical-align: middle; width: 810px;" width="1120"/>
                </a>

               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">
                The playgrounds are two outdoor spaces in conversation, in plain view of one another, gaining the measure of one another (one day the pre-school children will be in primary school). The entire building shelters a primitive natural environment which acts as a more or less long-term catalyst for biodiversity at the heart of the larger site. Indeed, this structure is alive in that its appearance changes. Through its function as a foundation for the landscape, it presents an envelope which will be different in five or ten years time, with all the uncertainty of nature, which does not necessarily appear where one might expect
               </div>
               <div style="background-color: white; box-sizing: border-box; color: #80888f; direction: ltr; font-family: 'Open Sans', sans-serif; font-size: 14px; line-height: 1.6; margin-bottom: 1.25rem; padding: 0px; text-align: justify; text-rendering: optimizelegibility;">
                <span style="line-height: 22.3999996185303px;">
                 The bark of the project, the living wall, is made of pre-fabricated blocks of concrete. These blocks present two different types of texture. The visible side is smooth, polished, it reflects the light. The other sides are ribbed, with a rough, rugged texture, melded into a single wall. This difference in surfaces helps to channel water towards the sides of blocks, thus avoiding trickling on the visible side and premature ageing.

                The opposition of these two textures also emphasizes the depth of the faade and enhances its relief. On the lower section, and up to a height of around two metres, the freestanding wall is smoothed or slopes outwards, barring access to outsiders but also potential predators. The indentations of the side faces of the wall also encourage vegetation (bowls for ferns, rough concrete for mousse); small hollows and folds are aimed at animals (overhangs for swallows, porous nooks for insects) and act as an invitation to nesting for several varieties of bird.

                The roof is a real hanging garden, twelve metres above the gymnasium. It is home to three levels of vegetation: A prairie of mesophilus plants planted in 50 centimetres of earth, a shrub-land fringe and a woodland island planted in 1 meter of earth. The continuity of environmental corridors created by flora enables natural communication between species. This elevated garden has two functions: first, for the fauna of the wall and for its own fauna, it is rich in resources (additional living habitats, nutrition, etc.) ensuring the success of biodiversity.
