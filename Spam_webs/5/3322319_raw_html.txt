         <p>
          Here is some good advice from a man who knows his trees, Christopher R. Martin, the official state forester and director of the state Department of Energy and Environmental Protection's division of forestry.
         </p>
         <p>
          When out for a look at the fall foliage, enjoy the overall display of color, the panoramic big views, but don't neglect a good close look at a single showy tree at the peak of its fall color.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           (Visit courant.com/fallfoliage to submit your fall foliage pictures.)
          </strong>
         </p>

         <p>
          "Don't miss the moment in front of you," Martin said. "Individual trees can be as spectacular as a 30-mile view."
         </p>
         <p>
          What makes the Connecticut fall foliage color so special is that it is the collective color of many species, each a little bit different, each adding its color to the overall display.
         </p>

         <p>
          The red maples have, of course, red leaves in the fall. The sugar maples are a mix of yellow, orange and red, perhaps the most colorful of all the trees. Birches and hickories are varying shades of yellow and gold. The ashes range from yellow to red-orange to a shade of purple that is distinctive.
         </p>
         <p>
          Indeed, a pleasant foliage outing might focus on the individual tree species that make up the fall mosaic of color. The ashes can be found all over the state, but are most numerous along the western edge of Fairfield and Litchfield counties.
         </p>

         <p>
          Macedonia Brook State Park in Kent, on the New York border, is a gem of a park with great foliage color, plenty of colorful sugar maples and ash trees, a most scenic brook, and great hiking opportunities. Take the Macedonia Ridge Trail, a loop trail with outlooks providing distant views into New York state, or the Cobble Mountain Trail. Color should be great anytime around Columbus Day.
         </p>
         <p>
          In eastern Connecticut, the James L. Goodwin State Forest and Conservation Center in Hampton also has excellent foliage viewing opportunities. This is another good place for sugar maples, hickories and those ashes. Jim Parda, a forester with the Connecticut Forest &amp; Park Association and program director at the center, suggests an early October visit to get a good look at the ashes. They tend to reach full color earlier than many other tree species.
         </p>

         <p>
          Black birch can have great yellow color in the fall. Watch for a huge stand of this species along a section of the Air Line Trail in East Hampton, a 50-mile-long linear park following an old rail line. The yellow leaves of the birches contrast beautifully with the rich evergreen foliage of eastern hemlocks growing among them.
         </p>
         <p>
          "The green and yellow is a nice combination," Parda said.
         </p>

         <p>
          Another tree worth a look  it tends to color late  is scarlet oak. The scarlet oak has a rich red leaf in the fall, as brilliant as the wonderful deep orange colors of the sugar maple. But the scarlet oak turns so late  late October on  that it often becomes the last blast of great color in the season, when many other trees have already shed their leaves.
         </p>
         <div class="trb_gptAd trb_articleAd_bottomBanner" data-adloader-adtype="cube" data-adloader-position="pageBottomCube" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" id="">
         </div>
         <p>
          Scarlet oaks are often found on dry, rocky or sandy soils in upland areas. A good place to get a look at them is Shenipsit State Forest in Somers, Stafford and Ellington. The scarlet oak has leaves 3- to 6-inches long, deeply lobed. A foliage outing to Shenipsit also could include a visit to the Civilian Conservation Corps museum, a former barracks for the CCC, one of the Great Depression-era job programs that put young men to work on conservation projects.
         </p>
         <p>
          For some good hickory color, along with plenty of color from oaks, Pachaug State Forest in Voluntown has trails and a nice lookout from Mount Misery, a fairly easy hike with plenty of forest views.
         </p>
