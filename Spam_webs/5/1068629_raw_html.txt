The differing yellow, green and grey blue primer coatings on the Divine Eagle suggest the usage of different materials like composite and aluminum alloys for different sections of the UAV.  For example, the grey blue forward dome on the port (left) body is likely to contain a satellite dish for long distance communications, suggesting that the material used in the grey blue sections are likely to be highly permeable to electromagnetic waves.  The grey blue is also to be found on the starboard side of the right body (facing outside), and if the airframe composition is symmetrical, likely to be found on the portside of the left body (also facing out).  Such electromagnetic permeables are likely to house the Divine Eagle\u0027s long range anti-stealth radars (radomes are made of radar transparent materials), indicating that its radar arrays are 10 meters long, which suggests transmitting lower frequency (L and S Band) radar waves (most stealth aircraft are optimized to evade higher frequency, such as X band, radar).              <h1>
              Department Of Defense Tests A Bullet That Can Steer
             </h1>
             <div class="field-subtitle">
              <p>
               Precision on the smallest scale
              </p>

            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="Artist Rendering Of Extreme Accuracy Tasked Ordnance " class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/exacto_projectile_full.jpg?itok=_SMrOEVN" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/exacto_projectile_full.jpg?itok=cCaC0ZX6" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/exacto_projectile_full.jpg?itok=r3-Dsk4w" height="773" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="Artist Rendering Of Extreme Accuracy Tasked Ordnance " class="full-width" height="464" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/exacto_projectile_full.jpg?itok=cCaC0ZX6" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Artist Rendering Of Extreme Accuracy Tasked Ordnance
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                DARPA
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Since the first gun appeared on a battlefield, bullets have dealt death in straight lines. As the shortest distance between two points, lines are great, but they mean enemies can hide behind walls or buildings and not get hit, a clearly undesirable prospect for anyone in the business of shooting enemies. Now, the Department of Defense is testing a bullet that can change direction in mid-air.
             </p>
             <p>
              With a name that calls to mind both precision and 1950s whiz-bang technical marvels, DARPAs
              <a href="http://www.darpa.mil/Our_Work/TTO/Programs/Extreme_Accuracy_Tasked_Ordnance_%28EXACTO%29.aspx">
               Extreme Accuracy Tasked Ordnance
              </a>
              , or EXACTO, is a .50 caliber round that can change direction as it flies. The details of how it works are completely secret.
             </p>
             <p>
              Its specifically
              <a href="http://www.stripes.com/news/us/dod-wants-bullet-that-can-change-direction-after-being-fired-1.319419">
               designed for snipers
              </a>
              , for whom even the slightest improvements can be a life-or-death difference.
             </p>
             <p>
              EXACTO isnt the first weapon to promise kills around corners. The U.S. Army is testing the 
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/technology/article/2010-05/us-special-forces-set-carry-laser-guided-smart-bullet-weapon-battle">
               XM-25 Counter Defilade Target Engagement Airburst Weapon System
              </a>
              . That long, Pentagonese name obscures a simple purpose: its a gun that shoots explosives that blow up after it goes a set distance, like exactly one foot over that low-lying wall or just past this corner where we just saw gunshots. The hope is that the blast is powerful enough to injure or otherwise incapacitate the hostile person its aimed at. The XM-25 should
              <a href="http://www.janes.com/article/44482/ausa-2014-xm25-counter-defilade-weapon-to-finish-development-in-2016">
               complete testing in 2016
              </a>
              , after which the Army is expected to buy thousands of them.
             </p>
