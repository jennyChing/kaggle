           Tony Maws is considered a pioneer in creative head-to-tail cookery. Hearty-yet-haute disheslike pork-belly-wrapped veal sweetbreads with green tomatoes and rye-flour spaghetti with eggplant and kid goat raguhelped earn him the reputation. But Mr. Maws believes using whole animals makes practical sense.
          </p>

             The Chef: Tony Maws

          <p>
           In 2002, after eight years of working in high-end kitchens in San Francisco, Santa Fe, N.M., Boston and Lyons, France, the Boston-born chef opened Craigie Street Bistrot in the basement of an apartment building in Cambridge, Mass. Inspired by Paris's Michelin-trained chefs opening casual bistros instead of buttoned-up restaurants, Mr. Maws liked the idea of "bucking the system," as he put itserving world-class food in an unpretentious neighborhood setting. At 32, with the help of friends, family and a $15,000 loan, he became a restaurateur. His mother manned the door and his-wife-to-be waited tables.
          </p>
          <p>
           Since the kitchen was small and money was tight, Mr. Maws shopped at farmers' markets and bought whole animals (lamb shanks were braised and offal was turned into charcuterie). Though the chipped plates and friendly service were disarming, Mr. Maws's foodrich, meaty and seriousmade critics take note. In 2005, Food and Wine magazine named him one of the country's 10 best new chefs.
          </p>

          <p>
           Craigie Street Bistrot relocated to a prominent brick building on Cambridge's Central Square in 2008 and reopened as Craigie on Main. To Mr. Maws, the new space felt like "the penthouse." Nowadays, the chefwho can be found in the kitchen almost nightlyis primarily interested in maintaining excellence and consistency. The James Beard Foundation named him Best Chef Northeast in May.
          </p>
          <p>
           For his first Slow Food Fast recipe, Mr. Maws looked to Mexico and his local farmers' market for inspiration. Here, caramelized summer squash saut, tossed with fresh cilantro, is paired with fried eggs and tomatillo salsa. Mr. Maws's love for eggs runs deep. "I'm a cook," he said. "What cook doesn't love eggs?"
          </p>
          <p>
           Though the egg makes this dish a natural for brunch, an elaborate version appears on Craigie on Main's dinner menu. At the restaurant, whole eggs are poached in a temperature-controlled water bath and served atop seasonal ragus. Currently the ragu has wild mushrooms and house-made morcilla, a Spanish pig's-blood sausage (last week, sweetbreads stood in for sausage). In both versions, the yolks, when broken, ooze into and enrich the vegetable medley.
          </p>
          <p>
           Tomatillos and summer squash, now abundant at Cambridge's market stalls, complement each other especially well, Mr. Maws said. Caramelizing the squash provides the richness we begin to crave this time of year, while the tartness of the tomatillos keeps things sunny.
          </p>
          <p>
           Mr. Maws advises cooks to make sure the tomatillos are well-charred before blitzing them with jalapeo and garlic in a blender. That charred flavor, he said, adds just the right amount of smokiness. For additional depth, Mr. Maws suggests using duck fat instead of solely olive oil.
          </p>
          <p>
           Frying eggs properly can be tricky. To prevent the yolk from breaking, Mr. Maws first cracks the eggs into a bowl and then gently slides them into a hot pan. Frying them over medium heat helps the whites cook evenly. But, if the bottom is done and the top still needs setting, he'll take the pan off the heat and cover it to steam the eggs quickly. "Don't be afraid to throw out a mistake," he said. "An overcooked egg bums me out."
          </p>

          <h6>
           Fried Eggs with Squash and Tomatillo
          </h6>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Total Time
           </strong>
           : 25 Minutes
           <strong>
            <br/>
            Serves
           </strong>
           : 4
          </p>
          <h6>
           Ingredients
          </h6>
          <p>
            jalapeo, halved lengthwise
           <br/>
            pound tomatillos (3 or 4)
           <br/>
           2 small garlic cloves, peeled
           <br/>
           5 tablespoons olive oil (or 3 tablespoons olive oil plus 2 tablespoons duck fat)
           <br/>
           4 cups 1-inch diced yellow and green squash (cut from about 1 pounds squash)
           <br/>
           Salt to taste
           <br/>
           3 sprigs cilantro plus 2 tablespoons chopped cilantro leaves, plus extra for garnish
           <br/>
           1 teaspoon fresh lime juice, plus extra for garnish
           <br/>
           4 eggs
          </p>
          <!--
-->
          <div class=" media-object margin " data-layout="
        margin">
           <div class="media-object-image enlarge-image renoImageFormat-D img-margin" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
            <div class="image-container responsive-media" data-layout-ratio="66.4122%" data-mobile-ratio="66.5738%" style="padding-bottom:66.5738%;">
             <meta content="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/OD-AK596_tomati_D_20110915223145.jpg" itemprop="representativeOfPage contentUrl"/>
             <meta content="Tomatillo" itemprop="caption description"/>
             <img alt="Tomatillo" data-enlarge="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/OD-AK596_tomati_E_20110915223145.jpg" data-in-at4units-src="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/OD-AK596_tomati_E_20110915223145.jpg" data-in-base-src="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/OD-AK596_tomati_D_20110915223145.jpg" data-intent="" src="http://si.wsj.net/public/resources/images/OD-AK596_tomati_E_20110915223145.jpg" title="Tomatillo"/>
             <span class="image-enlarge">
              ENLARGE
             </span>
            </div>
            <div class="wsj-article-caption" itemprop="caption">
             <span class="wsj-article-caption-content">
              Tomatillo
             </span>
             <span class="wsj-article-credit" itemprop="creator">

          </div>

          <h6>
           What To Do:
          </h6>
          <p>
           <strong>
            1.
           </strong>
           Turn on broiler. Place jalapeo (cut side down), tomatillos and garlic in an ovenproof dish and broil until tomatillos are charred, about 7-10 minutes.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            2.
           </strong>
           Meanwhile, set a large, heavy skillet over medium-high heat. Swirl in 2 tablespoons olive oil or duck fat. Once hot, add squash in a single, even layer. (If necessary, cook in 2 batches.) Season with salt and cook 4-5 minutes, or until caramelized. Stir and cook 4-5 minutes more, or until tender. Remove from heat, stir in chopped cilantro and season to taste with salt.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            3.
           </strong>
           Transfer tomatillo mixture to blender with cilantro sprigs. Blend on high until smooth, slowly adding 1 tablespoons olive oil. Season to taste with lime juice and salt.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            4.
           </strong>
           Crack eggs into small bowl. Wipe pan and set over medium heat. Once pan is hot, swirl in 1 tablespoons oil, or enough to coat bottom. Tip eggs gently into pan. Season with salt and fry 3-4 minutes, or until whites are cooked and yolks just begin to set.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            5.
           </strong>
           To serve, spoon 2 tablespoons warm tomatillo sauce into 4 shallow dishes. Mound roasted squash in center. Top with fried egg. Garnish with cilantro sprig and lime wedge.
          </p>
