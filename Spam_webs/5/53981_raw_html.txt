                  <h1 class="node-title" rel="nofollow">
                   Japan's first robot astronaut is both useful and adorable
                  </h1>
                  <h1 class="subheadline">
                   <div class="field field-name-field-subheadline field-type-text-long field-label-hidden view-mode-full">
                    <div class="field-items">
                     <div class="field-item even">
                      <p>
                       At just 13 inches tall, cheery-faced Kirobo is designed to help astronauts cope with loneliness during missions.
                      </p>
                     </div>

                  </h1>
                  <span class="submitted">

                  <div class="field field-name-field-node-image field-type-image field-label-hidden view-mode-full">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <figure class="clearfix field-item even">
                     <img alt="" class="image-style-fullnode-image" height="348" src="http://www.newstatesman.com/sites/default/files/styles/fullnode_image/public/articles_2013/kiboro-31.jpg?itok=iDvXry1C" typeof="foaf:Image" width="510"/>

                  <div class="field field-name-field-nodeimage-title field-type-text field-label-hidden view-mode-full">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item even">
                     Kirobo, bringing joy to the lonely on the ISS. (Photo: Kibo Robot Project)
                    </div>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden view-mode-full">
                   <div class="field-items">
                    <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
                     <p>
                      It's not quite as good as being able to take a pet dog or cat into orbit, but Kirobo - 13 inches tall, weighing 2kg - is a pretty good alternative. He
                      <a href="http://www.space.com/22235-japan-launches-talking-space-robot-astronaut.html">
                       went up to the ISS on a Japanese resupply mission in August
                      </a>
                      , but is only beginning his research mission today.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      He - well, "it" if we're being accurate - isn't meant to help astronauts with technical tasks, or carry out his own spacewalks. He's an emotional resource. Space can be lonely, and having someone to talk to can be comforting.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      Voice and face recognition combined with natural language processing give him the ability to converse with humans. His name is a merger of "kibo" - "hope in Japanese, and the name of the nation's research module on the ISS - and robot.
                     </p>
                     <p>
                      While he flew up to the ISS in August,
                      <a href="http://www.wired.co.uk/news/archive/2013-11/06/kirobo-space-robot">
                       he's only being joined today
                      </a>
                      by Japanese astronaut Koichi Wakata, who originally trained Kirobo to speak. Wakata is part of a three-man crew launching from Kazakhstan, and when he arrives he'll enjoy the distinction of two new records - the first astronaut to chat with a robot in space, and the ISS's first Japanese commander.
                     </p>
