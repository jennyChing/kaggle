     <div class="asset-header">
      <h1 class="entry-title" id="page-title">
       Beijing skylines yields dramatic view of choking city
      </h1>
      <div class="asset-meta">
       <span class="byline">
        <!--
&lt;span class="vcard author"&gt;Celeste Biever&lt;/span&gt; &lt;span class="separator"&gt;|&lt;/span&gt; -->
        <abbr class="published" title="2013-01-15T16:36:00+00:00">
         16:36 15 January 2013
        </abbr>
       </span>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="entry-categories floatclear">
      <div class="floatleft">
       <div>
        <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/blogs/shortsharpscience/environment/" rel="tag" title="Find environment news and search the latest global warming articles. Read up to the minute coverage of climate change from tsunamis and pollution to hurricanes.">
         Environment
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="floatleft">
       <div>
        <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/blogs/shortsharpscience/picture-of-the-day/" rel="tag" title="">
         Picture of the Day
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="asset-content entry-content">
      <p>
       <i>
        Fred Pearce, consultant, and Michael Marshall, reporter
       </i>
      </p>
      <i>
       <img alt="Lead.jpg" class="mt-image-none" height="400" src="https://www.newscientist.com/blogs/shortsharpscience/Lead.jpg" style="" width="600"/>
      </i>
      <p>
       <em>
        (Images: Ed Jones/AFP/Getty Images)
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
       </i>
       Beijing is choking - as these two pictures vividly demonstrate. Above is the city's skyline yesterday; below is the same view on 4 February 2012. The suffocating smog is not just
       <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/mg19726403.600-how-traffic-pollution-damages-the-heart.html">
        bad for health
       </a>
       , it may also have a more potent effect on climate change than
       <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/article/mg20527481.400-smoke-bomb-the-other-climate-culprits.html">
        previously thought
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <img alt="2nd.jpg" class="mt-image-none" height="400" src="https://www.newscientist.com/blogs/shortsharpscience/2013/01/15/2nd.jpg" style="" width="600"/>
      <span id="more">
      </span>
      <p>
       Dense smog shrouded Beijing over the weekend, causing levels of airborne soot, also known as black carbon, to go off the charts.
       <a href="http://usa.chinadaily.com.cn/opinion/2013-01/14/content_16111178.htm">
        Some reports
       </a>
       put levels of one type of tiny airborne soot particle at well over 30 times the
       <a href="http://whqlibdoc.who.int/hq/2006/WHO_SDE_PHE_OEH_06.02_eng.pdf">
        World Health Organization's recommended limit
       </a>
       in parts of the city, and hospital admissions were up.
      </p>
      <p>
       But as well as hurting health, soot has an effect on climate change - and a new study suggests this is more potent than we thought.
       <a href="http://cee.illinois.edu/research/bondresearch">
        Tami Bond
       </a>
       of the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign and colleagues report that its warming effect is almost twice the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's estimates, which would make soot the second biggest warmer after carbon dioxide (
       <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jgrd.50171/abstract">
        <i>
         Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres
        </i>
        , DOI: 10.1002/jgrd.50171
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The good news is that unlike CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , soot stays in the air for only a few days. So action to curb soot from coal fires, diesel exhausts and cooking stoves could be a quick antidote, says Bond. Such a measure would save lives, too. Soot is estimated to kill 2 million people a year, mostly through indoor pollution from cooking stoves.
      </p>
