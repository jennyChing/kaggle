         <h1 class="entry-title">
          How To Make Hoshigaki (Dried Persimmons)
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <div class="diagonal-line">
          </div>
          <div class="author">
           <img alt="Mr. Homegrown" class="avatar avatar-77 photo" height="77" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/01/Mr.-Homegrown_avatar_1390708646-77x77.jpg" width="77"/>

         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_8065" style="width: 650px">
          <img alt="" class="size-rootsimple wp-image-8065" height="479" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/800px-Dried_Kaki_Fruit-669x501.jpg" title="800px-Dried_Kaki_Fruit" width="640"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Hoshigaki image from Wikipedia
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          <em>
           Hoshigaki
          </em>
          are a Japanese delicacy made by, believe it or not, gently massaging persimmons while they air dry. I took a workshop this weekend taught by Laurence Hauben on how to make this remarkable fall treat. Its persimmon season right now, so if you want to try this at home you better jump on it. While a lot can go wrong in the month it takes to make
          <em>
           Hoschigaki
          </em>
          , the process is not complicated.
          <span id="more-8061">
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           What kind of persimmons to use
          </strong>
          <br/>
          The persimmons used to make
          <em>
           Hoshigaki
          </em>
          are astringent varieties such asHachiya. Ideally, choose fruit that still has part of the stem. There is a workaround later in these instructions if you cant find persimmons with stems. Use firm fruit, though a few soft spots are ok.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Slicing and peeling
          </strong>
          <br/>
          The first step is to cut the top off, while carefully leaving the stem that they will hang from. Note the knife trick you use to do this. Turn the fruit, not the knife:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8067" height="480" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/http___makeagif.com_media_11-12-2012_Q7Jv3M.gif" title="http___makeagif.com_media_11-12-2012_Q7Jv3M" width="640"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Next you peel the skin off the fruit:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-rootsimple wp-image-8070" height="479" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/peeled1-669x501.jpg" title="peeled" width="640"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Hanging
          </strong>
          <br/>
          Cut a 24 inch length of string and tie it into a loop.Take two similarly sized fruit and tie their stems together with the string:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-rootsimple wp-image-8073" height="479" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/pair2-669x501.jpg" title="pair" width="640"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          A 2 inch diameter bamboo pole is just about right to keep the persimmons separated.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-rootsimple wp-image-8074" height="479" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/hanging-669x501.jpg" title="hanging" width="640"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          A sunny window is a good place to hang the persimmons. The curtain, in the photo above, was only closed for the sake of the class. Its best, actually, if the fruit gets sun. Ive got mine hanging in our living room in a sunny window. And you want good air floweither a fan or an open window (if it isnt too cold out).
         </p>
         <p>
          If the fruit does not have a stem attached you can insert a screw:
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-rootsimple wp-image-8077" height="548" src="http://www.rootsimple.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/screw-669x573.jpg" title="screw" width="640"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Drying
          </strong>
          <br/>
          The first week you dont touch them. After a week has passed you begin to gently massage each persimmon every other day. Hauben suggested a light touch rather than a deep tissue massage.
         </p>
         <p>
          After a few weeks of massaging, fructose in the fruit will begin to come to the surface, making it look like the persimmons have been dipped in powdered sugar. They are done when they have a chewy texture.
         </p>
         <p>
          In Japan, persimmons are dried by hanging them from the eaves of houses. A friend of mine who tried that had every single one consumed by squirrels in just one day. So indoors is the way to go.
         </p>
         <p>
          Haubens class was really greatwe went home with a crate of fruit and even our own bamboo pole. She teaches in the Santa Barbara area and you can find out more about her classes on her website:
          <a href="http://www.marketforays.com/">
           www.marketforays.com
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          For more detailed Hochigaki directions there is an
          <a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/HoshiGaki/?ALLSTEPS">
           Instructable
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          Ill report back in a few weeks on how our batch went.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           <em>
            Update:
           </em>
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Heres a pic of our fruit after hanging inside for about a week. So far all looks good:

            <p>
             Im working on my fifth season of hoshigaki in the Los Angeles area. Each year varies, because of weather conditions.  This year is very hot and dry (only occasional rain or morning dampness)which makes for quicker drying.  When the weather is damp, green mold forms in the creases. Ive read that it can be removed with a damp toothbrush. Ive tried this and it works.
            </p>
            <p>
             About bugs, we have lots of flies (because we have chickens), but less at this time of year. I have not seen flies landing on the hoshigaki (right outside our windows and much observed).  Fruit flies are not interested either, probably because the persimmon creates a new skin after its peeled.  Also curiously, these have been impervious to bird and squirrel visitors, too.
            </p>
