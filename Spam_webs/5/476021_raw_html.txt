     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Tenerife airport disaster
      </b>
      was a fatal
      <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">
       runway
      </a>
      collision between two
      <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">
       Boeing 747s
      </a>
      on Sunday, March 27, 1977 at
      <a href="/wiki/Tenerife_North_Airport" title="Tenerife North Airport">
       Los Rodeos Airport
      </a>
      (now Tenerife North Airport) on the Spanish island of
      <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">
       Tenerife
      </a>
      , one of the
      <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
       Canary Islands
      </a>
      . The crash killed 583 people, making it the
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_aircraft_accidents_and_incidents_resulting_in_at_least_50_fatalities" title="List of aircraft accidents and incidents resulting in at least 50 fatalities">
       deadliest accident in aviation history
      </a>
      . As a result of the complex interaction of organizational influences, environmental preconditions, and unsafe acts leading up to this aircraft mishap, the disaster at Tenerife has served as a textbook example for reviewing the processes and frameworks used in aviation mishap investigations and accident prevention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A bomb explosion at
      <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria_Airport" title="Gran Canaria Airport">
       Gran Canaria Airport
      </a>
      , and the threat of a second bomb, caused many aircraft to be diverted to Los Rodeos Airport. Among them were
      <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">
       KLM
      </a>
      Flight 4805 and
      <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">
       Pan Am
      </a>
      Flight 1736  the two aircraft involved in the accident. At Los Rodeos Airport, air traffic controllers were forced to park many of the airplanes on the taxiway, thereby blocking it. Further complicating the situation, while authorities waited to reopen Gran Canaria, a dense fog developed at Tenerife, greatly reducing visibility.
     </p>
     <p>
      When Gran Canaria reopened, the parked aircraft blocking the taxiway at Tenerife required both of the 747s to taxi on the only runway in order to get in position for takeoff. The fog was so thick that neither aircraft could see the other, and the controller in the tower could not see the runway or the two 747s on it. As the airport did not have ground radar, the controller could find where each airplane was only by voice reports over the radio. As a result of several misunderstandings, the KLM flight tried to take off while the Pan Am flight was still on the runway. The resulting collision destroyed both aircraft, killing all 248 aboard the KLM flight and 335 of 396 aboard the Pan Am flight. Sixty-one people aboard the Pan Am flight, including the pilots and flight engineer, survived the disaster.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As the accident occurred in Spanish territory, Spain was responsible for investigating the accident. Investigators from the Netherlands and the United States also participated. The investigation revealed that the primary cause of the accident was the captain of the KLM flight taking off without clearance from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Traffic_Control" title="Air Traffic Control">
       Air Traffic Control
      </a>
      (ATC).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The investigation specified that the captain did not intentionally take off without clearance; rather he fully believed he had clearance to take off due to misunderstandings between his flight crew and ATC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dutch investigators placed a greater emphasis on this than their American and Spanish counterparts,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutch_response_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dutch_response-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but ultimately KLM admitted their crew was responsible for the accident, and the airline financially compensated the victims' relatives.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KLM_responsibility_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-KLM_responsibility-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The accident had a lasting influence on the industry, particularly in the area of communication. An increased emphasis was placed on using standardized phraseology in ATC communication by controllers and pilots alike, thereby reducing the chance for misunderstandings. As part of these changes, the word "takeoff" was removed from general usage, and is only spoken by ATC when clearing an aircraft to take off
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or when cancelling that same clearance. Less experienced flight crew members were encouraged to challenge their captains when they believed something was not correct, and captains were instructed to listen to their crew and evaluate all decisions in light of crew concerns. This concept was later expanded into what is known today as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crew_Resource_Management" title="Crew Resource Management">
       Crew Resource Management
      </a>
      . CRM training is now mandatory for all airline pilots.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_history">
       Flight history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Flight history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="800" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg/220px-KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg/330px-KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a2/KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg/440px-KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KLM_Magazine_that_contains_Captain_Jacob_Veldhuyzen_Van_Zanten.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Photograph of Veldhuyzen van Zanten in a KLM magazine
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      For both planes, Tenerife was an unscheduled stop. Their destination was
      <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria_Airport" title="Gran Canaria Airport">
       Gran Canaria International Airport
      </a>
      (also known as Las Palmas Airport), serving
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Palmas_de_Gran_Canaria" title="Las Palmas de Gran Canaria">
       Las Palmas
      </a>
      on the nearby island of
      <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">
       Gran Canaria
      </a>
      . Both are in the Canary Islands, an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_community_of_Spain" title="Autonomous community of Spain">
       autonomous community of Spain
      </a>
      located in the Atlantic Ocean off the southwest coast of
      <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
       Morocco
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Pan Am Flight 1736 had originated at
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">
       Los Angeles International Airport
      </a>
      , with an intermediate stop at New York's
      <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">
       John F. Kennedy International Airport
      </a>
      . The aircraft was a Boeing 747-121, registration N736PA, named
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Clipper_Victor" title="Clipper Victor">
        Clipper Victor
       </a>
      </i>
      . Of the 380 passengers (mostly of retirement age, but including two children), 14 had boarded in New York, where the crew was also changed. The new crew consisted of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(airlines)" title="Captain (airlines)">
       Captain
      </a>
      Victor Grubbs,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Officer_(civil_aviation)" title="First Officer (civil aviation)">
       First Officer
      </a>
      Robert Bragg, and Flight Engineer George Warns; there were 13 other crew members. The same aircraft had operated the inaugural 747 commercial flight on January 22, 1970.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airdisaster.com_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-airdisaster.com-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      KLM Flight 4805, a charter flight for Holland International Travel Group, had arrived from
      <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">
       Amsterdam Airport Schiphol
      </a>
      , the Netherlands.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airdisaster.com_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-airdisaster.com-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Its captain was
      <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Veldhuyzen_van_Zanten" title="Jacob Veldhuyzen van Zanten">
       Jacob Veldhuyzen van Zanten
      </a>
      , KLM's chief flying instructor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The First Officer was Klaas Meurs and the Flight Engineer was Willem Schreuder. The aircraft was a Boeing 747-206B, registration PH-BUF, named
      <i>
       Rijn (
       <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">
        Rhine
       </a>
       )
      </i>
      . The KLM jet had 14 crew members and 235 passengers, including 52 children. Most of the KLM passengers were Dutch; four Germans, two Austrians and two Americans were also on the plane. After the aircraft landed at Tenerife, the passengers were transported to the airport terminal. One of the inbound passengers, who lived on the island, chose not to re-board the 747, leaving 234 passengers on board.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOVA-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Fifty-two of the passengers were children who were with their families.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOVA-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Disaster">
       Disaster
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disaster">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Diversion_of_aircraft_to_Los_Rodeos">
       Diversion of aircraft to Los Rodeos
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diversion of aircraft to Los Rodeos">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Both flights had been routine until they approached the islands. At 1:15 pm, a bomb (planted by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Separatist" title="Separatist">
       separatist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
       anti-Francoist
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fuerzas_Armadas_Guanches" title="Fuerzas Armadas Guanches">
       Fuerzas Armadas Guanches
      </a>
      ) exploded in the terminal of Gran Canaria International Airport, injuring one person.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There had been a phone call warning of the bomb, and soon after another call claimed that a second bomb was at the airport. The civil aviation authorities had therefore closed the airport temporarily after the bomb detonated and diverted all of its incoming flights to Los Rodeos, including the two Boeing 747 aircraft involved in the disaster.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Pan Am crew indicated that they would prefer to circle in a holding pattern until landing clearance was given, but were ordered to divert to Los Rodeos.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In all, five
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span>
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      large aircraft were diverted to Los Rodeos, a regional airport that could not easily accommodate them. The airport had only one
      <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">
       runway
      </a>
      and one major
      <a href="/wiki/Taxiway" title="Taxiway">
       taxiway
      </a>
      parallel to it, with several small taxiways connecting the two. While waiting for Gran Canaria airport to reopen, the diverted aircraft took up so much space that they were parked on the long taxiway, meaning that it could not be used for
      <a href="/wiki/Taxiing" title="Taxiing">
       taxiing
      </a>
      . Instead, departing aircraft had to taxi along the runway to position themselves for takeoff, a procedure known as a runway
      <a href="/wiki/Backtaxi" title="Backtaxi">
       backtaxi
      </a>
      or backtrack.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the threat at Gran Canaria had been contained, authorities reopened that airport. The Pan Am aircraft was ready to depart from Tenerife, but the KLM plane and a refueling vehicle obstructed its access to the runway. The Pan Am aircraft was unable to maneuver around the fueling KLM, reach the runway and depart due to a lack of just 12ft (3.7m) of clearance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten had decided to fully refuel at Los Rodeos instead of Las Palmas, apparently to save time. The refueling took about 35 minutes. After that, the passengers were brought back to the plane. The search for a missing family of four delayed the flight even further.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Taxiing_and_takeoff_preparations">
       Taxiing and takeoff preparations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Taxiing and takeoff preparations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Following the tower's instructions, the KLM was cleared to taxi the full length of the runway and make a 180 turn to get into takeoff position.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the KLM was backtaxiing on the runway, the controller asked the flight crew to report when it was ready to copy the
      <a href="/wiki/Instrument_flight_rules#Separation_and_clearance" title="Instrument flight rules">
       ATC clearance
      </a>
      . Because the flight crew was performing the checklist, copying this clearance was postponed until the aircraft was in takeoff position on Runway 30.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shortly afterward, the Pan Am was instructed to follow the KLM down the same runway, exit it by taking the third exit on their left and then use the parallel taxiway. Initially, the crew was unclear as to whether the controller had told them to take the first or third exit. The crew asked for clarification and the controller responded emphatically by replying: "The third one, sir; one, two, three; third, third one". The crew began the taxi and proceeded to identify the unmarked taxiways using an airport diagram as they reached them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The crew successfully identified the first two taxiways (C-1 and C-2), but their discussion in the cockpit never indicated that they had sighted the third taxiway (C-3), which they had been instructed to use.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There were no markings or signs to identify the runway exits and they were in conditions of poor visibility. The Pan Am crew appeared to remain unsure of their position on the runway until the collision, which occurred near the intersection with the fourth taxiway (C-4).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The angle of the third taxiway would have required the plane to perform a turn of approximately 148, which would lead back toward the still-crowded main
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airport_ramp" title="Airport ramp">
       apron
      </a>
      . At the end of C-3, the Pan Am would have to make another 148 turn in order to continue taxiing towards the start of the runway. Taxiway C-4 would have required two 35 turns. A study carried out by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Line_Pilots_Association" title="Air Line Pilots Association">
       Air Line Pilots Association
      </a>
      after the accident concluded that making the second 148 turn at the end of taxiway C-3 would have been "a practical impossibility".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Subsequent performance calculations and taxi tests with a Boeing 747 turning off on an intersection comparable to the C-3 at Tenerife, as part of the Dutch investigation, indicate that in all probability the turns could have been made. The official report from the Spanish authorities explains that the controller instructed the Pan Am aircraft to use the third taxiway because this was the earliest exit that they could take to reach the unobstructed section of the parallel taxiway.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_conditions_at_Los_Rodeos">
       Weather conditions at Los Rodeos
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Weather conditions at Los Rodeos">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Tenerife / Los Rodeos airport is at 633 metres (2,077 feet)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Height_above_mean_sea_level" title="Height above mean sea level">
       above sea level
      </a>
      , which accounts for cloud behaviour that differs from that at most other airports. Clouds at 600m (2,000ft) above ground level at the nearby coast, are at ground level at Los Rodeos / Tenerife North. Drifting clouds of different densities cause wildly varying visibilities, from unhindered at one moment to below the minimums the next. The collision took place in a high density cloud.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Pan Am crew found themselves in poor and rapidly deteriorating visibility almost as soon as they entered the runway. According to the ALPA report, as the Pan Am aircraft taxied to the runway, the visibility was about 500m (1,600ft). Shortly after they turned onto the runway it decreased to less than 100m (330ft).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile, the KLM was still in good visibility, but with clouds blowing down the runway towards them. "The KLM aircraft completed its 180 degree turn in relatively clear weather and lined up on Runway 30. The next cloud was some 900m (3,000ft) down the runway and moving towards the aircraft at about 12 knots (6 meters per second)".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Immediately after lining up, the KLM pilot advanced the throttles and the aircraft started to move forward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The co-pilot advised the captain that ATC clearance had not yet been given, and Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten responded, "I know that. Go ahead, ask." Meurs then radioed the tower that they were "ready for takeoff" and "waiting for our ATC clearance". The KLM crew then received instructions which specified the route that the aircraft was to follow after takeoff. The instructions used the word "takeoff," but did not include an explicit statement that they were cleared for takeoff.
     </p>
     <p>
      Meurs read the flight clearance back to the controller, completing the readback with the statement: "We are now at takeoff."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten interrupted the co-pilot's read-back with the comment, "We're going."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The controller, who could not see the runway due to the fog, initially responded with "OK" (terminology which is nonstandard), which reinforced the KLM captain's misinterpretation that they had takeoff clearance. The controller's response of "OK" to the co-pilot's nonstandard statement that they were "now at takeoff" was likely due to his misinterpretation that they were in takeoff position and ready to begin the roll when takeoff clearance was received, but not in the process of taking off. The controller then immediately added "stand by for takeoff, I will call you,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      indicating that he had not intended the clearance to be interpreted as a takeoff clearance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A simultaneous radio call from the Pan Am crew caused mutual
      <a href="/wiki/Interference_(communication)" title="Interference (communication)">
       interference
      </a>
      on the radio frequency, which was audible in the KLM cockpit as a three-second-long whistling sound (or heterodyne). This caused the KLM crew to miss the crucial latter portion of the tower's response. The Pan Am crew's transmission was "We're still taxiing down the runway, the Clipper 1736!". This message was also blocked by the interference and inaudible to the KLM crew. Either message, if heard in the KLM cockpit, would have alerted the crew to the situation and given them time to abort the takeoff attempt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the fog, neither crew was able to see the other plane on the runway ahead of them. In addition, neither of the aircraft could be seen from the control tower, and the airport was not equipped with
      <a href="/wiki/Surface_movement_radar" title="Surface movement radar">
       ground radar
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      After the KLM plane had started its takeoff roll, the tower instructed the Pan Am crew to "report when runway clear." The Pan Am crew replied: "OK, we'll report when we're clear." On hearing this, the KLM flight engineer expressed his concern about the Pan Am not being clear of the runway by asking the pilots in his own cockpit, "Is he not clear, that Pan American?" Veldhuyzen van Zanten emphatically replied "Oh, yes" and continued with the takeoff.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Collision">
       Collision
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collision">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:382px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="182" data-file-width="733" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg/380px-Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg/570px-Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg/760px-Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg.png 2x" width="380"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_Tenerife_Disaster_EN.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Simplified map of runway, taxiways, and aircraft. The red star indicates the location of impact. Not to scale.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      According to the CVR, the Pan Am pilot said, "There he is!" when he spotted the KLM's landing lights through the fog just as his plane approached exit C-4. When it became clear that the KLM was approaching at takeoff speed, Grubbs exclaimed, "Goddamn, that son-of-a-bitch is coming straight at us!" while the co-pilot Robert Bragg yelled, "Get off! Get off! Get off!". The Pan Am crew applied full power to the throttles and took a sharp left turn towards the grass in an attempt to avoid a collision.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASN_4-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASN-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the time the KLM pilots saw the Pan Am, they were already traveling too fast to stop. In desperation the pilots prematurely
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotation_(aviation)" title="Rotation (aviation)">
       rotated
      </a>
      the aircraft and attempted to clear the Pan Am by climbing away, causing a severe
      <a href="/wiki/Tailstrike" title="Tailstrike">
       tailstrike
      </a>
      for 22m (72ft).
     </p>
     <p>
      The KLM was within 100m (330ft) of the Pan Am when it left the ground. Its nose gear cleared the Pan Am, but the engines, lower
      <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">
       fuselage
      </a>
      and main landing gear struck the upper right side of the Pan Am's fuselage at approximately 140 knots (260km/h; 160mph),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ripping apart the center of the Pan Am jet almost directly above the wing. The right side engines crashed through the Pan Am's upper deck immediately behind the cockpit.
     </p>
     <p>
      The KLM plane remained briefly airborne following the collision, but the impact with the Pan Am had sheared off the outer left engine, caused significant amounts of shredded materials to be ingested by the inner left engine, and damaged the wings. The KLM aircraft immediately went into a stall, rolled sharply, and hit the ground at a point approximately 150m (500ft) past the collision, sliding a further 300m (1,000ft) down the runway. The full load of fuel, which had caused the earlier delay, ignited immediately in a large fireball.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the 61 survivors of the Pan Am flight, John Coombs of Haleiwa, Hawaii, said that sitting in the nose of the plane probably saved his life: "We all settled back, and the next thing an explosion took place and the whole port side, left side of the plane, was just torn wide open."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Both airplanes were destroyed. All 234 passengers and 14 crew members in the KLM plane died, as did 326 passengers and 9 crew members aboard the Pan Am,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      primarily due to the fire and explosions resulting from the fuel spilled and ignited in the impact. The other 56 passengers and 5 crew members aboard the Pan Am aircraft survived, including the captain, first officer and flight engineer. Most of the survivors on the Pan Am walked out onto the left wing, the side away from the collision, through holes in the fuselage structure. The Pan Am's engines were still running for a few minutes after the accident despite First Officer Bragg's intention to turn them off. The top part of the cockpit, where the engine switches were located, had been destroyed in the collision, and all control lines were severed, leaving no method for the flight crew to control the aircraft's systems. Survivors waited for rescue, but it did not come promptly, as the firefighters were initially unaware that there were two aircraft involved and were concentrating on the KLM wreck some distance away in the thick fog. Eventually, most of the survivors on the wings dropped to the ground below.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten was KLM's chief of flight training. His photograph was used for publicity materials such as magazine advertisements, including the inflight magazine on board PH-BUF.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As such, KLM suggested that he should be part of the investigation team, before learning that he was the captain involved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He had given the co-pilot on the flight his Boeing 747 qualification check about two months before the accident.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOVA_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NOVA-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Investigation">
       Investigation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Investigation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      About 70 crash investigators from Spain, the Netherlands, the United States, and the two airline companies were involved in the investigation. Facts showed that there had been misinterpretations and false assumptions. Analysis of the CVR transcript showed that the KLM pilot was convinced that he had been cleared for takeoff, while the Tenerife control tower was certain that the KLM 747 was stationary at the end of the runway and awaiting takeoff clearance. It appears KLM's co-pilot was not as certain about take-off clearance as the captain.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Probable_cause">
       Probable cause
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Probable cause">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The investigation concluded that the fundamental cause of the accident was that Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten took off without takeoff clearance. The investigators suggested the reason for his mistake might have been a desire to leave as soon as possible in order to comply with KLM's duty-time regulations, and before the weather deteriorated further.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other major factors contributing to the accident were:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The sudden fog greatly limited visibility. The control tower and the crews of both planes were unable to see one another.
      </li>
      <li>
       Interference from simultaneous radio transmissions, with the result that it was difficult to hear the message.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The following factors were considered contributing but not critical:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Use of ambiguous non-standard phrases by the KLM co-pilot ("We're at take off") and the Tenerife control tower ("OK").
      </li>
      <li>
       The Pan Am aircraft had not exited the runway at C-3.
      </li>
      <li>
       The airport was (due to rerouting from the bomb threat) forced to accommodate a great number of large aircraft, resulting in disruption of the normal use of taxiways.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_report_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-official_report-38">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         38
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_response">
       Dutch response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dutch response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Dutch authorities were reluctant to accept the Spanish report blaming the KLM captain for the accident. The Netherlands Department of Civil Aviation published a response that, whilst accepting that the KLM aircraft had taken off "prematurely", argued that he alone should not be blamed for the "mutual misunderstanding" that occurred between the controller and the KLM crew, and that limitations of using radio as a means of communication should have been given greater consideration.
     </p>
     <p>
      In particular, the Dutch response pointed out that
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The crowded airport had placed additional pressure on all parties, KLM, Pan Am, and the controller;
      </li>
      <li>
       Sounds on the CVR suggested that during the accident the Spanish control tower crew had been listening to a
       <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">
        football
       </a>
       match on the radio and may have been distracted.
      </li>
      <li>
       The transmission from the tower in which the controller passed KLM their ATC clearance was ambiguous and could have been interpreted as also giving take-off clearance. In support of this part of their response, the Dutch investigators pointed out that Pan Am's messages "No! Eh?" and "We are still taxiing down the runway, the Clipper 1736!" indicated that Captain Grubbs and First Officer Bragg had recognised the ambiguity (this message was not audible to the control tower or KLM crew due to simultaneous cross-communication);
      </li>
      <li>
       The Pan Am had taxied beyond the third exit. Had the plane turned at the third exit as instructed, the collision would not have occurred.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutch_response_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-dutch_response-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_Box_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Black_Box-39">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         39
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Although the Dutch authorities were initially reluctant to blame Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten and his crew,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutch_response_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-dutch_response-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_Box_39-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Black_Box-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the airline ultimately accepted responsibility for the accident. KLM paid the victims or their families compensation ranging between $58,000 and $600,000.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KLM_responsibility_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-KLM_responsibility-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The sum of settlements for property and damages was $110 million
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (an average of $189,000 per victim,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      due to limitations imposed by European Compensation Conventions in effect at the time).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Speculations">
       Speculations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Speculations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This was one of the first accident investigations during which the contribution of "human factors" was studied.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpa22a_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-alpa22a-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The human factors included:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The flight was one of Captain Veldhuyzen van Zanten's first after spending six months training new pilots on a
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_simulator" title="Flight simulator">
        flight simulator
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="The source doesn't say this, only that he had been instructing for ten years (July 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       where he had been in charge of everything (including simulated ATC), hence having been away from the real world of flying for an extended period.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The flight engineer's and the first officer's apparent hesitation to challenge Veldhuyzen van Zanten further. The official investigation suggested that this might have been because the captain was not only senior in rank, but also one of the most respected pilots working for the airline.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Disaster_Volume_1_11-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Air_Disaster_Volume_1-11">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         11
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         42
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       This view is questioned by Jan Bartelski, a former KLM Captain and the president of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Federation_of_Air_Line_Pilots%27_Associations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Federation of Air Line Pilots' Associations (page does not exist)">
        International Federation of Air Line Pilots' Associations
       </a>
       (IFALPA), who knew both men and believes that this explanation is inconsistent with his experience of their personalities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-book-43">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         43
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The reason only the flight engineer on the KLM plane reacted to the radio transmission "OK, we'll report when we're clear" might lie in the fact that by then he had completed his pre-flight checks, whereas his colleagues were experiencing an increased workload, just at the same time as the visibility worsened.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpa22_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-alpa22-44">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         44
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The ALPA study group concluded that the KLM crew did not realise that the transmission "Papa Alpha one seven three six, report when runway clear" was directed at the Pan Am because this was the first and only time the Pan Am was referred to by that name. Before that, the Pan Am was called "Clipper one seven three six", with the proper callsign.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpa22b_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-alpa22b-45">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         45
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The extra fuel the KLM plane took on added several factors:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       it delayed takeoff an extra 35 minutes, which gave time for the fog to settle in;
      </li>
      <li>
       it added over forty tons of weight
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         46
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       to the plane, which increased the takeoff distance and made it more difficult to clear the Pan Am when taking off;
      </li>
      <li>
       it increased the size of the fire from the crash that ultimately killed everyone on board.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         47
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         48
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         49
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_response">
       Safety response
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tenerife_airport_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Safety response">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As a consequence of the accident, sweeping changes were made to international airline regulations and to aircraft. Aviation authorities around the world introduced requirements for
      <a href="/wiki/Voice_procedure" title="Voice procedure">
       standard phrases
      </a>
      and a greater emphasis on English as a common
      <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
       working language
      </a>
      . For example,
      <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">
       ICAO
      </a>
      calls for the phrase "line up and wait" as an instruction to an aircraft moving into position but not cleared for takeoff.
     </p>
     <p>
      Several national air safety boards began penalizing pilots for disobeying air traffic controllers' orders. Air traffic instruction should not be acknowledged solely with a colloquial phrase such as "OK" or even "
      <a href="/wiki/Voice_procedure" title="Voice procedure">
       Roger
      </a>
      " (which simply means the last transmission was received
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), but with a readback of the key parts of the instruction, to show mutual understanding. Additionally, the phrase "takeoff" is spoken only when the actual takeoff clearance is given or when cancelling that same clearance (i.e., "CLEARED FOR TAKE-OFF" or "CANCEL TAKE-OFF CLEARANCE"). Up until that point, aircrew and controllers should use the phrase "departure" in its place (e.g., "ready for departure").
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockpit_(aviation)" title="Cockpit (aviation)">
       Cockpit
      </a>
      procedures were also changed. Hierarchical relations among crew members were played down. More emphasis was placed on team decision-making by mutual agreement, part of what has become known in the industry as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crew_Resource_Management" title="Crew Resource Management">
       Crew Resource Management
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1978 a second airport was inaugurated on the island: the new
      <a href="/wiki/Tenerife_South_Airport" title="Tenerife South Airport">
       Tenerife South Airport
      </a>
      (TFS). This airport now serves the majority of international tourist flights. Los Rodeos, renamed to Tenerife North Airport (TFN), was then used only for domestic and inter-island flights. In 2002 a new terminal was opened and it carries international traffic once again, including budget airlines.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Spanish authorities installed a ground radar at Tenerife North following the accident.
     </p>

     </p>
     <p>
      The 30th anniversary marked the first time that Dutch and American next of kin, and aid helpers from Tenerife, joined in international commemoration service held at the Auditorio de Tenerife in Santa Cruz; the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Tenerife_Memorial_March_27,_1977" title="International Tenerife Memorial March 27, 1977">

          <q>
           As far as the allegations that co-pilots would not have the nerve to stand up to van Zanten during flight because of his senior position in the company, and that their career could be ruined by his adverse report, this was another example of completely false presumptions. Van Zanten was a serious and introverted individual but with an open-hearted and friendly disposition. He was a studious type and was regarded as the companys pilot expert on the Boeing 747 systems. Nevertheless, he would have been the last person on the flight desk not to accept his co-pilots advice or warning. He believed in partnership, to the extent that he insisted on his first officers addressing him during flight as Jaap and not Captain van Zanten. He had learnt much about cockpit management by representing KLM at an
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IATA" title="IATA">
            IATA
           </a>
           Conference in
           <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">
            Istanbul
           </a>
           and was trying to put this into practice ... Meurs was not the type to have been easily intimidated by a superior rank and would not have easily given in under stress. Although new to the 747 (he had only ninety hours on that type), he was formerly a temporary DC-8 captain. For personal reasons, he waived his seniority right for a DC-9 command and opted to fly the 747 as a first officer. Meurs was an extrovert and liked to enjoy life, a contrasting disposition to van Zanten. Both complemented each other in personalities as well as in their operational background. What van Zanten lacked in route experience, particularly in the Canary Islands area, Meurs compensated for by his ultimate knowledge of the local situation from his many past flights through Las Palmas. The reverse applied to the handling of the 747. As for his profiency as a co-pilot, I found Meurs cooperative, alert, and far from a meek and mild type. In fact, the opposite was more likely. He could be somewhat abrupt and direct in his manner, as was evident from the way he stopped van Zanten from opening the throttles
