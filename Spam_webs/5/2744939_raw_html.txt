       <div class="entry">
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <em>
          By Chris Ebert
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         In last weekends
         <span style="color: #0000ff">
          <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/?p=25590" style="color: #0000ff">
           analysis
          </a>
         </span>
         it was noted that:
        </p>
        <p style="padding-left: 30px;text-align: justify">
         <span style="color: #ff0000">
          The command from the top from now until at least October is as follows for the Bulls:
         </span>
        </p>
        <ul style="text-align: justify">
         <li>
          <span style="color: #ff0000">
           <strong>
            Do not allow the S&amp;P 500 to fall below 2100
           </strong>
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="color: #ff0000">
           <strong>
            If it does fall into the 2050-2100 range, bring it back above 2100 as quickly as possible
           </strong>
          </span>
         </li>
         <li>
          <span style="color: #ff0000">
           <strong>
            If it falls below 2050 get ready for war  a war we might not win
           </strong>
          </span>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p style="padding-left: 30px;text-align: justify">
         <span style="color: #ff0000">
          Thats a direct order!
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/doldrums.jpg">
          <img alt="doldrums" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-25614" height="150" src="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/doldrums.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         Today  a look at why that all-important 2100 level is likely to make the summer doldrums different this year. Typically summer is a quiet time for the stock market, not unlike the calm winds that often occupy parts of the ocean near the equator at this time of year.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Stock prices can move at any time of year, but in summertime the moves tend to be more subtle. Perhaps the obvious reason for this phenomenon is that many people take vacations in North America and Europe during the summer months, so there are fewer people trading in some of the biggest markets in the world.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         There are times in the summer months when it seems as though few if any large institutions are trading; and trading volume gives the appearance that some days are for retail traders only. While the absence of large institutions can contribute to stagnation in stock prices (retail traders generally lack the capital necessary to move prices) it can also lead to volatility and wild swings in prices under the right conditions.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         If one thinks of the large institutions as the captain of the ship, the smaller retail traders are like the thunderstorm that occasionally interrupts the summer doldrums. Without the institutions steering the ship, even a small storm can blow the ship off course in an otherwise tranquil environment. Even an insignificant bit of economic news can spook retail traders into selling stocks, which drives prices lower. Institutional traders then interrupt their holiday to snap up stocks at bargain prices and that pushes prices back to where they were originally. The ship returns to its intended course.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         As it turns out, 2015 may be more prone to these violent retail-induced storms than recent years. The reason the summer of 2015 is likely to be more volatile than usual is that the S&amp;P 500 is in an unusual place right now. It is currently skirting a line known here as the Orange Line of Violence; and thats something it has not done in the summer since back in the year 2012.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_25615" style="width: 589px">
         <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/OMS-07-04-15.jpg">
          <img alt="OMS 07-04-15" class="wp-image-25615" height="420" src="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/07/OMS-07-04-15.jpg" width="579"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          * see option descriptions below
         </p>
        </div>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         The Orange Line of Violence is a narrow zone in the S&amp;P 500 which represents extreme stagnation. Only when stock prices do not make any sustained progress  up or down  for several months does the S&amp;P approach
         <a class="more-link" href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/?p=25613#more-25613">
          (more)
         </a>
        </p>

        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <em>
          By Chris Ebert
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         The S&amp;P 500 has hovered around the 2100 level quite a bit recently. That didnt happen by chance; theres a very good reason that level is so important right now. It represents a battle zone; and perhaps there is no better way to view that battle zone than by looking at some simple stock options.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Covered Call options generally only return losses in a Bear market. Of course, there are always exceptions to any rule. But, for the most part, a Covered Call* (*see specific description below) will always return a profit during a Bull market, and only return a loss during a Bear market.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <strong>
          S&amp;P Cant Loiter on Bull-Bear Border
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/no-loitering-allowed.jpg">
          <img alt="no loitering allowed" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-25598" height="210" src="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/no-loitering-allowed.jpg" width="151"/>
         </a>
         Heres something to think about: If the S&amp;P were to hover on the borderline between a Bull market and a Bear market, how long could it remain there? The answer is, not long. Not long at all.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         The reason the S&amp;P 500 cannot straddle the dividing line between a Bull and Bear market is that the moment stock prices would move in one way or the other, one side or the other would declare victory and the battle would be over. If stock prices rose, even slightly, the Bulls would declare victory and the victory party would send stock prices even higher, reinforcing that victory.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         On the other hand, when the S&amp;P 500 is straddling the line, the slightest dip in stock prices will be a victory for the Bears, and not only will the Bears send prices lower, the fear generated in the minds of the Bulls by such a victory will push many of them to sell their stock positions, in turn adding fuel to the sell-off.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Since the S&amp;P 500 cannot straddle the Bull/Bear dividing line for any extended period of time, and since Covered Call profitability is one way of easily identifying the dividing line, then it stands to reason that the point of Covered Call profitability is a point that is unsustainable for the S&amp;P 500.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Covered Calls are either profitable (and it is a Bull market) or they are not (and it is a Bear market). The S&amp;P 500 rarely sticks around very long at the dividing line between Covered Call profit and loss. Thus, the S&amp;P 500 (the white line in the chart) will rarely hug the red line on the chart. Generally, the S&amp;P either bounces off the red line, or else it passes right thorough without stopping. But it almost never sticks around for a hug.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         The dividing line between a Bull market and Bear market is usually not a place of protracted battles. Rather, long drawn out battles tend to take place inside ones own territory. Thats the case at the moment, as the Bulls are engaged in a battle near the 2100 level for the S&amp;P, well inside Bull-market territory.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <strong>
          Nobody Likes a Correction
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Next, consider that for any Bear market, there is a zone just above it that is
         <em>
          not quite bearish
         </em>
         , but considered more of a
         <em>
          correction
         </em>
         . If Covered Calls are used to define a Bull and Bear market, then Covered Calls (which only return losses in a Bear market) must always be profitable in a Bull-market correction, by definition. Only when the correction becomes so severe that it morphs into a Bear market do Covered Calls suffer losses.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/OMS-06-27-15.jpg">

         </a>

        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <em>
          by Chris Ebert
         </em>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Not everyone has the time or the energy to follow the stock market. This can be a crucial flaw in an investment plan such as
         <strong>
          RRSP, 401k, 403b, IRA
         </strong>
         or similar types of accounts. Such accounts are designed to allow folks to contribute funds to the stock market when many of these same folks are working full-time jobs that preclude them from having the necessary time and energy to study the stock market. Thus, these investors are prone to wiping out their hard-earned investments when the stock market gets stormy.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/market-crash.jpg">
          <img alt="market crash" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-25570" height="150" src="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/market-crash.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         Most employer sponsored investment plans offer several ways to allocate funds. Generally there are three main types of offerings, low-risk
         <strong>
          Cash and Money-market
         </strong>
         funds, moderate-risk
         <strong>
          Bond
         </strong>
         funds, and high-risk
         <strong>
          Stock
         </strong>
         funds. Since stock funds tend to offer a chance to grow investments quickly (hence names including
         <strong>
          Aggressive Growth Funds
         </strong>
         or
         <strong>
          Growth-Income Funds
         </strong>
         ) they can be alluring to folks trying to save for retirement. But these funds, by their very nature, come with a risk. They can and do decline in value at times, and declines can be massive when the stock market is experiencing turmoil.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         It is difficult for many investors to avoid the downfalls of the stock market, due to the inherent difficulty in timing the market. If an investor knew when to get out of the stock market, he could avoid the downturns that draw down investment balances. Moreover, an investor who does get out of the stock market at the right time may have no idea when to get back in, thus missing opportunities for explosive gains. As a result, many simply take the good with the bad, the gains with the losses, in hopes that things work out over the long haul.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         One indicator that may help investors time the market to improve gains is known as the S&amp;P 500 Temperature. This temperature gauge was developed right here at
         <span style="color: #0000ff">
          <a href="http://www.zentrader.ca/blog/" style="color: #0000ff">
           zentrader
          </a>
         </span>
         and is updated here and on
         <span style="color: #0000ff">
          <a href="https://twitter.com/OptionScientist" style="color: #0000ff">
           twitter
          </a>
         </span>
         as conditions change.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         <strong>
          Heres How it Works
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         Whenever the S&amp;P 500 Temperature is above zero, it is generally a good time to own stocks, including stock Mutual Funds that are common in many investment plans. When the Temperature is below zero it is often not a good time to own stocks, but to allocate funds to cash or money-market funds. A cash fund tends to grow slowly or not at all, so it is not attractive when stocks are experiencing gains. But when stock prices are falling, moving balances into a cash fund can protect those gains.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: justify">
         As a general rule:
        </p>
        <ul style="text-align: justify">
         <li>
          <h3>
           Buy stocks and move investments into stock mutual funds whenever the S&amp;P 500 Temperature goes above zero
          </h3>
         </li>
         <li>
          <h3>
           Sell stocks and move investments into cash or money market funds when the S&amp;P 500 Temperature falls below zero
          </h3>
         </li>
        </ul>
