       <header>
        <h1 class="title">
         Can we have a coherent strategy for new homes?
        </h1>
       </header>
       <div class="content clearfix">
        <p>
         <img alt="Can we have a coherent strategy for new homes" src="/images/insight_articles/2015/Can-we-have-a-coherent-strategy-for-new-homes.jpg"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         As one house builder put it to me: "Our job isnt complicated. We buy land, acquire planning permission, build homes and then sell them on." However, if you were to ask politicians  and probably the wider public too  they would tell you that if any aspect of life in Britain is in crisis, then it is housing: each year we build about half the number of homes we need to house our population. On current trends, the nation will be short of almost 2 million homes by 2020.
        </p>
        <p>
         Good job then, that in the run up to the General Election, the main parties have published comprehensive strategies for tackling the housing crisis  indeed the Lyons Review, commissioned by Labour, is considered to be the most comprehensive analysis of the housing sector in a decade. Targets are back in vogue, with Labour committed to delivering 200,000 new homes a year by 2020 and, in this game of mine is bigger than yours, the Lib Dems plumping for 300,000. The Conservatives have wisely avoided an overall target but even David Cameron could not resist doubling his starter homes target from 100,000 to 200,000 homes, to be delivered over the course of the next Parliament.
        </p>
        <p>
         Are these targets achievable? Two facts worth bearing in mind:
        </p>
        <ol style="list-style-type: lower-roman;">
         <li>
          <p>
           it is widely assumed that 75% of new house building will need to be delivered by the private sector, so that is somewhere between 150,000 and 225,000 new homes each year by 2020, with the remaining 50,000-75,000 units to be provided by housing associations and local authorities;
          </p>
         </li>
         <li>
          <p>
           while the large housebuilding programmes of the late 50s and early 60s are usually cited as examples of what is achievable, they were undertaken at a time when the country was also embarked on the biggest ever slum clearance programme, demolishing as many as 100,000 homes a year.
          </p>
         </li>
        </ol>
        <p>
         But lets assume these targets are locked in. What is the strategy for achieving them? How do we address the fact that there are now 3.3 million young people aged between 20-34 (a fifth of women and a third of men in that age group) living with their parents? The Halifax reported this month that young people may be giving up on owning their own home as the proportion of people saving for a deposit dropped six percentage points: 43% are currently saving to buy a property compared to 57% who are not.
        </p>
        <p>
         Using the house builders earlier assessment, its about availability of land, success in planning, ability to build and then achieving sales. For the last of these four steps, we can see definite progress:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <p>
           Help to Buy has been a major stimulus to the house building industry, with almost 45,000 homes bought with its support in the first 23 months of operation. A commitment by both the Conservatives and Labour to retain Help to Buy until 2020 (albeit possibly with a purchase price down from 600,000 under Labour) is welcome news.
          </p>
         </li>
         <li>
          <p>
           The Conservatives Help to Buy ISA, announced in this years Budget, is another big financial boost, but this time for first time buyers (under 40) only. The financial benefits are substantial and are likely to prove very attractive.
          </p>
         </li>
         <li>
          <p>
           Acknowledging that these financial incentives are likely to see house prices rise further, Labour would require the banks to invest the cash receipts from this scheme into a future homes investment fund  it estimates this could amount to as much as 5 billion over the period 2015-2020 and could fund 150,000 new homes in Labours proposed housing growth areas.
          </p>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         The supply side, on the other hand, continues to bedevil politicians and industry alike: some people, it seems, just do not want to see homes built and politicians, by their actions, only aid and abet. A recent survey by Shelter, the housing charity, found that while most people are either positive or neutral about homes being built locally  and indeed supporters of local house building outnumber opponents by a ratio of 5:3 (48%:29%)  the level of active opposition runs at more than double the rate of active support. What this means, according to Shelter, is that people who are opposed to local housebuilding are three times more likely to actively oppose than natural supporters are to actively support an application. What motivates them? In the main, its pressure on local infrastructure and the loss of green space.
        </p>
        <p>
         Love it or hate it, the Local Plan system was supposed to deal with this  to encourage a local debate and then to settle on allocated sites (usually the minimum that they can get away with), providing developers with some certainty. The coalition government encouraged this, but it also established and encouraged Neighbourhood Plans (NP) to further draw communities into decision-making. Bearing in mind the greater likelihood of opponents of new homes to be more active locally, the outcome could have been predicted.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, Eric Pickles, Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government, has gone further than this, giving added weight to these Neighbourhood Plans (whether made or emerging). He has directly intervened, calling in and refusing planning applications because a NP has not allocated the site in question, often in cases where there is no Local Plan in place and no five-year housing land supply  a situation where the presumption in favour of sustainable development would normally kick in.
        </p>
        <p>
         Local active opponents now have a statutory mechanism to frustrate development. We know their main source of opposition is lack of infrastructure to absorb the impact of new homes  whether that be schools, roads or doctors surgeries. And yet the Conservatives Starter Homes initiative  up to 200,000 homes for the under-40s to be offered at a 20% discount to market price and to be built on unallocated brownfield land  proposes to waive any Section 106 or Community Infrastructure Levy contributions, including affordable housing. So local communities are going to be asked to accept development on a site unallocated in a Local Plan and to get no contribution to infrastructure improvements whatsoever, when we know this is by far their biggest concern. Plus, the combination of attractive homes at a discount, but with no affordable homes alongside, will only exacerbate house price values.
        </p>
        <p>
         On land itself, the parties are hardly making it any easier. The Green Belt has now become a totemic issue for the Conservatives: ever stricter pronouncements from Ministers can have left no one in any doubt that this a red line issue. Indeed, safeguarding of the Green Belt is a key feature of the Conservatives 2015 manifesto. For Labour, less so: its heartlands do not fall into Green Belt territory and the early years of a Labour government could provide some respite on this account (there is no mention of the Green Belt in its manifesto). But a brownfield first policy is a definite prospect which, from experience, only ever leads to fewer homes being built overall.
        </p>
        <p>
         What all parties can agree on is the need to release more public sector land for new homes and wider regeneration. And increasingly aware of communities desire to get the necessary infrastructure in place at the outset, local authorities are being encouraged to assemble land (with private sector partners), sweat their assets and take their share of the gain. If it brings more land to market, that is surely a welcome move.
        </p>
        <p>
         Housebuilding may not be a complicated business but the industry definitely has to jump through hoops.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
