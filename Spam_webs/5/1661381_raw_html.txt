         <h1 class="entry-title">
          How to Fire Your Design Clients the Right Way
         </h1>
         <div class="post-info">
          <span class="date published time" title="2015-03-31T08:00:46+00:00">
           March 31, 2015
          </span>
          By
          <span class="author vcard">
           <span class="fn">
            <a class="fn n" href="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/author/vgendelman" rel="author" title="Vladimir Gendelman">
             Vladimir Gendelman
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
          <span class="post-comments">
           <a href="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/firing-clients#comments">
            1 Comment
           </a>
          </span>
         </div>
         <script src="//s7.addthis.com/js/300/addthis_widget.js#pubid=ra-540dfc571d318e1d" type="text/javascript">
         </script>
         <div class="addthis_jumbo_share">
         </div>
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           <img alt="How to Fire Your Bad Clients The Right Way (with Examples)" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6533" height="314" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/firing-clients.gif" width="600"/>
          </p>
          <p>
           Letting go of a graphic design client is a lot like breaking up with a boyfriend or girlfriend; its not exactly pleasant, and it might even be someone you actually like, but sometimes it just has to be done. Breaking off a professional relationship nicely is a delicate operation, and chances are you dont have an HR department to rely on, so this responsibility falls directly on your shoulders.
          </p>
          <p>
           If its time to say goodbye to your client, you can reduce stress and discomfort by taking steps to fire them the
           <em>
            right
           </em>
           way.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Know when to fire
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Know when to fire" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6226" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/know-when-to-fire.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           The first step towards firing a design client is making the decision to go through with it, and that means having a good reason to do so. There are several ways to
           <a href="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/clientele-from-hell-when-to-fire-your-graphic-design-clients" title="Clientele from Hell: When to Fire Your Graphic Design Clients">
            know when to fire a client
           </a>
           . Sometimes its obvious, like if the client is abusive, you havent been paid, or another opportunity comes along that you want to pursue.
          </p>
          <p>
           Other times, it may be a matter of a client not listening to your ideas, paying you less than what youre worth, or eating up your time by being slow to communicate or finalize graphic designs. No one else can tell you when its time to fire a client, so remember to trust your own judgment.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Look at your contract first
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Look at your contract first" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6228" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/look-at-contract.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           Before you approach the client, protect your own interests by going over any
           <a href="http://99u.com/articles/21927/dont-get-screwed-the-contract-provisions-every-creative-needs-to-know" target="_blank" title="Dont Get Screwed: The Contract Provisions Every Creative Needs to Know">
            contract provisions
           </a>
           or agreements youve established in writing. This is to make sure your client doesnt try to pull a fast one on you and claim youre in breach of contract for leaving. Likewise, make sure that you wont actually
           <em>
            become
           </em>
           in breach of contract by failing to deliver what you agreed to.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sometimes, your working relationship can be so bad that you just have to bite the bullet and break off your contract, taking whatever penalties come with it. That decision is up to you, but its always a good idea to know what youre losing out on before you come to your final decision.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Do it in person (if you can)
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Do it in person (if you can)" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6229" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/do-in-person.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           The best way to fire a client is in person, preferably in a face-to-face meeting.  Find a time that works for your client and a venue that you feel comfortable with. If you think emotions might run high, it may be best to meet in a neutral zone (such as a public area) to ensure both parties keep their cool.
          </p>
          <p>
           If you absolutely have to, you can fire your client through an e-mail, letter or phone call if you just dont feel comfortable doing it in person. Its not the best way to handle things, but desperate times call for desperate measures. Of course, if your relationship with your client has been strictly through phone or e-mail, then you should use whatever method your client will prefer.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Be honest, but play to the room
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Be honest, but play to the room" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6230" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/be-honest.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           Your client will probably want to know why youre leaving them. Honesty is typically the best policy, especially if the client was rude, abusive or toxic. The truth could be just what your difficult client needs to set them straight, but the best course of action is to play to the room. Chances are if your client was hard to handle, they might not appreciate your honest opinion about them.
          </p>
          <p>
           If youre looking for a drama-free exit, it might be best to keep things brief and sugarcoat as much as you can. No client is worth outright lying for, but some truths may be worth keeping to yourself for the sake of civility. You know your client best, so pick the strategy that works for the situation while still making your voice heard.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Listen to them
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Listen to them" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6231" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/listen-to-them.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           Some clients might beg you to stay, while others might use this as their chance to tell you off. No matter what theyre saying, you should give them the chance to make their voice heard, even if nothing they say could possibly change your mind.
          </p>
          <p>
           That doesnt mean you need to sit there and take more of their abuse. All it means is that you shouldnt try to monopolize the conversation. Showing a willingness to hear them out can make all the difference in smoothing things over.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Set a deadline
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Set a deadline" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6232" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/set-a-deadline.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           Just because youre a freelance designer doesnt mean you shouldnt give your client notice when youre about to leave.  If you can, give them some time to prepare for your departure. The standard two-week notice is usually fine, or you might agree to leave upon the completion of your current project.
          </p>
          <p>
           Of course, there are those occasions when you just have to get out as soon as possible. Maybe you have new obligations that take precedence, or
           <a href="http://www.entrepreneur.com/article/242842" target="_blank" title="It's Not Me, It's You: When to End a Toxic Client Relationship">
            the client is so toxic
           </a>
           that you fear giving them notice would only exacerbate their abuse.  In the end, you have to do what works best for you.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Keep the door open
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Keep the door open" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6233" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/keep-the-door-open.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           If circumstances are forcing you to leave a client that you like working with, always make sure to keep the door open just in case they have future opportunities for you. To ease the transition gracefully, you might even recommend other designers to take your place (unless, of course, youre dealing with a toxic client to whom you wouldnt want to recommend
           <em>
            anyone
           </em>
           ).
          </p>
          <p>
           It doesnt hurt to check in on your former clients every now and then just to see how things are going. Keeping that line of communication open could lead to further work opportunities or possibly even recommendations to new potential clients.
          </p>
          <h2>
           Let it go
          </h2>
          <p>
           <img alt="Let it go" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6234" height="400" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/let-it-go.jpg" width="600"/>
           <br/>
           Leaving a good client behind can feel heartbreaking and leaving a bad client can be a stressful and dramatic experience. But when its done, its done. Find a way to let it go and walk away. It doesnt matter if firing your client went swimmingly or if it opened up a Pandoras Box of drama; once youve left them behind, you need to find a way to move on.
          </p>
          <p>
           Use the time and energy you once devoted to your former client to look for new opportunities, push your career further or
           <a href="http://www.hongkiat.com/blog/work-better-with-freelance-clients/" target="_blank" title="Freelancers: How to Work Better with Your Clients">
            better serve your other clients
           </a>
           . Take a deep breathyou made it through and your future is full of possibilities.
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="How to Fire Your Clients" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-6192" height="1250" src="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/media/2015/03/how-to-fire-your-clients.jpg" width="600"/>
          </p>

            <a href="http://www.companyfolders.com/blog/firing-clients">
             How to Fire Your Clients
            </a>

          </p>
          <p>
           Now we want to hear from you. If youve ever had to fire a client, nows your chance to share your experience with other designers. How did the client react? Would you do anything differently if you had to do it again? Leave your answers, anecdotes and examples in the comments below.
          </p>
