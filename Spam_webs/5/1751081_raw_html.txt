     <p>
      <b>
       Shelta
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'sh' in 'shy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// short 'e' in 'bed'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
           l
          </span>
          <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
           t
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
       language
      </a>
      spoken by
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_Travellers" title="Irish Travellers">
       Irish Travellers
      </a>
      , particularly in Ireland, but also parts of Great Britain.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is widely known as
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">
        the Cant
       </a>
      </b>
      , to its native speakers in Ireland as
      <b>
       Gammon
      </b>
      and to the linguistic community as Shelta.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was often used as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptolect" title="Cryptolect">
       cryptolect
      </a>
      to exclude outsiders from comprehending conversations between Travellers,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although this aspect is frequently over-emphasised.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The exact number of native speakers is hard to determine due to sociolinguistic issues
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      but
      <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
       Ethnologue
      </a>
      puts the number of speakers at 6,000 in Ireland, 30,000 in the UK (including Northern Ireland), and 50,000 in the US. The figure for at least the UK is dated to 1990; it is not clear if the other figures are from the same source.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e12_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-e12-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Linguistically Shelta is today seen as a
      <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">
       mixed language
      </a>
      that stems from a community of travelling people in Ireland that was originally predominantly Irish-speaking. The community later went through a period of widespread bilingualism that resulted in a language based heavily on
      <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">
       Hiberno-English
      </a>
      with heavy influences from Irish.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      As different varieties of Shelta display different degrees of anglicisation (see below), it is hard to determine the extent of the Irish
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum">
       substratum
      </a>
      but the Oxford Companion to the English Language puts it at 2,0003,000 words.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Names_and_etymology">
       Names and etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names and etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The language is known by various names. People outside the Traveller community often refer to it as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">
        (the) Cant
       </a>
      </i>
      , the etymology of which is still a matter of debate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Speakers of the language also refer to it as
      <i>
       (the) Cant
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Gammon
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or
      <i>
       Tarri
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Amongst linguists, the name
      <i>
       Shelta
      </i>
      is the most commonly used term.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The word Shelta appears in print for the first time in 1882 in the book
      <i>
       The Gypsies
      </i>
      by the "gypsiologist"
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Leland" title="Charles Leland">
       Charles Leland
      </a>
      , who claimed to have discovered it as the "fifth Celtic tongue". The etymology of the word has long been a matter of debate but modern
      <a href="/wiki/Celtic_studies" title="Celtic studies">
       Celticists
      </a>
      are convinced that Irish
      <i>
       <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga">
        sil
       </span>
      </i>
      <small>
       Irish pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Irish" title="Help:IPA for Irish">
        [ul]
       </a>
      </span>
      "to walk" is at the root, either via a term such as
      <i>
       <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga">
        siltir
       </span>
      </i>
      <small>
       Irish pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Irish" title="Help:IPA for Irish">
        [ultor]
       </a>
      </span>
      "a walker" or a form of the
      <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
       gerund
      </a>
      <i>
       <span lang="ga" xml:lang="ga">
        siladh
       </span>
      </i>
      (cf.
      <i>
       an lucht silta
      </i>
      <small>
       Irish pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Irish" title="Help:IPA for Irish">
        [n luxt ult]
       </a>
      </span>
      , "the walking people" (lit.
      <i>
       the people of walks
      </i>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the traditional Irish term for Travellers).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       Dictionary of Hiberno-English
      </i>
      cites it as possibly a corruption of the word "Celt".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolan_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dolan-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_history">
       Origins and history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins and history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguist" title="Linguist">
       Linguists
      </a>
      have been documenting Shelta since at least the 1870s. The first works were published in 1880 and 1882 by Charles Leland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Celtic language expert
      <a href="/wiki/Kuno_Meyer" title="Kuno Meyer">
       Kuno Meyer
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">
       Romani
      </a>
      expert
      <a href="/wiki/John_Sampson_(linguist)" title="John Sampson (linguist)">
       John Sampson
      </a>
      both assert that Shelta existed as far back as the 13th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the earliest but undocumented period linguists surmise that the Traveller community was
      <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
       Irish
      </a>
      speaking until a period of widespread
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingualism" title="Bilingualism">
       bilingualism
      </a>
      in Irish and
      <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">
       Hiberno-English
      </a>
      (or Scots in Scotland) set in, leading to
      <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
       creolisation
      </a>
      (possibly with a trilingual stage).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The resulting language is referred to as
      <i>
       Old Shelta
      </i>
      and it is suspected that this stage of the language displayed distinctive features, such as non-English syntactic and morphological features, no longer found in Shelta.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Within the
      <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">
       diaspora
      </a>
      , various sub-branches of Shelta exist.
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=English_Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="English Shelta (page does not exist)">
       English Shelta
      </a>
      is increasingly suffering from
      <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">
       anglicisation
      </a>
      whereas
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Irish-Traveller%27s_Cant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Irish-Traveller's Cant (page does not exist)">
       American Irish-Traveller's Cant
      </a>
      , originally also synonymous with Shelta, has by now been almost fully anglicised.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_features">
       Linguistic features
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linguistic features">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociologist" title="Sociologist">
       Sociologist
      </a>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sharon_Gmelch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sharon Gmelch (page does not exist)">
       Sharon Gmelch
      </a>
      describes the Travellers' language as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Irish Travelers use a secret argot or cant known as Gammon. It is used primarily to conceal meaning from outsiders, especially during business transactions and in the presence of police. Most Gammon utterances are terse and spoken so quickly that a non-Traveler might conclude the words merely had been garbled. Most Gammon words were formed from
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       by applying four techniques: reversal,
       <a href="/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">
        metathesis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        affixing
       </a>
       , and substitution. In the first, an Irish word is reversed to form a Gammon one 
       <i>
        mac
       </i>
       , or son, in Irish became
       <i>
        kam
       </i>
       in Gammon. In the second, consonants or consonant clusters were transposed. Thirdly, a sound or cluster of sounds were either prefixed or suffixed to an Irish word. Some of the more frequently prefixed sounds were
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        gr
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       . For example,
       <i>
        Obair
       </i>
       , work or job, became
       <i>
        gruber
       </i>
       in Gammon. Lastly, many Gammon words were formed by substituting an arbitrary consonant or consonant cluster in an Irish word. In recent years, modern slang and
       <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">
        Romani
       </a>
       (the language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">
        gypsies
       </a>
       ) words have been incorporated. The
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       are English. The first
       <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       collected from Irish Travelers was published in 1808, indicating that Gammon dates at least back to the 1700s. But many early Celtic scholars who studied it, including the eminent
       <a href="/wiki/Kuno_Meyer" title="Kuno Meyer">
        Kuno Meyer
       </a>
       , concluded it was much older.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Thus, it is not
      <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
       mutually intelligible
      </a>
      with either English or Irish, out of design.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
       Lexicon
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Many Shelta words have been disguised using techniques such as
      <a href="/wiki/Back_slang" title="Back slang">
       back slang
      </a>
      where sounds are transposed (for example
      <i>
       gop
      </i>
      "kiss" from Irish
      <i>
       pg
      </i>
      ) or the addition of sounds (for example
      <i>
       gather
      </i>
      "father" from Irish
      <i>
       athair
      </i>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McArthur_4-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-McArthur-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other examples include
      <i>
       lackeen
      </i>
      "girl" from Irish
      <i>
       cailn
      </i>
      , and the word
      <i>
       rodas
      </i>
      "door" from Irish
      <i>
       doras
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      It also contains a certain number of introduced lexical items from Romani such as the term
      <i>
       gadje
      </i>
      "non-Traveller" or "kushti" (from the
      <a href="/wiki/Romanichal" title="Romanichal">
       Romanichal
      </a>
      word for "good"). Ten percent of the Gammon language comes from
      <a href="/wiki/Romani_language" title="Romani language">
       Romani
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Loanwords">
       Loanwords
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

       </span>

     <p>
      Some Shelta words have been borrowed by mainstream English speakers, such as the word "bloke" meaning "a man" in the mid-19th century, originally likely to have been derived from the Irish word
      <i>

      </i>

      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There is no standard orthography. Broadly speaking, Shelta can either be written following an Irish-type orthography or an English-type orthography. For example, the word for "married" can either be spelled
      <i>
       lsped
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       lohsped
      </i>
      , a "woman" can either be spelled
      <i>
       byohr
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       beoir
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-name_5-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-name-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
