     <p>
      The Greek philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Thales" title="Thales">
       Thales of Miletus
      </a>
      accurately predicted a solar eclipse, according to
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)" title="Histories (Herodotus)">
        The Histories
       </a>
      </i>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
       Herodotus
      </a>
      . If Herodotus's account is accurate, this eclipse is the earliest recorded as being known in advance of its occurrence. Many historians believe that the predicted eclipse was the solar eclipse of May 28, 585 BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      How exactly Thales predicted the eclipse remains uncertain; some historians claim the eclipse was never predicted at all.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclipsemap_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-eclipsemap-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Others have argued for different dates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Herodotus, the appearance of the eclipse was interpreted as an omen, and interrupted a battle in a long-standing war between the
      <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">
       Medes
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Lydians" title="Lydians">
       Lydians
      </a>
      . The fighting immediately stopped, and they agreed to a truce. Because astronomers can calculate the dates of historical eclipses,
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
       Isaac Asimov
      </a>
      described this battle as the earliest historical event whose date is known with precision to the day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Herodotus.27s_account">
       Herodotus's account
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eclipse_of_Thales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Herodotus's account">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Herodotus's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)" title="Histories (Herodotus)">
        The Histories
       </a>
      </i>
      1.73-74 states that a war started in the period between the Medes and the Lydians. There were two reasons for the war; the two sides' clashing interests in
      <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
       Anatolia
      </a>
      , but also there was a motive of revenge. Some
      <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">
       Scythian
      </a>
      hunters employed by the Medes who once returned empty-handed were insulted by King
      <a href="/wiki/Cyaxares" title="Cyaxares">
       Cyaxares
      </a>
      . In revenge the hunters slaughtered one of his sons and served him to the Medes. The hunters then fled to
      <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">
       Sardis
      </a>
      , the capital of the Lydians. When Cyaxares asked for the Scythians to be returned to him,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alyattes_II" title="Alyattes II">
       Alyattes II
      </a>
      refused to hand them over; in response, the Medes invaded.
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Afterwards, on the refusal of Alyattes to give up his suppliants when Cyaxares sent to demand them of him, war broke out between the Lydians and the Medes, and continued for five years, with various success. In the course of it the Medes gained many victories over the Lydians, and the Lydians also gained many victories over the Medes. Among their other battles there was one night engagement. As, however, the balance had not inclined in favour of either nation, another combat took place in the sixth year, in the course of which, just as the battle was growing warm, day was on a sudden changed into night. This event had been foretold by Thales, the Milesian, who forewarned the Ionians of it, fixing for it the very year in which it actually took place. The Medes and Lydians, when they observed the change, ceased fighting, and were alike anxious to have terms of peace agreed on.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      As part of the terms of the peace agreement, Alyattes's daughter
      <a href="/wiki/Aryenis" title="Aryenis">
       Aryenis
      </a>
      was married to Cyaxares's son
      <a href="/wiki/Astyages" title="Astyages">
       Astyages
      </a>
      , and the Halys River (now known as the
      <a href="/wiki/K%C4%B1z%C4%B1l%C4%B1rmak_River" title="Kzlrmak River">
       Kzlrmak River
      </a>
      ) was declared to be the border of the two warring nations.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_eclipse">
       The eclipse
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eclipse_of_Thales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The eclipse">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The eclipse peaked over the
      <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
       Atlantic Ocean
      </a>
      at
      <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
       <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Eclipse_of_Thales&amp;params=37.9_N_46.2_W_type:event&amp;title=Battle+of+Halys+eclipse+peak">
        <span class="geo-nondefault">
         <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
          <span class="latitude">
           3754N
          </span>
          <span class="longitude">
           4612W
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          / 
        </span>
        <span class="geo-default">
         <span class="vcard">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           37.9N 46.2W
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
            /
           <span class="geo">
            37.9; -46.2
           </span>
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
            (
           <span class="fn org">
            Battle of Halys eclipse peak
           </span>
           )
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
       </a>
      </span>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
       umbral
      </a>
      path reached south-western
      <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
       Anatolia
      </a>
      in the evening hours, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halys_River" title="Halys River">
       Halys River
      </a>
      is just within the error margin for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta-T" title="Delta-T">
       delta-T
      </a>
      provided.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclipsemap_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-eclipsemap-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An alternative theory regarding the date of the battle suggests that Herodotus was recounting carelessly events that he did not witness personally and furthermore the solar eclipse story is a misinterpretation of his text.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worthen_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Worthen-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to this view, what happened could have been a
      <i>
       lunar
      </i>
      eclipse right before moonrise, at dusk. If the warriors had planned their battle activities expecting a full moon as in the previous few days, it would have been quite a shock to have dusk fall suddenly as an occluded moon rose. If this theory is correct, the battle's date would be not 585 BC (date given by Pliny based on date of solar eclipse), but possibly 3 Sept 609 BC or 4 July 587 BC, dates when such dusk-time lunar eclipses did occur.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worthen_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Worthen-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
