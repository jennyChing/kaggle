       <h1>
        Orwell: Timeless Guidelines for Writers
       </h1>
       <span class="author">
        By Maeve Maddox
       </span>
      </div>
      <div class="alignleft">
       <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
       </script>
       <!-- dwt 300x250 top -->
       <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-9065706378227871" data-ad-slot="3188506408" style="display:inline-block;width:300px;height:250px">
       </ins>
       <script>
        (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       If youve never read George Orwells 1946 essay 
       <a href="http://www.mtholyoke.edu/acad/intrel/orwell46.htm">
        Politics and the English Language
       </a>
       , treat yourself.
      </p>
      <p>
       Written more than half a century ago, it remains as timely in 2007 as it was when he wrote it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unfortunately.
      </p>
      <p>
       In this essay Orwell discusses the political use of language to manipulate and obscure:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Millions of peasants are robbed of their farms and sent trudging along the roads with no more than they can carry: this is called
        <em>
         transfer of population
        </em>
        or
        <em>
         rectification of frontiers
        </em>
        . People are imprisoned for years without trial, or shot in the back of the neck or sent to die of scurvy in Arctic lumber camps: this is called
        <em>
         elimination of unreliable elements
        </em>
        .
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Orwell drew on Communist rhetoric for many of his illustrations, but our own times have generated the political euphemism
       <strong>
        ethnic cleansing
       </strong>
       to cloak the heinous reality of dislocation, rape, and murder.
      </p>
      <p>
       Every word of the essay will reward your reading, but the section that I keep going back to is the one in which Orwell formulates six rules for clean, honest writing:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        1) Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Observing this rule will not only eliminate clich in your writing, it will preserve you from disseminating the pre-digested thoughts of others.
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        2) Never use a long word where a short one will do.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Many Latinate words in a row have the effect of softening and obscuring meaning. Be especially careful with strings of nouns ending in
       <em>
        -tion
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        3) If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       We can all benefit by going back over our work looking for such unnecessary words as
       <em>
        just, almost, apparently
       </em>
       , and a great many other superfluous adverbs.
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        4) Never use the passive where you can use the active.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Not only does the passive voice have the effect of slowing down writing, it enables the political writer to avoid placing responsibility. Compare:
       <br/>
       <em>
        The Indians were forced from their homes.
        <br/>
        The government of Georgia forced the Indians from their homes.
       </em>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        5) Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       For all that we should be free to use the word niggardly if we wish, we can usually get our point across with the more familiar and less controversial
       <em>
        stingy
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        6) Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       While we neednt write
       <em>
        billets doux
       </em>
       for
       <em>
        love letters
       </em>
       , wed be up a creek if we had to come up with an everyday English equivalent for such assimilated foreign expressions as
       <em>
        laissez-faire, dtente
       </em>
       , and
       <em>
        clich
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       TIP: Good writing is honest writing. Begin with a clear idea of what it is you want to say. Be prepared to write and rewrite until the words youve poured out on paper come as close as possible to the idea you wish to convey. Dont use big words to impress, but dont underestimate the intelligence of your reader.
      </p>
      <p>
       And go back to Orwell from time to time.
      </p>

         <p>
          In response to Ricardos query, heres what Wikipedia had to say on this point.
         </p>
         <p>
          Early Modern English distinguished between the plural you and the singular thou. This distinction was lost in modern English due to the importation from France of a Romance linguistic feature which is commonly called the T-V distinction. This distinction made the plural forms more respectful and deferential; they were used to address strangers and social superiors. This distinction ultimately led to familiar thou becoming obsolete in standard English (and Dutch), although this did not happen in other languages such as French.
         </p>
         <p>
          Interesting!

          I think the crucial thing that people often overlook is the first word of the essays title: Politics.

         </p>
         <p>
          The problem comes when people think these suggestions apply to ALL forms of writing, even fiction. You only have to skim Orwells own works to see that was not his intention.
