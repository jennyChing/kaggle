       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The Art of the Save: Triage and the E.R.
       </h1>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.nursingschoolhub.com/triage/">
          <img alt="triage" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1012" height="3031" src="http://www.nursingschoolhub.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/triage.jpg" width="900"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <center>
         <strong>
          Share this infographic on your site!
         </strong>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <textarea cols="70" onclick="this.select();" rows="3">
          <a href="http://www.nursingschoolhub.com/triage/">
           <img alt="Triage" border="0" src="http://www.nursingschoolhub.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/triage.jpg" width="500"/>
          </a>
          <br/>
          Source:
          <a href="http://www.nursingschoolhub.com/">
           NursingSchoolHub.com
          </a>
         </textarea>
        </center>
        <p>
         Triage: the evaluation and categorization of the sick or wounded when there are insufficient resources for medical care of everyone at once.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          The Earliest Days of Triage
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         760-710 BC: Greek physician Pikoulis determines arrow and spear wounds have high mortality rates
         <br/>
         1745: From this date on, combat surgeons are used
         <br/>
         1793: Baron Dominique Larrey (father of triage): put surgical teams near front lines in the Napoleonic wars
         <br/>
         1860s: American Civil War: doctors were clueless about triage
         <br/>
         1885: The British Army Medical staff formed
         <br/>
         1915: World War I. The French concept of combat surgery. Named it trierto sort out.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Most common modern day causes of trauma:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         1. Car accident
         <br/>
         2. Falls
         <br/>
         3. Violence
         <br/>
         4. Gunshot wounds
         <br/>
         5. Fires
         <br/>
         6. Burns
        </p>
        <p>
         WOW: Trauma is the leading cause of death in the U.S. for people ages 1 to 44, and costs approximately $260 billion annually.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Fewer than 8 percent of hospitals in the U.S. have a trauma center.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Established triage systems:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         START, or Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment
         <br/>
         Can be implemented by people without a high level of training.
         <br/>
         The categories in START are:
         <br/>
          the deceased, who are beyond help
         <br/>
          the injured who could be helped by immediate transportation
         <br/>
          the injured with less severe injuries whose transport can be delayed
         <br/>
          those with minor injuries not requiring urgent care.
        </p>
        <p>
         COLOR CODEs:
         <br/>
          Red tags  (immediate) Cannot survive without immediate treatment but who have a chance of survival.
         <br/>
          Yellow tags  (observation) Requires observation (and possible later re-triage). Their condition is stable for the moment and, they are not in immediate danger of death..
         <br/>
          Green tags  (wait) are reserved for the walking wounded.
         <br/>
          White tags  (dismiss) minor injuries
         <br/>
          Black tags:  Dead orthose whose injuries are so extensive that they will not be able to survive given the care that is available.
        </p>
        <p>
         ESI: Emergency Severity Index: patients are rated 1 (most urgent) to 5 (least resource intensive)
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Best Emergency Medicine Programs:
         </strong>
         <br/>
         1. Johns Hopkins Hospital, Baltimore, Md.
         <br/>
         2. Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minn.
         <br/>
         3. Massachusetts General Hospital, Boston, Mass.
         <br/>
         4. Cleveland Clinic, Cleveland, Ohio
         <br/>
         5. Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center, Los Angeles, Cal.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          How to Bring a Dead Person back to life (Reallyits happening)
         </strong>
         <br/>
         1. The Auto Pulse: A portable CPR machine, keeps the heart beating. Developed in Australia.
         <br/>
         2. Extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO): An artificial lung outside the body that keeps oxygen and blood flowing to the patients brain and vital organs. Australia..and now the U.S. too
         <br/>
         3. Cyogenics: The deep freeze (still untested)
         <br/>
         4. Adrenaline high: During an operation, a patients pulse suddenly stops because of anesthesia, a surgeon injects adrenalin directly into his heart. Sometimes he recovers, usually he dies. OR#5
         <br/>
         5. Cut open the patients chest and massage his heart immediately, without waiting to try adrenalin.
         <br/>
         OR: Use electricity to bring the body back to life: Dr. Henry Frankenstein (1931)  and many movies thereafter.
         <br/>
         But seriously..
         <br/>
         WATCH OUT FOR: The phenomenon of over-triage: occurs when patients are transferred twice, and discharged from a second facility in less than 24 hours.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Hollywood and the E.R.: George Clooney played Doug Ross on the hit TV series, E.R.
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Other star TV doctors:
        </p>
        <p>
         1. Hawkeye Pierce, MASH
         <br/>
         2. Bones McCoy, Star Trek
         <br/>
         3. DoogieHowser
         <br/>
         4. J.D. Dorian, Scrubs
         <br/>
         5. Derek Shepherd (McDreamy), Greys Anatomy
        </p>
