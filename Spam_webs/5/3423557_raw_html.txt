        <h1 class="entry-title">
         50 Things to Know Before and After You Retire: Planning a Happy Future by Planning Ahead
        </h1>
        <div class="entry-meta">
         <span class="sep">
          Posted on
         </span>
         <a href="http://50thingstoknow.com/50-things-know-retire-planning-happy-future-planning-ahead/" rel="bookmark" title="13:58">
          <time class="entry-date updated" datetime="2013-09-19T13:58:34+00:00" pubdate="">
           September 19, 2013
          </time>
         </a>
         <span class="by-author">
          <span class="sep">
           by
          </span>
          <span class="author vcard">
           <a class="url fn n" href="http://50thingstoknow.com/author/lbrennec/" rel="author" title="View all posts by 50TTK">
            50TTK
           </a>
          </span>
         </span>

       <div class="entry-content">
        <div class="shareaholic-canvas" data-app="share_buttons" data-app-id="15521260" data-link="http://50thingstoknow.com/50-things-know-retire-planning-happy-future-planning-ahead/" data-summary="" data-title="50 Things to Know Before and After You Retire: Planning a Happy Future by Planning Ahead">
        </div>
        <div class="mailmunch-forms-before-post" style="display: none !important;">
        </div>
        <div class="mailmunch-forms-before-post" style="display: none !important;">
        </div>
        <p align="center" style="text-align: left;">
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00FADRUFA/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B00FADRUFA&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=charlottewhitney-20">
          <img alt="ID-10084967" class="size-medium wp-image-452 alignleft" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/50thingstoknow.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/ID-10084967.jpg?resize=300%2C199"/>
         </a>
         Retirement conjures up images of playing golf in endless sunshine, lunching with friends for hours on end, and traveling to exotic corners of the earth. Add in playtimes with grandchildren, romantic evenings with your partner, and all the items on your bucket list. Who wouldnt want to retire? As the saying goes, In retirement every day is Saturday.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, most people understand that life is lots more complicated than this. Blissful retirement doesnt just happen. Many people have difficulty in adjusting from a very structured work life to one that is unstructured. In addition, problems with finances, health, family members, and friends can easily derail the golden years. In a nutshell, expect the unexpected, and plan accordingly.
        </p>
        <p>
         Its important to start early with sound habits to ensure that retirement will look much more like a perfect dream than an ugly nightmare. This book provides a guide to planning a perfect retirement. Unlike many retirement guidebooks, it goes well beyond 401Ks and pensions with considerations about many other life satisfaction measures, adding in some slack for those unexpected life events.
        </p>
        <h2>
         1. Start Now With Everything
        </h2>
        <p>
         Whatever your age its time to start. Start saving for your retirement. Start improving your health. Start doing a few of those things on your bucket list. Life is too short to wait until that elusive retirement comes along. Figure out what you love doing and build it into your life. Happiness should not be a goal, but part of your day-to-day life. Then, when you start your retirement journey you will have the self-knowledge to create the future you really want.
        </p>
        <h2>
         2. Make Excellent Health Your Top Priority
        </h2>
        <p>
         Youve heard this before: All the time and money in the world is meaningless without your health. Its time to quit procrastinating and lose that last ten pounds (or more); get on a rigorous fitness regimen; improve your diet; and get rid of any other health-damaging habits. For those people with already existing impediments, develop strategies for incorporating them into your retirement. Do you need to purchase a motorized scooter now? Join a smoking cessation program? Find a swimming pool with a pool lift? Anything you can do to be heart healthy and cancer free at retirement is your first step to achieving your retirement goals.
        </p>
        <h2>
         3. Decide To Reduce Stress From Your Life
        </h2>
        <p>
         Perhaps youre already running three miles daily, laughing freely with friends, and practicing yoga and meditation. If so, continue to enjoy life perhaps not totally stress-free, but certainly more relaxed. If you havent incorporated such practices in your life, seek out stress reduction techniques that work for you. Yoga may not be your thing, but you may find that you live for chi gong. Find your routine and stick with it. Your heart, muscles, and lungs will thank you. So will your spouse and kids.
        </p>
        <h2>
         4. Record What Makes You Happy
        </h2>
        <p>
         If this is a tough exercise for you, then start by examining your current life and take note of the times you really savor. You will never enjoy retirement until you know what you want to do with your new-found leisure. Keep in mind that this may be different from your bucket list (sky-diving, ballet lessons, dress-making) or places you want to go (Tuscany, Kenya, Antarctica). Some of the items on the bucket list may have to wait a bit until you have more time, but you can start right now spending time playing with pets and kids, gardening, and taking walks in the park. You might even be able to incorporate a trip to Tuscany and the sky-diving lessons as well. These activities should be part of your current life if theyre at the top of your happiness list. Still puzzled? Write down your happiest childhood moments. Look at themes. Here are some activities that have translated well from my childhood to adulthood: picnics, swimming, reading, music, hiking, card games, dancing, movies, and hanging out with friends.
        </p>
        <h2>
         5. Get A Physical Exam
        </h2>
        <p>
         Everyone needs to get an annual physical followed up with all the routine tests. Dealing with problems at an early phase can prevent much sorrow later on. Believe me. Colon cancer is prevalent in my girlfriends family. She has had numerous polyps removed before they could develop into cancer. She even has a tattoo on her colon marking a spot to watch. Gross? Maybe. Smart? Yes. Likewise, stay up to date with all your vaccinations. Your physician will recommend when to get shingles, pneumonia, and other age-related vaccinations. You may also need some extra immunizations if you travel to certain international destinations. Check with your doctor on these.
        </p>
        <h2>
         6. Decide On Medical Procedures Before You Retire
        </h2>
        <p>
         If your health insurance changes at the time you retire, you may want to have some procedures done pre-retirement while you are still covered. Same with dental and vision problems. Taking a sick leave while you are still working is no fun, but thousands of dollars for the same procedure post-retirement is simply bad financial planning.
        </p>
        <h2>
         7. Is Medicare Tied To Retirement?
        </h2>
        <p>
         Medicare is the government-based health program that most people elect at age sixty-five. It has nothing to do with whether you are retired; however, any other health coverage you may have may impact what you sign up for at age sixty-five. Medicare has different parts, the most common being A, B, and D. If you are retired and have been on employer-provided health insurance, it typically becomes your secondary insurance once you are enrolled in Medicare. Keep in mind that if you continue to work past your sixty-fifth birthday, you must still apply to and go on Medicare, although you may want to delay certain benefits. There are financial penalties if you ignore Medicare all together when you turn sixty-five. Three months before your sixty-fifth birthday start doing the research and get the most up-to-date information regarding your specific situation. Usually the process goes smoothly, but remember you are dealing with a large bureaucracy.
        </p>
        <h2>
         8. Expect Health Issues
        </h2>
        <p>
         Numerous injuries and health-related issues are associated with aging. Even the most active adults in the best of health have had to deal with knee, hip, shoulder, back, and/or foot problems. While these injuries are not usually life-threatening, they can be annoying and painful. So as you plan your retirement, build in some time for physical therapy and rehab. Consider it the cost of living to a ripe old age. If by some miracle you are able to avoid these injuries, enjoy your new found time indulging in your favorite recreational activities.
        </p>
        <h2>
         9. Preserve Your Mental Health
        </h2>
        <p>
         These days there is no stigma associated with getting therapy. In fact, in many circles its de rigueur. That aside, a professional therapist can help you get through times of crisis and work with you to alleviate depression and other anxiety-related issues. Unfortunately life is rarely smooth sailing and often just when you think youve passed through rough seas, another crisis occurs. In retirement you may be spared work stress, but the day-to-day issues of friends and family can bring you down. Find a good therapist who can help you through difficult times.
        </p>
        <h2>
         10. Embrace Your Faith And Spirituality
        </h2>
        <p>
         While this topic has a variety of meanings and differs from individual to individual, it is still an essential component of health and well-being. Discover your own interpretation of living in the light. You may find a beautiful new component of life satisfaction. Also, dont assume that one practice precludes others. I have known Jews who practice Siddha Yoga; Presbyterians who celebrate the full moon and harvest festivals; and Catholics who participate in Native American ceremonies.
        </p>
        <h2>
         11. Understand That There Is No Warranty
        </h2>
        <p>
         Unfortunately even the best planners can get blindsided by a debilitating accident, life-threatening disease, natural disaster, or unexpected crisis. Life is unpredictable. We all can recall incidents when lifes twists have been unfair and cruel. It sounds like a clich but sorrow and suffering are as much a part of life as happiness and joy. Experience the pain and when you are ready to move on, ask for help from your circle of family and friends. Resilience is a characteristic that will serve you well.
        </p>
        <div class="mailmunch-forms-in-post-middle" style="display: none !important;">
        </div>
        <h2>
         12. Deal With All Your Health Issues First
        </h2>
        <p>
         When youre young you feel invulnerable. Youve always been healthy, no need for health insurance. You may forget whether youve had all your immunizations. Youre secure with texting and driving or drinking and driving because youre young, alert, and have great reflexes. Wrong! To plan now for a happy, healthy retirement, get all your health-related ducks in a row. Work every day not on just good health, but on excellent health. Then turn your attention to:
        </p>
        <div class="mailmunch-forms-in-post-middle" style="display: none !important;">
        </div>
        <h2>
         13. Figuring Out Finances
        </h2>
        <p>
         Most professional retirement planners start here and understandably so. Retirement planning means financial planning. Having sufficient funds to retire is essential to terminating full-time employment. You will find that many people who reach retirement eligibility discover that they must continue to work because their savings are too meager to last another twenty to thirty years. This means that you must start saving early to get the compound benefits of what financial planners call the time value of money. Keep in mind that most retirement planners will recommend that you plan to live on eighty-percent of your pre-retirement income after your retire. However, many retirees indicate that they find themselves needing more income post-retirement. Why? Because they have more time to spend on travel, eating out, hobbies, and other recreational activities, all of which can be costly.
        </p>
        <h2>
         14. Live On Less Now
        </h2>
        <p>
         Financial guru and television personality Suze Orman recommends living on fifty-percent of your salary throughout your work life. For instance, if you make two-thousand a month, then save one-thousand each month and live on one-thousand. Sound impossible? Start researching websites on frugal living and follow some of the advice listed below. You may find that you can retire well before sixty-five by putting yourself on a budget RIGHT NOW.
        </p>
        <h2>
         15. Start Early
        </h2>
        <p>
         Start saving in your twenties, if possible. What you save then will multiply exponentially. Impossible, you say. College loans, car payments, groceries, utilities, entertainment, and monthly rent eat up your entire paycheck. That being said, you may want to consider some of the frugality measures listed later in this book. For the moment, go online and use the retirement calculators provided. You will see how much your money grows if you start saving immediately.
        </p>

        <h1>
         Book Description
        </h1>
        <p>
         When should I retire? Should I work part-time? Volunteer? Will I have enough money to do the things I want to do? Will I be healthy enough to travel? Where should I live? How can I understand Social Security and Medicare? Do I need life insurance? A will? How can I make sure I will have a happy retirement? If you have considered these questions, this book is for you.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          50 Things to Know Before and After You Retire: Planning a Happy Future by Planning Ahead
         </span>
         by Charlotte Whitney offers an approach to retirement planning that goes beyond financial planning. It takes into consideration that all people do not hold the same dreams and expectations for retirement. The book embraces an individualistic approach to help you create your perfect future. It forces you to consider questions you need to address before retirement, and it offers strategies for avoiding many common mistakes. Best of all, it helps you to start this utopian future now, not in twenty or thirty years. You deserve, after all, not just retirement from a job, but a happy future.
