       <h2 class="post-title">
        Toad In The Hole
       </h2>
       <p class="post-info">
        By Mike    on Saturday, September  5 2009, 16:01        -
        <a href="http://atomicshrimp.com/post/2009/09/05/Toad-In-The-Hole">
         Permalink
        </a>
       </p>
       <ul class="post-tags">
        <li>
         <a href="http://atomicshrimp.com/tag/Food" rel="tag">
          Food
         </a>
        </li>

         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="post-excerpt">
        <p>
         <img alt="toadintheholethumb.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadintheholethumb.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadintheholethumb.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         Toad in the hole is a classic English dish of sausages baked in batter - served with a rich onion gravy.
        </p>
        <p>
         No amphibians were harmed in the making of this meal.
        </p>
       </div>
       <div>
       </div>
       <div class="post-content">
        <h2 class="clearleft">
         The Recipe
        </h2>
        <h3>
         Ingredients:
        </h3>
        <p>
         <b>
          For the main dish
         </b>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          100g Plain flour
         </li>
         <li>
          1 Egg
         </li>
         <li>
          300ml Milk
         </li>
         <li>
          Pinch of salt
         </li>
         <li>
          A pack of six or eight sausages
         </li>
         <li>
          (Optionally) One rasher of bacon per sausage
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <b>
          For the gravy
         </b>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          A large onion
         </li>
         <li>
          1 Tablespoon sugar
         </li>
         <li>
          300ml chicken stock or other bouillon
         </li>
         <li>
          1-2 Tablespoons plain flour
         </li>
        </ul>
        <h3>
         Method:
        </h3>
        <p>
         Make the batter first - ideally, an hour or more before everything else. Leaving it to rest before baking helps to ensure a light, puffy rise.
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole2.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole2.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole2.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         Put the flour and salt in a bowl or jug, make a little dip or hollow in the middle.
        </p>
        <p>
         Add the egg and half the milk.
        </p>
        <p>
         Beat with a fork to mix a little (no need to be too thorough at this stage).
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole3.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole3.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole3.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         Add the rest of the milk and beat again until combined.
        </p>
        <p>
         Don't worry about small lumps - but make sure there aren't any pockets of unmixed flour stuck at the bottom.
        </p>
        <p>
         Put the batter in the fridge and leave it to rest for at least an hour - longer if possible.
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole4.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole4.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole4.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         If you're going for the bacon option, wrap each of the sausages spirally with one rasher.
        </p>
        <p>
         Arrange them in an ovenproof dish or tray and place in a preheated oven (180 C) for half an hour, or until they are done.
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole5.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole5.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole5.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         Start making the gravy - slice the onion into quarter rings and put in a pan to gently fry.
        </p>
        <p>
         Keep an eye on the onions, stirring them once in a while.
        </p>
        <p>
         Keep the heat fairly low so they just gently sizzle - we want them to brown evenly/
        </p>
        <p>
         When they just start to turn brown, add the sugar and stir it in to coat the onion pieces
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole6.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole6.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole6.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         When the sausages are a few minutes short of being cooked, turn the oven up to 200 C and leave it at this temperature, ready for the batter.
        </p>
        <p>
         When the sausages are done, working quickly (but with great care - there is very hot fat here), take the tray of sausages out of the oven
        </p>
        <p>
         Add the batter (be careful - it may spit and sizzle). Return the dish to the oven as soon as possible (but again, without compromising on safety).
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole7.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole7.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole7.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         Keep gently frying the onions, stirring occasionally.
        </p>
        <p>
         Cook them until they turn a deep brown tan colour. (they're still not finished in this image)
        </p>
        <p>
         When the onions are fully browned, add some flour, stir it in and fry for a little while.
        </p>
        <p>
         Add the stock and whisk with a fork to dissolve any lumps - turn up the heat and bring the gravy to a gentle simmer - it should thicken.
        </p>
        <p>
         Season the gravy to taste.
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
          <img alt="toadinthehole1.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole1.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole1.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
          With a little luck, the gravy will just be ready at the same time as everything else.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
         <img alt="toadinthehole9.JPG" class="media" src="/public/t/toadinthehole9.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0 1em 1em 0;" title="toadinthehole9.JPG, Sep 2009"/>
         I like to serve toad in the hole with coarsely mashed potatoes and garden peas (I'll leave the timings of cooking these to your own ingenuity...)
        </p>
        <p>
         (Apologies for this final photo - I was so keen to tuck in, I didn't think enough about getting a nice-looking pr
         <span style="line-height: 1.6em;">
          esentation picture)
         </span>
        </p>
        <h3>
         Variations
        </h3>
        <p>
         Adding the bacon is about as far from the norm as I dare take this recipe - it's such an ancient and hallowed thing - as well as being an example of the beauty of simplicity - I'd rather cherish it for what it is, than try to change it too much.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Other English Classics
        </h3>
        <p>
         Toad in the hole is just one a handful of classic English/British dishes whose names have attained mythic status (even if the dishes themselves may not be known or understood).
        </p>
        <p>
         Spotted Dick and Jam Roly Poly could be coming soon to these pages...
        </p>
        <p class="clearleft">
        </p>
