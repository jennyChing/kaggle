      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       8 Books Every New Entrepreneur Should Read
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">

      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <figure class="featured_image">
         <img alt="" itemprop="image" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/karstenstrauss/files/2014/09/rickBrendaBeerhorst-e1410550990524.jpg"/>
         <figcaption>
          Technology has made it easier than ever to start a company, but what skills and knowledge should new entrepreneurs fill their heads with and from which books should they get them from? (image credit: Rick &amp; Brenda Beerhorst on Flickr)
         </figcaption>
        </figure>
        <p>
         In this, perhaps, Golden Age of technology entrepreneurship,
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/profile/peter-thiel/">
          Peter Thiel
         </a>
         can rest assured he will find an eager audience for his new book,
         <i>
          ZERO TO ONE: Notes on startups, Or How to Build the Future
         </i>
         (apparently he couldnt decide).
        </p>
        <p>
         Thiel, who turns 47 next month, was a co-founder of PayPal, and a co-founder of the
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/andygreenberg/2013/08/14/agent-of-intelligence-how-a-deviant-philosopher-built-palantir-a-cia-funded-data-mining-juggernaut/" target="_blank">
          somewhat scary
         </a>
         data firm Palantir. Hes well-known as the first outside investor in
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/facebook-ipo/">
          Facebook
         </a>
         and oversees a hedge fund and his own venture capital firm, Founders Fund. Our crack team of wealth analysts here at Forbes have Thiels net worth pegged at $2.2 billion, as of Sept 12, 2014.
        </p>
        <p>
         Will Thiels contribution to the long list of books focused on entrepreneurship prove itself an essential read for would-be business pros and founders? Well, time will tell. His collection of ruminations will have to nudge aside some strong titles. Here are a few stellar examples of books full of wisdom and tools for entrepreneurs that are new to the game:
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           Business Model Generation
          </i>
         </b>
         by Alexander Osterwalder
         <b>
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         As one of the first steps in the startup game, its probably better to set down your business plan
         <em>
          right
         </em>
         . With ideas assembled from 470 business model practitioners from 45 countries, the title offers insights into the simple and basic, and advanced complex models used by entrepreneurs and companies throughout the world. A respected tool.
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           How to Win Friends and Influence People
          </i>
         </b>
         by Dale Carnegie
        </p>
        <p>
         Were going old-school here. First published in 1936, this was a self-help book before the genre was even taken seriously. Why is it still relevant? Because learning the pulls and sways, motivations and tendencies of other people is all-important to the entrepreneur. Whether negotiating with partners or inspiring employees, people-skills are essential. Carnegie also gets a plus for choosing such a great title.
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           The Power of Habit: Why We Do What We Do in Life and Business
          </i>
         </b>
         by Charles Duhigg
        </p>
        <p>
         Duhiggs foray into the tendencies of companies, leaders and individuals is all about insight. By understanding the patterns of human behavior  and the power of the human mind to form patterns or recalibrate old ones  predictions can be made that an entrepreneur can take advantage of.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_11433" style="width: 765px">
         <img alt="(Image credit: Cotaro70s on Flickr)" class="size-full wp-image-11433" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/karstenstrauss/files/2014/09/Cotaro70s.jpg"/>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          (Image credit: Cotaro70s on Flickr)
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           The Lean Startup: How Todays Entrepreneurs Use Continuous Innovation to Create Radically Successful Businesses
          </i>
         </b>
         by Eric Ries
        </p>
        <p>
         Its a buzzword by now, the lean startup, but only because it makes sense for so many young companies and first-time founders. Technology has made it possible for companies to be started quickly and cheaply, with an internet market of billions. A lean model does away with wasteful operations and anything a customer is not willing to pay extra for.
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           The Intelligent Investor
          </i>
         </b>
         by Benjamin Graham
        </p>
        <p>
         According to Warren Buffet, this is the best book on investing,
         <i>
          ever
         </i>
         .
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/markets/">
          Markets
         </a>
         have changed since Graham published his work in 1949 but his allegorical style, which humanizes the ups and downs of indexes, is probably how generations of new investment students ever since have been explained the basics of the stock markets for the first time. Grahams Mr. Market  as both a lovable and confounding character  is still worth a look to anyone wishing to understand the trading game.
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           Cold Calling Techniques (That Really Work)
          </i>
         </b>
         by Stephen Schiffman
        </p>
        <p>
         It might seem a tad archaic to those whose minds are permanently plugged into the latest communication technology but, like it or not, as an entrepreneur you are going to have to pick up the phone and convince someone you dont know well to do something that benefits you greatly. Imagine the power of developing a skill like thatthats what this book is for.
        </p>

It benefits to people who are already good listeners while doesnt offer the actual steps to achieve this goal.
