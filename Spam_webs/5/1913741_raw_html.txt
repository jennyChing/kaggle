        <p class="title">
         The Origin of Nik Virella
        </p>
        <p class="description">
         Get better acquainted with the artist bringing Marvel to the Wild West during Secret Wars in 1872!
        </p>
       </div>
       <ul class="story-meta-block">
        <li class="author">
         <img src="http://x.annihil.us/u/prod/marvel/i/authors/tim-o-shea.jpg"/>

       <div class="story-body">
        <div class="story-body-text">
         <p>
          Nik Virella clearly has only been begun to reach her potential greatness at Marvel.
         </p>
         <p>
          The artist made her debut for the House of Ideas earlier this year with RETURN OF THE LIVING DEADPOOL and up next will be bringing out of the most anticipated
          <b>
           <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/events/323/secret_wars_2015">
            Secret Wars
           </a>
          </b>
          slices to life in
          <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/series/19501/1872_2015_-_present">
           1872
          </a>
          with writer Gerry Duggan. We spoke to Virella about her career to date, how she creates her best work, and what familiar Marvel characters will look like when they hit the Wild West!
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Marvel.com: You burst on the Marvel scene with Deadpools blazing earlier this year on
           <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/series/19633/return_of_the_living_deadpool_2015_-_present">
            RETURN OF THE LIVING DEADPOOL
           </a>
           . How did that first assignment come about?
          </b>
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Nik Virella:
          </b>
          [Editor] Jordan D. White contacted me during the summer and brought me on board to work on RETURN OF THE LIVING DEADPOOL with Cullen Bunn. It was the first time I got to work with Marvel, Cullen, and Jordan. I was given full responsibilities of penciling, inking, and coloring, which for me is great to have that amount of control over my work.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Marvel.com: Youve mentioned that a scene from
           <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/issue/52385/return_of_the_living_deadpool_2015_3">
            RETURN OF THE LIVING DEADPOOL #3
           </a>
           features a horse you fell off of as a child. What inspired you to literally draw from your own childhood in that scene?
          </b>
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Nik Virella:
          </b>
          Sometimes Ill read scenes in a script that will inadvertently trigger a memory. In this case, Cullen wrote an introduction scene for a band of Deadpools on horseback charging across the landscape. Similarly, many years ago, I was on horseback with a group of riders on a beach, except my horsenamed Bozakjust decided to take off and I fell face first into the sand. So for sentimental reasons, I decided to immortalize and give Bozak a little cameo [
          <i>
           laughs
          </i>
          ].
         </p>
         <p>
          In
          <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/issue/52386/return_of_the_living_deadpool_2015_4">
           the fourth issue of RETURN OF THE LIVING DEADPOOL
          </a>
          , there was another scene which I drew from personal experience. It had a group of Pools going about their business and a few were playing a game of Skully. I played Skully a lot when I was a kid. For those who don't know what Skully is, it was a popular street game that originated in the 50s and 60s with chalk drawn boards and bottle caps filled with wax for game pieces. But the significance of adding the game into the narrative was to foreshadow the ending of the series.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Marvel.com: Your use of black and white dominance in much of the color range allowing Deadpool to pop off the page proves stunning. Was that something suggested by Cullen, or you?
          </b>
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Nik Virella:
          </b>
          The art direction had already been established in the first series, NIGHT OF THE LIVING DEADPOOL. So I continued to follow suit with the limited color palette and only highlight whatever was Deadpool or specifically Deadpool-centric. There were some color exceptions though, because many of the Pools were unique, which I colored accordingly to whatever their station or circumstance was.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Marvel.com: You are an artist comfortable doing period pieces, so how excited are you to tackle Gerry Duggans 1872?
          </b>
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Nik Virella:
          </b>
          Im very excited to be working with Gerry on 1872! I always wanted to draw a western and I loved Gerrys writing on
          <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/series/16542/deadpool_2012_-_present">
           DEADPOOL
          </a>
          , so Im pretty thrilled to be on this book. Im also looking forward to collaborating with Gerry on ideas for drawing a ton of Marvel characters in their western counterparts.
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Marvel.com: No doubt you can do action scenes involving horses well, but what kind of other action and fight choreography can we anticipate in 1872?
          </b>
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>
           Nik Virella:
          </b>
          I cant say too much, but you can expect a lot of gun slinging and other fun action-packed surprises in Avengers 1872!
         </p>
         <p>
          <b>

           continues in
           <a href="http://marvel.com/comics/issue/52072/1872_2015_1">
            1872 #1
           </a>
           , available June 3!
          </i>
         </p>
