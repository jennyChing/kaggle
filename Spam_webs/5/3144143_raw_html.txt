            <hgroup>
             <h1>
              Simple Algorithm May Predict Risk of Imminent RA
             </h1>
            </hgroup>
           </header>
           <div class="byline">
            By Kara Nyberg, PhD
            <br/>
            Reviewed by Sarah Ringold, MD, MS, Seattle Childrens Hospital
           </div>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <aside class="sidebar">
            <div class="media infographic">
             <img alt="image" src="http://clf1.medpagetoday.com/assets/images/cuso/examining-patients-joints-300x225.jpg"/>
            </div>
            <section class="actionpoints">
             <header>
              <h2>
               Take Note
              </h2>
             </header>
             <div class="content">
              <ul>
               <li>
                Researchers developed a scoring system that predicts the likelihood of progression to inflammatory arthritis in individuals presenting with nonspecific musculoskeletal symptoms.
               </li>
               <li>
                The risk score relies on 4 simple measures: small-joint tenderness, early morning stiffness  30 minutes, high titers of anti-CCP or RF, and positive ultrasonographic power Doppler signal.
               </li>
               <li>
                The risk score needs to be validated in a large prospective cohort of patients before being implemented in clinical practice.
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
            </section>
           </aside>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            For patients seeking medical care for nonspecific musculoskeletal symptoms, a new algorithm proves helpful for identifying those individuals at risk of developing imminent rheumatoid arthritis (RA).
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            When initiated promptly, contemporary treatments for RA may forestall or prevent joint destruction. If validated in a larger cohort of patients, this new tool could aid primary care physicians in determining which patients may have preclinical RA and thus require urgent referral to secondary care for further follow-up, says Paul Emery, MA, MD, FRCP, Director of the Leeds Musculoskeletal Biomedical Research Unit and Director of the Institute of Rheumatic and Musculoskeletal Medicine at the University of Leeds in the United Kingdom, whose team developed the algorithm.
           </p>
           <p>
            There are good treatments available for patients with RA and good evidence that outcomes are best when patients with RA are treated early, explains study co-author Jackie L. Nam, MD, a Clinical Research Fellow at the Leeds Musculoskeletal Biomedical Research Unit. This has placed increasing importance on the need to diagnose these patients earlyhence the impetus behind developing the risk-score tool.
           </p>
           <p>
            A key attribute of the scoring system is that it consists of only 4 factors that can be easily ascertained in clinical practice with medical examination, serologic testing, and imaging analysis. These include
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            :
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Small-joint tenderness
            </li>
            <li>
             Early morning stiffness lasting at least 30 minutes
            </li>
            <li>
             High titers of rheumatoid factor (RF) or anti-cyclic citrullinated peptides (anti-CCP)
            </li>
            <li>
             A positive signal on power Doppler ultrasonography
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            To develop the scoring system, the UK group relied on data from 100 consecutive adult patients with new nonspecific musculoskeletal symptoms presenting for care. The cohort was restricted to patients found to be positive for anti-CCP antibodies in an effort to enrich for imminent RA onset. The mean age of the group was 51 years and included 72 women.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            By prospectively following the cohort for a median of 19.8 months, the investigators found that half of the patients developed inflammatory arthritis (IA) after a median of 7.9 months. Notably, 43 of these 50 individuals (86%) fulfilled the 2010 American College or Rheumatology/European League Against Rheumatism criteria for RA.
            <sup>
             1,2
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            Multivariable Cox regression analysis culled out the 4 variables noted above for their ability to independently predict an increased risk of progression to IA. Point values were then assigned to these factors based on their magnitude of risk. The result is a 0-to-5 scale in which high levels of anti-CCP or RF tally 2 points, whereas small-joint tenderness, at least 30 minutes of early morning stiffness, and positive power Doppler all score 1 point.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            When the investigators analyzed IA progression according to total score, 3 risk groups emerged. None of 5 individuals with a score of 0 progressed to IA, thus making up the low-risk group. Among 29 patients with a score of 1 or 2, 31% progressed to IA and thus comprised the moderate-risk group. The 66 individuals with a score of 3 or higher comprised the high-risk group, with 62% progressing to IA.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            The study investigators acknowledge that although [this] is one of the larger prospective cohorts of individuals at risk followed up from an early phase of anti-CCP positivity and nonspecific symptoms to the development of IA, the number of patients is still relatively small to allow for full multivariable analyses.
            <sup>
             1
            </sup>
            In addition, the findings remain to be confirmed in a larger pool of patients and to see whether an expanded set of predictors might enhance predictive accuracy. Dr. Emery and Dr. Nam note that these analyses are currently underway.
           </p>
           <p>
            Annette van der Helm-van Mil, MD, PhD, an internist and rheumatologist at Leiden University Medical Center in the Netherlands, who was asked to provide her perspective on this study, finds the results provocative but notes that it would be premature to implement the algorithm in clinical practice at this time. In general the paper is interesting. More studies in this field are required to increase the understanding and to validate the findings, she said. At present this study does not influence daily care in the clinic, but I think that more studies of this type need to be performed.
           </p>
