   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Antimicrobial copper-alloy touch surfaces
      </b>
      can prevent frequently touched surfaces from serving as reservoirs for the spread of pathogenic microbes. This is especially true in healthcare facilities, where harmful viruses, bacteria, and fungi colonize and persist on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doorknob" title="Doorknob">
       doorknobs
      </a>
      , push plates,
      <a href="/wiki/Handrail" title="Handrail">
       railings
      </a>
      , tray tables,
      <a href="/wiki/Tap_(valve)" title="Tap (valve)">
       tap (faucet)
      </a>
      handles,
      <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_therapy" title="Intravenous therapy">
       IV
      </a>
      poles,
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       HVAC
      </a>
      systems, and other equipment. These microbes can often survive on surfaces for surprisingly long periods of time (sometimes more than 30 days).
     </p>
     <p>
      The surfaces of
      <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
       copper
      </a>
      and its alloys, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">
       brass
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">
       bronze
      </a>
      , are
      <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_properties_of_copper" title="Antimicrobial properties of copper">
       antimicrobial
      </a>
      . They have an inherent ability to kill a wide range of harmful microbes relatively rapidly  often within two hours or less  and with a high degree of efficiency. These antimicrobial properties have been demonstrated by an extensive body of research. The research also suggests that if touch surfaces are made with copper alloys, the reduced transmission of disease-causing organisms can reduce patient infections in hospital
      <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">
       intensive care units
      </a>
      (ICU) by as much as 58%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassandra_D._Salgado_2013_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cassandra_D._Salgado_2013-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily.com_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily.com-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Antimicrobial_properties">
       Antimicrobial properties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Antimicrobial properties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_properties_of_copper#Antimicrobial_efficiency_of_copper_alloy_touch_surfaces" title="Antimicrobial properties of copper">
       Antimicrobial properties of copper  Antimicrobial efficiency of copper alloy touch surfaces
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Copper alloy surfaces have intrinsic properties to destroy a wide range of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">
       microorganisms
      </a>
      . In the interest of protecting public health, especially in healthcare environments with their susceptible patient populations, an abundance of peer-reviewed antimicrobial efficiency studies have been and continue to be conducted around the world regarding copper's efficiency to destroy
      <a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli_O157:H7" title="Escherichia coli O157:H7">
       <i>
        E. coli
       </i>
       O157:H7
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Methicillin-resistant_Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus">
       methicillin-resistant
       <i>
        Staphylococcus aureus
       </i>
      </a>
      (MRSA),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus">
        Staphylococcus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clostridium_difficile" title="Clostridium difficile">
        Clostridium difficile
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus" title="Influenza A virus">
       influenza A virus
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Adenoviridae" title="Adenoviridae">
       adenovirus
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">
       fungi
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Much of this antimicrobial efficiency work has been or is currently being conducted at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southampton" title="University of Southampton">
       University of Southampton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Northumbria_University" title="Northumbria University">
       Northumbria University
      </a>
      (United Kingdom),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Stellenbosch" title="University of Stellenbosch">
       University of Stellenbosch
      </a>
      (South Africa),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panjab_University" title="Panjab University">
       Panjab University
      </a>
      (India),
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chile" title="University of Chile">
       University of Chile
      </a>
      (Chile),
      <a href="/wiki/Kitasato_University" title="Kitasato University">
       Kitasato University
      </a>
      (Japan), the Instituto do Mar
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Coimbra" title="University of Coimbra">
       University of Coimbra
      </a>
      (Portugal), and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska" title="University of Nebraska">
       University of Nebraska
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_University" title="Arizona State University">
       Arizona State University
      </a>
      (US)
     </p>
     <p>
      In the US, to qualify copper and its alloys as registered antimicrobial substances under that nation's federal pesticide regulations, an extensive additional body of efficiency testing under
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_Laboratory_Practice" title="Good Laboratory Practice">
       Good Laboratory Practice
      </a>
      guidelines by an EPA-approved laboratory was required by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USEPA" title="USEPA">
       USEPA
      </a>
      . After these tests were concluded in 2008, registrations of 282 different copper alloys were granted.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_trials">
       Clinical trials
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clinical trials">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">
       Microorganisms
      </a>
      are known to survive on inanimate touch surfaces for extended periods of time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This can be especially troublesome in hospital environments where patients with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunodeficiencies" title="Immunodeficiencies">
       immunodeficiencies
      </a>
      are at enhanced risk for contracting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nosocomial_infections" title="Nosocomial infections">
       nosocomial infections
      </a>
      (hospital-borne infections), often with fatal consequences.
     </p>
     <p>
      Touch surfaces commonly found in hospital rooms, such as bed rails, call buttons, keyboards, touch plates, chairs, door handles, light switches, grab rails, intravenous poles, dispensers (alcohol gel, paper towel, soap), dressing trolleys, and counter and table tops are known to be contaminated with high levels of potentially dangerous bacteria, including
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus">
        Staphylococcus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Methicillin-resistant_Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus">
       Methicillin-resistant
       <i>
        Staphylococcus aureus
       </i>
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      ), one of the most virulent strains of antibiotic-resistant bacteria and
      <a href="/wiki/Vancomycin-resistant_Enterococcus" title="Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus">
       Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus
      </a>
      (VRE).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Objects in closest proximity to patients have the highest levels of
      <i>
       staphylococcus
      </i>
      , MRSA, and VRE. This is why touch surfaces in hospital rooms can serve as abundant sources, or reservoirs, for the spread of bacteria from the hands of healthcare workers and visitors to patients.
     </p>
     <p>
      Hand and surface
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfection" title="Disinfection">
       disinfection
      </a>
      practices are the first line of defense against infection. However, these have fallen short of expectations as opportunistic organisms put patients and healthcare workers at risk. Since approximately 80% of infectious diseases are known to be transmitted by touch,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and pathogens found in healthcare facilities can survive on inanimate surfaces for days and even months,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the microbial burden of frequently touched surfaces is believed to play a significant role in infection causality.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Surfaces in hospitals and healthcare facilities are frequently touched and therefore could become reservoirs of infection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To evaluate their effectiveness as secondary infection control measures, these products have been made from copper and its alloys and deployed in hospital geriatric wards,
      <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">
       intensive care units
      </a>
      , and general medical wards around the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">
       Clinical trials
      </a>
      are being conducted on microbial strains unique to individual healthcare facilities around the world to evaluate to what extent copper alloys can reduce the incidence of infection in hospital environments.
     </p>
     <p>
      The success of these clinical trials to date, which are summarized here, are prompting hospitals around the world to specify antimicrobial copper touch surfaces as an additional weapon in the fight against infection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">
       Chile
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chile">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Chile, 70,000 nosocomial infections are reported each year, most commonly from common hospital-borne pathogens such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">
        S. aureus
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P._aeruginosa" title="P. aeruginosa">
        P. aeruginosa
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A.baumanii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A.baumanii (page does not exist)">
        A.baumanii
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In a 30-week clinical trial at the Hospital del Cobre ("Copper Hospital"), extensive microbial analyses were implemented at the facility's
      <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">
       intensive care unit
      </a>
      (ICU).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nine hundred ninety copper surfaces from 90 rooms containing 6 different copper objects were studied against an equivalent number of rooms and surfaces containing non-copper objects. Over-the-bed tables were made from copper alloy C70600. Bedrails were clad with copper alloy C11000 foils. Visitor chairs were fitted with copper alloy C70600 armrests. Copper alloy C71000 intravenous poles were provided. Writing pens used to input data on a touch screen were made from brass (70% Cu, 30% Zn).
     </p>
     <p>
      Results of this clinical trial demonstrated an approximately 90% reduction of microorganisms on the copper items compared to the controls after ten weeks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A reduction in the total microbial burden was seen for each class of microbe evaluated. Furthermore, continuous antimicrobial activity of copper persisted throughout the study.
     </p>
     <p>
      Copper was effective in reducing microbial loads on all 6 surfaces tested (i.e., bed rails by 91%, bed levers by 82%, tray tables by 83%, chair arms by 92%, monitor pen by 49%, and IV poles by 88%).
     </p>
     <p>
      Average microbial burden counts in rooms with copper touch surfaces were significantly lower than in rooms without copper surfaces.
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staphylococci" title="Staphylococci">
        Staphylococci
       </a>
      </i>
      were the most predominant microorganism isolated and copper was effective in reducing the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staphylococci" title="Staphylococci">
        Staphylococci
       </a>
      </i>
      microbial burden. Further studies regarding the clinical implications of copper's intrinsic ability to reduce microbial burdens in hospitals are being planned.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">
       Japan
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japan">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Researchers from the
      <a href="/wiki/Kitasato_University" title="Kitasato University">
       Kitasato University
      </a>
      School of Medicine conducted antimicrobial studies of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">
        S. aureus
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._coli" title="E. coli">
        E. coli
       </a>
      </i>

      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P._aeruginosa" title="P. aeruginosa">
        P. aeruginosa
       </a>
      </i>
      on various Japanese copper alloy coins and on copper alloy plates. The microbes were strains from hospital environments. Copper and its
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-silver" title="Nickel-silver">
       nickel-silver
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cupronickel" title="Cupronickel">
       cupronickel
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">
       brass
      </a>
      alloys were found to kill the bacteria within a short time. In another experiment, bacterial colonies were investigated on ball point pens made with and without copper alloys. Total bacterial colonies on the copper pens were much lower than on the non-copper pens: 2.1
      <a href="/wiki/Colony-forming_unit" title="Colony-forming unit">
       CFU
      </a>
      versus 47.8 CFU.
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staphyloccocus" title="Staphyloccocus">
        Staphyloccocus
       </a>
      </i>
      counts on copper ball point pens were also much lower: 0.7 CFUs versus 20.8 CFUs on non-copper pens.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sasahara_et_al.2C_2007_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sasahara_et_al.2C_2007-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Due to the success of these results, a 2-year clinical trial was conducted to monitor contamination levels of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nosocomial" title="Nosocomial">
       nosocomial
      </a>
      bacteria in the
      <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">
       dermatology
      </a>
      ward and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neonatal" title="Neonatal">
       neonatal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">
       intensive care unit
      </a>
      (NICU) at
      <a href="/wiki/Kitasato_University" title="Kitasato University">
       Kitasato University
      </a>
      Hospital. The antimicrobial efficiency of copper on floors, sinks, push plates, showerheads and doorknobs was evaluated by comparing bacterial loads on these surfaces and on their non-copper counterparts.
     </p>
     <p>
      The number of viable
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus">
        Staphylococcus
       </a>
       spp
      </i>
      . organisms on surfaces containing copper and its alloy were reduced by half to one-thirtieth of that on control surfaces, depending upon surface humidity and the frequency of contact. Similar definitive findings were also obtained for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_aeruginosa" title="Pseudomonas aeruginosa">
        Pseudomonas aeruginosa
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sasahara_et_al.2C_2007_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sasahara_et_al.2C_2007-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Various metals were evaluated for their antimicrobial efficacies, including copper alloys,
      <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
       zinc
      </a>
      , nickel,
      <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">
       tin
      </a>
      , silver, and gold.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Antimicrobial efficacies generally followed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acidity" title="Lewis acidity">
       Lewis acidity
      </a>
      values of the various metals. Silver, a prohibitively expensive
      <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">
       precious metal
      </a>
      , had the highest bactericidal activity; copper came in second. The study also found that
      <a href="/wiki/Contact_dermatitis" title="Contact dermatitis">
       contact dermatitis
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergies" title="Allergies">
       allergies
      </a>
      rarely occur if copper is used as a hygienic touch material.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The bactericidal activity of copper was also tested against two strains of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">
        S. aureus
       </a>
      </i>
      <i>
       in vitro
      </i>
      to determine whether copper alloys are effective in preventing the spread of contamination on the touch surface products used in the hospital ward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      and
      <i>
       S. aureus
      </i>
      counts fell below detection limits within 180 minutes. The results indicated that copper has a strong bactericidal effect against
      <i>
       S. aureus
      </i>
      , including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      .
     </p>

      When a copper plate was situated on a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      -infected floor in the dermatology ward around a bed of a MRSA-infected patient, the bacterial count of
      <i>
       S. aureus
      </i>
      , including
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      and the other
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus">
        Staphylococcus
       </a>
      </i>
      on the floor covered with a copper plate was significantly less than on a floor unprotected by copper alloys. These results suggested that the copper plate helped to prevent the spread of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
       MRSA
      </a>
      contamination in the hospital.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">
       South Africa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: South Africa">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Multidrug-resistant and extremely drug-resistant
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Mycobacterium_tuberculosis" title="Mycobacterium tuberculosis">
        Mycobacterium tuberculosis
       </a>
      </i>
      (MTB) is responsible for the spread of
      <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">
       tuberculosis
      </a>
      in South African hospitals. Test strains of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">
        Candida albicans
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_aeruginosa" title="Pseudomonas aeruginosa">
        Pseudomonas aeruginosa
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Klebsiella_pneumoniae" title="Klebsiella pneumoniae">
        Klebsiella pneumoniae
       </a>
      </i>
      meticillin-resistant
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">
        Staphylococcus aureus
       </a>
      </i>
      (MRSA), and MTB were isolated from South African patients at a hospital's intensive care unit in order to establish the minimum
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-vitro" title="In-vitro">
        in-vitro
       </a>
      </i>
      copper concentrations to produce
      <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">
       sterilization
      </a>
      against these microbes and
      <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">
       yeast
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Acinetobacter_baumannii" title="Acinetobacter baumannii">
        Acinetobacter baumannii
       </a>
      </i>
      was isolated from a patient in a burn unit and two clinical strains of MTB were collected and tested.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Copper and its alloys demonstrated antimicrobial activities against multiple-antibiotic-resistant nosocomial bacteria and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">
        C. albicans
       </a>
      </i>
      isolated from the hospital, whereas
      <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">
       stainless steel
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PVC" title="PVC">
       PVC
      </a>
      did not. Copper and its alloys showed a marked inhibitory effect (8898%) on MTB despite the strain's drug resistance. The researchers concluded that the minimum concentration of copper to be an effective antimicrobial agent is &gt;55% for yeasts and bacteria. Higher concentrations of copper were found to be necessary to inhibit MTB.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_.26_Ireland">
       United Kingdom &amp; Ireland
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom &amp; Ireland">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the United Kingdom, around 300,000 patients contract
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nosocomial_infection" title="Nosocomial infection">
       nosocomial infections
      </a>
      each year and at least 5,000 patients die of complications from infections contracted in hospitals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For these reasons, a cross-over clinical trial (a test method designed to eliminate variability bias from patients, staff, cleaning efficiency, outbreaks, etc.) evaluating antimicrobial copper alloys was carried out at
      <a href="/wiki/Selly_Oak_Hospital" title="Selly Oak Hospital">
       Selly Oak Hospital
      </a>
      over an 18-month period in 20072008 by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_Hospital_Birmingham" title="University Hospital Birmingham">
       University Hospital Birmingham
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHS_Trust" title="NHS Trust">
       NHS Trust
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Aston_University" title="Aston University">
       Aston University
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Frequently touched surfaces typically manufactured with standard materials (i.e., plastic, chrome, aluminum) were replaced with copper alloys. These included a copper alloy set of sink tap handles (60% Cu, 40% Zn) and a ward entrance door push plate (70% Cu, 30% Zn).
     </p>
     <p>
      Contamination reductions of 90100% were observed for
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">
        Staphylococcus aureus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">
        Escherichia coli
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Klebsiella_pneumoniae" title="Klebsiella pneumoniae">
        Klebsiella pneumoniae
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acinetobacter_baumanii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Acinetobacter baumanii (page does not exist)">
        Acinetobacter baumanii
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Enterococcus_spp.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Enterococcus spp. (page does not exist)">
        Enterococcus spp.
       </a>
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">
        Candida albicans
       </a>
      </i>
      on the copper alloy surfaces versus the non-copper standard surfaces.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A microorganism reduction of 100% was observed on the hot tap copper alloy handle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The high degree of clinical performance suggested that copper alloy surfaces may increase the effectiveness of existing infection control practices and may lower the risk of infections acquired in healthcare facilities.
     </p>
     <p>
      Based on the results of this and other laboratory and clinical studies, St. Francis Private Hospital, a 140-bed facility located in Mullingar, County Westmeath, Ireland, decided to become the first hospital in the world to fully specify hygienic copper door handles throughout its facility as part of its infection control program.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irish_Hospital_First_to_Harness_Copper_Technology_to_Fight_Infections_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Irish_Hospital_First_to_Harness_Copper_Technology_to_Fight_Infections-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A full upgrade of all door furniture (i.e., 250 doorsets, incorporating handles, push plates and privacy locks) to antimicrobial copper in the hospital and nursing home commenced in January 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Healthcare architects in the United Kingdom are now specifying antimicrobial copper. Refurbishment projects specifying copper products are anticipated across
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHS_Trust" title="NHS Trust">
       NHS Trust
      </a>
      facilities.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
       United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the US, a high degree of statistical significance is needed to provide a convincing argument to U.S. federal government healthcare authorities, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">
       Centers for Disease Control and Prevention
      </a>
      (CDC), regarding the effectiveness of copper alloys in reducing microbial loads and cross infection in healthcare environments. For this reason, clinical trials at three major US hospitals are currently examining environmental bacterial loads, infection rates, and impacts on cross-contamination in intensive care unit (ICU) rooms retrofitted with copper touch surfaces versus rooms without copper surfaces.
     </p>
     <p>
      The trials are funded by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Defense" title="U.S. Department of Defense">
       U.S. Department of Defense
      </a>
      (DOD) under the
      <a href="/wiki/Telemedicine_and_Advanced_Technology_Research_Center_(United_States_Army)" title="Telemedicine and Advanced Technology Research Center (United States Army)">
       Telemedicine and Advanced Technology Research Center (TATRC)
      </a>
      , a subordinate element of the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Medical_Research_and_Materiel_Command" title="United States Army Medical Research and Materiel Command">
       United States Army Medical Research and Materiel Command
      </a>
      (USAMRMC). DOD has extraordinary interests in the potential for antimicrobial copper surfaces to reduce hospital-acquired infections because it wants to prevent hospital-acquired infections among thousands of its enlisted armed forces servicemen and servicewomen who have been injured in recent conflicts. TATRC, which funds a Military Infectious Disease Program
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      has been granted funds by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">
       United States Congress
      </a>
      to evaluate the antimicrobial effectiveness of copper, brass and bronze alloys. The studies are coordinated through the Advanced Technology Institute in Charleston, South Carolina.
     </p>
     <p>
      Four-year clinical studies, published in 2013, were conducted at the intensive care units (ICUs) at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Sloan-Kettering_Cancer_Center" title="Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center">
       Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center
      </a>
      in New York City, one of the world's most prestigious cancer facilities, the
      <a href="/wiki/Medical_University_of_South_Carolina" title="Medical University of South Carolina">
       Medical University of South Carolina
      </a>
      , and the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ralph_H._Johnson_VA_Medical_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ralph H. Johnson VA Medical Center (page does not exist)">

      <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">

     <p>
      The studies revealed that the use of antimicrobial copper surfaces in the ICU's reduced the number of healthcare-acquired infections (HAIs) by 58% compared to patients treated in ICUs with non-copper touch surfaces. The antimicrobial copper surfaces were proved to work continuously.
     </p>
     <p>

Furthermore, of critical importance to health care professionals, the preliminary results indicated that patients in the coppered ICU rooms had a 40.4% lower risk of contracting a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hospital_acquired_infection" title="Hospital acquired infection">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_antimicrobial_halo_effect_of_copper_surfaces">
       The antimicrobial halo effect of copper surfaces
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The antimicrobial halo effect of copper surfaces">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Copper surfaces have an antimicrobial 'halo' effect on surrounding non-copper materials. This effect is also helpful in reducing the presence of bacteria in healthcare environments. Research implemented in the neonatal intensive care unit (ICU) at Aghia Sofia Children's Hospital in Greece showed that non-copper surfaces up to 50 centimeters from the antimicrobial copper surfaces exhibited a microbial reduction of 70% compared to surfaces not in such close proximity. The halo effect was first observed during in trials at a US outpatient clinic in 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="USEPA_registrations_of_antimicrobial_copper_alloy_touch_surfaces">
       USEPA registrations of antimicrobial copper alloy touch surfaces
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: USEPA registrations of antimicrobial copper alloy touch surfaces">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      On February 29, 2008, the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       United States Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      (EPA) approved the registrations of five different groups of copper alloys as 
      <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial" title="Antimicrobial">
       antimicrobial
      </a>
      materials with public health benefits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The EPA registrations now cover 479 different compositions of copper alloys within six groups
      <i>
       (an up-to-date list of all approved alloys is available).
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      All of the alloys have minimum nominal copper concentrations of 60%. The results of the EPA-supervised antimicrobial studies demonstrating copper's strong antimicrobial efficacies across a wide range of alloys have been published.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      These copper alloys are the only solid surface materials to be granted antimicrobial public health claims status by EPA. Before these registrations were granted, only antimicrobial gases, liquids, sprays, and concentrated powders, including sterilizers, disinfectants and antiseptics, were registered to make antimicrobial public health claims.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Microbes_tested_and_killed_in_EPA_laboratory_tests">
       Microbes tested and killed in EPA laboratory tests
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Microbes tested and killed in EPA laboratory tests">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The bacteria destroyed by copper alloys in the EPA-supervised antimicrobial efficiency tests include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">
         Escherichia coli
        </a>
       </i>
       O157:H7, a foodborne pathogen associated with large-scale food recalls.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Methicillin-resistant_Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus">
        Methicillin-resistant
        <i>
         Staphylococcus aureus
        </i>
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRSA" title="MRSA">
        MRSA
       </a>
       ), one of the most virulent strains of antibiotic-resistant bacteria and a common culprit of hospital- and community-acquired infections.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">
         Staphylococcus aureus
        </a>
       </i>
       , the most common of all bacterial
       <i>
        staphylococcus
       </i>
       (i.e., Staph) infections that cause life-threatening disease, including pneumonia and meningitis.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Enterobacter_aerogenes" title="Enterobacter aerogenes">
         Enterobacter aerogenes
        </a>
       </i>
       , a pathogenic bacterium commonly found in hospitals that causes opportunistic skin infections and impacts other body tissues.
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_aeruginosa" title="Pseudomonas aeruginosa">
         Pseudomonas aeruginosa
        </a>
       </i>
       , a bacterium in immunocompromised individuals that infects the pulmonary and urinary tracts, blood and skin.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Vancomycin-resistant_Enterococcus" title="Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus">
        Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus
       </a>
       (VRE), a pathogenic bacterium that is the second leading cause of hospital-acquired infections.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="EPA_test_protocols_for_copper_alloy_surfaces">
       EPA test protocols for copper alloy surfaces
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: EPA test protocols for copper alloy surfaces">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The registrations are based on studies supervised by EPA which found that copper alloys kill more than 99.9% of disease-causing bacteria within just two hours when cleaned regularly (i.e., the metals are free of dirt or grime that may impede the bacteria's contact with the copper surface).
     </p>
     <p>
      To attain the EPA registrations, the copper alloy groups had to demonstrate strong antimicrobial efficacies according to all of the following rigorous tests:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        efficiency as a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanitizer" title="Sanitizer">
         sanitizer
        </a>
       </i>
       : This test protocol measures surviving bacteria on alloy surfaces after two hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         50
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Residual self-sanitizing activity
       </i>
       : This test protocol measures surviving bacteria on alloy surfaces before and after six wet and dry wear cycles over 24 hours in a standard wear apparatus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         51
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Continuous reduction of bacterial
        <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">
         contamination
        </a>
       </i>
       : This test protocol measures the number of bacteria that survive on a surface after it has been re-inoculated eight times over a 24-hour period without intermediate cleaning or wiping.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         52
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health_claims_granted_by_EPA_in_antimicrobial_copper_alloy_registrations">
       Public health claims granted by EPA in antimicrobial copper alloy registrations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Public health claims granted by EPA in antimicrobial copper alloy registrations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The EPA copper alloy registrations were granted with public health claims, meaning that they permit manufacturers of copper-based products sold in the U.S. to claim on their labels the ability of copper, brass, and bronze to kill harmful, potentially deadly bacteria.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Product_stewardship_requirements_of_EPA">
       Product stewardship requirements of EPA
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Product stewardship requirements of EPA">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As a condition of registration established by EPA, the Copper Development Association (CDA) in the US is responsible for the
      <a href="/wiki/Product_stewardship" title="Product stewardship">
       product stewardship
      </a>
      of antimicrobial copper alloy products. CDA must ensure that manufacturers promote these products in an appropriate manner. Manufacturers must only promote the proper use and care of these products and must specifically emphasize that the use of these products is a supplement and not a substitute to routine hygienic practices.
     </p>
     <p>
      EPA mandated that all advertising and marketing materials for antimicrobial copper products contain the following statement:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       The use of a Copper Alloy surface is a supplement to and not a substitute for standard infection control practices; users must continue to follow all current infection control practices, including those practices related to cleaning and disinfection of environmental surfaces. The Copper Alloy surface material has been shown to reduce microbial contamination, but it does not necessarily prevent cross-contamination.
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Antimicrobial copper alloys are intended to provide supplemental antimicrobial action in between routine cleaning of environmental or touch surfaces in healthcare settings, as well as in public buildings and the home. Users must also understand that in order for antimicrobial copper alloys to remain effective, they cannot be coated in any way.
     </p>
     <p>
      CDA is currently implementing an outreach program through written communications, a product stewardship website,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and through a Working Group which meets periodically to expand educational efforts.
     </p>
     <p>
      More than 100 different potential product applications were cited in the registrations for their potential public health benefits.
     </p>

     </h4>
     <p>
      The EPA warranty statement is worded as follows:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       If used as intended, ANTIMICROBIAL COPPER ALLOYS are wear-resistant and the durable antibacterial properties will remain effective for as long as the product remains in place and is used as directed.
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Note: With the exception of the product name and the percentage of active ingredient, the EPA-approved Master Labels for the six groups of registered alloys are identical.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Antimicrobial_copper_products">
       Antimicrobial copper products
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Antimicrobial copper products">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Market">
       U.S. Market
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimicrobial_copper-alloy_touch_surfaces&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: U.S. Market">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Antimicrobial Copper (Cu
      <sup>
       +
      </sup>
      ) is a logo/mark required by the U.S. E.P.A. in order for bulk metal fabricators and manufacturing companies to make public health claims about antimicrobial copper in the U.S. market. The logo/mark indicates that the products have strong efficacies as sanitizers, residual self-sanitizing efficacies after multiple wet-dry cycles, and continuous reductions of bacterial contaminants.
     </p>
     <p>
      A list of bulk alloy suppliers that offer E.P.A.-registered antimicrobial copper to manufacturers is available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An list of approved manufacturers and retailers offering antimicrobial copper products to the regulated U.S. market is also available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These firms have completed all legal requirements to sell antimicrobial copper products in accordance with the U.S. E.P.A registrations. Products offered by the approved companies include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinks" title="Sinks">
       sinks
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keyboards" title="Keyboards">
       keyboards
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faucets" title="Faucets">
       faucets
      </a>
      , IV poles,
      <a href="/wiki/Table_(furniture)" title="Table (furniture)">
       tables
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Stretcher" title="Stretcher">
       stretcher
      </a>
      rails,
      <a href="/wiki/Railings" title="Railings">
       railings
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hampers" title="Hampers">
       hampers
      </a>
      , decorative and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architectural_hardware" title="Architectural hardware">
       architectural hardware
      </a>
      (levers, grips, knobs, pulls, towel bars, grab bars, kick plates, bath accessories, outlet covers, light switch covers), laboratory
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incubator_(neonatal)" title="Incubator (neonatal)">
       incubators
      </a>
      , cabinet hardware, decorative tile, and
      <a href="/wiki/Weight_training" title="Weight training">
       free weights
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The U.S. E.P.A. oversees a stewardship plan to promote the responsible use of Antimicrobial Copper.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
