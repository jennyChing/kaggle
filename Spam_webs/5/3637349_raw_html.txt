           <h1 class="asset-name entry-title" id="page-title">
            Oscilloscope Fun and Games
           </h1>
           <div class="publishdate">
            Posted Feb 12, 2008
            
                (Updated Nov 16, 2011)
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="asset-content entry-content">
           <div class="asset-body">
            <p>
             As I mentioned yesterday, I got slightly obsessed with researching oscilloscope hacks yesterday, after seeing this jaw-dropping graphic demo released at Assembly 2007:
            </p>
            <p>
             <object height="355" width="425">
              <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/s1eNjUgaB-g&amp;rel=1">
              </param>
              <param name="wmode" value="transparent">
              </param>
              <embed height="355" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/s1eNjUgaB-g&amp;rel=1&amp;showsearch=0" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="425" wmode="transparent">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             The author released the audio files (
             <a href="http://kapsi.fi/~jpa/stuff/other/youscope-wave.flac">
              FLAC
             </a>
             and
             <a href="http://mirror.kapsi.fi/koodaa.mine.nu/tvt/youscope-wave.wav">
              WAV
             </a>
             ), allowing other people to try it on their own hardware or software scopes and
             <a href="http://www.youtube.com/video_response_view_all?v=s1eNjUgaB-g">
              post the results
             </a>
             .
            </p>
           </div>
           <div class="asset-more" id="more">
            <p>
             Digging around for oscilloscope art led me to one of the first video games (but
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_video_game">
              not the first
             </a>
             ), William Higinbotham's
             <a href="http://www.bnl.gov/bnlweb/history/higinbotham.asp">
              Tennis for Two
             </a>
             from 1958.  Though it looks like Pong, its controls were more complex, letting the player set the force and trajectory of their stroke.  Here's a
             <a href="http://www.gamersquarter.com/tennisfortwo/">
              simulator for Windows
             </a>
             , if you want to try it yourself.
            </p>
            <p>
             <object height="355" width="425">
              <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/s2E9iSQfGdg&amp;rel=1">
              </param>
              <param name="wmode" value="transparent">
              </param>
              <embed height="355" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/s2E9iSQfGdg&amp;rel=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="425" wmode="transparent">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             Since oscilloscopes are X-Y vector displays, it's not surprising that people have attempted to play classic vector games on their scopes.
             <a href="http://www.youtube.com/user/MooseZOOO">
              Moose
             </a>
             hacked MAME to render frames as audio to the PC soundcard, letting him play
             <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pkZcWJoUfYM&amp;feature=related">
              Asteroids
             </a>
             and
             <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gCXrwcV59nA">
              Star Wars
             </a>
             on his Cole S1001 scope.
            </p>
            <p>
             <object height="355" width="425">
              <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/gCXrwcV59nA&amp;rel=1">
              </param>
              <param name="wmode" value="transparent">
              </param>
              <embed height="355" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/gCXrwcV59nA&amp;rel=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="425" wmode="transparent">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             Clones of
             <a href="http://web.mit.edu/edmond/www/projects/pong.html">
              Pong
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://youtube.com/watch?v=blmybPGOlfM">
              Breakout
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://www.kosma.pl/hardware/snake/">
              Snake
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://youtube.com/watch?v=1UdH43zbV_s">
              The Game of Life
             </a>
             , and
             <a href="http://youtube.com/watch?v=sTf2r2Kbz9c&amp;feature=related">
              Duck Hunt
             </a>
             have all been ported to oscilloscopes, but I think my favorite is
             <a href="http://pontoppidan.info/lars/index.php?proj=scopetris">
              Scopetris
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <object height="355" width="425">
              <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/Hui5Azx5jQo&amp;rel=1">
              </param>
              <param name="wmode" value="transparent">
              </param>
              <embed height="355" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/Hui5Azx5jQo&amp;rel=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="425" wmode="transparent">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             In "Altercations,"
             <a href="http://www.raysweeten.com/">
              Ray Sweeten
             </a>
             uses an oscilloscope and a tremendous amount of noise in real-time performance art.  This one's for fans of
             <a href="http://www.last.fm/music/Merzbow">
              Merzbow
             </a>
             .
             <br/>
             <object height="355" width="425">
              <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/l7ApYtQ5S7o&amp;rel=1">
              </param>
              <param name="wmode" value="transparent">
              </param>
              <embed height="355" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/l7ApYtQ5S7o&amp;rel=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="425" wmode="transparent">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             Finally, this is more of an audio visualization of a software hack, but Mike Kan created a real-time visualization of a Mac OS booting up and accessing the filesystem and memory.  Beautiful.
            </p>
            <p>
             <object height="355" width="425">
              <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/D5PS-zOciF8&amp;rel=1">
              </param>
              <param name="wmode" value="transparent">
              </param>
              <embed height="355" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/D5PS-zOciF8&amp;rel=1" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="425" wmode="transparent">
              </embed>
             </object>
            </p>
            <p>
             For more oscilloscope fun, see Phil Torrone's
             <a href="http://blog.makezine.com/archive/2007/04/oscilloscope_clock.html">
              great roundup
             </a>
             on Make, and
             <a href="http://www.google.com/cse?cx=008032414425079535247%3Akplxrakvu20&amp;q=oscilloscope&amp;sa=Search">
              related coverage
             </a>
             .  You can use freely-available software to make your own oscilloscope, or hack together one with a PIC controller, an old hard drive, or an old TV.
            </p>
            <p>
             P.S. I typed this entry on an
             <a href="http://www.dutchtronix.com/ScopeTerm.htm">
              oscilloscope terminal
             </a>
             .
            </p>
            <p>
             <b>
              February 15 Update:
             </b>
             Inspired by this post, Matt Comi wrote a Ruby script called
             <a href="http://www.bigbucketblog.com/2008/02/16/oscillofy/">
              Oscillofy
             </a>
             that turns any image into an oscilloscope-ready audio file.
            </p>
            <p>
             <b>
              February 14, 2011:
             </b>
             <a href="http://flashingleds.wordpress.com/2011/02/13/nintendoscope/">
              NintendoScope
             </a>
             plays GameBoy games on an oscilloscope.
            </p>

              </p>
              <p>
               'What intrigues me is that laser scanners, like oscilloscopes ...'
              </p>
              <p>
               Oscilloscopes are not laser scanners. They work by firing a beam of electrons onto a phosphorous coated screen (the part you can see). When the phosphorous is hit by the electrons it fluoresces.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscilloscope" rel="nofollow">
                http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscilloscope
