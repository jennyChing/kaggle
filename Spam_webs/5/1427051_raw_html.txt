   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Erds number
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Erds number
      </b>
      (
      <small>
       Hungarian pronunciation:
      </small>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Hungarian" title="Help:IPA for Hungarian">
        [rd]
       </a>
      </span>
      ) describes the "collaborative distance" between mathematician
      <span class="nowrap">
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Erd%C5%91s" title="Paul Erds">
        Paul Erds
       </a>
      </span>
      and another person, as measured by authorship of
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_important_publications_in_mathematics" title="List of important publications in mathematics">
       mathematical papers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The same principle has been applied in other fields where a particular individual has collaborated with a large and broad number of peers. The
      <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">
       American Mathematical Society
      </a>
      provides a free online tool to determine the Erds number of every mathematical author listed in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Reviews" title="Mathematical Reviews">
       Mathematical Reviews
      </a>
      catalogue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erd%C5%91s_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Paul Erds (19131996) was an influential mathematician who spent a large portion of his later life writing papers with a large number of colleagues, working on solutions to outstanding mathematical problems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newman2001_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newman2001-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He published more papers during his lifetime (at least 1,525
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) than any other mathematician in history.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newman2001_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-newman2001-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">
       Leonhard Euler
      </a>
      published more total pages of mathematics but fewer separate papers: about 800.)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Erds spent a large portion of his later life living out of a suitcase, visiting his over 500 collaborators around the world.
     </p>
     <p>
      The idea of the Erds number was originally created by the mathematician's friends as a tribute to his enormous output. However, in later years it gained prominence as a tool to study how mathematicians cooperate to find answers to unsolved problems. Several projects are devoted to studying connectivity among researchers, using the Erds number as a proxy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, Erds
      <a href="/wiki/Collaboration_graph" title="Collaboration graph">
       collaboration graphs
      </a>
      can tell us how authors cluster together, how the number of co-authors per paper evolves over time, or how new theories propagate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Several studies have shown that leading mathematicians tend to have particularly low Erds numbers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trails_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-trails-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, only 134,007 mathematicians have an Erds number, with a median value of 5.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2015)">
          when?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      In contrast, the median Erds number of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fields_Medalists" title="Fields Medalists">
       Fields Medalists
      </a>
      is 3.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Only 7,097 (about 5%) of mathematicians with a collaboration path have an Erds number of 2 or less.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oakland.edu_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-oakland.edu-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2015)">
          when?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      Collaboration distances will necessarily increase over long time scales, as mathematicians with low Erds numbers die and become unavailable for collaboration.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_application_in_mathematics">
       Definition and application in mathematics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erd%C5%91s_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition and application in mathematics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erdosnumber.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="214" data-file-width="487" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Erdosnumber.png/220px-Erdosnumber.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Erdosnumber.png/330px-Erdosnumber.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Erdosnumber.png/440px-Erdosnumber.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erdosnumber.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        If Alice collaborates with Paul Erds on one paper, and with Bob on another, but Bob never collaborates with Erds himself, then Alice is given an Erds number of 1 and Bob is given an Erds number of 2, as he is two steps from Erds.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      To be assigned an Erds number, someone must be a coauthor of a research paper with another person who has a finite Erds number. Paul Erds has an Erds number of zero. Anybody else's Erds number is
      <span class="texhtml">
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       + 1
      </span>
      where
      <span class="texhtml">
       <i>
        k
       </i>
      </span>
      is the lowest Erds number of any coauthor.
     </p>
     <p>
      Erds wrote around 1,500 mathematical articles in his lifetime, mostly co-written. He had 511 direct collaborators;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      these are the people with Erds number 1. The people who have collaborated with them (but not with Erds himself) have an Erds number of 2 (9267 people as of 2010
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project_File_Erdos2_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project_File_Erdos2-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ), those who have collaborated with people who have an Erds number of 2 (but not with Erds or anyone with an Erds number of 1) have an Erds number of 3, and so forth. A person with no such coauthorship chain connecting to Erds has an Erds number of
      <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">
       infinity
      </a>
      (or an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defined_and_undefined" title="Defined and undefined">
       undefined
      </a>
      one). Since the death of Paul Erds, the lowest Erds number that a new researcher can obtain is 2.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is room for ambiguity over what constitutes a link between two authors. The
      <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">
       American Mathematical Society
      </a>
      collaboration distance calculator uses data from
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Reviews" title="Mathematical Reviews">
       Mathematical Reviews
      </a>
      , which includes most mathematics journals but not other subjects, and which also includes some non-research publications. The Erds Number Project web site says:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       ... Our criterion for inclusion of an edge between vertices u and v is some research collaboration between them resulting in a published work. Any number of additional co-authors is permitted,...
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      but they do not include non-research publications such as elementary textbooks, joint editorships, obituaries, and the like. The Erds number of the second kind restricts assignment of Erds numbers to papers with only two collaborators.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Erds number was most likely first defined in print by Casper Goffman, an
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">
       analyst
      </a>
      whose own Erds number is 2.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project_File_Erdos2_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project_File_Erdos2-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Goffman published his observations about Erds' prolific collaboration in a 1969 article entitled "
      <i>
       And what is your Erds number?
      </i>
      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      See also some comments in an obituary by Michael Golomb.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>

     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="348" data-file-width="515" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg/220px-Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg/330px-Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg/440px-Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Erdos_with_Terence_Tao.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Paul Erds teaching
        <a href="/wiki/Terence_Tao" title="Terence Tao">
         Terence Tao
        </a>
        in 1985 at the University of Adelaide. Tao, who was 10 years old at the time, became a professional mathematician. He received the Fields Medal in 2006 and was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 2007. Tao has an Erds number of 2
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Erds numbers have been a part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
       folklore
      </a>
      of mathematicians throughout the world for many years. Among all working mathematicians at the turn of the millennium who have a finite Erds number, the numbers range up to 15, the median is 5, and the mean is 4.65;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Erd.C5.91s_Number_Project-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      almost everyone with a finite Erds number has a number less than 8. Due to the very high frequency of interdisciplinary collaboration in science today, very large numbers of non-mathematicians in many other fields of science also have finite Erds numbers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, political scientist
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Brams" title="Steven Brams">
       Steven Brams
      </a>
      has an Erds number of 2. In biomedical research, it is common for statisticians to be among the authors of publications, and many statisticians can be linked to Erds via
      <a href="/wiki/John_Tukey" title="John Tukey">
       John Tukey
      </a>
      , who has an Erds number of 2. Similarly, the prominent geneticist
      <a href="/wiki/Eric_Lander" title="Eric Lander">
       Eric Lander
      </a>
      and the mathematician
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Kleitman" title="Daniel Kleitman">
       Daniel Kleitman
      </a>
      have collaborated on papers,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and since Kleitman has an Erds number of 1,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a large fraction of the genetics and genomics community can be linked via Lander and his numerous collaborators. Similarly, collaboration with
      <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Simmons" title="Gustavus Simmons">
       Gustavus Simmons
      </a>
      opened the door for
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_by_Erd%C5%91s_number" title="List of people by Erds number">
       Erds numbers
      </a>
      within the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptographic" title="Cryptographic">
       cryptographic
      </a>
      research community, and many
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguists
      </a>
      have finite Erds numbers, many due to chains of collaboration with such notable scholars as
      <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
       Noam Chomsky
      </a>
      (Erds number 4),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/William_Labov" title="William Labov">
       William Labov
      </a>
      (3),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Liberman" title="Mark Liberman">
       Mark Liberman
      </a>
      (3),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Pullum" title="Geoffrey Pullum">
       Geoffrey Pullum
      </a>
      (3),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Sag" title="Ivan Sag">
       Ivan Sag
      </a>
      (4).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are also connections with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts" title="Arts">
       arts
      </a>
      fields.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to Alex Lopez-Ortiz, all the
      <a href="/wiki/Fields_Medal" title="Fields Medal">
       Fields
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nevanlinna_Prize" title="Nevanlinna Prize">
       Nevanlinna prize
      </a>
      winners during the three cycles in 1986 to 1994 have Erds numbers of at most 9.
     </p>
     <p>
      Earlier mathematicians published fewer papers than modern ones, and more rarely published jointly written papers. The earliest person known to have a finite Erds number is either
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dedekind" title="Richard Dedekind">
       Richard Dedekind
      </a>
      (born 1831, Erds number 7) or
      <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Georg_Frobenius" title="Ferdinand Georg Frobenius">
       Ferdinand Georg Frobenius
      </a>
      (born 1849, Erds number 3), depending on the standard of publication eligibility.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It seems that older historic figures such as
      <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">
       Leonhard Euler
      </a>
      (born 1707) do not (yet) have finite Erds numbers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Tompa
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      proposed a
      <a href="/wiki/Directed_graph" title="Directed graph">
       directed graph
      </a>
      version of the Erds number problem, by orienting edges of the collaboration graph from the alphabetically earlier author to the alphabetically later author and defining the
      <i>
       monotone Erds number
      </i>
      of an author to be the length of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longest_path" title="Longest path">
       longest path
      </a>
      from Erds to the author in this directed graph. He finds a path of this type of length 12.
     </p>
     <p>
      Also,
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Barr_(mathematician)" title="Michael Barr (mathematician)">
       Michael Barr
      </a>
      suggests "rational Erds numbers", generalizing the idea that a person who has written p joint papers with Erds should be assigned Erds number 1/p. From the collaboration multigraph of the second kind (although he also has a way to deal with the case of the first kind)with one edge between two mathematicians for
      <i>
       each
      </i>
      joint paper they have producedform an electrical network with a one-ohm resistor on each edge. The total resistance between two nodes tells how "close" these two nodes are.
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been argued that "for an individual researcher, a measure such as Erds number captures the structural properties of [the] network whereas the
      <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index">
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       -index
      </a>
      captures the citation impact of the publications," and that "One can be easily convinced that ranking in coauthorship networks should take into account both measures to generate a realistic and acceptable ranking."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dixit_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dixit-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Several author ranking systems based on
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenvector_centrality" title="Eigenvector centrality">
       eigenvector centrality
      </a>
      have been proposed, for instance the Phys Author Rank Algorithm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dixit_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Dixit-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004 William Tozier, a mathematician with an Erds number of 4, auctioned off a co-authorship on
      <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">
       eBay
      </a>
      , hence providing the buyer with an Erds number of 5. The winning bid of $1031 was posted by a Spanish mathematician, who however did not intend to pay but just placed the bid to stop what he considered a mockery.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">
       Variations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erd%C5%91s_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Variations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A number of variations on the concept have been proposed to apply to other fields.
     </p>
     <p>
      The best known is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacon_number" title="Bacon number">
       Bacon number
      </a>
      (as in the game
      <a href="/wiki/Six_Degrees_of_Kevin_Bacon" title="Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon">
       Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon
      </a>
      ), connecting actors that appeared in a film together to the actor
      <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Bacon" title="Kevin Bacon">
       Kevin Bacon
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Six_Degrees_of_Kevin_Bacon#History" title="Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon">
       It was created in 1994
      </a>
      , 25 years after Goffman's article on the Erds number.
     </p>
     <p>
      A small number of people are connected to both Erds and Bacon and thus have an
      <a href="/wiki/Erd%C5%91s%E2%80%93Bacon_number" title="ErdsBacon number">
       ErdsBacon number
      </a>
      , which combines the two numbers by taking their sum. One example is the actress-mathematician
      <a href="/wiki/Danica_McKellar" title="Danica McKellar">
       Danica McKellar
      </a>
      , best known for playing Winnie Cooper on the TV series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wonder_Years" title="The Wonder Years">
        The Wonder Years
       </a>
      </i>
      . Her Erds number is 4
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and her Bacon number is 2.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The lowest known ErdsBacon number is 3  for
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Kleitman" title="Daniel Kleitman">
       Daniel Kleitman
      </a>
      , a mathematics professor at MIT  his Erds number is 1 and his Bacon number is 2.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Further generalization is possible. For example, ErdsBaconSabbath numbers include the band
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">
       Black Sabbath
      </a>
      in the measure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The lowest known ErdsBaconSabbath number is 8, a value shared by physicist
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">
       Stephen Hawking
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      neuroscientist
      <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Levitin" title="Daniel Levitin">
       Daniel Levitin
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and inventor
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Kurzweil" title="Ray Kurzweil">
       Ray Kurzweil
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      all of whom have an Erds number of 4, a Bacon number of 2, and a Sabbath number of 2.
     </p>
     <p>
      English mathematician
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_Swinnerton-Dyer" title="Peter Swinnerton-Dyer">
       Peter Swinnerton-Dyer
      </a>
      has an Erds-Morphy number of 5.
     </p>
