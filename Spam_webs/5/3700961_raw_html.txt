          <p>
           NFL teams are asking the same question as weekend-warrior runners and cyclists: How much should you push yourself just before the big day?
          </p>
          <p>
           The surging Green Bay Packers have a counterintuitive answer. Its called Feel-Good Friday, a recent Packers creation in which Friday practice is canceled but deep-tissue massages or other treatments are mandatory.
          </p>
          <p>
           Both the Packers and Philadelphia Eagles are also early adopters of a new tacticpracticing the day before a gamethat league insiders say could explode in popularity. This is foreign to the NFL, where the day before kickoff has long been considered a time to do just about nothing.
          </p>
          <p>
           These teams are focusing on how best to train their players in the short window before a game. Its really the last 48 hours of how you go into a game, Packers coach
           <!--
-->
           Mike McCarthy
           <!--
-->
           said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Despite conflicting science on when athletes should train, the results are undeniable: The Packers (7-3) have been unstoppable lately, topping 50 points in college-style routs of the Bears and Eagles, while the Eagles (7-3) are in first in the NFC East. And the players are almost unanimous that the unusual approach has worked.
          </p>
          <p>
           Its something a lot more organizations are watching, said former Tampa Bay Buccaneers general manager Mark Dominik, who is now an analyst for ESPN. The players appreciate it and are staying fairly healthy. Thats the sort of thing that spreads around the league.
          </p>
          <p>
           At the heart of the matter is the NFL practice schedule, which for years has received little examination from coaches and executives.
          </p>
          <p>
           The format for normal weeksin which Sunday is game dayhas long been as follows: Monday is for film review. Tuesday is an off day for players. Wednesday and Thursday are for tougher practices. Friday is a final wrap-up practice.
          </p>
          <p>
           And then there is Saturday, the day before the game. Saturdays have traditionally been reserved for walk-throughs, a final rehearsal of plays that is usually done in sweatpants and is never taxing on the body. Many teams Saturday walk-throughs are so basic and low-intensity that they take place in hotel conference rooms. There are few exceptions to these rules.
          </p>
          <!--
-->

          <p>
           When
           <!--
-->
           Chip Kelly
           <!--
-->
           became the Eagles coach in 2013, he brought with him studies about player training habits. He installed Monday as an off day in order to guarantee rest and recovery. Then he tacked on a Saturday practice to the normal schedule, because, Kelly said, players should get the body moving before a game. (The Packers kept the regular Tuesday off day.)
          </p>
          <p>
           A fast practice the day before a game is unconventional in most sports. Basketball practices are squeezed in between gamesand even then, a team could go a week without a practice if there is a particularly hectic stretch. European soccer players rarely go full-speed outside of games. In football, an emphasis is put on recovery, so coaches have been keen on letting players lounge around.
          </p>
          <p>
           The Packers adopted a practice model similar to the Eagles this year, except they introduced a new tactic 48 hours before the game.
          </p>
          <p>
           On Fridays, there would be no practice. Instead, Packers players are given deep-tissue massages or a scar-tissue treatment known as the Graston technique, which involves steel rubbing tools to stomp out muscle injuries. There are also team meetings, according to a team official. Players have a fast practice on Saturday, but without pads. McCarthy, the coach, also has introduced longer water breaks.
          </p>
          <p>
           Despite research into the topic, there is still no scientific consensus on when athletes should rest or train before a big event. Cyclists, for instance, are known to push themselves hard the day before a race. Marathoners taper before a race but can jog or do sprints the day before.
          </p>
          <!--

          <p>
           Experts say that nearly all training within a day of a game should be focused on replenishment of glycogen, a carbohydrate storage material. Practicing the day before a game could in fact put a team at a disadvantage, said Shawn Arent, an associate professor in the department of exercise science and sport studies at Rutgers University.
          </p>
          <p>
           Carbohydrate is still king when it comes to fuel source, so if theyve depleted that, it would be the equivalent of a Nascar driver starting with half a tank of gas, Arent said. It takes 24 hours to replenish those stores.
          </p>
          <p>
           Arent said that the one safeguard teams have against working players too hard is their use of technology. Both the Eagles and Packers use GPS tracking on players that can monitor fatigue and send up-to-the-minute reports about a players health.
          </p>
          <p>
           A lot of coaches say, We need to work harder, where the better coaches say, Maybe we are working too hard, Arent said. Ive seen a lot of players who cant get into their upper heart ranges because they are exhausted. If a coach says, Weve reached our threshold for the week; time to back off, Im a big fan of that.
          </p>

          <p>
           The Eagles Kelly said that he is constantly monitoring and adjusting his techniques.
          </p>
          <p>
           Not everyone has been a fan of the new training methods. Earlier this season, Eagles cornerback Cary Williams ripped the teams practices as too grueling. Kelly countered by arguing that the Eagles are the leagues freshest team in the fourth quarter. Practices have been shortened slightly since then, something Eagles coaches said was part of the plan all along.
          </p>
          <p>
           Chip is doing what Chip always does: He reads the information. He gets the feedback from every player, from all the data he gets, all the different sports science things we do, said Eagles defensive coordinator Bill Davis. He adjusts daily, weekly. Were always moving the target to make sure that the players are at the optimal spot on Sunday.
          </p>
