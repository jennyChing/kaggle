     <section id="article-section-1">
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:68.4210526316%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/WaldseemullerLead/2e132d612.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        The rendering of America and Africa in Martin Waldseemuller's 1507 world map (Wikimedia Commons)
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       In June 2012, Brian McClendon, an executive at Google, announced that Google Maps and Google Earth were part of a far loftier pursuit than edging out Apple and Facebook in the
       <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/05/16/technology/google-escalates-the-competition-in-map-services.html">
        map services market
       </a>
       . Google, McClendon wrote in a
       <a href="http://google-latlong.blogspot.com/2012/06/never-ending-quest-for-perfect-map.html">
        blog post
       </a>
       , was engaged in nothing less than a "never-ending quest for the perfect map."
      </p>
      <p>
       "Weve been building a comprehensive base map of the entire globebased on public and commercial data, imagery from every level (satellite, aerial and street level) and the collective knowledge of our millions of users," McClendon noted. By strapping cameras to the backs of intrepid hikers, mobilizing users to fact-check map data, and modeling the world in 3D, he added, Google was moving one step closer to mapmaking perfection.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was the kind of technological triumphalism that Jerry Brotton would likely greet with a knowing smile.
      </p>
     </section>

      <div class="ad" data-ad-starts-hidden="true" data-injected-ad="true" id="ad-boxinjector1">

         </a>

      </div>
     </div>
     <section id="article-section-2">
      <p>
       "All cultures have always believed that the map they valorize is real and true and objective and transparent," Brotton, a professor of Renaissance studies at Queen Mary University of London, told me. "All maps are always subjective....Even todays online geospatial applications on all your mobile devices and tablets, be they produced by Google or Apple or whoever, are still to some extent subjective maps."
      </p>
      <p>
       There are, in other words, no perfect mapsjust maps that (more-or-less) perfectly capture our understanding of the world at discrete moments in time. In his new book,
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/A-History-World-12-Maps/dp/product-description/0670023396/ref=dp_proddesc_0?ie=UTF8&amp;n=283155&amp;s=books">
        <em>
         A History of the World in 12 Maps
        </em>
       </a>
       , Brotton masterfully catalogs the maps that tell us most about pivotal periods in human history. I asked him to walk me through the 12 maps he selected (you can click on each map below to enlarge it).
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         1. Cartography's Foundation: Ptolemy's
         <em>
          Geography
         </em>
         (150 AD)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <figure>
        <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg">
         <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:71.7543859649%;">
          <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Ptolemy_World2/67ca95ab1.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
         </picture>
        </a>
        <figcaption class="caption">
         A 15th-century reconstruction based on Ptolemy's projections of the world (Wikimedia Commons)
        </figcaption>
       </figure>
       <p>
        Humans have been sketching maps
        <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/worldnews/europe/spain/5978900/Worlds-oldest-map-Spanish-cave-has-landscape-from-14000-years-ago.html">
         for millennia
        </a>
        , but Claudius Ptolemy was the first to use math and geometry to develop a manual for how to map the planet using a rectangle and intersecting linesone that
        <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PtolemyWorldMap.jpg">
         resurfaced in 13th-century Byzantium
        </a>
        and was used until the early 17th century. The Alexandria-based Greek scholar, who may never have drawn a map himself, described the latitude and longitude of more than 8,000 locations in Europe, Asia, and Africa, projecting a north-oriented, Mediterranean-focused world that was missing the Americas, Australasia, southern Africa (you can see Africa skirting the bottom of the map and then blending into Asia), the Far East, the Pacific Ocean, and most of the Atlantic Ocean. Ptolemy's
        <a href="http://www.ibiblio.org/expo/vatican.exhibit/exhibit/d-mathematics/Ptolemy_geo.html">
         <em>
          Geography
         </em>
        </a>
        was a "book with a 1,500-year legacy," Brotton says.
       </p>
      </figure>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         2. Cultural Exchange: Al-Idrisi's
         <em>
          <em>
          </em>
         </em>
         World Map (1154)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <figure class="right">
        <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Al-Idrisi%27s_world_map.JPG">
         <picture class="right" style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:107.543859649%;">
          <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Idrisi1/6ecf73953.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
         </picture>
        </a>
        <figcaption class="credit">
         Wikimedia Commons
        </figcaption>
       </figure>
       <figcaption class="caption">
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Al-Sharif al-Idrisi, a Muslim from Al-Andalus, traveled to Sicily to work for the Norman King Roger II, producing anArabic-language geography guide that drew on Jewish, Greek, Christian, and Islamic traditions and contained two world maps: the small, circular one above, and 70 regional maps that
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/TabulaRogeriana_upside-down.jpg">
        could be stitched together
       </a>
       . Unlike east-oriented Christian world maps at the time, al-Idrisi's map puts south at top in the tradition of Muslim mapmakers, who considered Mecca due south (Africa is the crescent-shaped landmass at top, and the Arabian Peninsula is in the center). Unlike Ptolemy, al-Idrisi depicted a circumnavigable Africablue sea surrounds the globe. Ultimately, the map is concerned with representing physical geography and blending traditionsnot mathematics or religion. "There are no monsters on his maps," Brotton says.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         3. Christian Faith: Hereford's
         <em>
          Mappa Mundi
         </em>
         (1300)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/92/Hereford_Mappa_Mundi.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:112.98245614%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/MappaMundi/0ca93e6c9.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Wikimedia Commons
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       This map from England's Hereford Cathedral depicts "what the world looked like to medieval Christians," Brotton says. The organizing principle in the east-oriented map is time, not space, and specifically biblical time; with Christ looming over the globe, the viewer travels spiritually from the Garden of Eden at top down to the Pillars of Hercules near the Strait of Gibralter at bottom (for a more detailed tour, check out this
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mappa_mundi_Hereford_1300_explained.png">
        handy guide
       </a>
       to the map's landmarks). At the center is Jerusalem, marked with a crucifix, and to the right is Africa, whose coast is dotted with grotesque monsters in the margins. "Once you get to the edges of what you know, those are dangerous places," Brotton explains.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         4. Imperial Politics: Kwon Kun's Kangnido Map (1402)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/KangnidoMap.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:92.6315789474%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Kangnido/b8bd61ae3.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Wikimedia Commons
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       What's most striking about this Korean map, designed by a team of royal astronomers led by Kwon Kun, is that north is at top. "It's strange because the first map that looks recognizable to us as a Western map is a map from Korea in 1402," Brotton notes. He chalks this up to power politics in the region at the time. "In South Asian and Chinese imperial ideology, you look up northwards in respect to the emperor, and the emperor looks south to his subjects," Brotton explains. Europe is a "tiny, barbaric speck" in the upper left, with a circumnavigable Africa below (it's unclear whether the dark shading in the middle of Africa represents a lake or a desert). The Arabian Peninsula is to Africa's right, and India is barely visible. China is the gigantic blob at the center of the map, with Korea, looking disproportionately large, to its right and the island of Japan in the bottom right.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         5. Territorial Exploration: Waldseemuller's
         <em>
          Universalis Cosmographia
         </em>
         (1507)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7c/UniversalisCosmographia.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:55.6140350877%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Waldseemuller1/78140721b.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Viking/Penguin
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       This work by the German cartographer Martin Waldseemuller is considered the most expensive map in the world because, as Brotton notes, it is "America's birth certificate"a distinction that prompted the Library of Congress to
       <a href="http://www.loc.gov/today/pr/2003/03-110.html">
        buy it from a German prince
       </a>
       for $10 million. It is the first map to recognize the Pacific Ocean and the separate continent of "America," which Waldseemuller named in honor of the then-still-living Amerigo Vespucci, who identified the Americas as a distinct landmass (Vespucci and Ptolemy appear at the top of the map). The map consists of 12 woodcuts and incorporates many of the latest discoveries by European explorers (you get the sense that the woodcutter was asked at the last minute to make room for the Cape of Good Hope). "This is the moment when the world goes bang, and all these discoveries are made over a short period of time," Brotton says.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         6. Politicized Geography: Ribeiro's World Map (1529)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/Map_Diego_Ribero_1539.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:57.7192982456%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Ribero/b8794ef7b.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Wikimedia Commons
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       The Portuguese cartographer Diogo Ribeiro composed this map amid a bitter dispute between Spain and Portugal over the Moluccas, an island chain in present-day Indonesia and hub for the spice trade (in 1494, the two countries had
       <a href="http://www.princeton.edu/~achaney/tmve/wiki100k/docs/Treaty_of_Tordesillas.html">
        signed a treaty
       </a>
       dividing the world's newly discovered lands in two). After Ferdinand Magellan's expedition
       <a href="http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/356525/Ferdinand-Magellan/225336/Circumnavigation-of-the-globe">
        circumnavigated the globe
       </a>
       for the first time in 1522, Ribeiro, working for the Spanish crown, placed the "Spice Islands," inaccurately, just inside the Spanish half of his seemingly scientific world maps. Ribeiro may have known that the islands (which appear on the far-left and far-right sides of the map) actually belonged to Portugal, but he also knew who paid the bills. "This is the first great example of politics manipulating geography," Brotton says.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         7. Territorial Navigation: Mercator's World Map (1569)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/Mercator_1569.png">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:63.6842105263%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Mercator/a5917fdb8.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Wikimedia Commons
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Next to Ptolemy, Brotton says, Gerardus Mercator is the most influential figure in the history of mapmaking. The Flemish-German cartographer tried "on a flat piece of paper to mimic the curvature of the earths surface," permitting "him to draw a straight line from, say, Lisbon to the West Coast of the States and maintain an active line of bearing." Mercator, who was imprisoned by Catholic authorities for alleged Lutheran heresy, designed his map for European navigators. But Brotton thinks it had a higher purpose as well. "I think its a map about stoicism and transcendence," he says. "If you look at the world from several thousands miles up, at all these conflicts in religious and political life, youre like ants running around." Mercator has been accused of Eurocentrism, since his projection, which is still occasionally used today, increasingly distorts territory as you go further north and south from the equator. Brotton dismisses this view, arguing that Europe isn't even at the center of the map.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <big>
        <strong>
         8. Commercial Cartography: Blaeu's
         <em>
          Atlas maior
         </em>
         (1662)
        </strong>
       </big>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/Nova_et_Accuratissima_Terrarum_Orbis_Tabula_%28J.Blaeu%2C_1664%29.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:75.6140350877%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Atlas_maior/287f79188.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Viking/Penguin
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Working for the Dutch East India Company, Joan Blaeu produced a vast atlas with hundreds of baroque maps gracing thousands of pages. "He's the last of a tradition: the single, brilliant, magician-like mapmaker who says, 'I can magically show you the entire world,'" Brotton says. "By the late 17th century, with joint stock companies mapping every corner of the world, anonymous teams of people are crunching data and producing maps." Blaeu's market-oriented maps weren't cutting-edge. But he did break with a mapmaking tradition dating back to Ptolemy of placing the earth at the center of the universe. At the top of the map, the sun is at the center of personifications of the five known planets at the timein a nod to Copernicus's theory of the cosmos, even as the earth, divided into two hemispheres, remains at the center of the map, in deference to Ptolemy (Ptolemy is in the upper left, and Copernicus in the upper right). "Blau quietly, cautiously says I think Copernicus is probably right," Brotton says.
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <big>
        <strong>
         9. National Mapping: Cassini's Map of France (1744)
        </strong>
       </big>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://lcweb2.loc.gov/gmd/gmd5m/g5830m/g5830m/ghl00001/ghl00001.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:88.2456140351%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Cassini/3131230d0.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Library of Congress
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Beginning under Louis XIV, four generations of the Cassini family presided over the first attempt to survey and map every meter of a country. The Cassinis used the science of triangulation to create this nearly 200-sheet topographic map, which French revolutionaries nationalized in the late 18th century. This, Brotton says, "is the birth of what we understand as modern nation-state mapping ... whereas, before, mapmaking was in private hands. Now, in the Google era, mapmaking is again going into private hands."
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <big>
        <strong>
         10. Geopolitics: Mackinder's 'Geographical Pivot of History' (1904)
        </strong>
       </big>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9c/Heartland.png">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:64.7368421053%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Mackinder/9d31a4d8f.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Viking/Penguin
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Don't let the modesty of this "little line drawing" fool you, Brotton says: It "basically created the whole notion that politics is driven to some extent by geographic issues." The English geographer and imperialist Halford Mackinder included the drawing in a paper arguing that Russia and Central Asia constituted "the pivot of the world's politics." Brotton believes this ideathat control of certain pivotal regions can translate into international hegemonyhas influenced figures ranging from
       <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/history/worldwars/wwtwo/hitler_lebensraum_01.shtml">
        the Nazis
       </a>
       to
       <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nations_of_Nineteen_Eighty-Four">
        George Orwell
       </a>
       to
       <a href="http://strategicstudiesinstitute.army.mil/pubs/parameters/articles/00summer/fettweis.htm">
        Henry Kissinger
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         11. Geoactivism: Peters's Projection (1973)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <a href="http://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/34/Gall%E2%80%93Peters_projection_SW.jpg">
        <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:63.8596491228%;">
         <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Peters/9b27ad1d5.jpg" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
        </picture>
       </a>
       <figcaption class="caption">
        <p style="text-align: right;">
         Wikimedia Commons
        </p>
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       In 1973, the left-wing German historian Arno Peters unveiled an alternative to Mercator's allegedly Eurocentric projection: a world map depicting countries and continents according to their actual surface areahence the smaller-than-expected northern continents, and Africa and South America appearing, in Brotton's words, "like long, distended tear drops." The 'equal area' projection, which was nearly identical to an earlier design by the Scottish clergyman James Gall, was a hit with the press and progressive NGOs. But critics argued that any projection of a spherical surface onto a plane surface involves distortions, and that Peters had amplified these by committing serious mathematical errors. "No map is any better or worse than any other map," Brotton says. "It's just about what agenda it pursues."
      </p>

      <hr/>
      <p>
       <strong>
        <big>
         12. Virtual Mapping: Google Earth (2005)
        </big>
       </strong>
      </p>
      <figure style="max-width: 570.0px;">
       <picture style="position:relative; width:100%; height:0; overflow:hidden; padding-bottom:67.8947368421%;">
        <img alt="" class="lazyload" data-src="http://cdn.theatlantic.com/assets/media/img/posts/2013/12/Screen_Shot_2013_12_26_at_11.01.01_PM/bd35ba12d.png" style="position:absolute; width:100%; height:100%; top:0; left:0; border:0;"/>
       </picture>
       <figcaption class="caption">
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Google is at the forefront of innovations in digital mapmaking, Brotton says. But he also notes that the company sees maps as an adjunct to search and advertising. "My question is what gets on maps, who pays to get on maps now, and who can't pay and is therefore not on maps?" he asks. Back in Mercator's day, source code consisted of the projection the cartographer used and the data he fed into it. Now, Brotton notes, we don't know what source code Google and other online mapping applications are using. And this at a time when Google, which offers users
       <a href="http://www.computerweekly.com/feature/What-does-a-petabyte-look-like">
        more than 20 petabytes
       </a>
       of imagery, is working with far more material than a country can match. "Companies can now produce maps in more detail than, say, the U.K. Ordnance Survey, but without any peer-observation process," Brotton asserts.
      </p>
      <p>
       "We always get the map that our age deserves," he adds.
      </p>

        <p class="dek">
         The nuclear agreement highlights the limits of American powersomething the presidents opponents wont accept.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          Mankind faces a crossroads,
          <a href="http://www.skeptic.ca/Woody_Allen_Speech_to_Graduates.htm">
           declared
          </a>
          Woody Allen. One path leads to despair and utter hopelessness. The other, to total extinction. Let us pray we have the wisdom to choose correctly.
         </p>
         <p>
          The point is simple: In life, what matters most isnt how a decision compares to your ideal outcome. Its how it compares to
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/alternatives-obama-iran-nuclear-deal-israel/389751/">
           the alternative at hand
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          The same is true for the Iran deal,
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/world-middle-east-33518524">
           announced Tuesday
          </a>
          <span>
           between Iran and six world powers
          </span>
          . As Congress begins debating the agreement, its opponents have three real alternatives. The first is to kill the deal, and the
          <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/international/archive/2015/04/the-iran-nuclear-deal-by-the-numbers/389592/">
           interim agreement
          </a>
          that preceded it, and do nothing else, which means few restraints on Irans nuclear program. The second is war. But top American and Israeli officials have warned that military action against Iranian nuclear facilities could ignite a catastrophic regional conflict and would be ineffective, if not counterproductive, in delaying Irans path to the bomb. Meir Dagan, who oversaw the Iran file as head of Israels external spy agency, the Mossad, from 2002 to 2011,
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/06/04/world/middleeast/04mossad.html">
           has said
          </a>
          an attack would mean regional war, and in that case you would have given Iran the best possible reason to continue the nuclear program. Michael Hayden, who ran the CIA under George W. Bush from 2006 to 2009,
          <a href="http://foreignpolicy.com/2012/01/19/bushs-cia-director-we-determined-attacking-iran-was-a-bad-idea/">
           has warned
          </a>
          that an attack would guarantee that which we are trying to prevent: an Iran that will spare nothing to build a nuclear weapon.

        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          On Tuesday, following nearly two-and-a-half years of negotiations, a nuclear agreement was struck between Iran and six world powers. Under the accord 
          <span>

          </span>
          <span>
           United Nations
          </span>

          <a href="http://www.aljazeera.com/news/middleeast/2015/04/world-leaders-react-iran-nuclear-deal-150403020548163.html">
           reflecting President Vladimir Putins suggestions
          </a>
          . Israel was among the most vocal in its opposition to the framework,
          <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2015/04/05/politics/netanyahu-iran-deal/">
           calling it a a very bad deal,
          </a>
          while Saudi Arabia and the Gulf Cooperation Council states were less vocal, but also displeased. Others expressed pessimism that the deal could last.
          <span>
           In the United States, many Republicans blasted the framework, while some Democrats were nervous enough about it to vote with their GOP colleagues on a
           <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/04/07/us-iran-nuclear-congress-idUSKBN0MY2A020150407">

        <h3 class="hed">
         The Voyage of New Horizons: Jupiter, Pluto, and Beyond
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/alan-taylor/" title="Alan Taylor">
          Alan Taylor
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         <span>
          After traveling more than 3 billion miles over the past nine and a half years, NASAs New Horizons spacecraft is within hours of its rendezvous with Pluto.
         </span>
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p>
          After traveling nearly 3 billion miles over the past nine and a half years, NASAs New Horizons spacecraft is within hours of its rendezvous with Pluto. Back in 2006, when the space probe was launched, Pluto was classified as the ninth planet in the solar system, and was known to have three moons. During the long journey to this distant icy world, Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet, one of many smaller bodies orbiting the sun, and another two moons were discovered. In 2007, New Horizons flew past Jupiter and its moons on the way to Pluto, capturing many spectacular images. On the morning of July 14, 2015 (Earth time, Western Hemisphere), New Horizons will speed past the Pluto system at
          <span>
           about 9
          </span>
          <span>
           miles
          </span>
          <span>
           (14 kilometers) per second, making as many observations as possible. In the hours and days following, it will be sending the data to Earth, on its way to the
          </span>
          Kuiper belt, with plans to target another smaller body sometime around 2018. (More Pluto images will be added here as they become available.)
         </p>

He was filmed walking through his peanut farm in Plains, Georgia. Today, the improbability of his election to the presidency has faded from public memory, but it ranks as one of the most remarkable success stories in national politics.

He was a relentless campaigner, and left other Democratic primary contenders in his wake.         <h3 class="hed">
         Shoppers Buy More Junk Food When They Bring Their Own Bags
        </h3>
       </a>
       <ul class="metadata">
        <li class="byline">
         <a href="/author/joe-pinsker/" title="Joe Pinsker">
          Joe Pinsker
         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <div class="article-content ">
        <p class="dek">
         Taking along a canvas tote changes what people purchase.
        </p>
        <div class="excerpt">
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            Plastic-bag bans are currently on the books in
           </span>
           <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/01/30/nyregion/with-sweeping-plastics-ban-new-york-village-joins-environmental-vanguard.html?ref=topics">
            <span>
             about a dozen U.S. cities
            </span>
           </a>
           <span>
            a dozen cities that will be seeing fewer bags clustered in rivers, tangled in tree branches, and clogging storm drains. Those cities might also start noticing an uptick in junk-food purchases.
           </span>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p dir="ltr">
          <span>
           <span>
            Thats the implication of
            <a href="http://journals.ama.org/doi/abs/10.1509/jm.13.0228">
             a study
            </a>
            that tried to figure out whether peoples shopping habits changed when they brought their own grocery bags. For the study, Uma Karmarkar, a professor of marketing at Harvard Business School, and Bryan Bollinger, a professor of marketing at Dukes Fuqua School of Business, analyzed loyalty-card data from some 140,000 trips to a single grocery store in California in the mid-2000s. They found that people are 13 percent more likely to buy organic products when they bring their own bagsand theyre also 7 percent more likely to buy junk food, such as chips and cookies.
           </span>
          </span>
