                     <font size="+1">
                      MAC FLECKNOE
                     </font>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <b>
                      <i>
                       <font face="Arial" size="-1">
                        by: John Dryden
                       </font>
                      </i>
                     </b>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                    </p>

                  <ul>
                   <ul>
                    <dl>
                     <dt>
                      <img align="BOTTOM" border="0" height="24" naturalsizeflag="3" src="../a_pic.gif" width="26"/>
                      LL human things are subject to
                      decay,
                      <dt>
                       And, when fate summons, monarchs must obey.
                       <dt>
                        This Flecknoe found, who, like Augustus, young
                        <dt>
                         Was called to empire, and had governed long;
                         <dt>
                          In prose and verse was found without dispute,
                          <dt>
                           Through all the realms of Nonsense, absolute.
                           <dt>
                            This agd prince, now flourished in peace,
                            <dt>
                             And blessed with issue of a large increase,
                             <dt>
                              Worn out with business, did at length debate
                              <dt>
                               To settle the succession of the state;
                               <dt>
                                And, pondering which of all his sons was fit
                                <dt>
                                 To reign, and wage immortal war with wit,
                                 <dt>
                                  Cried,--"'Tis resolved! for nature pleads, that he
                                  <dt>
                                   Should only rule, who most resembles me.
                                   <dt>
                                    Shadwell alone my perfect image bears,
                                    <dt>
                                     Mature in dulness from his tender years;
                                     <dt>
                                      Shadwell alone, of all my sons, is he,
                                      <dt>
                                       Who stands confirmed in full stupidity.
                                       <dt>
                                        The rest to some faint meaning make pretence,
                                        <dt>
                                         But Shadwell never deviates into sense;
                                         <dt>
                                          Some beams of wit on other souls may fall,
                                          <dt>
                                           Strike through, and make a lucid interval;
                                           <dt>
                                            But Shadwell's genuine night admits no ray,
                                            <dt>
                                             His rising fogs prevail upon the day.
                                             <dt>
                                              Besides, his goodly fabric fills the eye,
                                              <dt>
                                               And seems designed for thoughtless majesty;
                                               <dt>
                                                Thoughtless as monarch oaks, that shade the plain,
                                                <dt>
                                                 And, spread in solemn state, supinely reign.
                                                 <dt>
                                                  Heywood and Shirley were but types of thee,
                                                  <dt>
                                                   Thou last great prophet of tautology!
                                                   <dt>
                                                    Even I, a dunce of more renown than they,
                                                    <dt>
                                                     Was sent before but to prepare the way;
                                                     <dt>
                                                      And, coarsely clad in Norwich drugget, came
                                                      <dt>
                                                       To teach the nation in thy greater name."
                                                      </dt>
                                                     </dt>
                                                    </dt>
