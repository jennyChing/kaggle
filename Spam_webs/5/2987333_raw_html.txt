     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ochre_and_the_earth_pigments">
       Ochre and the earth pigments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ochre and the earth pigments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Ochre is a family of earth pigments, which includes yellow ochre, red ochre, purple ochre, sienna, and umber. The major ingredient of all the ochres is
      <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide-hydroxide" title="Iron(III) oxide-hydroxide">
       iron(III) oxide-hydroxide
      </a>
      , known as
      <a href="/wiki/Limonite" title="Limonite">
       limonite
      </a>
      , which gives them a yellow color.
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <b>
        Yellow ochre
       </b>
       , FeO(OH)nH
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O, is a
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">
        hydrated
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_hydroxide" title="Iron hydroxide">
        iron hydroxide
       </a>
       (limonite) also called
       <b>
        gold ochre
       </b>
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Red ochre
       </b>
       , Fe
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       , takes its reddish color from the mineral
       <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">
        hematite
       </a>
       , which is an
       <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">
        anhydrous
       </a>
       iron oxide.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Purple ochre
       </b>
       , is identical to red ochre chemically but of a different hue caused by different light diffraction properties associated with a greater average particle size.
      </li>
      <li>
       <b>
        Brown ochre,
       </b>
       also FeO(OH), (
       <a href="/wiki/Goethite" title="Goethite">
        goethite
       </a>
       ), is a partly hydrated iron oxide.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Sienna" title="Sienna">
        Sienna
       </a>
       contains both limonite and a small amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Manganese_oxide" title="Manganese oxide">
        manganese oxide
       </a>
       (less than five percent), which makes it darker than ochre.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Umber" title="Umber">
        Umber
       </a>
       pigments contain a larger proportion of manganese (five to twenty percent) which make them a dark brown.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roelofs_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-roelofs-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      When natural sienna and umber pigments are heated, they are dehydrated and some of the limonite is transformed into hematite, giving them more reddish colors, called burnt sienna and burnt umber.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ochres are
      <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">
       non-toxic
      </a>
      , and can be used to make an oil paint that dries quickly and covers surfaces thoroughly.
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern ochre pigments often are made using synthetic iron oxide. Pigments which use natural ochre pigments indicate it with the name PY-43 (Pigment yellow 43) on the label, following the
      <a href="/wiki/Colour_Index_International" title="Colour Index International">
       Colour Index International
      </a>
      system.
     </p>
     <ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">
      <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">
       <div style="width: 155px">
        <div class="thumb" style="width: 150px;">
         <div style="margin:24px auto;">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LimoniteUSGOV.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="360" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/LimoniteUSGOV.jpg/120px-LimoniteUSGOV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/LimoniteUSGOV.jpg/180px-LimoniteUSGOV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/LimoniteUSGOV.jpg/240px-LimoniteUSGOV.jpg 2x" width="120"/>
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="gallerytext">
         <p>
          Limonite, a mineraloid containing iron hydroxide, is the main ingredient of all the ochre pigments.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
      </li>
      <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">
       <div style="width: 155px">
        <div class="thumb" style="width: 150px;">
         <div style="margin:17px auto;">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hematite.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="1581" data-file-width="1641" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Hematite.jpg/120px-Hematite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Hematite.jpg/180px-Hematite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Hematite.jpg/240px-Hematite.jpg 2x" width="120"/>
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="gallerytext">
         <p>
          Hematite is a more reddish variety of iron oxide, and is the main ingredient of red ochre. When limonite is roasted, it turns partially to the more reddish hematite and becomes red ochre or burnt sienna.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
      </li>
      <li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px">
       <div style="width: 155px">
        <div class="thumb" style="width: 150px;">
         <div style="margin:34px auto;">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goethite-190288.jpg">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_history.2C_art_and_culture">
       Use in history, art and culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in history, art and culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory_and_early_history">
       Prehistory and early history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prehistory and early history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       The use of ochre is particularly intensive: it is not unusual to find a layer of the cave floor impregnated with a purplish red to a depth of eight inches. The size of these ochre deposits raises a problem not yet solved. The colouring is so intense that practically all the loose ground seems to consist of ochre. One can imagine that the Aurignacians regularly painted their bodies red, dyed their animal skins, coated their weapons, and sprinkled the ground of their dwellings, and that a paste of ochre was used for decorative purposes in every phase of their domestic life. We must assume no less, if we are to account for the veritable mines of ochre on which some of them lived...
       <p style="text-align: right;">
        
        <span class="citation">
         <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Leroi-Gourhan" title="Andr Leroi-Gourhan">
          Leroi-Gourhan, A
         </a>
         . 1968.
         <i>
          The Art of Prehistoric Man in Western Europe.
         </i>
         London: Thames &amp; Hudson, p. 40.
        </span>
        <span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AOchre&amp;rft.btitle=Leroi-Gourhan%2C+A.+1968.+The+Art+of+Prehistoric+Man+in+Western+Europe.+London%3A+Thames+%26+Hudson%2C+p.+40.&amp;rft.genre=book&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook">
         <span style="display:none;">
         </span>
        </span>
       </p>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Iron oxide is one of the most common minerals found on earth, and there is much evidence that yellow and red ochre pigment was used in prehistoric and ancient times by many different civilizations on different continents. Pieces of ochre engraved with abstract designs have been found at the site of the
      <a href="/wiki/Blombos_Cave" title="Blombos Cave">
       Blombos Cave
      </a>
      in South Africa, dated to around 75,000 years ago.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Paintings of animals made with red and yellow ochre pigments have been found in
      <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">
       neolithic
      </a>
      sites at
      <a href="/wiki/Pech_Merle" title="Pech Merle">
       Pech Merle
      </a>
      in France (ca. 25,000 years old), and the
      <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Altamira" title="Cave of Altamira">
       cave of Altamira
      </a>
      in Spain (ca. 15,000-16,500 BC). The cave of
      <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">
       Lascaux
      </a>
      has an image of a horse colored with yellow ochre estimated to be 17,300 years old.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to some scholars, Neolithic burials used red ochre pigments symbolically, either to represent a return to the earth or possibly as a form of ritual rebirth, in which the color symbolizes blood and the
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Great_Goddess" title="Great Goddess">
       Great Goddess
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">
       Ancient Egypt
      </a>
      , yellow was associated with gold, which was considered to be eternal and indestructible. The skin and bones of the gods were believed to be made of gold. The Egyptians used yellow ochre extensively in tomb painting, though occasionally they used
      <a href="/wiki/Orpiment" title="Orpiment">
       orpiment
      </a>
      , which made a brilliant color but was highly toxic, since it was made with
      <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">
       arsenic
      </a>
      . In tomb paintings, men were always shown with brown faces, women with yellow ochre or gold faces.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Red ochre in Ancient Egypt was used as a rouge, or lip gloss for women.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ochre-colored lines were also discovered on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unfinished_Obelisk" title="Unfinished Obelisk">
       Unfinished Obelisk
      </a>
      at the northern region of the
      <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">
       Aswan
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Stone_quarries_of_ancient_Egypt" title="Stone quarries of ancient Egypt">
       Stone Quarry
      </a>
      , marking work sites. Ochre clays were also used
      <a href="/wiki/Medicinal_clay" title="Medicinal clay">
       medicinally
      </a>
      in Ancient Egypt: such use is described in the
      <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">
       Ebers Papyrus
      </a>
      from Egypt, dating to about 1550 BC.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ochre was the most commonly used pigment for painting walls in the ancient Mediterranean world. In
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       Ancient Greece
      </a>
      , red ochre was called ,
      <i>
       mltos
      </i>
      (hence
      <a href="/wiki/Miltiades_(name)" title="Miltiades (name)">
       Miltiades
      </a>
      , red-haired or ruddy). In Athens when
      <a href="/wiki/Ecclesia_(ancient_Athens)" title="Ecclesia (ancient Athens)">
       Assembly
      </a>
      was called, a contingent of public slaves would sweep the open space of the
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Agora_of_Athens" title="Ancient Agora of Athens">
       Agora
      </a>
      with ropes dipped in
      <i>
       miltos
      </i>
      : those citizens that loitered there instead of moving to the
      <a href="/wiki/Pnyx" title="Pnyx">
       Assembly area
      </a>
      would risk having their clothes stained with the paint. This prevented them from wearing these clothes in public again, as failure to attend the Assembly incurred a fine. It was also known as "raddle", "reddle" or "ruddle"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was used to mark sheep and can also be used as a waxy waterproof coating on structures. The reddle was sold as a ready-made mixture to farmers and herders by travelling workers called reddlemen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A reddleman named Diggory Venn was prominently described in
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">
       Thomas Hardy
      </a>
      's 1878 novel entitled
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_Native" title="The Return of the Native">
        The Return of the Native
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In classical antiquity, the finest red ochre came from a Greek colony on the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
       Black Sea
      </a>
      where the modern city of
      <a href="/wiki/Sinop,_Turkey" title="Sinop, Turkey">
       Sinop
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
       Turkey
      </a>
      is located. It was carefully regulated, expensive and marked by a special seal, and this color was called sealed Sinope. Later the Latin and Italian name
      <a href="/wiki/Sinopia" title="Sinopia">
       sinopia
      </a>
      was given to wide range of dark red ochre pigments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Romans used yellow ochre in their paintings to represent gold and skin tones, and as a background color. It is found frequently in the murals of
      <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">
       Pompeii
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">
       Picts
      </a>
      were said to paint themselves "Iron Red" according to the
      <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">
       Gothic
      </a>
      historian
      <a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">
       Jordanes
      </a>
      . Frequent references in Irish myth to "red men" (
      <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_languages" title="Goidelic languages">
       Gaelic
      </a>
      : Fer Dearg) make it likely that such a practice was common to the
      <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">
       Celts
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
       British Isles
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bog_iron" title="Bog iron">
       bog iron
      </a>
      being particularly abundant in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midlands_of_Ireland" title="Midlands of Ireland">
       midlands of Ireland
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ochre_in_the_Renaissance">
       Ochre in the Renaissance
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ochre in the Renaissance">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the Renaissance, yellow and red ochre pigments were widely used in painting panels and frescoes. The colors vary greatly from region to region, depending upon whether the local clay was richer in yellowish limonite or reddish hematite. The red earth from
      <a href="/wiki/Pozzuoli" title="Pozzuoli">
       Pozzuoli
      </a>
      near Naples was a salmon pink, while the pigment from
      <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
       Tuscany
      </a>
      contained manganese, making it a darker reddish brown called terra di siena, or Siana earth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 15th century painter
      <a href="/wiki/Cennino_Cennini" title="Cennino Cennini">
       Cennino Cennini
      </a>
      described the uses of ochre pigments in his famous treatise on painting.
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       This color is found in the earth in the mountains, where there are found certain seams resembling sulphur; and where the seams are, there is found sinoper, and terre-verte, and other kinds of color... And these colors showed up in this earth just the way a wrinkle shows up in the face of a man or woman.... I picked out the wrinkle of this color with a pen knife, and I do assure you that there never was a better color. And know that this ochre is an all-round color, especially for work in fresco; for it is used, with other mixtures, as I shall explain to you, for flesh colors, for draperies, for painted mountains, and buildings and horses, and in general for many purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">
       Modern history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The industrial process for making ochre pigment was developed by the French scientist Jean-tienne Astier in the 1780s. He was from
      <a href="/wiki/Roussillon" title="Roussillon">
       Roussillon
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vaucluse" title="Vaucluse">
       Vaucluse
      </a>
      department of
      <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">
       Provence
      </a>
      , and he was fascinated by the cliffs of red and yellow clay in the region. He invented a process to make the pigment on a large scale. First the clay was extracted from open pits or mines. The raw clay contained about 80 to 90 percent sand and 10 to 20 percent ochre. Then he washed the clay to separate the grains of sand from the particles of ochre. The remaining mixture was then decanted in large basins, to further separate the ochre from the sand. The water was then drained, and the ochre was dried, cut into bricks, crushed, sifted, and then classified by color and quality. The best quality was reserved for artists' pigments.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roelofs_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-roelofs-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Britain, ochre was mined at
      <a href="/wiki/Brixham" title="Brixham">
       Brixham
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      . It became an important product for the British fishing industry, where it was combined with oil and used to coat sails to protect them from seawater, giving them a reddish color. The ochre was boiled in great caldrons, together with
      <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">
       tar
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">
       tallow
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">
       oak bark
      </a>
      , the last ingredient giving the name of barking yards to the places where the hot mixture was painted on to the sails, which were then hung up to dry.
     </p>
     <p>
      As noted above, the industrial process for making ochre pigment was developed by the French scientist Jean-tienne Astier in the 1780s, using the ochre mines
      <a href="/wiki/Roussillon" title="Roussillon">
       Roussillon
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vaucluse" title="Vaucluse">
       Vaucluse
      </a>
      department of
      <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">
       Provence
      </a>
      , in France. Thanks to the process invented by Astier and refined by his successors, ochre pigments from Roussillon were exported across Europe and around the world. It was not only used for artists paints and house paints; it also became an important ingredient for the early rubber industry.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ochre from Roussillon was an important French export until the mid-20th century, when major markets were lost due to the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">
       Russian Revolution
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
       Spanish Civil War
      </a>
      . Ochre also began to face growing competition from newly synthetic pigment industry. The mines in Roussillon closed, though the production of natural ochre pigments continued at mines in
      <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">
       Cyprus
      </a>
      and other sites.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Australia_and_New_Zealand">
       In Australia and New Zealand
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Australia and New Zealand">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OchrePits.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3320" data-file-width="2382" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/OchrePits.JPG/220px-OchrePits.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/OchrePits.JPG/330px-OchrePits.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/OchrePits.JPG/440px-OchrePits.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OchrePits.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Multicolored ochre rocks used in
        <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">
         Aboriginal
        </a>
        ceremony and artwork.
        <a href="/wiki/Ochre_Pits" title="Ochre Pits">
         Ochre Pits
        </a>
        , Namatjira Drive,
        <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">
         Northern Territory
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Ochre has been used for millennia by Aboriginal people in Australia and by natives in New Zealand for body decoration, mortuary practices, cave painting, bark painting and other artwork, and the preservation of animal skins, among other uses. At
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mungo" title="Lake Mungo">
       Lake Mungo
      </a>
      , in Western
      <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">
       New South Wales
      </a>
      , burial sites have been excavated and burial materials, including ochre-painted bones, have been dated to the arrival of people in Australia
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Ochre pigments are plentiful across Australia, especially the
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Desert_cultural_bloc" title="Western Desert cultural bloc">
       Western Desert
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kimberley_(Western_Australia)" title="Kimberley (Western Australia)">
       Kimberley
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arnhem_Land" title="Arnhem Land">
       Arnhem Land
      </a>
      regions, and occur in many archaeological sites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Australia" title="National Museum of Australia">
       National Museum of Australia
      </a>
      has a large collection of samples of ochre from many sites across Australia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Mori people">
       Maori
      </a>
      people of New Zealand were in the early neolithic period of development when Europeans arrived, and were found to be making extensive use of mineral ochre mixed with fish oil. Ochre was the predominant coloring agent used by Maori, and was used to paint their large
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waka_taua" title="Waka taua">
        waka taua
       </a>
      </i>
      (war canoe). Ochre prevented the drying out of the wood in canoes and the carvings of meeting houses; later missionaries estimated that it would last for 30 years. It was also roughly smeared over the face, especially by women, to keep off insects. Solid chunks of ochre were ground on a flat but rough surfaced rock to produce the powder.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_North_America">
       In North America
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ochre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In North America">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">
       Newfoundland
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      its use is most often associated with the
      <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">
       Beothuk
      </a>
      , whose use of red ochre led them to be referred to as "Red Indians" by the first Europeans to Newfoundland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was also used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Archaic" title="Maritime Archaic">
       Maritime Archaic
      </a>
      as evidenced by its discovery in the graves of over 100 individuals during an archaeological excavation at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_au_Choix,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Port au Choix, Newfoundland and Labrador">
       Port au Choix
      </a>
      . Its use was widespread at times in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodlands_culture" title="Woodlands culture">
       Eastern Woodlands
      </a>
      cultural area of Canada and the US; the
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Ocher_people" title="Red Ocher people">
       Red Ocher people
      </a>
      complex refers to a specific archaeological period in the Woodlands ca. 1000-400 BC. California
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       Native Americans
      </a>
      such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Tongva_people" title="Tongva people">
       Tongva
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chumash_people" title="Chumash people">
       Chumash
      </a>
      were also known to use red ochre as body paint.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">
       Newfoundland
      </a>
      , red ochre was the pigment of choice for use in
      <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">
       vernacular
      </a>
      outbuildings and work buildings associated with the
      <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">
       cod
      </a>
      fishery. Deposits of ochre are found throughout Newfoundland, notably near Fortune Harbor and at Ocher Pit Cove. While earliest settlers may have used locally collected ochre, people were later able to purchase pre-ground ochre through local merchants, largely imported from
      <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
       England
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The dry ingredient, ochre, was mixed with some type of liquid raw material to create a rough paint. The liquid material was usually
      <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">
       seal oil
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Cod_liver_oil" title="Cod liver oil">
       cod liver oil
      </a>
      in Newfoundland and Labrador, while
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
       Scandinavian
      </a>
      recipes sometimes called for
      <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">
       linseed oil
      </a>
      . Red ochre paint was sometimes prepared months in advance and allowed to sit, and the smell of ochre paint being prepared is still remembered by many today.
     </p>
     <p>
      Variations in local recipes, shades of ore, and type of oil used resulted in regional variations in color. Because of this, it is difficult to pinpoint an exact shade or hue of red that would be considered the traditional "
      <a href="/wiki/Fishing_stage" title="Fishing stage">
       fishing stage
      </a>
      red".
      <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">
       Oral tradition
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Bonavista_Bay" title="Bonavista Bay">
       Bonavista Bay
      </a>
      area maintains that seal oil mixed with the ochre gave the sails a purer red color, while
      <a href="/wiki/Cod_liver_oil" title="Cod liver oil">
       cod liver oil
      </a>
      would give a "foxy" color, browner in hue.
     </p>
