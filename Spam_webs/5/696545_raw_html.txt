           <h3 class="feature-title">
            What is Anaerobic Digestion?
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            <strong>
             Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a biological process similar to composting
            </strong>
            ,
            <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
             <strong>
              but without air.
             </strong>
            </span>
            As in composting,
            <strong>
             micro-organismsbreak down organic matter into simpler smaller compounds
            </strong>
            and reduce its bulk or mass.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            However, unlike composting, which overall always consumes energy,
            <strong>
             anaerobic digestion can be used to create energy
            </strong>
            . That is because
            <strong>
             it produces a gas known as biogas
            </strong>
            , just over half of which ismethane. Everyone that lives in a developed nation and has cooked anything on a kitchen stove, using gas piped in from the street, has used methane, and knows how cleanly it burns, and how wonderfully hot the flame is, because "natural gas" is pure methane.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            In addition to biogas,
            <strong>
             the process also produces "digestate"
            </strong>
            , which is a
            <strong>
             solid fibrous residue, and
            </strong>
            any
            <strong>
             liquid
            </strong>
            which is left over.

             The Biogas Plant Renaissance
            </a>
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            Until the early 2000s there had been a long period of cheap fossil fuel sourced power. While power was so cheap, the anaerobic digestion process was largely ignored in the west.
            <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/biogas-production-in-china/" title="Agricultural Biogas Production in China">
             China
            </a>
            and India and some other nations realized its value especially to subsistence farmers, and developed early versions of the now increasingly popular buried, fixed dome, household and small community biogas digester systems which they invented. Ever larger numbersof these affordable energy plants are now being built and operated, so that by 2016 literally millions of people will be benefiting from the "biogas renaissance".
            <br/>
            <br/>
            In the western nations during the first half of the 20th century, anaerobic digestion had been popular among the water boards, and municipal corporations in charge of the safe disposal of sewage sludge, but almost all of those had, by then, been replaced by incinerators. Energy was so cheap that even those running those incinerator plants failed to truly realize that they were using energy, to destroy energy! That was an expensivewaste, which is now being put right as large numbers of sewage sludge incinerators are being shut-down and replaced with biogas plants, the latest of which can be so efficient in making power that they can generate enough electricity to run the entire wastewater treatment works (sewage works) solely using the sludge from the works as their bio-fuel!
            <br/>
            <br/>

           <h3 class="feature-title">
            <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/feature/quick-facts-process/" title="Quick Facts About The Process">
             Quick Facts About The Process
            </a>
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            1. There are four key biological/ chemical reaction stages of anaerobic digestion:
            <ul>
             <li>
              Hydrolysis
             </li>
             <li>
              Acidogenesis
             </li>
             <li>
              Acetogenesis
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-systems/anaerobic-digestion-model/">
               Methanogenesis
              </a>
             </li>
            </ul>
            2. Almost any organic matter can be digested anaerobically. Not all will produce enough
            <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/feature/biogas-plant-renaissance/">
             biogas
            </a>
            to be viable though.

3. Anaerobic digestion occurs in nature at all temperatures, but below 10 degrees centigradethe rate of biogas production is so slow that running a biogas process is usually considered not viable, for any form of biogas generation system.

4. There are two temperature ranges over which the many micro-organisms which produce biogas work most efficiently. These are known as the:
            <ul>
             <li>
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/the-anaerobic-digestion-process/mesophilic-anaerobic-digestion/" title="Mesophilic Anaerobic Digestion">
               Mesophilic
              </a>
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/the-anaerobic-digestion-process/thermophilic-anaerobic-digestion/" title="Thermophilic Anaerobic Digestion">
               Thermophilic
              </a>
             </li>
            </ul>
            Temperature ranges, with the optimum temperature usually considered to be at the top of the range.

5. The process is truly ancient:
            <ul>
             <li>
              Fermentation for the production of alcohol was known to the Ancient Egyptians and is recorded as far back as 2,000 BC.
             </li>
             <li>
              The
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/feature/upgrade-biogas-biomethane/">
               methane
              </a>
              producing stage of anaerobic digestion is performed by a type of micro-organism known as archaea. It's not a bacteria. In fact in comes from a distinctly different branch of the phylogenetic tree of life to bacteria.
             </li>
            </ul>

        <div class="widget widget_woothemes_features" id="woothemes_features-7">
         <h3>
          Reasons for Building an AD Plant  Other Than for Energy
         </h3>
         <div class="features columns-4">
          <div class="feature first">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            For Water Treatment
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            <ul>
             <li>
              Although Wastewater Treatment Plants, which treat foul sewage, are routinely aerobic processes, some are anaerobic especially in South America.
             </li>
             <li>
              Upflow Anaerobic Sludge Blanket (UASB) systems use the process and are primarily used for water treatment.
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="feature">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            For Odour Abatement
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            <ul>
             <li>
              Large intensive livestock farms that are situated close to housing may be able to obtain permission to extend their operations only if they install an AD plant to reduce the odour of "muck spreading".
             </li>
             <li>
              Digestion of manure substantially reduces spreading odours, especially if ground injection of digestate, is used.
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="feature">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            To Reduce Risk of Water Pollution (1)
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            <ul>
             <li>
              High intensity livestock farms in high rainfall areas, andnitrate sensitive zones, may experience a particularly high risk of river pollution and prosecution from nitrate contaminated run-off, when heavy rain occurs soon after spreading manure.
             </li>
             <li>
              Plant and soil uptake of thenutrients, from the digestate from a biogas plant, canbe more rapid and
              <strong>
               reducewatercourse pollution risks
              </strong>
              .
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="feature last">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            To Reduce Risk of Water Pollution (2)
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            <ul>
             <li>
              The quality of the seawater at
              <strong>
               Blue Flag bathing beaches
              </strong>
              wasshown to be impaired after heavy summerrainstorm events, in an estuarine environment.
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-plants/anaerobic-digestion-plants-scotland/" title="Anaerobic Digestion Plants in Scotland Before 2010">
               Installation of farm biogas plants
              </a>
              to digest all livestock farm cattle slurry, reduced thefaecalindicator organism counts on nearby beaches, avoiding damage to local tourism.
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </div>

        <div class="widget widget_woothemes_features" id="woothemes_features-8">
         <h3>
          3 Top AD Plant Add-Ons and Upgrades
         </h3>
         <div class="features columns-3">
          <div class="feature first">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            A Hydrolysis Stage
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            "Hard to digest" feed materials can benefit greatly by including a
            <a href="http://blog.anaerobic-digestion.com/thermal-hydrolysis-2/what-is-thermal-hydrolysis" target="_blank">
             hydrolysis stage
            </a>
            in which the substrate is subjected to high temperature and pressure, before digestion.

Hydrolysis breaks up intact cells allowing the digestion micro-organisms to carry out a much more complete digestion/ higher
            <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-plant-design/">
             gas yields
            </a>
            / reduced retention times and greater
            <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-plant-design/anaerobic-digestion-process-nutrients/">
             plant throughput
            </a>
            .
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="feature">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            Upgrade Biogas to Biomethane
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            <ul>
             <li>
              There is a lot of talk about creating added-value for the raw biogas from AD Plants, by "upgrading".
             </li>
             <li>
              Upgrading is the process of
              <a href="http://blog.anaerobic-digestion.com/biogas-2/how-to-eliminate-co2-from-biogas" target="_blank">
               removing the carbon dioxide
              </a>
              and other impurities from biogas, and compressing it.
             </li>
             <li>
              It produces a
              <strong>
               highly sought after product
              </strong>
              . It is sold as
              <strong>
               <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-technology/">
                CNG
               </a>
               as a vehicle fuel
              </strong>
              , or
              <strong>
               injected into the regional gas distribution network
              </strong>
              .
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="feature last">
           <h3 class="feature-title">
            Improved Biogas Reactor Mixing and Avoidance of Hard Crust
           </h3>
           <div class="feature-content">
            (Applies to Wet AD Processes Only)
            <ul>
             <li>
              Many early plants will benefit from
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-equipment/anaerobic-digestion-pumps/">
               improved reactor mixing
              </a>
              , which avoids "dead zones" within the digester tank.
             </li>
             <li>
              Some mixing systems can also break-up any tendency for a crust to form on the reactor surface.
             </li>
             <li>
              <a href="http://anaerobic-digestion.com/anaerobic-digestion-plant-design/anaerobic-digestion-feedstock/">
               Higher biogas volumes
              </a>
              mayrepay the cost of installation quite rapidly.
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </div>
