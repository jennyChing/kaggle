                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     Anime Review - Katanagatari
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8945685130827312290" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <h2 style="text-align: center;">
                      Katanagatari
                     </h2>
                     <div>
                      <div style="text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://myanimelist.net/anime/6594/Katanagatari">
                        MAL
                       </a>
                       . Genres:
                       <a href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/ActionHero">
                        action
                       </a>
                       ,
                       <a href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/HistoricalFantasy">
                        historical
                       </a>
                       ,
                       <a href="http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/ArtisticLicenseMartialArts">
                        martial arts
                       </a>
                       <br/>
                       <br/>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-18os3f6pr70/VNku_9W7tbI/AAAAAAAAETI/eHq4I_5B958/s1600/Katanagatari_anime-wallpaper.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                         <img border="0" height="250" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-18os3f6pr70/VNku_9W7tbI/AAAAAAAAETI/eHq4I_5B958/s1600/Katanagatari_anime-wallpaper.jpg" width="400"/>
                        </a>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <h3 style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                        Intro
                       </h3>
                       <div style="text-align: left;">
                        Imagine an anime about sword fighting. Easy, right? Now imagine one of its main characters as a sword. Not much difficult, ha? And now add to the mix that he is both the sword - and the human. And the swords the main duo is collecting are not really swords, at least, not all of them. Things are getting complicated? Good. Katanagatari doesn't make things simple. It doesn't give all the answers, it does not stick to some particular genre cliches. It does not pity the viewers - or the characters. It is just one of the most original, engaging anime of the recent years.
                       </div>
                       <div style="text-align: left;">
                        <br/>
                        <a name="more">
                        </a>
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-5fNSjR3wTCQ/VNkwz0WeRUI/AAAAAAAAETU/whzCyEvNgBg/s1600/vlcsnap-2010-01-28-00h06m51s1871.png" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                         <img border="0" height="225" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-5fNSjR3wTCQ/VNkwz0WeRUI/AAAAAAAAETU/whzCyEvNgBg/s1600/vlcsnap-2010-01-28-00h06m51s1871.png" width="400"/>
                        </a>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                        So what's different about the show? Many things. Just way too many. Let's start with the format. It aired in episodes of 50 minutes (even four years after only Fate/Stay Night UBW dares to put even some of its episodes into this format). And frankly, 50 minutes was perfect for Katanagatari. The stories were not simple. The show looked episodic, but wasn't. And we needed as much time with the characters as possible. Speaking about the story...
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-sY-3lCxiVWY/VNk05vM1W4I/AAAAAAAAETs/WGQFvKh5LJs/s1600/katanagatari_04_09.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                         <img border="0" height="180" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-sY-3lCxiVWY/VNk05vM1W4I/AAAAAAAAETs/WGQFvKh5LJs/s1600/katanagatari_04_09.jpg" width="320"/>
                        </a>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <h3 style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                        The Story
                       </h3>
                       <div style="text-align: left;">
                        I can't say that the story of Katanagatari is too complicated. I mean, alternative medieval Japan (Edo/Owari Period). Shogunate is in turmoil, there are numerous problems arising due to the wielders of 12 Great Swords - most of them are not strictly swords anyway, but they grant their owners unmatched powers. Shogunate Agent Togame is using Ichika, the 7th Master of a Kyoto Ryu to gather all swords for the shogune. How? By commanding him to fall in love with her. This is getting complicated, since Ichika spent all his life on a remote island in exile with his sister and is used to act more like a tool, a sword than a real person. So generally, what is going on in the show is Togane and Ichika travelling around Japan, combating the owners of 12 Great Swords and gathering all of WunderWaffen.
                       </div>
                       <div style="text-align: left;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                        <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-B7-Cr_QK-yc/VNp4VzNfhiI/AAAAAAAAEUE/g6Clr2v81L4/s1600/20b0c5021d81320c0cbead5c2a271fb0.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                         <img border="0" height="320" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-B7-Cr_QK-yc/VNp4VzNfhiI/AAAAAAAAEUE/g6Clr2v81L4/s1600/20b0c5021d81320c0cbead5c2a271fb0.jpg" width="228"/>
                        </a>
                       </div>
                       <div style="text-align: left;">
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div style="text-align: left;">
                        Sounds simple enough, doesn't it? Well, there is one big BUT here. Besides the major story we are witnessing a blooming romance between Ichika and Togame - and also a slow, but steady transformation from a sword into a living human, with own thoughts, feelings and ambition, which is done by Ichika. Usually this falls in the category of character development, but I feel that Katanagatari emphasized it so much, that it became a story in itself. Some scholars of literature and drama divide a plot into two parts - inner and outer action. Usually anime goes into one of two extremes - we either have loads of outer action with dismal inner one (sudden changes in characters motivation for no reason do not count as inner action!), or it's inner action and really nothing is happening on the outside (Tatami Galaxy, I see what you did there!). Katanagatari puts such things in balance.
                        <br/>
                        <br/>
                        <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                         <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-k83LFyhnhcI/VNp6ABJXGfI/AAAAAAAAEUg/6-zGnJIMZXY/s1600/218718.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                          <img border="0" height="250" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-k83LFyhnhcI/VNp6ABJXGfI/AAAAAAAAEUg/6-zGnJIMZXY/s1600/218718.jpg" width="400"/>
                         </a>
                        </div>
                        <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                         <br/>
                        </div>
                        <h3 style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                         The Characters
                        </h3>
                        <div>
                         I will not dwell upon this topic too much. I mean, when character development is part of the plot in itself, the characters gotta be good. And oh yes, they are. While I had a feeling that for the amount of screen time we didn't get too deep into most of side characters, the 12 Sword Wielders, the main character couple is always at the spotlight. And they do engage you, there is a lot of humor, a lot of moe-ish elements. Togame molding Ichika into a human being - and falling in love in what she partially creates is just beautiful. Character design, as well as the animation are great beyond doubt. The style is original, completely non-standard for anime, the colors are full and bright, it looks really more of a living painting that a living manga.
                        </div>
                        <div>
                         <br/>
                        </div>
                        <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                         <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-GkShZtKIVQQ/VNp7Z98GJuI/AAAAAAAAEUs/hw3OmuX3yNI/s1600/original.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                          <img border="0" height="200" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-GkShZtKIVQQ/VNp7Z98GJuI/AAAAAAAAEUs/hw3OmuX3yNI/s1600/original.jpg" width="320"/>
                         </a>
                        </div>
                        <div>
                         <br/>
                        </div>
                        <h3 style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                         Final Verdict
                        </h3>
                        <div>
                         I like originality in the media I consume. Hell, I love it. Katanagatari gave me all that. Artistic style that fits more to illustrations of an old fable that to a TV screen, characters that are not even meant to look realistic, while they act realistically enough - and are lovable for it. Simple story of fighting and gathering 12 Swords in 12 months turning out into a tale of love, humanity, finding reason to live and fight. And the twists of the outer plot following the logic of inner plot made a good summary in the end: who we are and what we are aiming for influences what we become, aim is paramount and we are more than simple tools in the hands of each other. I give Katanagatari
                         <b>
                          9/10
                         </b>
                         (1 point substracted for not really memorable music) and I highly recommend watching it at any point of your anime journey, whether you are just starting or have seen dozens of shows.
                        </div>
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
