               <h1 class="title" style="padding-top: 0px; margin: 0px; margin-bottom: 5px">
                Why Process Measures Are Often More Important Than Outcome Measures in Healthcare
               </h1>
               <p>
                <i>
                 Written by
                 <a data-uk-modal="" href="#author-27">
                  Tom Burton
                 </a>
                 . Posted in
                 <a href="/knowledge-center/search-results?type=category&amp;target=improving-clinical-quality">
                  Quality and Process Improvement
                 </a>
                </i>
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="grid-box width100">
               <div class="grid-block">
                <div class="grid-box width100">
                 <div>
                  <div class="add-to-folder" data-permalink="https://www.healthcatalyst.com/process-vs-outcome-measures-healthcare" data-title="Why Process Measures Are Often More Important Than Outcome Measures in Healthcare" id="article_12685">
                   <div class="add-to-folder-img">
                   </div>
                   <div class="add-to-folder-text" data-add-text="Add To My Folder" data-added-text="Added">
                    Add To My Folder
                   </div>
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
            </header>
            <div class="content clearfix">
             <p>
              Our healthcare industry is currently obsessed with outcome measures  and for good reason. Todays health systems must use the wealth of data they have on hand to drive better
              <a href="/linking-clinical-and-financial-data/">
               quality and cost outcomes
              </a>
              . But tracking outcome measures alone is insufficient to reach these goals.
             </p>
             <p>
              To successfully use data to improve quality and cost, health systems must get more granular with their data. They cant just look at outcome measures. They must also track the evidence-based process measures that drive better outcomes.
             </p>
             <h2>
              3 Types of Measures: Outcome Measures, Balance Measures, and Process Measures
             </h2>
             <p>
              But what exactly are evidence-based process measures? First it helps to understand the three types of measures we use in
              <a href="/healthcare-analytics-adoption-model/">
               healthcare analytics
              </a>
              :
             </p>
             <ol>
              <li>
               <b>
                Outcome measures:
               </b>
               The high-level
               <a href="/electronic-clinical-quality-measures-impact-data-quality">
                clinical
               </a>
               or financial outcomes that concern healthcare organizations are called outcome measures. They are the quality and cost targets you are aiming at improving. These measures are often reported to
               <a href="/simplifying-meaningful-use-stages">
                government
               </a>
               and commercial payers. Some examples of metrics for outcome measures include
               <a href="/success_stories/how-to-reduce-sepsis-mortality-rates-by-22">
                mortality rates
               </a>
               ,
               <a href="/success_stories/reduce-30-day-heart-failure-readmissions/">
                readmissions rates
               </a>
               , and surgical site
               <a href="/using_healthcare_data_to_optimize_clinician_time/">
                infection rates
               </a>
               .
              </li>
              <li>
               <b>
                Balance measures:
               </b>
               The metrics a health system must track to ensure an improvement in one area isnt negatively impacting another area are called balance measures. For example, lets say length of stay (LOS) in labor and delivery is the outcome metric. The hospital wants to reduce LOS and save money. The balance metric might be
               <a href="/improve-patient-satisfaction-5-lessons-from-disney">
                patient satisfaction
               </a>
               . If mothers feel rushed toward discharge, outcome there might be a negative impact on patient satisfaction even while improving LOS.
              </li>
              <li>
               <b>
                Process measures:
               </b>
               These measures are the specific steps in a process that lead  either positively or negatively  to a particular outcome metric. For example, lets say the outcome measure is LOS. A process metric for that outcome might be the amount of time that passes between when the physician ordered the discharge and when the patient was actually discharged. Digging even deeper, you might look at the turnaround time between final take-home medication being ordered and medication delivery to the unit. If it takes the pharmacy three hours to get the necessary medications to the floor  potentially delaying the discharge  youve pinpointed a concrete opportunity for
               <a href="/5-Deming-Principles-For-Healthcare-Process-Improvement">
                healthcare process improvement.
               </a>
              </li>
             </ol>
             <h2>
              Why Process Measures Are So Important
             </h2>
             <p>
              Process measures essentially represent a health systems efforts to incorporate and
              <a href="/anatomy-healthcare-delivery-model-transform-care">
               systematize
              </a>
              evidence-based best practices into its improvement efforts. To illustrate this, Ill use a
              <a href="/population-health-applications/">
               patient injury prevention
              </a>
              example. Lets say your organization needs to reduce the incidence of pressure ulcers commonly known as bed sores. That is your outcome measure. You know your baseline rate, and you want to reduce it, but how are you actually going to
              <a href="/healthcare-quality-improvement-ignite-aim-statements/">
               drive improvement
              </a>
              ?
             </p>
             <p>
              The answer is straightforward: by implementing and tracking the right process metrics. Process measures in this example are the steps that should be performed every time for every bed-bound patient in the intensive care unit (ICU) or in the med-surg units. The first and most important process measure would be performing a risk assessment using the Braden Scale for predicting pressure ulcer risk on all the appropriate units in the Hospital.
             </p>
             <p>
              Patients identified as at risk would then receive treatment for preventing bedsores according to your organizations chosen best-practice protocol. For example, you might set up protocols for reassessment, nutrition, lifting and repositioning the patient, providing a special mattress, and skin care. The important thing is that each of these steps in the process can be can be tracked and measured. Over time, as you develop an adequate sample size, you can begin to identify which process steps are the most important for preventing negative outcomes.
             </p>
             <h2>
              Determining Root Cause
             </h2>
             <p>
              One of the greatest benefits of having this process metric data on hand is the ability to identify what is really causing the problem with pressure ulcers in your organization. I can assure you that the problem does not stem from your people. It stems from your process. In most organizations, however, the system of incident reporting doesnt recognize this fact. There is a stigma attached to filling out the report: You failed. Youre a bad nurse because your patient got a bed sore. Often, many borderline cases go unreported because of the lack of a
              <a href="/improving-patient-safety-and-quality-in-healthcare">
               culture
              </a>
              based on a learning environment and too much focus on outcomes metrics.
             </p>
             <p>
              <b>
               Really, it is the process that failed, not the person.
              </b>
              One of my favorite quotes is from Paul Batalden, MD, and it goes like this: Every system is perfectly designed to get the results it gets. If you dont have a well-designed process in place to prevent pressure ulcers, it should be no surprise if you dont perform well on that outcome metric.
             </p>
             <p>
              By tracking process measures, you can pinpoint the root cause of the systems failure. You might find that you have a shortage of pressure-redistributing mattresses in the supply room, or that you didnt do a risk assessment for every patient that arrived on the unit, or that the right skincare products arent accessible on the floor when needed. Whatever you discover, you can then go in and fix the problem.
             </p>
             <p>
              What youre trying to do is move from a craftsmanship mentality to a system of production. And process metrics are the way that you do that. Theyre the checklists of systematically guaranteeing that the right care will be delivered to every patient, every time.
             </p>
             <h2>
              Process Measures and Reducing Variation
             </h2>
             <p>
              Process measures improve quality and cost by enabling organizations to reduce the amount of
              <a href="/role-clinical-variation-medical-practice">
               variation
              </a>
              in care delivery. Establishing process metrics at potential points of variation in a care process enables you to monitor and reduce inappropriate variation. A value stream map is a great tool for outlining a care process and identifying potential points of variation that can be measured.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="https://www.healthcatalyst.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/care-process-model-pregnancy.png" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article-int', 'https://www.healthcatalyst.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/care-process-model-pregnancy.png', '');">
               <img alt="" class=" aligncenter wp-image-10415" height="464" src="https://www.healthcatalyst.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/care-process-model-pregnancy.png" width="720"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p style="text-align: center;">
              <strong>
               <em>
                This image shows an example of a care process model for pregnancy that uses a value stream map approach to map a workflow. The black font shows the value added steps in the process and the red font identifies potential process metrics that could be tracked to measure variation in the process.
               </em>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p>
              A value stream map outlines the steps in a process that deliver value to a patient. Each of those steps may have a process measure useful in measuring process consistency. By measuring these steps, you can discover points of variation. For example, you might discover that one step in the process takes some units one hour to complete and other units five hours. Or it might cost some units $2,000 and other units $8,000. Those instances of variation provide opportunities to explore the data to understand why the variation exists. From there, its possible to standardize processes so that all patients consistently receive the highest-quality care at the lowest possible cost regardless of which unit, which hospital or which clinic they are visiting.
             </p>
             <h2>
              Why You Need an Enterprise Data Warehouse to Track Process Metrics
             </h2>
             <p>
              So why dont more healthcare organizations have these kinds of systems in place? The answer is that they dont have the infrastructure to handle both outcome and process metrics. If they only have the resources to track one of these, theyll choose outcome metrics, because those are the
              <a href="/meaningful-use-and-ACO-reporting">
               measures that must be submitted
              </a>
              to Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS).
             </p>
             <p>
              The reason organizations struggle to track both types of measures is because their
              <a href="/best-healthcare-analytics-approach">
               analytics
              </a>
              methodologies rely too much on manual work. When you dont have the right
              <a href="/late-binding-data-warehouse/">
               technology infrastructure
              </a>
              in place to automate the extraction and distribution of data, you end up having to do it all manually. You might be able to successfully use manual methods to
              <a href="/clinical-quality-improvement-in-healthcare" target="_blank">
               track improvement
              </a>
              for one process or even two, but once you get to the third, fourth or fifth process, the manual work becomes unsustainable. And without continuous measurement, you wont be able to sustain the gains you made in one process once you move on to the next.
             </p>
             <p>
              Thats where an
              <a href="/late-binding-data-warehouse-explained/">
               enterprise data warehouse
              </a>
              (EDW) with a robust and flexible analytics architecture comes in. An EDW forms the foundation for
              <a href="/requirements-for-ACO-analytics-solutions">
               healthcare analytics
              </a>
