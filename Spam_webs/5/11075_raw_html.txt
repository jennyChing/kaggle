               About
              </h3>
              <p>
               Erin is the founder of
               <a href="http://brokemillennial.com/" target="_blank">
                BrokeMillennial.com

               , where she uses sarcasm and humor to explain basic financial concepts to her fellow millennials. Erin lives in New York City and works for

                MagnifyMoney

        <div class="body clearfix" data-stock-symbols="$ALLY " id="article_body" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Article">
         <div data-part="contents" itemprop="articleBody">
          <figure class="photo-slim full-size">
           <img alt="cut credit card" data-caption="cut credit card" data-credit="shutterstock" data-mep="365889" src="http://o.aolcdn.com/dims-shared/dims3/GLOB/crop/3137x1699+1150+688/resize/604x327!/format/jpg/quality/85/http://hss-prod.hss.aol.com/hss/storage/midas/770cb74e298ab8bb897352dc732a58f0/200096593/108619787.jpg"/>
           <figcaption class="cap">
            <b class="credit">
             Shutterstock
            </b>
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
          Whether you get your financial tips by asking friends and family or checking out library books, attending seminars or searching online (at sites other than DailyFinance), impractical pieces of advice abound.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Too many personal finance experts tend to populate their cable appearances, books, columns and blogs with the same simple tidbits. But some of that common advice is also ... useless. For each of these three cliched tips, let's look at some better alternatives.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           1. In Debt? Cut Up Your Credit Cards
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Certain financial gurus advise people in debt to cut up all their plastic and consider using credit cards the eighth deadly sin. Here's some advice: don't.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          People land in debt for various reasons, and some -- like student loans -- don't have anything to do with credit cards.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          If being a unable to pass up a sale or discount clothing bin is your trigger for getting into massive amounts of debt, then put your cards in a lock box and back away. If you fell into some bad luck and used your credit card for an emergency, consider a
          <a href="http://www.dailyfinance.com/2014/06/04/balance-transfers-credit-card-debt-save-money/" target="_blank">
           balance transfer
          </a>
          .
          <br/>
          <br/>
          But just because someone is in debt and wants to get out of it doesn't mean they're going to stop spending money entirely. People still need to eat, gas up the car, and deal with the occasional unexpected expense.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Some may counter that it's best to use a debit card, but consider the ramifications of debit card fraud. A compromised debit card gives thieves direct access to your bank account. While most banks will cover the majority of money taken from your bank account, it's an extreme hassle to deal with. When a credit card is compromised, the issuer typically reacts quickly -- possibly even before the customer notices -- and offers 100 percent fraud protection. A credit card should be used for all online purchases, and you need one to rent a car -- otherwise you'll get a hard inquiry on your credit report for using a debit card.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          It also helps to have a low-interest credit card for emergencies. Think of it as a fire extinguisher housed in a glass case. You don't want to break that glass unless you really, really need it. But you do want the fire extinguisher to be there.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           2. Have a 20 Percent Buffer (or Any Buffer) in Checking
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Undoubtedly, it's preferably to have a buffer in your checking account to avoid overdraft fees, but two types of situations cause overdraft fees.
          <ul>
           <li>
            Person A is forgetful, forgets a recurring charge or neglects to check his or her balance before making a purchase.
           </li>
           <li>
            Person B uses overdrafts as a form of short-term borrowing because he or she does not have enough money to get by without going overdraft.
           </li>
          </ul>
          Person B doesn't need to be lectured about keeping a buffer in his or her checking account.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          About
          <a href="http://www.brookings.edu/about/projects/bpea/papers/2014/wealthy-hand-to-mouth" target="_blank">
           38 million American households spend all of their paycheck
          </a>
          , with more than two-thirds being part of the middle class, according to a study by Brookings Institution.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          It's simple for personal finance experts, writers and advisers to recommend tightening up the purse strings, doubling down on paying off debt, and moving out of the paycheck-to-paycheck lifestyle.
          <div class="pull-quote bt bb cc">
           <i class="open cc">
           </i>
           <p>
            Those looking to avoid overdraft fees should evaluate their banking products.
           </p>
           <i class="close cc">
           </i>
          </div>
          But those who don't have assets and who struggle each month to make ends meet don't need to hear people harping about avoiding overdraft fees by "just saving a little bit." Every little bit counts for them.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Instead, let's offer practical advice: Those looking to avoid overdraft fees should evaluate their banking products.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Investigate and find out if your bank reorders your transactions. Some banks will change the order in which they process your transactions at the end of the day, so they can maximize the number of overdraft fees they charge.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Internet-only banks are revolutionizing how people can interact with their banks and their own money. Internet-only banks often offer higher interest rates, don't charge monthly maintenance fees and reimburse customers for out-of-network ATM fees. For example,
          <a href="https://www.simple.com/faq" target="_blank">
           Simple
          </a>
          , an Internet-only bank based out of Portland, Oregon, doesn't charge overdraft fees.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <a href="http://www.ally.com/bank/interest-checking-account/" target="_blank">
           Ally Bank
          </a>
          (
          <a href="http://www.dailyfinance.com/quote/nyse/ally" target="_blank">
           ALLY
          </a>
          ) offers real overdraft protection by linking a savings account to checking. If you overdraft, it will take $100 out of your savings to cover your overdraft -- free of charge.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Americans who use overdraft fees as a form of short-term lending may want to set up a line of credit with a credit union or have a low-interest credit card for emergencies.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           3. Skip That Latte
          </strong>
          !
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Many years ago, David Bach created a unifying mantra for frugalistas and personal finance enthusiasts. The "latte factor" was that you could save big by cutting back on small things.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Bach's deeper concept -- that each individual needs to identify his or her latte factor -- got lost in the battle cries, with many people crusade specifically against your daily cup of coffee.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <div class="wss-course-inline pull-left hide" id="wssCourseInline-884">
          </div>
          <script type="text/javascript">
           Boot.getJS({src:'http://api.dailyfinance.com/dailyfinance/?service=mycourses&amp;rf=http://learn.dailyfinance.com&amp;callback=DAILYFINANCE.wssInlineCourse&amp;courseId=884',defer:'load'});
          </script>
          Yes, people should be aware of leaks in their budget. But everyone's budget looks different. If "Summer" buys a coffee each day, but rarely buys new clothing, and trims her budget by cutting cable and brown-bagging it to work, then leave her alone about her caffeine habit.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          People are allowed to live a little when it comes to their personal finances, whether your little splurge is organic food, name-brand snacks or triple-ply toilet paper. Most people, except for maybe those on TLC's "Extreme Cheapskates," could find more ways to trim the budget.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          But why keep trimming? It's important to save for the future, but it's also imperative to enjoy life in the present. Personal finance shouldn't be a culture of constant denial.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Create a budget, figure out if you can work in an indulgence or two, and don't live in complete deprivation
          <br/>
          <br/>
          For those working to dig out of seemingly insurmountable debt, then yes, it may be time to identify and limit your latte factor.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           Decide What's Right for You
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Personal finance experts, bloggers, reporters, advisers are well intentioned in their advice. Certain personal finance idols truly believe everyone should use cash, while others hawk prepaid debit cards claiming they're a great tool. Okay, maybe sometimes personal finance experts are a bit self-serving and not looking out for your best interests. Just keep in mind, personal finance is indeed personal. A generic piece of advice, like keep a 20 percent buffer in your checking account to avoid overdrafts, may not be helpful in your personal situation.

             It doesn't hurt to ask for a discount: The worst your doctor can say is "no." Many practitioners offer lower rates when you pay with cash or check instead of a card. (They also offer payment plan options if you can't afford to pay all at once.)
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Shutterstock" data-media-id="2774317" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/7/S2774317/slug/l/doctor-taking-notes-in-office-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/7/S2774317/slug/s/doctor-taking-notes-in-office-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/7/S2774317/slug/l/doctor-taking-notes-in-office-2.jpg">
             2. Ask for a discount
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Bring up as many concerns with your primary-care physician during an office visit as possible, advises
             <a href="http://www.theamericancollege.edu/why-us/faculty/adam-beck-esq" target="_blank">
              Adam Beck
             </a>
             , assistant professor of health insurance atThe American College, which trains people in the finance industry. "Your doctor will be able to test and treat you for a variety of potential ailments or conditions while paying one co-pay, as opposed to returning each time you think something is awry."
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Alex Raths" data-media-id="2774314" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/4/S2774314/slug/l/male-patient-tells-the-doctor-about-his-health-complaintsple-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/4/S2774314/slug/s/male-patient-tells-the-doctor-about-his-health-complaintsple-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/4/S2774314/slug/l/male-patient-tells-the-doctor-about-his-health-complaintsple-2.jpg">
             3. Make the most of your doctor visit
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Always review your medical bills the same way you would a restaurant bill. If you feel like a billing mistake was made -- or that you've been overcharged -- speak up."The
             <a href="http://www.BillAdvocates.com" target="_blank">
              Medical Billing Advocates of America
             </a>
             estimates that roughly eight out of 10 medical bills contain errors," says Allen Erenbaum of the
             <a href="http://www.consumerhealthalliance.org" target="_blank">
              Consumer Health Alliance
             </a>
             , a national association for non-insurance discount health care programs. "All prescription drugs and medical procedures have codes, and sometimes there could be a costly mistake."If you need help, you can find patient and billing advocates through
             <a href="http://www.MedicalBillMediation.com" target="_blank">
              MedicalBillMediation.com
             </a>
             andMedical Billing Advocates of America.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="ERproductions Ltd." data-media-id="2774315" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/5/S2774315/slug/l/doctor-preparing-patient-for-mri-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/5/S2774315/slug/s/doctor-preparing-patient-for-mri-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/431/5/S2774315/slug/l/doctor-preparing-patient-for-mri-2.jpg">
             4. Double-check those bills
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             "Establish a relationship with a primary-care physician and have all the routine screening done that is recommended for someone of your age and gender," advises John Garner, author of the "
             <a href="http://www.barnesandnoble.com/listing/2682611526797?r=1&amp;cm_mmc=GooglePLA-_-Book_45Up-_-Q000000633-_-2682611526797" target="_blank">
              Health Insurance Answer Book
             </a>
             ." "Catching problems early is not only less expensive, but it could save your life." (Of course, you are exercising daily and eating nutritiously.)
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Monkey Business Images/Shutterstock" data-media-id="2774349" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/9/S2774349/slug/l/doctor-listening-to-male-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/9/S2774349/slug/s/doctor-listening-to-male-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/9/S2774349/slug/l/doctor-listening-to-male-2.jpg">
             5. Stay on top of annual exams
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Make a habit of requesting the generic alternatives for prescriptions. Your doctors may write their prescriptions this way automatically, but it never hurts to remind them. Additionally, ask if there's a different form of the same medication."For example, if you are prescribed tablets, ask if you may take capsules or lozenges. Sometimes the difference in cost with your insurance can mean a difference in half the tablet price. I've saved lots of customers this way just by calling their doctor for them personally," says pharmacist Steve Levin, owner of
             <a href="http://www.woodlandhillspharmacy.com/" target="_blank">
              Woodland Hills Pharmacy
             </a>
             in California. The wisdom of buying cheaper generics also applies to over-the-counter medications. For example, Target sells a 100-tablet bottle ofTylenol for $6.99, while its store brand of acetaminophen costs$5.29 for 250 tablets.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Harald Theissen" data-media-id="2774325" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/5/S2774325/slug/l/many-different-colourful-pills-and-capsules-with-a-medicin-b-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/5/S2774325/slug/s/many-different-colourful-pills-and-capsules-with-a-medicin-b-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/5/S2774325/slug/l/many-different-colourful-pills-and-capsules-with-a-medicin-b-2.jpg">
             6. Request generics and alternate forms of medication
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             The emergency room may seem like your best bet when you don't have time to wait for an appointment, but it should only be used in life-threatening situations. The ER is much more expensive than a visit to your family doctor, sometimes by hundreds of dollars -- and that's before you even get to your actual treatment. If you can't wait to see a physician, or you're out of town, your best option is an urgent care center. They are often a little more expensive than visiting a general practitioner, but definitely less costly than the ER.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Brand X/Getty Images" data-media-id="2774343" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/3/S2774343/slug/l/doctor-performing-cpr-on-asian-patient-in-the-emergency-room-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/3/S2774343/slug/s/doctor-performing-cpr-on-asian-patient-in-the-emergency-room-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/3/S2774343/slug/l/doctor-performing-cpr-on-asian-patient-in-the-emergency-room-2.jpg">
             7. Avoid the ER when possible
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Medical bills can add up quickly, especially when a doctor starts doing test after test. Garner says to always ask questions such as, "Is this test or procedure necessary?" and don't settle for vague answers.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Image Source" data-media-id="2774320" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/0/S2774320/slug/l/doctor-checking-x-rays-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/0/S2774320/slug/s/doctor-checking-x-rays-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/0/S2774320/slug/l/doctor-checking-x-rays-2.jpg">
             8. Be a smart, questioning consumer
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Get the insurance coveragethat works best for your family's needs.If you're a relatively healthy person who goes to the doctor once a year and the dentist every six months, but usually nothing more, skip the ultra-expensive premium with a low deductible. Contrarily, if you find yourself visiting the doctor more often, an insurance plan with a low deductible could save you much more even though your premium is higher.To better understand your choices, consult a licensed health insurance agent.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="PhotoAlto/Alamy" data-media-id="2774322" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/2/S2774322/slug/l/doctor-placing-gauze-on-patients-arm-after-administering-a-s-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/2/S2774322/slug/s/doctor-placing-gauze-on-patients-arm-after-administering-a-s-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/2/S2774322/slug/l/doctor-placing-gauze-on-patients-arm-after-administering-a-s-2.jpg">
             9. Shop for the right insurance plan
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             "Non-insurance discount health plans can save you money on ancillary services your insurance typically does not cover, like dental care, vision, prescriptions, alternative medicine and more," explains Erenbaum. According to the Consumer Health Alliance, you can save 20 percent to 60 percent on services with a non-insurance discount plan. Check out
             <a href="https://www.americaspremierbenefits.com" target="_blank">
              America's Premier Benefits
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="http://www.newbenefits.com" target="_blank">
              New Benefits
             </a>
             ,
             <a href="https://www1.careington.com/Index.aspx" target="_blank">
              Careington
             </a>
             and
             <a href="http://www.dentalplans.com" target="_blank">
              DentalPlans
             </a>
             .
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Minerva Studio/Shutterstock" data-media-id="2774323" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/3/S2774323/slug/l/dentist-doing-a-dental-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/3/S2774323/slug/s/dentist-doing-a-dental-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/3/S2774323/slug/l/dentist-doing-a-dental-2.jpg">
             10. Join a discount health plan
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             "If you require a medication that isn't available as a generic yet, joining a discount club could reduce what you pay for prescription drugs dramatically," notes Beck. These discount cards are often free. Also ask if your pharmacy has any prescription reward programs. These provide the incentive for pharmacy loyalty, saving you money and ensuring repeat business for the pharmacy.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Natalia Bratslavsky/Shutterstock" data-media-id="2774344" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/4/S2774344/slug/l/tablets-and-capsules-in-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/4/S2774344/slug/s/tablets-and-capsules-in-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/4/S2774344/slug/l/tablets-and-capsules-in-2.jpg">
             11. Get a prescription discount card
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Your annual vision exam may require an updated prescription, but buying glasses or contacts from your doctor may mean paying up to 50 percent more. Instead, go online to find less expensive -- and sometimes more fashionable -- options.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Alamy" data-media-id="2774342" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/2/S2774342/slug/l/c1h6rd-ophthalmologist-examining-a-womans-eyes-with-a-phorop-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/2/S2774342/slug/s/c1h6rd-ophthalmologist-examining-a-womans-eyes-with-a-phorop-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/2/S2774342/slug/l/c1h6rd-ophthalmologist-examining-a-womans-eyes-with-a-phorop-2.jpg">
             12. Don't buy glasses or contacts from your doctor
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             Receiving medical care from a provider out-of-network can cost you much more than using someone who's in-network. Before booking an appointment, call to confirm the office's network status with your insurance company. Ensuring you only see in-network providers can become difficult in a hospital, but make sure the hospital staff knows you have a strong preference for in-network physicians.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Alexander Raths/Shutterstock" data-media-id="2774324" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/4/S2774324/slug/l/woman-getting-ultrasound-of-a-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/4/S2774324/slug/s/woman-getting-ultrasound-of-a-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/432/4/S2774324/slug/l/woman-getting-ultrasound-of-a-2.jpg">
             13. Always use in-network providers
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             <strong>
             </strong>
             "Many universities offer services like dental care and acupuncture for a fraction of what you would normally pay at a practice," says Erenbaum. "The work is done by students under their professor's guidance."The
             <a href="http://www.ada.org/267.aspx" target="_blank">
              American Dental Association
             </a>
             and the
             <a href="http://www.acaom.org/find-a-school" target="_blank">
              Accreditation Commission for Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine
             </a>
             list accredited schools.
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Brand X/Getty Images" data-media-id="2774340" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/0/S2774340/slug/l/acupuncture-acupuncturist-inserting-a-needle-into-a-clients-2.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/0/S2774340/slug/s/acupuncture-acupuncturist-inserting-a-needle-into-a-clients-2.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/0/S2774340/slug/l/acupuncture-acupuncturist-inserting-a-needle-into-a-clients-2.jpg">
             14. Consider university programs
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>
           <li class="knot-data-item">
            <div class="description">
             "Be aware and take advantage of treatment at free clinics when available," advises Beck. "Particularly in urban areas, there are opportunities to treat some conditions for free, namely those that pose a risk to public health. For example, if you are concerned about a sexually transmitted infection, a visit to the city health center may involve a depressing waiting room, but the screening and treatments will be free."
            </div>
            <a class="knot-anchor" data-credit="Robert Kneschke/Shutterstock" data-media-id="2774341" data-photo-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/1/S2774341/slug/l/different-people-sitting-in-a-waiting-room-of-a-hospital-1.jpg" data-thumbnail-src="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/1/S2774341/slug/s/different-people-sitting-in-a-waiting-room-of-a-hospital-1.jpg" data-tweet-link="" data-type="image" href="http://www.blogcdn.com/slideshows/images/slides/277/434/1/S2774341/slug/l/different-people-sitting-in-a-waiting-room-of-a-hospital-1.jpg">
             15. Take advantage of free treatments
            </a>
            <b class="knot-player">
            </b>
           </li>

         Increase your money and finance knowledge from home
        </h3>
        <div class="body clearfix">
         <div class="cell">

           The author of this article is quite young --- and her lack of financial experience shows in what she has written.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Advising anyone how to work the banking system and avoid changing a habit of not keeping track of your checking account balance (resulting in NSF checks) is essentially bad advice. The solution to that problem is simple. Don't spend more than you have in your checking account. No one ever got rich by paying overdraft fees to financial institutions.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Relying on a credit card to provide "cash" for emergencies is also bad advice. The tougher it is for you to save money to build a CASH emergency fund --- the more you need to do that. (Just for the record --- buying a new iPhone, or a new pair of cute shoes, or the latest video game is NOT an emergency.)

           <br/>
           <br/>
           When she gets a little older (and, hopefully) a little wiser) she will probably flinch with embarrassment when she looks back at this article.

          <p>
           I respectfully disagree with your advice to keep a credit card for emergencies. The "fire extinguisher" is definitely needed, but it is best to have it in the form of an emergency fund. The fact that so many households spend all of their pay might make saving such a fund seem impossible. But in the vast majority of cases, it isn't. For a period of time, many people really do need to cut back on their lattes - and anything else they can manage to do without - to get on the right track. It's not a forever deprivation. And sometimes, we start out by "depriving" ourselves of something only to find out in the end that we don't miss it. I'm with Dave Ramsey on this: It's not easy to get on the right track. Cutting costs, saving an emergency fund, and reducing debt all go against the grain - initially. I know that from personal experience. But I also know that people are resilient. We're tougher than we realize. A period of discomfort gives way to confidence and peace. It's worth it to feel deprived and out of our comfort zone for a while.

            <p>
             @ Prudence --- Good advice, but most people won't follow it. They are focused on staying in their comfort zone --- not on saving and having a better life.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Dave Ramsey is right on target about this. Saving is definitely not an easy habit to learn. It's basically simple --- but not easy. You have to REALLY want a better financial future much more than you want to continue doing the same stuff that has resulted in you being constantly broke and worrying about money.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             People who are broke and in debt tend to take advice from others they know who are just as broke as they are. (That's part of the comfort level.) They also usually make the pursuit of constant entertainment their first priority in life. Acquiring financial knowledge isn't a big priority for someone living from one paycheck to the next. Entertainment is No. 1. If they ever sit down and write out a list of everything they are spending $$ on that fits under the heading of "Entertainment", their list would be very long.
             <br/>
             <br/>
             Making a decision to cut expenses (starting with entertainment) and save money can be very scarey, at first. (The unknown path is always scarey.) It takes courage to break out of the same spending and debting pattern that everyone you know is following. But, you won't feel that uncomfortable forever. After a while --- when you see your savings balance growing --- THAT will feel better than mindless spending ever did.
            </p>
