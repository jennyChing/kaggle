         <h1>
          Should managers strive to be liked?
         </h1>
        </div>
        <div class="post">
         <h4>
          <div class="date">
           October 23, 2013
           <a href="http://www.thoughtleadersllc.com/2013/10/should-managers-strive-to-be-liked//#comments_template">
            2 Comments
           </a>

           <p>
            <em>
             <a href="http://www.thoughtleadersllc.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/20131023-Bill-Lumbergh-and-Michael-Scott.jpg" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">

             </a>
             Todays post is by Aaron McDaniel, author of
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1601632428/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=211189&amp;creative=373489&amp;creativeASIN=1601632428&amp;link_code=as3&amp;tag=tlb-20" target="_blank">
              The Young Professionals Guide to the Working World
             </a>
             (
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1601632428/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=211189&amp;creative=373489&amp;creativeASIN=1601632428&amp;link_code=as3&amp;tag=tlb-20" target="_blank">
              <strong>

              </strong>

             to get your copy).
             <br/>
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
            So, Peter, whats happening? Aahh, now, are you going to go ahead and have those TPS reports for us this afternoon?
           </p>
           <p>
            I can still picture Bill Lumbergh, played by Gary Cole (the infamous boss from the cult favorite Office Space) saying these magical words, reaffirming that he is the boss who everyone loved to hate.
           </p>
           <p>
            For managers, there are a number of scales that must be artfully balanced: giving specific direction versus the freedom to think freely; command and control versus consensus.
           </p>
           <p>
            One that is particularly hard to balance is between being liked, respected and even feared by their employees. The question beckons, should managers strive to be liked?
           </p>
           <p>
            The answer is simple yet complex. No, but also yes. Ultimately the goal should not to be liked but at the same time to be likeable.
           </p>
           <p>
            Bill Lumbergh struggled on both ends of this spectrum. He was disliked by his staff and was also a giant jerk (not that likeable). The boss of Dunder Mifflins Scranton branch (from the show The Office), Michael Scott (played by Steve Carrel), was too consumed with being liked by his staff that he became ineffective, letting them get away with many things, preventing them from reaching peak performance.
           </p>
           <p>
            Effective managers find balance: a way to be likeable, so that their employees will follow their lead and buy-in to their vision and goals. At the same time, they dont concern themselves with whether their team likes them. Respect is more important to employee empowerment than being liked.
           </p>
           <p>
            <span id="more-9041">
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            In my own career, there have been times where my management style led me all over the map with regards to likeability and striving to be liked.
           </p>
           <p>
            In my first management job, where I managed a group of unionized call center reps, I was very focused on being liked. I was not as strict with rules as I should have been, and my people pushed the rules with me (the inner Michael Scott came out a bit too much).
           </p>
           <p>
            In my next management role, I swung a bit too far the other direction. I was managing a team of installation and repair technicians during the launch of a new television service my company brought to market. With little sympathy, I routinely had to tell my employees to stay at a customers house well into the night (sometimes after they had been there for 16 hours) to finish a job. My inner Bill Lumbergh was a bit too active.
           </p>
           <p>
            Then, in my 3rd management position, I found a good balance. In managing a sales team, I was able to toggle between pushing my employees hard to meet objectives but also intertwined humor and informality into our team culture, increasing my likeability.
           </p>
           <p>
            While I had to learn this balance through trial and error (as many managers do), here are key ways to increase your likeability while not worrying about being liked:
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Learn to say No
            </strong>
            : This can be a hard one, especially for new managers. Its okay to stick to your guns and not let certain things fly. When saying no, recognize that your employee chose to ask you instead of doing something on their own and then explain why you are saying no. Most importantly, follow-through with your no and dont be a pushover.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Show your vulnerability
            </strong>
            : Managers can increase their likeability by admitting when they dont know the answers and by asking for help. Be willing to step down from being all-knowing and all-seeing in the eyes of your people.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Be self-deprecating
            </strong>
            : Using yourself as the punch line is an effective way to increase your likeability. I have let my teams make fun of me for certain things and it helps the team bond and buy-in to our goals. If it has to be at your expense sometimes (in good fun), then go for it.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Be flexible on the path but strict on the principles
            </strong>
            : Managers that strive to be liked are more apt to giving in and acting against their principles. Staying true to your principles and the teams core values may keep you from being liked, but it does increase the respect your team has for you.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Support your people
            </strong>
            : It is so important to have your teams back. When they are in trouble, it is the time for you to come in and remove obstacles to help them turn things around.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Show an interest in what is important to them
            </strong>
            : Showing your employees that you care does wonders for your likeability. Your genuine interest in their passions makes them more passionate about doing their job. This never requires you to be a pushover or to give in.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Celebrate the right way
            </strong>
            : When you recognize people for their accomplishments in a genuine and appropriate fashion, it improves your likeability in their eyes. The celebration helps them forgive all the pushing and the nos that you give them.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
              Be okay with not being invited to lunch or happy hour
            </strong>
            : After managing my sales team for a couple years, noticed that when my employees got together for lunch or happy hour I was never invited. At first I took offense. My first reaction was, why wasnt I included? But then I realized that was my inner Michael Scott jumping out. When I took a step back and thought about it, I was glad that I wasnt included with the group. My employees didnt see me as a friend, they saw me as a boss.
           </p>
           <p>
            Its fine to be friends with your employees, but that shouldnt be your primary objective.
           </p>
           <p>
            At the same time, dont want to swing too far the other direction, letting your inner Lumbergh come out. Use the tips outlined above to increase your likeability while still maintaining your place as an authority figure.
           </p>
           <p>
            The next time doubt enters your mind about how to interact with your team and how hard (or soft) to be on them, remember that effective managers balance being likeable without aspiring to be liked.
           </p>

               Thanks for sharing Aaron. As you point out, balance is key. I think its a huge mistake when managers separate their business and professional lives to the extent that they fail to connect with their staff. As a manager youll get far more accomplished if your people like you than if they dont. I say that because numerous studies from social psychology show that people will be more willing to do what you ask if they like you. A manger can make that happen without becoming someone who doesnt adhere to their rules and gives in constantly. Being strategic and looking for things you have in common with your direct reports and/or offering up genuine compliments are two simple ways to connect with people and build liking without compromising. In addition, Ive seen managers who choose to start connecting with people end up enjoying their team and job much, much more.
