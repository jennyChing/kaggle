     <p>
      <b>
       Kip Stephen Thorne
      </b>
      (born June1, 1940) is an American
      <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">
       theoretical physicist
      </a>
      , known for his contributions in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
       gravitational physics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
       astrophysics
      </a>
      . A longtime friend and colleague of
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">
       Stephen Hawking
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">
       Carl Sagan
      </a>
      , he was the
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">
       Feynman
      </a>
      Professor of Theoretical Physics at the
      <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">
       California Institute of Technology
      </a>
      (Caltech) until 2009
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-its.caltech.edu_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-its.caltech.edu-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is one of the world's leading experts on the astrophysical implications of
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
       Einstein's
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general theory of relativity
      </a>
      . He continues to do scientific research and was the scientific consultant and an executive producer for the 2014 science fiction film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">
        Interstellar
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Thorne was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Logan,_Utah" title="Logan, Utah">
       Logan, Utah
      </a>
      on June 1, 1940, the son of
      <a href="/wiki/Utah_State_University" title="Utah State University">
       Utah State University
      </a>
      professors D.Wynne Thorne and Alison (ne Cornish) Thorne, a
      <a href="/wiki/Soil_chemistry" title="Soil chemistry">
       soil chemist
      </a>
      and an
      <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">
       economist
      </a>
      , respectively.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Raised in an academic environment, two of his four siblings also became professors. Thorne's parents were members of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">
       The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
      </a>
      and raised Thorne in the LDS faith, though he now describes himself as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atheist" title="Atheist">
       atheist
      </a>
      . Regarding his views on science and religion, Thorne has stated: "There are large numbers of my finest colleagues who are quite devout and believe in God [...] There is no fundamental incompatibility between science and religion. I happen to not believe in God."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Thorne rapidly excelled at academics early in life, becoming one of the youngest full professors in the history of the
      <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">
       California Institute of Technology
      </a>
      . He received his
      <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">
       B.S.
      </a>
      degree from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltech" title="Caltech">
       Caltech
      </a>
      in 1962, and
      <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">
       Ph.D.
      </a>
      degree from
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton University
      </a>
      in 1965. He wrote his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctoral_thesis" title="Doctoral thesis">
       doctoral thesis
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Geometrodynamics" title="Geometrodynamics">
        Geometrodynamics
       </a>
       of Cylindrical Systems
      </i>
      , under the supervision of relativist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Archibald_Wheeler" title="John Archibald Wheeler">
       John Wheeler
      </a>
      . Thorne returned to Caltech as an associate professor in 1967 and became a professor of theoretical physics in 1970, the William R. Kenan,Jr. Professor in 1981, and the
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">
       Feynman
      </a>
      Professor of Theoretical Physics in 1991. In June 2009 he resigned his Feynman Professorship (he is now the Feynman Professor of Theoretical Physics, Emeritus) to pursue a career of writing and movie making. His first film project was
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">
        Interstellar
       </a>
      </i>
      , working with
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">
       Christopher Nolan
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Throughout the years, Thorne has served as a mentor and thesis advisor for many leading theorists who now work on observational, experimental, or astrophysical aspects of general relativity. Approximately 50 physicists have received Ph.D.s at Caltech under Thorne's personal mentorship.
     </p>
     <p>
      Thorne is known for his ability to convey the excitement and significance of discoveries in gravitation and astrophysics to both professional and lay audiences. In 1999, Thorne made some speculations on what the 21st century will find as the answers to the following questions:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Is there a "dark side of the universe" populated by objects such as black holes?
      </li>
      <li>
       Can we observe the birth of the universe and its dark side using radiation made from space-time warpage, or so-called "gravitational waves"?
      </li>
      <li>
       Will 21st century technology reveal quantum behavior in the realm of human-size objects?
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      His presentations on subjects such as
      <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">
       black holes
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_radiation" title="Gravitational radiation">
       gravitational radiation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">
       relativity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">
       time travel
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Wormhole" title="Wormhole">
       wormholes
      </a>
      have been included in
      <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">
       PBS
      </a>
      shows in the U.S. and in the United Kingdom on the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Thorne and Linda Jean Peterson married in 1960. Their children are Kares Anne and Bret Carter, an architect. Thorne and Peterson divorced in 1977. Thorne and his second wife, Carolee Joyce Winstein, a professor of
      <a href="/wiki/Kinesiology" title="Kinesiology">
       biokinesiology
      </a>
      and physical therapy at
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">
       USC
      </a>
      , married in 1984.
     </p>

     <p>
      Thorne's research has principally focused on
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       relativistic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics#Theoretical_astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
       astrophysics
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_star" title="Relativistic star">
       relativistic stars

      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">
       black holes
      </a>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_waves" title="Gravitational waves">

      </a>
      .

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gravitational_waves_and_LIGO">
       Gravitational waves and LIGO
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kip_Thorne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gravitational waves and LIGO">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Thorne's work has dealt with the prediction of gravity-wave strengths and their temporal signatures as observed on Earth. These "signatures" are of great relevance to
      <a href="/wiki/LIGO" title="LIGO">
       LIGO
      </a>
      (Laser Interferometer Gravitational Wave Observatory), a multi-institution gravity-wave experiment for which Thorne has been a leading proponent  in 1984, he cofounded the LIGO Project (the largest project ever funded by the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">
       NSF
      </a>
      ) to discern and measure any fluctuations between two or more 'static' points; such fluctuations would be evidence of
      <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
       gravitational waves
      </a>
      , as calculations describe. A significant aspect of his research is developing the
      <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
       mathematics
      </a>
      necessary to analyze these objects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thorne also carries out
      <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">
       engineering
      </a>
      design analyses for features of the LIGO that cannot be developed on the basis of
      <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">
       experiment
      </a>
      and he gives advice on
      <a href="/wiki/Data_analysis" title="Data analysis">
       data analysis
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">
       algorithms
      </a>
      by which the waves will be sought. He has provided theoretical support for LIGO, including identifying gravitational wave sources that LIGO should target, designing the baffles to control
      <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">
       scattered light
      </a>
      in the LIGO beam tubes, and  in collaboration with Vladimir Braginsky's (Moscow, Russia) research group  inventing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_Nondemolition_measurement" title="Quantum Nondemolition measurement">
       quantum nondemolition
      </a>
      designs for advanced
      <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
       gravity-wave
      </a>
      detectors and ways to reduce the most serious kind of
      <a href="/wiki/Noise" title="Noise">
       noise
      </a>
      in advanced detectors:
      <a href="/wiki/Johnson%E2%80%93Nyquist_noise" title="JohnsonNyquist noise">
       thermoelastic noise
      </a>
      . With
      <a href="/wiki/Carlton_M._Caves" title="Carlton M. Caves">
       Carlton M. Caves
      </a>
      , Thorne invented the back-action-evasion approach to quantum nondemolition measurements of the
      <a href="/wiki/Quadrature_amplitude_modulation" title="Quadrature amplitude modulation">
       quadrature amplitudes
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator" title="Harmonic oscillator">
       harmonic oscillators
      </a>
       a technique applicable both in
      <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">
       gravitational wave
      </a>
      detection and
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_optics" title="Quantum optics">
       quantum optics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Black_hole_cosmology">
       Black hole cosmology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kip_Thorne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Black hole cosmology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Hoop_Conjecture" title="Hoop Conjecture">
       Hoop Conjecture
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Thorne has made contributions to
      <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">
       black hole cosmology
      </a>
      . Thorne proposed his
      <a href="/wiki/Hoop_Conjecture" title="Hoop Conjecture">
       Hoop Conjecture
      </a>
      that cast aside the thought of a
      <a href="/wiki/Naked_singularity" title="Naked singularity">
       naked singularity
      </a>
      . The Hoop Conjecture describes an imploding star turning into a black hole when the critical circumference of the designed hoop can be placed around it and set into rotation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      That is, any object of mass M around which a hoop of circumference
      <img alt="\begin{matrix} \frac{4 \pi GM}{c^2} \end{matrix}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/0/0/30059ababf97bc94c4b0e745af600f94.png"/>
      can be spun must be a black hole. As a tool to be used in both enterprises, astrophysics and theoretical physics, Thorne has developed an unusual approach, called the "
      <a href="/wiki/Membrane_paradigm" title="Membrane paradigm">
       Membrane Paradigm
      </a>
      ", to the theory of black holes and used it to clarify the "Blandford-Znajek" mechanism by which black holes may power some
      <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">
       quasars
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">
       active galactic nuclei
      </a>
      . Thorne has investigated the
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">
       quantum
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">
       statistical mechanical
      </a>
      origin of the
      <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">
       entropy
      </a>
      of a black hole and the entropy of a cosmological horizon in an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">
       inflationary model
      </a>
      of the universe. With Wojciech Zurek he showed that the entropy of a black hole of known
      <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
       mass
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
       angular momentum
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">
       electric charge
      </a>
      is the
      <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">
       logarithm
      </a>
      of the number of ways that the hole could have been made. With
      <a href="/wiki/Igor_Dmitriyevich_Novikov" title="Igor Dmitriyevich Novikov">
       Igor Novikov
      </a>
      and Don Page he developed the general relativistic theory of thin
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
       accretion disks
      </a>
      around black holes, and using this theory he deduced that with a doubling of its mass by such
      <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
       accretion
      </a>
      a black hole will be spun up to 0.998 of the maximum
      <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">
       spin
      </a>
      allowed by general relativity, but not any farther. This is probably the maximum black-hole spin allowed in nature. He, along with his mentor
      <a href="/wiki/John_Archibald_Wheeler" title="John Archibald Wheeler">
       John Wheeler
      </a>
      , additionally proved that it was impossible for cylindrical
      <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
       magnetic field
      </a>
      lines to
      <a href="/wiki/Implosion_(mechanical_process)" title="Implosion (mechanical process)">
       implode
      </a>
      . Both
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">
       Hawking
      </a>
      and Thorne have theorized that a
      <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_singularity" title="Gravitational singularity">
       singularity
      </a>
      exists in the interior of a black hole.
     </p>

     <p>
      Thorne was one of the first people to conduct scientific research on whether the laws of physics permit space and time to be
      <a href="/wiki/Simply_connected_space" title="Simply connected space">
       multiply connected
      </a>
      (can there exist classical,
      <a href="/wiki/Wormhole#Traversable_wormholes" title="Wormhole">
       traversable wormholes
      </a>
      and "
      <a href="/wiki/Time_travel" title="Time travel">
       time machines
      </a>
      "?). With Sung-Won Kim, Thorne identified a universal physical mechanism (the explosive growth of
      <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_polarization" title="Vacuum polarization">
       vacuum polarization
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)#Quantum_fields" title="Field (physics)">
       quantum fields
      </a>
      ), that may always prevent spacetime from developing
      <a href="/wiki/Closed_timelike_curve" title="Closed timelike curve">
       closed timelike curves
      </a>
      (i.e., prevent "backward time travel"
      <i>
       ).
      </i>
      With Mike Morris and Ulvi Yurtsever he showed that traversable
      <a href="/wiki/Wormhole#Traversable_wormholes" title="Wormhole">
       Lorentzian wormholes
      </a>
      can exist in the structure of
      <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
       spacetime
      </a>
      only if they are threaded by quantum fields in
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_state" title="Quantum state">
       quantum states
      </a>
      that violate the
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_condition#Mathematical_statement" title="Energy condition">
       averaged null energy condition
      </a>
      (i.e. have negative renormalized energy spread over a sufficiently large region). This has triggered research to explore the ability of quantum fields to possess such extended
      <a href="/wiki/Negative_energy" title="Negative energy">
       negative energy
      </a>
      . Recent calculations by Thorne indicate that simple masses passing through traversable wormholes could never engender
      <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">
       paradoxes
      </a>
       there are
      <i>
       no
      </i>
      initial conditions that lead to paradox once time travel is introduced. If his results can be generalised, they would suggest that none of the supposed paradoxes formulated in time travel stories can actually be formulated at a precise physical level: that is, that
      <i>
       any
      </i>
      situation in a time travel story turns out to permit
      <i>
       many
      </i>
      consistent solutions.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Relativistic_stars.2C_multipole_moments_and_other_endeavors">
       Relativistic stars, multipole moments and other endeavors
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kip_Thorne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relativistic stars, multipole moments and other endeavors">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      With
      <a href="/wiki/Anna_N._%C5%BBytkow" title="Anna N. ytkow">
       Anna ytkow
      </a>
      , Thorne predicted the existence of
      <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">
       red supergiant stars
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">
       neutron-star
      </a>
      cores (
      <a href="/wiki/Thorne%E2%80%93%C5%BBytkow_object" title="Thorneytkow object">
       Thorneytkow objects
      </a>
      ). He laid the foundations for the theory of
      <a href="/wiki/Angular_frequency" title="Angular frequency">
       pulsations
      </a>
      of relativistic stars and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_radiation" title="Gravitational radiation">
       gravitational radiation
      </a>
      they emit. With
      <a href="/wiki/James_Hartle" title="James Hartle">
       James Hartle
      </a>
      , Thorne derived from general relativity the laws of motion and precession of black holes and other relativistic bodies, including the influence of the coupling of their
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multipole_moments" title="Multipole moments">
       multipole moments
      </a>
      to the spacetime curvature of nearby objects. Thorne has also theoretically predicted the existence of universally antigravitating "
      <a href="/wiki/Exotic_matter" title="Exotic matter">
       exotic matter
      </a>
      "  the element needed to accelerate the expansion rate of the universe, keep traversable wormhole "Star Gates" open and keep
      <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">
       timelike
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">
       geodesic
      </a>
      free float "
      <a href="/wiki/Alcubierre_drive" title="Alcubierre drive">
       warp drives
      </a>
      " working. With Clifford Will and others of his students, he laid the foundations for the theoretical interpretation of experimental
      <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity" title="Tests of general relativity">
       tests of relativistic theories of gravity
      </a>
       foundations on which Will and others then built. Thorne is currently interested in the origin of classical space and time from the
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_foam" title="Quantum foam">
       quantum foam
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">
       quantum gravity
      </a>
      theory.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_in_media">
       Adaptation in media
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

        edit

        ]
       </span>
      </span>

     <p>
      Thorne contributed ideas on wormhole travel to Carl Sagan for use in his novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Contact_(novel)" title="Contact (novel)">
        Contact
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tech_11-0">

        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Thorne and his friend, producer
      <a href="/wiki/Lynda_Obst" title="Lynda Obst">
       Lynda Obst
      </a>
      , also developed the concept for the
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">
       Christopher Nolan
      </a>
      film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_(film)" title="Interstellar (film)">
        Interstellar
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also wrote a tie-in book,
      <i>
       The Science of Interstellar
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Larry_Niven" title="Larry Niven">
       Larry Niven
      </a>
      's novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Mars" title="Rainbow Mars">
        Rainbow Mars
       </a>
      </i>
      , the time travel technology used in the novel is based on the wormhole theories of Thorne, which in the context of the novel was when time travel first became possible, rather than just fantasy. As a result, any attempts to travel in time prior to Thorne's development of wormhole theory results in the time traveller entering a fantastic version of reality, rather than the actual past.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Theory_of_Everything_(2014_film)" title="The Theory of Everything (2014 film)">
        The Theory of Everything
       </a>
      </i>
      , Thorne was portrayed by actor
      <a href="/wiki/Enzo_Cilenti" title="Enzo Cilenti">
       Enzo Cilenti
      </a>
