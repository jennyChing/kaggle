       <p>
        A few weeks back, I found myself at a wedding, seated at a table with complete strangers. I struck up a conversation with the man next to me. After a few minutes of pleasantries, our conversation took an unexpected turn: "Do you remember those three hikers?" he asked. I looked at him quizzically. "You remember," he added, "they were Berkeley students, caught in Iran. They spent a couple years in prison."
       </p>
       <p>
        "Yes," I replied evenly, "I remember."
       </p>
       <p>
        "You really have to try hard to get 'lost' in Iran," he said with a conspiratorial wink. "Those guys really caused a lot of trouble."
       </p>
       <p>
        This well-intentioned man clearly was unaware not only that I was one of the "hikers," but also that my husband,
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=opinion&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Shane+Bauer%22">
         Shane Bauer
        </a>
        (sitting next to me), my friend
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=opinion&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22Josh+Fattal%22">
         Josh Fattal
        </a>
        and I hadn't been "Berkeley students" for years when we were beckoned across an unmarked border by an armed Iranian patrol, kidnapped and incarcerated in Tehran's notorious Evin Prison. He no doubt also didn't know that Shane and I had been living in Damascus at the time, or that we were hiking in Iraqi Kurdistan when we were captured, a tourist destination touted as safe by both U.S. officials and PR firms, not Iran.
       </p>
       <p>
        My table companion also probably wasn't aware that
        <a href="/search/?action=search&amp;channel=opinion&amp;inlineLink=1&amp;searchindex=gsa&amp;query=%22The+Associated+Press%22">
         the Associated Press
        </a>
        recently reported that it was a series of secret talks between high-level U.S. and Iranian officials, facilitated and hosted by the Sultan of Oman, that paved the road for the historic agreement over Iran's nuclear program. According to these reports, it was the Omani-led negotiations to free Josh, Shane and me from prison that generated sufficient trust between nations, and put the sultan in a position to help end the U.S.-Iran stalemate.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Ironically," said the AP report, "efforts to win the release of the three American hikers turned out to be instrumental in making the clandestine diplomacy possible."
       </p>
       <p>
        The fact that our imprisonment was bound up with larger geopolitical issues was no surprise to me. But the new spin, that the 410 days I had spent in solitary, illegal detention in Iran had led to positive international ends, had yet to sink in. Imagine being kidnapped while on vacation and imprisoned. You and your friends get out alive, thankfully, and a few years later, just when life is starting to feel normal again, the way you've told your story again and again is challenged. "It was all for the greater good," the new story goes.
       </p>
       <p>
        Psychological studies show that a constructive inner narrative is an essential component of survival in and recovery from traumatic experiences. For months after I was captured, I woke up every morning in complete disbelief. "This doesn't make sense," I told myself. In short, being imprisoned as an enemy spy didn't fit into my personal narrative.
       </p>
       <p>
        Eventually, I was able to replace that negative story with the positive belief that, if and when I was freed, my experience would serve to help me fight injustice. That shift helped me keep my sanity and continues to motivate my work today. Still, years later, why is it difficult for me to embrace the idea that my wrongful detention was ultimately for the greater good?
       </p>
       <p>
        According to utilitarian philosophy, the suffering of my two companions and I could be justified by the benefits it has now been shown to have contributed to bringing about. After all, the recent nuclear agreement, in addition to stopping Iran's high-level uranium enrichment, has begun to result in the alleviation of economic sanctions, which long have made ordinary Iranians suffer. Above all, the deal has dramatically reduced the incalculable risk of a new war in the Middle East.
       </p>
       <p>
        Yet this pragmatic moral framework can be deeply problematic. In the long view of history, almost anything can be justified - holding people indefinitely without trial for decades for the sake of national security (e.g., Guantanamo), hostage-taking for diplomatic breakthroughs, slavery for economic development and war for the protection of "national interests." Who do we charge with the authority to make decisions that sacrifice the well-being of the few for the many? How can we reconcile these pragmatic decisions with human rights? Where do we draw the line and - perhaps more importantly - who draws it?
       </p>
       <p>
        After my release from prison, I had the privilege of joining the international campaign for the release of Shane and Josh. I worked closely with diplomats from Oman, Venezuela, Iraq and the United States. Throughout that year before their release, I heard numerous times from foreign diplomats that my government wasn't acting decisively - even that it was making our situation worse by refusing to engage, even indirectly, with the Iranian government. It gradually dawned on me that the United States might be stalling precisely because our case was tied up with larger issues, like a nuclear deal. Simply put, though our capture was a random event, our long imprisonment was not.
       </p>
       <p>
        It's dangerous and wrong for governments to use innocent people as pawns in a geopolitical game - even if they do sometimes yield positive results. Yet, ultimately, this historic compromise between the so-called enemy nations is the result of much more than cynical political maneuvers and clever diplomacy. It took decades of sacrifice from millions of Iranians to force this change in their government. The American people played a part, too. By eschewing a military attack on Syria last summer, Americans made it clear to our government that we will not support another U.S.-led war in the Middle East.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Iranian and American people have forced their governments to change - thereby creating a saner, safer world for all of us. That's something I'm proud to have played a part in, however small it might have been. Had I been given the choice - who knows? - I may have even chosen to make that sacrifice.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>

       </p>

       </p>
