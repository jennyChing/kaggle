               3 reasons why you find it hard to trust
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p class="blog-date">
              <span class="date-text">
               22/6/2015
              </span>
             </p>
             <p class="blog-comments">
              <a class="blog-link" data-disqus-identifier="192967237401769686" href="http://www.mattfoxcounselling.co.uk/wellbeing-lifelines-blog/3-reasons-why-you-find-it-hard-to-trust#comments">

              <br/>
              <br/>
              Do you ever have any of these narratives go on in your head? Its a bit like watching TV. On screen the people are saying one thing, but the subtitles are saying something completely different.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              At the heart of this thinking, there may be anxiety or fear. But below that, if you really look hard, its the big T word that looms large. Yes, TRUST. Theres something in you that makes it really difficult to completely trust anyone at face value.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              So why is that? There can be lots of reasons of course, but when trust becomes an all pervasive issue in your life, it starts having a real impact.

              <a class="blog-title-link blog-link" href="http://www.mattfoxcounselling.co.uk/wellbeing-lifelines-blog/the-one-thing-i-wish-i-could-do-more-of">
               The one thing I wish I could do more of
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p class="blog-date">
              <span class="date-text">
               10/6/2015
              </span>
             </p>
             <p class="blog-comments">
              <a class="blog-link" data-disqus-identifier="291211439996820276" href="http://www.mattfoxcounselling.co.uk/wellbeing-lifelines-blog/the-one-thing-i-wish-i-could-do-more-of#comments">

            <div class="blog-separator">

            <div class="blog-content">
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none" style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="The single thing I wish I could do more of |wellbeing lifelines |matt fox counselling" src="/uploads/2/8/0/1/28018853/6260227_orig.jpg" style="width:auto;max-width:100%"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              I was walking to work this week and two adults on bikes swerved passed me on the pavement narrowly missing me. Not anything unusual in that. I see it happening all the time.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              But I noticed my chest tightening, my breathing become shallower.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              In my mind, all sorts of thoughts were popping up, most of which werent too polite.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              You see when I was 8, I was knocked down by someone riding fast on a pavement. I wasnt too badly hurt but I was very scared and in shock as a result.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Now whenever I see someone riding close to me on the pavement, my body remembers that experience and I tense up. Its not that Im in real danger each time, but my automatic response is to brace myself, as if I might be.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Now this article isnt so much about the effects of trauma, though you might also wonder how your body responds to memories, both good and bad.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Its more about what you do with those memories as a response to the here and now. You may have noticed me alluding to my internal dialogue, including some choice swearing at the pavement cyclists. So what is it about that inner rant?
              <br/>
              <br/>

               The truth about being in control
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p class="blog-date">
              <span class="date-text">
               24/5/2015
              </span>
             </p>
             <p class="blog-comments">
              <a class="blog-link" data-disqus-identifier="142539676833014454" href="http://www.mattfoxcounselling.co.uk/wellbeing-lifelines-blog/the-truth-about-being-in-control#comments">
               Comments
              </a>
             </p>

            <div class="blog-separator">
            </div>
            <div class="blog-content">
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none" style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="The truth about control | matt fox counselling" src="/uploads/2/8/0/1/28018853/615434845.jpg" style="width:100%;max-width:800px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              Sometimes the world feels a scary place. And Im not talking just about floods, famine, wars, random acts of violence. Even the day to day can feel overwhelming.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Do you find yourself worrying what might happen when your family gets together,and who might say what to whom? Or your colleagues want to do a project in a way that doesnt feel right? Or even for simple things like your partner doesnt stack the dishwasher the way you would (or doesnt do it at all)?
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Perhaps its last minute changes of plan that dont sit well with you. Or the friend who drops in spontaneously. The laid back partner or friend who goes with the flow and never makes any plans.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              These acts of non-conformity or not going with the plan can feel very anxiety-making. Some are like affronts to all of what you feel is ok and acceptable. In extreme, my guess is that they make you not only very anxious but under the surface, angry too sometimes.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              So what is it about the need to keep things on the straight and narrow? Why do you feel so uncomfortable when things dont go the way you want them to?
