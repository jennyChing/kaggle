          <h1 class="article__title">
           Could a minimum-wage earner in 1978 earn enough in a summer to pay a full year's tuition?
          </h1>
          <p class="article__meta">
           By
           <a class="link" href="/truth-o-meter/staff/louis-jacobson/">
            Louis Jacobson
           </a>
           on Friday, August 8th, 2014 at 12:09 p.m.
          </p>
          <figure class="art-block">
           <div class="pfphoto">
            <img src="http://static.politifact.com.s3.amazonaws.com:80/politifact%2Fphotos%2FTuition_meme.jpg"/>
           </div>
           <figcaption class="art-block__caption">
            A reader sent us this social media meme, so we checked to see whether it was accurate.
           </figcaption>
          </figure>
         </header>
         <div class="article__text">
          <p>
           Everything was better in the old days, apparently -- including the chore of paying for college, at least according to a social media meme sent to us recently by a reader.
          </p>
          <p>
           The
           <a href="https://www.facebook.com/OurTimeOrg/photos/a.196549437047190.41358.160389977329803/721170997918362/?type=1&amp;theater" style="text-decoration:none;">
            <u>
             meme
            </u>
           </a>
           -- created by OurTime.org, an advocacy group for young Americans -- said, "In 1978, a student who worked a minimum-wage summer job could afford to pay a year's full tuition at the 4-year public university of their choice."
          </p>
          <p>
           Really? We figured this was worth a look.
          </p>
          <p>
           First, the minimum wage. Starting on Jan. 1, 1978, the
           <a href="http://www.dol.gov/whd/minwage/chart.htm" style="text-decoration:none;">
            <u>
             minimum wage
            </u>
           </a>
           was $2.65. Someone working at the minimum wage for 13 weeks, and 40 hours per week, in the summer of 1978 would have ended up with $1,378 for their labors.
          </p>
          <p>
           For the tuition they would have faced in the 1978-79 school year, we turned to
           <a href="http://nces.ed.gov/programs/digest/d12/tables/dt12_381.asp" style="text-decoration:none;">
            <u>
             figures
            </u>
           </a>
           from the National Center for Education Statistics, the federal governments repository of education data.
          </p>
          <p>
           The cost of tuition and fees (in that years dollars, not adjusted for inflation) was $688 for in-state residents attending a four-year, public university.
          </p>
          <p>
           So the memes claim is correct except for a few caveats -- two minor and one more significant:
          </p>
          <p>
            The cost of tuition and fees plus room and board was significantly higher that year --
           <strong>
           </strong>
           $2,145 -- but the meme was careful to cite "tuition" only, so well ignore the impact of room and board.
          </p>
          <p>
            The tuition-and-fees figure we used is a national average, so some states may have had in-state tuition rates out of reach of minimum wage workers.
          </p>
          <p>
            The memes only notable failing has to do with overly broad wording -- specifically its use of the phrase "of their choice." The data we used refers to in-state tuition, meaning that the student would have access to this sweet tuition rate
           <em>
            only
           </em>
           at their home-state university. Thats not the same thing as "of their choice."
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Our ruling
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The meme said
           <strong>
           </strong>
           that "in 1978, a student who worked a minimum-wage summer job could afford to pay a year's full tuition at the 4-year public university of their choice."
          </p>
          <p>
           If you use the national average the figure is correct. The only problem is the part about a university "of their choice." The data is correct for in-state tuition -- not for any university in the country, where out-of-state rates may well have kicked
           <strong>
           </strong>
           up the tuition amount beyond a summers minimum-wage haul.
          </p>
          <p>
           On balance, we rate the claim Mostly True.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            CORRECTION
           </em>
           <em>
            : After we published this article, a reader noticed that we had used the incorrect tuition figure from the National Center for Education Statistics chart. This version uses the correct figure. The rating remains at Mostly True.
           </em>
          </p>
