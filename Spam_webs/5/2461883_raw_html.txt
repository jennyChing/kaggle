   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    International Scale of River Difficulty
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

        <a href="/wiki/Canolfan_Tryweryn" title="Canolfan Tryweryn">

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       International Scale of River Difficulty
      </b>
      is an American system used to rate the difficulty of a stretch of river, or a single (sometimes
      <a href="/wiki/Whitewater" title="Whitewater">
       whitewater
      </a>
      )
      <a href="/wiki/Rapid" title="Rapid">
       rapid
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWCODE05_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWCODE05-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The scale was created by the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Whitewater" title="American Whitewater">
       American Whitewater Association
      </a>
      for the purpose of evaluating rivers throughout the world, hence
      <i>
       International
      </i>
      in the title.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The grade reflects the technical difficulty and skill level required associated with the section of river. The scale is of use to various
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_sports" title="Water sports">
       water sports
      </a>
      and activities, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Rafting" title="Rafting">
       rafting
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Riverboarding" title="Riverboarding">
       riverboarding
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Whitewater_canoeing" title="Whitewater canoeing">
       whitewater canoeing
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stand_up_paddle_surfing" title="Stand up paddle surfing">
       stand up paddle surfing
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Whitewater_kayaking" title="Whitewater kayaking">
       whitewater kayaking
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
       Classification
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Scale_of_River_Difficulty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are six categories, each referred to as "Grade" or "Class" followed by a number. The scale is not linear, nor is it fixed. For instance, there can be difficult grade twos, easy grade threes, and so on. The grade of a river may (and usually does) change with the level of flow. Often a river or
      <a href="/wiki/Rapid" title="Rapid">
       rapid
      </a>
      will be given a numerical grade, and then a plus (+) or minus (-) to indicate if it is in the higher or lower end of the difficulty level. While a river section may be given an overall grading, it may contain sections above that grade, often noted as features, or conversely, it may contain sections of lower graded water as well. Details of portages may be given if these pose specific challenges. A summary of river classifications as presented by the American Whitewater Association:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWCODE05_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AWCODE05-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <table class="wikitable" width="80%">
      <tr>
       <th>
        Class I:
        <br/>
        Easy
       </th>
       <td>
        Fast moving water with
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riffles" title="Riffles">
         riffles
        </a>
        and small waves. Few obstructions, all obvious and easily missed with little training. Risk to swimmers is slight; self-rescue is easy.
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg">
         <img alt="Black Fork Mohican River.jpg" data-file-height="2142" data-file-width="2687" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg/100px-Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg/150px-Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg/200px-Black_Fork_Mohican_River.jpg 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <th>
        Class II:
        <br/>
        Novice
       </th>
       <td>
        Straightforward rapids with wide, clear channels which are evident without scouting. Occasional maneuvering may be required, but rocks and medium-sized waves are easily avoided by trained paddlers. Swimmers are seldom injured and group assistance, while helpful, is seldom needed. Rapids that are at the upper end of this difficulty range are designated Class II+.
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG">
         <img alt="Madawaska River Whitney.JPG" data-file-height="1152" data-file-width="1536" height="75" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG/100px-Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG/150px-Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG/200px-Madawaska_River_Whitney.JPG 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <th>
        Class III:
        <br/>
        Intermediate
       </th>
       <td>
        Rapids with moderate, irregular waves which may be difficult to avoid and which can swamp an open canoe. Complex maneuvers in fast current and good boat control in tight passages or around ledges are often required; large waves or strainers may be present but are easily avoided. Strong eddies and powerful current effects can be found, particularly on large-volume rivers. Scouting is advisable for inexperienced parties. Injuries while swimming are rare; self-rescue is usually easy but group assistance may be required to avoid long swims. Rapids that are at the lower or upper end of this difficulty range are designated Class III- or Class III+ respectively.
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WataugaTheBigHole.jpg">
         <img alt="WataugaTheBigHole.jpg" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="614" height="75" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/WataugaTheBigHole.jpg/100px-WataugaTheBigHole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/WataugaTheBigHole.jpg/150px-WataugaTheBigHole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/WataugaTheBigHole.jpg/200px-WataugaTheBigHole.jpg 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <th>
        Class IV:
        <br/>
        Advanced
       </th>
       <td>
        Intense, powerful but predictable rapids requiring precise boat handling in turbulent water. Depending on the character of the river, it may feature large, unavoidable waves and holes or constricted passages demanding fast maneuvers under pressure. A fast, reliable eddy turn may be needed to initiate maneuvers, scout rapids, or rest. Rapids may require must make moves above dangerous hazards. Scouting may be necessary the first time down. Risk of injury to swimmers is moderate to high, and water conditions may make self-rescue difficult. Group assistance for rescue is often essential but requires practiced skills. For kayakers, a strong
        <a href="/wiki/Kayak_roll" title="Kayak roll">
         roll
        </a>
        is highly recommended. Rapids that are at the lower or upper end of this difficulty range are designated Class IV- or Class IV+ respectively.
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg">
         <img alt="Crooked Chute Petawawa.jpg" data-file-height="463" data-file-width="608" height="76" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg/100px-Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg/150px-Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg/200px-Crooked_Chute_Petawawa.jpg 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <th>
        Class V:
        <br/>
        Expert
       </th>
       <td>
        Extremely long, obstructed, or very violent rapids which expose a paddler to added risk. Drops may contain large, unavoidable waves and holes or steep, congested chutes with complex, demanding routes. Rapids may continue for long distances between pools, demanding a high level of fitness. What eddies exist may be small, turbulent, or difficult to reach. At the high end of the scale, several of these factors may be combined. Scouting is recommended but may be difficult. Swims are dangerous, and rescue is often difficult even for experts. Proper equipment, extensive experience, and practiced rescue skills are essential.
        <p>
         Because of the large range of difficulty that exists beyond Class IV, Class V is an open-ended, multiple-level scale designated by class 5.0, 5.1, 5.2, etc. Each of these levels is an order of magnitude more difficult than the last. That is, going from Class 5.0 to Class 5.1 is a similar order of magnitude as increasing from Class IV to Class 5.0.
        </p>
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green_wall.JPG">
         <img alt="Green wall.JPG" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="75" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Green_wall.JPG/100px-Green_wall.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Green_wall.JPG/150px-Green_wall.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Green_wall.JPG/200px-Green_wall.JPG 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <th>
        Class VI
        <br/>
        Extreme and Exploratory Rapids
       </th>
       <td>
        Runs of this classification are rarely attempted and often exemplify the extremes of difficulty, unpredictability and danger. The consequences of errors are severe and rescue may be impossible. For teams of experts only, at favorable water levels, after close personal inspection and taking all precautions. After a Class VI rapid has been run many times, its rating may be changed to an appropriate Class 5.x rating.
       </td>
       <td>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhine_falls.jpg">
         <img alt="Rhine falls.jpg" data-file-height="950" data-file-width="1156" height="82" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Rhine_falls.jpg/100px-Rhine_falls.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Rhine_falls.jpg/150px-Rhine_falls.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Rhine_falls.jpg/200px-Rhine_falls.jpg 2x" width="100"/>
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Caution_in_application">
       Caution in application
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Scale_of_River_Difficulty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Caution in application">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Classifications can vary enormously, depending on the skill level and experience of the paddlers who rated the river. For example, at the 1999 International Conference on Outdoor Recreation and Education, an author of a paddling guide pointed out that there is too much variation in what is covered by the Class I designation, and proposed making further distinctions within the Class I flat water designations and Class I+ moving water designations, with the goal of providing better information for
      <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">
       canoeists
      </a>
      , instructors leading trips, and families with young children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The grade of a river or rapid is likely to change along with the level of the water. High water usually makes rapids more difficult and dangerous, although some rapids may be easier at high flows because features are covered or washed out. At spate/
      <a href="/wiki/Flood_stage" title="Flood stage">
       flood stage
      </a>
      , even rapids which are usually easy can contain lethal and unpredictable hazards. Conversely, some rapids may be easier with lower water levels when dangerous
      <a href="/wiki/Whitewater#Holes" title="Whitewater">
       hydraulics
      </a>
      become easier to manage. Some rivers with high volumes of fast moving water may require little maneuvering, but will pose serious risk of injury or death in the event of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsize" title="Capsize">
       capsize
      </a>
      .
     </p>
