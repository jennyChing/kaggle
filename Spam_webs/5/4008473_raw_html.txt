    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       "Behind the Sofa"
      </b>
      is a British
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_culture" title="Pop culture">
       pop culture
      </a>
      phrase describing the fearful reaction of hiding behind a
      <a href="/wiki/Couch" title="Couch">
       sofa
      </a>
      to avoid seeing frightening parts of a
      <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">
       television programme
      </a>
      (with the implication that one wants to remain in the room to watch the rest of the programme). Although the phrase is sometimes employed in a serious context, its use is usually intended to be humorous and/or nostalgic, and to evoke a pleasant, safe fear in a domestic setting.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-telegraph-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_in_Doctor_Who">
       Origin in
       <i>
        Doctor Who
       </i>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Behind_the_sofa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin in Doctor Who">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The expression originated from popular media commentary on young children being frightened by episodes of the
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      science-fiction television series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">
        Doctor Who
       </a>
      </i>
      , particularly during the 1970s. The idea that young children would hide behind furniture when especially frightening scenes were being shown, as they were unwilling to miss the programme altogether, was also popularized in the media.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-times-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The phrase is strongly associated with
      <i>
       Doctor Who
      </i>
      in the United Kingdom, so much so that in 1991 the
      <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_Moving_Image_(London)" title="Museum of the Moving Image (London)">
       Museum of the Moving Image
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       London
      </a>
      named their exhibition celebrating the programme "Behind the Sofa".
     </p>
     <p>
      "Everyone remembers hiding behind the sofa,"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      journalist Sinclair McKay wrote of the programme during its thirtieth anniversary year of 1993. "Remember hiding behind the sofa every time
      <i>
       Doctor Who
      </i>
      came on the television?"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">
        Daily Mirror
       </a>
      </i>
      newspaper asked its readers in a feature article two years later. In a 2006 interview with
      <a href="/wiki/Sky_News" title="Sky News">
       Sky News
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Prince_Andrew,_Duke_of_York" title="Prince Andrew, Duke of York">
       Prince Andrew, Duke of York
      </a>
      said that he hid from
      <a href="/wiki/Dalek" title="Dalek">
       Daleks
      </a>
      behind a
      <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle" title="Windsor Castle">
       Windsor Castle
      </a>
      settee while watching
      <i>
       Doctor Who
      </i>
      as a child.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">
        The Economist
       </a>
      </i>
      has presented "hiding behind the sofa whenever the Daleks appear" as a British
      <a href="/wiki/Cultural_institution" title="Cultural institution">
       cultural institution
      </a>
      on par with
      <a href="/wiki/Bovril" title="Bovril">
       Bovril
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tea_(meal)" title="Tea (meal)">
       tea-time
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Paul Parsons, author of
      <i>
       The Science of Doctor Who
      </i>
      , explains the appeal of hiding behind the sofa as the activation of the fear response in the
      <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">
       amygdala
      </a>
      in conjunction with reassurances of safety from the brain's
      <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe" title="Frontal lobe">
       frontal lobe
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_popular_culture">
       In other popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Behind_the_sofa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In other popular culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The phrase has also found more general usage in the UK as a humorous or satirical metaphor for being in a state of fear or terror. For example, after he was sacked as the presenter of the comedy programme
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Have_I_Got_News_for_You" title="Have I Got News for You">
        Have I Got News for You
       </a>
      </i>
      in 2002 due to revelations about his private life,
      <a href="/wiki/Angus_Deayton" title="Angus Deayton">
       Angus Deayton
      </a>
      released a press statement which concluded: "I sincerely wish the show well in the future and look forward to watching this Friday's episode  from behind the sofa."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another example comes from sports coverage; in a live text commentary on a
      <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">
       cricket
      </a>
      match in the
      <a href="/wiki/2005_Ashes_series" title="2005 Ashes series">
       2005 Ashes series
      </a>
      for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardian_Unlimited" title="Guardian Unlimited">
       Guardian Unlimited
      </a>
      website, journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Rob_Smyth" title="Rob Smyth">
       Rob Smyth
      </a>
      wrote of one moment during the game: "Now that
      <a href="/wiki/Shane_Warne" title="Shane Warne">
       Warne's
      </a>
      gone, it's safe for
      <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Giles" title="Ashley Giles">
       Gilo
      </a>
      to come out from behind the sofa: his second ball is chipped tantalisingly over the blundering
      <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Hoggard" title="Matthew Hoggard">
       Hoggard
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Fielding_(cricket)#Fielding_position_names_and_locations" title="Fielding (cricket)">
       mid-off
      </a>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Jason_Gillespie" title="Jason Gillespie">
       Gillespie
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In scripted programming, a reference occurred in a 2001 episode of the BBC
      <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">
       sitcom
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(UK_TV_series)" title="Coupling (UK TV series)">
        Coupling
       </a>
      </i>
      , where the central character Steve, while extolling the virtues of a sofa, remarks on its usefulness in avoiding
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daleks" title="Daleks">
       Daleks
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coupling_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-coupling-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Coupling
      </i>
      writer
      <a href="/wiki/Steven_Moffat" title="Steven Moffat">
       Steven Moffat
      </a>
      went on to write for and then become executive producer of the revived series of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">
        Doctor Who
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
