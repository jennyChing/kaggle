       <p>
        <strong>
         <!-- google_ad_section_start(name=story_introduction, weight=high) -->
         PANTING from the high elevation, I step out onto the precarious stone ledge to gain a better view of the raging Jinsha River below.

        </strong>

      <p>
       Even from our altitude, more than 1000 metres above the torrent, the sound of the legendary waterway tumbling over submerged boulders is thunderous. On the far side of the river, which is a major tributary of the mighty Yangtze, the cliffs of Tiger Leaping Gorge reach skyward. They terminate at the foot of Yulong Mountains cracked glacier.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here its not just the altitude that has taken my breath away 
      </p>
      <p>
       Meandering its way through the rugged Yunnan province in South West China, the extraordinary gorge has been attracting throngs of tourists for decades. Home to the Naxi people, the gorge derived its name from the tale of a hunter who was chasing down a large tiger.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the canyons narrowest point, still an impressive 25m wide, the tiger supposedly leapt over the river and escaped unharmed. In the gorges name, the legend lives on.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227352945847">
       <div class="module-content">

         </div>

          <span class="caption-text">

      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       At the base of towering cliffs, the turbulent whitewater forges its way through the steep canyon, creating a stunning landscape. This view alone is worth the journey to Yunnan.
      </p>
      <p>
       But the gorge and its enveloping scenery hold a dark and dangerous secret, something that the tour agents omit from their sales pitches.
      </p>
      <p>
       Countless trekkers have fallen victim to the perils of the jagged and slippery cliffs over the years. An untold number of people, foreign tourists included, have lost their lives in the heart of the gorge.
      </p>
      <p>
       The local government manages to suppress these statistics in order to save the areas image from harm. If the truth was known, the tourism industry could fall into a serious decline.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227352945880">

      <p>
       Yet despite the hidden warnings, I flocked to the region in excited anticipation. I couldnt wait to walk the captivating 22 kilometre trek that stretches throughout the UNESCO World Heritage Site with my partner.
      </p>
      <p>
       Be warned, it isnt exactly an easy wander to get there, though.
      </p>
      <p>
       After arriving in the nondescript village of Qiaotou, three hours from the ancient city of Lijiang, we hitched a ride to the Upper Gorge. Here hordes of buses drop off camera-happy tourists, and we excitedly joined them. A series of boardwalks and staircases lead down to the river.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Then we made our back to Qiaotou and located the trailhead to the High Road. As we quickly gained elevation the views were immediately rewarding. Terraced rice fields staggered down to the Jinsha, which glowed shades of vibrant blues in the afternoon sun. Jade Dragon Snow Mountain soared above our heads, the southernmost glacier in the Northern Hemisphere looming between its peaks.
      </p>
      <p>
       Locals rode past us on motorbikes, waving enthusiastically with beaming smiles. Departing the village road led us onto a steep and dusty ascent past red and yellow arrows, which would remain as our trail markers for the duration of the trek. The vistas provided ample reason to stop for regular breaks.
      </p>
      <p>
       Local men with horses followed behind, beckoning us to give up walking and instead use their steeds to reach the top. Despite their persistence, we trudged on. In the past assaults have occurred on the trail, but few arrests have ever been made.
      </p>
      <p>
       It can be intimidating when these men on mares approach, but for the most part they are harmless. Still, trekking in a group is highly recommended to avoid the potential dangers. Finally escaping the exposed mountainside, we stepped into a magnificent pine forest and soaked in the aromatic smells of fresh air and woodlands.
      </p>
      <p>
       Two-and-a-half hours after beginning the trek we marched into a charming village and checked into the Naxi Family Guest House. Taking a seat in the gorgeous courtyard, delicious green tea in hand and Jade Dragon Snow Mountain rising in the distance, this was the perfect way to end the first day. A handful of other hikers trickled in, and we exchanged laughs and travel stories as the sun set over the gorge.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227352945936">

      <p>
       Waking at the break of dawn, we ate a large portion of fried rice and vegetables for breakfast to stock up on energy for the mornings upcoming challenge  the infamous 28 Bends. We charged towards the series of climbing switchbacks, eager to tackle them as fast as possible. Still the mountainous backdrop managed to halt us every few minutes as we basked in natures beauty.
      </p>
      <p>
       In an hour we had reached the summit of the 28 Bends.
      </p>
      <p>
       From this vantage point we could see straight down towards the Jinsha River and really appreciate the true scale of Tiger Leaping Gorge. At 3790 metres from river to peak, this is one of the deepest canyons in the world. At our viewing platform almost every single centimetre of its grand size filled our vision. Bursting with a sense of awe, we continued on.
      </p>
      <p>
       The trail thankfully remained somewhat level, with only minor elevation changes allowing us to regain our energy.
      </p>
      <p>
       The outlooks of imposing cliffs opposite the Halfway Lodge proved too enticing to ignore, and we stopped in for a short lunch break. We had read that the Halfway Lodge boasted the best Toilet With A View in the world. Having experienced the surreal snow-capped peaks that appeared to be painted by the brushes of giants directly opposite the latrine, its a claim that would be hard to dispute.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Resuming the hike, we climbed steadily as the mountains started to become steeper. The downhill side of the trail gave away to sheer drops, threatening us with peril should we make one wrong step on the loose rocks underfoot.
      </p>
      <p>
       I looked down to the base of the cliffs and wondered if this was a position where hapless souls may have met their maker. Beside us waterfalls cascaded over the path, creating an idyllic atmosphere. We survived the hazards of the trail and commenced our final descent out of the highlands.
      </p>
      <p>
       We reached the main road and continued on towards the Tibet Guest House in Walnut Grove. Attracted by the reputation of having the best food on the entire trail, it was here that we had decided to spend our last night. An affable Tibetan lady welcomed us into her home and checked us into a comfortable private room.
      </p>
      <p>
       We sipped on cold beers while admiring the glorious ravine.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227352946398">
       <div class="module-content">

      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       Soon some trekkers we had met the previous night arrived, and we celebrated the end of another day with a delectable meal and strong Chinese liquor.
      </p>
      <p>
       Our bus out of there didnt leave until midafternoon the following day, so we relaxed in the morning before leaving our Tibetan family and walking down through Walnut Grove towards the Middle Gorge. We hiked past farmers and pastures towards the Ray of Sunshine trail, which quickly turned into a narrow and heart thumping traverse along sketchy precipices.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227352946450">

      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       The backdrop was worth the danger as the canyon closed in around us. Skirting along the edges of the Jinsha River one final time, we found ourselves at the foot of a near-vertical ladder. Legs burning from the previous two days efforts, we cautiously climbed up the steps and onto another ladder. 168 rungs later, and we had crawled our way out of the gorge.
      </p>
      <p>
       While our journey into Chinas South West had been unplanned and open for any adventure, the mystique of Tiger Leaping Gorge had quickly filled our thoughts from the moment we had entered Yunnan. Finally conquering the three day trek was a rewarding experience.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Whether that tiger had indeed made the miraculous leap across the gorge all those years ago did not matter. With the Jinsha River flowing beneath us, and Jade Dragon Snow Mountain towering above us, there is no better place to leave a legend, footprints and memories behind.
      </p>
