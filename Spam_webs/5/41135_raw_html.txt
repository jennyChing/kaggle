  <meta content="The International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS) announced today that it's going to be adding a second to the clock on June 30, 2015.While leap secondssound entirely harmless, it's far more dangerous for the Internet than you might expect.

Why bother adding a second at all, then? There's a number of reasons one can be added; everything from earthquakes changing the Earth's rotation or simple adjustments to time to account for Earth's naturally slowing rotation. The latter is why it's being added this time around.

The US actually wants to get rid of leap secondsbecause they cause disruption to equipment, but hasn't managed to just yet.

The Internet intrinsically relies on the time being right; links around the world use the precise time from an atomic clock to ensure communications are in sync, as do your favorite services. Time is intricately woven into the fabric of the Internet, so what happens when you decide to add a second to the clock?

Well, the last time this happened in 2012 popular services like Reddit, Foursquare and Yelp had hours of downtime along with airlines like Virgin. This was becausethe updated time is distributed via Network Time Protocol (NTP), and some applications and systemslike Linux and MySQL noticed a lost second and froze due to a bug.

In this case, some Linux kernels had a bug that caused them to handle leap seconds incorrectly which caused a &quot;kernel panic&quot; (where the entire system freezes). This time around it should be fixed, however.

Some companies, like Google, work around the problem in advance by using a technique called &quot;leap smearing&quot; which means the company gradually adds milliseconds to the system clocks of it's servers ahead of the time, so when the time rolls around the serversdon't think anything is wrong.

Perhaps this time aroundcompanies will have more luck, with the Linux bug being resolved for a few years now. We've had 25 leap seconds added in the past, so eventually we'll get it right.

Bugsdohave a tendency to pop up in the oddest places, however, so if you notice your favorite site is down at 23:59 on June 30, it's probably thanks to the leap second.

        							&lt;p&gt;The International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS) &lt;a href="http://datacenter.iers.org/web/guest/eop/-/somos/5Rgv/latest/16"&gt;announced today&lt;/a&gt; that its going to be adding a second to the clock on June 30, 2015.While leap secondssound entirely harmless, its far more dangerous for the Internet than you might expect.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Why bother adding a second at all, then? Theres a number of reasons one can be added; everything from earthquakes changing the Earths rotation or simple adjustments to time to account for Earths naturally slowing rotation. The latter is why its being added this time around.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The US &lt;a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/science/science-news/11327571/Leap-second-French-time-lords-add-one-second-to-2015.html"&gt;actually wants to get rid of leap seconds&lt;/a&gt;because they cause disruption to equipment, but hasnt managed to just yet.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;The Internet &lt;a href="https://www.astro.virginia.edu/class/skrutskie/astr121/notes/tides.html"&gt;intrinsically relies on the time being right&lt;/a&gt;; links around the world use the precise time from an atomic clock to ensure communications are in sync, as do your favorite services. Time is intricately woven into the fabric of the Internet, so what happens when you decide to add a second to the clock?&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Well, the &lt;a href="http://spectrum.ieee.org/riskfactor/telecom/internet/leap-second-causes-hiccups-at-some-websites"&gt;last time this happened in 2012&lt;/a&gt; popular services like Reddit, Foursquare and Yelp had hours of downtime &lt;a href="http://www.datacenterknowledge.com/archives/2012/07/01/computer-snafu-snarls-air-travel-australia/"&gt;along with airlines like Virgin&lt;/a&gt;. This was becausethe updated time is distributed via Network Time Protocol (NTP), and some applications and systemslike Linux and MySQL noticed a lost second and froze due to a bug.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;In this case, some &lt;a href="http://serverfault.com/questions/403732/anyone-else-experiencing-high-rates-of-linux-server-crashes-during-a-leap-second"&gt;Linux kernels had a bug that caused them&lt;/a&gt; to handle leap seconds incorrectly which caused a kernel panic (where the entire system freezes). This time around it should be fixed, however.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Some companies, &lt;a href="http://googleblog.blogspot.co.nz/2011/09/time-technology-and-leaping-seconds.html"&gt;like Google&lt;/a&gt;, work around the problem in advance by using a technique called leap smearing which means the company gradually adds milliseconds to the system clocks of its servers ahead of the time, so when the time rolls around the serversdont think anything is wrong.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Perhaps this time aroundcompanies will have more luck, with the Linux bug being resolved for a few years now. Weve had 25 leap seconds added in the past, so eventually well get it right.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;p&gt;Bugs&lt;em&gt;do&lt;/em&gt;have a tendency to pop up in the oddest places, however, so if you notice your favorite site is down at 23:59 on June 30, its probably thanks to the leap second.&lt;/p&gt;
