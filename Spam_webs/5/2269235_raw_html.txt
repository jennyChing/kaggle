          <p>
           This week, Friday Feedback takes a second look at how a
           <a href="http://www.medpagetoday.com/Neurology/GeneralNeurology/45290" target="_blank">

          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            What type of evidence is needed to confirm or refute the health risks of marijuana?
           </strong>
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           The participants this week:
          </p>
          <p>
           Neil Brooks, MD, family physician in Vernon, Conn., and former president of the
           <a href="http://www.aafp.org/about.html" target="_blank">
            American Academy of Family Physicians
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
           Thomas J. Crowley, MD, professor emeritus in the division of substance dependence, at the University of Colorado Denver School of Medicine
          </p>
          <p>
           Chris Lillis, MD, primary care physician in Fredericksburg, Va., and board member of Doctors for America
          </p>
          <p>
           Tanzid Shams, MD, neurologist at the Neurological Institute of New York, Columbia University Medical Center in New York City
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-left tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           Boris Tabakoff, PhD, professor at the Skaggs School of Pharmacy &amp; Pharmaceutical Sciences, University of Colorado Anschutz Medical Campus in Denver
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong class="MsoNormal" style="mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;">
            Reefer Madness
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            What's Known
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.drsforamerica.org/authors/dr-chris-lillis" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Lillis
            </strong>
           </a>
           : "My first thought regarding medicinal uses for marijuana is simple: it is a substance that has been around for thousands of years and there have been exactly zero deaths from marijuana overdose  I only wish I could say the same for some pharmaceuticals. I also know several friends and patients who have found anti-emetic, analgesic, and anxiolytic benefits from marijuana, and the burgeoning evidence for
           <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23902406">
            anticonvulsant
           </a>
           effects are compelling."
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.ucdenver.edu/academics/colleges/medicalschool/departments/psychiatry/Faculty/Pages/Crowley,%20Thomas.aspx" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Thomas J. Crowley, MD
            </strong>
           </a>
           : "Certainly, brain functional or structural abnormalities can influence later substance problems and the often-associated risky conduct problems:
          </p>
          <div class="tablet-ad-wrap-container">
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
           <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           1. Hypoactive P300 responses in pre-pubertal children without substance exposure predict later conduct and substance problems (Iacono et al 2008).
          </p>
          <p>
           2. Among 12- to 14-year-old youngsters with little drug exposure, widespread brain hypoactivity on an inhibitory task predicts later substance and conduct problems (Norman et al 2011).
          </p>
          <p>
           3. Youth 13 to 15 years old with little alcohol exposure but at familial risk for alcoholism show brain hypoactivity in a risky-decision task (Cservenka, Nagel 2012).
          </p>
          <p>
           4. Among 14-year-old adolescents with little drug exposure, greater risk-taking (a predictor of substance involvement) is associated with reward-system hypoactivity (Schneider et al 2012)."
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.ucdenver.edu/academics/colleges/medicalschool/departments/Pharmacology/faculty/Documents/tabakoff.aspx" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Boris Tabakoff, PhD
            </strong>
           </a>
           : "A major issue in comparing two groups of subjects on a carefully measured variable such as density or shape of certain brain areas is not so much whether the two groups differ, but whether individuals in one of the groups are outside the normal range for humans for the measurement. The other issue unresolved in studies with measures at only one point in time is the old 'chicken or the egg' question. There is nothing to say that individuals with brain anatomical characteristics which differ from the nonsmokers of marijuana are not using marijuana because of the inherent brain differences rather than marijuana changing their brain. And, finally, one is confronted with a never-ending list of other possible differences between the groups, in addition to whether they do or do not smoke marijuana."
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           <strong>
            What's Next?
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://cumc.columbia.edu/dept/neurology/education/res/2013/shams.html" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Shams
            </strong>
           </a>
           : "The recent trend in legalization of medical marijuana has opened the field for detailed investigation of clinical applications of marijuana. As physicians, as journalists, and as patients there is a very human tendency, however fallible, to look for causality when only correlation is implied."
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Tabakoff
           </strong>
           : "Cause and effect are difficult to link in many medical conditions, especially if the subject is well sprinkled with previously held prejudices. The first thing that is mandatory in determining the effect of a substance on a tissue is to have good measurements of the tissue before it is exposed to a drug (a difficult but not impossible task in humans)."
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.google.com/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=neil%20brooks%20family%20physician&amp;source=web&amp;cd=2&amp;ved=0CDEQFjAB&amp;url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthgrades.com%2Fphysician%2Fdr-neil-brooks-x2npn&amp;ei=WJBRU43MBITQrQfZg4CQDg&amp;usg=AFQjCNFluia3Xndy23aAFWrw4XLG2pBLGQ&amp;bvm=bv.65058239,d.bmk" target="_blank">
            <strong>
             Brooks
            </strong>
           </a>
           : "A proposed study would be to go to a state, Colorado, where MJ [marijuana] is now [legally] available, and get volunteers who plan on using MJ and compare them to a control population over a period of a couple of years. It is unlikely that this study would be able to get approval since if the MJ is shown to be harmful, the study group was exposed to risk without any potential benefit. A further confounding factor is that, from what I understand, various strains of MJ act quite differently. If that is true, how does the study control for the type of MJ? I also assume that even knowing the number of doses used there is no standardization of how much is taken."
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Tabakoff
           </strong>
           : "The authors themselves state in the abstract of the paper, 'Future longitudinal studies could help determine whether cannabis use contributes to these observed shape differences or whether they are biomarkers of a vulnerability to the effect of cannabis that predate its misuse.' What is not said is there are many other options for interpreting such results, and this paper is just a step to generate curiosity for further work on this issue with humans and informative nonhuman animal models."
          </p>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right tablet-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right desktop-ad">
          </div>
          <div class="ad-slot ad-right mobile-ad">
          </div>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Brooks
           </strong>
           : "The best evidence is always from prospective double-blind studies, which will likely never be done with MJ. Retrospective analysis is fraught with problems, but in the long run it is probably the only way we are going to really understand if there are problems associated with MJ use."
          </p>
