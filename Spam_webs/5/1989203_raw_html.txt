          <h2 style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques overview
          </h2>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive behavioral therapy is one of the most popular psychotherapy. This widespread acceptance is due to its design. It does not have so much fragments and it is a treatment approach that is problem-based, short-term, and contextually relevant to the problems of the patient. Lots of researches and evidences are making Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques more fine-tuned. To say very simply cognitive behavioral therapy uses a school of psychotherapy to help people solve their emotional problems. Cognitive means mental processes like thinking. The word cognitive refers to everything that goes on in your mind such as dreams, previous memories, running images, thoughts, and attention. Behavior refers to everything that you do. This includes what you want to say, how you want try to solve problems, how you want to act, and avoidance. Therapy is a word used to describe a systematic approach to combating a problem, illness, or irregular condition. A central concept in Cognitive behavioral therapy is that you feel the way you think. Therefore, Cognitive behavioral therapy works on the principle that you can live more happily and productively if you are thinking in healthy ways. Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques work on three phases, where each phase has focus to specific techniques.
           <a href="http://www.webhealthsolution.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/2015-02-20_172010.jpg">
            <img alt="Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-4442" height="484" src="http://www.webhealthsolution.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/2015-02-20_172010.jpg" width="1072"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           In the initial stage, the patient is assessed for the nature of problem, rapport is established and patient is prepared with education for main treatment. The symptoms, related variables, and cognitive and emotional characteristics then lead us to a treatment plan. This plan typically begins with education of the patient about symptoms, cognitive behavioral understanding of those symptoms, and rationale for treatment. Active treatment occurs in middle phase. Cognitive-behavioral treatment strategies are applied in this phase. This phase involves regular treatment sessions and suitable homework. When symptoms disappear and the patient become ready for maintenance and relapse prevention, the therapy is tapered off. The final phase then focuses on generalization of the corrected behavior to daily activities and maintenance of techniques, and relapse prevention. During this phase, the number of session is reduced and greater responsibility given to patient for persistence of ongoing techniques. Long-term changes are maintained by some booster sessions.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Eligibility for Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           To start Cognitive behavioral therapy each patient needs assessment for cognitive, behavioral, and emotional symptoms. Assessments should also address detailed information about the patients eligibility factors. They are:
          </p>
          <ul style="text-align: justify;">
           <li>
            The patient should participate actively and should help finding new strategies;
           </li>
           <li>
            The patient should be good at completing homework;
           </li>
           <li>
            The therapist and patient will measure outcome and modify techniques accordingly;
           </li>
           <li>
            Relief of symptoms and good daily functioning will be prime focus of therapy;
           </li>
           <li>
            Time of therapy will be limited;
           </li>
           <li>
            Generalization of techniques into everyday life will assure maintenance of treatment gains and relapse prevention.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <h2 style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques in main phase
          </h2>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive Restructuring
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive strategies commonly reconstruct dysfunctional cognitions and intervene on automatic thoughts and their underlying schemas. Automatic thoughts are cognitions that flush rapidly and repeatedly through an individuals mind. Such thoughts can occur spontaneously or in response to stimuli, a situation, prompt, or other antecedent. Automatic thoughts may be valid worries (about events that have or actually could happen) or they may contain cognitive errors or distortions. The following cognitive strategies are commonly used to assess and intervene on automatic thoughts and cognitive errors.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Identifying Automatic Thoughts
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Imagery and role-playing is one technique for identifying automatic thoughts especially in child. While acting out the scenario, what s/he is thinking, feeling, etc. can be asked to find out the automatic thought. Such exercises are more applicable for children, which often helps them generate important information that they cannot report during an interview. Thought recording is another technique. Here events, thoughts, and feelings are recorded on a daily basis. In this technique, self-monitoring enable recording of event and do not need to rely on memory. Children can also do this with the help of adults.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Socratic Questioning or Examining the Evidence
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Socratic questioning is one of the main components of cognitive restructuring. Here, the patient is asked questions to discover automatic thoughts and their validity. When the patient answers, the answers are considered as hypotheses and those are evaluated by patient with evidence for automatic thoughts. This technique is an important way to begin teaching children that such thoughts are not true simply because they occur. Patient with distorted thoughts get more benefit from this because rational consideration of evidence awakes the patient that such thoughts are not true. Once automatic thoughts have been discovered in question, the therapist and patient can begin to revise them based on evidence and reality, and generate new coping thoughts that are more accurate.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Correct Misinterpretations
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Socratic questioning also can correct misinterpretations. Individuals with anxiety and depression may in particular misinterpret events, the behavior of others, thoughts, feelings, and other stimuli. Calling into question an individuals interpretation and noting how it impacts thoughts, feelings, and behaviors can be an important aspect of cognitive restructuring as well.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Behavioral Experiments
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques advocate the necessity of different behavioral experiments. These experiments are exercises that demonstrate errors in thinking in a concrete manner. For example, we want to teach that thought suppression leads to increased thought. Therefore, we give the patient an exercise for 2 minutes to think about a topic like white tiger and not to think about anything else. Then the patient finds that he/she cannot prevent forbidden thoughts. Therefore, instead of trying to suppress thoughts, patients are encouraged to observe their thoughts as they come and go without trying to suppress them. This technique typically results in a reduction in intrusive thoughts.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Modification of Imagery
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Anxious patients, in particular, often have cognitive imagery associated with their symptoms (e.g., imagery associated with a feared or traumatic event). Most imagery end in a negative event where patient feels embarrassed. We can modify the image by helping the patient continue the image to a positive resolution for example getting up off the floor after fainting, making statements to others when embarrassed, modifying the outcome of a traumatic event. This type exercise can decrease anxiety and catastrophic thinking. Role-playing exercises may serve a similar role.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Altering Core Beliefs
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           In addition to identifying automatic thoughts, Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques focus on identifying the core beliefs, or cognitive schemas, that underlie those thoughts. It is the persons maladaptive belief that generates those thoughts. For example, a child believes that everyone hates a stupid boy and he is a stupid boy. Therefore, during homework, he takes extra precaution and many automatic thought appears. Until core belief is addressed, the automatic thought will relapse.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Physiological Techniques
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Cognitive behavioral therapy also gives emphasis on many physiological techniques for modifying thoughts, feelings, and behavior. They are discussed below.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Regulated Breathing
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Breathing control exercises are one of physiological Cognitive behavioral therapy techniques. They are physically effective for counteracting hyperventilation, reducing physical tension, and decreasing physical sensations associated with anxiety. When patient understand the physiology behind the symptom, the patient have better control over the symptoms. Regular and regulated breathing in both low and high stressful situation is helpful.
          </p>
          <div class="wpInsert wpInsertInPostAd wpInsertMiddle" style="margin: 5px; padding: 0px;">

          </div>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Relaxation Training
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Relaxation training is another common physiological technique of Cognitive behavioral therapy. This technique relaxes whole body muscles sequentially. At first, the respiratory muscle, then other muscles in groups. Children should focus on large muscle groups only (arms, stomach, legs, and whole body at once). The goal of relaxation in this context is often to reduce disruptive behaviors that accompany anger, especially in impulsive children.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Exposure Techniques
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           The patients with anxiety symptoms have avoidance and safety behaviors. Therefore, they never get chance to know what would happen if they had done that. As such, exposure techniques develop a progressive hierarchy of feared situations. The patient experiences a graded series of exercises. Initially the anxiety is present, but anxiety typically decreases during such exercises. The patient experiences that the feared outcome will not occur. This exercise might also include specific discussion of what the patient thought would happen, whether it actually did happen, reasons for the outcome, etc. Obsessive-compulsive disorder can be treated by this therapy. Here, compulsion is safety behavior. Therefore, exposure exercises in these cases is refraining from compulsion and showing that their feared outcome does not occur (e.g., have the patient experience that if they do not check the door, a robber will not enter the house).
          </p>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Flooding is similar to exposure, but here exposure to target stimuli is not graded and begins by eliciting the full-blown fear response. Flooding works more quickly than hierarchical exposure, but substantial self-control is required here. Exposure can also be used to challenge core beliefs.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Self-Monitoring or Self-Management
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Self-monitoring is helpful for identifying automatic thought and to keep track of moods and plan for pleasant events. In the treatment of depression, we use it. It also helpful to increase awareness and train competing responses in habit disorders (Tourettes disorder, trichotillomania), assess and modify eating and exercise habits in the treatment of eating disorders, and track use of breathing and relaxation procedures in treatment of anxiety disorders.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Activity Scheduling
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Individuals with depression often have negative thinking, including negative evaluations of the self, the world, and the future (triad) and they lack reinforcement in their life. They do not involve in pleasant activities they previously enjoyed due to decreased motivation and interest. As such, by activity scheduling, the patient and therapist schedule some pleasurable activities. These activities gives the patient a feeling of competence, mastery, pleasure or other similar positive emotional and cognitive outcomes.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Behavior Modification
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Applied behavior analysis and behavior modification increases desirable behaviors and decreases undesirable behaviors by using re-enforcers. When reinforcement, in the form of token applied to a positive behavior, it increases the frequency of positive behavior. When reinforcement is removed from a negative behavior, or when a punishment is applied, it decreases the frequency of negative behavior.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Counterconditioning
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           This procedure reduces specific maladaptive behavior. However, this process requires a maladaptive behavior with an incompatible behavior to work.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Systematic Desensitization
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Systematic desensitization is another counterconditioning technique and it is most commonly used. It uses training relaxation techniques and an anxiety hierarchy simultaneously for reducing fear and anxiety over time. Its four stages are: 1) relaxation training; 2) constructing the anxiety hierarchy; 3) desensitization in imagination; and 4) in vivo desensitization.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Aversive Counterconditioning
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Aversive counterconditioning is another technique based on principles of classical conditioning. Here, target behavior or stimulus associated with it (conditioned stimulus) occurs due to an unpleasant response to a natural stimulus (unconditioned). Thus, maladaptive behavior is reduced. Addictions and problematic sexual fetishes suit this treatment. For example, the use of medications such as disulfiram that cause an individual to be physically ill when consuming alcohol and reduce the patients drinking behavior.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Covert Sensitization
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Covert sensitization relies on the same principles as aversive counterconditioning; however, the individual imagines an aversive condition while imagining engaging in maladaptive behavior, rather than actually experiencing the negative stimuli.
          </p>
          <h3 style="text-align: justify;">
           Habit Reversal
          </h3>
          <p style="text-align: justify;">
           Habit reversal procedures are most commonly used in the treatment of habit disorders, such as trichotillomania and skin picking, and Tourette syndrome and other tic disorders. Habit reversal involves three stages: 1) awareness training; 2) training in an incompatible competing response; and 3) social support, which is particularly important when using this technique with children and adolescents.
          </p>
