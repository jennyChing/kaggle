     <p>
      <b>
       Deuterocanonical books
      </b>
      is a term used since the 16th century in the
      <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
       Catholic Church
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">
       Eastern Christianity
      </a>
      to describe certain books and passages of the Christian
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
       Old Testament
      </a>
      that are not part of the current
      <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
       Hebrew Bible
      </a>
      . The term is used in contrast to the
      <a href="/wiki/Protocanonical_books" title="Protocanonical books">
       protocanonical books
      </a>
      , which are contained in the Hebrew Bible. This distinction had previously contributed to debate in the
      <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">
       early Church
      </a>
      about whether they should be classified as
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">
       canonical
      </a>
      texts. The term is used as a matter of convenience by the
      <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church" title="Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church">
       Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church
      </a>
      and other Churches to refer to books of their Old Testament which are not part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Masoretic_Text" title="Masoretic Text">
       Masoretic Text
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The deuterocanonical books are considered
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">
       canonical
      </a>
      by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholics" title="Catholics">
       Catholics
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" title="Eastern Orthodox">
       Eastern Orthodox
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox" title="Oriental Orthodox">
       Oriental Orthodox
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">
       Church of the East
      </a>
      , but are considered non-canonical by most
      <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">
       Protestants
      </a>
      . The word
      <i>
       deuterocanonical
      </i>
      comes from the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      meaning 'belonging to the second canon'.
     </p>
     <p>
      The original usage of the term distinguished these scriptures both from those considered
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">
        non-canonical
       </a>
      </i>
      and from those considered
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Protocanonical_books" title="Protocanonical books">
        protocanonical
       </a>
      </i>
      . However, some editions of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      include text from both deuterocanonical and non-canonical scriptures in a single section designated "
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">
       Apocrypha
      </a>
      ". This arrangement can lead to conflation between the otherwise distinct terms "deuterocanonical" and "apocryphal".
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_Old_Testament_canon" title="Development of the Old Testament canon">
       Development of the Old Testament canon
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Deuterocanonical is a term coined in 1566 by the theologian
      <a href="/wiki/Sixtus_of_Siena" title="Sixtus of Siena">
       Sixtus of Siena
      </a>
      , who had converted to
      <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
       Catholicism
      </a>
      from Judaism, to describe scriptural texts of the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
       Old Testament
      </a>
      considered
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">
       canonical
      </a>
      by the Catholic Church, but which are not present in the Hebrew Bible today, and which had been omitted by some early canon lists, especially in the
      <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">
       East
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbe_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-isbe-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cathenOT_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cathenOT-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Their acceptance among
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Christians" title="Early Christians">
       early Christians
      </a>
      was widespread, though not universal, and the Bible of the early Church always included, with varying degrees of recognition, books now called
      <i>
       deuterocanonical
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some say that their canonicity seems not to have been doubted in the Church until it was challenged by Jews after AD 100,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      sometimes postulating a hypothetical
      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Jamnia" title="Council of Jamnia">
       Council of Jamnia
      </a>
      . Regional councils in the
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">
       West
      </a>
      published official canons that included these books as early as the
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_4th_century#Defining_scripture" title="Christianity in the 4th century">
       4th
      </a>
      and 5th centuries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cathenOT_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cathenOT-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At Jerusalem there was a renewal, or at least a survival, of Jewish ideas, the tendency there being distinctly unfavourable to the deuteros. St.
      <a href="/wiki/Cyril_of_Jerusalem" title="Cyril of Jerusalem">
       Cyril of Jerusalem
      </a>
      , while vindicating for the Church the right to fix the Canon, places them among the apocrypha and forbids all books to be read privately which are not read in the churches. In Antioch and Syria the attitude was more favourable.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="St. Epiphanius of Salamis">
       St. Epiphanius of Salamis
      </a>
      hesitated about the rank of the deuteros. While he esteemed them, they did not hold the same place as the Hebrew books in his regard. On the other hand, the Oriental versions and Greek manuscripts of the period are more liberal. They have all the deuterocanonicals and, in some cases, certain apocrypha.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cedc_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-cedc-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the Latin Church, all through the
      <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
       Middle Ages
      </a>
      , there is evidence of hesitation about the character of the deuterocanonicals. One is favourable, the other unfavourable to their authority and sacredness. Wavering between the two are a number of writers whose veneration for these books is tempered by some perplexity as to their exact standing, and among those is
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Thomas_Aquinas" title="St. Thomas Aquinas">
       St. Thomas Aquinas
      </a>
      . Few are found to unequivocally acknowledge their canonicity. The prevailing attitude of Western medieval authors is substantially that of the Greek Fathers. The chief cause of this phenomenon in the West is to be sought in the influence, direct and indirect, of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Jerome" title="St. Jerome">
       St. Jerome
      </a>
      's depreciating Prologus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cedc_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-cedc-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Meanwhile, the protocanonical books of the Old Testament correspond with those of the Bible of the Hebrews, and the Old Testament as received by Protestants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cedc_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-cedc-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dead_Sea_scrolls">
       Dead Sea scrolls
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dead Sea scrolls">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Fragments of three deuterocanonical books have been found among the
      <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls" title="Dead Sea Scrolls">
       Dead Sea Scrolls
      </a>
      found at
      <a href="/wiki/Qumran" title="Qumran">
       Qumran
      </a>
      , in addition to several partial copies of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Enoch" title="Book of Enoch">
        I Enoch
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jubilees" title="Book of Jubilees">
        Jubilees
       </a>
      </i>
      from the Ethiopic deuterocanon.
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sirach" title="Sirach">
        Sirach
       </a>
      </i>
      , whose
      <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
       Hebrew
      </a>
      text was already known from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cairo_Geniza" title="Cairo Geniza">
       Cairo Geniza
      </a>
      , has been found in two scrolls (2QSir or 2Q18, 11QPs_a or 11Q5) in Hebrew. Another Hebrew scroll of
      <i>
       Sirach
      </i>
      has been found in
      <a href="/wiki/Masada" title="Masada">
       Masada
      </a>
      (MasSir).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abegg_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Abegg-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :597
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Tobit" title="Book of Tobit">
        Book of Tobit
       </a>
      </i>
      has been found in Qumran in four scrolls written in
      <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">
       Aramaic
      </a>
      and in one written in Hebrew.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abegg_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Abegg-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :636
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_Jeremiah" title="Letter of Jeremiah">
        Letter of Jeremiah
       </a>
      </i>
      (or
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Baruch" title="Book of Baruch">
        Baruch
       </a>
      </i>
      chapter 6) has been found in cave 7 (papyrus
      <a href="/wiki/7Q5" title="7Q5">
       7Q5
      </a>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">
       Greek
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abegg_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Abegg-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       :628
      </sup>
      It has been theorized by recent scholars
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      that the Qumran library was not entirely produced at Qumran, but may have included part of the library of the Jerusalem Temple, that may have been hidden in the caves for safekeeping at the time the Temple was destroyed by Romans in 70AD.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_the_Septuagint">
       Influence of the Septuagint
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence of the Septuagint">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The large majority of Old Testament references in the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
       New Testament
      </a>
      are taken from the
      <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">
       Koine Greek
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">
       Septuagint
      </a>
      (LXX) editions of which include the deuterocanonical books, as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">
       apocrypha
      </a>
      both of which are called collectively 
      <i>
       anagignoskomena
      </i>
      (things that are read or "profitable reading").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      No two Septuagint codices contain the same apocrypha,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the three earliest manuscripts of the LXX show uncertainty as to which books constitute the complete list of the Apocrypha.
      <a href="/wiki/Codex_Vaticanus" title="Codex Vaticanus">
       Codex Vaticanus
      </a>
      (B) lacks 14 Maccabees but includes 1 Esdras, while Sinaiticus (Aleph) omits Baruch, but includes 4 Maccabees.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Codex Alexandrinus includes the LXX, and Greek Psalm manuscripts from the fifth century contain three New Testament psalms: the Magnificat, the Benedictus, the Nunc Dimittis from Lukes birth narrative, and the conclusion of the hymn that begins with the Gloria in Excelsis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Beckwith states that manuscripts of anything like the capacity of Codex Alexandrinus were not used in the first centuries of the Christian era, and believes that the comprehensive codices of the Septuagint, which start appearing in the fourth century AD, are all of Christian origin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some deuterocanonical appear to have been written originally in
      <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
       Hebrew
      </a>
      , but the original text has long been lost. Archaeological finds, however, discovered some original texts among the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_scrolls" title="Dead Sea scrolls">
       Dead Sea scrolls
      </a>
      . The Septuagint was widely accepted and used by
      <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Judaism" title="Hellenistic Judaism">
       Greek-speaking Jews
      </a>
      in the 1st century, even in the region of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iudaea_Province" title="Iudaea Province">
       Roman Judea
      </a>
      , and therefore naturally became the text most widely used by early Christians, who were predominantly Greek speaking.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the New Testament, Hebrews 11:35 is understood by some as referring to an event that was recorded in one of the deuterocanonical books
      <a href="/wiki/2_Maccabees" title="2 Maccabees">
       2 Maccabees
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other New Testament authors also quote period literature which was familiar to the audience but that was not included in the Old Testament or the deuterocanonical books. For instance, Paul cites Greek writers and philosophers, and the author of Hebrews references oral tradition which spoke of an Old Testament prophet who was sawn in half in Hebrews 11:37, two verses after the 2nd Maccabees reference.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Jewish historian
      <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">
       Josephus
      </a>
      speaks of there being 22 books in the canon of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
       Hebrew Bible
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a Jewish tradition reported also by the Christian bishop
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athanasius" title="Athanasius">
       Athanasius
      </a>
      . However, included in Athanasius's list of 22
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
       Old Testament
      </a>
      books are
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Baruch" title="Book of Baruch">
       Baruch
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_Jeremiah" title="Letter of Jeremiah">
       Letter of Jeremiah
      </a>
      . At the same time, he mentioned that certain other books, including five deuterocanonical books but also the
      <a href="/wiki/Didache" title="Didache">
       Didache
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepherd_of_Hermas" title="Shepherd of Hermas">
       Shepherd of Hermas
      </a>
      , while not being part of the New Testament canon, "were appointed by the Fathers to be read". He excluded what he called "apocryphal writings" entirely.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canonOT_18-0">

        <span>

       </a>
      </sup>

      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">

      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">

        </span>

        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among the minority, in Trent, that showed opposition to these books' inclusion were Cardinals
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seripando" title="Seripando">
       Seripando
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cajetan" title="Thomas Cajetan">
       Cajetan
      </a>
      , the latter an opponent of Luther at Augsburg.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">

        <span>

        </span>

      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">

        </span>
        22
        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Trent confirmed the statements of earlier and less authoritative regional councils which also included the deuterocanonical books, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Hippo" title="Synod of Hippo">
       Synod of Hippo
      </a>
      (393), and the
      <a href="/wiki/Councils_of_Carthage" title="Councils of Carthage">
       Councils of Carthage
      </a>
      of 397. Much later (15th century), the
      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Florence" title="Council of Florence">
       Council of Florence
      </a>
      taught the divine inspiration of these books, but "did not formally pass on their canonicity."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canonOT_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-canonOT-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the canonical debate between Catholics and Protestants controversy remains as to the significance of Trent's omission of the Septuagint version of
      <a href="/wiki/1_Esdras" title="1 Esdras">
       1 Esdras
      </a>
      which Carthage may have ratified.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, there is ambiguity over the
      <a href="/wiki/Esdras#Differences_in_names" title="Esdras">
       naming of the books of Esdras
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       Canon of Carthage
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      lists two books of Esdras. This could mean 1 Esdras and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ezra-Nehemiah" title="Ezra-Nehemiah">
       Ezra-Nehemiah
      </a>
      as in the Septuagint or
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezra" title="Book of Ezra">
       Ezra
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Nehemiah" title="Book of Nehemiah">
       Nehemiah
      </a>
      as in the Vulgate.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_the_Vulgate">
       Influence of the Vulgate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influence of the Vulgate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">
       Jerome
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Vulgate#Prologues" title="Vulgate">
       Vulgate's prologues
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      describes a canon which excludes the deuterocanonical books, possibly excepting
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Baruch" title="Book of Baruch">
       Baruch
      </a>
      . In his
      <i>
       Prologues
      </i>
      , Jerome mentions all of the deuterocanonical and apocryphal works by name as being apocryphal or "not in the canon" except for
      <i>
       Prayer of Manasses
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Baruch
      </i>
      . He mentions
      <i>
       Baruch
      </i>
      by name in his
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.bombaxo.com/blog/?p=233" rel="nofollow">
       <i>
        Prologue to Jeremiah
       </i>
      </a>
      and notes that it is neither read nor held among the Hebrews, but does not explicitly call it apocryphal or "not in the canon".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The inferior status to which the deuterocanonical books were relegated by authorities like Jerome is seen by some as being due to a rigid conception of canonicity, one demanding that a book, to be entitled to this supreme dignity, must be received by all, must have the sanction of Jewish antiquity, and must moreover be adapted not only to edification, but also to the "confirmation of the doctrine of the Church".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Eventually however, Jerome's
      <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">
       Vulgate
      </a>
      did include the deuterocanonical books as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">
       apocrypha
      </a>
      . Jerome referenced and quoted from some as scripture despite describing them as "not in the canon". In his prologue to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judith" title="Judith">
       Judith
      </a>
      , without using the word canon, he mentioned that Judith was held to be scriptural by the
      <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">
       First Council of Nicaea
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In his reply to Rufinus, he affirmed that he was consistent with the choice of the church regarding which version of the deuterocanonical portions of Daniel to use, which the Jews of his day did not include:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       What sin have I committed if I followed the judgment of the churches? But he who brings charges against me for relating the objections that the Hebrews are wont to raise against the
       <a href="/wiki/Susanna_(Book_of_Daniel)" title="Susanna (Book of Daniel)">
        Story of Susanna
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/The_Prayer_of_Azariah_and_Song_of_the_Three_Holy_Children" title="The Prayer of Azariah and Song of the Three Holy Children">
        Song of the Three Children
       </a>
       , and the story of
       <a href="/wiki/Bel_and_the_Dragon" title="Bel and the Dragon">
        Bel and the Dragon
       </a>
       , which are not found in the Hebrew volume, proves that he is just a foolish sycophant. For I was not relating my own personal views, but rather the remarks that they [the Jews] are wont to make against us. (
       <i>
        Against Rufinus
       </i>
       , 11:33 [AD 402]).
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Thus Jerome acknowledged the principle by which the canon would be settled the judgment of the Church, rather than his own judgment or the judgment of Jews, though he wondered why one would sanction the version of a
      <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">
       heretic
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judaizer" title="Judaizer">
       judaizer
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Vulgate is also important as the touchstone of the canon concerning which parts of books are canonical. When the
      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">
       Council of Trent
      </a>
      listed the books included in the canon, it qualified the books as being "entire with all their parts, as they have been used to be read in the Catholic Church, and as they are contained in the old Latin vulgate edition".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This decree was clarified somewhat by
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">
       Pope Pius XI
      </a>
      on June 2, 1927, who allowed that the
      <a href="/wiki/Comma_Johanneum" title="Comma Johanneum">
       Comma Johanneum
      </a>
      was open to dispute, and it was further explicated by
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">
       Pope Pius XII
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Divino_afflante_Spiritu" title="Divino afflante Spiritu">
        Divino afflante Spiritu
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_in_Orthodox_Christianity_and_other_churches">
       Reception in Orthodox Christianity and other churches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception in Orthodox Christianity and other churches">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Outside the Roman Catholic Church, the term deuterocanonical is sometimes used, by way of analogy, to describe books that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">
       Eastern Orthodoxy
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">
       Oriental Orthodoxy
      </a>
      included in the
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
       Old Testament
      </a>
      that are not part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">
       Jewish
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">
       Tanakh
      </a>
      , nor the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">
       Protestant
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
       Old Testament
      </a>
      . Among Orthodox, the term is understood to mean that they were compiled separately from the primary canon, as explained in 2 Esdras, where Esdras is instructed to keep certain books separate and hidden.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodoxy">
       Eastern Orthodoxy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodoxy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Eastern Orthodox Churches have traditionally included all the books of the Septuagint in their Old Testaments. The Greeks use the word
      <i>
       Anagignoskomena
      </i>
      ( "readable, worthy to be read") to describe the books of the Greek
      <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">
       Septuagint
      </a>
      that are not present in the Hebrew
      <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">
       Tanakh
      </a>
      . When Orthodox theologians use the term "deuterocanonical," it is important to note that the meaning is not identical to the Roman Catholic usage. In Orthodox Christianity, deuterocanonical means that a book is part of the corpus of the Old Testament (i.e. is read during the services) but has secondary authority. In other words, deutero (second) applies to authority or witnessing power, whereas in Roman Catholicism, deutero applies to chronology (the fact that these books were confirmed later), not to authority.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Eastern Orthodox books of the the Old Testament include the deuterocanonical books listed above, plus
      <a href="/wiki/3_Maccabees" title="3 Maccabees">
       3 Maccabees
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/1_Esdras" title="1 Esdras">
       1 Esdras
      </a>
      (also included in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_Vulgate" title="Clementine Vulgate">
       Clementine Vulgate
      </a>
      ), while Baruch is divided from the Epistle of Jeremiah, making a total of 49 Old Testament books in contrast with the
      <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">
       Protestant
      </a>
      39-book canon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimbrough_35-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kimbrough-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Like the Roman Catholic deuterocanonical books, these texts are integrated with the rest of the Old Testament, not printed in a separate section.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other texts printed in Orthodox Bibles are considered of some value (like the additional
      <a href="/wiki/Psalm_151" title="Psalm 151">
       Psalm 151
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Prayer_of_Manasseh" title="Prayer of Manasseh">
       Prayer of Manasseh
      </a>
      ) or are included as an appendix (like the Greek
      <a href="/wiki/4_Maccabees" title="4 Maccabees">
       4 Maccabees
      </a>
      , and the Slavonic
      <a href="/wiki/2_Esdras" title="2 Esdras">
       2 Esdras
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimbrough_35-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kimbrough-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ethiopian_Orthodoxy">
       Ethiopian Orthodoxy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ethiopian Orthodoxy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">
       Amharic
      </a>
      Bible used by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Orthodox" title="Ethiopian Orthodox">
       Ethiopian Orthodox
      </a>
      Church (an Oriental Orthodox Church), those books of the Old Testament that are still counted as canonical, but not by all other Churches, are often set in a separate section titled
      <i>
       "Deeyutrokanoneekal"
      </i>
      (), which is the same word. The Ethiopian Orthodox Deuterocanon, in addition to the standard set listed above, along with the books of Esdras and
      <i>
       Prayer of Minasse
      </i>
      , also includes some books that are still held canonical by only the Ethiopian Church, including Enoch or
      <i>
       Henok
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Enoch" title="I Enoch">
       I Enoch
      </a>
      ),
      <i>
       Kufale
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jubilees" title="Jubilees">
       Jubilees
      </a>
      ) and 1, 2 and 3
      <a href="/wiki/Meqabyan" title="Meqabyan">
       Meqabyan
      </a>
      (which are sometimes wrongly confused with the "Books of Maccabees").
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_position">
       Jewish position
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Jewish position">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_Hebrew_Bible_canon" title="Development of the Hebrew Bible canon">
       Development of the Hebrew Bible canon
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Judaism and most Protestant versions of the Bible exclude these books. It is commonly said that
      <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">
       Judaism
      </a>
      officially excluded the deuterocanonicals and the additional Greek texts listed here from their Scripture in the
      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Jamnia" title="Council of Jamnia">
       Council of Jamnia
      </a>
      (c.7090AD), but this claim is also disputed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_in_Christian_churches_having_their_origins_in_the_Reformation">
       Reception in Christian churches having their origins in the Reformation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception in Christian churches having their origins in the Reformation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anglicanism">
       Anglicanism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Anglicanism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There is a great deal of overlap between the
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">
       Apocrypha
      </a>
      section of the original 1611
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" title="King James Bible">
       King James Bible
      </a>
      and the Catholic deuterocanon, but the two are distinct. The Apocrypha section of the original 1611
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" title="King James Bible">
       King James Bible
      </a>
      includes, in addition to the deuterocanonical books, the following three books, which were not included in the list of the canonical books by the
      <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">
       Council of Trent
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/1_Esdras" title="1 Esdras">
        1 Esdras
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">
        Vulgate
       </a>
       3 Esdras)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/2_Esdras" title="2 Esdras">
        2 Esdras
       </a>
       (Vulgate 4 Esdras)
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Prayer_of_Manasseh" title="Prayer of Manasseh">
        Prayer of Manasseh
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      These books make up the
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha#Clementine_Vulgate" title="Biblical apocrypha">
       Apocrypha
      </a>
      section of the
      <a href="/wiki/Vulgate#Clementine_Vulgate" title="Vulgate">
       Clementine Vulgate
      </a>
      :
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3_Esdras" title="3 Esdras">
       3 Esdras
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/1_Esdras" title="1 Esdras">
       1 Esdras
      </a>
      );
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4_Esdras" title="4 Esdras">
       4 Esdras
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/2_Esdras" title="2 Esdras">
       2 Esdras
      </a>
      ); and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Prayer_of_Manasseh" title="The Prayer of Manasseh">
       The Prayer of Manasseh
      </a>
      , where they are specifically described as "outside of the series of the canon". The 1609
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douay-Rheims_Bible" title="Douay-Rheims Bible">
       Douai Bible
      </a>
      includes them in an appendix, but they have been dropped from recent Catholic translations into English. They are found, along with the deuterocanonical books, in the
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">
       Apocrypha
      </a>
      section of Protestant bibles.
     </p>
     <p>
      Using the word apocrypha (Greek: hidden away) to describe texts, although not necessarily pejorative, implies to some people that the writings in question should not be included in the
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">
       canon
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Books_of_the_Bible" title="Books of the Bible">
       Bible
      </a>
      . This classification commingles them with certain non-canonical
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">
       gospels
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Testament_Apocrypha" title="New Testament Apocrypha">
       New Testament Apocrypha
      </a>
      . The
      <i>
       Style Manual for the Society of Biblical Literature
      </i>
      recommends the use of the term
      <i>
       deuterocanonical literature
      </i>
      instead of
      <i>
       Apocrypha
      </i>
      in academic writing.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Thirty-Nine_Articles" title="Thirty-Nine Articles">
       Thirty-Nine Articles
      </a>
      of Religion of the
      <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">
       Church of England
      </a>
      lists the deuterocanonical books as suitable to be read for "example of life and instruction of manners, but yet doth not apply them to establish any doctrine." The early lectionaries of the Anglican Church (as included in the
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">
       Book of Common Prayer
      </a>
      of 1662) included the deuterocanonical books amongst the cycle of readings, and passages from them were used in the services (such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Benedicite" title="Benedicite">
       Benedicite
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      Readings from the deuterocanonical books are now included in most, if not all, of the modern lectionaries in the Anglican Communion, based on the Revised Common Lectionary (in turn based on the post-conciliar Roman Catholic lectionary).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Presbyterianism">
       Presbyterianism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Presbyterianism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Confession_of_Faith" title="Westminster Confession of Faith">
       Westminster Confession of Faith
      </a>
      , a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvinist" title="Calvinist">
       Calvinist
      </a>
      document that serves as a systematic summary of doctrine for the
      <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">
       Church of Scotland
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian" title="Presbyterian">
       Presbyterian
      </a>
      churches worldwide, recognizes only the sixty-six books of the
      <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon#Canons_of_various_Christian_traditions" title="Biblical canon">
       Protestant canon
      </a>
      as authentic Scripture. Chapter 1, Article 3 of the Confession reads: "The books commonly called Apocrypha, not being of divine inspiration, are no part of the Canon of Scripture; and therefore are of no authority in the Church of God, nor to be any otherwise approved, or made use of, than other human writings."
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reformed_churches">
       Reformed churches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reformed churches">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Belgic_Confession" title="Belgic Confession">
       Belgic Confession
      </a>
      , used in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformed_churches" title="Reformed churches">
       Reformed churches
      </a>
      , devotes a section (Article 6) to "The difference between the canonical and apocryphal books" and asserts that "All which the Church may read and take instruction from, so far as they agree with the canonical books; but they are far from having such power and efficacy as that we may from their testimony confirm any point of faith or of the Christian religion; much less to detract from the authority of the other sacred books."
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="New_Testament_apocrypha_and_deuterocanonicals">
       New Testament apocrypha and deuterocanonicals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deuterocanonical_books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: New Testament apocrypha and deuterocanonicals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Antilegomena" title="Antilegomena">
       Antilegomena
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_New_Testament_canon" title="Development of the New Testament canon">
       Development of the New Testament canon
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       deuterocanonical
      </i>
      is sometimes used to describe the canonical
      <a href="/wiki/Antilegomena" title="Antilegomena">
       antilegomena
      </a>
      , those books of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
       New Testament
      </a>
      which, like the deuterocanonicals of the Old Testament, were not universally accepted by the early Church, but which are now included in the 27 books of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
       New Testament
      </a>
      recognized by almost all
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christians
      </a>
      . The deuterocanonicals of the New Testament are as follows:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Hebrews" title="Epistle to the Hebrews">
        Epistle to the Hebrews
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Epistle_of_James" title="Epistle of James">
        Epistle of James
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_of_Peter" title="Second Epistle of Peter">
        Second Epistle of Peter
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_of_John" title="Second Epistle of John">
        Second Epistle of John
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Third_Epistle_of_John" title="Third Epistle of John">
        Third Epistle of John
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Epistle_of_Jude" title="Epistle of Jude">
        Epistle of Jude
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
        Apocalypse of John
       </a>
       (also known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
        Book of Revelation
       </a>
       )
      </li>
     </ul>
