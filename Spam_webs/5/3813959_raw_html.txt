     <h1 class="post-title mob-marg-b-25 med-marg-b-50 big-marg-b-50" data-js="postTitle" itemprop="name" tabindex="0">
      Dropping Objects in Worlds Largest Vacuum Chamber
     </h1>
    </header>
    <section class="post-container clearfix relative" data-js="post" id="start-of-content" tabindex="0">
     <article class="content link-underline relative body-copy" data-js="content" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div align="center">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="360" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/E43-CfukEgs" width="640">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
       Dropping things can be fun. Dropping things in a vacuum is even cooler. You might think that dropping things in a giant vacuum chamber would be the ultimate in coolness. Well, its close. In fact, this is the best feather and heavy object dropping video.
      </p>
      <div align="center">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="360" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/ZVfhztmK9zI" width="480">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
       Yes, astronaut David Scott dropped a hammer and feather in a much larger vacuum chamber  the moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Heavier Objects Dont Hit the Ground First
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="http://www.wired.com/2013/10/do-heavier-objects-really-fall-faster/">
        Ive already covered the common ideas about dropping objects.
       </a>
       In general, most people think that heavier objects should fall faster than lighter objects. Really, what they mean is that heavier objects should fall with a greater acceleration than light objects, but they like to say faster.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here is the short answer.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        If there is no air resistance, after you let go of an object the only force on it is the gravitational force.
       </li>
       <li>
        The gravitational force is proportional to the mass of the object. More massive objects have a greater gravitational force.
       </li>
       <li>
        The acceleration of an object is proportional to the net force on the object and inversely proportional to the mass of the object.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Let me write this mathematically.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="La te xi t 1" class="aligncenter" height="123" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/la_te_xi_t_1.jpg" width="156"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       See. The masses cancel. Mass doesnt matter even though matter is made of mass (physics pun). Also, I wrote these equations as scalar instead of vectors just to make it look simpler.
      </p>
      <h2>
       The Bowling Ball and Feather in Real Speed
      </h2>
      <p>
       The bowling ball and feather drop in the BBC Human Universe video looks awesome. However, they ran the shot in slow motion to make it look more dramatic. Wouldnt in be cool to see it in real time? I think I can make that happen.
      </p>
      <p>
       Normally, I would take a video like this and find the real frame rate. Ive done this before
       <a href="http://www.wired.com/2011/10/video-analysis-of-a-falling-car/">
        with some of the MythBusters videos.
       </a>
       The basic idea is to look at a falling object. Since you know the acceleration should be -9.8 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       , you can just find the correct frame rate to give you that acceleration. Its pretty simple. However, that doesnt work in this case. The problem here is that there are two things I dont know. I dont know the distance scale and I dont know the frame rate. This means I need another strategy.
      </p>
      <p>
       Luckily, the video shows the same bowling ball and feather dropping with air and in real time. I can use that to find the scale of the video. In this case, I will use the close up shot that shows the bowling ball and I will find the diameter.
      </p>
      <figure class="wp-caption landscape aligncenter fader" data-js="fader">
       <img alt="Bowlingball 1" height="423" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/bowlingball_1.jpg" width="548"/>
       <figcaption class="wp-caption-text link-underline">
        Image: screenshot from youtube
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       If I use a bowling ball diameter of 21.59 cm, the falling ball seems to have the correct acceleration. Here is a plot of the vertical motion of that first fall.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Balldropwithair 1" class="aligncenter" height="448" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/balldropwithair_1.jpg" width="540"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Of course this is using the free video analysis program
       <a href="https://www.cabrillo.edu/~dbrown/tracker/">
        Tracker
       </a>
       . Also, remember that the kinematic equation for an object with a constant acceleration (in the y-direction) is:
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="La te xi t 1" class="aligncenter" height="55" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/la_te_xi_t_11.jpg" width="195"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term in front of
       <em>
        t
       </em>
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       in the fitting equation would be 1/2 of the acceleration. So, a coefficient of -4.73 would give an acceleration of 9.46 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       . This isnt 9.8 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       like I would expect, but its close enough.
      </p>
      <p>
       I can also get the total falling time from the video with a value of 2.04 seconds. This means that I can solve for the drop height of the ball.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="La te xi t 1" class="aligncenter" height="152" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/la_te_xi_t_12.jpg" width="224"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, I ignored the air resistance on the bowling ball during this drop. Is that ok? Lets say the ball has a mass of 6 kg. If you then create a numerical calculation for a falling ball both with and without air resistance, you get a time difference of just 0.048 seconds. Yes, you can try this calculation for yourself (as a homework exercise).
      </p>
      <p>
       Moving on to the slow motion video (without air), I get the following plot for the vertical position of the bowling ball.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Screenshot 11 3 14  10 25 am" class="aligncenter" height="451" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/screenshot_11_3_14_10_25_am.jpg" width="539"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       This gives an acceleration of 0.018 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
        but thats not a real second, thats a fake second (since the video isnt in real time). If I call this time unit s, I can set this acceleration equal to 9.8 m/s
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (real seconds here) and solve for the relationship between real and fake time.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="La te xi t 1" class="aligncenter" height="127" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/la_te_xi_t_13.jpg" width="262"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       This means the slow motion video would have to be recorded at 580 frames per second instead of 25 frames per second. Perfect. Now I just need to increase the speed. Heres what that would look like.
      </p>
      <figure class="wp-caption landscape aligncenter fader" data-js="fader">
       <img alt="Bbcballdrop" height="303" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/bbcballdrop.gif" width="374"/>
       <figcaption class="wp-caption-text link-underline">
        Speed corrected gif from BBC Human Universe video on youtube.
       </figcaption>
      </figure>
      <p>
       Pretty cool, right? Ok. I admit that I cheated a little bit. I used iMovie to speed up the video and there is a default 20x speed increase so I used that. Yes, its not 23 times faster but it still looks better.
      </p>
      <h2>
       Two More Points
      </h2>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Look at the feather.
       </strong>
       In the case with no air (and you can really see this in the slow motion video), the feather parts move right when the feather is released. Is this because there is air? Nope. Its because the tips of the feather are in equilibrium before the drop. This means that other parts of the stem have to pull up on the feather tips in order to make the net force zero. When the feather is released, this streching force is still acting on the tip of the feather and causes it to move up with respect to the rest of the feather.
      </p>
      <p>
       This is just like the famous (well, famous to me)
       <a href="http://www.wired.com/2011/09/modeling-a-falling-slinky/">
        falling slinky
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        You could drop something else.
       </strong>
       I think this would have been a perfect video to do something even cooler. Everyone drops a ball and a feather. Thats been done many times. Also, everyone already expects the bowling ball to hit the ground first. Their usual reasoning is that the bowling ball hits the ground first because its heavier.
      </p>
      <p>
       What if I told you that sometimes the heavier things fall with a lower acceleration? Yup. Its true. Suppose you took something like a large foam ice chest (cooler) and a small rock. The ice chest has a larger mass, but the rock would hit the ground first (in the presence of air). Its not just about mass. The cooler has a larger mass but it also has a much larger surface area to create a larger air resistance force.
      </p>
      <p>
       In this video, I drop a large foam board and a small piece of paper. Guess which one hits the ground first?
      </p>
      <div align="center">
       <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="360" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/xZs9rve5LdY" width="640">
       </iframe>
      </div>
      <p>
       It would be cool to see the same type of objects dropped in the giant vacuum chamber. Oh, and I probably could have figured out this slow motion frame rate with much less hassle if I had noticed this tiny time stamp in the lower left of the video.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/timestampbbc.jpg">
        <img alt="timestampbbc" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1622529" height="59" src="http://www.wired.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/timestampbbc.jpg" width="84"/>
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its tiny indeed  but it does show the time. I wish all slow motion videos had this feature (but a little bigger so I could see it).
      </p>
