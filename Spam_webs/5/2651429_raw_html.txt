          <img alt="Tony Blair took thousands of pounds from UI Energy Corporation" class="blkBorder" height="405" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/pix/2010/03/19/article-0-083C2670000005DC-412_233x405.jpg" width="233"/>

         <p>
          Tony Blair prides himself on his moral compass, and claims that his
profound faith has guided all the important decisions in his life.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          And
yet his conscience apparently gave no peep of protest as he set about
filling his pockets with Iraqi oil money.
         </p>
         <p>
          The news that our former Prime Minister took hundreds of thousands
of pounds from multinational oil giant UI Energy Corporation is
shocking enough - but even worse is the fact that he spent two years
trying to conceal it, dubiously citing commercial confidentiality.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          The first fact merely tells us that our former leader is driven by a
desperate desire to feather his nest, while the second shows that he
must have known such a deal was wrong.
         </p>
         <p>
          Legally, of course, his oil fortune is above board. But morally it is unconscionable.
         </p>
         <p>
          This is the man who took the deeply unpopular decision to join
forces with the Americans and topple Saddam. This is the man who rode
roughshod over any objections with terrifying claims about 'weapons of
mass destruction'. This is the man who said it wasn't about the oil.
         </p>
         <p>
          And in the years since, as we've learned about the dodgy
dossiers and the secret meetings and the spurious reasoning, there was
still one consolation: that whatever the dubious means he used to take
us to war, at least Tony Blair was doing what he thought was right.
          <br/>
         </p>

         <p>
          Can we believe that any more, as he pockets another 100,000 from a speech to the credulous
Americans who still worship him for his slavish support of George Bush?
Does it ring true now we know that Blair also went to great lengths to
hide 1 million he earned from advising the Kuwaiti royal family?
         </p>
         <p>
          Moral compass? He's driven by a greedometer. There's nothing
this 'man of principle' wants more than to join the ranks of the
multi-millionaires he idolises. He doesn't want respect, just riches.
         </p>
         <p>
          Meanwhile, since the Western invasion brought U.S. energy
firms into Iraq, its government predicts a 300 per cent increase in oil
production, which has already leapt from 300,000 barrels a day after
the first Gulf War to 2.5 million barrels. There's money being made by
the sackful here, and hundreds of thousands of pounds of it is going to
the man who made the whole bonanza possible in the first place.
         </p>
         <p>
          As Blair and his rich chums cash in, Iraq's soil is stained
with the blood of 179 British troops and hundreds of thousands of
civilians. What did they die for? The oil to fuel Blair's first private
jet?
         </p>
         <p>
          Our former Prime Minister famously claimed he felt the hand of
history on his shoulder. What we couldn't see was that he also had his
hand in the till.

           </p>
           <p>
            But he is right about one thing on the pamphlet. 'We can't go on like this'  -  well, not if the Tories want to win.
           </p>
           <p>
            
            <b>
             A bad week for Hilary Benn. First he backtracked on taxing
dog owners in case their mutt chomps the postman, then
             <br/>
            </b>
           </p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <h2>
          Handbags at dawn, boys
         </h2>
         <p>
          I'm certainly not advocating physical violence, but surely there's a
more manly way to respond to an insult than smearing someone in
chocolate cake?
         </p>
         <p>
          And yet that was apparently PR guru Matthew Freud's weapon of choice after being slighted by Hugh Grant at a party.
         </p>
         <p>
          The ever-gentlemanly Grant is alleged to have replied with a
punch, although judging by Freud's little-marked face the next day,
Hugh's right hook must be as floppy as his hair.
         </p>
         <p>
          The whole thing reminds me of that absurd fight at the end of
the Bridget Jones film.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          The only way it could have been more homoerotic
is if they'd wrestled naked in front of a fire, Women In Love-style.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Baloney or alimony?
          <br/>
         </h2>
         <p>
          The passionate and principled Marco Pierre White has signed up to
promote Bernard Matthews's turkey range.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          In entirely unrelated news,
the fiery chef is still grappling with ex-wife Mati over her divorce
payout.
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          'Bernard Matthews is an iconic British brand based on one man's
vision more than five decades ago,' White gushed this week. Baloney -
or alimony? Only time will tell.
         </p>
