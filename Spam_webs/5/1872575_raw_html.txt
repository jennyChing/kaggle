     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
       astrophysics
      </a>
      ,
      <b>
       accretion
      </b>
      is the growth of particles into a massive object by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
       gravitationally
      </a>
      attracting more matter, typically
      <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
       gaseous
      </a>
      matter in an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
       accretion disk
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This attracted matter accelerates the growth of the particles into boulder-sized
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetesimals" title="Planetesimals">
       planetesimals
      </a>
      . The more massive planetesimals accrete some smaller ones, while others shatter in collisions. Accretion disks are common around smaller stars or stellar remnants in a
      <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">
       close binary
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">
       black holes
      </a>
      in the centers of spiral
      <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">
       galaxies
      </a>
      . Some dynamics in the disk are necessary to allow orbiting gas to lose
      <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
       angular momentum
      </a>
      and fall onto the central massive object. Occasionally, this can result in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_surface_fusion" title="Stellar surface fusion">
       stellar surface fusion
      </a>
      . (See:
      <a href="/wiki/Bondi_accretion" title="Bondi accretion">
       Bondi accretion
      </a>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      The formation of Terrestrial planets differs from that of Jovian planets. The particles that made up the Terrestrial planets were made from metal and rock that condensed in the inner Solar System. However, the Jovian planets began as large, ice planetesimals, which then captured hydrogen and helium gas from the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" title="Solar nebula">
       solar nebula
      </a>
      . The planetesimals which form the two types of planets differ due to the
      <a href="/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)" title="Frost line (astrophysics)">
       frost line (astrophysics)
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     </h2>
     <p>
      The Jovian protoplanets probably
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">

          according to whom?

        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      had disks of their own, in close analogy to the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
       Solar System
      </a>
      as a whole. A Jovian protoplanet may accrete gas from its surrounding
      <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
       protoplanetary disk
      </a>
      .
