              </a>
              <span class="picGalCaption">
               A number of Habronattus spiders [H. pugilis female above], including the unknown specimen, were collected in the Santa Rita mountains south of Tucson, Arizona. Mr Shahans images were taken using a Pentax K-x, extension tubes and a reversed 50mm lens.
              </span>

              <a href="http://news.bbcimg.co.uk/media/images/66259000/jpg/_66259110_mysterybluefacedhabronattus.jpg">

              </a>
              <span class="picGalCaption">
               Further genetic work will be required to determine whether the spider is an undocumented hybrid or a new species. Mr Shahan said: The real excitement comes from not only witnessing their behaviour but observing the immense variety and beauty of facial markings."
              </span>
             </li>
             <li id="picGalleryNoScript_6">
              <a href="http://news.bbcimg.co.uk/media/images/66260000/jpg/_66260431_untitled_panorama1.jpg">

              </a>
              <span class="picGalCaption">
               Finding the tiny Habronattus spiders was not an easy task. Mr Shahan explained: These spiders are quite difficult to spot - as they are often mottled with brownish tan and black markings from a dorsal view, and are often hanging out in sandy to rocky areas, making them nearly invisible.
              </span>
             </li>
             <li id="picGalleryNoScript_7">
              <a href="http://news.bbcimg.co.uk/media/images/66260000/jpg/_66260086_habronattushallanimale.jpg">

              </a>
              <span class="picGalCaption">
               Mr Shahan concluded: I find [jumping spiders] exemplary for spreading an appreciation and admiration for spiders. They're colourful, ornate, beautiful little animals that many people can find attractive. I love salticids - and simply want more people to love them as well."
              </span>
