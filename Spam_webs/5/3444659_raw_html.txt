        <p>
         <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/07/canning-not-what-you-think.jpg" rel="lightbox[17054]">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-17127 aligncenter" height="377" src="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/07/canning-not-what-you-think.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Written by Anne S, Guest Writer
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         In the old days, canning was just part of the household routine, a useful way to preserve the bounty of the growing season for the cold dark days of winter. Mothers passed down the skills of canning to their daughters in the same way they passed down all the other essential house-keeping skills  by example and instruction.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unfortunately,
         <strong>
          somewhere along the way, the practice of canning fell by the wayside
         </strong>
         as mothers began to rely more and more on the mass-produced canned goods (and shipped-from-far-away fresh goods) from the grocery store.
        </p>
        <p>
         Thankfully,
         <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/2010/08/preserving-summers-bounty-recipes-and-tutorials-to-keep-you-busy-until-thanksgiving.html" target="_blank">
          canning is making a comeback
         </a>
         , but few of us have the benefit of real-life tutors to walk us through the process. Consequently,
         <strong>
          the whole idea can seem scary and more than a little bit complicated!
         </strong>
         Hopefully, I can help you ease your mind and get you started on your own canning career.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/07/jar-in-water-bath.jpg" rel="lightbox[17054]">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-17128 aligncenter" height="504" src="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/07/jar-in-water-bath.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <h3>
         Canning is Easier than You Think
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <em>
           Canning is not difficult,
          </em>
         </strong>
         at least not in the sense of needing a special skill set. When it gets right down to it,
         <strong>
          <em>
           canning is simply a matter of immersing hot jars into boiling water long enough
          </em>
         </strong>
         to suck out all the air, thereby sealing the jars. Thats it!
        </p>
        <p>
         Canning is super easy if you
         <strong>
          begin with basic recipes that involve simple ingredients and minimal steps, like these:
         </strong>
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="http://quickeasycheaphealthy.com/2011/07/pickles-3-ways-fresh-pack-canned-pickles/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://quickeasycheaphealthy.com/2011/07/pickles-3-ways-fresh-pack-canned-pickles/', 'Fresh Pack Dill Pickles']);" target="_blank">
           Fresh Pack Dill Pickles
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="http://quickeasycheaphealthy.com/2010/12/qech-gifts-from-your-kitchen-jam/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://quickeasycheaphealthy.com/2010/12/qech-gifts-from-your-kitchen-jam/', 'Mixed Berry Jam']);" target="_blank">
           Mixed Berry Jam
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="http://www.pickyourown.org/peachescanning.htm" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.pickyourown.org/peachescanning.htm', 'Canned Peaches']);" target="_blank">
           Canned Peaches
          </a>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         One of my favorite websites,
         <a href="http://pickyourown.org/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://pickyourown.org/', 'Pick Your Own']);" target="_blank">
          Pick Your Own
         </a>
         , has a fabulous and detailed
         <a href="http://pickyourown.org/allaboutcanning.htm" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://pickyourown.org/allaboutcanning.htm', 'section on canning ']);" target="_blank">
          section on canning
         </a>
         with step-by-step tutorials complete with pictures. All the information you need to get started is right there on that site.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Canning is Faster Than You Think
        </h3>
        <p>
         Canning doesnt take all day. At least it doesnt have to. There certainly are people who set aside a day (or days) to can produce from their farm or garden, but thats because they have a lot of extra produce to preserve.
        </p>
        <p>
         Since I dont have a prolific garden (just a couple tomato plants, a jalapeno pepper plant, and a few herbs this year), I dont have a lot to preserve: most of what I preserve comes from what I pick myself at local farms or buy in bulk at the farmers market. Consequently,
         <strong>
          for me 
          <em>
           and likely, for you
          </em>
           small batch canning is a much more practical way to go.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         The honest truth is that
         <em>
          <strong>
           one batch of canned jam (or pickles, or
           <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/2011/09/how-to-peel-peaches-and-tomatoes-for-canning.html" target="_blank">
            peaches
           </a>
           , or what-have-you) takes about an hour, start to finish
          </strong>
         </em>
         . By start, I mean washing the produce, and by finish, I mean a row of gleaming sealed jars. When people can for an entire day  or longer  they are simply repeating this process over and over.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <em>
           So heres my advice: start small.
          </em>
         </strong>
         One batch at a time, one hour at a time.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Canning is Cheaper Than You Think
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Canning doesnt really require a lot of special equipment.
         </strong>
         You just need a really big pot (I used my stock pot for a while, until I found a canning pot at a thrift store), some canning tongs (which are very inexpensive),
         <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/2012/04/31-ways-to-use-a-mason-jar-in-your-kitchen.html" target="_blank">
          good jars
         </a>
         , and
         <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/index.php?adclick=104" target="_blank">
          lids
         </a>
         . Even if you bought all the supplies new, a basic canning kit would cost a total of $100 or less. And you can
         <a href="http://quickeasycheaphealthy.com/2012/04/how-to-get-canning-supplies-for-cheap-or-free/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://quickeasycheaphealthy.com/2012/04/how-to-get-canning-supplies-for-cheap-or-free/', 'get all your canning supplies for much less than that  even for free']);" target="_blank">
          get all your canning supplies for much less than that  even for free
         </a>
          if you know where to look.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/07/pickles.jpg" rel="lightbox[17054]">
          <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-17129 aligncenter" height="333" src="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/07/pickles.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <h3>
         Canning is Safer Than You Think
        </h3>
        <p>
         One of my biggest concerns that kept me from canning for the longest time was the issue of safety. I read horror stories and dire cautions online about botulism from home-canned goods. I still have a healthy fear of botulism, but after safely consuming jar after jar of home-canned food, my mind rests a little easier now.
        </p>
        <p>
         The truth is,
         <strong>
          all you have to do is follow a few simple safety rules, and you can avoid botulism
         </strong>
         :
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Always,
          <strong>
           <em>
            always
           </em>
           make sure your canning water is at a full rolling boil.
          </strong>
          When you immerse the jars in the water, the boil might die down briefly. Dont start your timer until the water returns to its full boil.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Follow the timing guidelines for boiling the jars
          </strong>
          (its different for each type of food or recipe), and adjust for altitude as necessary.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Pay careful attention to the headspace
          </strong>
          (the distance between the surface of the food and the lid of the jar. The general rule is: 1/4 inch for jams and jellies, 1/2 inch for fruits, 1 inch for vegetables.
         </li>
         <li>
          Unless you are pickling them,
          <strong>
           all vegetables must be canned using a pressure canner.
          </strong>
          Some vegetables, like pumpkin, should never be canned at home at all. All fruits and pickled vegetables can be safely canned in a boiling water bath.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Always start with hot, sterilized jars and lids.
          </strong>
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Dont re-use metal lids
          </strong>
          (some people do, but I prefer to play it safe).
          <a href="http://www.keeperofthehome.org/index.php?adclick=104" target="_blank">
           Tattlers lids
          </a>
          are the only kind that can safely be re-used. Dont use metal bands if they are rusted or bent.
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Dont use jars not intended for canning
          </strong>
          (like jars left over from store-bought products).
         </li>
         <li>
          <strong>
           Dont use jars with knicks or cracks.
          </strong>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         If you still have concerns about canning and need a little more guidance,
         <a href="http://nchfp.uga.edu/" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'http://nchfp.uga.edu/', 'The National Center for Home Food Preservation']);" target="_blank">
          The National Center for Home Food Preservation
         </a>
         has been an invaluable resource for me. All the instructions, recipes, and suggestions err on the side of caution, so I know if I follow their guides, Ill be safe!
        </p>
        <h2>
         Do you do home canning? If you havent tried yet, what is it thats holding you back?
        </h2>

               <div class="comment-content">
                <p>
                 If I were reading your article without knowing a thing about canning, I would think it was awesome &amp; it would inspire me to run right out &amp; start canning.   However, Ive been canning for almost 40 years and there are 2 things that to me are glarinly mis-stated.
                </p>
                <p>
                 When you say all you have to do is put a jar in boiling water that covers the jar and thats all there is to it, that screams TROUBLE to me.
                </p>
                <p>
                 There are 2 different types of canning methods and while I know very clearly that you are speaking of Water Bath Canning for high acid vegetables and fruit (including pickles) someone else may not know that.
                 <br/>
                 They may assume that anything can be canned this way, even though somewhere else you do mention high acid &amp; pickles.   In canning, its always better to be safe than sorry and make sure all the Ts are crossed &amp; the is dotted.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The other was that you cant can pumpkin.  The USDA doesnt recommend you can pureed or pumpkin butter because of the density of it, but you can can pumpkin cubes or sections.. However, again, it cant be done in a water bath canning process and you must use a true pressure canner..   and in a canner, you dont cover the jars with water.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Home canned goods last many years when theyre kept in a dark cool place such as a basement.  Theyll be good much longer than any commercially produced goods if theyve been canned correctly.
                 <br/>
                 I personally tend to start dumping anything beyond the 5 or 6 year mark, but technically they can still be eaten, though the nutrition lessesns over the years.    I can all manner of fruit, veggies, &amp; meat.  Its a wonderful way to store your harvest and not have to worry about the electricity going off and my food in my freezer ruining.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Hope you enjoy all your canning endevors.. I wouldnt want to live without it.
                 <br/>
                 :)
                </p>

              <p>
               If you dont have a water bath canner yet you might be able to consider a pressure canner to do double duty.  Ive been canning for 35 years and have found some shortcuts.  One, for around 10 years, is using the pressure canner without a gauge for water bath products (fruits and high acid)   I dont feel the need to cover the jars more than halfway with water because the canner lid is sealed tight and the top half is steam  which is hotter than boiling water. When its fully boiling  I turn the heat down so I can still hear boiling within the canner but not a ton of steam coming out the vent.
              </p>
              <p>
               Just a thought, anyway.  I feel it takes less energy and, of course less water to use this method.  Plus it saves me from storing pressure canners (I have two) AND boiling water bath pots.

            <p>
             My mom was a serious canner.  Im lucky enough to have one of her 1970s mirro canners.. Turns out parts are still available for them.
            </p>
            <p>
             I can (pressure can) meats.  Chicken breasts/pork roasts/beef/hamburger.  Hamburger is the only on that needs pre-cooked, and thats primarily due to fat content.
            </p>
            <p>
             90 minutes at 10psi.  DONE..  On the shelf, no refrigeration/freezing no thawing..  Pre-cooked..  It ends up being faster than fast food, and of course cheaper and safer.
            </p>
            <p>
             I buy chicken breasts (boneless) when theyre less than $2 a pound, same with pork roasts..  If youre willing to give their meat department time (usually a few hours) theyll cut the pork into stew meat sized cubes for you which makes canning a lot quicker.  The chicken breasts will usually slide into the jars with a minimum of effort and I dont cut them up..
            </p>
            <p>
             Chicken and pork come out fork tender, so theres no need to worry about getting them out of the can.
            </p>
            <p>
             USDA says once PRESSURE canned, theyre good for a year.  I can attest to safely feeding my family older cans, without issue, so long as the seal remains intact.  They dont get the freezer burn that long term frozen foods suffer from.. Theres certainly less waste..
            </p>
            <p>
             Hope this helps someone.. :)
            </p>
