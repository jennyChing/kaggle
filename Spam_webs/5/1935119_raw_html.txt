         <h1 class="name post-title entry-title" itemprop="itemReviewed" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Thing">
          <span itemprop="name">
           Fourth Wall Funk: James Brown &amp; Get On Up
          </span>
         </h1>
         <p class="post-meta">
          <span class="post-meta-author">
           Posted by:
           <a href="http://thehollywoodbillboard.com/author/quinn-vincent-hough/" title="">
            Quinn Vincent Hough
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="tie-date">
           08/01/2014
          </span>
          <span class="post-comments">
           <a href="http://thehollywoodbillboard.com/fourth-wall-funk/#respond">
            0
           </a>
          </span>
         </p>
         <div class="clear">
         </div>
         <div class="entry">
          <p>
           Do you wanna get down? Yeah! Do you want a lights-out, shut down performance from
           <span id="more-2367">
           </span>
           <strong>
            Chadwick Boseman
           </strong>
           as James Brown? YEAH! Do you want a disjointed film that tells you when to laugh or cry? Heh. Director
           <strong>
            Tate Taylor
           </strong>
           has all the right moves with
           <em>
            Get On Up
           </em>
           , but just not in the right spots.
          </p>
          <p>
           The musical biopic has two major flaws: (1) the narratives sudden shifting of time and (2) a complete destruction of the fourth wall. Taylor opens
           <em>
            Get On Up
           </em>
           by focusing on the 1988 arrest of
           <strong>
            James Brown
           </strong>
           after he terrorized a group of individuals and fled from the police. Its an interesting place to start, but carries little emotion and ultimately shifts the focus to the makeup on Bosemans face. Taylor seems to be showing off the makeup departments work and informing us that his lead actor was completely transformed for the role. Its too bad, because the brilliant performance of Boseman speaks for itself.
          </p>
          <p>
           After the odd open, the action jumps back and forth from 1968 to 1939 and finally to 1964 when Bosemans James Brown peers into the camera (breaking the fourth wall for the 1
           <sup>
            st
           </sup>
           of 14 times) and says, You cats ready? The entire sequence is far too long and uses biopic clichs that weve seen in recent films like
           <em>
            Walk The Line
           </em>
           and
           <em>
            Ray
           </em>
           . Its becoming all too common to immediately address the characters darkest hour, shift back to an unpleasant childhood experience and then to the character on top of the world. Taylor employs such techniques.
          </p>
          <p>
           The performance of
           <strong>
            Dan Aykroyd
           </strong>
           as James Browns agent, Ben Bart, appears to have connected with audiences and critics, but it seems based on nostalgia rather than an overall fit for the film. From the moment Aykroyd appears on screen it takes away from the authenticity factor because
           <em>
            we all know Dan Aykroyd
           </em>
           . Furthermore, the accent and choppy phrasing reminds of
           <em>
            Saturday Night Live
           </em>
           , plus Aykroyd just tries too hard. Look closely. A character actor would have done just fine.
          </p>
          <p>
           Lets talk about the breaking of the fourth wall. Taylor uses it effectively at times as Boseman figuratively winks at the camera and transitions the story, however its used quite cheaply when James Brown pounds on his wifes and shyly looks at the camera as if to say did I do that? It expresses the idea of remorse rather than actually having the character show it. At one point, Aykroyds character tells Brown to say youre a ballplayer, right before Boseman turns to the camera. You know  because he played Jackie Robinson in
           <em>
            42
           </em>
           . Its all a bit cute and convenient.
          </p>
          <p>
           Fortunately,
           <em>
            Get On Up
           </em>
           takes a giant leap forward in the powerful second half when Boseman simply takes over. Its hard to comprehend the talent needed to pull of the stage performances and the complete persona of James Brown. Everybody can do a Godfather of Soul impression, however Boseman embodies the legend in speech, swagger and movement. The body and voice express confidence when Brown feels most alone in public, and the actor conveys the true loneliness in his eyes. The rat-a-tat speech is contrasted by small turns of the body that keep Bosemans James Brown engaged with those closest around him  but at a distance. A dressing room scene between Boseman and the astonishing
           <strong>
            Viola Davis
           </strong>
           (Browns mother) brings out the humanity of both, and alludes to why James Brown was James Brown. The supporting players of
           <strong>
            Octavia Spencer
           </strong>
           (long-time friend of director Taylor),
           <strong>
            Jill Scott
           </strong>
           and
           <strong>
            Nelson Ellis
           </strong>
           are superb in their own right and help unlock James Browns hidden persona.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Get On Up
           </em>
           is a must-watch solely because of its star Chadwick Boseman. The techniques of Tate Taylor are clunky at times, and the film is perhaps a bit too long, but the director does manage to bring all the pieces together and make the biopic stand on its own. With a little more structure, and a little less breaking of the fourth wall,
           <em>
            Get On Up
           </em>
           would have brought out the humanity of James Brown rather than the idea of him.
          </p>
