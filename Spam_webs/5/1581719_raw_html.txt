   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Ultrashort pulse
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">
       optics
      </a>
      , an
      <b>
       ultrashort pulse
      </b>
      of light is an
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_pulse" title="Electromagnetic pulse">
       electromagnetic pulse
      </a>
      whose time duration is of the order of a picosecond (10
      <sup>
       12
      </sup>
      second) or less. Such pulses have a broadband
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_spectrum" title="Optical spectrum">
       optical spectrum
      </a>
      , and can be created by
      <a href="/wiki/Mode-locking" title="Mode-locking">
       mode-locked
      </a>
      oscillators. They are commonly referred to as ultrafast events. Amplification of ultrashort pulses almost always requires the technique of
      <a href="/wiki/Chirped_pulse_amplification" title="Chirped pulse amplification">
       chirped pulse amplification
      </a>
      , in order to avoid damage to the gain medium of the amplifier.
     </p>
     <p>
      They are characterized by a high peak
      <a href="/wiki/Intensity_(physics)#Alternative_definitions_of_.22intensity.22" title="Intensity (physics)">
       intensity
      </a>
      (or more correctly,
      <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">
       irradiance
      </a>
      ) that usually leads to nonlinear interactions in various materials, including air. These processes are studied in the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_optics" title="Nonlinear optics">
       nonlinear optics
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the specialized literature, "ultrashort" refers to the
      <a href="/wiki/Femtosecond" title="Femtosecond">
       femtosecond
      </a>
      (fs) and
      <a href="/wiki/Picosecond" title="Picosecond">
       picosecond
      </a>
      (ps) range, although such pulses no longer hold the record for the shortest pulses artificially generated. Indeed, x-ray pulse durations on the
      <a href="/wiki/Attosecond" title="Attosecond">
       attosecond
      </a>
      time scale have been reported.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 1999
      <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">
       Nobel Prize in Chemistry
      </a>
      was awarded to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmed_H._Zewail" title="Ahmed H. Zewail">
       Ahmed H. Zewail
      </a>
      for using ultrashort pulses to observe
      <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">
       chemical reactions
      </a>
      on the timescales they occur on, opening up the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Femtochemistry" title="Femtochemistry">
       femtochemistry
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrashort_pulse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ultrashort_pulse.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="538" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Ultrashort_pulse.svg/400px-Ultrashort_pulse.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Ultrashort_pulse.svg/600px-Ultrashort_pulse.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Ultrashort_pulse.svg/800px-Ultrashort_pulse.svg.png 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ultrashort_pulse.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A positively chirped ultrashort pulse of light in the time domain.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      An ultrashort pulse is a
      <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">
       wave
      </a>
      whose
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Modulus" title="Modulus">
       field amplitude
      </a>
      follows a
      <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_function" title="Gaussian function">
       Gaussian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Envelope_(waves)" title="Envelope (waves)">
       envelope
      </a>
      and whose
      <a href="/wiki/Instantaneous_phase" title="Instantaneous phase">
       instantaneous phase
      </a>
      has a
      <a href="/wiki/Chirp" title="Chirp">
       frequency sweep
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrashort_pulse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-move" role="presentation">
      <tr>
       <td class="mbox-image">
        <div style="width:52px">
         <img alt="" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="40" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Splitsection.svg/40px-Splitsection.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Splitsection.svg/60px-Splitsection.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Splitsection.svg/80px-Splitsection.svg.png 2x" width="40"/>
        </div>
       </td>
       <td class="mbox-text">
        <span class="mbox-text-span">
         It has been suggested that this section be
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Splitting" title="Wikipedia:Splitting">
          split
         </a>
         into a new article titled
         <i>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_phase" title="Spectral phase">
           Spectral phase
          </a>
         </i>
         . (
         <a href="/wiki/Talk:Ultrashort_pulse" title="Talk:Ultrashort pulse">
          Discuss
         </a>
         )
         <small>
          <i>
           Proposed since September 2013.
          </i>
         </small>
        </span>
       </td>
      </tr>
     </table>
     <p>
      The real electric field corresponding to an ultrashort pulse is oscillating at an angular frequency
      <i>
       
      </i>
      <sub>
       0
      </sub>
      corresponding to the central wavelength of the pulse. To facilitate calculations, a complex field
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      ) is defined. Formally, it is defined as the
      <a href="/wiki/Analytic_signal" title="Analytic signal">
       analytic signal
      </a>
      corresponding to the real field.
     </p>
     <p>
      The central angular frequency
      <i>
       
      </i>
      <sub>
       0
      </sub>
      is usually explicitly written in the complex field, which may be separated as a temporal intensity function
      <i>
       I
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      ) and a temporal phase function
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      ):
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="E(t) = \sqrt{I(t)}e^{i\omega_0t}e^{i\psi(t)}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/d/1/cd10e88a93707e8d06483c4819ec0858.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The expression of the complex electric field in the frequency domain is obtained from the
      <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform" title="Fourier transform">
       Fourier transform
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      ):
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="E(\omega) = \mathcal{F}(E(t))" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/5/5/a55788fde74df2520f94fa0273c1d881.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Because of the presence of the
      <img alt="e^{i\omega_0t}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/9/09992ee0b59fbadfaa553bd41a9c8c9e.png"/>
      term,
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) is centered around
      <i>
       
      </i>
      <sub>
       0
      </sub>
      , and it is a common practice to refer to
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      -
      <i>
       
      </i>
      <sub>
       0
      </sub>
      ) by writing just
      <i>
       E
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ), which we will do in the rest of this article.
     </p>
     <p>
      Just as in the time domain, an intensity and a phase function can be defined in the frequency domain:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="E(\omega) = \sqrt{S(\omega)}e^{i\phi(\omega)}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/2/672373f7c26bdf54e3323cc0f0f2975e.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The quantity
      <i>
       S
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) is the
      <i>
       intensity spectral density
      </i>
      (or simply, the
      <i>
       spectrum
      </i>
      ) of the pulse, and
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) is the
      <i>
       phase spectral density
      </i>
      (or simply
      <i>
       spectral phase
      </i>
      ). Example of spectral phase functions include the case where
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) is a constant, in which case the pulse is called a
      <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth-limited_pulse" title="Bandwidth-limited pulse">
       bandwidth-limited pulse
      </a>
      , or where
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) is a quadratic function, in which case the pulse is called a
      <a href="/wiki/Chirp" title="Chirp">
       chirped
      </a>
      pulse because of the presence of an instantaneous frequency sweep. Such a chirp may be acquired as a pulse propagates through materials (like glass) and is due to their
      <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">
       dispersion
      </a>
      . It results in a temporal broadening of the pulse.
     </p>
     <p>
      The intensity functionstemporal
      <i>
       I
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       t
      </i>
      ) and spectral
      <i>
       S
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) -- determine the time duration and spectrum bandwidth of the pulse. As stated by the
      <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">
       uncertainty principle
      </a>
      , their product (sometimes called the time-bandwidth product) has a lower bound. This minimum value depends on the definition used for the duration and on the shape of the pulse. For a given spectrum, the minimum time-bandwidth product, and therefore the shortest pulse, is obtained by a transform-limited pulse, i.e., for a constant spectral phase
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ). High values of the time-bandwidth product, on the other hand, indicate a more complex pulse.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pulse_shape_control">
       Pulse shape control
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrashort_pulse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pulse shape control">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although optical devices also used for continuous light, like beam expanders and spatial filters, may be used for ultrashort pulses, several optical devices have been specifically designed for ultrashort pulses. One of them is the
      <a href="/wiki/Prism_compressor" title="Prism compressor">
       pulse compressor
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a device that can be used to control the spectral phase of ultrashort pulses. It is composed of a sequence of prisms, or gratings. When properly adjusted it can alter the spectral phase
      <i>
       
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       
      </i>
      ) of the input pulse so that the output pulse is a
      <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth-limited_pulse" title="Bandwidth-limited pulse">
       bandwidth-limited pulse
      </a>
      with the shortest possible duration. A
      <a href="/wiki/Femtosecond_pulse_shaping" title="Femtosecond pulse shaping">
       pulse shaper
      </a>
      can be used to make more complicated alterations on both the phase and the amplitude of ultrashort pulses.
     </p>
     <p>
      To accurately control the pulse, a full characterization of the pulse spectral phase is a must in order to get certain pulse spectral phase (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth-limited_pulse" title="Bandwidth-limited pulse">
       transform-limited
      </a>
      ). Then, a
      <a href="/wiki/Spatial_light_modulator" title="Spatial light modulator">
       spatial light modulator
      </a>
      can be used in the 4f plane to control the pulse.
      <a href="/wiki/Multiphoton_intrapulse_interference_phase_scan" title="Multiphoton intrapulse interference phase scan">
       Multiphoton intrapulse interference phase scan
      </a>
      (MIIPS) is a technique based on this concept. Through the phase scan of the spatial light modulator, MIIPS can not only characterize but also manipulate the ultrashort pulse to get the needed pulse shape at target spot (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth-limited_pulse" title="Bandwidth-limited pulse">
       transform-limited pulse
      </a>
      for optimized peak power, and other specific pulse shapes). This technique features with full calibration and control of the ultrashort pulse, with no moving parts, and simple optical setup.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_techniques">
       Measurement techniques
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrashort_pulse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measurement techniques">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Several techniques are available to measure ultrashort optical pulses:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       intensity
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_autocorrelation" title="Optical autocorrelation">
        autocorrelation
       </a>
       : gives the pulse width when a particular pulse shape is assumed.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_interferometry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spectral interferometry (page does not exist)">
        Spectral interferometry
       </a>
       (SI): a linear technique that can be used when a pre-characterized reference pulse is available. Gives the intensity and phase. The algorithm that extracts the intensity and phase from the SI signal is direct.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_phase_interferometry_for_direct_electric-field_reconstruction" title="Spectral phase interferometry for direct electric-field reconstruction">
        Spectral phase interferometry for direct electric-field reconstruction
       </a>
       (SPIDER): a nonlinear self-referencing technique based on spectral shearing interferometry. The method is similar to SI, except that the reference pulse is a spectrally shifted replica of itself, allowing one to obtain the spectral intensity and phase of the probe pulse via a direct
       <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">
        FFT
       </a>
       filtering routine similar to SI, but which requires integration of the phase extracted from the interferogram to obtain the probe pulse phase.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency-resolved_optical_gating" title="Frequency-resolved optical gating">
        Frequency-resolved optical gating
       </a>
       (FROG): a nonlinear technique that yields the intensity and phase of a pulse. It's just a spectrally resolved autocorrelation. The algorithm that extracts the intensity and phase from a FROG trace is iterative.
      </li>
      <li>
       Grating-eliminated no-nonsense observation of ultrafast incident laser light e-fields (
       <a href="/wiki/GRENOUILLE" title="GRENOUILLE">
        GRENOUILLE
       </a>
       ), a simplified version of FROG. (
       <i>
        Grenouille
       </i>
       is French for "
       <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">
        frog
       </a>
       ".)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Methods of characterizing and controlling the ultrashort optical pulses:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Multiphoton_intrapulse_interference_phase_scan" title="Multiphoton intrapulse interference phase scan">
        Multiphoton intrapulse interference phase scan
       </a>
       (MIIPS), a method to characterize and manipulate the ultrashort pulse.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_packet_propagation_in_nonisotropic_media">
       Wave packet propagation in nonisotropic media
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrashort_pulse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wave packet propagation in nonisotropic media">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      To partially reiterate the discussion above, the
      <a href="/wiki/Slowly_varying_envelope_approximation" title="Slowly varying envelope approximation">
       slowly varying envelope approximation
      </a>
      (SVEA) of the electric field of a wave with central wave vector
      <img alt=" \textbf{K}_0 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/2/f/52f2e77f344a2764fcde4bd4963c3b5e.png"/>
      and central frequency
      <img alt=" \omega_0 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/4/d/d4df4d2cdd8cdb93b8868b4d5c22bcc2.png"/>
      of the pulse, is given by:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="
\textbf{E} ( \textbf{x} , t) = \textbf{ A } ( \textbf{x} , t) \exp ( i \textbf{K}_0 \textbf{x} - i \omega_0 t )
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/2/1/021ed5b23c295b662857a47bcf499af8.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      We consider the propagation for the SVEA of the electric field in a homogeneous dispersive nonisotropic medium. Assuming the pulse is propagating in the direction of the z-axis, it can be shown that the envelope
      <img alt=" \textbf{A} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/f/1/5f1636690b55943f8e36fb3a864a2684.png"/>
      for one of the most general of cases, namely a biaxial crystal, is governed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equation" title="Partial differential equation">
       PDE
      </a>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="
\frac{\partial \textbf{A} }{\partial z } =
~-~ \beta_1 \frac{\partial \textbf{A} }{\partial t}
~-~ \frac{i}{2} \beta_2 \frac{\partial^2 \textbf{A} }{\partial t^2}
~+~ \frac{1}{6} \beta_3 \frac{\partial^3 \textbf{A} }{\partial t^3}
~+~ \gamma_x \frac{\partial \textbf{A} }{\partial x}
~+~ \gamma_y \frac{\partial \textbf{A} }{\partial y}
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/2/5/b254333f5593f988ef8a1653b0c72d03.png"/>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <img alt="
~~~~~~~~~~~
~+~ i \gamma_{tx} \frac{\partial^2 \textbf{A} }{\partial t \partial x}
~+~ i \gamma_{ty} \frac{\partial^2 \textbf{A} }{\partial t \partial y}
~-~ \frac{i}{2} \gamma_{xx} \frac{\partial^2 \textbf{A} }{ \partial x^2}
~-~ \frac{i}{2} \gamma_{yy} \frac{\partial^2 \textbf{A} }{ \partial y^2}
~+~ i \gamma_{xy} \frac{\partial^2 \textbf{A} }{ \partial x \partial y} + \cdots
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/0/4/404fc6101de54d7b23bbe83b4b1f0c6e.png"/>
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      where the coefficients contains diffraction and dispersion effects which have been determined analytically with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_algebra" title="Computer algebra">
       computer algebra
      </a>
      and verified numerically to within third order for both isotropic and non-isotropic media, valid in the near-field and far-field.
      <img alt=" \beta_1 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/f/2/9f26b68b727cdcf2c659189280f6ce55.png"/>
      is the inverse of the group velocity projection. The term in
      <img alt=" \beta_2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/1/6716d7289516889f04c804964a686d62.png"/>
      is the group velocity
      <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">
       dispersion
      </a>
      (GVD) or second-order dispersion; it increases the pulse duration and chirps the pulse as it propagates through the medium. The term in
      <img alt=" \beta_3 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/e/4/4e41100322890bbbfa1bc602a512753d.png"/>
      is a third-order dispersion term that can further increase the pulse duration, even if
      <img alt=" \beta_2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/1/6716d7289516889f04c804964a686d62.png"/>
      vanishes. The terms in
      <img alt=" \gamma_x " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/6/1/661599649d069b3434676a8732eaede0.png"/>
      and
      <img alt=" \gamma_y " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/d/2/4d232c084dd24fccf5308ba8961a880d.png"/>
      describe the walk-off of the pulse; the coefficient
      <img alt=" \gamma_x ~ (\gamma_y ) " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/3/1/531a0ac1bee2968009fca9ed6254438b.png"/>
      is the ratio of the component of the group velocity
      <img alt=" x ~ (y) " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/3/7a37ce47903becb8993df5498298d6c1.png"/>
      and the unit vector in the direction of propagation of the pulse (z-axis). The terms in
      <img alt="\gamma_{xx}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/2/8d211eb7b67b683c6c468bb46a64167c.png"/>
      and
      <img alt=" \gamma_{yy} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/4/d/54d568142b5c02367bc2a23ecbe55f35.png"/>
      describe diffraction of the optical wave packet in the directions perpendicular to the axis of propagation. The terms in
      <img alt=" \gamma_{tx} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/e/0/2e05589191442a8ceb0c20298642ac6c.png"/>
      and
      <img alt=" \gamma_{ty} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/d/a/6da30ca7443ca9389da25c5f14b3f4c9.png"/>
      containing mixed derivatives in time and space rotate the wave packet about the
      <img alt="y" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/5/415290769594460e2e485922904f345d.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      axes, respectively, increase the temporal width of the wave packet (in addition to the increase due to the GVD), increase the dispersion in the
      <img alt="x" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      and
      <img alt="y" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/5/415290769594460e2e485922904f345d.png"/>
      directions, respectively, and increase the chirp (in addition to that due to
      <img alt=" \beta_2 " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/7/1/6716d7289516889f04c804964a686d62.png"/>
      ) when the latter and/or
      <img alt=" \gamma_{xx} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/2/8d211eb7b67b683c6c468bb46a64167c.png"/>
      and
      <img alt=" \gamma_{yy} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/4/d/54d568142b5c02367bc2a23ecbe55f35.png"/>
      are nonvanishing. The term
      <img alt=" \gamma_{xy} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/9/2/892d7cc696a0a9b78a5d646dedd6b9bd.png"/>
      rotates the wave packet in the
      <img alt=" x-y " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/9/d/19d59c7d61b117f2865b20b59c8b70f7.png"/>
      plane. Oddly enough, because of previously incomplete expansions, this rotation of the pulse was not realized until the late 1990s but it has been
      <i>
       experimentally
      </i>
      confirmed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To third order, the RHS of the above equation is found to have these additional terms for the uniaxial crystal case:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <img alt="
\cdots
~+~ \frac{1}{3} \gamma_{t x x } \frac{\partial^3 \textbf{A} }{ \partial x^2 \partial t}
~+~ \frac{1}{3} \gamma_{t y y } \frac{\partial^3 \textbf{A} }{ \partial y^2 \partial t}
~+~ \frac{1}{3} \gamma_{t t x } \frac{\partial^3 \textbf{A} }{ \partial t^2 \partial x} + \cdots
" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/4/f/b4f9c244b2fe1a3091d7bde89cc70fe3.png"/>
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The first and second terms are responsible for the curvature of the propagating front of the pulse. These terms, including the term in
      <img alt="\beta_3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/e/4/4e41100322890bbbfa1bc602a512753d.png"/>
      are present in an isotropic medium and account for the spherical surface of a propagating front originating from a point source. The term
      <img alt=" \gamma_{txx} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/4/4/8446285880920249ce9705b89c4aef11.png"/>
      can be expressed in terms of the index of refraction, the frequency
      <img alt=" \omega " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/d/1/4d1b7b74aba3cfabd624e898d86b4602.png"/>
      and derivatives thereof and the term
      <img alt=" \gamma_{ttx} " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/7/2/572ece8248b7e18fb182bfddfb7b3059.png"/>
      also distorts the pulse but in a fashion that reverses the roles of
      <img alt=" t " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png"/>
      and
      <img alt=" x " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png"/>
      (see reference of Trippenbach, Scott and Band for details). So far, the treatment herein is linear, but nonlinear dispersive terms are ubiquitous to nature. Studies involving an additional nonlinear term
      <img alt=" \gamma_{nl} |A|^2 A " class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/7/1/4718e45837d624627f2b6d328ca32394.png"/>
      have shown that such terms have a profound effect on wave packet, including amongst other things, a
      <i>
       self-steepening
      </i>
      of the wave packet.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The non-linear aspects eventually lead to
      <a href="/wiki/Soliton_(optics)" title="Soliton (optics)">
       optical solitons
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Despite being rather common, the SVEA is not required to formulate a simple wave equation describing the propagation of optical pulses. In fact, as shown in,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kinsler2010_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kinsler2010-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      even a very general form of the electromagnetic second order wave equation can be factorized into directional components, providing access to a single first order wave equation for the field itself, rather than an envelope. This requires only an assumption that the field evolution is slow on the scale of a wavelength, and does not restrict the bandwidth of the pulse at allas demonstrated vividly by.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genty2007_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-genty2007-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="High_harmonics">
       High harmonics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrashort_pulse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: High harmonics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      High energy ultrashort pulses can be generated through
      <a href="/wiki/High_harmonic_generation" title="High harmonic generation">
       high harmonic generation
      </a>
      in a
      <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_optics" title="Nonlinear optics">
       nonlinear medium
      </a>
      . A high intensity ultrashort pulse will generate an array of
      <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">
       harmonics
      </a>
      in the medium; a particular harmonic of interest is then selected with a
      <a href="/wiki/Monochromator" title="Monochromator">
       monochromator
      </a>
      . This technique has been used to produce ultrashort pulses in the
      <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
       extreme ultraviolet
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soft-X-ray&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soft-X-ray (page does not exist)">
       soft-X-ray
      </a>
      regimes from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_infrared" title="Near infrared">
       near infrared
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Ti-sapphire_laser" title="Ti-sapphire laser">
       Ti-sapphire laser
      </a>
      pulses.
     </p>
