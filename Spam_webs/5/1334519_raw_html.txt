     <p>
      <b>
       Crossing the T
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       Capping the T
      </b>
      is a classic
      <a href="/wiki/Naval_warfare" title="Naval warfare">
       naval warfare
      </a>
      tactic attempted from the late 19th to mid 20th century, in which a line of
      <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">
       warships
      </a>
      crossed in front of a line of enemy ships, allowing the crossing line to bring all their guns to bear while receiving fire from only the forward guns of the enemy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It became possible to bring
      <i>
       all
      </i>
      of a ship's main guns to bear only in the late 19th and the early 20th centuries with the advent of steam-powered battleships with rotating gun turrets, which were able to move faster and turn quicker than sailing ships, which had fixed guns facing sideways. The tactic became largely obsolete with the introduction of
      <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">
       missiles
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">
       aircraft
      </a>
      as long-range strikes are less dependent on the direction the ships are facing.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tactics">
       Tactics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crossing_the_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tactics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      When going into battle, ships would assume a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_line" title="Battle line">
       battle line
      </a>
      formation called "line astern", in which one vessel followed another in one or more parallel lines. This allowed each ship to fire over wide arcs without lofting
      <a href="/wiki/Salvo" title="Salvo">
       salvos
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Shell_(projectile)" title="Shell (projectile)">
       projectiles
      </a>
      over friendly vessels. Each ship in the line generally engaged its opposite number in the enemy battle line which moved in a
      <a href="/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)" title="Parallel (geometry)">
       parallel
      </a>
      course.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, moving ahead of the enemy line on a
      <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
       perpendicular
      </a>
      course (
      <b>
       crossing the
       <i>
        T
       </i>
      </b>
      ) enabled a ship to launch salvoes at the same target with both the forward and rear turrets, maximizing the chances for a hit. It also made ranging error less critical for the ship doing the crossing, while simultaneously more critical for the ship being crossed. In military terms, this is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Enfilade_and_defilade" title="Enfilade and defilade">
       enfilade
      </a>
      fire. The tactic, designed for heavily armed and armoured battleships, was used with varying degrees of success with more lightly armed and armoured
      <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">
       cruisers
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cruiser" title="Heavy cruiser">
       heavy cruisers
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Advances in gun manufacture and
      <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">
       fire-control systems
      </a>
      allowed engagements at increasingly long range, from approximately 6,000 yards (5.5km) at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tsushima" title="Battle of Tsushima">
       Battle of Tsushima
      </a>
      in 1905 to 20,000 yards (18km) at the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jutland" title="Battle of Jutland">
       Battle of Jutland
      </a>
      in 1916. The introduction of
      <a href="/wiki/Brown_powder" title="Brown powder">
       brown powder
      </a>
      , which burned less rapidly than
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">
       black powder
      </a>
      , allowed longer barrels, which allowed greater accuracy; and because it expanded less sharply than black powder, it put less strain on the insides of the barrel, allowing guns to last longer and to be manufactured to tighter tolerances. The addition of
      <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
       radar
      </a>
      allowed World War II ships to fire further, more accurately, and at night.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Battles">
       Battles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crossing_the_T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Battles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Notable battles in which warships crossed the T include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tsushima" title="Battle of Tsushima">
        Battle of Tsushima
       </a>
       (1905)  Japanese
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_Togo" title="Admiral Togo">
        Admiral Togo
       </a>
       , by use of
       <a href="/wiki/Wireless" title="Wireless">
        wireless
       </a>
       communications and the proper deployment of
       <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">
        reconnaissance
       </a>
       had positioned his fleet in such a way as to bring the
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russian
       </a>
       fleet to battle, "irrespective of speeds."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Togo had preserved for himself the interior lines of movement, while forcing the longer lines of movement upon his opponent, whichever course the Russian admiral should take; and by his selected positioning had the effect of "throwing the Russian
       <a href="/wiki/Broadside" title="Broadside">
        broadsides
       </a>
       more and more out of action."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       "He had headed him"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       (crossed his T). The Russian admiral, other than retreat or surrender, had no other option(s) other than "charging Togo's battle line" or "accepting a formal pitched battle."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Admiral
       <a href="/wiki/Zinovy_Rozhestvensky" title="Zinovy Rozhestvensky">
        Zinovy Rozhestvensky
       </a>
       chose the latter, resulting in his total defeat in naval history's only decisive fleet action fought solely by modern battleships. Rozhestvensky was severely wounded during the battle, and was taken prisoner. Seven battleships were sunk and one was captured by the Japanese.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Battle_of_Elli" title="Naval Battle of Elli">
        Battle of Elli
       </a>
       (1912) 
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral" title="Rear Admiral">
        Rear Admiral
       </a>
       Pavlos Kountouriotis aboard the
       <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
        Greek
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">
        cruiser
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_cruiser_Georgios_Averof" title="Greek cruiser Georgios Averof">
        <i>
         Georgios Averof
        </i>
       </a>
       at a speed of 20 knots crossed the T of the
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkish
       </a>
       fleet on December 13, 1912. The Averof concentrated her fire against the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman
       </a>
       flagship, forcing the Turks to retreat.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jutland" title="Battle of Jutland">
        Battle of Jutland
       </a>
       (1916)  Admiral
       <a href="/wiki/John_Jellicoe,_1st_Earl_Jellicoe" title="John Jellicoe, 1st Earl Jellicoe">
        Sir John Jellicoe
       </a>
       , leader of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Grand_Fleet" title="British Grand Fleet">
        British Grand Fleet
       </a>
       , was able to cross the T twice against the German
       <a href="/wiki/High_Seas_Fleet" title="High Seas Fleet">
        High Seas Fleets
       </a>
       , but the German Fleet was both times able to escape by reversing course in poor visibility. Although the High Seas Fleet was thereby rendered strategically impotent, being unwilling to face the Grand Fleet again, the British were unable to gain the crushing "Second
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">
        Trafalgar
       </a>
       " they had desired.
       <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">
        Jutland
       </a>
       is sometimes referred to as the
       <i>
        Battle of Lost Opportunities.
       </i>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_Esperance" title="Battle of Cape Esperance">
        Battle of Cape Esperance
       </a>
       (1942)  First
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       (U.S.) naval night battle victory over the Japanese when a U.S. force of
       <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">
        cruisers
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">
        destroyers
       </a>
       under
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Scott_(admiral)" title="Norman Scott (admiral)">
        Norman Scott
       </a>
       crossed the T of a cruiserdestroyer force under
       <a href="/wiki/Aritomo_Got%C5%8D" title="Aritomo Got">
        Aritomo Got
       </a>
       . Got's force was approaching
       <a href="/wiki/Guadalcanal" title="Guadalcanal">
        Guadalcanal
       </a>
       on October 11, 1942 to bombard
       <a href="/wiki/Henderson_Field_(Guadalcanal)" title="Henderson Field (Guadalcanal)">
        Henderson Field
       </a>
       in support of a
       <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Express" title="Tokyo Express">
        Tokyo Express
       </a>
       reinforcement mission when it was surprised and defeated by Scott's force in a confused night battle. Goto died of his wounds shortly after the battle, and lost the cruiser
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Furutaka" title="Japanese cruiser Furutaka">
        <i>
         Furutaka
        </i>
       </a>
       along with three destroyers.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leyte_Gulf#The_Battle_of_Surigao_Strait_.2825_October.29" title="Battle of Leyte Gulf">
        Battle of Surigao Strait
       </a>
       (1944)  The last time a battle line crossed the T, this engagement took place during the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leyte_Gulf" title="Battle of Leyte Gulf">
        Battle of Leyte Gulf
       </a>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       . Early on October 25, 1944, Rear Admiral
       <a href="/wiki/Jesse_B._Oldendorf" title="Jesse B. Oldendorf">
        Jesse B. Oldendorf
       </a>
       was guarding the southern entrance to the
       <a href="/wiki/Leyte_Gulf" title="Leyte Gulf">
        Leyte Gulf
       </a>
       at the northern end of
       <a href="/wiki/Surigao_Strait" title="Surigao Strait">
        Surigao Strait
       </a>
       . He commanded a line of six battleships (
       <a href="/wiki/USS_West_Virginia_(BB-48)" title="USS West Virginia (BB-48)">
        <i>
         West Virginia
        </i>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/USS_Tennessee_(BB-43)" title="USS Tennessee (BB-43)">
        <i>
         Tennessee
        </i>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/USS_California_(BB-44)" title="USS California (BB-44)">
        <i>
         California
        </i>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/USS_Maryland_(BB-46)" title="USS Maryland (BB-46)">
        <i>
         Maryland
        </i>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/USS_Pennsylvania_(BB-38)" title="USS Pennsylvania (BB-38)">
        <i>
         Pennsylvania
        </i>
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/USS_Mississippi_(BB-41)" title="USS Mississippi (BB-41)">
        <i>
         Mississippi
        </i>
       </a>
       ), flanked by numerous heavy and light cruisers. A smaller
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Navy" title="Imperial Japanese Navy">
        Japanese
       </a>
       force under
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_Admiral" title="Vice Admiral">
        Vice Admiral
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoji_Nishimura" title="Shoji Nishimura">
        Shoji Nishimura
       </a>
       came up the strait, aware of the formidable strength of the American force but nonetheless pressing on. Half of Nishimura's fleet was eliminated by the Americans' destroyer torpedoes, but the Japanese admiral continued on with his remaining few ships. Oldendorf's battleships were arrayed in a line, and they unleashed their radar-directed fire-power upon Japanese vessels, whose return fire was ineffectual due to the lack of radar fire control and earlier battle damage. Nishimura went down with his ship. Despite this however,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_battleship_Yamashiro" title="Japanese battleship Yamashiro">
        <i>
         Yamashiro
        </i>
       </a>
       's guns, along with cruiser
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cruiser_Mogami" title="Japanese cruiser Mogami">
        <i>
         Mogami
        </i>
       </a>
       managed to severely damage an American destroyer. This was the last time the 'T' was crossed in an engagement between battleships, and the last occasion on which battleships fought each other.
      </li>
     </ul>
