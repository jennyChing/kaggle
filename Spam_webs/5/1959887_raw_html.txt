     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Going Clear: Scientology and the Prison of Belief
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2015
      <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">
       documentary film
      </a>
      about
      <a href="/wiki/Scientology" title="Scientology">
       Scientology
      </a>
      . Directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Alex_Gibney" title="Alex Gibney">
       Alex Gibney
      </a>
      , it is based on
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Wright" title="Lawrence Wright">
       Lawrence Wright
      </a>
      's book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Going_Clear" title="Going Clear">
        Going Clear: Scientology, Hollywood and the Prison of Belief
       </a>
      </i>
      (2013). Produced by
      <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">
       HBO
      </a>
      , the film premiered at the 2015
      <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">
       Sundance Film Festival
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Park_City,_Utah" title="Park City, Utah">
       Park City, Utah
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The film received widespread praise from critics for its deconstruction of the church's claims through a combination of presenting a condensed history of Scientology and its founder,
      <a href="/wiki/L._Ron_Hubbard" title="L. Ron Hubbard">
       L. Ron Hubbard
      </a>
      , how celebrities interact with the church, the stories of a number of ex-members, and the abuse and exploitation the ex-members described seeing and experiencing. The
      <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scientology" title="Church of Scientology">
       Church of Scientology
      </a>
      responded vehemently to the film, complaining to film critics about their reviews and denouncing the filmmakers and their interviewees.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      was released in a limited number of theaters on March 13, 2015 and aired on HBO on March 29, 2015. It was a major ratings success and by mid-April 2015 had attracted 5.5 million viewers, making it the second most-watched HBO documentary in the past decade. It was subsequently released internationally, showing in theaters and on television despite a sustained campaign by the Church of Scientology to block its release.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">
       Synopsis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Going_Clear_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      is based closely on Lawrence Wright's book, covering much of the same ground with the aid of archive footage, dramatic reconstructions, and interviews with eight former Scientologists:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ-26-Jan-2015_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSJ-26-Jan-2015-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-15-Jan-2015_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-15-Jan-2015-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Haggis" title="Paul Haggis">
       Paul Haggis
      </a>
      , the Oscar-winning director;
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rathbun" title="Mark Rathbun">
       Mark Rathbun
      </a>
      , the church's former second-in-command;
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Rinder" title="Mike Rinder">
       Mike Rinder
      </a>
      , the former head of the church's
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Special_Affairs" title="Office of Special Affairs">
       Office of Special Affairs
      </a>
      ; the actor
      <a href="/wiki/Jason_Beghe" title="Jason Beghe">
       Jason Beghe
      </a>
      ; Sylvia 'Spanky' Taylor, former liaison to
      <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">
       John Travolta
      </a>
      ; and former Scientologists Tom DeVocht, Sara Goldberg, and Hana Eltringham Whitfield.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScreenDaily-28-Jan-2015_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ScreenDaily-28-Jan-2015-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The film breaks into three distinct acts. In the first, the former Scientologists describe how they got into Scientology; the second act recounts the history of Scientology and its founder
      <a href="/wiki/L._Ron_Hubbard" title="L. Ron Hubbard">
       L. Ron Hubbard
      </a>
      , and in the final act, the film airs allegations of the abuse of church members and misconduct by its leadership, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/David_Miscavige" title="David Miscavige">
       David Miscavige
      </a>
      , who is accused of intimidating, beating, imprisoning, and exploiting subordinates. It highlights the role played by celebrity members, such as
      <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">
       John Travolta
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cruise" title="Tom Cruise">
       Tom Cruise
      </a>
      , through the use of video clips contrasting their statements on Scientology with the experiences of former Scientologists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-26-Jan-2015_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guardian-26-Jan-2015-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      To support its thesis, the film utilises footage of ex-Scientologists being harassed and surveilled (as per Hubbard's dictum that the church's critics were all criminals whose crimes needed to be exposed),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and describes the imprisonment of senior Scientology executives in a facility known as "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Hole_(Scientology)" title="The Hole (Scientology)">
       The Hole
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variety-26-Jan-2015_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Variety-26-Jan-2015-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One Scientologist was said to have been forced to clean a bathroom with his tongue.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-26-Jan-2015_7-0">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Going_Clear_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3425" data-file-width="2106" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG/170px-Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG/255px-Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG/340px-Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alex_Gibney_2011_Shankbone.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Alex Gibney, director of
        <i>
         Going Clear
        </i>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The film is based on the
      <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">
       Pulitzer Prize
      </a>
      winning journalist Lawrence Wright's book
      <i>
       Going Clear: Scientology, Hollywood, and the Prison of Belief
      </i>
      , which was published in January 2013 and was a
      <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">
       National Book Award
      </a>
      finalist. HBO announced in December 2014 that Alex Gibney, an Oscar-winning director who made
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Taxi_to_the_Dark_Side" title="Taxi to the Dark Side">
        Taxi to the Dark Side
       </a>
      </i>
      (2007) and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Armstrong_Lie" title="The Armstrong Lie">
        The Armstrong Lie
       </a>
      </i>
      (2013), was directing a film based on the book, to be released at the 2015 Sundance Festival. It was the first time that HBO had tackled Scientology directly, though not the first time it had clashed with the church, which had mounted demonstrations outside HBO's headquarters after a 1998 documentary presented in a positive light anti-depressant drugs  fiercely opposed by Scientology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPost-1-Dec-2014_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-HuffPost-1-Dec-2014-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gibney began working on
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      in 2013 after becoming intrigued by Wright's book. He collaborated with Wright, who came on board as a producer, to explore the book's underlying theme of "how people become prisoners of faith in various ways".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-15-Jan-2015_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-15-Jan-2015-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gibney saw Scientology as one of the toughest subjects he has had to tackle in his career as a documentarian, alongside government complicity in torture, corporate financial malfeasance, and clerical sexual abuse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-15-Jan-2015_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-15-Jan-2015-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Fear of Scientology's litigiousness rendered American networks unwilling to license any material to the filmmakers, which Gibney found "astounding".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vulture-30-Jan-2015_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Vulture-30-Jan-2015-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He commented that he "found it interesting that universally this subject  more than any other  provoked all the networks to decline to license. I think at the end of the day, that tells you more about Scientology than it does about the networks, which is how ruthless they've been in trying to silence any criticism."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trinh_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trinh-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The church's reputation for harassing its critics made it necessary for Gibney to use
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burner_phone" title="Burner phone">
       burner phones
      </a>
      to contact interviewees and film in secret: "Sometimes for the on-camera interviews we'd set up gear in somebody's house and I'd make sure I'd be there hours before. Then the person would show up there so it was like they were just going to somebodys house."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trinh_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trinh-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Explaining why he chose to make a film about Scientology, Gibney told Reuters that he considered it "an important topic. Not only about this church of Scientology, which everybody's fascinated with partially because of the celebrities, but partially because of the way that the church seems to turn people to do things that I think they would normally never do if had they not entered the church."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost-28-Jan-2015_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WashPost-28-Jan-2015-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gibney, Wright, and the former Scientologists who appeared in the film told a post-screening question-and-answer session that they hoped the film would raise public awareness about the alleged abuses committed by the Church of Scientology, and would prompt the media and law enforcement agencies to investigate further.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ-26-Jan-2015_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSJ-26-Jan-2015-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gibney later called in a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">
        Los Angeles Times
       </a>
      </i>
      opinion piece for Scientology's tax exemption to be revoked in the light of the allegations of abuse documented in the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="International">
       International
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Going_Clear_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International">

       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The film has sold worldwide but is scheduled only for non-theatrical release in most countries. The Church of Scientology undertook an intensive campaign to block its release internationally. According to Alex Gibney, "Every step of the way, every distributor, every festival has received multiple threatening letters from the Church of Scientology. Some have come very close to buckling." The
      <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Film_Festival" title="Sydney Film Festival">
       Sydney Film Festival
      </a>
      was among those threatened but the screening of
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      went ahead; Gibney declared himself to be "delighted with the way the Australians handled it."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hollywood-7-Jul-2015_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hollywood-7-Jul-2015-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, Australian airline
      <a href="/wiki/Qantas" title="Qantas">
       Qantas
      </a>
      , which employs Scientologist
      <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">
       John Travolta
      </a>
      as an ambassador, was reported to have refused to show the film on its aircraft.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The UK satellite channel
      <a href="/wiki/Sky_Atlantic" title="Sky Atlantic">
       Sky Atlantic
      </a>
      is a co-distributor of the film, along with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HBO_Documentary_Films" title="HBO Documentary Films">
       HBO Documentary Films
      </a>
      , and originally planned to broadcast
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      soon after its US TV premire. However, this was stalled due to potential legal problems. Because
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
       Northern Ireland
      </a>
      is not subject to the
      <a href="/wiki/Defamation_Act_2013" title="Defamation Act 2013">
       Defamation Act 2013
      </a>
      , which reformed the libel laws in other parts of the UK, and because Sky cannot differentiate its signal between regions, the film may be subject to legal challenge in Northern Ireland. The Church of Scientology successfully blocked the publication or distribution of the original book
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      in the UK and indicated a willingness to sue if the film is broadcast, saying in a statement that it "will be entitled to seek the protection of both UK and Irish libel laws in the event that any false or defamatory content in this film is broadcast within these jurisdictions." Sky said that it had delayed rather than cancelled the broadcast and, "at present, Skys transmission date for Going Clear has not been confirmed."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

        <span>
         [
        </span>

      </i>
      five stars, describing it as having "the scary intensity of a thriller."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following the initial HBO broadcast of
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">
        Saturday Night Live
       </a>
      </i>
      aired a music video featuring the "Church of Neurotology", a parody of Scientology's 1990 music video "We Stand Tall", clips from which were shown in the documentary.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientology_reaction">
       Scientology reaction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Going_Clear_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scientology reaction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles,_Fountain_Avenue.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="714" data-file-width="1000" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles%2C_Fountain_Avenue.jpg/220px-Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles%2C_Fountain_Avenue.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles%2C_Fountain_Avenue.jpg/330px-Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles%2C_Fountain_Avenue.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles%2C_Fountain_Avenue.jpg/440px-Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles%2C_Fountain_Avenue.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Church_of_Scientology_building_in_Los_Angeles,_Fountain_Avenue.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Church of Scientology stood in strong opposition to the film.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Ten days before the film's premiere, the Church of Scientology took out full-page advertisements in
      <i>
       The New York Times
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">
        Los Angeles Times
       </a>
      </i>
      to denounce
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-15-Jan-2015_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT-15-Jan-2015-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      comparing it to the story "
      <a href="/wiki/A_Rape_on_Campus" title="A Rape on Campus">
       A Rape on Campus
      </a>
      " published by
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW-28-1-2015_31-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EW-28-1-2015-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gibney subsequently said that he was grateful for the church's advertising, as it had attracted much publicity for the film; he only wished "they'd put in showtimes".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The head of HBO Documentary Films, Sheila Nevins, commented that when she saw the adverts she knew that
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      would be a big success: "Docs dont get full page ads, and when they do, they do really well."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guerassio_32-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guerassio-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The church also published a "special report" attacking the film on one of its websites, started a new
      <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">
       Twitter
      </a>
      account which claimed to be "taking a resolute stand against the broadcasting and publishing of false information"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EW-28-1-2015_31-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EW-28-1-2015-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and bought numerous Google search results relating to the film in order to direct searchers to its anti-
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      pages.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-Feb-3-2015_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-Feb-3-2015-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, the church posted a series of short films on its website attacking the filmmakers and their interviewees, with titles such as "Alex Gibney Documentary 'Going Clear' Propaganda", "Marty Rathbun: A Violent Psychopath," "Mike Rinder: The Wife Beater," and "Sara Goldberg: The Home Wrecker."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trinh_12-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trinh-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The church complained that Gibney had declined to interview 25 of its members whom it had put forward to him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-26-Jan-2015_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-AP-26-Jan-2015-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Gibney, Miscavige, Travolta, and Cruise all declined interviews and the church instead offered "a delegation of 25 unidentified individuals, presumably to smear the people in our film", which did not interest him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The church also denounced the film's interviewees as "the usual collection of obsessive, disgruntled former Church members kicked out as long as 30 years ago for malfeasance, who have a documented history of making up lies about the Church for money".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ-26-Jan-2015_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-WSJ-26-Jan-2015-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Two of those interviewed in the film, journalist
      <a href="/wiki/Tony_Ortega_(journalist)" title="Tony Ortega (journalist)">
       Tony Ortega
      </a>
      and former Scientologist Marc Headley, reported that investigators from the church had surveilled them at Salt Lake City airport as they made their way to the festival.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Gibney, the church mounted an "organized" and "brutal" response to the appearance of its former members in the film: "Some of them have had physical threats, people threatening to take their homes away, private investigators following them. That's the part that's really heartbreaking."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In March 2015, a New York private investigator named Eric Saldarriaga pled guilty to the federal charge of conspiracy to commit computer hacking after he illegally gained access to at least 60 email accounts on behalf of undisclosed clients.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among those targeted were Mike Rinder and Tony Ortega,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      both interviewees in the film.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A few weeks after the film's TV premire, Paul Haggis reported that a suspected Scientology spy posing as a reporter for
      <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">
       <i>
        Time
       </i>
       magazine
      </a>
      had attempted to interview him in a possible attempt to obtain material to use against him. (The Church denied the claim.)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The film-makers reported receiving "lots of cards and letters" from the church, though in their case it had limited its response to "loads of legal paperwork".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015_5-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-26-Jan-2015-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      HBO had earlier said that it had put "probably 160 lawyers" onto the task of reviewing the film in anticipation of challenges from the notoriously litigious church.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPost-1-Dec-2014_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-HuffPost-1-Dec-2014-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The church contacted film critics complaining that their reviews of
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      were "filled with bald faced lies" and demanding the critics to publish a church statement. Jason Bailey of Flavorwire wrote that "pretty much every critic who wrote about
      <i>
       Going Clear
      </i>
      " received an email from Scientology spokesperson Karin Pouw. He commented that the church did not seem to realise that film critics do not usually try to interview people for reviews, and noted how neatly it "comports with the film's portrayal of the Church as a hive of shady, paranoid control freaks."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-Feb-3-2015_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-Feb-3-2015-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an email to Flavorwire, Gibney observed that "anytime someone writes something  film criticism or social criticism  about Scientology, the Church of Scientology counter-attacks by smearing critics."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Indiewire" title="Indiewire">
       Indiewire
      </a>
      's Max O'Connell criticised the church's approach for being counterproductive. He predicted that "their campaign against the film is going to be the best publicity that Alex Gibney and company could ever hope for, if also a hassle for critics and filmmakers and (this is no small thing) a nightmare for the ex-CoS members who dared to speak out against the Church's practices. But then, they don't seem terribly aware that attacking everyone who criticizes you doesn't do a lot of good for your image."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-Feb-3-2015_33-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-Feb-3-2015-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sheila Nevins of HBO commented that she could not believe how aggressive the church had been. Not only did it effectively provide free advertising for the film, but its hostility had made HBO Documentary Films even more determined to produce the film: "I thought, 'They really don't want us to do it. All the reason more to do it.'"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guerassio_32-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Guerassio-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
