down for a big chunk of the trading day. Erickson says that over time, these devices \u201chave become more and more complex, and you have more and more of them, they\u2019re individually more and more complex, and there\u2019s continually new software demands being placed on them.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EErickson explains that many companies don\u2019t realize that major chunks of their company and their operating systems are \u201cjust a couple of software misconfigurations away from being turned off or unavailable.\u201d And it\u2019s not just a time-and-money quandary when things go down. Hunsader has been in software development for the financial industry since 1986, and Nanex processes market data of stocks, options, futures and everything trading in the U.S. and worldwide. He explains that problems present from the start can take time to make themselves known. \u201cAs your product matures, after a while the only bugs left are the ones that nobody foresaw, and they tend to be the real difficult ones to figure out. So if technology is more complex with fewer errors, the few errors are significant.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EFor the NYSE, shutdowns aren\u2019t necessarily a problem, but bad timing can create a nightmare. \u201cThe nightmare is it happening one second before close,\u201d says Hunsader. \u201cThe best time for something to cause trouble is 15:59:59. The problem is that so much of the system depends on those closing prices. You would back up everything.\u201d He says that trades and options would have to be rolled back, and an error in the last few minutes of the day could cause the market not to open the next morning.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe good news is that things like the stock market, which are frequently affected by panic, aren\u2019t affected by network issues most of the time. Hunsader says it doesn\u2019t have any psychological impact on the market. If it had been an attack, well, \u201cI think it would be all the difference, because we\u2019d all be thinking if they can take a server out, maybe they can do it again. Or even worse, to be able to do it without being detected or... the smart thing would have been to rake the system for money.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAirlines are a little more sensitive: a down network means down planes, which can ruin everyone\u2019s holiday weekends and cost millions in a matter of hours. But these problems aren\u2019t so simple as someone kicking a power cord out of the back of a Netgear product.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Ch2\u003ETesting, Testing...\u003C\/h2\u003E\u003Cp\u003ERight now there\u2019s not really a technology in place that lets network experts test configurations in a vacuum. Erickson says changes are planned ahead of time, with group consensus being the only reliable estimate of what\u2019s going to happen. Once it goes live, testing new system arrangements (like with the NYSE or United) is a race against time. \u201cIf you mis-configure a device that happens to be the core device at that moment in time, it doesn\u2019t matter how much redundancy you have.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EThe lack of a standardized language or set of practices means that experts are in demand. Erickson says to be a network\u2019s perfect manager, \u201cyou\u2019d need to be able to understand all of the devices you\u2019ve got in your network. There are tons of vendors, hundreds if not thousands of devices.\u201d\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EIs there someone trying to standardize these tools? Not really. competitors have no impetus to collaborate when they can earn more from beating one another on innovation. But the reality of the market is that companies aren\u2019t often replacing every unit every time there\u2019s an upgrade, so legacy systems and legacy software will always be a problem.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EErickson says that in the absence of customers saying, \u201cwe need some sort of unifying standard here so we can have confidence our network is doing what we expect it to do all the time,\u201d then it\u2019s just not going to happen.\u003C\/p\u003E\u003Cp\u003EAnd even if there was desire, it\u2019s a huge undertaking. \u201cFor a company to solve              <h1>
              New Hormone Gel Could Regenerate Teeth in Place, Making Drilled Cavities Obsolete
             </h1>
             <div class="panel-pane pane-byline">
              <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
              <span class="author">
               <span class="label">
                By
               </span>
               <a datatype="" href="/popsci-authors/rebecca-boyle" property="prefLabel" typeof="Concept">
                Rebecca Boyle
               </a>
              </span>
              <span class="date" data-timestamp="1277837829">
               <span class="label">
                posted
               </span>
               Jun 29th, 2010 at 2:57pm
              </span>
              <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <!--&lt;/div&gt;-->
          </div>
          <div class="panel-pane pane-block pane-post-social-post-social-block">
           <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
           <div class="postButtons" style="float:left;">
           </div>
           <a class="customIcon pw-widget-customIcon" href="#" name="customIcon">
            <img alt="" data-src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/all/modules/bonnier/post_social/theme/post_icon_24.png" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
            <noscript>
             <img alt="" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/all/modules/bonnier/post_social/theme/post_icon_24.png" typeof="foaf:Image"/>
            </noscript>
           </a>
           <span class="pw-counter-custom1">

         <article class="content-basic basic-sidebar">
          <div class="content-main basic-sidebar">
           <div class="panel-pane pane-entity-field pane-node-body">
            <!--&lt;div class="pane-content"&gt;-->
            <div class="field-body">
             <p>
             </p>
             <div class="media media-element-container media-default full-width">
              <img alt="" class="full-width" data-lgsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/large_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2010/06/acs%20tooth.gif?itok=Z9sEzaXf" data-medsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2010/06/acs%20tooth.gif?itok=eNJ_7Mhw" data-smsrc="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/small_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2010/06/acs%20tooth.gif?itok=NgQR6T38" height="450" src="/sites/all/modules/bonnier/sandcastle/images/placeholder.gif" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="1000"/>
              <noscript>
               <img alt="" class="full-width" height="270" src="http://www.popsci.com/sites/popsci.com/files/styles/medium_1x_/public/import/2013/images/2010/06/acs%20tooth.gif?itok=eNJ_7Mhw" style="" typeof="foaf:Image" width="600"/>
              </noscript>
              <div class="field-title">
               <p>
                Nano-tooth
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-credit">
               <p>
                ACS Nano
               </p>
              </div>
              <div class="field-body">
               <p>
                Scientists used atomic force microscopy to show a hormone gel can stimulate tooth regeneration in rats.
               </p>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              Scientists must really dread the dentist -- they're always coming up with new solutions to help people avoid that cursed drill. The latest: a hormone gel that regenerates tooth cells in as little as a month.
             </p>
             <p>
              The gel, the first of its kind, could eliminate the need to fill cavities or drill into the root canal of an infected tooth,
              <a href="http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/37978810/ns/health-oral_health/">
               Discovery News reports
              </a>
              . It is reported in the journal
              <em>
               ACS Nano
              </em>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              A dentist would place a dab of the gel near a cavity, where it would encourage cells to grow, healing the tooth from within. It contains a peptide called MSH, melanocyte-stimulating hormone, which has been shown to encourage bone regeneration.
             </p>
             <p>
              A team of French scientists tested the gel on mice that had cavities. After about a month, the cavities had disappeared, according to Nadia Benkirane-Jessel, a scientist at the Institut National de la Sante et de la Recherche Medicale and a co-author of the paper. Not only did new tooth cells grow, but they were also stronger,
              <a href="http://pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/nn100713m?prevSearch=melanocyte&amp;searchHistoryKey=">
               the paper says
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              Along with preventing drill-related discomfort, tooth regeneration could have physiological benefits. Drilling into teeth can destroy nerve cells and blood vessels, so replacing dead or diseased cells with new tissue would be a dramatic improvement.
             </p>
             <p>
              More work needs to be done to prove the gel technique works in humans, however. Even then, it will probably only work in a few cases, Benkirane-Jessel says. Most cavities will still have to be filled.
             </p>
             <p>
              We've reported on a suite of tooth tech lately, including a
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/technology/article/2010-01/cavity-killer">
               gel that halts decay
              </a>
              between teeth;
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/science/article/2010-05/new-technique-uses-bodys-stem-cells-regenerate-teeth">
               stem-cell implants
              </a>
              that regenerate entire teeth;
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/scitech/article/2009-08/scientists-create-new-mouse-tooth-stem-cells">
               "tooth seeds"
              </a>
              that re-grow lost molars; and even a
              <a href="http://www.popsci.com/gear-amp-gadgets/article/2009-06/miniature-plasma-blowtorch-kills-plague-cleans-teeth">
               cavity-killing lightsaber
              </a>
              .
             </p>
             <p>
              They're pretty cool solutions, but ultimately they won't replace the advice your mom drilled into your head: Brush your teeth!
             </p>
             <p>
              [
              <a href="http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/37978810/ns/health-oral_health/">
               Discovery News
              </a>
              ]
             </p>
