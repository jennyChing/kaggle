                    <div id="ld_lwnekb_23100">
                     <h1 class="board_item_title">
                      <span class="inline_editor_value">
                       The Ultimate Rules for Cheese Storage and Shelf Life
                      </span>
                     </h1>
                    </div>
                    <div class="author">
                     <div class="hover_menu hidden white_bg show_nub" id="__w2_yMJZfx3_menu">
                      <div class="hover_menu_contents" id="__w2_yMJZfx3_menu_contents">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <a action_mousedown="UserLinkClickthrough" class="user" href="/Bratz-Blaq-1" id="__w2_yMJZfx3_link">
                      Bratz Blaq
                     </a>
                    </div>
                    <div class="row board_item_description">
                     <span id="ld_lwnekb_23101">
                      <div class="ExpandedQText ExpandedPostContent" id="__w2_O9akW3x_expanded">
                       <span class="inline_editor_value">
                        <div id="__w2_hwOnIkk_outer">
                         <div id="__w2_hwOnIkk_container">
                          <div class="qtext_image_wrapper">
                           <img class="landscape qtext_image qtext_image_large zoomable_in zoomable_in_feed" master_h="300" master_src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-425a1b115789b7738332e722eb61fd96?convert_to_webp=true" master_w="1000" src="http://qph.is.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-c2703ddfb5a28bcf1f4bc3e1d22f305f?convert_to_webp=true"/>
                          </div>
                          <br/>
                          Age is never an issue to cheese and wine - the older the better. As long as you use and store cheese properly, you need not to consume every bits of it in one serving.
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          Here are some guidelines you cant afford to miss, if you want to
                          <span class="qlink_container">
                           <a class="external_link" href="http://www.australianfinefoods.com/cheese-storage-tips" onmouseover='return require("qtext").tooltip(this, "australianfinefoods.com")' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
                            <u>
                             preserve and extend the shelf life of your well-loved cheese
                            </u>
                           </a>
                          </span>
                          :
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          <b>
                           Smell
                          </b>
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          Only certified cheesemongers know how a cheese should smell like. Of course, it can be learned, no need to worry. It is the rule of thumb to smell your cheese before actually purchasing it. Smelling it is the same like actually tasting it. Remember that there are good-smelling cheese and there are bad-smelling cheese. Trust your sensory instinct.
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          <b>
                           Taste
                          </b>
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          Given that smelling alone cant satisfy you, why not have a bite? The basic rule here is if it doesnt taste bad, then it hasnt gone bad. Fresh and quality cheese has their unique, distinct taste, but theres no type of cheese that tastes offensive or sends shivers of disgust down your spine.
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          <b>
                           Family
                          </b>
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          <ul>
                           <li>
                            Soft cheeses
                            <br/>
                           </li>
                          </ul>
                          <br/>
                          Fresh and soft cheeses have shorter shelf life as compared to aged and hard cheeses. Normally, it lasts 7-10 days after you open and use it. Of all the cheeses, this family is the easiest to decipher. If it smells and tastes like the milk left in the back of your fridge for a couple of days, then its a sure sign that you have to toss it in the garbage bin along with the milk.
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          <ul>
                           <li>
                            Aged cheeses
                            <br/>
                           </li>
                          </ul>
                          <br/>
                          Age is an advantage to aged cheeses. Its low moisture content extends its shelf life and durability. Exposure to air can actually heighten the experience and make the flavor assertive. Itll dry out and harden; if you see mold develop, theres no need to worry, just scrape it off.
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          <b>
                           Temperature
                          </b>
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          The ideal temperature for all kinds of cheeses is warm. Place them in the warmest location in your refrigerator, away from pungent food. Keep in mind that cheese is alive, and cold temperature will limit or kill bacterial activity. Make sure to wrap it properly using cheese wax paper and never with plastic alone. You can also store it in a tupperware container.
                          <br/>
                          <br/>
                          Preserving your well loved dairy products can be as easy as 1,2,3. You just have to follow each rule religiously, so youll never go wrong.
