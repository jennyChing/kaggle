        <span class="author">

       <div class="content">
        <p>
         <em>
          It doesnt matter how old you get, there is always something new to learn. Sometimes these new things are words or concepts you have heard all your life, but perhaps you never understood.
          <strong>
           Learn Something New
          </strong>
          is a series that will highlight some of the things I learn, big and small in the coming days.  Douglas
         </em>
        </p>
        <hr/>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Syllabus
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Spend any time in school and you will be presented with a syllabus, if not countless syllabi, at the beginning of each class. I was familiar with the concept of a syllabus, but in reading a new book,
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0393343405/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0393343405&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=thewelchwritecom&amp;linkId=KBIGGKGEGRTENGXW" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.amazon.com']);">
          The Swerve: How the World Became Modern by Stephen Greenblatt
         </a>
         , I came across a short description of the origins of the syllabus, which date far back to Ancient Greece.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0393343405/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0393343405&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=thewelchwritecom&amp;linkId=KBIGGKGEGRTENGXW" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.amazon.com']);">
           <em>
            Papyrus rolls were carefully indexed, labeled (with a protruding tag called in Greek a sillybos), and stacked on shelves or stored in the leather baskets.
           </em>
            The Swerve
          </a>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0393343405/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0393343405&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=thewelchwritecom&amp;linkId=KBIGGKGEGRTENGXW" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.amazon.com']);">
          <img alt="" border="0" src="http://ws-na.amazon-adsystem.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&amp;ASIN=0393343405&amp;Format=_SL160_&amp;ID=AsinImage&amp;MarketPlace=US&amp;ServiceVersion=20070822&amp;WS=1&amp;tag=thewelchwritecom"/>
         </a>
         <img alt="" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=thewelchwritecom&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=0393343405" style="border: none !important; margin: 0px !important;" width="1"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);">
          <img alt="Papyrus scroll" border="0" height="341" src="http://welchwrite.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/papyrus-scroll.jpg" width="560"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus_Oxyrhynchus_29" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 11px; line-height: 13.305599212646484px; text-align: center; background-image: none; background-color: #f9f9f9; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;" title="Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29">
          P. Oxy. I 29
         </a>
         <span style="color: #252525; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 11px; line-height: 13.305599212646484px; text-align: center; background-color: #f9f9f9;">
          , one of the
         </span>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyrhynchus_Papyri" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 11px; line-height: 13.305599212646484px; text-align: center; background-image: none; background-color: #f9f9f9; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;" title="Oxyrhynchus Papyri">
          Oxyrhynchus papyri
         </a>
         <span style="color: #252525; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 11px; line-height: 13.305599212646484px; text-align: center; background-color: #f9f9f9;">
          , includes a fragment of
         </span>
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 11px; line-height: 13.305599212646484px; text-align: center; background-image: none; background-color: #f9f9f9; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial;" title="Euclid's Elements">
          Euclids
          <em>
           Elements
          </em>
         </a>
         <span style="color: #252525; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 11px; line-height: 13.305599212646484px; text-align: center; background-color: #f9f9f9;">
          . via Wikipedia
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         I vaguely remember such a description from somewhere in my deep, dark, educational past, but it made be want to learn a bit more about syllabi, their history and their uses.
        </p>
        <p>
         For most of us on the modern day, a syllabus is a table of contents, a calendar or even a contract between the teacher (usually a college professor) and their students. It usually lays out the concepts that will be studied, books that will be used, a calendar for topics, quizzes, tests and other major events during the term of the class. They can be detailed or general, depending on the teacher and the educational institution for which they work. This is quite a change from what was, in antiquity, basically a table of contents or short index of the papyrus roll it was attached to.
        </p>
        <p>
         Like many ancient words, syllabus seems to be a misreading/miscopy of a Ancient Greek word which was introduced as scrolls were copied over and over. It is quite amazing how language changes over time, usually through mistakes or misreadings than concious choice.
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p style="margin: 0.5em 0px; line-height: 22.399999618530273px; color: #252525; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 14px;">
          According to the
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; background: none;" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
           Oxford English Dictionary
          </a>
          , the word
          <em>
           syllabus
          </em>
          derives from
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Latin" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; background: none;" title="Modern Latin">
           modern Latin
          </a>
          <em>
           syllabus
          </em>
          list, in turn from a misreading of the Greek 
          <em>
           sittybas
          </em>
          parchment label, table of contents, which first occurred in a 15th century print of
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cicero" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; background: none;" title="Cicero">
           Cicero
          </a>
          s letters to Atticus.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_1-1" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-OED-1" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [1]
           </a>
          </sup>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-2" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [2]
           </a>
          </sup>
          Earlier Latin dictionaries such as
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_and_Short" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; background: none;" title="Lewis and Short">
           Lewis and Short
          </a>
          contain the word
          <em>
           syllabus
          </em>
          ,
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-3" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [3]
           </a>
          </sup>
          relating it to the non-existent Greek word , which appears to be a mistaken reading of
          <em>
           syllaba
          </em>
          syllable; the newer
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_Latin_Dictionary" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; background: none;" title="Oxford Latin Dictionary">
           Oxford Latin Dictionary
          </a>
          does not contain this word.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epectasis_4-0" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-Epectasis-4" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [4]
           </a>
          </sup>
          The apparent change from
          <em>
           sitty-
          </em>
          to
          <em>
           sylla-
          </em>
          is explained as a
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercorrection" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; background: none;" title="Hypercorrection">
           hypercorrection
          </a>
          by analogy to .
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epectasis_4-1" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-Epectasis-4" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [4]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
         <p style="margin: 0.5em 0px; line-height: 22.399999618530273px; color: #252525; font-family: sans-serif; font-size: 14px;">
          Because the word
          <em>
           syllabus
          </em>
          is formed in Latin by mistake, the Latinate plural form
          <em>
           syllabi
          </em>
          might be considered a hypercorrection.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-5" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [5]
           </a>
          </sup>
          The OED, however, admits both
          <em>
           syllabuses
          </em>
          and
          <em>
           syllabi
          </em>
          as the plural form.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED_1-2" style="line-height: 1; unicode-bidi: -webkit-isolate;">
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus#cite_note-OED-1" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0b0080; white-space: nowrap; background: none;">
            [1]
           </a>
          </sup>
          
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabus" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://en.wikipedia.org']);">
           Wikipedia
          </a>
         </p>
        </blockquote>

        var msg;
        try {
            var parts = resp.msg.split(' - ',2);
            if (parts[1]==undefined){
                msg = resp.msg;
            } else {
                i = parseInt(parts[0]);
                if (i.toString() == parts[0]){
                    index = parts[0];
                    msg = parts[1];
                } else {
                    index = -1;
                    msg = resp.msg;
                }
            }
        } catch(e){
            index = -1;
            msg = resp.msg;

            if (index== -1){

                }
            }
