        <h2>
         Adjusting to an hour of lost sleep
        </h2>
        <p>
         Theres no more denying it: spring is on its way. And while most of us are psyched about kicking winter to the curb, theres that hour of lost sleep last night that we still need to survive. Youre not alone if youre already dreading the sleep hangover about to take over your life.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Coping with Daylight Saving Time" height="284" src="/images/19-03D72185_edited.jpg" style="margin: 5px 10px 5px 0px; float: left;" width="425"/>
         Why does Daylight Saving Time (DST) have to be so bittersweet?
        </p>
        <p>
         Just like traveling across time zones disrupts sleep, the start DST can make us restless for a few nights as we adjust to going to bed and waking up earlier. A study in
         <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304394006007038" target="_blank">
          Neuroscience Letters
         </a>
         found that during the time change transition after "springing forward," our quality of sleep decreases and we actually sleep an average of one hour less per night. Even the end of DST in the fall (though we get our hour back) can disturb our sleep and create a myriad of health issues. Did you know, car crashes increase on the Monday following a time change and during the following week, heart attacks increase?
        </p>
        <p>
         Cheer up. Springing ahead doesnt have to lead to falling apart. If youre healthy and have good sleep habits, it shouldnt take more than a day or two to adjust to the time change. If youre an overachiever when it comes to sleep  and we hope you are  weve got some coping strategies for you.
        </p>
        <h3>
         Tips for coping with the spring time change
        </h3>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Exercise 
         </strong>
         Get your heart pumping a little harder and serotonin (the stress hormone) is naturally released. If you can exercise outside earlier in the day, even better. Think of sleep as fuel and
         <a href="/blog/yoga-sleep" target="_self">
          exercise
         </a>
         as the engine. Getting more exercise will lead to better sleep, which will give you more energy for exercise. Pretty healthy circular argument, right?
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Nap this afternoon 
         </strong>
         Were big believers in the power of the
         <a href="/blog/7-habits-highly-effective-nappers" target="_self">
          almighty nap
         </a>
         . During the time change transition, nap earlier in the day and keep your nap to 20 minutes. Long enough to revive you but not so long it interferes with your nighttime sleep.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Set an alarm for bedtime 
         </strong>
         Even if youre not tired, go to bed at your normal time (adjusted for the time change) and read quietly until you get sleepy. Your sleep habits will fall back into step within a couple of nights.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <img alt="Coping with Daylight Saving Time" height="283" src="/images/19-ISS_4726_02483_edited.jpg" style="margin: 5px 0px 5px 10px; float: right;" width="425"/>
          Curb alcohol consumption 
         </strong>
         Sleep and alcohol are always a risky combination. If youd like to indulge, do it at least 2 hours before bedtime.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Say no to sleep medication 
         </strong>
         It might be tempting to pop a sleeping pill to help you sleep better for a few nights, but dont do it. Getting the dosage right can be confusing and you dont want to add to your sleep hangover because of a miscalculation.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Lighten up 
         </strong>
         <a href="/blog/light-sleep-technology" target="_self">
          Light
         </a>
         is a powerful tool when it comes to regulating sleep patterns. Bright light earlier in the day and subdued lighting in the evening will help your circadian rhythm right itself quickly.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Watch out for sleepy drivers 
         </strong>
         While youre practicing your sleep better strategies, the guy beside you may be asleep at the wheel. Take your time, keeping your eye out for the sleepy drivers as well as the panicked ones who forgot to change their alarm clock.
        </p>
