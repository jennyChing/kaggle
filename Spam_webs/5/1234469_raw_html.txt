              <p>
               NEW YORK (
               <a href="http://www.mainstreet.com" target="blank">
                MainStreet
               </a>
               )  A recently completed study of 20 years of research on
               <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/edible-cannabis-company-gfarmalabs-goes-public-get-your-piece-of-the-pot-stock" target="blank">
                cannabis use
               </a>
               confirmed at least one of the claims of marijuana legalization advocates but dismantled many of arguments pot proponents use to bolster their cause.
              </p>
              <p>
               Published online this month by the reputable journal
               <em>
                Addiction
               </em>
               of the Society for the Study of Addiction, the monograph by Wayne Hall, a professor at the University of Queensland Centre for Youth Substance Abuse Research in Australia, indicates that there are some real dangers in using marijuana including psychosocial problems and mental health issues in adulthood, as well as other adverse effects.
              </p>
              <p>
               The research was funded by the Australian governments prestigious National Health and Medical Research Council. The salient conclusions are that while acute cannabis use does not produce fatal overdoses, there are many other hazards.
              </p>
              <p>
               For example, the report says that deleterious outcomes of acute pot use are:
              </p>
              <ul>
               <ul>
                <li>
                 <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/marijuana-positive-drivers-increase-fatal-motor-vehicle-crashes" target="blank">
                  Driving while cannabis-intoxicated doubles the risk of a car crash
                 </a>
                 ; this risk increases substantially if users are also alcohol-intoxicated.
                </li>
                <li>
                 Cannabis use during pregnancy slightly reduces birth weight of the baby.
                </li>
               </ul>
              </ul>
              <p>
              </p>
              <div class="googleAd unit" data-ad-defer="true" data-ad-sizes="320,50" data-custom-size-mapping="articleMobileMapping" data-devices="mobile|tablet" data-is-placeholder="true" id="div-gpt-ad-0.7236628636480142">
              </div>
              It also states that among the detrimental effects of chronic marijuana use are:
              <ul>
               <ul>
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  Regular cannabis users can develop a
                  <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/will-marijuana-economy-cause-addiction-boost" target="blank">
                   dependence syndrome
                  </a>
                  , the risks of which are around one in ten of all cannabis users and one in six
                  <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/not-your-fathers-marijuana-pots-strength-has-increased-amid-legalizatio" target="blank">
                   among those who start in adolescence
                  </a>
                  .
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Regular cannabis users
                  <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/can-cannabis-cure-schizophrenia-gwph-thinks-so" target="blank">
                   double their risks of experiencing psychotic symptoms and disorders
                  </a>
                  , especially if they have a personal or family history of
                  <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/does-marijuana-induce-psychosis" target="blank">
                   psychotic disorders
                  </a>
                  , and if they start using cannabis in their mid-teens.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Regular adolescent cannabis users have lower educational attainment than non-using peers, but we dont know whether the link is causal.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Regular adolescent cannabis users are more likely to use other illicit drugs, but we dont know whether the link is causal.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Regular cannabis use that begins in adolescence and continues throughout young adulthood appears to produce intellectual impairment, but the mechanism and reversibility of the impairment is unclear.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  <a data-add-tracking="true" href="http://www.mainstreet.com/article/medical-marijuana-efficacy-shown-through-multiple-studies" target="blank">
                   Regular cannabis use
                  </a>
                  in adolescence approximately doubles the risk of being diagnosed with schizophrenia or reporting psychotic symptoms in adulthood.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Regular cannabis smokers have a higher risk of developing chronic bronchitis.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </ul>
              </ul>
              <p>
              </p>
              <div class="googleAd unit" data-ad-defer="true" data-ad-sizes="320,50" data-custom-size-mapping="articleMobileMapping" data-devices="mobile|tablet" data-is-placeholder="true" id="div-gpt-ad-0.4792317449078286">
              </div>
              There remains no compelling evidence that using marijuana causes the mental health and social problems discussed in the report," said Mason Tvert, director of communications of the Marijuana Policy Project in Denver. responded. "It simply shows that teens who are prone to developing these problems are the ones most likely to break the law and try marijuana. Its a case of correlation, not causation.
              <p>
              </p>
              <div class="OB_TEXT_INACTIVE ob-text" data-ob-template="thestreet" data-src="https://www.mainstreet.com/article/marijuana-use-is-truly-dangerous-20-year-study-reveals" data-widget-id="AR_4">
              </div>
              <script>
              </script>

             <div class="page-end">

            </div>
            <div class="article-page">
             <div class="article-body" itemprop="articleBody">
              <p>
               Tvert dismissed the focus of some critical points in the report about the dangers marijuana can present.
              </p>
              <p>
               Marijuana use by adolescents and driving under the influence are already illegal and will always be illegal, so this does little to affect the debate about whether adults should be allowed to use marijuana responsibly," he said. "Nobody wants teens to use marijuana, but our current policy of prohibition has utterly failed to prevent them from using it. Its time to start regulating the substance and actually controlling it, as opposed to leaving it in the hands of illegal dealers who dont ask customers for proof of age.
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
              <div class="OB_TEXT_INACTIVE ob-text" data-ob-template="thestreet" data-src="https://www.mainstreet.com/article/marijuana-use-is-truly-dangerous-20-year-study-reveals" data-widget-id="AR_4">

             <div class="article-body" itemprop="articleBody">
              <p>
               He pointed out that it was ironic that law enforcement officials spend a lot of time arresting responsible adult marijuana consumers instead of going after people driving under the influence of marijuana, alcohol and other drugs.
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
              <div class="googleAd unit" data-ad-defer="true" data-ad-sizes="320,50" data-custom-size-mapping="articleMobileMapping" data-devices="mobile|tablet" data-is-placeholder="true" id="div-gpt-ad-0.47914334174247664">
              </div>
              That reaction differed considerably from that of Dr. Stuart Gitlow, the president of the American Society of Addiction Medicine and the executive director and associate clinical professor of Addictive Disease Physician Training program at the Mt. Sinai School of Medicine in New York.
              <p>
               Marijuana proponents are fond of saying that marijuana is safer than alcohol, referring, of course, to the fact that one can overdose and die from acute use of alcohol, but not from marijuana," Gitlow said. "What they ignore, however, is what this article clearly demonstrates: regular use of cannabis leads to a significant likelihood of addiction, just as it does with alcohol, and use of cannabis to the point of intoxication causes drugged driving accidents, just as it does with alcohol.
              </p>
              <p>
               Further, regular use of marijuana leads to a significantly higher risk of psychosis, of lower educational attainment, and of having cognitive impairment, he continued.
              </p>
              <p>
               The potential medical benefits of cannabis, Gitlow argued, does not mean that it is safe as a substance.
              </p>
              <p>
               "It is critically important that the dangerous components of marijuana be removed from any such preparation or that consumers understand the enormous risks present in using the plant as if it were a beneficial medication, h said.
