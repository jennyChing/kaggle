       <h1>
        Geoff McFetridge builds an ocean-going bike in great short film
       </h1>
       <div class="meta" data-id="54e7384e5c3e3c4ace00db9c">
        Posted by
        <a href="/authors/rob-alderson">
         Rob Alderson
        </a>
        ,

     <div class="slice copy ">
      <p>
       Were always interested in what Los Angeles-based artist and illustrator Geoff McFetridge has been up to. A video of him talking about making a bike sounded especially promising, but a video of him talking about making a totally absurd ocean-going bike? Swoon.
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        The Sea Rambler
       </em>
       is described as A Human Powered Surf Exploration Vehicle that Geoff made for the Patagonia outdoor clothing store in California. But the contraption is both simultaneously the focus of the film, and completely incidental to it, as Geoff meditates on creativity with quiet, insightful honesty. He talks about the difference between creativity as coming up with an idea and in a second sense thats more to do with engineering, an area where, he readily admits, I dont know what Im doing at all.
      </p>
      <p>
       As
       <em>
        The Sea Rambler
       </em>
       takes shape into an invention that seems to spring straight from the pages of a Roald Dahl book, Geoff explains that he intends to bring viewers into the creative process by making decisions in the work that are totally clear. That way, he insists, you can take somebody on that journey. All aboard then!
      </p>

           <a href="https://app.hiive.co.uk/">

Long the lacklustre foxhole of stuffy Academicians and part-time painters, this year marks the greatest effort the RA has made yet to reinvigorate the English summer stalwart.

Among his modernisms for the show is the decision to repaint the three central galleries in colours lifted straight from his work: hot pink, turquoise and baby blue. Far from playing to mere spectacle, Craig-Martins trademark penchant for polychrome is a bold statement that does away with both the white cube
           <em>

         <h2 class="list-article-title">
          <a href="/articles/beautiful-collaborations-id-bts">
           Beautiful people, beautiful collaborations in new i-D film
          </a>
         </h2>
         <div class="list-article-byline">
          <span class="author">
           Sponsored Article 
          </span>
          <time datetime="2015-06-08T09:00:00+00:00" pubdate="pubdate">
           08 June 2015
          </time>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="list-article-excerpt truncate">
         <p>
          Beauty is a slippery notion  its scruffy for some, preened and polished for another, punk for someone else. It can be all these things together, shaped and subverted by subcultures  a fact that the people over at
          <em>
           iD
          </em>
          know more than most. Examining this idea of the beauty of subcultures and merging life and art, a new project from iD,
          <em>
           Beautiful Collaborations
          </em>
          , draws us into the world of the stylists and makeup artists that create punks, grungy girls and boys and ravers from their beautiful blank canvases. The Microsoft HP Spectrex360 tablet helped to create
          <a href="http://i-d.vice.com/en_gb/video/evolution-subculture-beauty-documentary?ocid=oneconsprempc_D_highimpactrich_PI_KSP_GBR_Storylab">
           the film
          </a>
          , which takes us from the 80s to the present day through the wild  makeup, clothes and personalities of the New Romantics through to Seapunks (a new one on us, too).
         </p>

          <a href="/articles/johan-kramer-manel-vich-barcelona">
           A charming portrait of Barcelona's 77-year-old matchday announcer
          </a>
         </h2>
         <div class="list-article-byline">
          <span class="author">
           Rob Alderson 
          </span>
          <time datetime="2015-06-05T09:00:00+00:00" pubdate="pubdate">
           05 June 2015
          </time>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="list-article-excerpt truncate">
         <p>
          Tomorrow night Barcelona take on Juventus in the Champions League final, a game which (for better or for worse) sums up much about modern football. Its easy to forget quite how much the game has changed but one man who has literally watched that happen is Manel Vich, the 77-year-old who has been Barcas matchday announcer for nearly six decades. He first stood in as a favour to the stadium manager back in the old Les Corts ground in 1956 but has gone on to become part of the clubs fabric. Incredibly hes missed only three games during that time  two because he was in hospital and once because the match clashed with his daughters wedding.
         </p>
