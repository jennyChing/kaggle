/**
 * Gets the "real" position of an element
 * Courtesy Peter-Paul Koch 
 * http://www.quirksmode.org/js/findpos.html
 */         
function findPos(obj) {
    var curleft = curtop = 0;
    if (obj.offsetParent) {
        do {
            curleft += obj.offsetLeft;
            curtop += obj.offsetTop;
        } while (obj = obj.offsetParent);
        return [curleft,curtop];
    }
}

      getPremiumNotice().style.display = "block";

                 Ebola scare forces Bay Area medical facilities to step up preparedness
                </h1>

                 <p class="bodytext">
                  As a killer from a distant continent alarms our nation, Bay Area health care providers are scrambling to change their age-old routines to contain the spread of the deadly Ebola virus.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Employees are posing as patients for secret safety drills at hospitals and clinics. EMS crews are stocking bleach, heavy-duty gowns and goggles on ambulances. Hospitals are waiting for deliveries of safety suits and other equipment.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Bay Area preparations come as a second nurse has tested positive for the Ebola virus in Dallas and 75 other hospital workers are being monitored for symptoms.  Airline passengers also are being sought, because the nurse flew Frontier Airlines Flight 1143 from Cleveland to Dallas the day before becoming ill. The CDC is asking people on that flight to call them for interviews and followup, and the plane has been grounded.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  And in an urgent move to help local health care workers cope, the CDC announced Tuesday it will fly in special rapid response teams to any hospital in the country that diagnoses an Ebola case.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "The biggest difference now is that there is no room for error. None," said Dr. Daniel J. Diekema, president of the Society for Healthcare Epidemiology of America. "With Ebola, the immediate consequences of a single minor break in protocol are extremely severe."
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  It's no longer business as usual for our region's front-line disease detectives. The ease of global travel -- and the infection of a Dallas nurse, who treated the country's first Ebola patient -- are leading to new, exhaustive Ebola prevention practices, as well as raising concern about the adequacy of hospitals' personal protective equipment, staff training and number of experts trained in infectious disease.
                 </p>

                 <p>
                  If you seek treatment for sudden sickness, what matters now is not your name, age, weight and other traditional details. What counts, instead, are your answers to two simple questions: "Do you have chills and fever?" and "Have you traveled to West Africa in the past 21 days?"
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "We ask what we call 'the Golden Questions,' " said Dr. Charles Weiss, head of the infectious diseases committee at Palo Alto Medical Foundation, which has 40 locations in four Bay Area counties. "We view this as a golden opportunity to identify a possible case."
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  So far, there have been two suspected cases in California, triggering a series of emergency steps. The patients -- a Liberian visitor at Centinela Hospital Medical Center in Inglewood and a Kaiser member in South Sacramento who had traveled to West Africa -- were rushed into isolation and treated. Neither patient was infected.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  For decades, hospitals have contained other outbreaks of infectious disease in simple ways, with hand-washing, gloves and masks, said Diekema.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Mistakes happen, he said. But they are rarely deadly.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Federal health officials are facing criticism after the Dallas nurse tested positive for the virus, and on Tuesday Dr. Thomas R. Frieden, director of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, said the agency will step up its support for local hospitals if a case surfaces. The CDC will dispatch experts in infectious disease control, laboratory science, personal protective equipment and decontamination.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Meanwhile, U.S. Reps. Mike Honda, Barbara Lee and other members of Congress urged greater funding of agencies that contain epidemics, noting that a key program at the CDC has been cut by 16 percent over the last four years.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Although protective gear is abundant, deliveries of special suits to hospitals have slowed because so many have been ordered, said infection control manager Renee Juster of John Muir Health.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  And there aren't enough doctors to treat infectious disease outbreaks, according to the Society of Healthcare Epidemiology. Last year, 40 of the 100 training programs in the nation were unable to fill all their openings.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  National Nurses United, which has been surveying nurses across the U.S., has been calling on U.S. hospitals to immediately upgrade emergency preparations.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "I am apprehensive that I don't have the proper gear and the training needed to put on the gear," said Zenei Cortez, a post-anesthesia care nurse with Kaiser Permanente in South San Francisco.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  But Kaiser officials say they are focusing first trainings on primary care doctors and emergency room and "call center" staff.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  To be sure, the risk of Ebola infection remains very low. Up to 49,000 Americans die of influenza every year. Tens of thousands die in car crashes.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  But preparations are underway for that single Ebola case -- because it could quickly turn threatening to us all.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  The California Department of Public Health has sent a checklist of procedures to every health care facility in the state about how to treat an Ebola patient. It requires a full-time "infection control" employee for hospitals with 200 or more beds, and a part-time staffer for smaller hospitals.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Weiss said Palo Alto Medical Foundation has started a "secret shopper" type program -- where employees pose as patients -- to test whether their new protocols are working.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  In Walnut Creek and Concord, John Muir Health is implementing a "buddy system" so fellow workers can ensure that protective suits and other equipment will be put on and taken off safely.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Stanford Hospital has implemented CDC guidelines for screening and patient care, and also conducts regular training and "preparedness drills" to assure its readiness to respond, said spokeswoman Lisa Kim.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Ambulance workers and firefighters in Santa Clara County often confront infectious diseases, such as tuberculosis and flu -- but now they ask more probing questions, and have on hand a formal list of essentials, such as gloves, goggles, gowns, shoe covers and special respirators, said Michael Petrie, Santa Clara County's director of emergency medical services. They have added containers of a bleach-based disinfectant mixture.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Public health agencies like the Santa Clara County Department of Public Health are monitoring cases, and they're urging hospitals to provide training and practice in the quick identification of patients, said Dr. Sarah Cody, the county's health officer.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  "Ebola is new to all of us," said Cody, "and we are learning from both CDC guidance and experience on the ground in Dallas about how to be prepared in the unlikely event that a patient with Ebola arrives."
                 </p>

                  <p class="infoboxhead">
                   SYMPTOMS OF EBOLA:
                   <br/>
                  </p>
                  <p class="infoboxtext">
                   Fever (greater than 101.5 F)
                   <br/>
                   Severe headache
                   <br/>
                   Muscle pain
                   <br/>
                   Weakness
                   <br/>
                   Diarrhea
                   <br/>
                   Vomiting
                   <br/>
                   Abdominal (stomach) pain
                   <br/>
                   Unexplained hemorrhage (bleeding or bruising)
                   <br/>
                   Symptoms may appear anywhere from 2 to 21 days after exposure to Ebola, but the average is 8 to 10 days.
                   <br/>
                   <br/>
                  </p>
                  <p class="infoboxhead">
                   HOW EBOLA IS TRANSMITTED:
                   <br/>
                  </p>
                  <p class="infoboxtext">
                   Direct contact with bodily fluids or through broken skin or mucous membranes in, for example, the eyes, nose or mouth, or by handling soiled clothing or unsterilized medical equipment, including needles and syringes, used by an infected person. It's not possible to catch it through social contact or by simply being in the same room or space. It is not spread through the air or by water, or in general, by food.
