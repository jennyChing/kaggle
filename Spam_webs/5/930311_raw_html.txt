         <div class="content-scroll" id="featured-content">
          <span class="author-byline">
           by
           <strong>
            <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/author/michelle-hammond/" rel="author" title="Posts by Michelle Hammond">
             Michelle Hammond
            </a>
           </strong>
           <span class="published-date">
            <i class="glyphicon glyphicon-calendar" title="Published date">
            </i>
            <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/2015/01/channel-inner-caveman/" title="4:27 pm">
             <time class="entry-date" datetime="2015-01-19T16:27:59+00:00">
              January 19, 2015
             </time>
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
          <p>
           Last week I  read
           <a href="http://lifehacker.com/break-bad-habits-by-tricking-your-inner-caveman-1678100814" rel="nofollow">
            an article on
            <em>
             Lifehacker
            </em>
           </a>
           ,  which said the only way to break bad habits is by tricking your inner  caveman.
          </p>
          <p>
           According to  the article, we all have an inner caveman that controls many of our impulses  and behaviours. If you want to break a habit  like smoking, drinking coffee or  your 3pm chocolate fix  you need to make small changes that your inner caveman  wont notice.
          </p>
          <p>
           The problem  is that our inner caveman doesnt really know whats good for us in the  long-term Ease your way into change so you can trick your inner caveman into  thinking that everythings totally cool, it said.
          </p>
          <p>
           I thought it  was interesting that the inner caveman was portrayed in a negative light; like  some sort of ignorant, uncivilised beast you need to control or trick. I think  this is wrong.
          </p>
          <p>
           From the time  we can talk, were told to suppress many of our natural impulses and desires.  Walk, dont run. Stand in line. Wait your turn. Dont scream in public. Dont  laugh too loudly. Dont buy that two-for-one chocolate deal at the supermarket  checkout. It goes on.
          </p>
          <p>
           We exert so  much energy NOT experiencing things that we forget what its like to actually  experience them  or we become too scared to do anything on a whim.
          </p>
          <p>
           I think this  can be hugely detrimental to your overall
           <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/category/lifestyle/health-and-fitness-tips/" title="more on health and wellbeing">
            health and wellbeing
           </a>
           , not only  because its unnatural but also because it can lead to regret.
          </p>
          <p>
           Last year,  Simon wrote an Esky article about
           <a href="http://escapologist.com.au/2014/02/amygdalae-support-group-emotional-brain/">
            the amygdalae
           </a>
            two almond-shaped groups of nuclei  located in the brain, which help process emotional reactions.
          </p>
          <p>
           The  amygdalae, when called upon, heighten your senses and force you to pay  attention right now. They compel you to act immediately, often without a plan,  Simon wrote.
          </p>
          <p>
           Your  rational brain will come up with millions of reasons for you not to do  something But heres the thing: regrets that result from inaction always burn  for longer than regrets derived from trying something that didnt work out.
          </p>
          <p>
           In a sense,  the amygdalae is just a fancier term for inner caveman. By ignoring your  inner caveman, you could be doing yourself a disservice.
          </p>
          <p>
           I think  caveman-like characteristics can actually work to your advantage  in the  kitchen, in the boardroom and even in the bedroom.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Five ways channelling your inner  caveman could actually improve your life
           </strong>
           :
          </p>
          <ul type="disc">
           <li>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Its good for your       health.
             </strong>
             One of       the simplest and most obvious ways you can channel your inner caveman is       to
             <em>
              eat
             </em>
             like a caveman. Theres       even a name for it  the Paleo (as in, Paleolithic) diet. Load up on lots       of vegetables, grass-fed meats, fish, eggs and nuts. Dairy, potatoes and       anything that contains sugar or wheat are strictly out of bounds. Same       goes for coffee and alcohol. Youll feel less bloated and more energetic.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul type="disc">
           <li>
            <p>
             <strong>
              It boosts your       brain power.
             </strong>
             One       of the main reasons people are attracted to the Paleo diet is because of       its reputation as a means to lose weight. But it has also been known to       reduce anxiety and boost brain power. Part of the reason for this is the       elimination of grains. There are countless studies  and even
             <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Grain-Brain-Surprising-Sugar-Your-Killers/dp/031623480X" rel="nofollow">
              books
             </a>
              that suggest grain is bad for       your brain.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul type="disc">
           <li>
            <p>
             <strong>
              It makes you more       productive.
             </strong>
             Cavemen       didnt have iPhones. Or email accounts. Or diaries. Beyond the four seasons       of the year, there was no way for them to plan anything. They lived in the       moment, in the full sense of the term, not only because handheld devices       didnt exist but because
             <em>
              they had to
             </em>
             .       You can do this too. Rather than attempting to do 10 things at once, focus       on the task directly in front of you. Youll get more done.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul type="disc">
           <li>
            <p>
             <strong>
              It strengthens your       relationships.
             </strong>
             When       you live in the moment, youre more engaged. And in todays disconnected,       social media-obsessed world, thats a rare quality worth having. Whether youre       lunching with a client or chatting to your spouse over dinner, commit       yourself to that activity. Youll build stronger, more meaningful       relationships.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <ul type="disc">
           <li>
            <p>
             <strong>
              It makes you       happier.
             </strong>
             Im not       going to beat around the bush (metaphorically speaking, of course). There       are countless health benefits of sex, including happier moods, stress       relief and better sleep. So why all the fuss? Cavemen didnt go on dates.       Or drink champagne. Or eat oysters. They just had sex whenever they felt       like it. Its easy to come up with excuses as to why you shouldnt get       down and dirty with your significant other. Youre too tired, too       hot/cold, not thin enough The list goes on. If you feel the need to       satisfy your primal urge, dont overthink it  just do it.
            </p>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           Are you in  touch with your inner caveman? How has it helped or hindered you? Let us know  at
           <a href="mailto:esky@escapologist.com.au">
            esky@escapologist.com.au
           </a>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Until next  time,
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Michelle Hammond
           </strong>
