       When the first people set foot on Mars, what will be the most efficient gait for them to use? To find out, Australian artist
       <a href="www.adamnorton.net">
        Adam Norton
       </a>
       is strapping people into his Martian gravity simulator and getting them to perform laps (see video above).
       <br/>
       <br/>
       The design is based on
       <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1CUhz0U-Gc">
        NASA lunar gravity simulators
       </a>
       used before the Apollo moon landings to investigate how astronauts should safely move around in low gravity. They found that it was easier to bounce than walk. Since mass and horizontal inertia are the same as on Earth, but the force pulling down is much less, you don't need to take as many steps to get around.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       The gravity on Mars is a little more than twice that of the moon's, so Norton's device is inclined at a less extreme angle. He's investigating how this affects speed and endurance and the transition speed from walking to running. So far, he found that it's easier to run and jump on the simulator than on the ground. "I think a long bounding stride will become the norm on Mars," says Norton.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       The Mars gravity simulator was recently exhibited at the
       <a href="http://www.performancespace.com.au/2011/awfully-wonderful-science-fiction-in-contemporary-art/">
        Awfully Wonderful: Science Fiction in Contemporary Art event
       </a>
       in Sydney, Australia.
       <br/>
       <br/>
       If you enjoyed this video, check out
       <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/blogs/nstv/2010/12/table-top-simulator-creates-powerful-twisters.html">
        a simulator that can recreate powerful twisters
       </a>
       or
       <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/articlevideo/mg20627546.000/75144379001-simulator-gets-to-the-bottom-of-troughled-tsunamis.html">
        a device that simulates tsunamis
       </a>
       .
