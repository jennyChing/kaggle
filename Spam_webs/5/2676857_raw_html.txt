        <p>
         <font color="#006666">
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
           Electric 
      lighting burns up to 25% of the average home energy budget.
           <br/>
          </font>
         </font>
         <b>
          <font color="#006666">
           <b>
            <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
             <br/>
             The electricity used over the lifetime of a single incandescent bulb costs 5 
        to 10 times the original purchase price of the bulb itself.
            </font>
           </b>
          </font>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Light Emitting Diode (LED)
          </font>
         </b>
         <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           <font color="#006666">
            and
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>
         <b>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           Compact 
          Fluorescent Lights (CFL)
           <font color="#006666">
            bulbs have revolutionized energy-efficient 
            lighting.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            CFLs are simply miniature versions of full-sized fluorescents. They screw 
            into standard lamp sockets, and give off light that looks similar to the 
            common incandescent bulbs - not like the fluorescent lighting we associate 
            with factories and schools.
           </font>
          </font>
          <font size="2">
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            LEDs
           </font>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            <font color="#006666">
             are small, very efficient solid bulbs.
             <a href="http://eartheasy.com/energy-efficiency/energy-efficient-led-lighting">
              New 
                LED bulbs
             </a>
             are grouped in clusters with diffuser lenses which have broadened the applications for LED use 
          in the home
            </font>
           </font>
           <font color="#006666">
            . LED technology is advancing rapidly, with many new bulb styles available.
           </font>
          </font>
         </b>
         <b>
          <font size="2">
           <font color="#006666">
            Initially more expensive than CFLs, LEDs bring more value since they last longer. Also, the price of LED bulbs is going down each year as the manufacturing technology continues to improve.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </font>
          </font>
         </b>
        </p>

       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="10" height="121" valign="top">
        <font face="Arial Narrow">
         <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          <br/>
          LEDs 
      (Light Emitting Diodes)
         </font>
         <font color="#336600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           are  solid light bulbs which are extremely energy-efficient. When first developed, LEDs were limited to single-bulb use in applications such 
        as instrument panels, electronics, pen lights and, more recently, strings 
        of indoor and outdoor Christmas lights.
          </b>
         </font>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="14" height="250" valign="top">
        <font color="#336600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          Manufacturers have  expanded 
      the application of LEDs by "clustering" the small bulbs. The first clustered  bulbs were used for battery powered items such as flashlights and headlamps. Today, LED bulbs are made using as many as 180 bulbs per cluster, and encased in diffuser lenses which spread the light in wider beams. Now available with standard bases which fit common household light fixtures, LEDs are the next generation in home lighting.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          A significant feature of LEDs is that the light is directional, as opposed to incandescent bulbs which spread the light more spherically. This is an advantage with recessed lighting or under-cabinet lighting, but it is a disadvantage for table lamps. New LED bulb designs address the directional limitation by using diffuser lenses and reflectors to disperse the light more like an incandescent bulb.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          The high cost of producing LEDs has been a roadblock to widespread use. However, researchers at Purdue University have
         </b>
         <a href="http://www.eartheasy.com/blog/2009/01/led-breakthrough-may-revolutionize-lighting/">
          developed
         </a>
         <b>
          a process for using inexpensive silicon wafers to replace the expensive sapphire-based technology. This promises to bring LEDs into competitive pricing with CFLs and incandescents. LEDs may soon become the standard for most lighting needs. We are following these developments with interest and will report the
         </b>
         <a href="http://www.eartheasy.com/blog/2009/04/led-lighting-update/">
          latest updates
         </a>
         <b>
          in this research.
          <br/>
         </b>
        </font>

        </a>

       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="13" height="370" valign="top">
        <p>
         <font color="#006666" face="Arial Narrow" size="3">
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
           <br/>
           Long-lasting
          </font>
          <b>
           <font color="#336600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
            -
           </font>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            <font color="#336600">
             LED 
        bulbs last up to 10 times as long as compact fluorescents, and far longer 
        than typical incandescents.
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </font>
           </font>
          </b>
          <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
           <font color="#CC6600" size="3">
            Durable
           </font>
           <b>
            <font color="#336600" size="3">
             <font size="2">
              -
             </font>
            </font>
            <font color="#336600" size="2">
             since LEDs do not have a 
          filament, they are not damaged under circumstances when a regular incandescent 
          bulb would be broken. Because they are solid, LED bulbs hold up well 
          to jarring and bumping.
            </font>
            <font color="#336600">
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </font>
           </b>
           <font color="#CC6600" size="3">
            Cool
           </font>
           <b>
            <font color="#336600" size="2">
             - these bulbs do not cause heat build-up; LEDs produce 3.4 btu's/hour, compared 
              to 85 for incandescent bulbs.
            </font>
            <font color="#006666" face="Arial Narrow" size="3">
             <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
              <b>
               <font color="#006666" face="Arial Narrow" size="3">
                <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
                 <b>
                  <font color="#336600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
                   <b>
                    Common incandescent bulbs get hot and contribute to heat build-up in a room. LEDs prevent this heat build-up, thereby helping to reduce
                   </b>
                  </font>
                 </b>
                </font>
               </font>
              </b>
             </font>
            </font>
            <font color="#336600" size="2">
             air conditioning costs in the home.
            </font>
            <font color="#336600">
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </font>
           </b>
           <font color="#006666" face="Arial Narrow" size="3">
            <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
             <font color="#CC6600" size="3">
              Mercury-free
             </font>
            </font>
           </font>
           <b>
            <font color="#006666" face="Arial Narrow" size="3">
             <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
              <b>
               <font color="#336600" size="2">
                - no mercury is used in the manufacturing of LEDs.
               </font>
              </b>
             </font>
            </font>
            <font color="#336600">
             <br/>
             <br/>
            </font>
           </b>
           <font color="#CC6600" size="3">
            More efficient
           </font>
           <b>
            <font color="#336600" size="3">
             <font size="2">
              -

        <b>
         <font color="#FFFFFF" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          LED Terminology
         </font>
        </b>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="14" height="210" valign="top">
        <p>
         <b>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <br/>
           Coloring Rendering Index (CRI
          </font>
          <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           ) -
           <font color="#336600">
            CRI represents the quality of light and its faithfulness to render colors   correctly, that is, to enable us to perceive colors as we know them. The ideal CRI is 100, and some incandescent bulbs approach this level. LEDs and CFLs use different design components in trying to equal the CRI of incandescent bulbs.  LED bulbs CRI ratings range from 70 to 95, and the best CFLs have ratings in the mid 80s.

The LED
           </font>
          </font>
         </b>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <font color="#336600">
           CREE CR6 bulb
          </font>
          <b>
           <font color="#336600">
            , for example, features a CRI of 90 Warm White   making it one of the highest in the industry.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            <strong>
             <font color="#CC6600">
              Correlated Color Temperature (CCT)
             </font>
             -
            </strong>
            is the measure used to   describe the relative color appearance of a white light source. CCT indicates   whether a light source appears more yellow/gold/orange or more blue, in terms of   the range of available shades of "white." CCT is given in kelvins (unit of   absolute temperature). 2700K is "Warm" and 5000K is "Cool". The typical light color we are used to in indoor home lighting is "warm", 2700 - 2800K.
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </font>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            <b>
             <font color="#CC6600">
              Lumen
             </font>
             <font color="#336600">
              -  a unit of standard measurement that is used to describe the amount of light contained in an area as perceived by the human eye. The more lumens, the brighter the light. You can use lumens to compare the brightness of any bulb, regardless of the technology behind it, and regardless of whether it's  incandescent, CFL or LED.
             </font>
            </b>
           </font>
           <font color="#336600">
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </font>
           <font color="#CC6600">
            Luminous Flux
           </font>
           <font color="#336600">
            - the flow of light measured in lumens. With light bulbs, it provides an estimate of the apparent amount of light the bulb will produce. Depending on the application, much of an incandescent's light is wasted because it's emitted in every direction. LED bulbs, on the other hand, put out directional light, sending all of the light exactly where it's needed.
            <br/>
            <br/>
            In practical application, when buying a light bulb, we should  look for bhlbs which produce more light but consumes less energy. Understanding lumens as a measure of brightness makes it easier to select the most efficient bulb for your application. This is also important
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>

        <font color="#FFFFFF" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
         <b>
          <font size="3">
           CFL Lighting: Benefits
          </font>
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" height="249" valign="top" width="25">
       </td>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="13" height="249" valign="top">
        <p>
         <font color="#006666">
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
           <br/>
           Efficient:
          </font>
         </font>
         <b>
          <font color="#006666">
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            CFLs are four times more efficient and last up to 10 times longer than incandescents. 
        A 22 watt CFL has about the same light output as a 100 watt incandescent. 
        CFLs use 50 - 80% less energy than incandescents.
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </font>
          </font>
         </b>
         <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          Less 
          Expensive:
         </font>
         <b>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           Although initially more expensive, you save money in the long run because 
            CFLs use 1/3 the electricity and last up to 10 times as long as incandescents. 
            A single 18 watt CFL used in place of a 75 watt incandescent will save about 
            570 kWh over its lifetime. At 8 cents per kWh, that equates to a $45 savings.
           <br/>
           <br/>
          </font>
         </b>
         <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          Reduces 
              Air and Water Pollution:
         </font>
         <b>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           Replacing a single incandescent bulb with a CFL will keep a half-ton of CO2 
              out of the atmosphere over the life of the bulb. If everyone in the U.S. used 
              energy-efficient lighting, we could retire 90 average size power plants. Saving 
              electricity reduces CO2 emissions, sulfur oxide and high-level nuclear waste.
           <br/>
           <br/>
          </font>
         </b>
         <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          High-Quality 
                  Light:
         </font>
         <b>
          <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <font color="#006666">
            Newer CFLs give a warm, inviting light instead of the "cool white" 
                  light of older fluorescents. They use rare earth phosphors for excellent 
                  color and warmth. New electronically ballasted CFLs don't flicker or hum.
            <br/>
            <br/>
           </font>
          </font>
         </b>
         <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          Versatile:
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
          <b>
           <font color="#006666">
            <font size="2">
             CFLs can be applied nearly anywhere that incandescent lights are used. Energy-efficient 
                        CFLs can be used in recessed fixtures, table lamps, track lighting, ceiling 
                        fixtures and porchlights. 3-way CFLs are also now available for lamps with 
                        3-way settings. Dimmable CFLs are also available for lights using a dimmer 
                  switch.
            </font>
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
       </td>

       <td bgcolor="#b0cf54" colspan="14" height="34" valign="middle">

         </a>
        </b>
        <font color="#FFFFFF" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
         <b>
          Choosing 
      a CFL
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" height="1311" rowspan="11" valign="top" width="25">
       </td>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="13" height="59" valign="top">
        <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          <br/>
          CFLs 
      come in many shapes and sizes. When purchasing CFLs, consult the seller 
      for recommendations and consider the following:
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="13" height="131" valign="top">
        <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           
          </font>
          <font color="#CC6600">
           Choose your preferred light quality
          </font>
          <br/>
          CFL bulbs have a Kelvin or 'K' number listed on the packaging. CFLs with K numbers between   2700-3000 give off a soft bright light like incandescents. CFLs with K numbers   between 3500-6000 give off a bright light. As you go up the K number scale the   light gets bluish and closer to daylight.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          For example:
          <br/>
          <font color="#009999">
           Approx. 2700K = Warm White
          </font>
          (looks just like 
      incandescent)
          <br/>
          <font color="#009999">
           Approx. 5000K = Cool White
          </font>
          (white/blue, bright light)
          <br/>
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="13" height="119" valign="top">
        <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          <font color="#CC6600">
            
      Choose the shape.
          </font>
          CFLs are available in a variety of shapes to 
      fit a range of lamps and lighting fixtures. See below on this page for 
      the most popular CFL shapes.
         </b>
        </font>
        <p>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <b>
           <font color="#CC6600">
            
           </font>
           <font color="#006666">
            <font color="#CC6600">
             Match lumens
            </font>
            to the incandescent being replaced. 
        Lumens indicate the amount of light being generated. (Watts is a measure 
        of energy use, not light strength.) Lumen output is printed on the bulb package or on the bulb product page if purchasing bulbs online.
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#b0cf54" colspan="13" height="30" valign="middle">
        <font color="#FFFFFF">
         <font color="#FFFFFF" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          <b>
           CFL Light Bulb Models
          </b>
         </font>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="13" height="124" valign="top">
        <font color="#006666">
         <font face="Arial Narrow" size="2">
          <b>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            <br/>
            CFLs are available in a variety of styles or shapes. Some have two, four, or 
      six tubes. Older models, and specialty models, have
           </font>
           <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            separate 
          tubes and ballasts.
           </font>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            Some CFLs have the tubes and ballast permanently connected. This allows you 
          to change the tubes without changing the ballast. Others have circular or spiral-shaped 
          tubes. In general, the size or total surface area of the tube determines how 
          much light the bulb produces.
           </font>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            <br/>
            <br/>
            The following CFL bulb models come with standard sockets for easy installation 
              in most common household applications.
           </font>
          </b>
         </font>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="10" height="660" rowspan="6" valign="top">
        <b>
         <font color="#006666" face="Arial Narrow">
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <font color="#CC3300">
            <br/>
            Spiral Lamps
           </font>
           <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
            <br/>
            These bulbs are designed as a continuous tube in a spiral shape which 
        has similar outside shape and light casting qualities to a standard incandescent 
        bulb.
           </font>
          </font>
          <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           Spiral 
          CFL bulbs are made in several sizes to fit most common fixtures.
          </font>
         </font>
        </b>
        <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          <font color="#CC3300">
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Triple 
            Tube Lamps
          </font>
          <br/>
          These CFLs have more tubing in a smaller area, which generates even more 
            light in a shorter bulb. They pack high light output into a very small 
            space and can be used in fixtures designed for incandescent bulbs, such 
            as table lamps, 
            reading lamps, open hanging lamps, and bare bulb applications.
         </b>
         <b>
          <font color="#CC3300">
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Standard Lamps
          </font>
          <br/>
          These are regular CFL spiral lamps which are placed inside a dome cover and fitted with a standard base which fits common lamp sockets. 
                They are designed to give the appearance of the traditional light bulb 
                for consumers looking for the more familiar light bulb appearance. The 
                glass diffuser provides a quality of light similar to the 'soft-white' 
                type of incandescent bulbs.
         </b>
         <b>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <font color="#CC3300">
           Globe Lamps
          </font>
          <br/>
          This shape is commonly used in bathroom vanity mirrors or open hanging 
                    lamps, and bare bulb applications. Bathroom vanities usually require multiple 
                    bulbs, which generate radiant heat. The CFL globe will reduce this heat 
                    buildup while saving energy. The glass diffuser provides a soft-white 
                    light.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <font color="#CC3300">
           Flood Lamps
          </font>
          <br/>
          These lamps are designed to be ideal for recessed and track lighting fixtures, 
                        indoors and outdoors. They provide diffused, soft, white light, and generate 
                        less heat than will an incandescent flood or a halogen bulb. CFL flood lamps are available in varying sizes and wattages.
          <font color="#CC3300">
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Candelabra
          </font>
          <br/>
          The screw-in torpedo-shape and the small-base of this bulb is designed 
                            for smaller light fixtures throughout the house, from chandeliers to sconces. 
                            To use a smaller candelabra-based bulb in a regular socket, you can use 
                            a socket reducer.
         </b>
        </font>

       <td bgcolor="#b0cf54" colspan="14" height="29" valign="middle">
        <font color="#FFFFFF" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
         <b>
          Limitations of CFL lightbulbs
         </b>
        </font>
        <a name="3c">
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="14" height="56" valign="top">
        <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <b>
          <br/>
          Although 
      CFLs are an excellent source of energy-efficient lighting, they are not always 
      the best choice for all lighting applications. Here are a few limitations to 
      consider:
         </b>
        </font>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="14" height="362" valign="top">
        <ul>
         <li>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            <font color="#CC6600">
             On/Off 
          cycling:
            </font>
            CFLs are sensitive to frequent on/off cycling. Their 
          rated lifetimes of 10,000 hours are reduced in applications where the 
          light is switched on and off very often. Closets and other places where 
          lights are needed for brief illumination should use incandescent or LED bulbs.
           </b>
          </font>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            Dimmers:
           </b>
          </font>
          <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif">
           <b>
            <font color="#006666">
             <font size="2">
              Dimmable CFLs are  available for lights using a dimmer 
                                      switch, but check the package;
             </font>
            </font>
           </b>
          </font>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            not all CFLs can be used on dimmer switches. Using a regular CFL with a dimmer can shorten the bulb life span.
           </b>
          </font>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            Outdoors:
           </b>
          </font>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            CFLs can be used outdoors, but should be covered or shaded from the 
                                        elements. Low temperatures may reduce light levels - check the package 
                                        label to see if the bulb is suited for outdoor use.
           </b>
          </font>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            Retail 
                                            lighting:
           </b>
          </font>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            CFLs are not spot lights. Retail store display lighting usually requires 
                                            narrow focus beams for stronger spot lighting. CFLs are better for area 
                                          lighting.
           </b>
          </font>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </li>
         <li>
          <b>
           <font color="#CC6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            Mercury 
                                            content:
           </font>
           <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            CFLs contain small amounts of mercury which is a toxic metal. This metal 
                                          may be released if the bulb is broken, or during disposal.  For more information about mercury and CFLs, see below.
           </font>
          </b>
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         <font color="#006699">
          <b>
           <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
            The principle reason for reduced lifespan of CFLs is heat. CFLs exhibit shorter   lifespans in light fixtures and sockets where there is low air-flow and heat   build-up such as recessed lighting. For these types of sockets it is recommended to ues specially designed
           </font>
          </b>
          <font color="#006666" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
           <b>
            CFLs for recessed   lighting or
            <a href="http://eartheasy.com/energy-efficiency/energy-efficient-led-lighting">
             LEDs
            </a>
            . Another main reason for reduced lifespan of CFLs is too-frequent on/off cycling. These bulbs should be used where they will be left on for steady periods without being flicked on and off.
           </b>
          </font>
         </font>
         <br/>
         <br/>
        </p>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td bgcolor="#FFFFF0" colspan="14" height="352" valign="top">
        <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
         Mercury 
      and CFLs
        </font>
        <font color="#333333" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
         <br/>
         Mercury is a toxic metal associated with contamination of water, fish, 
      and food supplies, and can lead to adverse health affects. A CFL bulb 
      generally contains an average of 5 mg of mercury (about one-fifth of that 
      found in the average watch battery, and less than 1/100th of the mercury 
      found in an amalgam dental filling). A power plant will emit 10mg of mercury 
      to produce the electricity to run an incandescent bulb compared to only 
      2.4mg of mercury to run a CFL for the same time. The net benefit of using 
      the more energy efficient lamp is positive, and this is especially true 
      if the mercury in the fluorescent lamp is kept out of the waste stream 
      when the lamp expires.
         <br/>
        </font>
        <p>
         <font color="#FF6600" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="3">
          Handling and Disposal of CFLs
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <br/>
         </font>
         <font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2">
          <font color="#333333">
           The mercury in compact 
        fluorescent bulbs poses no threat while in the bulb, but if you 
        break one:
           <br/>
           - open a window and leave the room for 15 minutes or more
           <br/>
           -  use a wet rag to clean it up and put all of the pieces, 
        and the rag, into a plastic bag
           <br/>
           - place all materials in a second sealed plastic bag
           <br/>
           - call your local recycling center to see if they accept this material, 
        otherwise put it in your local trash. Wash your hands afterward.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Burned out CFLs can be dropped off at Home Depot and Ikea stores. Another solution is to save spent CFLs for a community household hazardous 
        waste collection, which would then send the bulbs to facilities capable 
        of treating, recovering or recycling them. For more information on CFL 
        disposal or recycling, you can contact your local municipality.
          </font>
         </font>
        </p>
