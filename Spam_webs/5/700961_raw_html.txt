   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Said the actress to the bishop
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=As_the_actress_said_to_the_bishop&amp;redirect=no" title="As the actress said to the bishop">
       As the actress said to the bishop
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      "That's what she said" redirects here. For other uses, see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/That%27s_what_she_said_(disambiguation)" title="That's what she said (disambiguation)">
       That's what she said (disambiguation)
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      "
      <b>
       Said the actress to the bishop
      </b>
      " is an informal (and usually vulgar) exclamation, said for humour in the form of a
      <a href="/wiki/Punch_line" title="Punch line">
       punch line
      </a>
      after an inadvertent
      <a href="/wiki/Double_entendre" title="Double entendre">
       double entendre
      </a>
      . The equivalent phrase in North America is "
      <b>
       that's what
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       said
      </b>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tifo_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tifo-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsvine_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-newsvine-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Both phrases are examples of
      <a href="/wiki/Wellerism" title="Wellerism">
       Wellerisms
      </a>
      , a literal "turn" of a phrase, changing its meaning. The versatility of the phrase and its popularity lead some to consider it a
      <a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Clich">
       clich
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History.2C_background_and_variations">
       History, background and variations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Said_the_actress_to_the_bishop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History, background and variations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term, or its variant "as the actress said to the bishop", may have been used as far back as
      <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_era" title="Edwardian era">
       Edwardian times
      </a>
      , and is apparently
      <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">
       British
      </a>
      in origin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Partridge_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Partridge-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The phrase is frequently used by the fictional character
      <a href="/wiki/Simon_Templar" title="Simon Templar">
       Simon Templar
      </a>
      (alias "The Saint") in a long-running series of mystery books by
      <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Charteris" title="Leslie Charteris">
       Leslie Charteris
      </a>
      . The phrase first appears in the inaugural Saint novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Meet_the_Tiger" title="Meet the Tiger">
        Meet the Tiger
       </a>
       ,
      </i>
      published in 1928.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tifo_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tifo-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The phrase "as the girl said to the soldier" is recorded in a sound test for
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">
       Alfred Hitchcock
      </a>
      's 1929 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Blackmail_(1929_film)" title="Blackmail (1929 film)">
        Blackmail
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huffpo_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-huffpo-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a very early recording by Len Spencer of the song "All Coons Look Alike to Me" from 1896.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After the introduction to the song there is the very suggestive line, "That's what she said, yeah", putting the origin of this one-liner very far back.
     </p>
     <p>
      Kingsley Amis uses the line in his novel "Lucky Jim" (1954 chapter 12). An adulterous married woman [Carol Goldsmith] who is ambiguously interested in Jim Dixon says to him, "If you don't know what to do [about Christine Callaghan, another young woman Jim is attracted to, but who is involved with an artist son of Jim's academic boss] I can't show you, as the actress said to the bishop".
     </p>
     <p>
      By 1973, "that's what
      <i>
       she
      </i>
      said" had already been characterized as an "ancient
      <a href="/wiki/One-line_joke" title="One-line joke">
       one-liner
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the early 1990s, it was popularized as a recurring joke in the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">
        Saturday Night Live
       </a>
      </i>
      sketch "
      <a href="/wiki/Wayne%27s_World" title="Wayne's World">
       Wayne's World
      </a>
      ". In the
      <a href="/wiki/Wayne%27s_World_(film)" title="Wayne's World (film)">
       movie of the same name
      </a>
      , the character, Wayne Campbell, uses the phrase after his partner Garth says, "Hey, are you through yet? 'Cause I'm getting tired of holding this", in regard to a picture he is holding.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsvine_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-newsvine-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the original
      <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
       BBC
      </a>
      version of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Office_(UK_TV_series)" title="The Office (UK TV series)">
        The Office
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Gervais" title="Ricky Gervais">
       Ricky Gervais
      </a>
      's character
      <a href="/wiki/David_Brent" title="David Brent">
       David Brent
      </a>
      frequently used the phrase "as the actress said to the bishop" as an inappropriate joke. When the show was adapted for American audiences, the phrase was translated to "that's what she said" for
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_Carell" title="Steve Carell">
       Steve Carell
      </a>
      's character
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Scott_(The_Office)" title="Michael Scott (The Office)">
       Michael Scott
      </a>
      . "That's what she said" became a
      <a href="/wiki/Catchphrase" title="Catchphrase">
       catchphrase
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       The Office
      </i>
      and was used for the show's "That's What She Said" Sweepstakes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
