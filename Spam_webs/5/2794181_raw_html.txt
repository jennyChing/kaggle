     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        The Rock
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1996 American
      <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">
       action film
      </a>
      that primarily takes place on
      <a href="/wiki/Alcatraz_Island" title="Alcatraz Island">
       Alcatraz Island
      </a>
      and in the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">
       San Francisco Bay Area
      </a>
      . It was directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bay" title="Michael Bay">
       Michael Bay
      </a>
      , produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Simpson" title="Don Simpson">
       Don Simpson
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Bruckheimer" title="Jerry Bruckheimer">
       Jerry Bruckheimer
      </a>
      and stars
      <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">
       Sean Connery
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cage" title="Nicolas Cage">
       Nicolas Cage
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ed_Harris" title="Ed Harris">
       Ed Harris
      </a>
      . The film is dedicated to Simpson,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who died five months before its release. The film received generally favorable reviews from critics and was nominated for an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Sound_Mixing" title="Academy Award for Best Sound Mixing">
       Academy Award for Best Sound Mixing
      </a>
      at the
      <a href="/wiki/69th_Academy_Awards" title="69th Academy Awards">
       69th Academy Awards
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rock_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A group of rogue
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps_Force_Reconnaissance" title="United States Marine Corps Force Reconnaissance">
       U.S. Force Recon Marines
      </a>
      , led by disenchanted
      <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general_(United_States)" title="Brigadier general (United States)">
       Brigadier General
      </a>
      Frank Hummel and his adjutant Major Tom Baxter, seize a stockpile of deadly
      <a href="/wiki/VX_(nerve_agent)" title="VX (nerve agent)">
       VX gas
      </a>
      armed
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M55_rocket" title="M55 rocket">
       M55 rockets
      </a>
      . The next day, Hummel and his men seize control of
      <a href="/wiki/Alcatraz_Island" title="Alcatraz Island">
       Alcatraz Island
      </a>
      and take several tourists hostage. Hummel threatens to launch the rockets against
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      unless the government pays $100 million from a military slush fund, which he will distribute to his men and the families of Recon Marines who died on clandestine missions under his command and whose deaths were not compensated.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">
       The Pentagon
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">
       FBI
      </a>
      develop a plan to retake the island with a
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_SEALs" title="United States Navy SEALs">
       U.S. Navy SEAL
      </a>
      team, enlisting the FBI's top chemical weapons specialist, Doctor Stanley Goodspeed. Goodspeed's confidence, already shaky as he is a "lab rat" with minimal combat training and experience, is further tested when his fiance Carla reveals she is pregnant.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Director_of_the_Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation">
       FBI director
      </a>
      James Womack is forced to turn to federal prisoner John Mason, a 60-year-old
      <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">
       British national
      </a>
      who has been imprisoned without charges for three decades. Mason is the only Alcatraz inmate ever to escape the island, doing so in 1963 through uncharted underground tunnels.
     </p>
     <p>
      While in custody at the
      <a href="/wiki/Fairmont_San_Francisco" title="Fairmont San Francisco">
       Fairmont Hotel
      </a>
      , Mason flees. He steals a
      <a href="/wiki/Hummer_H1" title="Hummer H1">
       Hummer H1
      </a>
      and Goodspeed pursues in a commandeered
      <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_F355" title="Ferrari F355">
       Ferrari F355
      </a>
      , resulting in a chase through the streets. Mason seeks out his estranged daughter Jade; Goodspeed arrives with a team to re-arrest Mason, revealing to Jade that he is aiding the FBI.
     </p>
     <p>
      Goodspeed, Mason, and the SEALs infiltrate Alcatraz. However, Hummel's Marines are alerted to their presence and ambush them in a shower room. All the SEALs are killed, leaving only Mason and Goodspeed alive. Mason sees his chance to escape custody and disarms Goodspeed, but Goodspeed convinces him to help defuse the rockets, since Mason's daughter is also at risk.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mason and Goodspeed eliminate several teams of Marines and disable 12 of the 15 rockets by removing their guidance chips. Hummel threatens to execute a hostage if they do not surrender and return the guidance chips. Mason destroys the chips, then surrenders to Hummel, trying to reason with him as well as buy Goodspeed some time. Though Goodspeed disables another rocket, the Marines capture him. With the incursion team lost, the military initiates their backup plan: an air strike by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F/A-18" title="F/A-18">
       F/A-18s
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Thermite" title="Thermite">
       thermite
      </a>
      plasma, which will neutralize the poison gas but kill everyone on the island.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mason and Goodspeed escape, and Mason explains why he was held prisoner: he was a British
      <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">
       SAS
      </a>
      Captain who stole a microfilm containing details of the United States's most closely guarded secrets, refusing to give it up when captured because he knew he would be killed if he did. When the deadline for the transfer of the ransom arrives, Hummel and his men fire one of the operational rockets, only for Hummel to change his mind and changing the coordinates for the rocket to splash into San Francisco Bay. When confronted by two of his men, Captain Frye and Darrow, Hummel orders them to exit Alcatraz with a few hostages and the remaining rockets to cover their retreat, while he will assume blame. Realizing they will not be paid their $1 million apiece, Frye and Darrow mutiny against Hummel and Baxter, killing the latter and mortally wounding the former.
     </p>
     <p>
      Darrow and Frye proceed with the plan to fire on San Francisco. While Mason deals with the remaining Marines, Goodspeed seeks out the last rocket, which Hummel reveals the location for with his dying breath. As the jets approach, Goodspeed kills both Darrow and Frye, disarming the rocket. He signals the jets that the threat is over.
     </p>
     <p>
      Goodspeed tells Mason that Womack tore up his pardon, then informs his superiors that Mason was killed. Mason gives Goodspeed a note that holds the location of the microfilm. Some time later, Goodspeed and Carla recover the microfilm containing a half-century of state secrets, including who actually
      <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">
       killed
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">
       John F. Kennedy
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rock_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Hensleigh" title="Jonathan Hensleigh">
       Jonathan Hensleigh
      </a>
      participated in writing the script, which became the subject of a dispute with the
      <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America" title="Writers Guild of America">
       Writers Guild of America
      </a>
      . In this case, the spec script (by David Weisberg and Douglas Cook) was reworked by several writers, but other than the original team, Mark Rosner was the only one granted official credit by guild arbitration. The rule is that the credited writing team must contribute 50% of the final script (effectively limiting credits to the screenplay's initial authors, plus one re-write team). Despite their work on the script, neither Hensleigh nor
      <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Sorkin" title="Aaron Sorkin">
       Aaron Sorkin
      </a>
      was credited in the film. The director
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bay" title="Michael Bay">
       Michael Bay
      </a>
      wrote an open letter of protest, in which he criticized the arbitration procedure as a "sham" and a "travesty". He said Hensleigh had worked closely with him on the movie and should have received screen credit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">
       Quentin Tarantino
      </a>
      was also an uncredited screenwriter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QT_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-QT-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
       L.A.
      </a>
      -based British screenwriting team
      <a href="/wiki/Dick_Clement" title="Dick Clement">
       Dick Clement
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ian_La_Frenais" title="Ian La Frenais">
       Ian La Frenais
      </a>
      were brought in at Connery's request to rewrite his lines, but ended up altering much of the film's dialogue. It was Nicolas Cage's idea that his character would not swear; his euphemisms include "gee whiz." Bay had worked closely with Ed Harris to develop his character as concretely as possible, later adding a sympathetic edge to Hummel.
     </p>
     <p>
      There were tensions during shooting between director
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bay" title="Michael Bay">
       Michael Bay
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Studios_(division)" title="The Walt Disney Studios (division)">
       Walt Disney Studios
      </a>
      executives who were supervising the production. On the commentary track for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criterion_Collection" title="Criterion Collection">
       Criterion Collection
      </a>
      DVD, Bay recalls a time when he was preparing to leave the set for a meeting with the executives when he was approached by
      <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">
       Sean Connery
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">
       golfing
      </a>
      attire. Connery, who also produced the film, asked Bay where he was going, and when Bay explained he had a meeting with the executives, Connery asked if he could accompany him. Bay complied and when he arrived in the conference room, the executives' jaws dropped when they saw Connery appear behind him. According to Bay, Connery then stood up for Bay and insisted that he was doing a good job and should be left alone.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The scene in which FBI director Womack is thrown off the balcony was filmed on location at the
      <a href="/wiki/Fairmont_San_Francisco" title="Fairmont San Francisco">
       Fairmont Hotel
      </a>
      in San Francisco. The filming led to numerous calls to the hotel by people who saw a man dangling from the balcony.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the scene in which Paxton demands to know from Womack who Mason is, Paxton utters, "Yeah, I know all the cloak and dagger stories." This line was a direct reference to Forsythe's earlier film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cloak_%26_Dagger_(1984_film)" title="Cloak &amp; Dagger (1984 film)">
        Cloak &amp; Dagger
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      The film was selected for a limited edition DVD release by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criterion_Collection" title="Criterion Collection">
       Criterion Collection
      </a>
      , a distributor of primarily
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthouse" title="Arthouse">
       arthouse
      </a>
      films it categorizes as "important classic and contemporary films" and "cinema at its finest". In an essay supporting the selection of
      <i>
       The Rock
      </i>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">
       Roger Ebert
      </a>
      , who was strongly critical of most of Bay's later films, gave the film a 3 1/2 out of four stars, calling it "an action picture that rises to the top of the genre because of a literate, witty screenplay and skilled craftsmanship in the direction and special effects."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2014,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(magazine)" title="Time Out (magazine)">
        Time Out
       </a>
      </i>
      polled several film critics, directors, actors and stunt actors to list their top action films.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       The Rock
      </i>
      was listed at 74th place on this list.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
