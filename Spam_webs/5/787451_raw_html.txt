     <div class="block-system-main block block-system" id="block-system-main">
      <div class="block__content">
       <div class="t7-clear-me">
       </div>
       <div class="node node-blog-entry node-promoted node-115251 comment-2 clearfix" data-nid="115251" data-type="blog_entry">
        <div class="node__content mode__full">
         <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden">
          <div class="field__item">
           <p>
            This blog curates the voices of the Division of
            <a href="http://www.psychologytoday.com/basics/psychoanalysis" title="Psychology Today looks at Psychoanalysis">
             Psychoanalysis
            </a>
            (39) of the American Psychological Association. Shelley Galasso Bonanno, M.A., a practicing psychotherapist in the metropolitan Detroit area writes this post:
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <div class="insertArea">
            <div class="article-inline-half">
             <div class="insert-inner">
              <div class="insert-image">
               <img alt="" src="https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half/public/blogs/94075/2013/01/115251-113226.png?itok=5De1KD2t" title=""/>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           <p>
            While the old adage, A picture is worth a thousand words" gained favor nearly a century before the advent of social media sites, the saying was never more applicable than it is today, in the notion that our complex selves can somehow be miraculously conveyed with just a single still image posted to social media sites, such as
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/social-networking" title="Psychology Today looks at Facebook">
             Facebook
            </a>
            . Never mind that the fantasied perfect picture is impossible to achieve. Postings to social media sites generally portray who we would like others (and ourselves) to perceive us to be. Because so often many of our friends rarely if ever come face to face with us or even converse with us verbally, we can craft an idealized image and present it to the world as complete reality. We post ourselves, smiling and engaged, using only the best, most flattering photographs, crafting an idealized image and presenting it to the world as a complete reality.
           </p>
           <p>
            The news feed we create is an idealized global village of sorts. We post status updates to our Facebook tribe that promote us as we choose to present ourselves. And more often than not, this feeds our ego, leaving us feeling validated and admired when we are acknowledged with click of the Facebook Like button, giving us a thumbs up in response to our post. Day after day, we update our status, seeking continued validation of the fantasy. And given the absence of a Dislike button, it seems easy to avoid potential rejection and emotional vulnerability.
           </p>
           <p>
            I am reminded of the flip books of my
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/child-development" title="Psychology Today looks at childhood">
             childhood
            </a>
            in which a series of pictures that varied only slightly from one page to the nextbrought the drawings to life when the pages were quickly turned. These simple drawings created an illusion, appearing to collapse the separate images into an integrated and complete animation. And so it may be with social media sites: We create an illusion of ourselves through our postings, and others responses support our perception that these glimpses somehow represent our complex and cohesive psychological selves.And, we Like it.
           </p>
           <p>
            In contrast, as we read others social media posts, we often compare ourselves and our lives to our friends apparent accomplishments. Are we good enough? Attractive enough? Active enough in our own lives? Even while knowingly posting self-aggrandizing photos and news of ourselves, we may disregard the likelihood that others are doing the same and choose to presume that their lives in fact constitute the sum total of the few clicks they choose to share on Facebook. Perhaps this was never more eloquently stated (in 140 words or less) than by a Tweet shared on Twitter: My theory is I can only be on FB when everything in my life is great or it will plunge me into a deep
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/depression/depression-and-society" title="Psychology Today looks at depression">
             depression
            </a>
            <strong>
             .
            </strong>
            <strong>
            </strong>
            It seems that our social media world, so often a useful form of communication, can become detrimental to our emotional
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/health" title="Psychology Today looks at health">
             health
            </a>
            , not through others actions but by our own need to portray ourselves  and view others  in a glamorized light.
           </p>
           <p>
            Recently, a colleague discussed the excessive use of social media sites by some patients, who compare their own apparently mundane existence to the seemingly exciting and resourceful lives of others as portrayed on Facebook. The compulsive
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/environment" title="Psychology Today looks at nature">
             nature
            </a>
            of such information seeking has been disadvantageous to these patients, serving to plummet their already low
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/self-esteem" title="Psychology Today looks at self-esteem">
             self-esteem
            </a>
            into deeper depths. While they know this, day after day they continue to seek out news feed postings again and again. This all too common scenario is not a pleasure seeking behavior, but rather an exercise in supporting and reinforcing an already negative
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/identity" title="Psychology Today looks at self-image">
             self-image
            </a>
            . And while
            <a class="inline-links topic-link" href="/basics/psychoanalysis" title="Psychology Today looks at psychoanalysis">
             psychoanalysis
            </a>
            is one such place where the notion of an idealized transference is not uncommon, psychoanalysis allows us a time and a place to explore and work through our idealized versions of not only our analysts, but perhaps ourselves as well.
           </p>
           <p>
            Do our Facebook relationships really differ from those whom we interact with outside of our virtual lives? Hopefully so. Our day to day relationships offer us the unique opportunity to reveal many aspects of our multifaceted psychological selves. While some pictures may indeed be worth a thousand words, a picture posted on a social media site is more apt to represent just one pixel of our intricate, psychological self-image.
           </p>
           <p>
            ---

             <div class="content">
              <div class="field field-name-comment-body field-type-text-long field-label-hidden">
               <div class="field__item">
                <p>
                 I thought this article and the posts were very interesting! I am 25 yr old in Canada and have never had a facebook page. Initially, I didn't get it because I wanted to be cool and different but after a while I just didn't care. I admit from time to time I consider getting it because I feel left out of this exclusive club that I can never be a part of. But deep inside my gut I always knew facebook would cause inner turmoil and bring drama into my life so I stayed clear.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Never having facebook I have watched it evolve and see how it plays such a huge role in many peoples lives for all sorts of reasons. What I find most interesting is how it has changed the dynamics of many of my own relationships. I have gotten scolded by friends and family for not putting more effort into communicating and showing interest in their lives, and always being difficult to get a hold of (despite being avaiable by phone or e-mail any time of day). I do agree with the concept of facebook feeding people's egos. When I see how hard my friends work to keep up with the Jones' fb style, sometimes I just think its rediculous. I was never big into phones and computers so I often find I am the only one in my group (or the bus, or school, or anywhere with anyone) not staring at a screen when we are all in the same place together, what the heck! I dodge people all day in the hallways who are not looking up while walking with their eyes on their phone. Sometimes I think its sad because it seems like they are missing out on what's going on around them in the present moment. I find many people at the college I go to are awkward to talk with and act bothered if I try and make conversation with them even though we are the same age. I do not posess a ph.D but I can't help but feel their lack of social skills such as generating conversation on the spot might be attributed to their fb usage over the years. One of my most treasured friendships has totally dissolved over the last couple of years for whatever reason, but sometimes I think its because she got fb and I didn't. She lives, eats, breathes fb, which I cannot really grasp as a lifestyle, whereas she cannot grasp a life without it. I could be overanalyzing the situation but overall I am glad I never got it because I didnt get caught up in a lot of BS, but then again it sure looks like everyone is having a great time!
                </p>
                <p>
                 Just as a closing note I do realize facebook has many advantages and I am sure has helped people all over the world, even saved lives which is great! 8)If it makes people happy then that is good.
                </p>
                <p>
                 One more thing I am so sick of the word facebook ha ha!
                </p>
