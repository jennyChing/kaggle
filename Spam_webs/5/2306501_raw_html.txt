     <p>
      <b>
       The Empress (III)
      </b>
      is the third
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trump_card" title="Trump card">
       trump
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Major_Arcana" title="Major Arcana">
       Major Arcana
      </a>
      card in traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Tarot" title="Tarot">
       Tarot
      </a>
      decks. It is used in
      <a href="/wiki/Tarot_card_games" title="Tarot card games">
       Tarot card games
      </a>
      as well as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarot_reading" title="Tarot reading">
       divination
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Empress_(Tarot_card)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Empress sits on a throne wearing a starry
      <a href="/wiki/Crown_(headgear)" title="Crown (headgear)">
       crown
      </a>
      , holding a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scepter" title="Scepter">
       scepter
      </a>
      in one hand. The Scepter is representative of her power over life, her crown has twelve stars representing her dominance over the year, and her throne is in the midst of a field of grain, representative of her dominion over growing things. The Empress is representative of the productivity of the subconscious, seeded by ideas. She is meant to be the embodiment of the growth of the natural world, fertility, and what one knows or believes from the heart.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Empress_(Tarot_card)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Empress seems to have had a rather uneventful five centuries.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Edward_Waite" title="Arthur Edward Waite">
       Waite
      </a>
      and the other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occultist" title="Occultist">
       occultists
      </a>
      are responsible for the starry crown, the emblem of Venus, the waterfall, and the vegetation and wildlife. In historical decks, the Empress sits on a
      <a href="/wiki/Throne" title="Throne">
       throne
      </a>
      , almost always holding a
      <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">
       shield
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Globus_cruciger" title="Globus cruciger">
       orb
      </a>
      in one hand and a scepter in the other. The shield typically bears an eagle, the heraldic emblem of the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      . The Empress
      <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_of_Italy" title="Adelaide of Italy">
       Adelaide
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">
       beatified
      </a>
      by the Catholic Church, seems a likely historical person this card may allude to.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">
       Interpretation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Empress_(Tarot_card)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interpretation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Edward_Waite" title="Arthur Edward Waite">
       Waite
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Pictorial_Key_to_the_Tarot#III._The_Empress" title="wikisource:The Pictorial Key to the Tarot">
        The Pictorial Key to the Tarot
       </a>
      </i>
      , The Empress is the inferior (as opposed to nature's superior)
      <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">
       Garden of Eden
      </a>
      , the "Earthly Paradise". Waite defines her as not being
      <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Heaven" title="Queen of Heaven">
       Regina Coeli
      </a>
      (the Blessed Virgin Mary), but rather a
      <a href="/wiki/Refugium_Peccatorum" title="Refugium Peccatorum">
       Refugium Peccatorum
      </a>
      - a fruitful mother of thousands:
      <i>
       she is above all things universal
       <a href="/wiki/Fecundity" title="Fecundity">
        fecundity
       </a>
       and the outer sense of the Word, the repository of all things nurturing and sustaining, and of feeding others.
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Empress is mother, a creator and nurturer. In many decks she can be shown as pregnant. She can represent the creation of life, of romance, of art or business. The Empress can represent the germination of an idea before it is ready to be fully born, and the need to be receptive to change.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simplytarot_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-simplytarot-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
