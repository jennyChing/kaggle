     <p>
      <b>
       Cornel Ronald West
      </b>
      (born June 2, 1953) is an American philosopher, academic, activist, author,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_intellectual" title="Public intellectual">
       public intellectual
      </a>
      , and prominent member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Socialists_of_America" title="Democratic Socialists of America">
       Democratic Socialists of America
      </a>
      . The son of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baptist" title="Baptist">
       Baptist
      </a>
      minister, West received his undergraduate education at
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
       Harvard University
      </a>
      , graduating with a bachelor's degree in 1973, and received a Ph.D at
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton University
      </a>
      in 1980, becoming the first African American to graduate from Princeton with a Ph.D in philosophy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He taught at Harvard in 2001 before leaving the school after a highly publicized dispute with then-president
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">
       Lawrence Summers
      </a>
      . He was The Class of 1943 Professor of African American Studies at Princeton
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      before leaving the school in 2011 to become Professor of Philosophy and Christian Practice at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Theological_Seminary_in_the_City_of_New_York" title="Union Theological Seminary in the City of New York">
       Union Theological Seminary
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
       New York City
      </a>
      . He has also spent time teaching at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">
       University of Paris
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The bulk of West's work focuses on the role of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">
       race
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">
       gender
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_(social)" title="Class (social)">
       class
      </a>
      in American society and the means by which people act and react to their "radical conditionedness." West draws intellectual contributions from multiple traditions, including
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Black_church" title="Black church">
       the black church
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">
       Marxism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Neopragmatism" title="Neopragmatism">
       neopragmatism
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">
       transcendentalism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pragmatic_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pragmatic-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among his most influential books are
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Race_Matters" title="Race Matters">
        Race Matters
       </a>
      </i>
      (1994) and
      <i>
       Democracy Matters
      </i>
      (2004).
     </p>
     <p>

      <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/C-SPAN" title="C-SPAN">

      <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">

      and programs such as
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Time_With_Bill_Maher" title="Real Time With Bill Maher">

       <a href="/wiki/The_Colbert_Report" title="The Colbert Report">

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornel_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      West was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">
       Tulsa, Oklahoma
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-influx_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-influx-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and grew up in
      <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">
       Sacramento, California
      </a>
      , where he graduated from
      <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_High_School_(Sacramento,_California)" title="John F. Kennedy High School (Sacramento, California)">
       John F. Kennedy High School
      </a>
      . His mother, Irene (Bias), was a teacher and principal, and his father, Clifton Louis West, Jr., was a
      <a href="/wiki/General_contractor" title="General contractor">
       general contractor
      </a>
      for the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">
       Defense Department
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Irene B. West Elementary School in
      <a href="/wiki/Elk_Grove,_California" title="Elk Grove, California">
       Elk Grove, California
      </a>
      , is named for his mother.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As a young man, West marched in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">
       civil rights
      </a>
      demonstrations and organized protests demanding black studies courses at his high school, where he was class president. He later wrote that, in his youth, he admired "the sincere black militancy of
      <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">
       Malcolm X
      </a>
      , the defiant rage of the
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Panther_Party" title="Black Panther Party">
       Black Panther Party
      </a>
      [...] and the livid
      <a href="/wiki/Black_theology" title="Black theology">
       black theology
      </a>
      of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Hal_Cone" title="James Hal Cone">
       James Cone
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1970, after graduating from high school, he enrolled at
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">
       Harvard College
      </a>
      and took classes from philosophers
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nozick" title="Robert Nozick">
       Robert Nozick
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cavell" title="Stanley Cavell">
       Stanley Cavell
      </a>
      . In 1973, he graduated
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_honors" title="Latin honors">
        magna cum laude
       </a>
      </i>
      in Near Eastern languages and civilization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West credited Harvard with exposing him to a broader range of ideas, influenced by his professors as well as the Black Panther Party. West says his Christianity prevented him from joining the BPP, instead choosing to work in local breakfast, prison, and church programs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornel_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_appointments">
       Academic appointments
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornel_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Academic appointments">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1980, West earned a Ph.D. from
      <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
       Princeton
      </a>
      , where he was influenced by
      <a href="/wiki/Richard_Rorty" title="Richard Rorty">
       Richard Rorty
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Neopragmatism" title="Neopragmatism">
       neopragmatism
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mendieta_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mendieta-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The title of his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissertation" title="Dissertation">
       dissertation
      </a>
      was
      <i>
       Ethics, historicism and the Marxist tradition
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which was later revised and published under the title
      <i>
       The Ethical Dimensions of Marxist Thought
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mendieta_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mendieta-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In his mid-20s, he returned to Harvard as a
      <a href="/wiki/W._E._B._Du_Bois" title="W. E. B. Du Bois">
       W. E. B. Du Bois
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">
       Fellow
      </a>
      before becoming an Assistant Professor at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Theological_Seminary_in_the_City_of_New_York" title="Union Theological Seminary in the City of New York">
       Union Theological Seminary in the City of New York
      </a>
      . In 1984, he went to
      <a href="/wiki/Yale_Divinity_School" title="Yale Divinity School">
       Yale Divinity School
      </a>
      in what eventually became a joint appointment in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Studies" title="American Studies">
       American Studies
      </a>
      . While at Yale, he participated in campus protests for a clerical labor union and
      <a href="/wiki/Divestment" title="Divestment">
       divestment
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">
       apartheid
      </a>
      South Africa. One of the protests resulted in his being arrested and jailed. As punishment, the University administration canceled his leave for the spring term in 1987, leading him to commute from Yale in
      <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">
       New Haven, Connecticut
      </a>
      , where he was teaching two classes, across the Atlantic Ocean to the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">
       University of Paris
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henderson-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He then returned to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_Theological_Seminary_in_the_City_of_New_York" title="Union Theological Seminary in the City of New York">
       Union
      </a>
      for one year before going to Princeton to become a professor of Religion and director of the Program in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_studies" title="African American studies">
       African-American Studies
      </a>
      from 1988 to 1994.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henderson-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After Princeton, he accepted an appointment as professor of African-American studies at Harvard University, with a joint appointment at the
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Divinity_School" title="Harvard Divinity School">
       Harvard Divinity School
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aaseng_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Aaseng-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West taught one of the University's most popular courses, an introductory class on African-American studies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1998, he was appointed the first
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphonse_Fletcher" title="Alphonse Fletcher">
       Alphonse Fletcher
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endowed_professor" title="Endowed professor">
       University Professor
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West utilized this new position to teach in not only African-American studies, but also in divinity, religion, and philosophy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aaseng_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Aaseng-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West left Harvard after a widely publicized dispute with then-President
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">
       Lawrence Summers
      </a>
      in 2002. That year, West returned to Princeton, where he helped create one of the worlds leading centers for African-American studies according to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shirley_Tilghman" title="Shirley Tilghman">
       Shirley Tilghman
      </a>
      , Princeton's president in 2011.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2011_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT2011-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2012, West left Princeton and returned to the seminary where he began his teaching career, Union Theological Seminary.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His departure from Princeton, unlike his departure from Harvard, was on good terms and he remains an
      <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">
       emeritus
      </a>
      professor at Princeton.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2011_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NYT2011-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The recipient of more than 20 honorary degrees and an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Book_Award" title="American Book Award">
       American Book Award
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pragmatic_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pragmatic-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      he has written or contributed to over twenty published books. West is a long-time member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Socialists_of_America" title="Democratic Socialists of America">
       Democratic Socialists of America
      </a>
      , for which he now serves as an honorary chair.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henderson_21-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Henderson-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is also a co-founder of the
      <a href="/wiki/Network_of_Spiritual_Progressives" title="Network of Spiritual Progressives">
       Network of Spiritual Progressives
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West is on the advisory board of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Bridges_to_Justice" title="International Bridges to Justice">
       International Bridges to Justice
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, he received a special recognition from the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Cultural_Council" title="World Cultural Council">
       World Cultural Council
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      West is also a member of
      <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Phi_Alpha" title="Alpha Phi Alpha">
       Alpha Phi Alpha
      </a>
      fraternity and its
      <a href="/wiki/World_Policy_Council" title="World Policy Council">
       World Policy Council
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">
       think tank
      </a>
      whose purpose is to expand Alpha Phi Alpha's involvement in politics and social and current policy to encompass international concerns.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPC.235_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-WPC.235-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1995,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">
        The New Republic
       </a>
      </i>
      literary editor
      <a href="/wiki/Leon_Wieseltier" title="Leon Wieseltier">
       Leon Wieseltier
      </a>
      charged him with opportunism, crass showmanship, and lack of scholarly seriousness. Wieseltier specifically dismissed West's books as "almost completely worthless" because, he said, they are "noisy, tedious, slippery ... sectarian, humorless, pedantic and self-endeared."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Nonetheless, West remains a widely cited scholar in the popular press.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dispute_with_Lawrence_Summers">
       Dispute with Lawrence Summers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornel_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dispute with Lawrence Summers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 2000, economist and former U.S. Treasury Secretary
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" title="Lawrence Summers">
       Lawrence Summers
      </a>
      became president of Harvard. Soon after, Summers held a private meeting with West, where he reportedly rebuked West for missing too many classes, contributing to
      <a href="/wiki/Grade_inflation" title="Grade inflation">
       grade inflation
      </a>
      , neglecting serious scholarship, and spending too much time on his economically profitable projects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Summers reportedly suggested that West produce an academic book befitting his professorial position, as his recent output had consisted primarily of co-written and edited volumes. According to some reports, Summers also objected to West's production of a CD, the critically panned
      <i>
       Sketches of My Culture
      </i>
      , and to his political campaigning, including an alleged three weeks to promote
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Bradley" title="Bill Bradley">
       Bill Bradley
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)_presidential_primaries,_2000" title="Democratic Party (United States) presidential primaries, 2000">
       presidential
      </a>
      campaign.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West contended he had missed only one class during his tenure at Harvard "in order to give a keynote address at a Harvard-sponsored conference on AIDS." Summers also allegedly suggested that since West held the rank of
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University_Professor" title="Harvard University Professor">
       Harvard University Professor
      </a>
      and thus reported directly to the President, he should meet with Summers regularly to discuss the progress of his academic production.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indignant_47-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-indignant-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Summers refused to comment on the details of his conversation with West, except to express hope that West would remain at Harvard. Soon after, West was hospitalized for
      <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">
       prostate cancer
      </a>
      . West complained that Summers failed to send him get-well wishes until weeks after his surgery, whereas newly installed Princeton president
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shirley_Tilghman" title="Shirley Tilghman">
       Shirley Tilghman
      </a>
      had contacted him frequently before and after his treatment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indignant_47-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-indignant-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2002 West left Harvard University to return to Princeton. West lashed out at Summers in public interviews, calling him "the
      <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Sharon" title="Ariel Sharon">
       Ariel Sharon
      </a>
      of higher education" on NPR's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tavis_Smiley_Show" title="The Tavis Smiley Show">
        Tavis Smiley Show
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In response to these remarks, five Princeton faculty members, led by professor of molecular biology Jacques Robert Fresco, said they looked with "strong disfavor upon his characterization" of Summers and that "such an analogy carries innuendoes and implications... that many on the Princeton faculty find highly inappropriate, indeed repugnant and intolerable."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
       Harvard University
      </a>
      's undergraduate student newspaper,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Harvard_Crimson" title="The Harvard Crimson">
        The Harvard Crimson
       </a>

      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_and_Order:_Criminal_Intent" title="Law and Order: Criminal Intent">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_race_in_the_United_States">
       Views on race in the United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornel_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Views on race in the United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      West has called the U.S. a "racist patriarchal" nation where
      <a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">
       white supremacy
      </a>
      continues to define everyday life. "White America," he writes, "has been historically weak-willed in ensuring racial justice and has continued to resist fully accepting the humanity of blacks." This has resulted, he says, in the creation of many "degraded and oppressed people hungry for identity, meaning, and self-worth." West attributes most of the black community's problems to "existential angst derive[d] from the lived experience of ontological wounds and emotional scars inflicted by white supremacist beliefs and images permeating U.S. society and culture."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">

        </span>

        <span>

     <p>

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">
       September 11, 2001 attacks
      </a>
      gave white Americans a glimpse of what it means to be a black person in the United Statesfeeling "unsafe, unprotected, subject to random violence, and hatred" for who they are.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-democracy_matters_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-democracy_matters-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      "The ugly terrorist attacks on innocent civilians on 9/11," he said, "plunged the whole country into the blues."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-democracy_matters_52-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-democracy_matters-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      West was arrested on October 13, 2014, while protesting against the
      <a href="/wiki/Shooting_of_Michael_Brown" title="Shooting of Michael Brown">
       shooting of Michael Brown
      </a>
      and participating in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferguson_October" title="Ferguson October">

      <i>

      case the previous year.

      Five days later, he was arrested during an Occupy Wall Street protest in

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_Barack_Obama">
       Views on Barack Obama
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornel_West&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Views on Barack Obama">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Cornel West publicly supported
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_Democratic_Presidential_candidate" title="2008 Democratic Presidential candidate">
       2008 Democratic Presidential candidate
      </a>
      Senator
      <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
       Barack Obama
      </a>
      . He spoke to over 1,000 of his supporters at the
      <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Theater" title="Apollo Theater">
       Apollo Theater
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Harlem" title="Harlem">
       Harlem
      </a>
      , on November 29, 2007.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      West criticized President Obama when Obama won the
      <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">
       Nobel Peace Prize
      </a>
      in 2009, saying that it would be difficult for Obama to be "a war president with a peace prize". West further retracted his support for Obama in an April 2011 interview, stating that Obama is "a black mascot of Wall Street oligarchs and a black puppet of corporate plutocrats. And now he has become head of the American killing machine and is proud of it."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
       <a href="#cite_note-71">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        71
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72">
       <a href="#cite_note-72">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        72
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In November 2012, West said in an interview that he considered Obama a "
      <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Republican" title="Rockefeller Republican">
       Rockefeller Republican
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">
       blackface
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73">
       <a href="#cite_note-73">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        73
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74">
       <a href="#cite_note-74">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        74
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Melissa_Harris-Perry" title="Melissa Harris-Perry">
       Melissa Harris-Perry
      </a>
      , a professor of
      <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">
       political science
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Tulane_University" title="Tulane University">
       Tulane University
      </a>
      , criticized what she described as the "utter hilarity" of West's statements, writing that his comments are a "classic
      <a href="/wiki/Psychological_projection" title="Psychological projection">
       projection
      </a>
      of his own comfortably ensconced life at Harvard and Princeton Universities" and that West "offers thin criticism of president Obama and stunning insight into the delicate ego of the self-appointed black leadership class".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75">
       <a href="#cite_note-75">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        75
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      West later called Harris-Perry a "fake and a fraud".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76">
       <a href="#cite_note-76">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        76
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2011, West participated in a "Poverty Tour" with
      <a href="/wiki/Tavis_Smiley" title="Tavis Smiley">
       Tavis Smiley
      </a>
      , his co-host on the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_Radio_International" title="Public Radio International">
       Public Radio International
      </a>
      program
      <i>
       Smiley &amp; West
      </i>
      . The tour became a two-part special on their radio program as well as a five-night special on the PBS television program
      <i>
       Tavis Smiley
      </i>
      . They recounted their experience on the tour in their 2012 bestselling book
      <i>
       The Rich and the Rest of Us
      </i>
      . The stated aim of the tour was to highlight the plight of the
      <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">
       impoverished
      </a>
      population of the United States prior to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2012" title="United States presidential election, 2012">
       2012 Presidential Election
      </a>
      , whose candidates West and Smiley stated had ignored the plight of the poor.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77">
       <a href="#cite_note-77">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        77
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2014, West gave an interview slamming Obama, calling him a "counterfeit" who posed as a progressive. West defined Obama's presidency as "a Wall Street presidency, a drone presidency, a national security presidency."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
       <a href="#cite_note-78">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        78
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
