          <div class="post-inner group">
           <h1 class="post-title">
            Adwords Keyword Tool Used For Market Research
           </h1>
           <p class="post-byline">
            by
            <a href="http://www.demondemon.com/author/paul-rone-clarke/" rel="author" title="Posts by Paul Rone-Clarke">
             Paul Rone-Clarke
            </a>
             March 26, 2015
           </p>
           <div class="clear">
           </div>
           <div class="entry">
            <div class="entry-inner">
             <p>
              Some things change very little, using the Google ad words tool as a primer to start building your website is as sound advice now as it was 10 years ago.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Using-Adwords-Data-For-Generic-SEO-And-Marketing.png" rel="lightbox-0">
               <img alt="Using Adwords Data For Generic SEO And Marketing" border="0" height="357" src="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Using-Adwords-Data-For-Generic-SEO-And-Marketing_thumb.png" style="background-image: none; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin-left: auto; display: block; padding-right: 0px; margin-right: auto; border-width: 0px;" title="Using Adwords Data For Generic SEO And Marketing" width="640"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              A word of warning however. The information the tool gives is specifically for pay per click campaigns. Ive seen it used as if the traffic levels and competition levels it reports were also relevant for ranking in natural search. This isnt the case.
             </p>
             <p>
              But that doesnt mean that the information cant be used creatively to add real value right from the beginning of a site build. In the example in the video Im looking at Dog training. as a keyword.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Keyword-Setup.jpg" rel="lightbox-1">
               <img alt="Keyword Setup" border="0" height="642" src="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Keyword-Setup_thumb.jpg" style="background-image: none; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin-left: auto; display: block; padding-right: 0px; margin-right: auto; border-width: 0px;" title="Keyword Setup" width="539"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <p>
              The targeting options on the bottom right allow you to hone in should you be looking to rank locally or just want a local pay per click presence. Language is also important. I could do a post just on translating a page, but if you already go to the trouble to do that youll likely already know about the extra markets a multi language approach can lead to. The ability to filter out negative keywords is also handy when creating an advertising campaign, but for the purposes of this example I wont be using that feature.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Seasonality-Graph.jpg" rel="lightbox-2">
               <img alt="Seasonality Graph" border="0" height="256" src="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Seasonality-Graph_thumb.jpg" style="background-image: none; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin-left: auto; display: block; padding-right: 0px; margin-right: auto; border-width: 0px;" title="Seasonality Graph" width="736"/>
              </a>
              Press Get Ideas and wait a few moments for the Google keyword tool to do its thing and youll be presented with a page of results. At the top youll notice this histogram.
             </p>
             <p>
              This is the first piece of value.
             </p>
             <p>
              In general terms it highlights the seasonality of the niche you are in. In the case of this niche, we can see that there is around a 15% to 20% variance between the low point in June and a high point in January.A little local knowledge tells me that this is because the national dog show Crufts takes place in January and interests spikes up as a result. Other than that however, I would suggest that the graph above shows a pretty stable trend without a great deal of seasonality.
             </p>
             <p>
              Use seasonality data to decide when to advertise, what times of the year you might want to add more content to your site on a regular basis and importantly, not to panic should your market drop off at certain times of the year. In some niches a strong seasonal variation is perfectly normal and expected behaviour. Be aware of it and plan around it if this applies to you.
             </p>
             <h1>
              Google Tells You What Other Terms Are Relevant
              <a href="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Ad-groups-by-relevance.jpg" rel="lightbox-3">
               <img align="left" alt="Ad groups by relevance" border="0" height="627" src="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/Ad-groups-by-relevance_thumb.jpg" style="background-image: none; float: left; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin: 10px 10px 0px 0px; display: inline; padding-right: 0px; border-width: 0px;" title="Ad groups by relevance" width="292"/>
              </a>
             </h1>
             <p>
              Google are not known for being the most transparent of operations, however, a look at the suggested keywords and the keywords like expanded keyword lists that it shows you here is an insight into how they check for context and relevance on page and this information can be used for building content for PPC landing pages, optimized SEO purposes and even #tags.
             </p>
             <h6>
              Dont Over-Op Compound Phrases
             </h6>
             <p>
              Youll notice that the list here contains certain words many times over. It is important not to include the same keyword timer and again otherwise you stand a chance of getting a Panda/keyword spam slap. But there are words which are obviously associated that you should be using. Puppy is one, Behaviour is another as is course and tips.
             </p>
             <p>
              Notice that many of the phrases have a number in brackets next to them. Clicking on that number drills down and brings up a list that is just relevant to the phrase it is next to. The fourth here has the prefix Keywords like: and this is often a gold mine of good words to add context to any pages you build for your website.
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <h2>
              Drilling Down The Keyword Tool
             </h2>
             <p>
              <a href="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/The-keywords-like-expanded-list.jpg" rel="lightbox-4">
               <img alt="The keywords like expanded list" border="0" height="565" src="http://www.demondemon.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/The-keywords-like-expanded-list_thumb.jpg" style="background-image: none; float: none; padding-top: 0px; padding-left: 0px; margin-left: auto; display: block; padding-right: 0px; margin-right: auto; border-width: 0px;" title="The keywords like expanded list" width="446"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <p>
              Here we have a lot of other terms that Google has handily pre-sorted for us crate, clicker, border collie, K9 etc.
             </p>
             <h2>
              Build Your Landing Pages With These Terms
             </h2>
             <p>
              Your landing pages may have several purposes.
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               To provide a high SEO value to allow you to rank highly in organic search.
              </li>
              <li>
               Give a high quality score for Adwords (and other) PPC campaigns
              </li>
              <li>
               To engage visitors
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              There is no trick to it. Creating a page on your website that fulfils all of these functions simultaneously is quite possible from a content point of view. While you may want to have different elements such as social media call to action on a visitor engagement page and not on a sales promotion page, the content source would be quite similar, the keywords you would use to establish context and relevance both to the search engine crawlers and your visitors would be based on the same set of associated terms and phrases. Used differently? Yes, but pretty much from the same source. The Google keyword tool in Adwords is a great place to find this information.
             </p>
             <h1>
              The Commercial Information
             </h1>
             <p>
             </p>
             <center>
              <iframe allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/rfNN2eu6Vio" width="560">
              </iframe>
             </center>
             Finally we have the commercial information shown on the right hand side of the page. The average monthly searches I find it to be reasonably accurate and can be used for both deciding what might be viable to attempt to rank for in organic search, and for a rough idea of the market size for PPC.
             <p>
              Remember: You will have set a market reach before you started the keyword search in Google. In this case it was UK only and English only. Bear that in mind when looking at these figures. Global reach is often a far higher figure, much harder to rank for but of course more rewarding should you succeed.
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <h3>
              The Competition Red Herring
             </h3>
             <p>
              The competition column is often the one that causes the most heartache. It is specifically and only useful in terms of Pay Per Click. More often than not it is not useful at all for measuring organic ranking competition.
             </p>
             <p>
              For instance, in this example the word Dog is shown to have low competition..
             </p>
             <p>
              Commercially, in PPC terms this is the case. Take these results and phrases completely literally. This is people typing just the term Dog into Google and pressing return. No extra terms, no embellishment  just dog.
             </p>
             <h3>
              Every Time Someone Clicks Your Ad  You Pay
             </h3>
             <p>
              There is little or no commercial intent in the term dog. It has no buying imperative and gives a very broad set of results.
             </p>
             <p>
              Who would type this (and only this) into a search engine?
             </p>
             <p>
              A window shopper  not a buyer. Someone looking for nice pictures, or researching a paper looking for the associated Wiki page? Who knows. but almost definitely not someone looking to buy anything. Yet as an advertiser you would pay for every click in PPC if you bid for this word.
             </p>
             <h5>
              In PPC commercial intent is everything.
             </h5>
             <p>
              As soon as you add training or collar you are introducing a product or service into the mix, and some level of commerciality begins to creep in, and the suggested price per click goes up. A much higher likelihood that the searcher is, at some point, going to spend some money
             </p>
             <p>
              However, in natural search the term dog has some of the very biggest sites on the internet ranking in the top 20. WIKI, charities, international news organisations and world renown pet organisations. The competition for natural search isnt Low at all. Its incredibly high!
             </p>

               A bit like a shopkeeper with a sale sign outside the shop  and no sale once you get inside. Stop being evil Google
