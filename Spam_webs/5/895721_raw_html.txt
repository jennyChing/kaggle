      <article class="post-10605 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-book-reviews category-gallery-and-museum-art category-painting category-painting-a-day category-artist-tools-and-techniques" id="post-10605">
       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2014/12/04/daily-painting-carol-marine/" rel="bookmark">
          <em>
           Daily Painting
          </em>
          , Carol Marine
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="Daily Painting: Paint Small and Often To Become a More Creative, Productive, and Successful Artist" height="3860" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2014-12/daily_painting_450.jpg" width="450"/>
         <br/>
         Ive been following the daily painting phenomenon since
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2005/10/14/a-painting-a-day/">
          2005
         </a>
         , when I wrote about a blog called
         <em>
          <a href="http://duanekeiser.blogspot.com/">
           A Painting a Day
          </a>
         </em>
         by Virginia painter Duane Keiser.
        </p>
        <p>
         Keiser had committed himself to painting one small painting each day and posting it to his blog. I commented at the time that I thought this was a terrific idea, and lamented that I didnt have the time and discipline to follow suit.
        </p>
        <p>
         I watched with interest as other artists took up the practice, one of whom was Texas painter
         <a href="http://carolmarine.blogspot.com/">
          Carol Marine
         </a>
         , an early adopter who started her painting a day practice in 2006. I wrote about her in early
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2007/01/30/carol-marine/">
          2007
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         I continued to follow the idea, as  
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2008/08/21/painting-a-day-blogs-round-7/">
          painting a day
         </a>
          grew into a genuine internet phenomenon  part of a fundamental change in the way artists world-wide interact with their audience. (And, years later than I should have, I finally
         <a href="http://charleyparker.com/">
          joined in
         </a>
         .)
        </p>
        <p>
         In addition to taking note of new artists taking up the practice, for which painting a day became too narrow a term and daily painting is more widely applicable, Ive also watched some of the earlier adopters continue to make progress (which is, after all, the primary goal of the practice).
        </p>
        <p>
         Marine, in particular, has become noted not only for her small paintings, with their inventive compositions, geometrically strong forms and bold colors, but as one of the primary proponents of encouraging others to take up daily painting  thorough articles on her
         <a href="http://carolmarine.blogspot.com/">
          blog
         </a>
         , a series of
         <a href="http://www.dailypaintworks.com/ArtByteGalleryStore/carol-marine-1/">
          online tutorials
         </a>
         and in-person
         <a href="http://carolmarine.blogspot.com/p/workshops.html">
          workshops
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         In addition, Marine and her husband, programmer David Marine, established
         <a href="http://www.dailypaintworks.com/">
          Daily Paintworks
         </a>
         , which has become a very popular group showcase and auction system for hundreds of daily painters.
        </p>
        <p>
         She has also published a few books through online sources, but has recently published a book dedicated to the subject of daily painting through
         <a href="http://www.randomhouse.com/book/234546/daily-painting-by-carol-marine">
          Random House
         </a>
         , titled:
         <em>
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Daily-Painting-Creative-Productive-Successful/dp/0770435335/?tag=argonzark">
           Daily Painting: Paint Small and Often To Become a More Creative, Productive, and Successful Artist
          </a>
         </em>
         (Amazon link).
        </p>
        <p>
         I have to admit that as much as I enjoy Marines work, I expected a book on this topic to be somewhat lightweight, filled with lots of her appealing paintings, and a bit of breezy commentary about the practice of daily painting.
        </p>
        <p>
         I was wrong.
        </p>
        <p>
         I received a review copy of
         <em>
          Daily Painting
         </em>
         , and I was delighted to find it extensive, well thought out, beautifully designed, and dense with information.
        </p>
        <p>
         The book actually succeeds on three levels: as an introduction to the practice of daily painting and a detailed guide to following it; as a coach-like encouragement to follow through, keep on track and overcome problems like artists block; and as a basic guide to the fundamentals of oil painting.
        </p>
        <p>
         In addition to topics related directly to daily painting, such as choosing subjects, photographing and posting your paintings to a blog, promoting your work and selling small paintings online; she also does a fine job of covering painting basics like materials, composition, proportion, value, color mixing and brush work.
        </p>
        <p>
         Marines primary subject matter is still life, though she also paints landscapes and figures, and the book is rich with photos of her work; but she also draws on the work of other daily painters, such as Karin Jurick, Belinda Del Pesco, Qiang Huang, Michael Naples and a number of others, to add variety in subject matter, medium and style.
        </p>
        <p>
         Woven throughout the instruction and information is the core message of the book  and a valid and valuable one it is  summed up in the books subtitle: Paint Small and Often To Become a More Creative, Productive, and Successful Artist.
        </p>
        <p>
         She states in the initial chapter that painting small and often: minimizes emotional involvement in individual paintings, reduces fear, encourages experimentation, provides structure and promotes rapid growth as a painter.
        </p>
        <p>
         I agree wholeheartedly; and for anyone interested in taking up the practice, I highly recommend
         <em>
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Daily-Painting-Creative-Productive-Successful/dp/0770435335/?tag=argonzark">
           Daily Painting
          </a>
         </em>
         .
        </p>

         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2014/03/28/duane-keiser-update/" rel="bookmark">
          Duane Keiser (update)
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="Duane Keiser" height="5362" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2014-03/keiser_450.jpg" width="450"/>
         <br/>
         I first wrote about Virginia painter Duane Keiser back in
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2005/10/14/a-painting-a-day/">
          2005
         </a>
         , when I noticed his blog,
         <em>
          <a href="http://duanekeiser.blogspot.com/p/about.html">
           a painting a day
          </a>
         </em>
         , on which he was featuring small, postcard size paintings  one a day as he painted them on a makeshift cigar box easel  and placing them for sale on eBay.
        </p>
        <p>
         At the time, this was a novel idea, and I dont think Keiser, or anyone else, could have anticipated that it would blossom into the painting a day phenomenon, or that it would help pave the way for a fundamental change in the way large numbers of artists would come to use the internet to connect directly with collectors.
        </p>
        <p>
         Since then, I reported on Keiser at various points as I chronicled the growth of the painting a day approach, and Ive also more recently highlighted a couple of his interesting experiments in repainting the same canvas repeatedly (see my links below).
        </p>
        <p>
         It occurred to me, however, that Ive been remiss in not revisiting Keisers continuing work as a painter, particularly his ongoing posts to
         <em>
          <a href="http://duanekeiser.blogspot.com/">
           a painting a day
          </a>
         </em>
         , which are a continual delight.
        </p>
        <p>
         You can also see his work on his
         <a href="http://www.duanekeiser.com/">
          website
         </a>
         , and a number of interesting videos of his process on
         <a href="http://www.youtube.com/user/duanekeiser/videos">
          YouTube
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Keiser has a keen eye for subtle color, a command of painterly textures and a finessed attention to edges. His seemingly simple subjects quietly reveal themselves as sophisticated balancing acts of suggestion and definition, dynamically playing one element against another within a unified, understated whole.
        </p>

        <h1 class="entry-title">
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2014/02/18/duane-keisers-transitory-paintings/" rel="bookmark">
          Duane Keisers Transitory Paintings
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="Duane Keiser's Transitory Paintings" height="3572" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2014-02/keiser_transitory_450.jpg" width="450"/>
         <br/>
         Its not uncommon for artists to paint over, or scrape off and repaint, existing paintings. Oil paintings, in particular, lend themselves to this process, and a number of historical paintings have been shown by art forensics to have been painted over or repainted many times.
        </p>
        <p>
         Normally, the goal is a finished painting that is better in some way than the previous version. However, Duane Keiser, a painter I have written about previously here on
         <em>
          <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2005/10/14/a-painting-a-day/">
           Lines and Colors
          </a>
         </em>
         ,  has taken the concept of repainting in a different direction.
        </p>
        <p>
         Keiser has been engaged in a daily painting practice for a long time, and is in fact the originator of the 
         <a href="http://duanekeiser.blogspot.com">
          Painting a Day
         </a>
          phenomenon, as started on his blog of the same name back in 2004.
        </p>
        <p>
         In 2011, I reported on his video of a painting experiment called
         <em>
          <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2011/03/19/duane-keisers-peel/">
           Peel
          </a>
         </em>
         , in which he painted a tangerine as he peeled it  showing the different stages of peeling the fruit in multiple images 
         <em>
          on the same panel
         </em>
         , painting and repainting over images that most other painters might have considered finished (and salable).
        </p>
        <p>
         I marveled at the artistic confidence involved in a process like that, and I marvel at it again in Keisers recent experimental
         <em>
          <a href="http://www.duanekeiser.com/#!transient/c1q78">
           Transitory Paintings
          </a>
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         In these two separate panels, Keiser has painted a landscape and a room interior of his studio. In each case, he is repeatedly repainting the same panel to reflect changing conditions of his subject in different seasons, light and time of day.
        </p>
        <p>
         Keiser has a slowly changing slideshow of each painting in various stages on his
         <a href="http://www.duanekeiser.com/#!transient/c1q78">
          website
         </a>
         . Keep in mind while viewing these (and the screen captures Ive posted above) that these are not a series of related paintings, but single paintings that are being repainted over and over.
        </p>
        <p>
         The landscape has actually been sold, to friends of the artist, with the understanding that Keiser will occasionally remove the painting, paint over it with a new version of the scene, and then return it to them  a constantly evolving painting.
        </p>

      <article class="post-7037 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-displaying-art-on-the-web category-painting category-painting-a-day category-artist-tools-and-techniques" id="post-7037">
       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2013/02/06/my-turn-as-a-painterblogger/" rel="bookmark">
          My turn as a painter/blogger
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="Chaley Parker painting" height="337" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2013-02/charleyparker_com_ridley_creek_shadows_450" width="450"/>
         <br/>
         Back in 2005,  the year in which I began writing
         <em>
          <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2005/10/14/a-painting-a-day/">
           Lines and Colors
          </a>
         </em>
         , I reported about an artist from Virginia named
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2005/10/14/a-painting-a-day/">
          Duane Keiser
         </a>
         , who had the year before begun a practice of painting a small painting every day, posting it to a blog titled 
         <a href="http://duanekeiser.blogspot.com/">
          a painting a day
         </a>
          and placing it up for auction on eBay.
        </p>
        <p>
         It seemed a unique thing at the time, and as far as I can establish, Keiser was indeed the originator of the practice.
        </p>
        <p>
         In my article, I remarked on what a great idea this was, and how I wished I could emulate the discipline. It promised the kind of advancement as a painter that only regular painting can provide, aided by the incentive of selling the paintings as they were done.
        </p>
        <p>
         The following year, I reported on others who had become 
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2006/04/03/painting-a-day-blogs/">
          painting a day
         </a>
          participants, like Karin Jurick, David R. Darrow, Shelly Grund, Elin Pendleton and, in particular,
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2006/11/16/julian-merrow-smith/">
          Julian Merrow-Smith
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Over the years since then, Ive watched and reported on the burgeoning of the painting a day  phenomenon, although it has become so widespread and generalized that the term has lost much of its meaning, and I now tend to use the more general term painter/bloggers.
        </p>
        <p>
         This is a practice in which hundreds of artists, at all levels of experience, are painting and posting their work on blogs and connecting with buyers through online auctions, PayPal payments, Etsy shops, group sites like Daily Paintworks and other means of direct sales  either bypassing or supplementing the traditional gallery system.
        </p>
        <p>
         After following this for some time, and having gradually brought my own painting practice somewhat up to speed, Ive decided to jump in and try it for myself.
        </p>
        <p>
         The regimen of painting every day remains appealing, and the idea of selling the work makes it more likely I will adhere to the practice and hopefully will allow me to devote more time to painting.
        </p>
        <h4>
         What Ive learned and decided so far.
        </h4>
        <p>
         Ive tried to observe some of the best practices from those who have been doing this for a while; and as I begin my process, Ill try to make occasional reports on
         <em>
          Lines and Colors
         </em>
         for those who might be interested in what I learn.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive also tried to follow my own advice in my article on 
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2007/05/31/how-not-to-display-your-artwork-on-the-web/">
          How Not to Display Your Artwork on the Web
         </a>
          and the follow up series on 
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/how-to-display-your-artwork-on-the-web/">
          How to Display your Art on the Web
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         So far Ive established a few things that seem basic and essential, and Ive also made a few decisions to vary from the mainstream.
        </p>
        <p>
         Though I endeavor to paint every day, my goal for posting finished paintings will not be a painting a day but a more modest one of two small paintings a week, which I will try to post consistently on Mondays and Wednesdays. [Addendum: Ive changed this to Mondays and Thursdays.]
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Chaley Parker painting  blog" height="336" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2013-02/charleyparker_com_450" width="450"/>
        </p>
        <h4>
         The blog
        </h4>
        <p>
         Like most painter/bloggers, Ive created a
         <a href="http://charleyparker.com/">
          blog
         </a>
         , on which I will post my paintings as I put them up for auction.
        </p>
        <p>
         Unlike many, however, Ive decided that on that blog I will
         <em>
          only
         </em>
         post paintings, and not water it down with posts about works in progress, studio photos, the new brushes I just got, the weather, or any other topics not directly related to the paintings. I will continue to insist that the home page of a website, or the top of a blogs home page, should always be aimed at the
         <em>
          first-time visitor
         </em>
         , not those familiar with the site who are returning.
        </p>
        <p>
         For the design of the blog itself, Ive deliberately chosen a dark neutral background, as I think it shows off the color of images to best advantage. Many painters without design experience dont realize how much a colored web page background, that may seem appealing for the blog itself, can compete with and detract from the presentation of their work.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive made a point of dedicating the sidebar to introducing myself, and describing the intention of the site, the size, medium and support of the paintings, the process, and my policies for selling and shipping the paintings. Ive also made sure to provide a contact email address.
        </p>
        <p>
         The current painting will appear at the top of the page, and visitors will have the ability to click on the image to view it large, or click on a link to view or leave comments. Comments will not only add interest for those visiting the site, but hopefully provided informative feedback on the paintings.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Chaley Parker painting  on eBay" height="336" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2013-02/charleyparker_ebay_450" width="450"/>
        </p>
        <h4>
         The auctions
        </h4>
        <p>
         Ive chosen to use eBay for the auctions, primarily because I already have an account and there is no upfront expense as with the
         <a href="http://www.dailypaintworks.com/original-fine-art-auctions">
          Daily Paintworks auctions
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Most of my paintings are 57 or 68, and in keeping with the kind of size-based scale I would apply to gallery pricing, Ive decided to set their auction minimums at $100 and $125, respectively.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive also decided to limit my auction periods to 3 days, though this is one of the elements of the process of which Im least confident, so it may change. [Addendum: Yes, changed to seven days.]
        </p>
        <p>
         On the blog, Ive provided a consistent line below each painting on the entry to show the paintings status: At Auction, Available or Sold, and a clear link to the auction.
        </p>
        <p>
         There will be an archive of past paintings, sold or otherwise, and a page for available works that are not at auction. Paintings not sold at auction will be priced at what I would consider gallery minimums at $200 for the 57 and $250 for the 68.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Contact and promotion
        </h4>
        <p>
         Near the top of the blog, Ive included a sign-up for a newsletter, by which those interested can receive an email notice whenever I post a new painting.
        </p>
        <p>
         For this, Ive chosen to use
         <a href="http://mailchimp.com/">
          MailChimp
         </a>
         , which allows you to create and manage an email newsletter list for up to 2,000 subscribers and 12,000 emails per month
         <a href="http://mailchimp.com/pricing/">
          for free
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive created a new email address, specifically for contact through the painting blog.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive created a new Twitter account,
         <a href="https://twitter.com/CParkerArt">
          @CParkerArt
         </a>
         , specifically for announcing paintings as I post them. I had to log out of my Lines and Colors Twitter account to create the new account. Im using
         <a href="http://tweetdeck.com/">
          TweetDeck
         </a>
         to manage multiple Twitter accounts now that theyre established.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ive also given the blog some basic search engine optimization (more on that later) and I will probably leverage my ability to place an ad on my own general topic blog (i.e. this one) to perhaps send some visitors, and some Google love, to my new painting blog.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Waiting
        </h4>
        <p>
         One thing Ive decided to do differently than most painter/bloggers is to delay posting the paintings for several weeks.
        </p>
        <p>
         Common practice is to post a painting as soon as its done, hot off the easel, so to speak. This, however, means that the purchaser, whether direct or through an auction process, must usually wait several weeks for an oil painting to dry enough to be shipped.
        </p>
        <p>
         At the risk of appearing out of sync with the seasons, particularly as they change, Ive decided to wait three to four weeks for the paintings to dry, and then apply a light coat of retouch varnish  with another week for that to dry, before posting the paintings and putting them up for auction.
        </p>
        <p>
         This will allow me to ship the paintings as as soon as the auction closes. Ive tried to weigh the the plusses and minuses of both approaches, but if I buy something, Im much happier when I receive it sooner rather than later.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Varnishing
        </h4>
        <p>
         My decision to varnish the paintings is as much a desire to even out the paintings surface appearance as to provide protection. I seldom use medium when painting, so even though Im using a very high quality paint (more on that later), passages in which Ive used thinly applied chromatic blacks in particular (e.g. Alizarin plus Viridian or Ultramarine Blue plus Burnt Umber) can look flat or sunken in compared to more thickly applied areas of brighter color.
        </p>
        <p>
         After a bit of research, Ive decided to use
         <a href="http://www.gamblincolors.com/oil.painting.techniques/varnishes.html">
          Gamblins Gamvar
         </a>
         synthetic resin varnish, thinned with odorless mineral spirits (OMS to varnish 5:1), to act as a retouch varnish. This can be applied much sooner than a final varnish as it allows the paint to continue to dry. A light coating of this also has much less gloss than a full coat of final varnish.
        </p>
        <h4>
         Photographing the paintings
        </h4>
        <p>
         Unfortunately, I cant say Ive arrived at a completely satisfactory method of photographing the paintings. Ill go into more detail on what Im doing in that respect in a future post.
        </p>
        <h4>
         The start
        </h4>
        <p>
         Ive been pretty consistent in recent weeks with painting every day and finishing two small painting each week. Ive just posted
         <a href="http://www.charleyparker.com/">
          my first painting
         </a>
         (above, top), which was painted at a nearby state park several weeks ago (with a few finishing touches in the studio).
        </p>
        <h4>
         Progress reports
        </h4>
        <p>
         Ill try to occasionally report on my progress here, as I learn what works for me and what doesnt.
        </p>
        <p>
         Of course, Im always open to comments or suggestions from others who have engaged in the process.
        </p>
        <p>
         Its all about learning.
        </p>

          Link:
         </span>

          www.charleyparker.com
         </a>

      <article class="post-4860 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-gallery-and-museum-art category-painting-a-day" id="post-4860">
       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         <a href="http://linesandcolors.com/2011/10/21/karin-juricks-museum-hours/" rel="bookmark">
          Karin Juricks
          <em>
           Museum Hours
          </em>
         </a>
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <img alt="Karin Jurick's Museum Hours" height="3087" src="http://www.linesandcolors.com/images/2011-10/jurick_450.jpg" width="450"/>
         <br/>
         Who would think that paintings of people with their backs to you could be so compelling?
        </p>
        <p>
         When I first wrote about Atlanta based painter Karin Jurick back in
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2006/05/24/karin-jurick/">
          2006
         </a>
         , one of the things I admired, in addition to her bright, fresh, painterly approach, was her series of paintings of art museum patrons, in situ, as it were, their backs to the viewer as they stood engrossed in the artwork before them.
        </p>
        <p>
         This has turned out to be one of Juricks favored themes in the subsequent years, and she has recently published a collection of over 100 of those paintings in a book titled
         <em>
          <a href="http://www.blurb.com/bookstore/detail/2565201">
           Museum Hours
          </a>
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         You can see a
         <a href="http://www.blurb.com/books/2565201">
          preview
         </a>
         of the book on the Blurb site; be sure to use the controls below the image to view the preview in full screen mode, allowing you the pleasure of seeing Juricks work larger than it is usually reproduced on her
         <a href="http://www.karinjurick.com/">
          site
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://karinjurick.blogspot.com/">
          blog
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         The book has a nice feature in the form of an illustrated index of the paintings. In addition to the titles of her paintings, it lists the works being viewed by the patron, (of which Jurick has painted at least a partial interpretation), and the museums in which they are to be found.
        </p>
        <p>
         Juricks work is part of a three-woman show, along with
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2010/01/06/karen-hollingsworth-update/">
          Karen Hollingsworth
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://suzyschultz.net/">
          Suzy Shultz
         </a>
         ,  at the 16
         <a href="http://16patton.com/">
          Patton Gallery
         </a>
         in Ashville, North Carolina that opens tomorrow, October 22, 2011. I dont know how long it runs; the gallerys website, such as it is, hasnt been updated with show information and does a poor job of presenting the artists they represent. [
         <strong>
          Addendum:
         </strong>
         Jurick was kind enough to let me know the show runs until November 26, 2011. See also my recent post on
         <a href="http://www.linesandcolors.com/2011/10/21/new-series-from-karen-hollingsworth/">
          Karen Hollingsworth
         </a>
         .]
        </p>
        <p>
         You can see a preview of Juricks pieces in the show on her
         <a href="http://www.karinjurick.com/ZemArt/16pnewyorklife.html">
          website
         </a>
         . She has chosen a theme of New York Life for her part of the show, and the selection includes some of her art patrons pieces, as well as other New York scenes (love the Flatiron Building).
        </p>
