      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       How "Uptown Funk" Proves Bruno Mars Is Exactly Like Coldplay
      </h1>
      <div class="previous_page">
       Continued from page 2
      </div>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">
        Comment Now
       </a>
       <div class="comments_follow followable_block">

         <div class="follow_unireg">
          <a class="follow_unireg_link" href="javascript://follow">

     <div class="article_body article" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         Why do I find this insidious? Because if Mars did indeed assume Hathaways style without acknowledgment, then he has, in fact, usurped the tragic essence of a
         <a href="http://www.bet.com/music/photos/2012/12/music-and-suicide-a-tragic-history.html#!071712-music-best-rb-albums-Donny-Hathaway">
          paranoid schizophrenic
         </a>
         , one who dealt with the twin problems of mental illness and
         <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2013/12/donny-hathaways-way-underrated-tragically-brief-soul-catalog/282046/">
          relative obscurity
         </a>
         by jumping off the fifteenth floor of the Essex Hotel (at least,
         <a href="http://lonesomebeehive.wordpress.com/2011/05/01/rock-n-roll-suicide-donny-hathaway/">
          according to this post
         </a>
         ). Something about this does not sit well with me.
        </p>
        <p>
         But lets move on: Locked Out of Heaven, as you all know, was the best
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9p6LwqcqwKc">
          Police song in years
         </a>
         . It was also a particularly canny move for Mars, a pervasive
         <a href="http://thesmeezingtons.com/">
          industry insider
         </a>
         by 2012, and someone with a shrewd ear for looming trends. As such, its not unreasonable to speculate that he wouldve sensed the impending explosion of Gotyes similarly Sting-like Somebody That I Used To Know, recorded one year prior.
        </p>
        <p>
         Presently, at the tail end of 2014, we have Uptown Funk, borrowing from Princeser
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xect030DfSQ">
          fictionalized competitors
         </a>
         : Morris Day and the Time, and their classic,
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P9DdexHiGmU">
          Jungle Love
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         Same beat? Check.
        </p>
        <p>
         Same instrumentation? Pretty much.
        </p>
        <p>
         Same call-and-response structure? You bet.
        </p>
        <p>
         Same key? Almosttheyre a half-step apart, as thoughone was trying to sneak away from the other.
        </p>
        <p>
         You might say, but this last example was Mark Ronsons doing; Mars was only a feature. You might even call me out on the usage of Billionaire for similar reasons.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, the line between feature and artist on Uptown Funk is blurry, considering that Brunos is the only melodic voice we hear, and that we would need someone to point outthat the bloke playing guitar in the video is, in fact, Mark Ronson.
        </p>
        <p>
         In either instance (Uptown Funk or Billionaire), what you might see as a critic fudging the examples Id classify differentlyId call it further proof that Mars tends to hide behind other peoples curtains when donning other peoples caps.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now that we have demonstrated just farhismimicrycan spread, lets return to our premise: what does any of this have to do with Coldplay?
        </p>
        <p>
         In a previous article, I claimed, with
         <a href="http://time.com/99458/coldplay-ghost-stories-album-review/">
          citation
         </a>
         , that Coldplay remains popular because they continue to strive to rip off better music; when they reach a plateau of influence, theyll call in their influences influencespeople like Brian Enoto help crystalize their results.
        </p>
        <p>
         Later on, I remarked that Coldplay has fooled us all: they are not a band, but a servicethey are aggregators of other,
         <em>
          better
         </em>
         music; what is more, the music they represent is one that carries a vastly different sound (and ethos) than what has been driving the pop charts in recent years.
        </p>
        <p>
         I believe the same can be said for Bruno Mars: he is a portal to an older, more timeless musicsomething quite different from the pop we see today, a genre in which Mars, when hes not mimicking others (and this does happen on occasion, Ill admit) is also a complicit agitator (Grenade, Just The Way You Are).
        </p>
        <p>
         Okay, so both artists deal in rip-offs much of the timeat least, in their exportable singles. This has been provenat least, to my standards.
        </p>
        <p>
         But so what?
        </p>
        <p>
         Ripping something off is, intrinsically, neither bad nor good; as the old adage goes (
         <a href="http://www.brainyquote.com/quotes/quotes/i/igorstravi155783.html">
          a paraphrase of Igor Stravinsky
         </a>
         , I might add) good composers borrow, great composers steal.
        </p>
        <p>
         Furthermore, borrowing material as often as Mars does not put him on the same footing as Coldplay: many artists are, at some level, rip-off acts. Why arent we singling out Lady Gaga (Madonna, Cher, Marilyn Manson) or Muse (Radiohead), or Fun (
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AGNzG6hwbPg">
          Paul Simon
         </a>
         , Queen) or Lorde (Jeff Buckley, Imogen Heap), Or Hozier (
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I2uVRMBD5RY">
          Feist
         </a>
         ) or for that matter, Procol Harum (
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mb3iPP-tHdA">
          Johann Sebastian Bach
         </a>
         )?
        </p>
        <p>
         For two reasons: because those who only rip-off other artists (without adding anything to the pot) dont usually get this much time in the zeitgeist before disappearing into obscurity; and because neither Bruno Mars nor Coldplay have done much to evolve upon the foundation of their influenceswhereas Gaga, Fun, Lorde, Hozier, and Procol Harum all have.
        </p>
        <p>
         In Coldplays case, I believe the lack of innovative evidence speaks for itselfand if you think thats lazy of me, consider I provided ruminations on this in a
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/nickmessitte/2014/05/16/how-coldplay-is-providing-you-with-a-service-and-why-you-should-be-grateful/">
          previous piece
         </a>
         , and that, at some point, this article does indeed have to end.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the case of Bruno Mars, I feel the evidence is apparent in the aforementioned examples, but if you need furtherproof, take a gander at
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e-fA-gBCkj0">
          most
         </a>
         of the
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ekzHIouo8Q4">
          videos
         </a>
         for the
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nPvuNsRccVw">
          songs
         </a>
         off
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AHDtXqjgEj4">
          <em>
           Unorthodox Jukebox
          </em>
         </a>
         they are recreations part-and-parcel with his sound, but are so composed, so wink-wink, and ultimately, so precious that they are basically little Wes Anderson movies, minus the endearing presences of Bill Murray, Owen Wilson, Jason Schwartzman, and Wes Anderson.
        </p>
        <p>
         Think of it in terms of simple math (or maths, if youre reading this from across the pond): taking the above examples into consideration, its hard to argue against the assertion that both Bruno Mars and Coldplay are only the sum of their influences, and nothing more.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Their
         </em>
         influences, on the other hand, achieved icon status by being
         <em>
          more
         </em>
         than the sums of their respective parts: Michael Jackson is undeniably more than
         <a href="https://darrencostello1.wordpress.com/2013/02/12/who-inspired-michael-jackson/">
          James Brown
         </a>
         ; Prince is undeniably
         <a href="http://www.popmatters.com/feature/94059-maybe-im-just-like-my-father-princes-anxiety-of-influence-and-purple-/">
          more than Michael Jackson
         </a>
         ; Jeff Buckley is undeniably more than
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8CvgIWG5rk8">
          Led Zeppelin
         </a>
         or
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ysIK-cMhD-s">
          Nusrat Fateh Ali Kahn
         </a>
         ; Lorde is undeniably more than
         <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/nickmessitte/2013/12/04/pure-heroine-we-found-lorde-in-a-hopeless-place/">
          Jeff Buckley
         </a>
         ; Hozier is undeniably more than Feist; Radiohead is undeniably
         <a href="http://colin-greenwood-interviews.blogspot.com/">
          more than U2
         </a>
         ; U2 is undeniably more than
         <a href="http://rockhall.com/blog/post/5-essential-peter-gabriel-songs-rock-hall-2014/">
          Peter Gabriel
         </a>
         (indeed, is more than a lot of its separate influences, from the Ramones to David Bowie).
        </p>
        <p>
         Coldplay and Bruno Mars, on the other hand, are only iterations, as illustrated by the above examples (in Marss case) and plenty of other online literature (in the
         <a href="http://www.dailyrecord.co.uk/entertainment/music/music-news/new-single-from-british-band-coldplay-1104672">
          case
         </a>
         of
         <a href="http://www.torontosun.com/2011/06/04/coldplay-in-copycat-flap-again">
          Coldplay
         </a>
         ).
        </p>
        <p>
         Now, having made these pejorative points, lets put a positive spin on it, for contrary to the stream of mitigated negatives I have loosed upon your brain, I do not believe Brunospresence (nor that of Coldplay) in the mainstream to be an evil phenomenon:
        </p>
        <p>
         No. I would argue that the existencenot just the existence, but the enduring popularityof these two entities are important and even inherently
         <em>
          good
         </em>
         things.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whats important about Uptown Funk is not how much cultural currency Mark Ronson has, but how much social potency Morris Day and the Time still have.
        </p>
        <p>
         What matters about Billionaire is not how much cultural significance Travie McCoy has, but how much societal vigor Sublime still retains.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whats jaw dropping about Locked Out of Heaven is not how much cache Bruno Mars has, but how much relevance the Police still wield.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whats fantastic about the success of Magic is not the popularity Coldplay evince, but the staying power a
         <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A6kB3pPU_io">
          mournful, heart-on-his-sleeve
         </a>
         Eric Claptonmediating on the most awful of personal subjects (the death of ones child)still maintains.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whats interesting about all of this is what it says about you: no matter how sour the pop music of today tastes, you havent let it beat you down; you still yearn for yesterdays majesty, and youll take it anywhere you can find it.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         *
         <b>
          An earlier version of this article did not include the link between Hoziers Take Me To Church and Feists How Come You Never Go Thereone of whichseems to borrow from the other, though not lyrically (Many thanks to @ChillyGonzales on twitter for pointing this out). The previousversionalso mis-labelled the comedian Steve Byrne as Steve Holmes (Many thanks to JC in the comments for pointing this out).
         </b>
        </p>
