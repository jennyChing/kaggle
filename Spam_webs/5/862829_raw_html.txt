     <h1 class="noshow">
      Top Sights of Belfast and Northern Ireland  | Travelhoppers
     </h1>
     <div class="equal" id="mainContent">
      <div class="innertube">
       <div class="entry equal">
        <h2 class="travelhoppin" style="font-size:24px;">
         Top Sights of Belfast and Northern Ireland
        </h2>
        <div id="summary">
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.travelhoppers.com/category/adventure/">
           <span class="featureCategory">
            Adventure
           </span>
          </a>
          <br/>
          Posted by:
          <strong>
           <a href="http://www.travelhoppers.com/author/megan-eaves/" rel="author" title="Posts by Megan Eaves">
            Megan Eaves
           </a>
          </strong>
         </p>
        </div>
        <p id="storyDate">
         Monday, July 18, 2011
        </p>
        <div class="innertube">
         <!-- Start Shareaholic LikeButtonSetTop Automatic -->
         <!-- End Shareaholic LikeButtonSetTop Automatic -->
         <p>
          Traveling through the back roads of Northern Ireland or dining in one of Belfasts contemporary restaurants, you would never know that the country was once plagued by violence. The Northern Ireland of today is quite a different place to the images that filled the worlds TV screens in the 70s and 80s, when The Troubles were at their worst. In fact, these days, Northern Ireland is positively serene, and the fact that it is still developing as a travel destination makes it the ideal place for history buffs and adventurous types seeking an authentic view of the country.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Photo by Jeff Meade" class="size-full wp-image-5670 alignright" height="159" src="http://www.travelhoppers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/Photo-by-Jeff-Meade.jpg" style="margin: 15px;" width="284"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          One of the four countries in the United Kingdom, Northern Ireland is often mistaken as being part of the Republic of Ireland, from which it split in 1922 during the creation of the Irish Free State. Six northern counties opted to remain part of the United Kingdom, while the rest of Ireland formed its own self-governed republic. Since then, many people in Northern Ireland have been divided on the issue of remaining part of the U.K. or reuniting with the rest of the island of Ireland. Much of this history, and indeed the history of violence in Northern Ireland, is preserved in the many political murals that decorate outdoor wall spaces across the country, especially in Belfast and the city of Londonderry (known to Irish Nationalists as Derry).
         </p>
         <h1>
          Visiting Northern Ireland
         </h1>
         <h2>
          Belfast
         </h2>
         <p>
          <img alt="Photo by Flickr user rebekah.grmela" class="size-full wp-image-5673 alignright" height="204" src="http://www.travelhoppers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/Photo-by-Flickr-user-rebekah.grmela.jpg" style="margin: 15px;" width="272"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Belfast is the capital city of Northern Ireland and the second largest city on the island of Ireland, after Dublin. Though cosmopolitan and modern, with all the typical British high street shops and bustling contemporary restaurants, the historical weight of the violence that happened here still hangs over the city like an old coat. The best way to get a feel for Belfast is to take a city bus tour or a private 
          <strong>
           black taxi tour
          </strong>
           (conducted in old, London-style taxis), which allows you to get up close with the sectarian neighborhoods where violence was once prevalent, giving you an amazing view of the close proximity in which the two different sides lived along the parallel
          <strong>
           Falls and Shankhill Roads
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          But Belfast goes well beyond war tourism, with a number of historic sites that pre-date The Troubles, sometimes by centuries. For example, few people realize that it was in Belfasts harbor that the ill-fated Titanic was built and is now commemorated in the
          <strong>
           Titanics Dock and Pumphouse
          </strong>
          , a museum dedicated to the citys early shipbuilding trade. The citys fine, late 19th century
          <strong>
           City Hall
          </strong>
          overlooks numerous high street stores from Donegall Square, while the grand
          <strong>
           St. Annes Cathedral
          </strong>
           a re-imagined Romanesque church  is the citys Church of Ireland (Protestant) cathedral.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Photo by Ross Hawkes" class="size-full wp-image-5672 alignright" height="237" src="http://www.travelhoppers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/Photo-by-Ross-Hawkes.jpg" style="margin: 15px;" width="315"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Architecture buffs should also take a stroll through the opulent grounds of
          <strong>
           Queens University
          </strong>
          in South Belfast, with its perfectly manicured lawns and well preserved Victorian buildings. Adjacent, the
          <strong>
           Botanic Gardens
          </strong>
          offer a peaceful place to spend the afternoon.
         </p>
         <h2>
         </h2>
         <h2>
         </h2>
         <h2>
          Beyond Belfast
         </h2>
         <p>
          Belfast certainly holds a lot of interest for history buffs, but the countryside and coastlines are the real draws of Northern Ireland. The rugged north coast, which butts up to the miry Straits of Moyle, is of particular interest for its many sweeping views and, of course, the mythic
          <strong>
           Giants Causeway
          </strong>
          , a UNESCO World Heritage Site made up of interlocking volcanic basalt columns. Legend has it that the columns were once part of a bridge built by a mythic Irish giant, Finn McCool, to cross the Straits and conquer his Scottish counterpart.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Photo by Yves Cosentino" class="size-full wp-image-5676 alignleft" height="213" src="http://www.travelhoppers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/Photo-by-Yves-Cosentino.jpg" style="margin: 15px;" width="284"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          The nearby village of
          <strong>
           Bushmills
          </strong>
          is home to the
          <strong>
           Old Bushmills Distillery
          </strong>
          , which brews Bushmills Irish Whiskey in one of the oldest working distilleries in Ireland.
         </p>
         <p>
          The southern counties of Northern Ireland also hold their share of delights. The rolling green countryside here is akin to posh southern England or Ireland, with well-groomed manor estates and quaint cottages. The village of
          <strong>
           Enniskellin
          </strong>
          in Co. Fermanagh is a quaint town situated on the banks of massive Loch Erne, which is a popular place for water sports and recreational activities. Enniskillen Castle is an incredibly well-preserved 16th century fortification that now houses the Fermanagh County Museum and a number of military history exhibitions.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Photo by Flickr user Jule_Berlin" class="size-full wp-image-5678 alignright" height="257" src="http://www.travelhoppers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/07/Photo-by-Flickr-user-Jule_Berlin.jpg" style="margin: 15px;" width="194"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          To really get away from it all, the
          <strong>
           Moune Mountains
          </strong>
          (pictured top) in Co. Down rise up from the shores of the Irish Sea into the highest peaks in Northern Ireland. Here, walking paths lead through misty vales, revealing remains of old cottages and ancient ruins.
         </p>
         <p>
          <a href="http://www.travelhoppers.com/specials/?country=131102" target="_blank">
           <img alt="Ireland Travel Specials" class="size-full wp-image-5515 alignleft" height="133" src="http://www.travelhoppers.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/06/special-frog-ireland1.gif" style="margin-top: 15px; margin-bottom: 15px;" title="special frog ireland" width="200"/>
          </a>
         </p>
