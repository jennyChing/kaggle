     <h1 class="Heading1 headsingle">
      10 of the Earths Strangest Yet Most Beautiful Landscapes
     </h1>
    </header>

       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Blood-Falls-Antarctica.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 1 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Blood Falls, Antarctica
         </h2>
         <p>
          Blood Falls' grisly appearance comes from its iron-laden waters, which rust when they come in contact with the air, reddening the briny outflow as it trickles down Taylor Glacier onto ice-covered West Lake Bonney.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Chocolate-Hills-Philippines.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 2 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Chocolate Hills, Philippines
         </h2>
         <p>
          Measuring up to 400 feet tall, the hills are made of limestone containing marine fossils dating back millions of years. The hills' origins remain a mystery, but legend says that a giant wept them as he grieved the death of his human beloved. Today, the confectionary landscape remains a popular tourist attraction and awaits approval as a UNESCO World Heritage Monument.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Fly-Geyser.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 3 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Fly Geyser
         </h2>
         <p>
          Spewing scalding water in all directions, the aptly named Fly Geyser sits about 10 miles from Nevada's Black Rock Desert. The water here can measure a piping 200 degrees!
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Giants-Causeway-Northern-Ireland.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 4 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Giant's Causeway, Northern Ireland
         </h2>
         <p>
          Northern Ireland's only UNESCO World Heritage Site consists of some 40,000 hexagonal basalt columns, which jut from the North Channel along the edge of the Antrim Plateau. During a period of intense volcanic activity about 50 to 60 million years ago, differences in the lava cooling rate caused the columns to form, while further weathering created circular formations nicknamed "giant's eyes".
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Kilauea-Volcano-Hawaii.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 5 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Kilauea Volcano, Hawaii
         </h2>
         <p>
          One of the world's most active and perilous volcanoes, Kilauea has been erupting for more than three decades, fitfully coughing basaltic lava into the Pacific Ocean below. You can easily spot the billowing plumes of scorching gas in the daytime. But if you can, visit after sunset, when the lava flows glow more visibly, creating a beautifully infernal light show.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Lake-Hillier-Australia.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 6 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Lake Hillier, Australia
         </h2>
         <p>
          Lake Hillier sits like a giant punchbowl at the edge of Middle Island in Western Australia's Recherche Archipelago, surrounded by a thick forest of paperbark and eucalyptus trees. Theories differ on the origins of the lake's bubblegum hue. Some believe it comes from a dye produced by microorganisms, while others suspect the red halophilic bacteria that thrive in the lake's salt deposits. In any case, the lake's color isn't a trick of the light - it's positively pink, no matter where you look.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Midway-Geyser-Basin-Yellowstone-National-Park-Wyoming.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 7 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Midway Geyser Basin, Yellowstone National Park, Wyoming
         </h2>
         <p>
          The smallest of Yellowstone's geyser basins, Midway Geyser Basin (also dubbed "Hell's Half Acre") actually contains two of the park's largest hydrothermal features: Grand Prismatic Spring and Excelsior Geyser. The spring's psychedelic coloration comes from pigmented bacteria in the surrounding microbial mats.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Mono-Lake-California.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 8 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Mono Lake, California
         </h2>
         <p>
          Twisting limestone pinnacles, called tufa towers, line its shores, some reaching heights of over 30 feet. Tufa towers grow only underwater, but Los Angeles' diversion of Mono Lake's tributary streams beginning in 1941 exposed the gnarled formations. Mono Lake, which is at least 760,000 years old, has no outlet to the ocean, causing salt to accumulate and create harsh alkaline conditions.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Spotted-Lake-British-Columbia.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 9 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Spotted Lake, British Columbia
         </h2>
         <p>
          British Columbia's Spotted Lake is landlocked, resulting in salty, alkaline waters. Over the summer, much of the water dries, leaving behind a lava lamp pattern of mineral "spots" that can appear white, pale yellow, green, or blue, depending on their composition. Though the lake is fenced off, it is visible from the highway.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="oneByOne_item">
        <div class="slide_img">
         <img alt="" src="http://www.todayoutlook.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/Zhangjiajie-National-Park-China.jpg"/>
        </div>
        <div class="slide_content">
         <p>
          Viewing 10 of 10
         </p>
         <h2 class="Heading4 title">
          Zhangjiajie National Park, China
         </h2>
         <p>
          Years of erosion by vegetation and expanding ice carved Zhangjiajie National Park's narrow, terraced sandstone pillars, some of which climb over 650 feet. Zhangjiajie National ParkChina's first national parkis one of many within the Wulingyuan Scenic and Historic Interest Area in Hunan Province, named a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1992.
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>
