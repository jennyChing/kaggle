     <p>
      A
      <b>
       blue hole
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">
       cave
      </a>
      (inland) or underwater
      <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">
       sinkhole
      </a>
      . They are also called
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_cave" title="Vertical cave">
       vertical caves
      </a>
      . There are many different blue holes located around the world, typically in low-lying coastal regions. The best known examples can be found in
      <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">
       Belize
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">
       Bahamas
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">
       Guam
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      (in the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">
       Great Barrier Reef
      </a>
      ), and
      <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
       Egypt
      </a>
      (in the
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Hole_(Red_Sea)" title="Blue Hole (Red Sea)">
       Red Sea
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Blue holes are roughly circular, steep-walled depressions, and so named for the dramatic contrast between the dark blue, deep waters of their depths and the lighter blue of the shallows around them. Their water circulation is poor, and they are commonly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anoxic_sea_water" title="Anoxic sea water">
       anoxic
      </a>
      below a certain depth; this environment is unfavorable for most sea life, but nonetheless can support large numbers of
      <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">
       bacteria
      </a>
      . The deep blue color is caused by the high transparency of water and bright white
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">
       carbonate
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">
       sand
      </a>
      . Blue light is the most enduring part of the spectrum; other parts of the spectrumred, yellow, and finally greenare absorbed during their path through water, but blue light manages to reach the white sand and return upon reflection.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wondermondo_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wondermondo-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The deepest blue hole in the world-at 392 meters (1,286ft) is
      <a href="/wiki/Pozzo_del_Merro" title="Pozzo del Merro">
       Pozzo del Merro
      </a>
      in Italy. The deepest blue hole in the world with underwater entranceat 202 metres (663ft)is
      <a href="/wiki/Dean%27s_Blue_Hole" title="Dean's Blue Hole">
       Dean's Blue Hole
      </a>
      , located in a bay west of
      <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Town" title="Clarence Town">
       Clarence Town
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Long_Island,_Bahamas" title="Long Island, Bahamas">
       Long Island
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">
       Bahamas
      </a>
      . Other blue holes are about half that depth at around 100120 metres (330390ft). The diameter of the top entrance ranges typically from 2535 metres (82115ft) (Dean's Blue Hole) to 300 metres (980ft) (
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Blue_Hole" title="Great Blue Hole">
       Great Blue Hole
      </a>
      in Belize).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
       Formation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Blue holes formed during past
      <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">
       ice ages
      </a>
      , when
      <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
       sea level
      </a>
      was as much as 100120 metres (330390ft) lower than at present. At those times, these formations were targets of the same erosion from rain and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" title="Chemical weathering">
       chemical weathering
      </a>
      common in all
      <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
       limestone
      </a>
      -rich terrains; this ended once they were submerged at the end of the ice age.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most blue holes contain freshwater and saltwater. The
      <a href="/wiki/Halocline" title="Halocline">
       halocline
      </a>
      is the point in these blue holes where the freshwater meets the saltwater and where a corrosive reaction takes place that eats away at the rock. Over time this can create side passages, or horizontal "arms", that extend from the vertical cave. These side passages can be quite long; e.g., over 600 metres (2,000ft) in the case of the Sawmill Sink in the Bahamas.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
       Occurrence
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Blue holes are typically found on shallow
      <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_platform" title="Carbonate platform">
       carbonate platforms
      </a>
      , exemplified by the
      <a href="/wiki/Bahama_Banks" title="Bahama Banks">
       Bahama Banks
      </a>
      , as well as on and around the
      <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatn Peninsula">
       Yucatn Peninsula
      </a>
      , such as at the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Blue_Hole" title="Great Blue Hole">
       Great Blue Hole
      </a>
      at Lighthouse Reef Atoll,
      <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">
       Belize
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Many deep spring basins formed by
      <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">
       karst
      </a>
      processes and located inland also are called blue holes, for example,
      <a href="/wiki/Blue_Hole_(Castalia)" title="Blue Hole (Castalia)">
       Blue Hole (Castalia)
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">
       Ohio
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life_forms">
       Life forms
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life forms">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In the past, many different fossils have been discovered that indicate the type of life forms that have existed in various blue holes. These fossils have indicated different types of flora and fauna life forms that have existed in there before. For example, one may find fossils of crocodiles and tortoises in a blue hole.
