       <p>
        You know whats annoying? People whispering amongst each other while youre in their presence. Or how about the awkwardness you may experience when youre amongst foreigners and they switch to their native tonguewhileyoure just standing there with a blank stare like, uhh, hey guys. However, I think there is a worse state of oblivion in terms of language barriers.The inability to speak nor understand the language of your heritage that everyone else in your family knows is irritating. Now that is disconnecting. Unfortunately, there is a bit of an emotional, cultural, and social disconnection that I have with my family because I neither speak nor understand our native language Kinyarwanda from Rwanda.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        I was born and raised in America and have visited Rwanda from time to time. My mom actively spoke Kinyarwanda to me when I was little but stopped once my preschool teacher complained to her simply because I spoke the language once in class. I hold no resentment towards my mom, she did this because she wanted what she thought was best for me. She had only been in America for around 4 or 5 years; it was just the two of us together at the time. So if anything she probably wanted to adjust to this country as much as she could and if an American says her daughter speaking this foreign language is a problem then she was bound to believe that. Due to that event, overtimemy tongue could no longer grasp the language. As I grew older with the language already gone, my household began to grow with more family members such as aunts, uncles, and cousins who were all fluent in Kinyarwanda but me. Now this language barrier does not cause complete disconnectionto the point that I cant experience the deep love you share with people that you have lived with your whole life; but there are simple things that I cant quite partake in. Within these simple things I miss out on, there is a complex emotional detachment that isnt easily explained, only felt. I cant laugh at certain jokes because the humor is untranslatable. I cant understand the culture with as much depth because I dont know the stress on certain words that really help to emulate the mood of the culture. Its difficult to casually socialize at events with many Rwandans because of the natural assumption that I speak Kinyarwanda. Then there is the disappointment once they realize I have no idea what theyre saying. Rested assure, I have lived like this since I can remember so I deal with it fine, Im not deeply saddened about it, but I honestly wish I grew up knowing the language.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Just take a moment to imagine on a regular basis that the people you see the most and possibly love the most frequently speaking in a language that you dont understand. Plus, you are the only one who does not understand a thing. Its simple, you are the lonely child on the swing while everyone else is playing on the monkey bars. Well, its not that sad because they use English as much as Kinyarwanda but it is still to some extent isolating. Although, my family does not purposely leave me out but Kinyarwanda is their first language so they are bound to use it frequently and frankly I do not blame them. In fact, I support bilingualism and it worries me to see some of my little cousins possibly not having the opportunity to learn Kinyarwanda too because its supposedly not a useful language according to a family member. Sure, theyre probably not going to use it in the world as much as Spanish, French, or Arabic but the evidence shows that bilingual individuals tend to have enhanced cognitive skills and emotional intelligence. That is quite useful.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Marion &amp; Shook, 2012 suggest that bilingual individuals have enhanced cognitive skills. Although, they may struggle with competing languages conflicting in their brains when coming up with a certain word, this language co activation actually aids to their control mechanisms. Bilingual individuals have greater attention skills and can process more information with this control. The fMRI scans display activation in the Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex (DPFC), a brain region identified with cognitive skills like attention. These skills resonate among bilingual individuals as early as seven months and can play a role in the prevention/delay of Alzheimer because of the mental stimulation it brings. Therefore, bilingual individuals show greater aptitude in learning because of their ability to keep focus.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        In addition to the cognitive benefits of being bilingual there are advantages in emotional intelligence. People who have experienced more than one language may have a greater understanding for different cultures, ideas, or people; with greater experience comes an open mind. Therefore, bilingual individuals have a tendency to hold more empathy due to their formation of sensitivity from a wider social cultural exposure. Bilingual individuals have a stronger grasp on the theory of mindthe ability to understand others belief, desires, or states.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Rubio-Fernndez &amp; Glucksberg, 2012 assessed the theory of mind in monolingual and bilingual Princeton students by identifying whether or not the participant exhibited egocentrismthe boastful attitude one has by showing off greater knowledge. The presence of egocentrism would suggest a lack of theory of mind in the participants. In this study 23 monolingual and 23 bilingual Princeton students were asked to carry out the false belief task known as the Sally-Anne task. In this task participants were familiarized with the characters Sally and Anne who each had a favorite toy they kept in their own separate containers. Then, participants were shown a video of Sally taking Annes toy while Anne was gone and putting it in Sallys container instead. Following the video the participants were questioned with what container would Anne look in for her toy? The correct answer was Annes container. Even though the toy was in Sallys container Anne would look in her own container since thats the usual spot for her toy. In order to identify the presence of egocentrism in participants, researchers utilized eye-tracking techniques. After watching the video and being asked the question, if the participants gazed towards Sallys container or had a longer latency period when looking at Sallys container then they were egocentric. This is because of the desire to show more knowledge of where the toy actually is instead of reacting towards the correct answer, Annes container. Furthermore, the results showed 56.5% of the bilingual participants and 26.1% of the monolingual participants gazed at Annes container, the correct answer. Also, among those who looked at the wrong answer and then the right answer, the bilingual participants had a faster latency period with 619 milliseconds vs. the monolingual participants speed of 812 milliseconds. In turn, the bilingual group was less egocentric and more empathetic; these results were attributed to their wider experience and control mechanisms.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Overall, languages are fascinating and helpful from personal life, travel, connection, learning, to careers. But no worries, its never to late to learn a new language. I challenge you to learn another language if you havent already. Even if you have two languages down go ahead and learn another. You do not have to be raised bilingual. Currently I am studying French and hope to study abroad in a French speaking country and a Spanish speaking country so I can become fluent in both languages. I might also take up Arabic next year and add an Arabic speaking country to my study abroad list. Then, later in my life when I find time perhaps I will spend a long period in Rwanda and learn Kinyarwanda. Maybe Ill be able to laugh wildly with my family when a joke in Kinyarwanda is being said and no one will have to attempt to explain it to me with the meaning being lost when it crosses languages. Maybe Ill be able to have a real conversation with my grandparents instead of our simple I love you; one day I can explain to them why I love them and they can tell me too. My final advice from a monolingual persons perspective is to teach your children how to speak your native language or at least a second language in general, it is worth it, trust me.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>

        <h1>
         From a Monolinguals Perspective
        </h1>
       </div>
       <!-- .entry-header -->

         <div class="entry-content row">
          <div class="col-sm-12">
           <p>
            You know whats annoying? People whispering amongst each other while youre in their presence. Or how about the awkwardness you may experience when youre amongst foreigners and they switch to their native tonguewhileyoure just standing there with a blank stare like, uhh, hey guys. However, I think there is a worse state of oblivion in terms of language barriers.The inability to speak nor understand the language of your heritage that everyone else in your family knows is irritating. Now that is disconnecting. Unfortunately, there is a bit of an emotional, cultural, and social disconnection that I have with my family because I neither speak nor understand our native language Kinyarwanda from Rwanda.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            I was born and raised in America and have visited Rwanda from time to time. My mom actively spoke Kinyarwanda to me when I was little but stopped once my preschool teacher complained to her simply because I spoke the language once in class. I hold no resentment towards my mom, she did this because she wanted what she thought was best for me. She had only been in America for around 4 or 5 years; it was just the two of us together at the time. So if anything she probably wanted to adjust to this country as much as she could and if an American says her daughter speaking this foreign language is a problem then she was bound to believe that. Due to that event, overtimemy tongue could no longer grasp the language. As I grew older with the language already gone, my household began to grow with more family members such as aunts, uncles, and cousins who were all fluent in Kinyarwanda but me. Now this language barrier does not cause complete disconnectionto the point that I cant experience the deep love you share with people that you have lived with your whole life; but there are simple things that I cant quite partake in. Within these simple things I miss out on, there is a complex emotional detachment that isnt easily explained, only felt. I cant laugh at certain jokes because the humor is untranslatable. I cant understand the culture with as much depth because I dont know the stress on certain words that really help to emulate the mood of the culture. Its difficult to casually socialize at events with many Rwandans because of the natural assumption that I speak Kinyarwanda. Then there is the disappointment once they realize I have no idea what theyre saying. Rested assure, I have lived like this since I can remember so I deal with it fine, Im not deeply saddened about it, but I honestly wish I grew up knowing the language.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Just take a moment to imagine on a regular basis that the people you see the most and possibly love the most frequently speaking in a language that you dont understand. Plus, you are the only one who does not understand a thing. Its simple, you are the lonely child on the swing while everyone else is playing on the monkey bars. Well, its not that sad because they use English as much as Kinyarwanda but it is still to some extent isolating. Although, my family does not purposely leave me out but Kinyarwanda is their first language so they are bound to use it frequently and frankly I do not blame them. In fact, I support bilingualism and it worries me to see some of my little cousins possibly not having the opportunity to learn Kinyarwanda too because its supposedly not a useful language according to a family member. Sure, theyre probably not going to use it in the world as much as Spanish, French, or Arabic but the evidence shows that bilingual individuals tend to have enhanced cognitive skills and emotional intelligence. That is quite useful.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Marion &amp; Shook, 2012 suggest that bilingual individuals have enhanced cognitive skills. Although, they may struggle with competing languages conflicting in their brains when coming up with a certain word, this language co activation actually aids to their control mechanisms. Bilingual individuals have greater attention skills and can process more information with this control. The fMRI scans display activation in the Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex (DPFC), a brain region identified with cognitive skills like attention. These skills resonate among bilingual individuals as early as seven months and can play a role in the prevention/delay of Alzheimer because of the mental stimulation it brings. Therefore, bilingual individuals show greater aptitude in learning because of their ability to keep focus.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            In addition to the cognitive benefits of being bilingual there are advantages in emotional intelligence. People who have experienced more than one language may have a greater understanding for different cultures, ideas, or people; with greater experience comes an open mind. Therefore, bilingual individuals have a tendency to hold more empathy due to their formation of sensitivity from a wider social cultural exposure. Bilingual individuals have a stronger grasp on the theory of mindthe ability to understand others belief, desires, or states.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Rubio-Fernndez &amp; Glucksberg, 2012 assessed the theory of mind in monolingual and bilingual Princeton students by identifying whether or not the participant exhibited egocentrismthe boastful attitude one has by showing off greater knowledge. The presence of egocentrism would suggest a lack of theory of mind in the participants. In this study 23 monolingual and 23 bilingual Princeton students were asked to carry out the false belief task known as the Sally-Anne task. In this task participants were familiarized with the characters Sally and Anne who each had a favorite toy they kept in their own separate containers. Then, participants were shown a video of Sally taking Annes toy while Anne was gone and putting it in Sallys container instead. Following the video the participants were questioned with what container would Anne look in for her toy? The correct answer was Annes container. Even though the toy was in Sallys container Anne would look in her own container since thats the usual spot for her toy. In order to identify the presence of egocentrism in participants, researchers utilized eye-tracking techniques. After watching the video and being asked the question, if the participants gazed towards Sallys container or had a longer latency period when looking at Sallys container then they were egocentric. This is because of the desire to show more knowledge of where the toy actually is instead of reacting towards the correct answer, Annes container. Furthermore, the results showed 56.5% of the bilingual participants and 26.1% of the monolingual participants gazed at Annes container, the correct answer. Also, among those who looked at the wrong answer and then the right answer, the bilingual participants had a faster latency period with 619 milliseconds vs. the monolingual participants speed of 812 milliseconds. In turn, the bilingual group was less egocentric and more empathetic; these results were attributed to their wider experience and control mechanisms.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Overall, languages are fascinating and helpful from personal life, travel, connection, learning, to careers. But no worries, its never to late to learn a new language. I challenge you to learn another language if you havent already. Even if you have two languages down go ahead and learn another. You do not have to be raised bilingual. Currently I am studying French and hope to study abroad in a French speaking country and a Spanish speaking country so I can become fluent in both languages. I might also take up Arabic next year and add an Arabic speaking country to my study abroad list. Then, later in my life when I find time perhaps I will spend a long period in Rwanda and learn Kinyarwanda. Maybe Ill be able to laugh wildly with my family when a joke in Kinyarwanda is being said and no one will have to attempt to explain it to me with the meaning being lost when it crosses languages. Maybe Ill be able to have a real conversation with my grandparents instead of our simple I love you; one day I can explain to them why I love them and they can tell me too. My final advice from a monolingual persons perspective is to teach your children how to speak your native language or at least a second language in general, it is worth it, trust me.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
