   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Witchcraft and divination in the Hebrew Bible
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=Witchcraft_and_divination_in_the_Bible&amp;redirect=no" title="Witchcraft and divination in the Bible">
       Witchcraft and divination in the Bible
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        This article discusses the Hebrew Bible. See
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_witchcraft" title="Christian views on witchcraft">
         Christian views on witchcraft
        </a>
        for Christian interpretations.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Various forms of
      <b>
       <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">
        witchcraft
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">
        divination
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
        Hebrew Bible
       </a>
      </b>
      are mentioned in a generally disapproving tone. The
      <a href="/wiki/Masoretic_Text" title="Masoretic Text">
       Masoretic Text
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">
       Torah
      </a>
      forbids:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        nahash
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C19:26_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C19:26-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        nahash
       </i>
       translates as
       <i>
        snake
       </i>
       , and as a verb it literally translates as
       <i>
        hissing
       </i>
       . The verb form can be extended to mean
       <i>
        whispering
       </i>
       , so it has historically been understood to refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Incantation" title="Incantation">
        enchantment
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        onan
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C19:26_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C19:26-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        onan
       </i>
       literally translates as
       <i>
        clouds
       </i>
       , possibly referring to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nephomancy" title="Nephomancy">
        nephomancy
       </a>
       . Some translations take this as an allusion to
       <a href="/wiki/Flock_(birds)" title="Flock (birds)">
        bird flocks
       </a>
       , and therefore translate it as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Augury" title="Augury">
         augury
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        kashaph
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CExodus.7C22:18_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CExodus.7C22:18-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        kashaph
       </i>
       is of ambiguous meaning, being either from a root meaning
       <i>
        mutter
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       or from a compound of the words
       <i>
        kash
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        herb
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        hapalah
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        using
       </i>
       ) - hence meaning
       <i>
        herb user
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         5
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">
        Septuagint
       </a>
       renders the same phrase as
       <i>
        pharmakia
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        poison
       </i>
       ), so it may refer to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_potion" title="Magic potion">
        magic potions
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         6
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       being a
       <i>
        ba'al ob
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C20:27_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C20:27-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CDeuteronomy.7C18:11_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CDeuteronomy.7C18:11-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        ba'al ob
       </i>
       literally means
       <i>
        master of spirits
       </i>
       . The corresponding parts of the Septuagint refer to
       <i>
        eggastrimuthos
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ventriloquism#Origins" title="Ventriloquism">
         gastromancy
        </a>
       </i>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         9
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Necromancy" title="Necromancy">
        necromantic
       </a>
       ventriloquism, in which the voice seems to be located in the stomach.
      </li>
      <li>
       being a
       <i>
        yidde'oni
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C20:27_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C20:27-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CDeuteronomy.7C18:11_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CDeuteronomy.7C18:11-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        yidde'oni
       </i>
       literally means
       <i>
        gainer of information from ghosts
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       being a
       <i>
        doresh el ha-metim
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CDeuteronomy.7C18:11_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CDeuteronomy.7C18:11-8">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         8
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        doresh el ha-metim
       </i>
       literally means
       <i>
        (one who) questions corpses
       </i>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        qasam qesem
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        qasam qesem
       </i>
       literally means
       <i>
        distributes distributions
       </i>
       , possibly referring to
       <a href="/wiki/Cleromancy" title="Cleromancy">
        cleromancy
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        khabar kheber
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        khabar kheber
       </i>
       literally means
       <i>
        join joinings
       </i>
       , possibly referring to
       <a href="/wiki/Spell_(paranormal)" title="Spell (paranormal)">
        charms
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The exact difference between the three forbidden forms of necromancy is a matter of uncertainty;
      <i>
       yidde'oni
      </i>
      is usually only mentioned together with
      <i>
       ba'al ob
      </i>
      , and its semantic similarity to
      <i>
       doresh el ha-metim
      </i>
      raises the question of why all three are mentioned in the same verse of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" title="Deuteronomy">
       Deuteronomy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Classical Jewish sources argued that
      <i>
       yidde'oni
      </i>
      might be another form of ventriloquism, in which the voice is cast into at a bone which is placed into the ventriloquist's mouth.
      <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">
       Rashi
      </a>
      describes the
      <i>
       doresh el ha-metim
      </i>
      as a person who would sleep in
      <a href="/wiki/Cemetery" title="Cemetery">
       cemeteries
      </a>
      , after having starved themselves, in order to become
      <a href="/wiki/Spirit_possession" title="Spirit possession">
       possessed
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Witch_of_Endor" title="Witch of Endor">
       Witch of Endor
      </a>
      was a
      <i>
       ba'al ob
      </i>
      ;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">
       Septuagint
      </a>
      again renders this as
      <i>
       eggastrimuthos
      </i>
      , thus describing her as a (necromantic) ventriloquist, although
      <i>
       (one who has)
       <a href="/wiki/Familiar_spirit" title="Familiar spirit">
        familiar spirits
       </a>
      </i>
      is the more common English translation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Like the law
      <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi" title="Code of Hammurabi">
       code of Hammurabi
      </a>
      , the Torah assigns the death penalty to practitioners of certain forms of
      <i>
       witchcraft
      </i>
      /
      <i>
       divination
      </i>
      ; the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holiness_Code" title="Holiness Code">
       Holiness Code
      </a>
      of Leviticus ascribes the death penalty for two of the three necromantic practices, namely
      <i>
       Ba'al ob
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Yidde'oni
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C20:27_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CLeviticus.7C20:27-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while the
      <a href="/wiki/Covenant_Code" title="Covenant Code">
       Covenant Code
      </a>
      of Exodus ascribes it for
      <i>
       kashaph
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C.7CExodus.7C22:18_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C.7CExodus.7C22:18-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
