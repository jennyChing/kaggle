     <p>
      The
      <b>
       double-headed eagle
      </b>
      is a common
      <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">
       symbol
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">
       heraldry
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Vexillology" title="Vexillology">
       vexillology
      </a>
      . It is most commonly associated with the
      <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
       Byzantine Empire
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Empire" title="Serbian Empire">
       Serbian Empire
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">
       Russian Empire
      </a>
      and their successor states. In Byzantine heraldry, the heads represent the Emperor having authority over both secular and religious matters, Byzantine emperors were regarded as Christ's viceregent on Earth. It also signified the dominance of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Emperors" title="Byzantine Emperors">
       Byzantine Emperors
      </a>
      over both East and West. In the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      's heraldry, it represented the
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">
       church
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">
       state
      </a>
      . Several Eastern European nations adopted it from the Byzantines and continue to use it as their national symbol.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Double-headed_eagle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Byzantine_Eagle.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="630" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Byzantine_Eagle.svg/180px-Byzantine_Eagle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Byzantine_Eagle.svg/270px-Byzantine_Eagle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Byzantine_Eagle.svg/360px-Byzantine_Eagle.svg.png 2x" width="180"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Byzantine_Eagle.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The double-headed eagle used during the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaiologos_dynasty" title="Palaiologos dynasty">
         Palaiologos dynasty
        </a>
        period as a symbol of the Emperor and high-ranking members of the Imperial family.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Double-headed eagles have been present in imagery for millennia. The two-headed eagle can be found in the archaeological remains of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">
       Hittite
      </a>
      civilization, dating from a period that ranges from the 20th century BC to the 7th century BC. The
      <a href="/wiki/Gandaberunda" title="Gandaberunda">
       Gandaberunda
      </a>
      is another example of a mythological two-headed bird, which is in common use in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      (The Kingdom of Mysore) .
     </p>
     <p>
      Cylindric seals discovered in
      <a href="/wiki/Bo%C4%9Fazkale" title="Boazkale">
       Boazkale
      </a>
      , an old
      <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">
       Hittite
      </a>
      capital in modern-day
      <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
       Turkey
      </a>
      , represent clearly a two-headed eagle with spread wings. The aesthetics of this symmetrical position explains in part the birth of this religious figure: It originally dates from c. 3800 BC, and was the Sumerian symbol for the god of
      <a href="/wiki/Lagash" title="Lagash">
       Lagash
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ninurta" title="Ninurta">
       Ninurta
      </a>
      son of
      <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">
       Enlil
      </a>
      . It can also be seen in the same region in three monumental settings: Circa 1900 BC, during the Hittite surge from north-central Anatolia down into
      <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
       Babylonia
      </a>
      ; in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alacah%C3%B6y%C3%BCk" title="Alacahyk">
       Alacahyk
      </a>
      around 1400 BC; and in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yazilikaya" title="Yazilikaya">
       Yazilikaya
      </a>
      before 1250 BC. Here the context looks slightly different and totally religious: The eagle returns to its ancient origins as a symbol of divine power. The two-headed eagle is seen less and less during the last
      <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">
       Hittite
      </a>
      period (from the 9th to the 7th century BC) and totally disappears after the end of the empire.

      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      Further information:
      <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_flags_and_insignia" title="Byzantine flags and insignia">
       Byzantine flags and insignia
      </a>
     </div>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

       </div>
      </div>

     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
       Constantinople
      </a>
      was the successor of Rome, and the Byzantine Greeks continued the use of the old imperial "single-headed" eagle motif. Although the roots of the transformation to double-headed are almost certainly connected with old depictions in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">
       Asia Minor
      </a>
      , the details of its adoption are uncertain. It appears in Byzantine artwork as early as the 10th century, but it's confirmed in use by the Empire as such only much later, in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaiologos_dynasty" title="Palaiologos dynasty">
       Palaiologos dynasty
      </a>
      period, when it was used as a symbol of the Emperor and high-ranking members of the Imperial family.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Ancients used no flags in the modern sense. The Romans used various
      <i>
       signa
      </i>
      , such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">
       bronze
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Aquila_(Roman)" title="Aquila (Roman)">
       aquilas
      </a>
      (adopted as the legions' symbol by
      <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Marius" title="Gaius Marius">
       Marius
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Vexilloid" title="Vexilloid">
       vexilloids
      </a>
      , and, if the emperor was present, pikes or banners with the
      <a href="/wiki/Imaginifer" title="Imaginifer">
       emperor's portrait
      </a>
      . With the adoption of
      <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
       Christianity
      </a>
      as state religion during the later Empire, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chi-Rho" title="Chi-Rho">
       Chi-Rho
      </a>
      and the cross became more and more used in military standards, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Labarum" title="Labarum">
       labarum
      </a>
      . The Roman single-headed eagle however continued to be used as a symbol of imperial authority.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to a popular story (which however lacks any direct support), the single-headed eagle was modified to double-headed by Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Isaac_I_Komnenos" title="Isaac I Komnenos">
       Isaac I Komnenos
      </a>
      (10571059) being influenced from local traditions about such a (mythical) beast (the
      <i>
       haga
      </i>
      ) in his native Paphlagonia in Asia Minor. Local legends talked about this giant eagle with two heads that could easily hold a bull in its claws; the
      <i>
       haga
      </i>
      was seen as a representation of power, and people would often "call" it for protection. Isaac Komnenos, deeply influenced by these beliefs, had already used it as a family emblem.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_Roman_Empire">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Reichsadler" title="Reichsadler">
       Reichsadler
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="544" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg/150px-Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg/225px-Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg/300px-Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg.png 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quaterionenadler_David_de_Negker.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>

        </a>
        , c. 1510

     <p>
      The first mention of a double-headed eagle in the West dates from 1250, in a roll of arms of Matthew of Paris for Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">
       Frederick II
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
       Holy Roman Empire
      </a>
      . Usually depicted black on a gold background, it replaced the earlier single-headed eagle, and was subsequently adopted in the coats of arms of many German cities and aristocratic families. After the dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, the double-headed eagle was retained by the
      <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">
       Austrian Empire
      </a>
      , and served also as the coat of arms of the
      <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">
       German Confederation
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      The double-headed eagle was a main element of the coat of arms of the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">
       Russian Empire
      </a>
      , modified in various ways from the reign of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_III" title="Ivan III">
       Ivan III
      </a>
      (14621505) onwards, with the shape of the eagle getting its definite Russian form during the reign of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_I_of_Russia" title="Peter I of Russia">
       Peter the Great
      </a>
      (16821725). It continued in Russian use until abolished (being identified with
      <a href="/wiki/Tsarist_autocracy" title="Tsarist autocracy">
       Tsarist rule
      </a>
      ) with the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">
       Russian Revolution
      </a>
      in 1917; it was restored in 1993 after that year's
      <a href="/wiki/1993_Russian_constitutional_crisis" title="1993 Russian constitutional crisis">
       constitutional crisis
      </a>
      and remains in use up to the present, although the eagle charge on the present coat of arms is golden rather than the traditional, imperial black.
     </p>

     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Mysore" title="Kingdom of Mysore">
       Kingdom of Mysore
      </a>
      , initially a vassal state of the
      <a href="/wiki/Vijayanagara_Empire" title="Vijayanagara Empire">
       Vijayanagara Empire
      </a>
      , became independent (ca. 1565) with the decline of the latter. The
      <a href="/wiki/Gandaberunda" title="Gandaberunda">
       Gandaberunda
      </a>
      was used as a symbol of the ruling Wodeyar dynasty, having been found on a sculpture on the roof of the Rameshwara temple in the temple town of Keladi in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shivamogga" title="Shivamogga">
       Shivamogga
      </a>
      . The Karnataka Government adopted this symbol as the state symbol; it appears on bus terminals and tickets issued by the Karnataka State Road Transport Corporation. Coins (gold pagoda or gadyana) from the rule of
      <a href="/wiki/Achyuta_Deva_Raya" title="Achyuta Deva Raya">
       Achyuta Deva Raya
      </a>
      (reigned 15291542) are thought
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">

          by whom?
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      to be the first to use the Gandaberunda on currency.
     </p>

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
       Serbia
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Nemanji%C4%87_dynasty" title="Nemanji dynasty">
       Nemanji dynasty
      </a>
      adopted a double-headed eagle. The white eagle was retained by most Serbian medieval dynasties, as well as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kara%C4%91or%C4%91evi%C4%87" title="Karaorevi">
       Karaorevi
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
       Serbia
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">
       Montenegro
      </a>
      . It was the
      <a href="/wiki/Charge_(heraldry)" title="Charge (heraldry)">
       charge
      </a>
      in the coat of arms of the Bulgarian Tsar
      <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Alexander_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Alexander of Bulgaria">
       Ivan Alexander
      </a>
      (reigned 13311371).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>

       ]
      </sup>
      George Kastrioti (
      <a href="/wiki/Skanderbeg" title="Skanderbeg">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottomans" title="Ottomans">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Albania" title="Flag of Albania">
       flag of Albania
      </a>
      . During the next centuries, the eagle was made to hold a sword and/or a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scepter" title="Scepter">
       scepter
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Globus_cruciger" title="Globus cruciger">
