         Kids read mean tweets in emotional PSA against cyberbullying
        </h1>

         <div class="meta image-caption">
          <div class="origin-logo origin-image">

         <div class="meta">
          <time class="timeago" datetime="2015-03-16T19:19:43Z">
           Monday, March 16, 2015
          </time>

         <div class="body-text">
          Cyberbullying is no joke -- that's the point a non-profit organization is trying to push with its latest PSA showing teens reading mean tweets about themselves in a less-than-funny spoof on the popular Jimmy Kimmel bit.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <div class="shortcode-container">
           <div class="youtubeWrapper">
            <iframe class="youtube-player" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/2f_kavukuKI" type="text/html">
            </iframe>
           </div>
          </div>
          <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2f_kavukuKI" rel="" target="">
           Tap to see video if viewing on News app
          </a>
          .
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Kimmel has gotten plenty of laughs on his late night show by having celebrities, sports stars and
          <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RDocnbkHjhI" rel="" target="">
           even President Barack Obama
          </a>
          deadpan in front of a camera as they read tweets criticizing anything and everything about their existence. But the gag loses its fun when its kids reading cruel comments written by their peers.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          At first a laugh track plays as the teens, who appear to be middle and high school aged, read things like, "Allan's voice is so annoying it makes me wish I was deaf."
          <br/>
          <br/>
          But the chuckles fade away as the insults continue.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          "Is it racist if I hate black people because of how much I h8 Sierra," one girl reads.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Finally, one girl walks off camera after reading, "No one likes you. Do everyone a favour. Just kill yourself."
          <br/>
          <br/>
          "For regular kids, words can cut like a knife," Stu Auty, President of the Canadian Safe School Network, said in a press release. "Cyber-bullying is an epidemic that invades their lives and leaves many feeling like there's no way out."
          <br/>
          <br/>
          The Canadian Safe School Network is
          <a href="https://www.indiegogo.com/projects/kids-read-mean-tweets--3" rel="" target="">
           raising money
          </a>
          in hopes of buying ad space during the upcoming Stanley Cup playoffs.
