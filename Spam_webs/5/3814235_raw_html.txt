          <div class="headline headline-h3">
           A Clock In The Sidewalk
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            In lower Manhattan, blocks from where the World Trade Center once stood, embedded deep into the sidewalk, is a clock. It's a simple clock, the hours and minutes are neatly displayed by spade hands, while roman numerals and train track minutes markers circle the dial. All of this is cloudy, but visible under the scratched and stained crystal that occupies a break in the pavement at the intersection of Maiden Lane and Broadway. And it has been ticking away there, under the feet of Manhattan, for over a century.
           </p>
           <p>
            In 1896, Manhattan jeweler William Barthman decided to place a clock in the sidewalk outside his eponymous jewelry store as a way to lure customers in the door. His original contraption was a three-window jump hour clock, mechanical with a technologically innovative battery backup. It was even illuminated by hidden lightbulbs after dark. The clock was designed by Mr. Barthman along with store employee Frank Homm, a process which took over two years. The finished clock was installed in the fall of 1899 and became a public curiosity immediately.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="pull-quote pull-quote-full-width">
           <p>
            Perhaps the most novel device in time-recording instruments, is the sidewalk clock displayed in front of the store of William Barthman...the hour and minute numerals revolve as in a panorama before an opening in the sidewalk.
           </p>
           <small>
            The Technical World Magazine, September 1906
           </small>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            In a world of analog clocks, the Barthman clock was alien in the turn-of-the-century New York landscape. Only a decade before, New York had been lit by gaslight, and the municipal arc light system was still considered a modern marvel. The clock would have been one of the only other objects lighting up lower Manhattan after dark. "Perhaps the most novel device in time-recording instruments, is the sidewalk clock displayed in front of the store of William Barthamn," The Technical World Magazine proclaimed in September 1906. "The hour and minute numerals revolve as in a panorama before an opening in the sidewalk." The clock would remain this way for just over 40 years, though not without issue.
           </p>
          </div>

           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_09" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10328/SidewalkClock_09.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=820&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=e0aca30db057c8d9a9cf2df7d2302a38"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="1">
               1 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            Though Mr. Barthman had dreamed up the idea for the clock, Frank Homm had been the technical mastermind behind its creation and maintenance. He was in charge of regulating the clock almost daily, and it often needed minor repairs. But in July 1932,
            <em>
             The New Yorker
            </em>
            chronicled Barthman's struggles with keeping the flip clock accurate after Homm's death in 1917: "The clock behaved perfectly until the ingenious Mr. Homm died...Nobody but Mr. Homm seemed to understand it and after he passed away it took to having hallucinations and going on tears occasionally, and sometimes the Barthmans would have to go out and hide their shame with a piece of cardboard." Vibrations from the subway tunnels beneath, constant disturbances from footsteps above, and wear-and-tear dealt by the elements meant the clock needed constant attention if it was to run accurately.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_25" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10329/SidewalkClock_25.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=44e42fb7f9609fe4a917809f7bf7d4bd"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="2">
               2 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            It turned out that in Homm's absence, the best solution was to devise a new clock entirely. Sometime around 1940, Barthman swapped out Homm's jump hour clock for a more traditional looking round clock. Though modified a few times since its initial installation (including the addition of a large brass bezel), this is the clock you can still find on the downtown corner today. And it was this version of Barthman's clock that photographer Ida Wyman immortalized in her 1947 photograph
            <em>
             Sidewalk Clock
            </em>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="headline headline-h3">
           Ida Wyman And Sidewalk Clock (1947)
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            Ida Wyman ended up a photographer almost by accident. Growing up in New York City, she originally planned on pursuing a career in nursing. But she was a good student, graduated high school early, and during some time off before nursing school fell in love with photography. She had already learned the basics in her high school's camera club and decided to save up for a camera of her own. But once she had the camera, film became a carefully rationed commodity and this scarcity actively shaped her shooting style. "I rarely took more than one shot of something - I couldn't afford to waste film," she remembers. "Two or three shots at the most." This careful, restrained approach to photography is something completely foreign to most of us in the age of giant memory cards and long battery life.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="Ida_Portrait" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10330/Ida_Portrait.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=160ebbd252c4a0f4fe7c041fba7092bd"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="3">
               3 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            Just as unusual these days is the reality that not everyone had a camera on them at all times. Walking around New York City in the 1940s, Wyman remembers carrying a camera being "like walking with a small dog or a child" - it put people at ease but also made them curious. It meant she could easily approach and talk to people, a process she is quick to point out as critical to her work. Wyman spent much of her time wandering the streets of New York, and she had seen Barthman's clock a number of times before taking
            <em>
             Sidewalk Clock
            </em>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="Ida_Wyman_Sidewalk_Clock_NYC_1947_Small" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee-2.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10331/Ida_Wyman_Sidewalk_Clock_NYC_1947_Small.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=82d90ba3cac6495275ff8c1dbbe6f29a"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="4">
               4 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            One day she was strolling through lower Manhattan and was particularly drawn to the clock in the sidewalk. "It was a lovely thing," she remembers fondly. "The glass wasn't even abraded at the time." New York had many street clocks (though not in the sidewalk), so seeing the time in public wasn't itself unusual. In fact, "very few people I knew had wristwatches [back then]," recalls Wyman. "My father had a watch that he received as a wedding present, with a chain and his initials on the back, but it was only for nice affairs and such. You were getting to be someone recognizable as an adult [when you received a watch]. You were no longer a child." But something about people walking over this particular clock drew Wyman in.
           </p>
           <p>
            As a young woman stepped by on her way to work, Wyman released the shutter. The lady's high-heeled shoe and stocking-clad leg are crisply rendered in the top half of the frame. Other feet and shadows populate the upper edge of the photograph, and the diagonal lines of glass disks in the sidewalk lead the viewer's eye down to the clock, which appears slightly blurred and radiates cracks into the surrounding pavement. The contrast between the solidity of the moving leg and the dynamism of the motionless clock is captivating.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_20" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10332/SidewalkClock_20.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=95a93d751e73ee666d66f3352ac99f73"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="5">
               5 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            I first encountered Wyman's photograph though an exhibition held last year at The Jewish Museum New York,
            <em>
             The Radical Camera: New York's Photo League, 1936-1951.
            </em>
            When asked about the cultural significance of
            <em>
             Sidewalk Clock
            </em>
            , Jewish Museum curator Mason Klein called it "a metaphor for documentary photography itself" but also suggested it be looked at as an early feminist photograph, a symbol of the the increasingly important role of women in both commerce and public life as a result of World War II, a role that would continue to grow over the coming decades.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="pull-quote pull-quote-full-width">
           <p>
            Ive always been a straightforward documentary photographer...The picture [Sidewalk Clock] was never a symbol to me, more a curiosity.
           </p>
           <small>
            Ida Wyman, Photographer (Sidewalk Clock, 1947)
           </small>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            It's isn't difficult to buy into this interpretation, especially considering Wyman's own personal history. Early in her career, she was the first woman to work at the Acme News Service - "I was their first girl mailroom boy " she says with a self-aware laugh, "and then I became their first girl printer." Still though, she insists there was no feminist motive to taking the shot. "I've always been a straightforward documentary photographer," she says. "The picture was never a symbol to me, more a curiosity."
           </p>
           <p>
            And it is worth mentioning that Wyman went on to become an active member of the New York Photo League, one of the most influential organizations in the development of twentieth century photography. Members were concentrated in New York City, and included the likes of Sol Libsohn and Sid Grossman (co-founders), Jerome Liebling, Jack Manning, Aaron Siskind, and dozens of other big names. The Photo League combined amateur and professional photographers, offered some of the first accessible classes in photography, and encouraged photographers to use their cameras to capture the realities of early twentieth century urban life.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="headline headline-h3">
           Barthman's "Side Walk Clock" Archive
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image large">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_04" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee-2.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10333/SidewalkClock_04.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=820&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=b9f9b76bebd4cb8748635e728ea7c1cb"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="6">
               6 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            And Wyman was by no means the only person to find Barthman's Clock curious. "There have been poems written about the clock, stories written about the clock," says Richard Granatoor, William Barthman Jeweler's current General Manager.Luckily, the jeweler has kept an amazing archive of the public's fascination, including newspaper clippings, advertisements, and photographs dating back to the clock's installation in the final months of the nineteenth century.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_05" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee-2.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10334/SidewalkClock_05.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=9ecbf992b4d66cfd3e879259bbde9970"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="7">
               7 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="pull-quote pull-quote-full-width">
           <p>
            There have been poems written about the clock, stories written about the clock...it is one of the few real landmarks of lower Manhattan.
           </p>
           <small>
            Richard Granatoor - General Manager, William Barthman Jewelers
           </small>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            A 1941 Subway advertisement proclaims "Our Town Oddities:
            <strong>
             A clock
            </strong>
            impeded in the sidewalk in front of a jewelry store at Broadway and Maiden Lane keeps good time although
            <strong>
             walked on every day
            </strong>
            ." While in September 1948,
            <em>
             The New York Times
            </em>
            featured a photo of the clock accompanied by the caption "Time is money - a sidewalk reminder in the financial district." This interpretation of the clock is called upon time and time again across decades.
           </p>
           <p>
            William Barthman's clock became such a well-known novelty that he was even asked about it when traveling abroad. An article in the July 16, 1932
            <em>
             The New Yorker
            </em>
            recounts, "The clock seems to have quite a reputation. In 1928, F. William [Barthman] registered at a hotel in Cairo and the clerk asked him at once if he was related to the Barthmans who had a clock in the sidewalk outside their shop." Barthman's clock was a piece of urban lore that traveled the world.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_24" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10335/SidewalkClock_24.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=1a1764bdc5a9269c570da8592b203f43"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="8">
               8 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            Unsurprisingly, local jewelers in other parts of the world decided to copy the Gotham landmark. Windsor, England jeweler Dyson &amp; Sons went so far as to exchange correspondence with Barthman to show off their copy-cat clock. Possibly by coincidence, possibly on purpose, there is a photo in Bathman's records sent to them by Dyson of a young woman calibrating her watch to the Dyson clock, the bottom half of which looks suspiciously similar to Wyman's
            <em>
             Sidewalk Clock
            </em>
            .
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image small">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_35" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee-2.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10336/SidewalkClock_35.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=580&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=c34620822b4866c6fb68084104ef530c"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="9">
               9 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="headline headline-h3">
           The Future Of The Clock
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            A lot has changed in horology since Wyman captured Barthman's clock on film."I remember one day, not too many years ago, I was walking in New York and I asked a gentleman for the time," Wyman recounts. "When he pulled out his phone, I was shocked. It had never occurred to me to use your phone to tell the time, though I guess everybody does these days," she says with a small chuckle.Sure there has been the quartz crisis and countless "deaths" and "rebirths" of the wristwatch, but I mean more than just mechanical and stylistic changes. How we measure and use our time has changed a lot as well.
           </p>
           <p>
            "Not doing anything was not considered wasting time back then," Wyman told me when I asked her to expand on the above story. She sounded a little wistful and lamented that this idea has largely fallen by the wayside. For better or for worse, planning our lives to the second and constantly verifying that we are on schedule is business as usual for most of us these days.
           </p>
          </div>
          <div class="inline-image large">
           <a class="popup-with-move-anim" href="#article-popup">
            <div class="embed-img">
             <img alt="SidewalkClock_43" class="lazy img-not-loaded" data-src="https://hodinkee-2.imgix.net/uploads/block/inline_image/content_image/10337/SidewalkClock_43.jpg?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=820&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=5476532c7e997ac4ef3819124d171aa0"/>
             <div class="view-image-wrap">
              <svg class="icon view-image-icon">
               <use xlink:href="#icon-view-image">
               </use>
              </svg>
              <div class="view-image-index" data-image-index="10">
               10 OF 37
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </a>
          </div>
          <div class="body-copy">
           <p>
            It has been said before here on HODINKEE, but one of the things that makes a mechanical watch so charming is that it is powered by you. Its functioning is affected by how you wear it and it quite literally needs your motion to keep ticking. And this is exactly the relationship Barthman's clock has had with New York City.
           </p>
           <p>
            During the gloomy days of the Depression, coincidentally just after Mr. Homm's death, the clock was failing and erratic day to day. As we geared up for World War II, the clock got a new look and began marching regularly again. More recently, under cloud of smoke and running feet on September 11th, 2001, the clock kept ticking through it all. And when lower Manhattan went dark during Hurricane Sandy, the sidewalk clock stopped too. But, after just a few days, the lights came back on and, just as you would expect, the clock came back to life.
           </p>
           <p>
            Barthman Jewelers has moved a few doors down Broadway and had a replica clock made to put in front of their new location. But soon you'll be seeing it above the front door of the store, not under foot. When they asked the New York City government for permission to embed the clock out front, the authorities had a simple answer: there is only one New York Sidewalk Clock.
           </p>
           <p>
            For more information on Ida Wyman, please visit her
            <a href="http://www.idawyman.com">
             official website
            </a>
            . You can still find
            <a href="http://www.williambarthman.com">
             William Barthman Jewelers
            </a>
            in lower Manhattan, just a few doors down from the sidewalk clock, at 176 Broadway.
           </p>
           <p>
            A special thank you to Ida Wyman, Richard Granatoor, and Mason Klein for their help with this story.
           </p>
          </div>

           <a data-link="ajax" data-side="light" href="/blog/talking-watches-with-jonathan-ward">
            <span>
             Talking Watches
            </span>
            With Jonathan Ward
           </a>
          </h3>
          <div class="article-entry-byline-max hide-from-leftColBreak hide-until-desktopNarrow">
           <div class="byline">
            <address class="byline-author">
             <div class="byline-author-link">
              <img alt="avatar_ben_clymer_80x80" class="byline-author-img imgix-fluid" src="https://hodinkee.imgix.net/uploads/wondercms/user/avatar/3/avatar_ben_clymer_80x80.png?ixlib=rb-0.3.4&amp;w=40&amp;h=40&amp;dpr=2&amp;s=e3b20b6e3d5891148a88af0d1d0db263"/>

           </div>
          </div>
          <div class="article-entry-lede hide-until-tablet">
           <p>
            Jonathan Ward's Icon 4x4's are built from the ground up, keeping the vintage styling that we all love so much in the early Ford Broncos and Toyota FJ's, but now they are carbon coated, and feature absolutely cutting-edge technology.
