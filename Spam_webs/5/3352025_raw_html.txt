   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    One-way speed of light
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alpha_Capricorni_(1).jpg">
        <img alt="alt text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1350" data-file-width="1800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Alpha_Capricorni_%281%29.jpg/220px-Alpha_Capricorni_%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Alpha_Capricorni_%281%29.jpg/330px-Alpha_Capricorni_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Alpha_Capricorni_%281%29.jpg/440px-Alpha_Capricorni_%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alpha_Capricorni_(1).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The double star Alpha Capricorni. Measurements on light from double stars were used to show that the one-way speed of light is independent of the velocity of the light source.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      When using the term '
      <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
       the speed of light
      </a>
      ' it is sometimes necessary to make the distinction between its one-way speed and its two-way speed. The
      <b>
       "one-way" speed of light
      </b>
      from a source to a detector, cannot be
      <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">
       measured
      </a>
      independently of a convention as to how to synchronize the clocks at the source and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detector" title="Detector">
       detector
      </a>
      . What can however be experimentally measured is the
      <b>
       round-trip speed
      </b>
      (or
      <b>
       "two-way" speed of light
      </b>
      ) from the source to the detector and back again.
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
       Albert Einstein
      </a>
      chose a synchronization convention (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein_synchronization" title="Einstein synchronization">
       Einstein synchronization
      </a>
      ) that made the one-way speed equal to the two-way speed. The constancy of the one-way speed in any given inertial frame, is the basis of his
      <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
       special theory of relativity
      </a>
      although all experimentally verifiable predictions of this theory do not depend on that convention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-zhang-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Experiments that attempted to directly probe the one-way speed of light independent of synchronization have been proposed, but none has succeeded in doing so.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Those experiments directly establish that synchronization with slow clock-transport is equivalent to Einstein synchronization, which is an important feature of special relativity. Though those experiments don't directly establish the isotropy of the one-way speed of light, because it was shown that slow clock-transport, the
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Laws_of_motion" title="Laws of motion">
       laws of motion
      </a>
      , and the way
      <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">
       inertial reference frames
      </a>
      are defined, already involve the assumption of isotropic one-way speeds and thus are conventional as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sep-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In general, it was shown that these experiments are consistent with anisotropic one-way light speed as long as the two-way light speed is isotropic.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-zhang-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 'speed of light' in this article refers to the speed of all
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
       electromagnetic radiation
      </a>
      in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space" title="Free space">
       vacuum
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_two-way_speed">
       The two-way speed
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The two-way speed">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="381" data-file-width="512" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg/200px-Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg/300px-Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg/400px-Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg.png 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Michelson_interferometer_with_labels.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Round-trip light path in a
        <a href="/wiki/Michelson_interferometer" title="Michelson interferometer">
         Michelson interferometer
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The two-way speed of light is the average speed of light from one point, such as a source, to a mirror and back again. Because the light starts and finishes in the same place only one clock is needed to measure the total time, thus this speed can be experimentally determined independently of any clock synchronization scheme. Any measurement in which the light follows a closed path is considered a two-way speed measurement.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many
      <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_special_relativity" title="Tests of special relativity">
       tests of special relativity
      </a>
      such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="MichelsonMorley experiment">
       MichelsonMorley experiment
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Kennedy%E2%80%93Thorndike_experiment" title="KennedyThorndike experiment">
       KennedyThorndike experiment
      </a>
      have shown within tight limits that in an inertial frame the two-way speed of light is isotropic and independent of the closed path considered. Isotropy experiments of the Michelson-Morley type do not use an external clock to directly measure the speed of light, but rather compare two internal frequencies or clocks. Therefore such experiments are sometimes called "clock anisotropy experiments", since every arm of a Michelson interferometer can be seen as a
      <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation#Simple_inference_of_time_dilation_due_to_relative_velocity" title="Time dilation">
       light clock
      </a>
      having a specific rate, whose relative orientation dependences can be tested.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will2_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-will2-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 1983 the
      <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">
       metre
      </a>
      has been
      <i>
       defined
      </i>
      as the distance traveled by light in vacuum in
      <span class="frac nowrap">
       <sup>
        1
       </sup>
       
       <sub>
        <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
         299,792,458
        </a>
       </sub>
      </span>
      <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">
       second
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Res1_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Res1-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This means that the speed of light can no longer be experimentally measured in SI units, but the length of a meter can be compared experimentally against some other standard of length.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_one-way_speed">
       The one-way speed
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="272" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg/150px-Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg/225px-Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg/300px-Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simple_stellar_aberration_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Unidirectional light path in the
        <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">
         Aberration of light
        </a>
        .
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although the average speed over a two-way path can be measured, the one-way speed in one direction or the other is undefined (and not simply unknown), unless one can define what is "the same time" in two different locations. To measure the time that the light has taken to travel from one place to another it is necessary to know the start and finish times as measured on the same time scale. This requires either two synchronized clocks, one at the start and one at the finish, or some means of sending a signal instantaneously from the start to the finish. No instantaneous means of transmitting information is known. Thus the measured value of the average one-way speed is dependent on the method used to synchronize the start and finish clocks. This is a matter of convention. The
      <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_transformation" title="Lorentz transformation">
       Lorentz transformation
      </a>
      is defined such that the one-way speed of light will be measured to be independent of the inertial frame chosen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Some authors such as Mansouri and Sexl (1977)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sexl_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sexl-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sexl2_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-sexl2-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as well as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clifford_Will" title="Clifford Will">
       Will
      </a>
      (1992)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Will-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      argued that this problem doesn't affect measurements of the
      <a href="/wiki/Isotropy" title="Isotropy">
       isotropy
      </a>
      of the one-way speed of light, for instance, due to direction dependent changes relative to a "preferred" (aether) frame . They based their analysis on a specific interpretation of the
      <a href="/wiki/Test_theories_of_special_relativity#Robertson-Mansouri-Sexl_framework" title="Test theories of special relativity">
       RMS test theory
      </a>
      in relation to
      <a href="#Experiments_in_which_light_follows_a_unidirectional_path">
       experiments in which light follows a unidirectional path
      </a>
      and to
      <a href="#Slow_clock-transport">
       slow clock-transport
      </a>
      experiments. Will agreed that it is impossible to measure the one-way speed between two clocks using a time-of-flight method without synchronization scheme, though he argued:
      <i>
       "...a test of the isotropy of the speed between the same two clocks as the orientation of the propagation path varies relative to  should not depend on how they were synchronized..."
      </i>
      . He added that aether theories can only be made consistent with relativity by introducing
      <a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc_hypothesis" title="Ad hoc hypothesis">
       ad-hoc hypotheses
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Will-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In more recent papers (2005, 2006) Will referred to those experiments as measuring the "
      <i>
       isotropy of light speed using one-way propagation
      </i>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-will2_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-will2-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, others such as Zhang (1995, 1997)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-zhang-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Anderson
      <i>
       et al
      </i>
      . (1998)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      objected to this interpretation of RMS. For instance, Anderson
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      pointed out that the conventionality of simultaneity must already be considered in the preferred frame, so all assumptions concerning the isotropy of the one-way speed of light and other velocities in this frame are conventional as well. Therefore, RMS remains a useful test theory to analyze tests of Lorentz invariance and the two-way speed of light, though not of the one-way speed of light. They concluded:"
      <i>
       ...one cannot hope even to test the isotropy of the speed of light without, in the course of the same experiment, deriving a one-way numerical value at least in principle, which then would contradict the conventionality of synchrony.
      </i>
      "
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Using
      <a href="#Generalizations_of_Lorentz_transformations_with_anisotropic_one-way_speeds">
       generalizations of Lorentz transformations with anisotropic one-way speeds
      </a>
      , Zhang and Anderson pointed out that all events and experimental results compatible with the Lorentz transformation and the isotropic one-way speed of light, must also be compatible with transformations preserving two-way light speed constancy and isotropy, while allowing anisotropic one-way speeds.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Synchronization_conventions">
       Synchronization conventions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synchronization conventions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The way in which distant clocks are synchronized can have an effect on all time-related measurements over distance, such as speed or acceleration measurements. In isotropy experiments, simultaneity conventions are often not explicitly stated but are implicitly present in the way coordinates are defined or in the laws of physics employed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein_convention">
       Einstein convention
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Einstein convention">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein_synchronization" title="Einstein synchronization">
       Einstein synchronization
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      This method synchronizes distant clocks in such a way that the one-way speed of light becomes equal to the two-way speed of light. If a signal sent from A at time
      <img alt="t_1" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/3/6/7363212f8e5a9d7ace25c6991a33c6fe.png"/>
      is arriving at B at time
      <img alt="t_2" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/7/4/974cb705b0c744f1855953c1086337df.png"/>
      and coming back to A at time
      <img alt="t_3" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/f/68fdce7b170b2754d096f218b8668f22.png"/>
      , then the following convention applies:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="t_{2}=t_{1}+\tfrac{1}{2}\left(t_{3}-t_{1}\right)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/2/3/a2316b39ff84168c74e1525d3e9cf149.png"/>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The details of this method, and the conditions that assure its consistency are discussed in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein_synchronization" title="Einstein synchronization">
       Einstein synchronization
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Slow_clock-transport">
       Slow clock-transport
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Slow clock-transport">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It is easily demonstrated that if two clocks are brought together and synchronized, then one clock is moved rapidly away and back again, the two clocks will no longer be synchronized due to
      <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation" title="Time dilation">
       time dilation
      </a>
      . This was measured in a variety of tests and is related to the
      <a href="/wiki/Twin_paradox" title="Twin paradox">
       twin paradox
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      If however one clock is moved away slowly in frame S and returned the two clocks will be very nearly synchronized when they are back together again. The clocks can remain synchronized to an arbitrary accuracy by moving them sufficiently slowly. If it is taken that, if moved slowly, the clocks remain synchronized at all times, even when separated, this method can be used to synchronize two spatially separated clocks. In the limit as the speed of transport tends to zero, this method is experimentally and theoretically equivalent to the Einstein convention.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sep-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Though the effect of time dilation on those clocks cannot be neglected anymore when analyzed in another relatively moving frame S'. This explains why the clocks remain synchronized in S, whereas they are not synchronized anymore from the viewpoint of S', establishing
      <a href="/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity" title="Relativity of simultaneity">
       relativity of simultaneity
      </a>
      in agreement with Einstein synchronization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Therefore testing the equivalence between these clock synchronization schemes is important for special relativity, and some
      <a href="#Experiments_in_which_light_follows_a_unidirectional_path">
       experiments in which light follows a unidirectional path
      </a>
      have proven this equivalence to high precision.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-standard_synchronizations">
       Non-standard synchronizations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-standard synchronizations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      As demonstrated by
      <a href="/wiki/Hans_Reichenbach" title="Hans Reichenbach">
       Hans Reichenbach
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Gr%C3%BCnbaum" title="Adolf Grnbaum">
       Adolf Grnbaum
      </a>
      , Einstein synchronization is only a special case of a more broader synchronization scheme, which leaves the two-way speed of light invariant, but allows for different one-way speeds. The formula for Einstein synchronization is modified by replacing  with :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep_4-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sep-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="t_{2}=t_{1}+\varepsilon\left(t_{3}-t_{1}\right)" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/6/a/c6a3ee18ef3fceda5a1daab2964feb00.png"/>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
       can have values between 0 and 1. It was shown that this scheme can be used for observationally equivalent reformulations of the Lorentz transformation, see
      <a href="#Generalizations_of_Lorentz_transformations_with_anisotropic_one-way_speeds">
       Generalizations of Lorentz transformations with anisotropic one-way speeds
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As required by the experimentally proven equivalence between Einstein synchronization and slow clock-transport synchronization, which requires knowledge of
      <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation" title="Time dilation">
       time dilation
      </a>
      of moving clocks, the same non-standard synchronisations must also affect time dilation. It was indeed pointed out that time dilation of moving clocks depends on the convention for the one-way velocities used in its formula.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winnie_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-winnie-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      That is, time dilation can be measured by synchronizing two stationary clocks A and B, and then the readings of a moving clock C are compared with them. Changing the convention of synchronization for A and B makes the value for time dilation (like the one-way speed of light) directional dependent. The same conventionality also applies to the influence of time dilation on the
      <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
       Doppler effect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Only when time dilation is measured on closed paths, it is not conventional and can unequivocally be measured like the two-way speed of light. Time dilation on closed paths was measured in the
      <a href="/wiki/Hafele%E2%80%93Keating_experiment" title="HafeleKeating experiment">
       HafeleKeating experiment
      </a>
      and in experiments on the
      <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation_of_moving_particles" title="Time dilation of moving particles">
       Time dilation of moving particles
      </a>
      such as Bailey
      <i>
       et al
      </i>
      . (1977).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus the so-called
      <a href="/wiki/Twin_paradox" title="Twin paradox">
       twin paradox
      </a>
      occurs in all transformations preserving the constancy of the two-way speed of light.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Inertial_frames_and_dynamics">
       Inertial frames and dynamics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Inertial frames and dynamics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      It was argued against the conventionality of the one-way speed of light that this concept is closely related to
      <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">
       dynamics
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Laws_of_motion" title="Laws of motion">
       laws of motion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">
       inertial reference frames
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep_4-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-sep-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Salmon described some variations of this argument using
      <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">
       momentum
      </a>
      conservation, from which it follows that two equal bodies at the same place which are equally accelerated in opposite directions, should move with the same one-way velocity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salmon_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-salmon-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, Ohanian argued that inertial reference frames are defined so that Newton's laws of motion hold in first approximation. Therefore, since the laws of motion predict isotropic one-way speeds of moving bodies with equal acceleration, and because of the experiments demonstrating the equivalence between Einstein synchronization and slow clock-transport synchronization, it appears to be required and directly measured that the one-way speed of light is isotropic in inertial frames. Otherwise, both the concept of inertial reference frames and the laws of motion must be replaced by much more complicated ones involving anisotropic coordinates.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, it was shown by others that this is principally not in contradiction with the conventionality of the one-way speed of light.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sep_4-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-sep-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Salmon argued that momentum conservation in its standard form assumes isotropic one-way speed of moving bodies from the outset. So it involves practically the same convention as in the case of isotropic one-way speed of light, thus using this as an argument against light speed conventionality would be circular.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salmon_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-salmon-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      And in response to Ohanian, both Macdonald and Martinez argued that even though the laws of physics become more complicated with non-standard synchrony, they still are a consistent way to describe the phenomena. They also argued that it's not necessary to define inertial frames in terms of Newton's laws of motion, because other methods are possible as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition, Iyer and Prabhu distinguished between "isotropic inertial frames" with standard synchrony and "anisotropic inertial frames" with non-standard synchrony.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments_which_appear_to_measure_the_one-way_speed_of_light">
       Experiments which appear to measure the one-way speed of light
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Experiments which appear to measure the one-way speed of light">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments_which_claimed_to_use_a_one-way_light_signal_but_which_actually_made_a_round_trip_measurement">
       Experiments which claimed to use a one-way light signal but which actually made a round trip measurement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Experiments which claimed to use a one-way light signal but which actually made a round trip measurement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Greaves.2C_Rodriguez_and_Ruiz-Camacho_experiment">
       The Greaves, Rodriguez and Ruiz-Camacho experiment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Greaves, Rodriguez and Ruiz-Camacho experiment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      In the October 2009 issue of the American Journal of Physics Greaves, Rodriguez and Ruiz-Camacho reported a measurement of the one-way speed of light.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      J. Finkelstein showed that this experiment actually measures the round trip (two-way) speed of light.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments_in_which_light_follows_a_unidirectional_path">
       Experiments in which light follows a unidirectional path
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Experiments in which light follows a unidirectional path">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg">
        <img alt="alt text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6784" data-file-width="9218" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg/220px-Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg/330px-Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg/440px-Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ole_R%C3%B8mer_-_Obser._Primi_Jovialium_Parisiis_-_pp1%2B4.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        List of eclipses of the Jupiter moon Io that formed the basis of Ole Rmer's discovery of the finite speed of light.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Many experiments intended to measure the one-way speed of light, or its variation with direction, have been (and occasionally still are) performed in which light follows a unidirectional path.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faq_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-faq-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Claims have been made that those experiments have measured the one-way speed of light independently of any clock synchronisation convention, but they have all been shown to actually measure the two-way speed, because they are consistent with generalized Lorentz transformations including synchronizations with different one-way speeds on the basis of isotropic two-way speed of light (see sections
      <a href="#The_one-way_speed">
       the one-way speed
      </a>
      and
      <a href="#Generalizations_of_Lorentz_transformations_with_anisotropic_one-way_speeds">
       generalized Lorentz transformations
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-zhang-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      These experiments also confirm agreement between clock synchronization by slow transport and Einstein synchronization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Even though some authors argued that this is sufficient to demonstrate the isotropy of the one-way speed of light,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sexl2_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sexl2-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will_11-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Will-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      it has been shown that such experiments cannot, in any meaningful way, measure the (an)isotropy of the one way speed of light unless inertial frames and coordinates are defined from the outset so that space and time coordinates as well as slow clock-transport are described isotropically
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (see sections
      <a href="#Inertial_frames_and_dynamics">
       inertial frames and dynamics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="#The_one-way_speed">
       the one-way speed
      </a>
      ). Regardless of those different interpretations, the observed agreement between those synchronization schemes is an important prediction of special relativity, because this requires that transported clocks undergo
      <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation" title="Time dilation">
       time dilation
      </a>
      (which itself is synchronization dependent) when viewed from another frame (see sections
      <a href="#Slow_clock-transport">
       Slow clock-transport
      </a>
      and
      <a href="#Non-standard_synchronizations">
       Non-standard synchronizations
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_JPL_experiment">
       The JPL experiment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The JPL experiment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      This experiment, carried out in 1990 by the
      <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
       NASA
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">
       Jet Propulsion Laboratory
      </a>
      , measured the time of flight of light signals through a fibre optic link between two hydrogen maser clocks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1992 the experimental results were analysed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clifford_Will" title="Clifford Will">
       Clifford Will
      </a>
      who concluded that the experiment did actually measure the one-way speed of light.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will_11-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Will-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1997 the experiment was re-analysed by Zhang who showed that, in fact, only the two-way speed had been measured.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="R.C3.B8mer.27s_measurement">
       Rmer's measurement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rmer's measurement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The first experimental determination of the speed of light was made by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ole_Christensen_R%C3%B8mer" title="Ole Christensen Rmer">
       Ole Christensen Rmer
      </a>
      . It may seem that this experiment measures the time for light to traverse part of the Earth's orbit and thus determines its one-way speed, however, this experiment was carefully re-analysed by Zhang, who showed that the measurement does not measure the speed independently of a clock synchronization scheme but actually used the Jupiter system as a slowly-transported clock to measure the light transit times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Australian physicist Karlov also showed that Rmer actually measured the speed of light by implicitly making the assumption of the equality of the speeds of light back and forth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments_that_can_be_done_on_the_one-way_speed_of_light">
       Experiments that can be done on the one-way speed of light
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Experiments that can be done on the one-way speed of light">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="781" data-file-width="800" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg/200px-GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg/300px-GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg/400px-GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GRB080319B_illustration_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Artist's illustration of a bright
        <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">
         Gamma-ray burst
        </a>
        . Measurements on light from such objects were used to show that the one-way speed of light does not vary with frequency.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Although experiments cannot be done in which the one-way speed of light is measured independently of any clock synchronization scheme, it is possible to carry out experiments that measure a change in the one-way speed of light due, for example, to the motion of the source. Such experiments are the
      <a href="/wiki/De_Sitter_double_star_experiment" title="De Sitter double star experiment">
       De Sitter double star experiment
      </a>
      (1913), conclusively repeated in the x-ray spectrum by K. Brecher in 1977;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or the terrestrial experiment by Alvger,
      <i>
       et al
      </i>
      . (1963);
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      they show that, when measured in an inertial frame, the one-way speed of light is independent of the motion of the source within the limits of experimental accuracy. In such experiments the clocks may be synchronized in any convenient way, since it is only a change of speed that is being measured.
     </p>
     <p>
      Observations of the arrival of radiation from distant astronomical events have shown that the one-way speed of light does not vary with frequency, that is, there is no vacuum
      <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">
       dispersion
      </a>
      of light.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, differences in the one-way propagation between left- and right-handed photons, leading to vacuum
      <a href="/wiki/Birefringence" title="Birefringence">
       birefringence
      </a>
      , were excluded by observation of the simultaneous arrival of distant star light.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For current limits on both effects, often analyzed with the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard-Model_Extension" title="Standard-Model Extension">
       Standard-Model Extension
      </a>
      , see
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_searches_for_Lorentz_violation" title="Modern searches for Lorentz violation">
       Vacuum dispersion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_searches_for_Lorentz_violation" title="Modern searches for Lorentz violation">
       Vacuum birefringence
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments_on_two-way_and_one-way_speeds_using_the_Standard-Model_Extension">
       Experiments on two-way and one-way speeds using the Standard-Model Extension
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Experiments on two-way and one-way speeds using the Standard-Model Extension">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_searches_for_Lorentz_violation" title="Modern searches for Lorentz violation">
       Modern searches for Lorentz violation
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      While the experiments above were analyzed using
      <a href="#Generalizations_of_Lorentz_transformations_with_anisotropic_one-way_speeds">
       generalized Lorentz transformations
      </a>
      as in the
      <a href="/wiki/Test_theories_of_special_relativity" title="Test theories of special relativity">
       Robertson-Mansouri-Sexl test theory
      </a>
      , many modern tests are based on the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard-Model_Extension" title="Standard-Model Extension">
       Standard-Model Extension
      </a>
      (SME). This test theory includes all possible Lorentz violations not only of special relativity, but of the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">
       Standard Model
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       General relativity
      </a>
      as well. Regarding the isotropy of the speed of light, both two-way and one-way limits are described using coefficients (3x3 matrices):
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kost4_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-kost4-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <img alt="\tilde{\kappa}_{e-}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/4/6/5460a78060ad4a1d3c1c122202a72422.png"/>
       representing anisotropic shifts in the two-way speed of light,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hohensee_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hohensee-43">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         43
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hohensee2_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hohensee2-44">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         44
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="\tilde{\kappa}_{o+}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/2/3/1231a1cbd3cef7ba9dec4aa6c3aff814.png"/>
       representing anisotropic differences in the one-way speed of counterpropagating beams along an axis,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hohensee_43-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hohensee-43">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         43
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hohensee2_44-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hohensee2-44">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         44
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <img alt="\tilde{\kappa}_{tr}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/a/eea29ebdc90144182e11304144cd7557.png"/>
       representing isotropic (orientation independent) shifts in the one-way
       <a href="/wiki/Phase_velocity" title="Phase velocity">
        phase velocity
       </a>
       of light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobar_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tobar-45">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         45
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A series of experiments have been (and still are) performed since 2002 testing all of those coefficients using, for instance, symmetric and asymmetric
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_resonator" title="Optical resonator">
       optical resonators
      </a>
      . No Lorentz violations have been observed as of 2013, providing current upper limits for Lorentz violations:
      <img alt="\tilde{\kappa}_{e-}=\scriptstyle(-0.31\pm0.73)\times10^{-17}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/0/69070c2ec400a6bfe237c664974cc804.png"/>
      ,
      <img alt="\tilde{\kappa}_{o+}=\scriptstyle 0.7\pm1\times10^{-14}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/b/0/1b03fb0f5d3c237e656a03b8d2351afc.png"/>
      , and
      <img alt="\tilde{\kappa}_{tr}=\scriptstyle -0.4\pm0.9\times10^{-10}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/4/a/44aedfc43a82e3c6cc855f95c9604843.png"/>
      . For details and sources see
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_searches_for_Lorentz_violation#Speed_of_light" title="Modern searches for Lorentz violation">
       Modern searches for Lorentz violation#Speed of light
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the partially conventional character of those quantities was demonstrated by
      <a href="/wiki/Alan_Kosteleck%C3%BD" title="Alan Kosteleck">
       Kostelecky
      </a>
      <i>
       et al
      </i>
      , pointing out that such variations in the speed of light can be removed by suitable coordinate transformations and field redefinitions. Though this doesn't remove the Lorentz violation
      <i>
       per se
      </i>
      , since such a redefinition only transfers the Lorentz violation form the photon sector to the matter sector of SME, thus those experiments remain valid tests of Lorentz invariance violation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kost4_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-kost4-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are one-way coefficients of the SME that cannot be redefined into other sectors, since different light rays from the same distance location are directly compared with each other, see the previous section.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_in_which_the_one-way_speed_of_light_is_not_equal_to_the_two-way_speed">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Theories in which the one-way speed of light is not equal to the two-way speed">

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_equivalent_to_special_relativity">
       Theories equivalent to special relativity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Theories equivalent to special relativity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lorentz_ether_theory">
       Lorentz ether theory
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Lorentz ether theory">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">
       Lorentz ether theory
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lorentz.jpg">
        <img alt="alt text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="268" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Lorentz.jpg/120px-Lorentz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Lorentz.jpg/180px-Lorentz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Lorentz.jpg/240px-Lorentz.jpg 2x" width="120"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lorentz.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Hendrik Antoon Lorentz
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1904 and 1905,
      <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Lorentz">
       Hendrik Lorentz
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincar">
       Henri Poincar
      </a>
      proposed a theory which explained this result as being due the effect of motion through the aether on the lengths of physical objects and the speed at which clocks ran. Due to motion through the aether objects would shrink along the direction of motion and clocks would slow down. Thus, in this theory, slowly transported clocks do not, in general, remain synchronized although this effect cannot be observed. The equations describing this theory are known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentz_transformations" title="Lorentz transformations">
       Lorentz transformations
      </a>
      . In 1905 these transformations became the basic equations of Einstein's special theory of relativity which proposed the same results without reference to an aether.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the theory, the one-way speed of light is principally only equal to the two-way speed in the aether frame, though not in other frames due to the motion of the observer through the aether. However, the difference between the one-way and two-way speeds of light can never be observed due to the action of the aether on the clocks and lengths. Therefore, the Poincar-Einstein convention is also employed in this model, making the one-way speed of light isotropic in all frames of reference.
     </p>
     <p>
      Even though this theory is
      <a href="/wiki/Observational_equivalence" title="Observational equivalence">
       experimentally indistinguishable
      </a>
      from special relativity, Lorentz' theory is no longer used for reasons of philosophical preference and because of the development of
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations_of_Lorentz_transformations_with_anisotropic_one-way_speeds">
       Generalizations of Lorentz transformations with anisotropic one-way speeds
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Generalizations of Lorentz transformations with anisotropic one-way speeds">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      A sychronisation scheme proposed by Reichenbach and Grnbaum, which they called -synchronization, was further developed by authors such as Edwards (1963),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edw_46-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-edw-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Winnie (1970),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-winnie_17-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-winnie-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Anderson and Stedman (1977), who reformulated the Lorentz transformation without changing its physical predictions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhang_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-zhang-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For instance, Edwards replaced Einstein's postulate that the one-way speed of light is constant when measured in an inertial frame with the postulate:
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       The two way speed of light in a vacuum as measured in two (inertial) coordinate systems moving with constant relative velocity is the same regardless of any assumptions regarding the one-way speed
       <i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edw_46-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-edw-46">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          46
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </i>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      So the average speed for the round trip remains the experimentally verifiable two-way speed, whereas the one-way speed of light is allowed to take the form in opposite directions:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="c_{\pm}=\frac{c}{1\pm\kappa}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/f/4/4f469357f8630adc83d5d716d54a5299.png"/>
       .
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
       can have values between 0 and 1. In the extreme as  approaches 1, light might propagate in one direction instantaneously, provided it takes the entire round-trip time to travel in the opposite direction. Following Edwards and Winnie, Anderson
      <i>
       et al.
      </i>
      formulated generalized Lorentz transformations for arbitrary boosts of the form:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anderson_2-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-anderson-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="\begin{align}
d\tilde{t}'= &amp; \tilde{\gamma}\left[1+\kappa\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{v}}/c-\kappa'\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{v}}'/c\right]d\tilde{t}-\left(\kappa'+\tilde{\gamma}\tilde{\mathbf{v}}'\right)\cdot d\tilde{\mathbf{x}}/c\\
 &amp; -\left[\tilde{\gamma}\left(1+\kappa\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{v}}/c\right)-1\right]\frac{\kappa'\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{v}}}{\tilde{\mathbf{v}}^{2}c}\tilde{\mathbf{v}}\cdot d\tilde{\mathbf{x}}+\tilde{\gamma}\kappa\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{v}}\left(\kappa\cdot d\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\right)/c,\\
d\tilde{\mathbf{x}}'= &amp; -\tilde{\gamma}\tilde{\mathbf{v}}d\tilde{t}+d\tilde{\mathbf{x}}+\left[\tilde{\gamma}\left(1+\kappa\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{v}}/c\right)-1\right]\frac{\tilde{\mathbf{v}}\cdot d\mathbf{x}}{\tilde{\mathbf{v}}^{2}}\tilde{\mathbf{v}}-\tilde{\gamma}\tilde{\mathbf{v}}\left(\kappa\cdot d\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\right)/c,\\
\tilde{\gamma}= &amp; \gamma\left(1-\kappa\cdot \mathbf{v}/c\right),\\
\tilde{\mathbf{v}}= &amp; \frac{\mathbf{v}}{1-\kappa\cdot \mathbf{v}/c},
\end{align}" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/0/c/20c1cc740e9d6a1c55afcb865820db9c.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      (with  and ' being the synchrony vectors in frames S and S', respectively). This transformation indicates the one-way speed of light is conventional in all frames, leaving the two-way speed invariant. =0 means Einstein synchronization which results in the standard Lorentz transformation. As shown by Edwards, Winnie and Mansouri-Sexl, by suitable rearrangement of the synchrony parameters even some sort of "absolute simultaneity" can be achieved, in order to simulate the basic assumption of Lorentz ether theory. That is, in one frame the one-way speed of light is chosen to be isotropic, while all other frames take over the values of this "preferred" frame by "external synchronization".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sexl_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-sexl-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      All predictions derived from such a transformation are experimentally indistinguishable from those of the standard Lorentz transformation; the difference is only that the defined clock time varies from Einstein's according to the distance in a specific direction.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_not_equivalent_to_special_relativity">
       Theories not equivalent to special relativity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Theories not equivalent to special relativity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Test_theories">
       Test theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Test theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Test_theory" title="Test theory">
       Test theory
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Test_theories_of_special_relativity" title="Test theories of special relativity">
       Test theories of special relativity
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      A number of theories have been developed to allow assessment of the degree to which experimental results differ from the predictions of relativity. These are known as test theories and include the Robertson and Mansouri-Sexl
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sexl_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-sexl-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      (RMS) theories. To date, all experimental results agree with special relativity within the experimental uncertainty.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another test theory is the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard-Model_Extension" title="Standard-Model Extension">
       Standard-Model Extension
      </a>
      (SME). It employs a broad variety of coefficients indicating Lorentz symmetry violations in special relativity,
      <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
       general relativity
      </a>
      , and the
      <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">
       Standard Model
      </a>
      . Some of those parameters indicate anisotropies of the two-way and one-way speed of light. However, it was pointed out that such variations in the speed of light can be removed by suitable redefinitions of the coordinates and fields employed. Though this doesn't remove Lorentz violations
      <i>
       per se
      </i>
      , it only shifts their appearance from the photon sector into the matter sector of SME (see above
      <a href="#Experiments_on_two-way_and_one-way_speeds_using_the_Standard-Model_Extension">
       Experiments on two-way and one-way speeds using the Standard-Model Extension
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kost4_42-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-kost4-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Aether_theories">
       Aether theories
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Aether theories">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">
       Luminiferous aether
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Before 1887 it was generally believed that light travelled at a constant speed relative to the hypothesised medium of the aether. For an observer in motion with respect to the aether, this would result in slightly different two-way speeds of light in different directions. In 1887, the
      <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="MichelsonMorley experiment">
       MichelsonMorley experiment
      </a>
      showed that the two-way speed of light was constant regardless of direction or motion through the aether.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Preferred_reference_frame">
       Preferred reference frame
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=One-way_speed_of_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Preferred reference frame">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      A preferred reference frame is a reference frame in which the laws of physics take on a special form. The ability to make measurements which show the one-way speed of light to be different from its two-way speed would, in principle, enable a preferred reference frame to be determined. This would be the reference frame in which the two-way speed of light was equal to the one-way speed.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Einstein's special theory of relativity, all inertial frames of reference are equivalent and there is no preferred frame. There are theories, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">
       Lorentz ether theory
      </a>
      that are experimentally and mathematically equivalent to special relativity but have a preferred reference frame. In order for these theories to be compatible with experimental results the preferred frame must be undetectable. In other words it is a preferred frame in principle only, in practice all inertial frames must be equivalent, as in special relativity.
     </p>
