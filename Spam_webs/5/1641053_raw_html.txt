           </div>
          </figure>
         </span>
         <span style="font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 13.5pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
          The state predicts Colorados oil and gas industry will use 6.2 billion gallons of water for fracking next year.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Thats enough water to fill more than 9,000 Olympic swimming poolsor twice as much water as the city of Boulder uses annually.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:16.0pt;margin-left:&lt;br /&gt;
0in;line-height:22.5pt">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          But the industry is quick to point out that those billions of gallons equal less than one percent of the states total annual water use.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Colorado Oil and Gas Association President Tisha Shuller stresses that the water used for fracking is a drop in the bucket compared to the amount farmers and ranchers use. Growing food consumes more than 85 percent of Colorados water every year.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          But Ken Carlson, director of the Energy and Water Sustainability Center at Colorado State University, says comparing water use from oil and gas development and agriculture is a bit misleading, a red herring.
          <span class="apple-converted-space">
          </span>
          Carlson says
          <span class="apple-converted-space">
          </span>
          <a href="http://www.westernresourceadvocates.org/frackwater/fracking_our_future_july_2012.pdf" target="_blank">
           <b>
            research (PDF)
           </b>
          </a>
          shows that most water used for fracking is usually gone foreverinjected deep into the ground, where it remains in perpetuity.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          In comparison, Carlson says 40 percent of the water used for agriculture returns to rivers and streams. And he says a city, which is the second largest use of water in Colorado, returns nine gallons of the 10 gallons that are withdrawn90 percent.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          For example, Mike Eberhard, a completions manager for Anadarko Petroleum Corporation, says that if a well in Weld County requires5 million gallons of fresh water for fracking, about 200,000 gallons of the watera tiny fractionwill return to the surface.The fracking water that does return is called
          <a href="http://www.cpr.org/news/story/slideshow-tour-through-colorado-oil-and-gas-fields">
           flowback
          </a>
          .
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          But an additional 800,000 gallons of produced water will also return to the surface, along with oil and gas. Produced water is natural water that is deep in the earth. The water is generally salty, containing minerals such as barium, calcium, iron and magnesium, in addition to sodium chloride.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Traditionally oil and gas companies have had to dispose of all of the water. Theyve either
          <a href="http://www.cpr.org/news/story/drilling-oil-and-gas-drives-colo-trucking-boom">
           trucked water
          </a>
          to an injection well, pumped it deep underground or evaporated it in open pits.Today more and more companies are treating water and using it to drill again.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          At WPX Energy on the Western Slope, the company recycled more than 250 million gallons of water at its treatment facility in Parachute, Colo. WPX officials say thats close to 100 percent of the water the company used in fracking operations for new wells.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          WPX engineer Tyler Bittner says the water the company recycled in 2013 would be enough to grow 769 acres of corn.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          WPX treats water at an in-house recycling plant, which is an extensive operation of intricate piping systems, huge tanks, and storage ponds.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Plant supervisor Brad Kessler says the motivation for investing in that recycling plant goes beyond saving water.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:16.0pt;margin-left:&lt;br /&gt;
0in;line-height:22.5pt">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Kessler says it's also about taking trucks off the road, noting that WPX has eliminated about 100,000 truckloads of water a year by putting in piping and pumping infrastructure in the Piceance Basin in Garfield County, Colo.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Other big players in the industry, like Anadarko and Noble Energy, are also investing millions in building water pipelines.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          CSU water expert Ken Carlson says a lot of recycling is going on in southern and western Colorado but it remains low in Weld County, where most of the states drilling is underway.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Concrete numbers dont exist because the state doesnt require operators to recycle any water. In fact, it wasnt until last year that the state even asked operators to start reporting recycling amounts. How good that data is depends on how accurate operators are when companies report the information.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          The numbers that are known are anecdotal.Some of the largest operators in the state claim theyre recycling 50 percent of the water they use. High Sierra Energy, the largest recycler in the Weld County, estimates that its more like 10 percent in that region.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Environmentalists say theyre happy to see more oil and gas operators recycle water. But, theyre cautious.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Jeremy Nichols of Wild Earth Guardians, a western environmental advocacy organization, worries about the operators who arent recycling.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Its easier for a company to back a water truck up to a fire hydrant in Greeley than it is to build a water treatment facility to recycle processed water and recycle frack water thats been used for other wells, Nichols says.
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Nichols says recycling as much water as possible is a must at time when the state is grappling with
          <span class="apple-converted-space">
          </span>
          <a href="http://www.cpr.org/news/story/meet-man-tasked-solving-colorados-looming-water-crisis">
           <b>
            huge projected water shortages
           </b>
          </a>
          .
          <p>
          </p>
         </span>
        </p>
        <p style="margin: 0in 0in 16pt; line-height: 22.5pt;">
         <span style="font-size: 13.5pt; font-family: Georgia, serif;">
          Meanwhile the state is emphasizing water conservation to help but the real question is whether substantial amounts of recycled water from the oil and gas industry could one day be added to the mix.
         </span>
        </p>
