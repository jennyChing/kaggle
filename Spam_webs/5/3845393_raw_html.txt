     <div class="areapanel" id="center">
      <div class="space margin">
       <div class="space pagetext" id="pageelement_611908786">
        <img alt="Kids That Are Given Big Plates Eat More Food" class="subheading block dynamicimage" src="/account/images/heading.img?color=717f60&amp;text=3f2e24&amp;accent=32a232&amp;compliment=ffffff&amp;navigation=000000&amp;heading=3f2e24&amp;title=020202&amp;logo=6b778e&amp;link=2c3ab7&amp;title.text=Kids+That+Are+Given+Big+Plates+Eat+More+Food&amp;font=Arial&amp;fontsize=19&amp;stage.width.max=790&amp;stagecolor=eff2ec&amp;width=790&amp;cache=1436894619608"/>
        <h2 class="subheading hide">
         Kids That Are Given Big Plates Eat More Food
        </h2>
        <a class="link" href="/store/3393215/childrensbooks.html">
         <img alt="Kids That Are Given Big Plates Eat More Food" class="right thumbnail" src="/account/images/thumbnail.img?color=717f60&amp;text=3f2e24&amp;accent=32a232&amp;compliment=ffffff&amp;navigation=000000&amp;heading=3f2e24&amp;title=020202&amp;logo=6b778e&amp;link=2c3ab7&amp;picture.image.url=%2Ffiles%2F3393215%2Fuploaded%2Fezine.jpg&amp;picture.width.max=120&amp;picture.height.max=120&amp;picture.image.mask.apply=false&amp;stage.width.max=120&amp;state=center&amp;cache=1436894619608" title="Shop Now!"/>
        </a>
        <div class="panel">
         <div id="article-content">
          <p>
           <span style="color:#000000;">
            <span style="font-size: 16px;">
             Small children who are given large plates and then allowed to serve themselves take more food and consume more calories. A test to see whether adult research on dishware size and food intake also holds true for children. We found that children served themselves about 90 more calories when they used the larger plate at lunch. It turns out however, that the kids had a case of eyes bigger than stomach. They ate approximately half of every additional calorie that they served themselves.
            </span>
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="color:#000000;">
            <span style="font-size: 16px;">
             Obesity in children is a growing problem in the United States. About 17 percent of children ages 2 to 19 are obese. In adults, the size of the dinner plate is known to affect how much food they put on it and how much they eat. Children eat more food when they are served larger portions. But it is not known whether the use of larger, adult sized plates would cause kids to take and eat more food if they served themselves. Using a small child's plate first and then an adult sized one. The children had their choice of an entree and side dishes (pasta with meat sauce, chicken nuggets, mixed vegetables and applesauce). They all got fixed portions of milk and bread with each meal. Each portion was weighed before and after the children ate and calculated their caloric intake.
            </span>
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="color:#000000;">
            <span style="font-size: 16px;">
             The two factors, plate size and being allowed to take their own food seemed to work together. Overall, the adult sized dishware by itself did not promote eating more. The child's body-mass index did not seem to predict who would take more food. It was the child's liking for the food that predicted what they would serve themselves. Those who liked the entree helped themselves to about 104 calories more at the meal. Children look to their environment for some direction when being put in the position of making decisions about how much food to serve themselves.
            </span>
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="color:#000000;">
            <span style="font-size: 16px;">
             The differences in calories were not large. But if this continued on a daily basis, it could contribute to the child's overall energy intake and their weight status. Using smaller plates might give children guidance on portion sizes. In the end, it is the portion that is served rather than the plate size and whether or not the child likes the food that influences how much they eat and how much they serve themselves. As portion size increases, so does the amount they eat. Parents should follow the U.S. Department of Agriculture's recommendations, which suggest filling half the plate with fruits and vegetables.
            </span>
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           <span style="color:#000000;">
            <span style="font-size: 16px;">
             Parents also should choose plate sizes for serving their children based on what will be on the plate. For foods you want them to eat a lot of, such as fruits and vegetables, I would put it on larger plates. The fried chicken nuggets should be served on a small plate. The kids served themselves more fruits on their larger plates, but not more vegetables.
            </span>
           </span>
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
