				&lt;p class="byline large"&gt;&lt;a href="/contributor/KathyShaidle/250"&gt;by Kathy Shaidle&lt;/a&gt; &lt;a href="/contributors/contributor_feed/KathyShaidle/"&gt;&lt;img src="/images/global/feed-32x32.png" height="12" width="12" class="leftX" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

      <p>
       I stand to win a great deal of money in a game show that will never be invented, Dick Cavett said somewhere, reflecting on his rare and unprofitable ability to ad-lib rhyming couplets in iambic pentameter.
      </p>
      <p>
       I, on the other hand, am one of those bores without number whos convinced I could clean up on
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       based solely on my talent for shouting the correct answers at my TV, punctuated with the phrases Hurry up! and You moron!
      </p>
      <p>
       As anyone whos played
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       for real and won (or lost) will tell you, a game that seems like a cinch to any over-read geek playing on his sofa is a far more challenging competition when youre sharing a bright, hot television studio with an audience, a host, two other contestantsand that damn buzzer.
      </p>
      <div class="pullquote">
       Im one of those all women and still dont really understand what this one is in aid ofsomething to do with being wolf-whistled at by construction workers.
      </div>
      <p>
       The shows longest-reigning champion, Ken Jennings, calls the buzzer 
       <a href="http://www.ken-jennings.com/faq">
        a cruel mistress
       </a>
       . You see, you have to wait until host Alex Trebek has completely finished reading the clue before you hit the button. Do so one half-second too early, or too late, and you lose your chance to answer. Whereas I read the clues very quicklymy eyes swallow those chunks of text wholeso I always start screaming, What is
       <em>
        Twelfth Night
       </em>
       ?!!? before hes halfway through.
      </p>
      <p>
       Except I never phrase my answer in the form of a question, because it feels so corny and besides, I hate rules. Then theres the betting. I may be able to sweep most non-sports-related categories on
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       , but I still cant figure out how contestants manage to calculate their wagers, especially when they win by exactly one dollarthat little flourish that distinguishes a true champ.
      </p>
      <p>
       I know: its because Im a girl. In fact, being a girl is supposedly one of the biggest handicaps for any
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       contestant.
      </p>
      <p>
       Even devoted fans of the show
       <a href="http://www.salon.com/2014/03/10/alex_trebeks_jeopardy_gender_obsession/">
        have complained for some time
       </a>
       that a) there arent enough women contestants, b) there havent been enough female
       <em>
        winners
       </em>
       , c) the questions are gendered (
       <a href="http://www.salon.com/2013/09/29/what_is_sexism_alex_close_the_jeopardy_gender_gap/">
        this
       </a>
       boomer-feminist analyzed over 1000 questions to prove it), and d) Alex Trebek is a condescending sexist pig.
      </p>
      <p>
       These issues are being raised once again because, as I write this,
       <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/wonkblog/wp/2014/05/29/how-to-dominate-jeopardy-like-julia-collins/">
        Julia Collins
       </a>
       has won 18 games in a row, raking in U.S. $391,000. Thats the third longest winning streak in the shows history. (Ten years after his appearance, Jennings still tops the list at a staggering 74 consecutive wins.)
      </p>
      <p>
       To no ones surprise, Collins triumph has led some feminists to whinge about all the other women whove failed to make it this far during the shows multigenerational run. Others have
       <a href="http://www.vulture.com/2014/05/women-are-dominating-jeopardy-this-year.html">
        countered
       </a>
       that women are doing much better on
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       of late. Either way, the experiences of a tiny number of females are once again being
       <a href="http://takimag.com/article/one_grey_lady_fires_another_kathy_shaidle/page_2#axzz33JOGXJWQ">
        imbued with Some Kind of Meaning
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       One explanation for the latter phenomenonthe sudden surge in female winnerscomes from 2003 College Tournament champion turned daily
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       vlogger/wager analyst
       <a href="http://www.vulture.com/2014/05/women-are-dominating-jeopardy-this-year.html">
        Keith Williams
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <div style="float:left;margin-right:7px">
        <div id="div-gpt-ad-1348014429997-3" style="width:300px; height:250px;">
         <script type="text/javascript">
          googletag.cmd.push(function() { googletag.display('div-gpt-ad-1348014429997-3'); });
         </script>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        In the past, they were a lot more focused on what we call hard knowledge and historical facts, dates, who wrote what book, and so on. Now youll see entire categories devoted to Taylor Swift or celebrity reality shows. I wouldnt say that women are necessarily better in those categories, but that is a consideration.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Then theres that betting thing. Trebek earned his reputation as a misogynist in part by
       <a href="http://www.slate.com/blogs/xx_factor/2014/03/05/gender_differences_in_jeopardy_alex_trebek_says_women_wager_less_in_daily.html">
        observing
       </a>
       that female contestants think of those dollar amounts on the blue screens just below their waists as real cash, and are therefore hesitant to risk it. Men, however, rightly view those digits as virtual house money. That cash isnt real until they win it, and they stand a better chance of doing so by placing a big, bold bet.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hey, Im just glad well-off, white American women have discovered something new to bitch about.
       <a href="http://takimag.com/article/tweeting_our_way_to_justice_kathy_shaidle#axzz33JOGXJWQ">
        #FreeOurGirls
       </a>
       was getting boring. Thank God
       <a href="http://takimag.com/article/soulless_in_santa_barbara_matt_forney#axzz33JOGXJWQ">
        Elliot Rodger
       </a>
       came along, so they could invent a new hashtag, #YesAllWomen.
      </p>
      <p>
       (Im one of those all women and still dont really understand what this one is in aid ofsomething to do with being wolf-whistled at by construction workers. Which,
       <a href="http://takimag.com/article/the_consolations_of_ugliness/print">
        as Ive written before
       </a>
       , has never happened to me, alas. The closest I ever came was a couple of years ago, when a workman repairing a Toronto sidewalk yelled, I love your T-shirt!, which read ITS NOT RACIST IF ITS TRUE.)
       <br/>
       <br/>
       Someone who picked up on the #YesAllWomen meme? Notorious
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       contestant
       <a href="http://www.macleans.ca/culture/television/arthur-chu-revenge-of-a-very-different-nerd/">
        Arthur Chu
       </a>
       , who gained notoriety during his brief but eventful run as that angry nerd.
      </p>
      <p>
       Following Elliot Rodgers killing spree, Chu composed
       <a href="http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2014/05/27/your-princess-is-in-another-castle-misogyny-entitlement-and-nerds.html">
        a widely-read essay
       </a>
       confessing the multitude of woman-hating sins committed regularly within his (and, supposedly, Rodgers) male nerd culture. (Mea culpa, meus Maximus Prime?)
      </p>
      <p>
       Meanwhile,
       <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/global-development/2014/jun/01/girls-toilet-rape-murder-anger-embarrassment?CMP=EMCNEWEML6619I2">
        two Indian teenagers
       </a>
       were gang-raped and murdered when they were out looking for a place to take a crap. Its a situation that plays out all over the world every day, but its easier and more fun to complain about the tribulations of women on
       <em>
        Jeopardy!
       </em>
       rather than in it.
