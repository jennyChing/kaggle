          WWII Prisoners Built Improvised Cameras to Document Their Lives
         </a>
        </h2>
        <div class="postmetadata addthis_native_toolbox visible-xs">
         <ul>

         <p>
          <img alt="prisoner" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-119531" height="333" src="http://petapixel.com/assets/uploads/2013/08/prisoner.jpg" width="620"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Ever since photography was invented in the 1800s, there have been people willing to risk life and limb to bring images to the public eye. Among the craziest examples are prisoners of war during World War II  people who built makeshift cameras out of smuggled parts in order to capture what life was like inside their prison camps.
          <br/>
          <span id="more-119530">
          </span>
         </p>
         <h3>
          A US Marine in China and Japan
         </h3>
         <p>
          Terence Sumner Kirk was a Marine who was captured in China on the day the Japanese bombed Pearl Harbor. He then spent over 1,300 days  up until the end of the war  in prison as a Japanese POW.
         </p>
         <p>
          While inside a camp and being used for slave labor, Kirk decided that he wanted to document the conditions inside the camp, including how malnourished he and his fellow prisoners were  so he set to work building a pinhole camera.
         </p>
         <p>
          First, he had a Japanese interpreter smuggle in camera parts, including a glass plate and scraps of cardboard. Once he had what he needed, Kirk snapped photos while other Marines made sure the guards didnt catch him. Kirk shot a total of eight photographs, but only managed to develop six of the exposures.
         </p>
         <p>
          To ensure that the images would survive the war, Kirk wrapped them in oilcloth and buried them in the camp latrine. People generally dont go digging around toilet holes, and the photos did indeed survive.
         </p>
         <p>
          After Kirk became a free man in 1945, and after a US government gag order lifted in 1983, Kirk published a book containing his photos, titled 
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/159228826X/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=159228826X&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=peta0c-20" target="_blank" title="">
           The Secret Camera
          </a>
          . His images can also be viewed over
          <a href="http://www.thesecretcamera.com/Prison_Camp_Photos.html" target="_blank" title="">
           on the books official website
          </a>
          (note: theres some nudity).
         </p>
         <p>
          The photos are now the only ones we have of the inside of a Japanese POW camp inside China, and the only pictures that were available for war crime trials that were held after WWII. Kirk
          <a href="http://www.nbcnews.com/id/12759234/ns/us_news-life/t/wwii-pow-who-improvised-camera-dies/#.Uf3oNmTXRYi" target="_blank" title="">
           passed away in 2006
          </a>
          after years of touring the US to lecture about his experiences as a Japanese POW.
         </p>
         <h3>
          French Officers in Austria
         </h3>
         <p>
          Around the same time Kirk was shooting his covert pictures, an almost identical situation was unfolding on the other side of the world.
         </p>
         <p>
          In 1940, a Nazi prison camp in Austria called
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oflag_XVII-A" target="_blank" title="">
           Oflag 17a
          </a>
          housed 5,000 French officers in 40 barracks. A number of the prisoners wanted to document their plight, so they set to work assembling a motion picture camera
          <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-europe-23423329" target="_blank" title="">
           through a process similar to Kirks
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          After discovering that the Nazi guards would only check incoming food by slicing it straight down the middle, the prisoners began having camera pieces smuggled in the ends of
          <em>
           sausages
          </em>
          .
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_119532" style="width: 630px">
          <img alt="An image of a prisoner holding the makeshift camera" class="size-full wp-image-119532" height="386" src="http://petapixel.com/assets/uploads/2013/08/prisoner2.jpg" width="620"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           An image of a prisoner holding the makeshift camera
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Once they had the parts and materials they needed, they build a makeshift camera by hollowing out a dictionary from the camps library. The spine was turned into a shutter, and 8mm film reels were placed within.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="bookcamera" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-119533" height="620" src="http://petapixel.com/assets/uploads/2013/08/bookcamera.jpg" width="586"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Using this DIY camera, the prisoners managed to capture over 30 minutes of footage on 14 reels showing living conditions, camp food, labor tasks, intrusive searches, and more  all without being caught by the guards.
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_119534" style="width: 630px">
          <img alt="A still frame from the footage captured by the prisoners" class="size-full wp-image-119534" height="333" src="http://petapixel.com/assets/uploads/2013/08/still.jpg" width="620"/>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           A still frame from the footage captured by the prisoners
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          To preserve the film until they were released, the prisoners nailed the film reels into the heels of their makeshift shoes. Snippets of the footage that survived can be seen
          <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-europe-23423329" target="_blank" title="">
           here
          </a>
          and
          <a href="http://gizmodo.com/the-astonishing-tale-of-nazi-prisoners-a-smuggled-came-988296822" target="_blank" title="">
           here
          </a>
          .
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <p>
          So there you have it: two crazy examples in which WWII prisoners risked life and limb to build DIY cameras so that future generations could have a visual understanding of things they experienced.
         </p>
         <hr/>
         <p>
          (via
          <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-europe-23423329" target="_blank" title="">
           BBC News
          </a>
          via

           Gizmodo
          </a>
          )
         </p>
