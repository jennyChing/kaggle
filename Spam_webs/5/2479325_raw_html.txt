     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsythia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      They are
      <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">
       deciduous
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrubs" title="Shrubs">
       shrubs
      </a>
      typically growing to a height of 13m (3ft 3in9ft 10in) and, rarely, up to 6m (20ft) with rough grey-brown bark. The leaves are opposite, usually simple but sometimes trifoliate with a basal pair of small leaflets, and range from
      <span class="nowrap">
       210 cm (14 in.)
      </span>
      in length and, rarely up to
      <span class="nowrap">
       15 cm (6 in.)
      </span>
      long; the margin is serrated or entire.
     </p>
     <p>
      The flowers are produced in the early spring before the leaves, bright yellow with a deeply four-lobed flower, the petals joined only at the base. These become pendant in rainy weather thus shielding the reproductive parts.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is widely stated that forsythia flowers are able to produce
      <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">
       lactose
      </a>
      (the milk sugar). Lactose is very rarely established in other natural sources except milk. However, the presence of lactose could not be confirmed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The actual fruit is a dry capsule, containing several winged seeds.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foc_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-foc-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The genus is named after
      <a href="/wiki/William_Forsyth_(horticulturist)" title="William Forsyth (horticulturist)">
       William Forsyth
      </a>
      (17371804) a Scottish botanist who was royal head gardener and a founding member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">
       Royal Horticultural Society
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsythia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Garden history">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Forsythia_suspensa_4090392_%E3%83%AC%E3%83%B3%E3%82%AE%E3%83%A7%E3%82%A6%EF%BC%88%E9%80%A3%E7%BF%B9%EF%BC%89.JPG">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Forsythia_suspensa_4090392_%E3%83%AC%E3%83%B3%E3%82%AE%E3%83%A7%E3%82%A6%EF%BC%88%E9%80%A3%E7%BF%B9%EF%BC%89.JPG" title="Enlarge">

       </div>

     <p>
      Two species of forsythia are at the heart of the selected forms, for both species are variable, and garden hybrids:
      <i>
       Forsythia suspensa
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       F. viridissima
      </i>
      . "These two species are, as it were, the founder-members of the forsythia family" writes Alice Coats; they were the earliest species brought into Western gardens from the Far East and they have each played a role in the modern garden shrubs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coats_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Coats-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Forsythia suspensa
      </i>
      , the first to be noticed by a Westerner, was seen in a Japanese garden by the botanist-surgeon
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Peter_Thunberg" title="Carl Peter Thunberg">
       Carl Peter Thunberg
      </a>
      , who included it (as a lilac) in his
      <i>
       Flora Japonica
      </i>
      1784. Thunberg's professional connections lay with the
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">
       Dutch East India Company
      </a>
      , and
      <i>
       F. suspensa
      </i>
      reached Holland first, by 1833. In England, when it was being offered by
      <a href="/wiki/Veitch_Nurseries" title="Veitch Nurseries">
       Veitch Nurseries
      </a>
      in Exeter at mid-century, it was still considered a rarity. Not all the varieties of
      <i>
       suspensa
      </i>
      are splaying and drooping, best seen hanging over a retaining wall; an erect form found by Fortune near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peking" title="Peking">
       Peking
      </a>
      in 1861 was for a time classed as a species
      <i>
       F. fortunei
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coats_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Coats-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Forsythia viridissma
      </i>
      , meanwhile, had overtaken it in European gardens. The Scottish plant-hunter
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fortune" title="Robert Fortune">
       Robert Fortune
      </a>
      "discovered" it in a mandarin's garden of the coastal city of
      <a href="/wiki/Zhoushan" title="Zhoushan">
       Chusan
      </a>
      (Zhoushan) before he ever saw it growing wild in the mountains in Chusan's province,
      <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang" title="Zhejiang">
       Zhejiang
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coats_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Coats-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Forsythia
      </i>
      
      <i>
       intermedia
      </i>
      , as its name suggests, is a
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)#Hybrid_plants" title="Hybrid (biology)">
       hybrid
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       F. suspensa
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       F. viridissima
      </i>
      , introduced in continental Europe about 1880. Repeated crosses of the same two parents have made reiterations of
      <i>
       F.
      </i>
      
      <i>
       intermedia
      </i>
      quite variable. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bud_sport" title="Bud sport">
       bud sport
      </a>
      of a particularly showy (
      <i>
       spectabilis
      </i>
      ) form is widely marketed as
      <i>
       F.
      </i>
      
      <i>
       intermedia
      </i>
      'Lynwood Variety'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coats_13-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Coats-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This
      <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">
       cultivar
      </a>
      has gained the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">
       Royal Horticultural Society
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">
       Award of Garden Merit
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as have
      <i>
       F.
      </i>
      
      <i>
       intermedia
      </i>
      <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
       Week End
      </span>
      'Courtalyn'
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <i>
       F.
      </i>
      <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
       MARE D'OR
      </span>
      'Courtasol'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      About the time of the First World War further species were discovered by plant hunters in China:
      <i>
       F. giraldian
      </i>
      (found in Gansu, 1910) and
      <i>
       F. ovata
      </i>
      (collected from seed in Korea by
      <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Henry_Wilson" title="Ernest Henry Wilson">
       E.H. "Chinese" Wilson
      </a>
      ) have been particularly useful as seed parents in 20th-century American crosses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coats_13-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Coats-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">
       Cultivation and uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsythia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2014-10-29_13_06_11_Forsythia_foliage_during_autumn_in_Ewing,_New_Jersey.JPG">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2014-10-29_13_06_11_Forsythia_foliage_during_autumn_in_Ewing,_New_Jersey.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Autumn leaf color
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">
       hybrids
      </a>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Forsythia_%C3%97_intermedia" title="Forsythia  intermedia">
        Forsythia  intermedia
       </a>
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       F. suspensa
      </i>
      
      <i>
       F. viridissima
      </i>
      ) and
      <i>
       Forsythia
      </i>
      
      <i>
       variabilis
      </i>
      (
      <i>
       F. ovata
      </i>
      
      <i>
       F. suspensa
      </i>
      ) have been produced in cultivation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Forsythia intermedia
      </i>
      is a hybrid between
      <i>
       F. suspensa
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       F. viridissima
      </i>
      . Many
      <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">
       cultivars
      </a>
      have been selected from this cross including dwarf and compact forms.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Forsythias are popular early spring flowering shrubs in gardens and parks. Two are commonly cultivated for ornament,
      <i>
       Forsythia  intermedia
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Forsythia suspensa
      </i>
      . They are both spring flowering shrubs, with yellow flowers. They are grown and prized for being tough, reliable garden plants.
      <i>
       Forsythia  intermedia
      </i>
      is the more commonly grown, is smaller, has an upright habit, and produces strongly coloured flowers.
      <i>
       Forsythia suspensa
      </i>
      is a large to very large shrub, can be grown as a weeping shrub on banks, and has paler flowers. Many named garden cultivars can also be found.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Forsythia is frequently forced indoors in the early spring.
     </p>
     <p>
      Commercial
      <a href="/wiki/Plant_propagation" title="Plant propagation">
       propagation
      </a>
      is usually by cuttings, taken from green wood after flowering in late spring to early summer; alternatively, cuttings may be taken between November and February.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhs_7-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Low hanging boughs often take root, and can be removed for transplanting. A common practice (known as
      <a href="/wiki/Layering" title="Layering">
       layering
      </a>
      ) is to place a weight over a branch to keep it on the ground and, after it has rooted, to dig up the roots and cut the rooted part from the main branch; this can then be planted.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Forsythia_suspensa" title="Forsythia suspensa">
        Forsythia suspensa
       </a>
      </i>
      is considered one of the 50 fundamental herbs in
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_herbology" title="Chinese herbology">
       Chinese herbology
      </a>
      . Forsythia sticks are used to bow a Korean string instrument called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ajaeng" title="Ajaeng">
        ajaeng
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
