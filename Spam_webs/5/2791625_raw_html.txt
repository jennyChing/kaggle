   </h1>

     <p>
      <b>
       Picturesque
      </b>
      is an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesthetic" title="Aesthetic">
       aesthetic
      </a>
      ideal introduced into English cultural debate in 1782 by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Gilpin_(clergyman)" title="William Gilpin (clergyman)">
       William Gilpin
      </a>
      in
      <i>
       Observations on the River Wye, and Several Parts of South Wales, etc. Relative Chiefly to Picturesque Beauty; made in the Summer of the Year 1770
      </i>
      , a practical book which instructed Englands leisured travelers to examine the face of a country by the rules of picturesque beauty. Picturesque, along with the aesthetic and cultural strands of
      <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">
       Gothic
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Revival" title="Celtic Revival">
       Celticism
      </a>
      , was a part of the emerging
      <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
       Romantic
      </a>
      sensibility of the 18th century.
     </p>
     <p>
      The term picturesque needs to be understood in relationship to two other aesthetic ideals: the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Beauty" title="Beauty">
        beautiful
       </a>
      </i>
      and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sublime_(philosophy)" title="Sublime (philosophy)">
        sublime
       </a>
      </i>
      . By the last third of the 18th century,
      <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">
       Enlightenment
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rationalist" title="Rationalist">
       rationalist
      </a>
      ideas about
      <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">
       aesthetics
      </a>
      were being challenged by looking at the experiences of beauty and sublimity as being non-rational. Aesthetic
      <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">
       experience
      </a>
      was not just a rational decision one did not look at a pleasing curved form and decide it was beautiful; rather it came naturally as a matter of basic human instinct.
      <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">
       Edmund Burke
      </a>
      in his 1757
      <i>
       Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and the Beautiful
      </i>
      argued that the soft gentle curves appealed to the male sexual desire, while the sublime horrors appealed to our desires for self-preservation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Picturesque arose as a mediator between these opposed ideals of beauty and the sublime, showing the possibilities that existed in between these two rationally idealized states. As
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gray" title="Thomas Gray">
       Thomas Gray
      </a>
      wrote in 1765 of the Scottish Highlands: The mountains are ecstatic []. None but those monstrous creatures of God know how to join so much beauty with so much horror.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      See also
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Gilpin_(clergyman)#Gilpin_and_the_picturesque" title="William Gilpin (clergyman)">
       Gilpin and the picturesque
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background_and_development">
       Historical background and development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Picturesque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background and development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turner_Tintern1.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1099" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Turner_Tintern1.jpg/220px-Turner_Tintern1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Turner_Tintern1.jpg/330px-Turner_Tintern1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Turner_Tintern1.jpg/440px-Turner_Tintern1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turner_Tintern1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         The Chancel and Crossing of
         <a href="/wiki/Tintern_Abbey" title="Tintern Abbey">
          Tintern Abbey
         </a>
         , Looking towards the East Window
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">
         J. M. W. Turner
        </a>
        , 1794
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The word
      <i>
       picturesque
      </i>
      , meaning literally in the manner of a picture; fit to be made into a picture, was a word used as early as 1703 (
      <i>
       Oxford English Dictionary
      </i>
      ), and derived from an Italian term
      <i>
       pittoresco
      </i>
      , in the manner of a painter. Gilpins
      <i>
       Essay on Prints
      </i>
      (1768) defined
      <i>
       picturesque
      </i>
      as a term expressive of that peculiar kind of beauty, which is agreeable in a picture (p. xii).
     </p>
     <p>
      The pictorial genre called Picturesque appeared already in the 17th century and flourished in the 18th. As well as portraying beauty in the classical manner, eighteenth-century artists could overdo it from top to bottom. Their preromantic sensitivity could aspire to the sublime or be pleased with the picturesque. According to
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hussey" title="Christopher Hussey">
       Christopher Hussey
      </a>
      , While the outstanding qualities of the sublime were vastness and obscurity, and those of the beautiful smoothness and gentleness, the characteristics of the picturesque were roughness and sudden variation joined to irregularity of form, colour, lighting, and even sound.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first option is the harmonic and classical (i.e. beauty); the second, the grandiose and terrifying (i.e. the sublime); and the third, the rustic, corresponding to the picturesque and connecting qualities of the first two options. This triple definition by Hussey, although modern, is true to the concept of the epoch, as
      <a href="/wiki/Uvedale_Price" title="Uvedale Price">
       Uvedale Price
      </a>
      explained in 1794. The examples Price gave of these three aesthetic tendencies were
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handel" title="Handel">
       Handel
      </a>
      as the sublime, a pastorale by
      <a href="/wiki/Arcangelo_Corelli" title="Arcangelo Corelli">
       Arcangelo Corelli
      </a>
      as the beautiful, and a painting of a
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age_painting" title="Dutch Golden Age painting">
       Dutch landscape
      </a>
      as the picturesque.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the mid 18th century the idea of purely scenic pleasure touring began to take hold among the English leisured class. This new image disregarded the principles of symmetry and perfect proportions while focusing more on "accidental irregularity," and moving more towards a concept of individualism and rusticity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/William_Gilpin_(priest)" title="William Gilpin (priest)">
       William Gilpin
      </a>
      s work was a direct challenge to the ideology of the well established
      <a href="/wiki/Grand_Tour" title="Grand Tour">
       Grand Tour
      </a>
      , showing how an exploration of rural Britain could compete with
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">
       classically
      </a>
      oriented tours of the Continent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooper_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hooper-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The irregular, anti-classical, ruins became sought after sights.
     </p>
     <p>
      Picturesque-hunters began crowding the
      <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">
       Lake District
      </a>
      to make sketches using
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claude_Glass" title="Claude Glass">
       Claude Glasses
      </a>
       tinted portable mirrors to frame and darken the view, and named after the 17th century landscape painter
      <a href="/wiki/Claude_Lorrain" title="Claude Lorrain">
       Claude Lorrain
      </a>
      , whose work Gilpin saw as synonymous with the picturesque and worthy of emulation. As Malcolm Andrews remarks, there is something of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big-game_hunter" title="Big-game hunter">
       big-game hunter
      </a>
      in these tourists, boasting of their encounters with savage landscapes, capturing wild scenes, and fixing them as pictorial trophies in order to sell them or hang them up in frames on their drawing room walls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OTRS_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-OTRS-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gilpin himself asked: shall we suppose it a greater pleasure to the sportsman to pursue a trivial animal, than it is to the man of taste to pursue the beauties of nature?
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooper_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hooper-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After 1815 when Europe was available to travel again after the wars, new fields for picturesque-hunters opened up in Italy. Anna Jameson wrote in 1820: Had I never visited Italy, I think I should never have understood the word
      <i>
       picturesque
      </i>
      , while
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">
       Henry James
      </a>
      exclaimed in
      <a href="/wiki/Albano_Laziale" title="Albano Laziale">
       Albano
      </a>
      in the 1870s: I have talked of the picturesque all my life; now at last I see it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_works">
       Notable works
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Picturesque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable works">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Combe" title="William Combe">
        William Combe
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rowlandson" title="Thomas Rowlandson">
        Thomas Rowlandson
       </a>
       published an 1809 poem with pictures called
       <i>
        The Tour of Doctor Syntax in Search of the Picturesque
       </i>
       which was a satire of the ideal and famously skewered Picturesque-hunters.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Gilpin_(priest)" title="William Gilpin (priest)">
        William Gilpin
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Three Essays: On Picturesque Beauty; On Picturesque Travel; and on Sketching Landscape: to which is Added a Poem, On Landscape Painting
       </i>
       was published in London, 1792.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hussey" title="Christopher Hussey">
        Christopher Hussey
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        The Picturesque: Studies in a Point of View,
       </i>
       1927 focused modern thinking on the development of this approach. The picturesque idea continues to have a profound influence on
       <a href="/wiki/Garden_design" title="Garden design">
        garden design
       </a>
       and planting design.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Payne_Knight" title="Richard Payne Knight">
        Richard Payne Knight
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        An Analytical Inquiry into the Principles of Taste,
       </i>
       soon followed, and went into several editions that the author revised and expanded.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Uvedale_Price" title="Uvedale Price">
        Uvedale Price
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        An Essay on the Picturesque, as Compared with the Sublime and the Beautiful; and on the Use of Studying Pictures, for the Purpose of Improving Real Landscape
       </i>
       , revised. edition London, 1796.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Repton" title="Humphry Repton">
        Humphry Repton
       </a>
       applied picturesque theory to the practice of landscape design. In conjunction with the work of Price and Knight, this led to the 'picturesque theory' that designed landscapes should be composed like landscape paintings with a foreground, a middle ground and a background. Repton believed that the foreground should be the realm of art (with formal geometry and ornamental planting), that the middleground should have a parkland character of the type created by
       <a href="/wiki/Capability_Brown" title="Capability Brown">
        Lancelot "Capability" Brown
       </a>
       and that the background should have a wild and 'natural' character.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">
        John Ruskin
       </a>
       identified the "picturesque" as a genuinely modern aesthetic category, in
       <i>
        The Seven Lamps of Architecture.
       </i>
