      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicization" title="Anglicization">

       German

     </p>

      </a>
      " (Cityregion Arnhem-Nijmegen (neighbouring city, 15km (9mi) north)), a metropolitan area with 736,107 inhabitants (January 2011).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Population_centres">
       Population centres
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Population centres">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The municipality is formed by the city of Nijmegen, incorporating the former villages of
      <a href="/wiki/Hatert" title="Hatert">
       Hatert
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="extiw" href="//nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hees_(Nijmegen)" title="nl:Hees (Nijmegen)">
       Hees
      </a>
      and
      <a class="extiw" href="//nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neerbosch" title="nl:Neerbosch">
       Neerbosch
      </a>
      , as well as the urban expansion project of
      <a class="extiw" href="//nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waalsprong" title="nl:Waalsprong">
       Waalsprong
      </a>
      , situated north of the river
      <a href="/wiki/Waal_(river)" title="Waal (river)">
       Waal
      </a>
      and including the village of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lent,_Netherlands" title="Lent, Netherlands">
       Lent
      </a>
      and the hamlet of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%27t_Zand_(Nijmegen)" title="'t Zand (Nijmegen)">
       't Zand
      </a>
      , as well as the new suburbs of Nijmegen-Oosterhout and
      <a href="/wiki/Ressen,_Nijmegen" title="Ressen, Nijmegen">
       Nijmegen-Ressen
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The first mention of Nijmegen in history is in the 1st century BC, when the
      <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
       Romans
      </a>
      built a military camp on the place where Nijmegen was to appear; the location had great strategic value because of the surrounding hills, which gave (and continue to give) a good view over the Waal and
      <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">
       Rhine
      </a>
      valley.
     </p>
     <p>
      By 69, when the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batavians" title="Batavians">
       Batavians
      </a>
      , the original inhabitants of the Rhine and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maas_River" title="Maas River">
       Maas
      </a>
      delta,
      <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Batavi" title="Revolt of the Batavi">
       revolted
      </a>
      , a village called
      <i>
       Oppidum Batavorum
      </i>
      had formed near the Roman camp. This village was destroyed in the revolt, but when it had ended the Romans built another, bigger camp where the
      <a href="/wiki/Legio_X_Gemina" title="Legio X Gemina">
       Legio X
       <i>
        Gemina
       </i>
      </a>
      was stationed. Soon after, another village formed around this camp.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 98 Nijmegen was the first of two settlements in what is now the Kingdom of the Netherlands to receive
      <a href="/wiki/Municipium" title="Municipium">
       Roman city rights
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 103 the X
      <i>
       Gemina
      </i>
      was restationed to Vindobona, modern day
      <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">
       Vienna
      </a>
      , which may have been a major blow to the economy of the village around the camp, losing around 5000 inhabitants. In 104 Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">
       Trajan
      </a>
      renamed the town, which now became known as
      <i>
       Ulpia Noviomagus Batavorum
      </i>
      , Noviomagus for short (the origin of the current name Nijmegen).
     </p>
     <p>
      Beginning in the second half of the 4th century, Roman power decreased and Noviomagus eventually became part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">
       Frankish
      </a>
      kingdom. It has been contended that in the 8th century Emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">
       Charlemagne
      </a>
      maintained his
      <i>
       palatium
      </i>
      in Nijmegen on at least four occasions. During his brief deposition of 830, the emperor
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">
       Louis the Pious
      </a>
      was sent to Nijmegen by his son
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lothar_I" title="Lothar I">
       Lothar I
      </a>
      . Thanks to the Waal river, trade flourished.
     </p>
     <p>
      The powerful
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor">
       Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor
      </a>
      was born at Nijmegen in 1165. In 1230 his son
      <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">
       Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor
      </a>
      granted Nijmegen
      <a href="/wiki/City_rights_in_the_Low_Countries" title="City rights in the Low Countries">
       city rights
      </a>
      . In 1247, the city was ceded to the count of
      <a href="/wiki/Guelders" title="Guelders">
       Guelders
      </a>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Collateral_(finance)" title="Collateral (finance)">
       collateral
      </a>
      for a loan. The loan was never repaid, and Nijmegen has been a part of Gelderland ever since. This did not hamper trade; Nijmegen even became part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">
       Hanseatic League
      </a>
      in 1364.
     </p>
     <p>
      The arts also flourished in this period. Famous medieval painters like the
      <a href="/wiki/Limbourg_brothers" title="Limbourg brothers">
       Limbourg brothers
      </a>
      were born and educated in Nijmegen.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Revolt" title="Dutch Revolt">
       Dutch Revolt
      </a>
      , trade came to a halt and even though Nijmegen became a part of the Republic of United Provinces in 1585, it remained a border town and had to endure multiple sieges.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_on_the_waal_janvangoyen.jpg">

       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_on_the_waal_janvangoyen.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Waal river near Nijmegen, 1641.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>

     <p>
      In 1678 Nijmegen was host to the negotiations between the European powers that aimed to put an end to the constant warfare that had ravaged the continent for years. The result was the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Nijmegen" title="Treaty of Nijmegen">
       Treaty of Nijmegen
      </a>
      that, unfortunately, failed to provide for a lasting peace.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the second half of the 19th century, the fortifications around the city became a major problem. There were too many inhabitants inside the walls, but the fortifications could not be demolished because Nijmegen was deemed as being of vital importance to the defence of the Netherlands. When events in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_war" title="Franco-Prussian war">
       Franco-Prussian war
      </a>
      proved that old-fashioned fortifications were no more of use, this policy was changed and the fortifications were dismantled in 1874. The old castle had already been demolished in 1797, so that its bricks could be sold.
     </p>
     <p>
      Through the second half of the 19th century and the first half of the 20th century, Nijmegen grew steadily. The Waal was bridged in 1878 by a
      <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen_railway_bridge" title="Nijmegen railway bridge">
       rail bridge
      </a>
      and in 1936 by a car bridge, which was claimed to be Europe's biggest bridge at the time. In 1923 the current
      <a href="/wiki/Radboud_University_Nijmegen" title="Radboud University Nijmegen">
       Radboud University Nijmegen
      </a>
      was founded and in 1927 a channel was dug between the Waal and Maas rivers.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1940, the Netherlands was invaded by
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      with Nijmegen being the first Dutch city to fall into German hands. On February 22, 1944, Nijmegen was heavily bombed by
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       American
      </a>
      planes, causing great damage to the city centre. The American pilots thought they were bombing the German city of
      <a href="/wiki/Kleve" title="Kleve">
       Kleve
      </a>
      . Alleged by the Germans to have been a deliberate act, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIOD" title="NIOD">
       NIOD
      </a>
      announced in January 2005 that its study of the incident confirmed that it was an accident caused by poor communications and chaos in the airspace. Over 750 people died in the bombardment.
     </p>
     <p>
      During September 1944, the city saw heavy fighting during
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Market_Garden" title="Operation Market Garden">
       Operation Market Garden
      </a>
      . The objective in Nijmegen was mainly to prevent the Germans from destroying the bridges. Capturing the road bridge allowed the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/XXX_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XXX Corps (United Kingdom)">
       XXX Corps
      </a>
      to attempt to reach the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1st_British_Airborne_Division" title="1st British Airborne Division">
       1st British Airborne Division
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Arnhem" title="Arnhem">
       Arnhem
      </a>
      . The bridge was heavily defended by over 300 German troops on both the north and south sides with close to 20
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-tank_guns" title="Anti-tank guns">
       anti-tank guns
      </a>
      and two
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_guns" title="Anti-aircraft guns">
       anti-aircraft guns
      </a>
      , supported with artillery.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Germans' late attempt to blow the road bridge was probably foiled by a local Dutch resistance hero,
      <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Hoof" title="Jan van Hoof">
       Jan van Hoof
      </a>
      , who is said to have cut the wires to the bridge.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Germans made repeated attacks on the bridge using bombs attached to driftwood, midget submarines and later resorted to shelling the bridge with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/88mm" title="88mm">
       88mm
      </a>
      barrages. Troops were positioned on the bridge giving an excellent arc of fire in case of attack. Troops that couldn't fit onto the bridge were positioned in a bombed-out house slightly upstream of the bridge. During the shelling, the house was hit, killing six soldiers and wounding one more.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nijmegen was liberated from German captivity by the British
      <a href="/wiki/Grenadier_Guards" title="Grenadier Guards">
       Grenadier Guards
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Guards_Armoured_Division" title="Guards Armoured Division">
       Guards Armoured Division
      </a>
      , as well as elements of the American
      <a href="/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division" title="82nd Airborne Division">
       82nd Airborne Division
      </a>
      in September 1944. This city would later be used as a springboard for
      <a href="/wiki/Operation_Veritable" title="Operation Veritable">
       Operation Veritable
      </a>
      , the invasion across the Rhine River by Allied Troops.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="2728" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg/220px-Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg/330px-Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg/440px-Nijmegen_Stevenskerk_R10.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

     <p>
      More recently, on February 23, 1981, the Nijmegen
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_Department" title="Police Department">
       Police Department
      </a>
      and the Dutch
      <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">
       Army
      </a>
      stormed the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Piersonstraat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Piersonstraat (page does not exist)">
       Piersonstraat
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zeigelhof&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zeigelhof (page does not exist)">
       Zeigelhof
      </a>
      , a squatted housing block in the city centre of Nijmegen. Using two hundred
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Riot_van&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Riot van (page does not exist)">
       riot vans
      </a>
      , three
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopard_MBT" title="Leopard MBT">
       Leopard MBTs
      </a>
      , three
      <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">
       armoured personnel carriers
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">
       helicopter
      </a>
      , twelve hundred policemen, and seven hundred fifty members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" title="Armed forces">
       armed forces
      </a>
      , they evicted the squatters and demolished the block, while clouding the entire area in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teargas" title="Teargas">
       teargas
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/CS_gas" title="CS gas">
       CS gas
      </a>
      . This had an enormous backlash in local politics. While the city government wanted the squatters out to build a parking garage, most of the population wanted affordable housing to be built in the area.
     </p>
     <p>
      As of this date, Nijmegen is still known as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">
        Havana
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Waal_(river)" title="Waal (river)">
        Waal
       </a>
      </i>
      among some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-wing" title="Right-wing">
       Right-wingers
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Socialist Party (Netherlands)">
       Socialist Party
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/GreenLeft" title="GreenLeft">
       Green Party
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Labour Party (Netherlands)">
       Labour
      </a>
      have a solid two-thirds majority in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_Council" title="City Council">
       City Council
      </a>
      , making Nijmegen the only major city in the
      <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
       Netherlands
      </a>
      with a solely
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">
       Left-wing
      </a>
      government. The current mayor is
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hubert_Bruls&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hubert Bruls (page does not exist)">
       Hubert Bruls
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Nijmegen celebrated its 2000th year of existence in 2005. It is considered the oldest city in the Netherlands. In gaining this qualification, it has competed with the city of
      <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">
       Maastricht
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2005, the city centre of Nijmegen was the site of the assassination of political activist
      <a href="/wiki/Louis_S%C3%A9v%C3%A8ke" title="Louis Svke">
       Louis Svke
      </a>
      by a former activist (Marcel T.). Marcel T. was arrested in 2007 in Spain and extradited to the Netherlands. Marcel T. was also accused of bank robbery. Marcel T. committed his acts out of revenge for a forcible eviction from the squatter scene by Louis Svke.
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Amphitheatre" title="Amphitheatre">

      <a href="/wiki/Valkhof_museum" title="Valkhof museum">
       Valkhof museum
      </a>

Additionally, they usually have temporary exhibitions of more and less famous artists. Not many very old buildings are left in town: first the Americans carpet-bombed it in February 1944, later the Germans shelled it for about five months after the liberation in September 1944, and finally there were a number of vigorous city planners in the 1950s, 60s and 70s who finished the demolition. There are still a few noteworthy sights, however. Valkhof hill downtown features a Carolingian chapel (eighth, ninth century AD) and a small remainder of an imperial castle that was demolished in 1798.

     <p>
      Nijmegen is famous for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Four_Day_March_Nijmegen" title="International Four Day March Nijmegen">
       International Four Day March Nijmegen
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
       Dutch
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="nl">
       <i>
        Internationale Vierdaagse Afstandsmarsen Nijmegen
       </i>
      </span>
      , informally
      <i>
       Nijmeegse Vierdaagse
      </i>
      ), an annual event starting on the third Tuesday in July, comprising four days of
      <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">
       walking
      </a>
      (distances ranging from 30 to 50km (19 to 31 miles) a day), and the accompanying festivities (the
      <i>
       Vierdaagsefeesten
      </i>
      including the independent festival
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/De-Affaire" title="De-Affaire">
        de-Affaire
       </a>
      </i>
      ), which have been drawing the largest crowds for any Dutch event in the past few years.
     </p>
     <p>
      The event invites WWII allied veterans to help celebrate their participation in the liberation of the Netherlands from German occupation. Participants from Britain, Canada, the USA, Australia and New Zealand have attended the event. Nowadays, the event is international, inviting teams from all countries. It comprises an average of 40.000 participants from 30 countries.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <i>
       Vierdaagse
      </i>
      of 2006 two people died due to the extreme hot weather (both had pre-existing health issues). It caused the organisation to cancel the rest of the event. This resulted in extra safety measures during the 2007
      <i>
       Vierdaagse
      </i>
      ; for instance, a professional weatherman was added to the organisation staff, more free water refill stations were added along the route and an extensive research program was developed to monitor the effects of hiking long distances on the human body.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
       Religion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Religion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1968, prominent liberal theologians in the Roman Catholic Church issued what is now known as the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen_Statement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nijmegen Statement (page does not exist)">
       Nijmegen Statement
      </a>
      , demanding sweeping reforms in the Vatican's Holy Office, previously known as The Inquisition, and calling for greater scope for theological inquiry. Among its signatories was the then progressive theologian Fr.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Ratzinger" title="Joseph Ratzinger">
       Joseph Ratzinger
      </a>
      , then a member of the faculty at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of Tbingen">
       University of Tbingen
      </a>
      but later a much more conservative figure as the head of the successor to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Office" title="Holy Office">
       Holy Office
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Doctrine_of_the_Faith" title="Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith">
       Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith
      </a>
      , and later still
      <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">
       Pope Benedict XVI
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Nijmegen Statement said: "Any form of Inquisition however subtle, not only harms the development of sound theology, it also causes irreparable damage to the credibility of the church". The signatories, a group of predominantly German-speaking theologians asserted that "the freedom of theologians, and theology in the service of the church, regained by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_II" title="Vatican II">
       Vatican II
      </a>
      , must not be jeopardised again." The signatories pledged their loyalty to the Pope, but argued that the teaching office of pope and bishops "cannot and must not supersede, hamper and impede the teaching task of theologians as scholars."
     </p>
     <p>
      There are several English-language religious meetings in Nijmegen. See the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.apostolic-ministry.info/expat_nijmegen/english_church_nijmegen.html" rel="nofollow">
       external link
      </a>
      for a list of church services in English (the link from the official site is obsolete as it has not being updated for some time).
     </p>

      <br style="clear: left"/>
      The city council has 39 seats. After the 2002 municipal elections, the three major parties,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GroenLinks" title="GroenLinks">
       GroenLinks
      </a>
      (9 seats),
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Labour_Party" title="Dutch Labour Party">
       PvdA
      </a>
      (8 seats) and
      <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Socialist Party (Netherlands)">
       SP
      </a>
      (6 seats) formed a coalition. Because these are all left-wing parties, Nijmegen received the nickname '
      <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">
       Havana
      </a>
      on the Waal'. Although such majorities are no exception (compare
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amsterdam_(municipality)" title="Amsterdam (municipality)">
       Amsterdam
      </a>
      ) and sometimes also form coalitions (see
      <a href="/wiki/Muntendam" title="Muntendam">
       Muntendam
      </a>
      ), this is unusual for a city this size. Since such a left-wing coalition might be possible at a national level after the 2006 general election, the achievements of this council are often scrutinised. After the 2006 municipal election such a coalition became possible in many more municipalities, making the example even more interesting.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006_Dutch_municipal_election" title="2006 Dutch municipal election">
       municipal elections of 7 March 2006
      </a>
      saw an increase of 4,6% of the votes for these three parties taken together, which could be seen as increased support for the coalition. However, nationally these parties scored much better, recovering from an electoral blow of the 2002 elections. Then again, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leefbaar" title="Leefbaar">
       Leefbaar
      </a>
      parties that caused the loss then and lost most of their votes this time have no branch in Nijmegen, which makes this comparison less valid. Among the three big parties, there was a shift from GroenLinks, who lost 6.5%, to PvdA, who won 6.4% and SP, who won 2.3%. As a result, it is no longer the biggest party. The seat assignment is now as shown in the table. The three-party coalition was returned to office.
     </p>
     <p>
      After the 2010 Dutch municipal election, the PvdA lost three of its eleven seats. Short before the elections, there were problems with the SP. Therefore, GroenLinks and the PvdA formed a coalition with the social-liberal D66.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_municipal_elections,_2014" title="Dutch municipal elections, 2014">
       municipal elections of 19 March 2014
      </a>
      saw the Socialist Party narrowly becoming the largest party in the Nijmegen city council after gaining three more seats. The Greens were only some 200 votes behind, while the Liberal Democrats (D'66) gained another seat, while Labour lost half their support, becoming as small as the liberal-conservative VVD. The coalition government was formed between the three centre-left and leftist parties SP, GroenLinks and PvdA, and a local party called The Nijmegen Group (De Nijmeegse Fractie). It also had informal support from the United Senior Party (VSP).
     </p>
     <p>
      Later in 2014, a city council member of the VVD, Paul Eigenhuijsen, left the VVD group. The former leader of the group, Hayke Veldman, had gone to the House of Representatives, and thus left the city council. Eigenhuijsen had been second on the party list, but he was not elected to the position of leader. Thereafter, he left the group and started his own one-man group, called Liberal Nijmegen.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Education">
       Education
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nijmegen is host to
      <a href="/wiki/Radboud_University_Nijmegen" title="Radboud University Nijmegen">
       Radboud University Nijmegen
      </a>
      . Founded in 1923 as the first
      <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">
       Catholic
      </a>
      university in the Netherlands, it used to be called
      <i>
       Catholic University of Nijmegen
      </i>
      until 2004, when it took its current name. As of 2013, it had 18,891 students and 5,050 staff. Radboud University runs the
      <a class="extiw" href="//nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Field_Magnet_Laboratory" title="nl:High Field Magnet Laboratory">
       High Field Magnetic Laboratory
      </a>
      which is able to achieve some of the highest fields available in Europe at 33
      <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">
       teslas
      </a>
      (continuous) and 60 teslas (pulsed). The facility is available to outside users, primarily for research purposes.
     </p>
     <p>
      The education and social work departments of the
      <a href="/wiki/HAN_University_of_Applied_Sciences" title="HAN University of Applied Sciences">
       HAN University of Applied Sciences
      </a>
      , school for higher-level vocational training are also located in Nijmegen, as are that school's medical departments.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition to these institutions, there is also an intermediate-level vocational school (
      <a class="extiw" href="//nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regionaal_opleidingencentrum" title="nl:Regionaal opleidingencentrum">
       ROC Nijmegen
      </a>
      ) and a number of secondary schools: Groenschool Nijmegen, Kandinsky College, Nijmeegse Scholengemeenschap Groenewoud (NSG), Citadel College, Stedelijke Scholengemeenschap Nijmegen (SSGN), Canisius College, St. Jorisschool, Mondial College, the Stedelijk Gymnasium (formally the "Latijnse school", founded in the 16th century), the Karel de Grote College, Montessori College and the Dominicus College. Of note is also
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leefwerkschool_Eigenwijs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leefwerkschool Eigenwijs (page does not exist)">
       Leefwerkschool Eigenwijs
      </a>
      , which caters to students from all over the Netherlands who have been repeatedly expelled from "regular" high schools. Leefwerkschool Eigenwijs has its roots in the local activist movement of the early 1980s and is the only school of its kind recognised in the Netherlands.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nijmegen is also an important centre of
      <a href="/wiki/Psycholinguistics" title="Psycholinguistics">
       Psycholinguistics
      </a>
      , home to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute" title="Max Planck Institute">
       Max Planck Institute
      </a>
      of Psycholinguistics and the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Donders_Institute_for_Brain,_Cognition_and_Behaviour&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behaviour (page does not exist)">
       Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behaviour
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">
       Nobel Prize
      </a>
      for Physics in 2010 was awarded to
      <a href="/wiki/Andre_Geim" title="Andre Geim">
       Andre Geim
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Novoselov" title="Konstantin Novoselov">
       Konstantin Novoselov
      </a>
      while at Radboud University "for groundbreaking experiments regarding the two-dimensional material graphene."
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="More_room_for_the_river_Waal">
       More room for the river Waal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: More room for the river Waal">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In order to prevent flooding in the near future, the Dutch government is changing the course of more than 30 rivers throughout the country. These measures taken along the rivers IJssel, Lek, Maas and Waal are known as Room for the River.
     </p>
     <p>
      Room for the river Waal as it passes Nijmegen is one of these measures. The river Waal not only has a sharp bend near Nijmegen, it also forms a bottleneck. In the years 1993 and 1995 this led to high water and floods. To prevent this from happening again and in order to protect the inhabitants of the city against the water, action is needed.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the case of Nijmegen, this involves moving the Waal dike in Lent and constructing an ancillary channel in the flood plains. This will create an island in the Waal and a unique urban river park with lots of possibilities for recreation, culture, water and nature. The solution is far-reaching, yet sustainable and safe. The large scale project involves the construction of three bridges, new dikes and concrete water barriers and the excavation of a large channel, among others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
       Climate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Climate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nijmegen is one of the warmest cities of the Netherlands, especially during summer, when the highest temperatures in the country are usually measured in the triangle Roermond  Nijmegen  Eindhoven. The lack of north-south oriented mountain ranges in Europe make this area prone to sudden shifts in weather, giving the region a semi-continental climate.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some of the northernmost wineries in the world are found just outside of Nijmegen, around
      <a href="/wiki/Groesbeek" title="Groesbeek">
       Groesbeek
      </a>
      , a suburban village south-east of Nijmegen.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/2006_European_heat_wave" title="2006 European heat wave">
       2006 European heat wave
      </a>
      , closest official weather station Volkel reached a high of 36.7C (98.1F) on 19 July. The heat wave coincided with that year's Four Day Marches, which were cancelled after the first day, when two people died of
      <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">
       hyperthermia
      </a>
      -related causes. Temperatures on that day, 18 July, reached around 36C (96.8F) in the city.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nijmegen is in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USDA" title="USDA">
       USDA
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_zone" title="Hardiness zone">
       Hardiness zone
      </a>
      7b and
      <a href="/wiki/American_Horticultural_Society" title="American Horticultural Society">
       AHS
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_zone#AHS_Heat_Zones" title="Hardiness zone">
       Heat zone
      </a>
      3.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the following table the monthly precipitation is measured in Nijmegen itself, the monthly sunshine at nearby official weather station
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EHDL" title="EHDL">
       Deelen
      </a>
      (25 kilometres (16 miles) north) and the other numbers at closest official weather station
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EHVK" title="EHVK">
       Volkel
      </a>
      (23km (14mi) south-west).
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">
       Transport
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nijmegen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Transport">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Nijmegen has five train stations:
      <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen_railway_station" title="Nijmegen railway station">
       Nijmegen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen_Dukenburg_railway_station" title="Nijmegen Dukenburg railway station">
       Nijmegen Dukenburg
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen_Heyendaal_railway_station" title="Nijmegen Heyendaal railway station">
       Nijmegen Heyendaal
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen_Lent_railway_station" title="Nijmegen Lent railway station">
       Nijmegen Lent
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen_Goffert_railway_station" title="Nijmegen Goffert railway station">
       Nijmegen Goffert
      </a>
      . The central station is connected to the national Intercity network. The bus company Breng (a subsidiary of Connexxion) operates the city buses in the Arnhem-Nijmegen metropolitan area.
     </p>
     <p>
      Like most Dutch cities, bicycles are an important mode of transport. The city is connected to Arnhem, 18km (11mi) to the north, by a "fietssnelweg" (fast cycle highway) which crosses the Snelbinder bridge in the city. During 2010-2012 the cycle highway received upgrades to further encourage the use of bicycles for transport between Nijmegen and Arnhem.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The river is a busy freight transport route, with barges to the city as well as passing through on the way between the industrial regions of Germany and the docks at Amsterdam, Rotterdam and Hook of Holland. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maas-Waal_Canal" title="Maas-Waal Canal">
       Maas-Waal Canal
      </a>
      also carries freight through the city.
     </p>
     <p>
      On 23 November 2013, a new bridge, de Oversteek (the Crossing), that connects the western part of the city to the northern shore, was opened. It relieves congestion of the old road bridge which was built in 1936. It is called de Oversteek since its built on the location of an important rivercrossing by the Allied forces during Operation Market Garden in WWII, September 1944. The plan was to liberate the Waalbrug from the northern shore, which they managed to do, but with great losses. The bridge was opened by two of the soldiers, a daughter of General Gavin made a speech and familymembers of WWII soldiers were present. To remember the 48 American soldiers that died, every night 48 pairs of streetlights will go on one by one, from north to south.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
