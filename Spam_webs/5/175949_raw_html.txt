     <p>
      The
      <b>
       pistol offense
      </b>
      is an
      <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">
       American football
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Formation_(American_football)" title="Formation (American football)">
       formation
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/American_football_strategy" title="American football strategy">
       strategy
      </a>
      partially developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Ault" title="Chris Ault">
       Chris Ault
      </a>
      in 2005 while he was head coach at the
      <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Wolf_Pack_football" title="Nevada Wolf Pack football">
       University of Nevada
      </a>
      . It is a hybrid of the traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Shotgun_formation" title="Shotgun formation">
       shotgun
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Single_set_back" title="Single set back">
       single back
      </a>
      offenses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the pistol offense, also commonly referred to as the "pistol formation", the
      <a href="/wiki/Quarterback" title="Quarterback">
       quarterback
      </a>
      lines up four yards behind the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_(American_football)" title="Center (American football)">
       center
      </a>
      , which is much closer than the seven-yard setback in a traditional shotgun formation. The
      <a href="/wiki/Running_back" title="Running back">
       running back
      </a>
      then lines up three yards directly behind the quarterback, which is in contrast to the shotgun, where they are beside each other. It is argued that the position of the quarterback in the pistol formation strikes an advantageous compromise: the quarterback is close enough to the
      <a href="/wiki/Line_of_scrimmage" title="Line of scrimmage">
       line of scrimmage
      </a>
      to be able to read the defense, as with run situation sets such as the
      <a href="/wiki/I_formation" title="I formation">
       I Formation
      </a>
      , but far enough back to give him extra time and a better vision of the field for passing plays, as in the
      <a href="/wiki/Shotgun_formation" title="Shotgun formation">
       shotgun
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The pistol formation is thus very versatile, particularly if the quarterback himself is a threat to run the ball, which makes it difficult for the defense to correctly anticipate the play.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This flexibility is enhanced by the
      <a href="/wiki/Option_offense" title="Option offense">
       Read Option
      </a>
      , where the quarterback reacts to the response of the defensive players to the
      <a href="/wiki/Snap_(American_and_Canadian_football)" title="Snap (American and Canadian football)">
       snap
      </a>
      , and makes a rapid decision whether to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handoff_(American_football)#Offensive_otherwise_noted" title="Handoff (American football)">
       hand off
      </a>
      the ball to the running back, keep it and complete a
      <a href="/wiki/Forward_pass" title="Forward pass">
       pass
      </a>
      to a downfield receiver, or keep it and run himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pistol_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Michael Taylor of Mill Valley created and developed the Pistol Offense (called the "Shotgun I" at that time) in 1999 while playing softball. Michael's teammate was a college football coach at Division III Ohio Northern University named Tom Kaczkowski, and during a conversation, he mentioned that his team needed to go into a new direction because his running back was quick, but his quarterback was tall and slow. Armed with this new idea of Shotgun I, Michael spent weeks developing new formations and schemes as a way of maintaining a downhill running game, while allowing his quarterback to be comfortable in the pass game. The plays worked; the backfield set was exclusively two back and included a full complement of runs to both the I back and the offset back (aligned next to the quarterback).
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nevada,_Reno" title="University of Nevada, Reno">
       University of Nevada
      </a>
      head coach
      <a href="/wiki/Chris_Ault" title="Chris Ault">
       Chris Ault
      </a>
      popularized the single back alignment (and renamed it the "Pistol") in 2005.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While the pistol offense has been experimented with by dozens of
      <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">
       college football
      </a>
      teams such as
      <a href="/wiki/LSU_Tigers_football" title="LSU Tigers football">
       LSU
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_Orange_football" title="Syracuse Orange football">
       Syracuse
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Hoosiers_football" title="Indiana Hoosiers football">
       Indiana
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Tigers_football" title="Missouri Tigers football">
       Missouri
      </a>
      , Ault's Nevada Wolf Pack is most strongly associated with the formation. Using the Pistol Offense, during the 2009 season, Nevada led the nation in rushing at 345 yards a game and were second in total offense at 506 yards. The Wolf Pack also became the first team in college football history with three 1,000-yard rushers in the same season: quarterback
      <a href="/wiki/Colin_Kaepernick" title="Colin Kaepernick">
       Colin Kaepernick
      </a>
      and running backs Luke Lippincott and
      <a href="/wiki/Vai_Taua" title="Vai Taua">
       Vai Taua
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_Championship_Subdivision" title="Football Championship Subdivision">
       Football Championship Subdivision
      </a>
      team
      <a href="/wiki/2010_James_Madison_Dukes_football_team" title="2010 James Madison Dukes football team">
       James Madison University
      </a>
      used "The Pistol" to help beat #13 ranked
      <a href="/wiki/2010_Virginia_Tech_Hokies_football_team" title="2010 Virginia Tech Hokies football team">
       Virginia Tech
      </a>
      on September 11, 2010. The pistol has also made the transition to the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">
       NFL
      </a>
      , mainly being used by the
      <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Panthers" title="Carolina Panthers">
       Carolina Panthers
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Cam_Newton" title="Cam Newton">
       Cam Newton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Griffin_III" title="Robert Griffin III">
       Robert Griffin III
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">
       Washington Redskins
      </a>
      , as well as the aforementioned
      <a href="/wiki/Colin_Kaepernick" title="Colin Kaepernick">
       Colin Kaepernick
      </a>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_49ers" title="San Francisco 49ers">
       San Francisco 49ers
      </a>
      , who in the NFL Playoffs versus the
      <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay_Packers" title="Green Bay Packers">
       Green Bay Packers
      </a>
      set the all-time single game rushing record for a quarterback with 181 yards. Along with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildcat_offense" title="Wildcat offense">
       wildcat
      </a>
      , the pistol has added more of a college "playmaker" aspect to the professional game.
     </p>
     <p>
      On December 5, 2010, the
      <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Steelers" title="Pittsburgh Steelers">
       Pittsburgh Steelers
      </a>
      used the Pistol offense so quarterback
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Roethlisberger" title="Ben Roethlisberger">
       Ben Roethlisberger
      </a>
      could play with a bad foot.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">
       Advantages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pistol_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Advantages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The pistol formation can be used in a variety of ways, because the quarterback is closer to the
      <a href="/wiki/Line_of_scrimmage" title="Line of scrimmage">
       line of scrimmage
      </a>
      than a traditional shotgun formation. This allows him to see more easily over the line and make downfield reads. He will also get the ball snapped to him faster, which can alter timing patterns greatly for a preparing defense. The pistol offense can effectively use draw plays, counters, and options using three-
      <a href="/wiki/Wide_receiver" title="Wide receiver">
       wide receiver
      </a>
      formations or multiple
      <a href="/wiki/Tight_end" title="Tight end">
       tight ends
      </a>
      combined with a fullback for pass protection. In a pistol formation, handoffs occur 2-3 yards closer to the line of scrimmage than in the shotgun. In the traditional shotgun, run plays are most effectively run to the side opposite the running back, without a cutback to the other side. In the pistol, they can be effectively executed to either side of the QB, opening up more options for the offense. This can make for a more effective running game, but may limit pass efficiency due to quicker recognition of play action by linebackers and defensive backs. This formation works well with dual threat quarterbacks who can both throw and run and is also used when quarterback's mobility has been limited by injury.
     </p>
