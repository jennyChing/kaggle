        <h1>
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/18-underrated-primal-paleo-blogs-you-should-be-reading/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to 18 Underrated Blogs You Should Be Reading">
          18 Underrated Blogs You Should Be Reading
         </a>
        </h1>
        <div class="wwsgd" style="display:none;">
         <div style="border: thin dotted black; padding: 10px 10px 0 10px ; margin-bottom: 10px;">
          <p>
           Welcome! If you want to lose weight, gain muscle, increase energy levels or just generally look and feel healthier you've come to the right place.

           Here's where to start:

            and
            <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/primal-blueprint-101/?utm_source=mda_wwsgd&amp;utm_medium=link&amp;utm_campaign=mda_wwsgd_pb_101" target="_blank" title="Primal Blueprint 101">
             Primal Blueprint 101
            </a>
            pages to learn more about the Primal Lifestyle.
           </li>
           <li>
            Subscribe to my
            <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/subscribe-to-blog/?utm_source=mda_wwsgd&amp;utm_medium=link&amp;utm_campaign=mda_wwsgd_newsletter" target="_blank" title="Subscribe">

           <li>
            Cut to the chase by visiting

            </a>
            . There you'll find
            <a href="http://primalblueprint.com/categories/Store/Books-and-Media/?utm_source=mda_wwsgd&amp;utm_medium=link&amp;utm_campaign=mda_wwsgd_books" target="_blank" title="Books and Media">
             books
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://primalblueprint.com/categories/Store/Services/?utm_source=mda_wwsgd&amp;utm_medium=link&amp;utm_campaign=mda_wwsgd_services" target="_blank" title="Services">
             support options
            </a>
            , and the best
            <a href="http://primalblueprint.com/categories/Store/Supplements/?utm_source=mda_wwsgd&amp;utm_medium=link&amp;utm_campaign=mda_wwsgd_supplements" target="_blank" title="Supplements">
             supplements
            </a>
            on the planet to help you take control of your health for life.
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           Thanks for visiting!
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignright" height="212" src="http://i247.photobucket.com/albums/gg158/MDA2008/MDA2010/blog.jpg" title="B-L-O-G" width="320"/>
         Blogging is the lifeblood of the growing Primal/paleo movement, as you know, and new veins, arteries, and capillaries are popping up every day. Im calling this the underrated blog post, but really, given the steadily increasing span of the
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/contest-poll-the-2010-grokfeast-challenge/" target="_self" title="2010 Grokfeast Challenge">
          community
         </a>
         , even the most underrated blogger has a fair amount of readership. In fact, as I review my list of underrated blogs, they all get a significant amount of readers and comments. Oh well, theyre still worth listing. I suppose you could say were all underrated in the grand scheme of things.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/stuff-that-i-read-or-watch-and-you-should-too/" target="_self" title="Stuff That I Read (or Watch), And You Should Too">
          I did a similar thing a year ago
         </a>
         , and its time to do it again. The blogs I listed in 2010 remain essential, but these all deserve consideration to be included in your blog rotation. Theyre not all Primal, or even strictly paleo, and some of them rarely ever mention exercise and nutrition, but they will enrich your lives and broaden your knowledge base.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-21143">
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://healthcorrelator.blogspot.com/" target="_blank" title="Health Correlator">
          <strong>
           Health Correlator
          </strong>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Ned Kock is a stats guy who examines
         <em>
          correlations
         </em>
         in health statistics and discusses what conclusions can be drawn from them. He never loses sight of the limitations of numbers even has he acknowledges their power, and Nedruns a real tight ship that doesnt get nearly enough attention (although thats changing, hopefully moreso after today). A couple weeks ago,
         <a href="http://freetheanimal.com/" target="_blank" title="Free the Animal">
          Richard Nikoley
         </a>
         tweeted that Ned was the most underrated blogger around, and I think I agree.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.mattmetzgar.com/" target="_blank" title="Matt Metzgar">
           Matt Metzgar
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Sort of the Jack Handy of paleo blogging (only not as funny), Matt gives short summaries of papers, two or three line posts on his difficulties reaching a full squat and possible solutions, a paragraph or two on running barefoot through his neighborhood and the looks he gets, a short but sweet series on prebiotics, pesticides, or fasted training it never takes more than a minute to read Matts stuff, but Im always interested to see what hes got cooking.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://thatpaleoguy.blogspot.com/" target="_blank" title="That Paleo Guy">
           That Paleo Guy
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Jamie, formerly of Primal Muse, is a cycling coach/nutritional consultant (I think) in New Zealand. Besides trading witty tweets with Dr. Emily Deans, he also enjoys posting long (but never long-winded, a rare skill) posts on nutrition, training, and the confluence of the two, especially in regards to cycling. I come from the endurance world, and I appreciate his take on staying paleo in the cyclists world of sugar gels, pasta meals, and osteoporosis.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://evolutionarypsychiatry.blogspot.com/" target="_blank" title="Evolutionary Psychiatry">
           Evolutionary Psychiatry
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Emily Deans, MD, is a superstar. She graciously wrote a
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/your-brain-and-the-primal-blueprint/" target="_self" title="Your Brain and the Primal Blueprint">
          guest post
         </a>
         a couple weeks back, as you probably recall, in which I plugged her blog, but I thought it deserved another. Every time I read a blog by Emily (or any of the other MDs on board with this stuff), I get happy because I realize that the tide may be turning after all. Whats cool about her blog is that although she focuses on the essentiality of fixing your food, exercise, stress, and sleep to avoid or mitigate mental illness, she recognizes the place of medication in certain cases. Thats Primal in a nutshell, really: taking advantage of all the unique benefits of both modernity and tradition when they make sense.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://blog.cholesterol-and-health.com/" target="_blank" title="The Daily Lipid">
          <strong>
           The Daily Lipid
          </strong>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Chris Masterjohn may occasionally eat wheat, soak legumes, and question the universality of gluten intolerance, but man is it hard to argue with his dedication to sticking to what the evidence shows. Plus, he loves liver, egg yolk, and dates Melissa McEwen. Read this man.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://huntgatherlove.com/" target="_blank" title="Hunt Gather Love">
           Huntgatherlove
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Speaking of Melissa, shes also got a good blog. She first appeared (to my knowledge) in that
         <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/01/10/fashion/10caveman.html" target="_blank" title="Cavemen in the City">
          NY Times piece
         </a>
         highlighting cavemen in the city. Read her, too.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://hunter-gatherer.com/" target="_blank" title="Hunter-Gatherer">
           Hunter-Gatherer
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         From NY Times to Colbert to a Trappist monastery, John Durant takes paleo to new arenas. Hes working on a book, is big in the NY barefoot running community, and shows intense interest in what zoos feed their animals  a man of many interests.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://perfecthealthdiet.com/" target="_blank" title="Perfect Health Diet">
           Perfect Health Diet
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Dynamic duo Shou-Ching and Paul Jaminet are scientists (he a former astrophysicist, she a molecular biologist) who healed their long-standing medical issues with a low-carb, paleo-esque diet. Their book and blog attempts to fashion a sustainable, healthy, longevity-promoting, science-based way of eating and supplementing. They use a little more rice, a little less protein than I might, but the Perfect Health Diet deserves a VP spot in the Primal/paleo tent.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://blog.sethroberts.net/" target="_blank" title="Seth's Blog">
           Seths Blog
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Seth Roberts is great. He only writes about nutrition every so often, but hes a great believer in the power of self-experimentation (eating more animal fat improved his sleep and he has the stats to prove it, for example), especially if you figure out how to quantify and record the results. Having had dinner with him, I can say that hes really engaging in person, too.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://evolvify.com/" target="_blank" title="Evolvify">
           Evolvify
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Witty, slightly sarcastic Andrew tackles evolutionary psychology more than biology, the latter of which he mostly leaves to others. Hes fond of saying the rabbit hole goes much deeper than diet and exercise, and Id agree with that. Common topics include the intertwining of sexual attraction, relationships, business, and politics with evolution. If youre fairly comfortable with what youre putting in your mouth and how youre stressing your physical capacity, you might find Evolvifys examination of the other stuff interesting.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.eatmoveimprove.com/" target="_blank" title="Eat Move Improve">
           Eat Move Improve
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Rarely updated, but when it is, its always worth reading. Steven Low and Chris Salvato are fitness professionals (either budding or established) with gymnastics and parkour backgrounds who provide excellent training progressions, intuitive ways to determine why your knee or your lower back hurt and what to do about it, and they are extremely thorough. My personal favorites include
         <a href="http://www.eatmoveimprove.com/2009/11/shoes-sitting-and-lower-body-dysfunctions/" target="_blank" title="Shoes, Sitting, and Lower Body Dysfunctions">
          Shoes, Sitting, and Lower Body Dysfunctions
         </a>
         and
         <a href="http://www.eatmoveimprove.com/2010/02/so-you-hurt-your-lower-back/" target="_blank" title="So, You Hurt Your Lower Back">
          So, You Hurt Your Lower Back.
         </a>
         That last one helped me figure out (and fix!) a tweaked SI joint once.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.drbriffa.com/" target="_blank" title="Dr. Briffa">
           Dr. Briffa
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Dr. John Briffa is, as the name suggests, an MD. Hes always good for analyzing the latest study your skeptical friends gleefully shove your way.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://toddhargrove.wordpress.com/" target="_blank" title="Better Movement">
           Better Movement
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Todd Hargrove brings a fascinating neuro-centric perspective on movement, pain, and exercise that makes a lot of sense (though perhaps not intuitively, initially). I was particularly fond of
         <a href="http://toddhargrove.wordpress.com/2011/02/21/is-the-mind-body-connection%E2%80%9D-new-agey-or-scientific/" target="_blank" title="Is the Mind Body Connection New Agey or Scientific?">
          this post
         </a>
         from a few months ago, in which Todd explains the distinction between the mind body connection and mind over matter, which are entirely different concepts. Very cool blog.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://diystrengthgear.blogspot.com/" target="_blank" title="DIY Strength Training">
           DIY Strength Training
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.marksdailyapple.com/primal-blueprint-fitness/" target="_self" title="Primal Blueprint Fitness">
          PBF essential movements
         </a>
         with bodyweight not quite enough for you? The local globo gym not supportive of your Vibram-and-grunting habits? Forget all that and make your own workout equipment to supplement your training. DIY Strength Training regularly updates with video tutorials explaining how to make your own strength training gear. Yeah, the resultant equipment isnt shiny and pretty, and its actually quite grimy, but you dont need all that to get strong.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.primaltoad.com/" target="_blank" title="Primal Toad">
           Primal Toad
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         A young, tenacious dude excited about Primal living whos really interested in spreading the word and helping others make the leap, too. I dig the guy, especially after meeting him in person at PrimalCon. Plus, hes always got some deal or another cooking, so be sure to check out his blog and see whats Toads up to.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://thehealthyskeptic.org/" target="_blank" title="The Healthy Skeptic">
           The Healthy Skeptic
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Chris Kresser is a practitioner out of the Bay Area who also happens to be a fantastic health blogger. Chrisll usually pick a topic  like, maybe, hypothyroidism or diabesity  and run with a series of posts devoted to that topic. Its a good way to really flesh out a subject. In particular, I liked his
         <a href="http://thehealthyskeptic.org/9-steps-to-perfect-health-1-dont-eat-toxins" target="_blank" title="9 Steps To Perfect Health  #1: Dont Eat Toxins">
          latest series
         </a>
         on 9 Steps to Perfect Health. Again, hes not exactly underrated, what with his practice and his readers, but here he is all the same.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.gnolls.org/" target="_blank" title="Gnolls.org">
           Gnolls.org
          </a>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         J. Stanton is a truly underrated paleo blogger, but that seems to be changing. I myself need to catch up on his work. If you do, too, check out the blog.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <a href="http://www.dietdoctor.com/" target="_blank" title="Diet Doctor">
           Diet Doctor
          </a>
          (plus the
          <a href="http://www.kostdoktorn.se/" target="_blank" title="Kost Doktorn">
           Swedish version
          </a>
         </strong>
         )
        </p>
        <p>
         Dr. Andreas Eenfeldt hails from Sweden, where hes helped usher in the burgeoning low-carb, high-fat revolution. As the name of his blog implies, hes a doctor of family medicine whos seen  firsthand  what decades of low-fat dogma has wrought on the health of Swedes (and everyone else). He gave a great
         <a href="http://vimeo.com/29464690" target="_blank" title="Evolution of a Diet Revolution">
          talk at the Ancestral Health Symposium
         </a>
         , and hes got a great blog.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Internet is a massive place, so I know Ive missed more than a few.
         <strong>
          Now its on you  what are your favorite blogs? Who have I missed?
         </strong>
         Who is everyone else missing? It can be a Primal blog, or it can be one having nothing at all to do with nutrition, fitness, or health. I just want to know what you guys are reading.
         <strong>
          Share your niche, underground and underrated blogs with the world in the comment section.
         </strong>
         <br/>
        </p>

                  <p>
                   In my opinion, fat from animal sources (in their original, natural package) is  more satiating than the same fat in isolated form. The higher satiety level is probably enough to have an effect in the long run.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   The reason is that animal fat contains a number of micronutrients, particularly the all-important fat-soluble vitamins. Satiety of a food class is strongly correlated with how nutrient-dense a that food class is. This has to take into account both macro and micronutrient content.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   I suspect even that nutrient databases tend to underestimate the micronutrient content of animal fat, possibly because the isolation process destroys some of those nutrients.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                   Ever wonder why fish oil softgels have quite a lot of soy oil added to them? It is because once the o-3 fats are isolated, they become highly unstable. Soy oil helps stabilize them; I dont know how much. 38 g of sardines will give you more net o-3 than 2 fish oil softgels (see: bit.ly/gsaJI3).
