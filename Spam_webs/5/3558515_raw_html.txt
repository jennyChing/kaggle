        <div class="sem-blog-entry-content">
         <p>
          <img alt="time-is-money" class="alignright size-full wp-image-10210" height="200" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/time-is-money.jpg?v=5" width="200"/>
          Im a data guy. Creative, too, but very much driven by data and ROI.
         </p>
         <p>
          If youre ever struggling to come up with content for your site/blog (or if you simply wish to better understand the type of content putting money into your pocket), read on!
         </p>
         <p>
          Despite being free,
          <strong>
           Google Analytics is an extremely powerful tool
          </strong>
          . Its easy to set up, too. Simply sign up for a Google account and
          <a href="http://www.semrush.com/blog/publications/how-to-use-analytics-to-determine-the-dollar-value-of-content/" target="_blank">
           install your tracking code
          </a>
          , and youre ready to start collecting data.
         </p>
         <p>
          Its through a bit of additional planning and setup, however, that it
          <i>
           really
          </i>
          starts to benefit your business.
          <span id="more-10110">
          </span>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Setting goals
         </h2>
         <p>
          Any time we take on a new client, one of the first things I look at is whether or not they have goals set up in Google Analytics. It baffles me just how many times theres absolutely nothing being tracked, especially when its their second or third time working with an agency.
         </p>
         <p>
          Another frequent issue is finding goals that have been set up without any sort of assigned monetary value. Even I made this mistake when starting out 
          <strong>
           its extremely common
          </strong>
          .
         </p>
         <p>
          If youre new to this process (or simply want a refresher), Google has some great fundamental resources to help you get the ball rolling:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           <a class="broken_link" href="https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/1032415" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
            Set up and edit Goals
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a href="https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/3046660" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
            Goal types
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a class="broken_link" href="https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/2907896" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
            Goal Value
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          E-commerce sites make it easy to evaluate revenue and ROI because Google Analytics collects all sales data (
          <a class="broken_link" href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/methods/gaJSApiEcommerce" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
           with a bit of tweaking
          </a>
          ). If you
          <i>
           arent
          </i>
          running an e-commerce site,
          <strong>
           its vital that you assign appropriate monetary values to the completion of goals on your website
          </strong>
          . Doing so will open you up to
          <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t9-CS2v8wcc" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
           a whole new world
          </a>
          of tracking capabilities  it doesnt matter if youre a large corporation or one-(wo)man operation running a site about your cat.
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="analytics-no-goal-values" class=" wp-image-10212 aligncenter" height="253" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/analytics-no-goal-values.jpg?v=5" width="506"/>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Determining Goal Value
         </h2>
         <p>
          Not all goals are created equal.
         </p>
         <p>
          A blog subscriber  a newsletter signup  a service inquiry.
         </p>
         <p>
          For example, if the average one-time sale of your primary service is $10,000 and 15% of your leads make that purchase, each lead is worth $1,500. If you want to factor in profit margin and that margin is, say, 40%, each lead is actually worth $600.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Formulas
          </strong>
          <br/>
          $10,000 x 15% = $1,500/lead
          <br/>
          $10,000 x 40% x 15% = $600/lead
         </p>
         <p>
          Establishing these figures also lets you know how much money you can spend on lead acquisition and still be profitable.
         </p>
         <p>
          For micro conversions that lead people down your funnel, their value is typically much less. Someone subscribing to your newsletter might be worth $5, while someone subscribing to your blog may only be worth $2.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once these figures are determined and goals are set, you can start looking at the individual value each page and article brings to your website.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Understanding Page Value
         </h2>
         <p>
          Page Value is a hit-level metric, meaning that it applies only to a specific page and not to the overall session of a users visit. By comparison, Goal Completions and Goal Value are session-level metrics and not directly attributable to one page. In other words, it would be inaccurate to look at the number of goal completions provided for a specific page. Individual pages dont deliver goals and conversions; they simply move people through your funnel and
          <i>
           toward
          </i>
          a conversion.
         </p>
         <p>
          One of the best articles you could ever read on this topic and its importance is Avinash Kaushiks article on
          <a href="http://www.semrush.com/blog/publications/how-to-use-analytics-to-determine-the-dollar-value-of-content/" target="_blank">
           session- and hit-level metrics
          </a>
          . As such, what you want to focus on when looking at the impact of specific content on your site is the Page Value metric.
         </p>
         <p>
          For an in-depth explanation of how Page Value is calculated, see
          <a href="http://analytics.blogspot.com/2012/07/understanding-and-using-page-value.html" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
           this article
          </a>
          by Google.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Evaluating Page Value
         </h2>
         <p>
          Start by going to Behavior &gt; Site Content &gt; All Pages. This is what youll see:
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="analytics-behavior-site-content" class="aligncenter wp-image-10213" height="334" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/analytics-behavior-site-content.png?v=5" width="445"/>
         </p>
         <h2>
          Applying filters
         </h2>
         <p>
          Depending on where your content resides and what it is you wish to analyze, you may want to add a filter that includes traffic from only a specific directory. If, for example, you want to evaluate
          <em>
           only your blog posts
          </em>
          , and your blog resides in
          <strong>
           /blog/
          </strong>
          , click the advanced link next to the search box and use the following filter:
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="analytics-blog-traffic-filter" class="aligncenter wp-image-10214" height="96" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/analytics-blog-traffic-filter.png?v=5" width="497"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Once applied, only pages within the
          <strong>
           /blog/
          </strong>
          directory will be displayed.
         </p>
         <p>
          Looking through the results, however, you may see certain categories or types of URLs that you dont want to be included:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           /page/2/
          </li>
          <li>
           /page/3/
          </li>
          <li>
           /category/uncategorized/
          </li>
          <li>
           /tag/
          </li>
          <li>
           etc.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          Without the optimization of your blogs settings/plugins, there may be a handful of others.
         </p>
         <p>
          Instead of excluding each of these one filter at a time, you can use a simple line of
          <a href="https://support.google.com/analytics/answer/1034324?hl=en" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="">
           regex
          </a>
          to capture everything at once:
         </p>
         <p>
          /page/|/uncategorized/|/tag/
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="analytics-exclude-regex-filter" class="aligncenter wp-image-10215" height="152" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/analytics-exclude-regex-filter.png?v=5" width="497"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          The final thing to consider before evaluating Page Value is sample size. A page may
          <i>
           appear
          </i>
          to have significant value, but if the number of Unique Pageviews is too low, that value can be misleading. Send more traffic its way and see what happens before making any conclusions.
         </p>
         <p>
          Also note that too low will, of course, depend on the amount of traffic your site receives as a whole.
         </p>
         <p>
          At the very least, I recommend setting a filter to exclude pages with less than 10 Unique Pageviews. This helps weed out the anomalies:
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="analytics-exclude-unique-pageviews-filter" class="aligncenter wp-image-10216" height="208" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/analytics-exclude-unique-pageviews-filter.png?v=5" width="497"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          With all filters applied, you now have a slightly tighter set of data with which to work.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Interpreting Page Value
         </h2>
         <p>
          Sort by Page Value and start looking at trends, patterns and top performers:
         </p>
         <p style="text-align: center;">
          <img alt="analytics-blog-page-value" class="aligncenter wp-image-10217" height="496" src="//d2lrxcz2ihvajy.cloudfront.net/blog/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/analytics-blog-page-value.png?v=5" width="473"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Perusing this data, you can see exactly what type of content, articles and categories are providing the most value.
         </p>
         <p>
          Though the traffic in this example is limited, these same steps work for any site, regardless of current popularity.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Conclusion
         </h2>
         <p>
          With a bit of preparation and filter usage, you can know
          <em>
           exactly
          </em>
          what content is most valuable to your website and continue creating similar pieces. Whether for you or a client, taking a bit of extra time to gather and evaluate this information is a no-brainer.
         </p>
