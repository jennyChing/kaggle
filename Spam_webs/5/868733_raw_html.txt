      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Top seller: Jamie Elliott is king of the kids." src="/content/dam/images/1/m/w/d/p/3/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.1mw5vz.png/1430369240167.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Top seller: Jamie Elliott is king of the kids.
        <cite>
         <i>
          Photo: Getty Images
         </i>
        </cite>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         <a href="http://twitter.com/theagesport" target="_blank">
          Follow the Age Sport on Twitter
         </a>
        </strong>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Jamie Elliott sells more merchandise than any other Collingwood player. The Magpies have confirmed thatare more No.5s requested for the backs of black and white jumpers than Scott Pendlebury's No.10 or Dane Swan's No.36.
      </p>
      <p>
       Collingwood says their small forward enjoys particularpopularity among kids - he signed easily the most memberships in a recent telethon and narrowlytrails Pendlebury in Instagram followers.Like the young shaggy-hairedversion of Dale Thomas, Elliott'sappeal rests on an eye-catching talent for the spectacular - in his case, anability to take hangers and kick goals.
      </p>
      <p>
       But this capacity for taking flight - which has put Elliott up there, so to speak,with Pendlebury and Swan - also presents a challenge for the Pies, who understand the necessity ofkeeping their talented small forward's feet on the ground - on the field, and elsewhere.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       On Anzac Day, Elliott featured in one spectacular piece of play that earned him a tick, and another that brought a friendly censure from the club's forward line coach, Steve Grace.
      </p>
      <p>
       The former was Collingwood's contentious opening goal, when Elliott stood in front of Jesse White, snatched the crumb that fell front and centre, and goaled from the proverbial row eight. If Elliott was literally out of bounds when he booted the goal, he was playing very much within team boundaries- hecrumbedfrom a tall teammate, rather than leaping to attempt a high mark.
      </p>
      <p>
       Later, in the final quarter, the ball was kicked to about 15 metres from goal, where TravisCloke was stationed. This time, rather than remaining earth-bound, Elliott leapt at the ball at the same time as Cloke, with the result that a likely goal (not certain,this would have beenCloke's shot) was spoiled.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Jamie effectively spoiled Trav," said Grace, who believes Elliott's"explosive" speed and power can see him become the AFL's best defensive forward.
      </p>
      <p>
       Elliott is an exceptional high mark, possibly the best for his size in the competition. But, as he and the Collingwood coaches recognise, 178-centimetreforwards can't rely on an aerial game. At that height, flying ain't much of a living. Eventually small marking players find themselves manned by quick 190cm-plus players - think Ben Hart or even Dustin Fletcher. Hawthorn's Mark Williams and Fremantle/Collingwood's Paul Medhurst did not sustain success as small marking forwards.
      </p>
      <p>
       Grace calls the art of crumbing- which Elliott demonstrated in the out of bounds goals and which he's working on - "a high growth area for him". At this stage of Elliott's career, there's what Grace described as a"50/50 split" between aerial-sourced goals - marks, including those on the lead -and ground level majors. "He's extremely smart in his leading patterns."
      </p>
      <p>
       Elliott played as a target forward - effectively full-forward - in his junior days. "My role has changed," he told ABC radio, following his four-goal haul against St Kilda. "I've had to learn - crumbing hasn't come naturally to me, so I'm trying to add everything to my game to be more dangerous."
      </p>
      <p>
       Elliott said he did not want to remove marking - "I need to keep that up" - but he wanted to improve his crumbing "and getting to the drop of the ball".
      </p>
      <p>
       Grace summed up Elliott's challenge. "It's not a matter of changing his game. It's a matter of growing his game." Elliott's license to fly was greater when deep in attack. "The closer to goal he is, the greater license."
      </p>
      <p>
       The forward coach worked with Hayden Ballantyne and Michael Walters at Fremantle andreckoned that, with his speed, Elliott had the capacity to be the game's best defensive forward. "I've got no doubt he can do it...He's got some defensive KPIshe has to meet and he takes great pride in those... he stacks up [defensively]extremely well."
      </p>
      <p>
       Grace speaks of Elliott's work on"disciplining the method" and remaining more involved in the game. The ball, as he observed, is on the deck more often than not in the scoring territory.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thomas was arguably installed as a marketing machine at Collingwood before he had established himself, though he did develop into an All-Australian and pivotal premiership player. The Pies are mindful of the pitfalls for a player who receives premature adulation by dint of some spectacular grabs or goals.
      </p>
      <p>
       They are confident, though, that Elliott's feet remain on terra firma. "He's got a balanced view of life," Grace said. "He's got firm family orientation." Elliott, he said, was a "training beast" who wanted to extract all that he could from his talent.
      </p>
      <p>
       He's aiming high. But onlyflying occasionally.
      </p>
