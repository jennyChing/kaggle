            <a href="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/making-incredible-sauce-at-home/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Making Incredible Sauces at Home">
             Making Incredible Sauces at Home
            </a>
           </h1>
           <div class="meta single">
            <span class="meta-author">
             <a href="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/author/rgjones/" rel="author" title="Posts by G. Stephen Jones">
              G. Stephen Jones
             </a>
             
            </span>
            <span class="meta-date">
             September 16, 2012
            </span>
            <span class="meta-comments">
             
             <a href="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/making-incredible-sauce-at-home/#comments" title="Comments for Making Incredible Sauces at Home">
              6 Comments
             </a>
            </span>
           </div>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
            <img alt="Making Incredible Sauce at Home" class="caption aligncenter" height="360" src="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/c79100bd9a1c81231de8bfb99f98f395.jpg" title="Making Incredible Sauce at Home" width="500"/>
           </p>
           <h3>
            <span class="heading3">
             My 5 Step Method for Preparing Professional Quality Brown Sauces
            </span>
           </h3>
           <p>
            As a home cook, one of the hardest things for me to accomplish when first starting out was making a rich velvety brown sauce to serve on steak, lamb, veal, pork, or even chicken. I could put together a pretty good pan sauce using the dripping after sauting or roasting a piece of meat but it never quite had that incredible intensity that I experience when dining out at a great restaurant.
           </p>
           <p>
            It wasnt until I spent some time reading about sauce making and speaking with a few chef friends that I learned it isnt so much the how to but the ingredients that make the difference. Using my 5-step method to making a great brown sauce is easy if you have all the necessary ingredients and I will give you some great resources for find them.
           </p>
           <h4 class="heading4">
            <span class="heading4">
             What is a Sauce?
            </span>
           </h4>
           <p>
            According to Food Lovers Companion, a sauce is a thickened, flavored liquid designed to accompany food in order to enhance and bring out its flavor. Now that can cover a lot of territory. It goes on to say, In the days before refrigeration, however, sauces were more often used to smother the taste of foods that had begun to go bad. Im sure we have all had experiences that have proven this true even in the days of refrigerationThink back to your high school cafeteria.
           </p>
           <p>
            But in the 19th century, the French created an intricate process for making sauces that is still being taught in cooking schools all over the world. This process involves numerous steps and if you have the time, I highly recommend James
            <span class="heading4">
             Petersons, Sauces and Raymond Sokolovs The Sauciers Apprentice. They are entirely devoted to just this subject.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p style="text-align: center;">
           </p>
           <div align="center" style="margin-top: 30px; margin-bottom: 30px">
            <div class="sam-container sam-place" data-sam="1" id="c7170_7_4">
             <img height="60" src="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/little_rg.png" style="opacity:0.4; margin-right: 20px" width="60"/>
             <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
             </script>
             <!-- RG Top 25 468x60 -->
             <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-4335816852793865" data-ad-slot="3287677284" style="display:inline-block;width:468px;height:60px">
             </ins>
             <script>
              (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
             </script>
            </div>
           </div>
           <h4>
            <span class="heading4">
             Why is it so difficult to make great sauces at home?
            </span>
           </h4>
           <p>
            As Chef Alton Brown says in his cookbook, Im Just Here For The Food, By and large, most home cooks dont do sauceand thats too bad. Traditional sauces are indeed scary.
           </p>
           <p>
            The process just to prepare the key ingredients that go into a sauce takes a lot of time. It starts by making a stock with roasted beef and/or veal bones, reducing them for at least 12 hours, continuously skimming the pot,straining the liquid to remove the bones, reducing some more, adding a roux (a mixture of flour and butter used as a thickening agent) and you now have a nice brown sauce or sauce espagnole.
           </p>
           <p>
            A professional chef will then reduce this brown sauce further to make a demi glace, the mother of all sauces. These guys spend a lot of time in cooking school learning how to do this and take great pride in the sauces they can make with it. These stock reductions are the foundation to hundreds of classic sauces being served in fine restaurants.
           </p>
           <h4 class="heading4">
            <span class="heading4">
             Why cant I just use a bouillon cube?
            </span>
           </h4>
           <p>
            Unless you want to ruin an expensive cut of meat by covering it with a salty, corn syrup reduction, I would stay away from bouillon cubes or any of those cheap packets of instant sauces you see in your local supermarket. Just look at the ingredients to see if whats inside is real or simply processed. You cant build a sound house without a strong foundation. The same is true when making sauces.
           </p>
           <h4>
            <span class="heading4">
             Whats a home cook to do?
            </span>
           </h4>
           <p>
            Since making a great sauce at home depends of finding a good stock reduction or demi glace, I would like to offer you the following resources.
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Make it yourself. A great experience but one most of us will not take on.
            </li>
            <li>
             Make friends with the chef at your favorite upper end restaurant and see if he or she will share some of their brown gold with you. Be prepared to beg or pay through the nose to get them to part with this stuff. Not likely, but worth a try.
            </li>
            <li>
             Hire a personal chef to make it for you. You may end up having to subscribe to years worth of dinners, which isnt all that bad, but you will have your demi.
            </li>
            <li>
             Buy it a high-end gourmet store. If you really search hard, you may be able to find stock reductions in the refrigerator section of some really high end stores. You wont get much, but you dont need a lot and it wont be cheap.
            </li>
            <li>
             Williams-Sonoma is now selling their own stock reductions. I have not had that much experience with them but they usually sell high quality items.
            </li>
            <li>
             Find demi glace and stock reductions that are used in high-end restaurants and are available to home cooks. More Than Gourmet makes the best products I know of that fit that description. You can learn all about these products and buy them on-line here 
             <a href="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/secret-to-great-sauces">
              Commercial Demi Glace
             </a>
            </li>
           </ul>
           <h4 class="heading4">
            My Quick &amp; Easy 5 Step Method Quick Look
           </h4>
           <ol>
            <li>
             Saut a shallot in butter
            </li>
            <li>
             Deglaze pan with wine
            </li>
            <li>
             Add
             <a href="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/cooking-stocks-at-home">
              demi glace
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             Reduce
            </li>
            <li>
             Season with salt &amp; pepper
            </li>
           </ol>
           <h4>
            <span class="heading4">
             More Details
            </span>
           </h4>
           <ol>
            <li>
             Saut a chopped shallot or small onion in one ounce of butter (1/4 stick) for 1-2 minutes until translucent.
            </li>
            <li>
             Deglaze with 1/2-cup red wine and reduce to an essence (approximately one tablespoon of remaining liquid). Be sure to remove the pan from the heat before deglazing.
            </li>
            <li>
             Add 8 ounces of
             <a href="http://www.reluctantgourmet.com/cooking-stocks-at-home">
              demi-glace
             </a>
             .
            </li>
            <li>
             Reduce the sauce until it is thick enough to coat a spoon.
            </li>
            <li>
             Season with freshly ground pepper to taste.
            </li>
           </ol>
           <p>
            One last item that is optional but often used by professional chefs is a pat of butter. It adds a bit more flavor and shine to the finished sauce.
           </p>
           <h4>
            <span class="heading4">
             Alternatives
            </span>
           </h4>
           <p>
            At this point you have a delicious sauce that you can serve or use as a base and layer in more flavors by adding additional ingredients including fresh herbs and spices, fruits, chutneys, relish, or cream.
           </p>
           <p>
            If you are adding mushrooms or other ingredients that need to cook a bit, add them to the pan right after you add the wine and let them cook while the wine is reducing.
           </p>

                 Hi Arnie, if you do make it yourself, theres no reason why you cant freeze it. If you want to make it in one dinner batches, you might try freezing it in one of those plastic ice cube trays, then you have them at the ready. Its a lot of work to make it yourself but thats what they use in top end restaurants for making classic sauces. If you decide to purchase a commercial brand, you are close but its not hockey sticks, but pucks. You can buy the Demi Glace Gold in 1.5 ounce puck size containers and 1 puck reconstitutes into about 8 ounces. There on links on the page for where to purchase them.
