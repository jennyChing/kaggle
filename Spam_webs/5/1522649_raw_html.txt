                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://www.artistsandart.org/2011/04/tulipmania-and-dutch-golden-age.html">
                      Tulipmania and the Dutch Golden Age
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-5125403317814795009">
                     With strange perfumes he did the roses taint,
                     <br/>
                     And flowrs themselves were taught to paint.
                     <br/>
                     The tulip, white, did for complexion seek,
                     <br/>
                     And learnd to interline its cheek;
                     <br/>
                     Its onion root they then so high did hold,
                     <br/>
                     That one was for a meadow sold.
                     <br/>
                     (Andrew Marvell, The Mower Against Gardens)
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-hrMsK3FVNbM/TZpAQUl1keI/AAAAAAAAcNw/LRLPGLIoYTY/s1600/Jean+Joseph-Xavier+Bidauld.jpg" imageanchor="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;" target="_blank" title="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration">
                       <img border="0" height="400" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-hrMsK3FVNbM/TZpAQUl1keI/AAAAAAAAcNw/LRLPGLIoYTY/s400/Jean+Joseph-Xavier+Bidauld.jpg" width="305"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <b>
                       Jean Joseph-Xavier Bidauld
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     There was a moment in the 17th century when Europe went mad - for tulips, elegant Turkish wildflowers with a smooth, green stem, with an exceptional capacity for mutation, infinitely changing their shape.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-O6JllZA8zVg/TZpARJuqQVI/AAAAAAAAcN4/kFaQ3IyAhLY/s1600/SAMUEL+VAN+HOOGSTRATEN+1647+Portrait+of+Johan+Cornelisz.+Vijgeboom+and+His+Wife.jpg" imageanchor="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;" target="_blank" title="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration">
                       <img border="0" height="303" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-O6JllZA8zVg/TZpARJuqQVI/AAAAAAAAcN4/kFaQ3IyAhLY/s400/SAMUEL+VAN+HOOGSTRATEN+1647+Portrait+of+Johan+Cornelisz.+Vijgeboom+and+His+Wife.jpg" width="400"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <b>
                       SAMUEL VAN HOOGSTRATEN, Portrait of Johan Cornelisz. Vijgeboom and His Wife, 1647
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     There were numerous types of tulips: cup-shaped, bright colored or ranging from jewel tones to pastel shades, with feathery petals, or with petals that are either twisted, curled or waved. The tulip images were embroidered on dresses and woven into tapestries,
                     <a href="http://www.artistsandart.org/2009/11/pieter-claesz-still-life-breakfasts.html">
                      painted in a great number of still lifes
                     </a>
                     . The archives of St. Peters Basilica in Rome contain glorious pictures of tulips along with samples of Renaissance silk, the 17th century books, the lists of indulgences sold in Germany.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-YJ9esjdk85s/TZpAML7DjjI/AAAAAAAAcNE/f70uFf1x62c/s1600/Ambrosius+Bosschaert+flowers+in+a+vase+1606.jpg" imageanchor="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;" target="_blank" title="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration">
                       <img border="0" height="400" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-YJ9esjdk85s/TZpAML7DjjI/AAAAAAAAcNE/f70uFf1x62c/s400/Ambrosius+Bosschaert+flowers+in+a+vase+1606.jpg" width="335"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <b>
                       Ambrosius Bosschaert, Flowers in a Vase, 1606
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Transported from Turkey as bulbs, then passed on to the Dutch middlemen, tulips set in the European gardens. Tulip mania gripped all of Europe, infecting even the poorest people and not just the Dutch Republic. Mike Dash mentions in his book Tulipomania: The Story of the World's Most Coveted Flower and the Extraordinary Passions It Aroused "...the personalities involved in the creation of the tulip market, such as the orphans who made a fortune selling their late father's tulip bulbs and the man who owned a dozen extremely rare bulbs and wouldn't part with them at any price.
                     <br/>
                     "...the Fuggers, the fabulously rich Bavarian family of bankers who were to the 15th century what the Rothschilds and the Rockefellers were to the 20th... were growing tulips in Augsburg by the beginning of the 1570s.
                     <br/>
                     "There were tulips in Vienna by 1572. They were in Frankfurt by 1593, and they reached the south of france by 1598.. Bulbs were sent to England as early as 1582, where they were soon grown in great quantity."
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-bl8Pa8R81Ak/TZpANMMH59I/AAAAAAAAcNM/GR4jE77DG14/s1600/Balthasar+van+der+Ast+++Fruchtkorb+%25281632%2529.jpg" imageanchor="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;" target="_blank" title="17th century tulip still life painting, Tulipmania of Dutch Golden Age, fun facts about Tulipmania of  Dutch Golden Age, still life paintings, graphics,decorative arts,book illustration">
                       <img border="0" height="225" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-bl8Pa8R81Ak/TZpANMMH59I/AAAAAAAAcNM/GR4jE77DG14/s400/Balthasar+van+der+Ast+++Fruchtkorb+%25281632%2529.jpg" width="400"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <b>
                       Balthasar van der Ast, Fruchtkorb (1632)
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     The price for tulips rose continually to vertiginous heights for 40 years in the early 17th century.
                     <br/>
                     At the height of the Tulipmania in November 1636, a single bulb of the legendary Semper augustus variety fetched the equivalent of 10 years' wages for the average worker; a couple of Viceroy bulbs cost the equivalent of an Amsterdam canal house. One unfortunate foreign sailor made himself rather unpopular with his employer by slicing up what he thought was an onion as a garnish for his herring. An English amateur botanist, intrigued by an unknown bulb lying in his host's conservatory, proceeded to dissect it, and was put in jail until he could raise an astronomical 4000 guilders. (Lonely Planet Amsterdam By Karla Zimmerman, Caroline Sieg, Ryan Ver Berkmoes).
                     <br/>

                      Top 10 The Most Expensive Art and Other Things Sold At Auctions in 2010
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8700970890727693690">
                     <h2>
                      1. Most expensive painting
                     </h2>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_niZGRkyACV0/TR-j9vCkkZI/AAAAAAAAAiw/ZhlXtpiJHf8/s1600/8.jpg" imageanchor="Most expensive painting" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="320" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_niZGRkyACV0/TR-j9vCkkZI/AAAAAAAAAiw/ZhlXtpiJHf8/s320/8.jpg" width="256"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     Picasso's Nude, Green Leaves and Bust, sold for $106.5 million at Christie's and set a world record for any work of art sold at auction breaking up a previous record - $104 million paid for Giacometti sculpture.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <h2>
                      2. Most expensive book
                     </h2>
                     Sold at Sotheby's London for $11,542,683, one of the most monumental printed books ever produced, John James Audubon's Birds of America, set a new world record for any printed book ever sold at auction. It is estimated there are only around 100 copies of this book which is more than three feet tall and contains 435 hand-colored prints. It was bought by London dealer Michael Tollemache for 7,321,250, making it the most expensive printed book in the world. In 2000, a different copy sold for $8.8 million.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://www.artistsandart.org/2010/12/80-rearest-snuff-boxes-1.html">
                      40 Rearest Snuff Boxes
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-3016636170786052695">
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      Give me women, wine, and snuff
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      Untill I cry out "hold, enough!"
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      You may do so sans objection
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      Till the day of resurrection:
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      For, bless my beard, they aye shall be
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      My beloved Trinity.
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      John Keats
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Christopher Columbus introduced tobacco into Europe after his voyage (1494-96) to South America. There he saw American Indians snuffing an unknown powder, some of which Columbus brought back to Europe, where it quickly became fashionable.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/SsIwZUViD2I/AAAAAAAAHho/3pEwJxtC6U0/s1600-h/mother-of-pearl+snuffbox+1765.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                       <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5386921315802419042" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/SsIwZUViD2I/AAAAAAAAHho/3pEwJxtC6U0/s400/mother-of-pearl+snuffbox+1765.jpg" style="cursor: pointer; display: block; height: 361px; margin: 0px auto 10px; text-align: center; width: 400px;"/>
                      </a>
                      <span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">
                       Mother of Pearl Snuff Box, 1765
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     In the 16th century French Ambassador in Lisbon Jean Nicot sent snuff tobacco, popular in Portugal, to Queen of France Catherine de Medici, who suffered from migraines. And that immortalized his name. It was believed that sniffing tobacco healed migraine. At the court of Francis II of France, son of Catherine, snuff tobacco became fashionable amongst the ladies and gentlemen of the aristocracy, and at the reign of Louis XIV it was part of the court etiquette.
                     <br/>
                     Aristocracy was passionate about its snuff habits and even built special rooms for storing their snuff.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/SsIwY7vAYJI/AAAAAAAAHhg/yOFIse7Eax4/s1600-h/chrysoprase++berlin+1755.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                       <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5386921309198377106" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/SsIwY7vAYJI/AAAAAAAAHhg/yOFIse7Eax4/s400/chrysoprase++berlin+1755.jpg" style="cursor: pointer; display: block; height: 308px; margin: 0px auto 10px; text-align: center; width: 400px;"/>
                      </a>
                      <span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">
                       Chrysoprase, Berlin, 1755
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Splendid noblemen used to treat each other with snuff tobacco while the containers for it were luxury items and were considered as jewelery variety, they were supposed to be handled with the utmost elegance and intended to impress. Made of silver and gold, rich in detail, adorned with precious stones, sometimes playing popular melodies while being opened, snuff-boxes rooted into the everyday life of all levels of the population and were an indispensable accessory for men and women too. Even Andersen's tin soldier in the fairy tale admired the beautiful dancer leaning against the snuff-box on the table.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/SsIwYm29roI/AAAAAAAAHhY/n_TQI7TH-WI/s1600-h/1765+black+marble+snuffbox-Berlin.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                       <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5386921303594610306" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/SsIwYm29roI/AAAAAAAAHhY/n_TQI7TH-WI/s400/1765+black+marble+snuffbox-Berlin.jpg" style="cursor: pointer; display: block; height: 332px; margin: 0px auto 10px; text-align: center; width: 400px;"/>
                      </a>
                      <span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">
                       Black Marble Snuff Box, Berlin, 1765
                      </span>
                     </div>

                      Dance Cards - Carnets de Bal - Ballspenden
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6072021145129822937">
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/S7-8mJ7_0-I/AAAAAAAATx0/e44-szGaiPI/s1600/CARNET+DE+BAL+DU+XVIIIEME+SIECLE.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                       <img alt="Dance Cards - Carnet de Bal - Ballspende" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5458288637084947426" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/S7-8mJ7_0-I/AAAAAAAATx0/e44-szGaiPI/s400/CARNET+DE+BAL+DU+XVIIIEME+SIECLE.jpg" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 400px; height: 258px;"/>
                      </a>
                      <span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">
                       Dance Card, 18th century
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     DUCHESS OF BERWICK. So strange Lord Windermere isnt here. Mr.Hopper is very late, too.  You have kept those five dances for him, Agatha?
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     LADY AGATHA.  Yes, mamma.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     DUCHESS OF BERWICK.  [Sitting on sofa.]  Just let me see your card. Im so glad Lady Windermere has revived cards. - Theyre a mothers only safeguard.
                     <br/>
                     You dear simple little thing!  [Scratches out two names.]  No nice girl should ever waltz with such particularly younger sons!  It looks so fast!  The last two dances you might pass on the terrace with Mr. Hopper.
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: center; font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">
                      Lady Windermere's Fan: A Play About a Good Woman by Oscar Wilde
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <br/>

                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/S7-8lDtMXNI/AAAAAAAATxs/ED10iGjWOKg/s1600/edwardian+carnet+de+bal.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">

                      </a>
                      <span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">
                       Edwardian Dance Card
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Sometimes the ladies would have dance cards on one side of which the various dance titles and composers would be listed that an orchestra would play throughout the ball - usually 18-24 dances per evening, with spaces on the other in which they or their partners would write in their names for the dances they preferred with the helpful little pencil that was attached to the card by a ribbon, a useful memory aid when there were many dances.
                     <br/>
                     They were generally given only to ladies, while gentlemen were evidently expected to remember to whom they had promised dances. But in a while men started using them as well - dance cards helped avoid confusion.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/S7-8k-tdvaI/AAAAAAAATxk/qTzpVLH4q9U/s1600/GOLD+AND+ENAMEL+DANCE+CARD+WITH+CONCEALED+WATCH+c850.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                       <img alt="Dance Cards - Carnet de Bal - Ballspende" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5458288616891334050" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_0n9IExEpmh8/S7-8k-tdvaI/AAAAAAAATxk/qTzpVLH4q9U/s400/GOLD+AND+ENAMEL+DANCE+CARD+WITH+CONCEALED+WATCH+c850.jpg" style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 400px; height: 367px;"/>
                      </a>
                      <span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">
                       Gold and Enamel Dance Card with Concealed Watch, c.1850
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Dance cards originated in the 18th century, but their use first became widespread in 19th century Vienna and later beyond in Europe and the US. At first it was a fan with the name of a partner inscribed on the back; later, manufacturers created real objects d'art, made of precious materials like silver, ivory or pearl and porcelain. The dance cards remained in use well into the 1920s.
