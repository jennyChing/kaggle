        <header class="entry-header">
         <h1 class="entry-title">
          Social Media for HR (Challenges)
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="sep">
           Posted on
          </span>
          <a href="http://dmti.in/blog/social-media-for-hr-challenges/" rel="bookmark" title="11:29 am">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2013-12-05T11:29:11+00:00">
            December 5, 2013
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="by-author">
           <span class="sep">
            by
           </span>
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://dmti.in/blog/author/sunder-ramachandran/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Sunder Ramachandran">
             Sunder Ramachandran
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p>
          I recently had the opportunity to lead Indias 1st Social Media for HR course for MBA HR students at ITM Business School. This course was designed by
          <a href="http://www.dmti.in/" target="_blank">
           Digital Marketing Training Institute
          </a>
          . The course was a 1st in many senses i.e. flipped classrooms, twitter backchannel, tweet-chats with HR leaders, google-hangouts &amp; more. You can learn about all the good part in this blog:
          <a href="http://dmti.in/blog/social-media-for-hr/" target="_blank">
           <strong>
            Social for HR
           </strong>
          </a>
         </p>
         <p>
          But, like with most things in life, there were challenges as well and I thought it was my responsibility to blog about some of my frustrations &amp; challenges with the students. My attempt here is to reflect on the experience &amp; also enable the readers (especially MBA students) to introspect &amp; fix these issues. It always surprised me how easy these issues were to fix.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 1: The Tier 1 vs Tier 2B-school debate -
          </strong>
          This is a sensitive topic &amp; most business school alumni get quite territorial about it. I did not go to a B-school so its easy for me to be objective about this one. The B-school I taught is in the Tier 2 / Tier 3 league (based on rankings / other data in the public domain). All said &amp; done, Tier 1 does attract a somewhat more smarter, career focused sort of an individual. This was sometimes a challenge area in the setup that I was facilitating so I had to be reasonable about what students brought to the table in terms of inherent skills / smarts. Obviously, there were exceptions to the rule who will give most Tier 1 peers a run for their money but I talk for the majority of the audience that seem to fit my description.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge:
          </strong>
          I had to present everything in a consumable format. Nothing too complex, or requiring too much analysis. Everytime, I made an assumption about their existing levels of knowledge, I was reminded of the importance of keeping it simple.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 2: The 21 year old syndrome -
          </strong>
          Most / All students in my class were straight out of college so there were limited / almost no perspectives on what work looks like in the real world in 2013. This has nothing to do with this particular B-school as its more a reflection of what management education in India looks like.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge:
          </strong>
          I had to often correct myself when I made an assumption that the students related to an example I had shared. The trick was to make them visualise. When you are engaging a student who has no work experience, everything is hypothetical  the problem as well as the solution. This calls for some serious facilitation skills.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 3: Academic orientation -
          </strong>
          We are all products of the Indian education system which is content rich &amp; interaction poor. This makes us put the teacher on a pedestal.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge:
          </strong>
          I was using the flipped model of teaching with this class &amp; it took me 3 weeks (of the 8 week course) to get this going with the students. Making learning conversational sounds in but needs the learner to reorient themselves. That can be quite tough when they have never experienced it. Asking them to engage in meaningful discussions often drew a blank expression adding to my frustration as a facilitator. The lesson was to stay the course &amp; keep trying without losing patience.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 4: Not keeping their eyes &amp; ears open -
          </strong>
          Social is all about that but students in general lacked awareness about whats going on around them in the world of business. Most were not reading a pink paper on a daily basis or subscribing to any business magazines. Most were ignorant of company / market trends.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge
          </strong>
          : This lack of awareness presented a roadblock in them being able to appreciate the business challenges to which social media is a potential solution. It often frustrated me as to why they would not engage in regular reading which gave them insights into the world of business. Reading, listening, observing are all a strong second to experiencing things first hand. This is so doable but often not done.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 5: Limited self awareness -
          </strong>
          Why an MBA was a mystery question for most of them. Asking, Why an MBA in HR would send most running into their caves. This again is the common state of affair in most B-schools today. The reasons for choosing HR as a specialisation range from I think I am good with people to Good communication skills to the plain &amp; honest I could not get marketing / finance specialisation.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge -
          </strong>
          Engaging with social media requires a certain amount of self awareness &amp; being sure of yourself. Increased self awareness leads to increased confidence which manifests in the quality of your social interactions. Students often felt shy / conscious of initiating chats or reaching out to professionals on social media due to this reason.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 6: Not staying invested -
          </strong>
          Social media is all about learning to play a role in the wider community. In order to contribute to the community, you need to stay invested. Developing a point of view takes time but is a worthwhile investment. Students would often drop in &amp; out of the HR communities that I had introduced them to.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge
          </strong>
          :  Leverage happens over a period of time. Since not many students were consistent with their participation in social communities, there were only a handful who truly leveraged the contacts &amp; the power of the network. Most students found it difficult to understand &amp; appreciate the concept of a personal learning network.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Reality 7: Give to get
          </strong>
          - Most students expected instant results. I opened up my rolodex and introduced them to some of the top HR / Learning leaders in the country but very few realised the potential &amp; the value of contributing before leveraging the relationship.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Challenge
          </strong>
          - Most students never established a meaningful conversation or connection with the leaders whom they were introduced with. A lot of them also felt overwhelmed &amp; could not muster the courage to reach out to these senior folks.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           In summary
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          This experience was a good reminder that An eager learner always takes the cake, while an eager teacher / trainer is only a good to have.
         </p>
         <p>
          I have to say that everything that I have learnt about facilitation as a skill in the last decade got tested in these 8 weeks. My key message to the students was that no subject is ever finished. No theme is isolated from another. Knowledge is continuous and ideas flow. The only thing that all of us can do to stay relevant is to keep our eyes &amp; ears open &amp; respond to change.
         </p>
         <p>
          The best way to learn is to teach. If you get an opportunity to teach, lap it up. You would be surprised at the enrichment that the experience can provide.
         </p>
