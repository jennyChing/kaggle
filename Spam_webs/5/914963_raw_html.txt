         <p>
          Now that youre pregnant, youve probably discovered how many decisions you have to make a day  and how momentous some of them can seem because they now involve that precious little person who is about to join your family. One way to ease your mind about these big decisions is do a lot of research about the topic youre considering.
         </p>
         <p>
          When it comes to saving your babys cord blood, the more you know the better. Cord blood, which contains stems cells that can treat a host of diseases, can be stored in a private or public bank. What should you take into account as you make your decision about where to store your babys cord blood? First, read what you can about
          <a href="/cord-blood/faqs.aspx">
           cord blood banking
          </a>
          . Also look into
          <a href="/pregnancy/cord-blood/cord-blood-research.aspx">
           cord blood research
          </a>
          in terms of your familys medical history to see what diseases it can treat.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Private Cord Blood Banking
         </h2>
         <p>
          The American Academy of Pediatrics suggests that you consider private cord banking if you have a family member with a disease that may be treatable with stem cells. Thats because your babys cord blood may be a better match for that sick family member than a nonfamilial donation. Some families also choose to save their babys cord blood as a kind of health insurance  in the event that down the line more diseases may be treatable with stem cells, and youll have those genetically unique cells available to your child and your family.
         </p>
         <h2>
          How to Choose a Private Cord Blood Bank
         </h2>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 1: Talk with your practitioner about private cord blood banking
          </strong>
          . Its best to discuss the issue early in your second trimester so that you have more than enough time to make your decision (even though it may be difficult to think about your babys cord blood when you havent even felt those first little kicks yet!).
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 2: Make sure your practitioner is willing and able to collect the cord blood.
          </strong>
          Its rare that a doctor or midwife couldnt (or wouldnt) do this simple and quick procedure, but he or she may charge you a fee.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 3: Do your research.
          </strong>
          Any cord blood bank youre considering should be accredited with the
          <a href="http://www.aabb.org" rel="nofollow" target="_new">
           American Association of Blood Banks
          </a>
          (AABB), which has a list of accredited banks. You can also visit parentsguidecordblood.org, a site that offers detailed information about every private (and public) bank in the US.
         </p>
         <p>
          Once youve narrowed your choices, its worth calling them to find out more information about their services. Youll want the banks representative to explain key things to you: How the bank collects and stores the blood (there are different collection and storage methods and youll want to make sure that the bank complies with federal standards); how viable the banks cord-blood samples are compared with other banks (youll want to choose a bank that has demonstrated good odds of getting a usable blood sample); and how stable the company is (you dont want the bank to go out of business, so explore the pros and cons of choosing a lesser-known, smaller bank versus a larger, well-known bank that has been operating for longer). Parentsguidecordblood.org has a comprehensive list of questions to ask the bank.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 4: Decide on a private cord blood bank.
          </strong>
          Ideally, youll want to enroll with your bank of choice by the end of your second trimester or week 27 (since babies can be born early), but if thats not possible, aim to sign up before week 34. Most banks charge a collection fee of $1,500 to $2,000, and a yearly fee of about $125, and many offer payment plans. And if there is a pressing family medical need (such as a family member whos in need of a transplant), some private banks offer free or discounted banking.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 5: Wait for your cord-blood collection kit in the mail.
          </strong>
          Once you sign up, the cord blood bank will mail you a collection kit so that youll have it ready for the big day (or night!). The kit will probably have a medical form for you to fill out, plus sealed medical supplies your practitioner will use to collect the cord blood. Fill out the form, sign it, and put it back in the kit (but leave the kits medical supplies sealed). Pack the kit away in your hospital bag so you wont have to scramble to find it.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 6: Give the cord-blood kit to your practitioner (or the staff).
          </strong>
          This will remind him or her about your cord blood banking decision, and it will alert the medical staff that theyll need to collect some blood from you before delivery (the kit comes with the materials your practitioner will need to collect and send in your blood).
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 7: Give birth!
          </strong>
          Right after you deliver (whether its a vaginal or cesarean birth), your practitioner will clamp the umbilical cord (a painless, safe, and easy process) and collect the cord blood with the supplies provided in your kit. (You wont even notice this is happening. Youll be gazing at that baby!) Your partner can even cut the cord because that doesnt affect the collection process.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 8: Call the bank once the collection is complete.
          </strong>
          You'll probably want to assign this task to your spouse or birthing partner since you'll be a bit (or very!) distracted. Once the bank gets your call, it will arrange for a courier to pick up the cord blood kit.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 9: Hand off the cord blood kit.
          </strong>
          Your spouse or birthing partner will give the cord-blood kit to a secure courier who sends the kit off to the laboratory for storage. It will arrive at the lab no later than 36 hours after you deliver your precious bundle.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 10: Wait to be notified.
          </strong>
          The private cord blood bank will contact you to let you know that your cord blood arrived safely and to tell you how much they were able to collect and process.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Step 11: Rest easy.
          </strong>
          Each year the private cord blood bank will bill you for an annual fee (about $125) and your babys cord blood will be available to you at any time should you need it.
         </p>
