     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        El Dorado
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 1966 American
      <a href="/wiki/Western_(genre)" title="Western (genre)">
       Western
      </a>
      film produced and directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hawks" title="Howard Hawks">
       Howard Hawks
      </a>
      and starring
      <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">
       John Wayne
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mitchum" title="Robert Mitchum">
       Robert Mitchum
      </a>
      . Written by
      <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Brackett" title="Leigh Brackett">
       Leigh Brackett
      </a>
      and loosely based on the novel
      <i>
       The Stars in Their Courses
      </i>
      by
      <a href="/wiki/Harry_Brown_(writer)" title="Harry Brown (writer)">
       Harry Brown
      </a>
      , the film is about a gunfighter who comes to the aid of an old frienda drunken sheriff struggling to defend a rancher and his family against another rancher trying to steal their water. The gunfighter and drunken sheriff are helped by an aging Indian fighter and a young gambler. The supporting cast includes
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Caan_(actor)" title="James Caan (actor)">
       James Caan
      </a>
      as the young gambler,
      <a href="/wiki/Charlene_Holt" title="Charlene Holt">
       Charlene Holt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Fix" title="Paul Fix">
       Paul Fix
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hunnicutt" title="Arthur Hunnicutt">
       Arthur Hunnicutt
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Michele_Carey" title="Michele Carey">
       Michele Carey
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_George" title="Christopher George">
       Christopher George
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      was filmed on location in
      <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">
       Tucson, Arizona
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kanab,_Utah" title="Kanab, Utah">
       Kanab, Utah
      </a>
      , and was shot in
      <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">
       Technicolor
      </a>
      . The paintings in the credits are by Olaf Wieghorst, who plays Swede Larsen in the film. The musical score was composed by
      <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Riddle" title="Nelson Riddle">
       Nelson Riddle
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
       Paramount Pictures
      </a>
      delayed the release of the film in the United States to avoid competing against another Paramount film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Smith" title="Nevada Smith">
        Nevada Smith
       </a>
      </i>
      with Steve McQueen. The film was first released in Japan on December 17, 1966, and was finally released in the United States on June 7, 1967. The film received critical praise and was successful at the box office, generating North American rentals of $5,950,000 on box-office receipts of $12 million.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maccarthy-625_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-maccarthy-625-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      is the second of three films directed by Hawks about a sheriff defending his office against belligerent outlaw elements in the town, after
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Bravo_(movie)" title="Rio Bravo (movie)">
        Rio Bravo
       </a>
      </i>
      (1959) and before
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Lobo" title="Rio Lobo">
        Rio Lobo
       </a>
      </i>
      (1970), both also starring Wayne. The plotlines of all three films are almost similar enough to qualify
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       Rio Lobo
      </i>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Remake" title="Remake">
       remakes
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Members of the
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Writers_of_America" title="Western Writers of America">
       Western Writers of America
      </a>
      chose the film's theme as one of the Top 100 Western songs of all time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Top100_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Top100-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
       Plot
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Dorado_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cole Thornton (
      <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">
       John Wayne
      </a>
      ), a gunslinger-for-hire, is hired by wealthy rancher Bart Jason (
      <a href="/wiki/Ed_Asner" title="Ed Asner">
       Ed Asner
      </a>
      ) to help him in a range war with the McDonald family in the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Eldorado,_Texas" title="Eldorado, Texas">
       El Dorado
      </a>
      . The local sheriff, an old friend of Thornton, J.P. Harrah (
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mitchum" title="Robert Mitchum">
       Robert Mitchum
      </a>
      ) gives Cole more details that Jason had deliberately left out, including the possibility of having to side against Harrah. Unwilling to fight his friend, Thornton quits, to the relief of saloon owner Maudie (
      <a href="/wiki/Charlene_Holt" title="Charlene Holt">
       Charlene Holt
      </a>
      ), who is in love with Thornton (and was for a time a romantic interest of Harrah's).
     </p>
     <p>
      The McDonalds learn of Thornton's presence in town. Fearing that he might come for them, Kevin McDonald (
      <a href="/wiki/R._G._Armstrong" title="R. G. Armstrong">
       R.G. Armstrong
      </a>
      ) puts his youngest son, Luke, on guard. When Thornton passes by on his way back from rejecting Jason's offer, Luke (
      <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Crawford" title="Johnny Crawford">
       Johnny Crawford
      </a>
      ), who has fallen asleep, wakes and fires a wild warning shot whereupon Thornton reflexively shoots him. Luke is still alive when Thornton finds him, but he refuses treatment based upon the belief that a gut-shot man wouldn't have a chance anyway and commits suicide when Thornton turns his back on him.
     </p>
     <p>
      Thornton subsequently brings the boy's body to the McDonald ranch and offers an explanation. The only McDonald daughter, Joey (
      <a href="/wiki/Michele_Carey" title="Michele Carey">
       Michele Carey
      </a>
      ), impulsively rides off before Thornton can finish his story and ambushes him shortly thereafter. Her shot is not fatal, but the bullet lodges next to Thornton's spine and in time begins to trouble him by occasionally pressing against the
      <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">
       spinal cord
      </a>
      , causing temporary paralysis of his right side. The local doctor, Dr. Miller (
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Fix" title="Paul Fix">
       Paul Fix
      </a>
      ), does not have the skill to remove the bullet and Thornton soon departs El Dorado for a new job.
     </p>
     <p>
      Several months later, Thornton runs into another gunslinger-for-hire named Nelson McLeod (
      <a href="/wiki/Christopher_George" title="Christopher George">
       Christopher George
      </a>
      ) and a young greenhorn called Mississippi (
      <a href="/wiki/James_Caan" title="James Caan">
       James Caan
      </a>
      ), who has come for revenge against one of McLeod's men. McLeod has been hired by Jason for the same job Thornton turned down and Thornton hears from McLeod about how Harrah has turned into a drunk after an unhappy love affair. Thornton decides to return to El Dorado, hoping to save Harrah from being gunned down by McLeod and his men. He is followed by Mississippi who also wishes to help, despite his lack of experience and terrible aim with a gun.
     </p>
     <p>
      Once Thornton and Mississippi arrive in El Dorado, they hear more of the story behind Harrah's change. The two men then join with Deputy Sheriff Bull (
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hunnicutt" title="Arthur Hunnicutt">
       Arthur Hunnicutt
      </a>
      ) in order to get Harrah sober and cleaned up. Mississippi contributes an old folk recipe for a hangover that he learned from his old friend and mentor, Johnny Diamond. The recipe includes such ingredients as
      <a href="/wiki/Cayenne_pepper" title="Cayenne pepper">
       cayenne
      </a>
      , mustard,
      <a href="/wiki/Syrup_of_ipecac" title="Syrup of ipecac">
       ipecac
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Asafoetida" title="Asafoetida">
       asafoetida
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Croton_oil" title="Croton oil">
       croton oil
      </a>
      and gunpowder, and he promises it will make any man unable to drink liquor for a while. The concoction proves to be violently effective, and leaves Harrah sober and furious. Within a day of their arrival, McLeod and his men also come to El Dorado and are hired on by Bart Jason. When one of them shoots one of the McDonalds, Thornton, Harrah, Bull and Mississippi chase the shooter and his friends into an old church and then into Jason's saloon. Harrah arrests Jason and takes him to the jail for his part in the shooting of one of the McDonalds. Later that night, Thornton and Mississippi decide to patrol the town in the hope of keeping the peace and are deputized by Bull. There is another shootout with McLeod and his men, which results in a minor leg injury for Harrah.
     </p>
     <p>
      The next day, Maudie sends a message to Thornton and his friends, stating that McLeod's men are frightening her and her patrons. When Thornton and Mississippi go to help her, they are ambushed and Thornton has an attack that leaves him partially paralyzed and captured by McLeod. Subsequently, McLeod trades the injured Thornton for Bart Jason, a trade to which Harrah agrees, despite knowing that doing so will mean that nothing will stand in the way of McLeod going after the McDonalds.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sure enough, McLeod and his men shortly thereafter kidnap one of the McDonalds in order to force Kevin McDonald to sign over his water rights to Jason. Thornton and the others are forced to quickly come up with a plan to rescue Kevin McDonald's son and neutralize Jason and McLeod. Despite Thornton's paralysis and Harrah's leg injury, the two of them, along with Bull and Mississippi, return to town on wagons. While Thornton distracts Jason and McLeod outside of the front of the saloon, Mississippi, Harrah and Bull attack from the rear. The kidnapped McDonald is rescued, with a little help from Joey McDonald, Jason, McLeod and his men are killed, and order is restored to El Dorado. Thornton also begins to imply that he may discontinue his wandering ways in order to stay in the town with Maudie.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
       Production
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Dorado_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Brackett" title="Leigh Brackett">
       Leigh Brackett
      </a>
      wrote the original script which she described as "the best script I had ever done in my life. It wasn't tragic, but it was one of those things where Wayne died at the end." However she says the closer they got to production "the more we got into doing
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Bravo_(film)" title="Rio Bravo (film)">
        Rio Bravo
       </a>
      </i>
      over again the sicker I got, because I hate doing things over again. And I kept saying to Howard I did that, and he'd say it was okay, we could do it over again."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Shot at
      <a href="/wiki/Old_Tucson_Studios" title="Old Tucson Studios">
       Old Tucson Studios
      </a>
      just west of
      <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">
       Tucson, Arizona
      </a>
      , as well as in and around
      <a href="/wiki/Kanab,_Utah" title="Kanab, Utah">
       Kanab, Utah
      </a>
      , from October 1965 to January 1966, the film was Wayne's 138th picture and was actually filmed before
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_War_Wagon" title="The War Wagon">
        The War Wagon
       </a>
      </i>
      , but its release was delayed so that Paramount's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Smith" title="Nevada Smith">
        Nevada Smith
       </a>
      </i>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Steve_McQueen" title="Steve McQueen">
       Steve McQueen
      </a>
      would not have to compete with a John Wayne film at the box office.
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      finally reached the theatres in June 1967, a month after
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_War_Wagon" title="The War Wagon">
        The War Wagon
       </a>
      </i>
      had opened.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davis_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-davis-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Film footage from
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      was later incorporated into the opening montage of Wayne's final film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Shootist" title="The Shootist">
        The Shootist
       </a>
      </i>
      , to illustrate the backstory of Wayne's character. Director
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Siegel" title="Don Siegel">
       Don Siegel
      </a>
      used no scenes from any film shot before 1948 in
      <i>
       The Shootist
      </i>
      's frontispiece even though Wayne's career as a leading man began in 1930 with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Trail" title="The Big Trail">
        The Big Trail
       </a>
      </i>
      and had continued throughout the following two decades.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the film, Mississippi gives Sheriff Harrah a concoction meant to deter him from further drinking. The ingredients include (among others)
      <a href="/wiki/Cayenne_pepper" title="Cayenne pepper">
       Cayenne pepper
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ipecac" title="Ipecac">
       ipecac
      </a>
      (an emetic),
      <a href="/wiki/Mustard_(condiment)" title="Mustard (condiment)">
       mustard
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Croton_oil" title="Croton oil">
       croton oil
      </a>
      (which induces diarrhea),
      <a href="/wiki/Asafoetida" title="Asafoetida">
       asafoetida
      </a>
      (often misspelled
      <i>
       acifedida
      </i>
      ) and gunpowder.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Poem">
       Poem
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Dorado_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Poem">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The poem repeated in the film is "
      <a href="/wiki/Eldorado_(poem)" title="Eldorado (poem)">
       Eldorado
      </a>
      ", a
      <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">
       ballad
      </a>
      poem by
      <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">
       Edgar Allan Poe
      </a>
      . Caan continuously mispronounces the verse "Ride, boldly ride" as "Ride, Bodie, ride," confusing audience members unfamiliar with Poe's poem.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">
       In popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Dorado_(1966_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In popular culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The similarity between
      <i>
       Rio Bravo
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      gave rise to an amusing exchange in the 1995 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Get_Shorty" title="Get Shorty">
        Get Shorty
       </a>
      </i>
      . In this scene, Los Angeles drug dealer Bo Catlett (
      <a href="/wiki/Delroy_Lindo" title="Delroy Lindo">
       Delroy Lindo
      </a>
      ) breaks into the home of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_film" title="B film">
       B film
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Horror_film" title="Horror film">
       horror film
      </a>
      actress Karen Flores (
      <a href="/wiki/Rene_Russo" title="Rene Russo">
       Rene Russo
      </a>
      ) in order to steal a valuable film script. He accidentally touches the TV remote and switches on a cable channel, which is showing
      <i>
       Rio Bravo
      </i>
      . This awakens Flores and her boyfriend, mafia enforcer Chili Palmer (
      <a href="/wiki/John_Travolta" title="John Travolta">
       John Travolta
      </a>
      ). The pair confront Catlett and, in an attempt to talk his way out of the situation, Catlett confuses details about the respective casts of
      <i>
       Rio Bravo
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      , incorrectly stating that
      <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">
       Dean Martin
      </a>
      had acted in the latter. Palmer, a film buff and would-be film producer, is appalled at this lack of knowledge. He proceeds to give Catlett a tongue-in-cheek lecture setting the facts straight, pointing out that
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      was essentially a remake of
      <i>
       Rio Bravo
      </i>
      , with Robert Mitchum playing the role of Dean Martin's drunk, James Caan doing
      <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Nelson" title="Ricky Nelson">
       Ricky Nelson
      </a>
      's role, and that the only person playing the same role in both films was John Wayne: "he played John Wayne".
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 2007 film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Transformers_(film)" title="Transformers (film)">
        Transformers
       </a>
      </i>
      , the character "Bumblebee", when communicating with the film's main characters, pulls on radio/television airwaves to speak; one such set of dialogue being John Wayne's line: "Any more questions you wanna ask?", from
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       El Dorado
      </i>
      was also referenced in a later Transformers sequel,
      <a href="/wiki/Transformers:_Age_of_Extinction" title="Transformers: Age of Extinction">
       Age of Extinction
      </a>
      , when an elderly theater owner cited it as his favorite film.
