       <h1 class="entry-title">
        When Bad Things HappenWhat to Do if Youre Attacked.
       </h1>
       <div class="entry-meta">
        <span class="posted-on">
         <i class="fa fa-calendar">
         </i>
         <a href="http://nateandrachael.com/travel-safety-what-to-do-if-youre-attacked/" rel="bookmark">
          <time class="entry-date published" datetime="2014-02-13T16:30:03+00:00">
           February 13, 2014
          </time>
          <time class="updated" datetime="2014-02-13T16:55:28+00:00">
           February 13, 2014
          </time>
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="byline">
         <i class="fa fa-user">
         </i>
         <span class="author vcard">
          <a class="url fn n" href="http://nateandrachael.com/author/rachael/">
           Rachael
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
       </div>
       <!-- .entry-meta -->
      </header>
      <!-- .entry-header -->
      <div class="entry-content">
       <p>
        <em>
         On Friday, January 3, 2014, my family (my parents, husband, two young daughtersages 1 and 3) and I were playing on Isla Verde beach in Puerto Rico when a man came out of the shadows, held a knife to my moms throat, and fought to keep her restrained while his accomplice took our belongings. Ive written out my version of the attack, and
         <a href="http://nateandrachael.com/knife-attack/" target="_blank">
          you can read it here
         </a>
         .
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Its been five weeks since the attack in Puerto Rico, and were recovering well.
       </p>
       <p>
        But I still have one more
        <em>
         heavy
        </em>
        post to write. In a perfect world, no one would ever need the information Im about to share. (I wrote
        <a href="http://nateandrachael.com/travel-safety-precautions/" target="_blank">
         this travel safety precautions post
        </a>
        in an attempt to help others stay safe.)
       </p>
       <p>
        But this isnt a perfect world.
       </p>
       <p>
        If you are ever attacked, I hope this post can be of some help and comfort.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: center;">
        <a href="http://nateandrachael.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/Attacked1.jpg">
         <img alt="What to do if you're attacked. " class="aligncenter wp-image-6151" height="614" src="http://nateandrachael.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/Attacked1.jpg" width="358"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        What to do if youre attacked.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Give your attackers what they want.
        </strong>
        If youre being robbed, hand over your money. Its not worth getting hurt or killed to protect your wallet. Read
        <a href="http://nateandrachael.com/travel-safety-precautions/" target="_blank">
         this post
        </a>
        on travel safety precautions for info on how to potentially limit what you lose.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Expect to have a fight, flight, or freeze response.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        My friend
        <a href="http://www.firefliesandmudpies.com/" target="_blank">
         Melissa at Fireflies and Mudpies
        </a>
        is a school counselor, and she recently finished assault training. Here is what she wrote me after our attack:
       </p>
       <p>
        When you are attacked, the brains amygdala takes over and puts you into your natural fight, flight or freeze response. Most assault victims (e.g. the ones with the knives against their throats) freeze. They physically cant get away or move, so its pretty amazing that your Mom was such a fighter!
       </p>
       <p>
        When your amygdala takes over, its hard to use your brain (the prefrontal cortex part) to make decisions  and all you might remember are sensory detailssmells, sounds, temperature, etc. Sometimes those sensory experiences trigger anxiety or panic attacks after the assault  something is similar or makes the same noise. Sometimes those sensory experiences are what keeps a person in control.their brain cant even fathom what is happening to them, so they focus on the ticking of the clock, roll of the waves, etc.
       </p>
       <p>
        This was definitely the case in our situation. I have
        <em>
         no clue
        </em>
        how or when I decided to pick up my girls and run. I just did it. All I really remember is crying, holding my crying girls, and peeing my pants while the second attacker tried to pull me back.
       </p>
       <p>
        My mom clearly had the fight response. She didnt know the assailant had a knife to her neck; otherwise, she probably would have frozen instead.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Try to remember identifying details of your attackers.
        </strong>
        After the attack, the police officers asked us to describe the two men. We couldnt remember much. They were wearing hats. Or were they? They were smoking cigarettes. Or was only one of them smoking? They had beards. No, they were clean-shaven. Its all a blur. We couldnt find them in a lineup if we tried.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Get tested. And tested again.
        </strong>
        Although my mom insisted she was fine, the police officers in Puerto Rico called the paramedics. They wanted to be sure she was physically okay. Back home, she had to be tested for diseases that are transmitted through blood. My mom bit the attackers arm hard enough to draw blood. So she had to be tested twice (some diseases dont show up right away) to be sure she didnt catch some nasty transmittable disease. (Thankfully, shes fine!)
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Dont blame yourselfnot even a little bit
        </strong>
        . Even if you made mistakes, you are the
        <em>
         victim
        </em>
        . The attackers are the ones at fault. Wise people reminded me of this after our attack and encouraged me not to focus on what we could have done differently.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Model healthy healing for kids. Your reaction to the attack will become your childs reaction
        </strong>
        .
        <a href="http://www.psychowith6.com/" target="_blank">
         Melanie Wilson
        </a>
        , a psychologist and writer, said this: Your daughter will react to your reaction. You will teach her how to respond to situations like these. When you are feeling more confident, explain to her that its normal to be very afraid when something like this happens, but that God has given you peace.
       </p>
       <p>
        Were trying to find the right balance of talking about the attack in a healthy way and not blowing it out of proportion. This leads to my next point
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Help your children process.
        </strong>
        For us, this has meant listening and answering when our three-year-old has questions. A few things shes asked:
       </p>
       <p>
        Why did the bad guys try to hurt Mamaw?
       </p>
       <p>
        How many bad guys are in Puerto Rico?
       </p>
       <p>
        Did the police put the bad guys in their long time out?
       </p>
       <p>
        Why did they want her money?
       </p>
       <p>
        We try to never sound shocked or surprised by her stories, questions, or ideas. For instance, just this morning (5 weeks after the attack), she was hiding in her closet pretending like she was running away from the bad guys in Puerto Rico. I didnt have an exaggerated reaction or show disapproval. I acknowledged what she was doing, and a few minutes later she moved on to something else.
       </p>
       <p>
        A harder issue has been when she sees someone making a bad choice (e.g. stealing a toy from a friend, pushing) and she labels them bad guys like the ones in Puerto Rico. We tell her that people and friends make bad choices, but her friends arent bad guys.
       </p>
       <p>
        We also have been talking a lot about forgiveness. We pray for the bad guys, and we talk about how great it is that God forgives anyone who asks.We want her to know that God is a God who forgives and redeems. She keeps asking us if God will take the blade (how did she learn that word?) away from the bad guys.
       </p>
       <p>
        Here are a few books that were recommended to me:
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0439669413/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0439669413&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=thepipe-20&quot;&gt;Sometimes%20I'm%20Bombaloo%20(Scholastic%20Bookshelf)&lt;/a&gt;&lt;img%20src=&quot;http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=thepipe-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=0439669413&quot;%20width=&quot;1&quot;%20height=&quot;1&quot;%20border=&quot;0&quot;%20alt=&quot;&quot;%20style=&quot;border:none%20!important;%20margin:0px%20!important;&quot;%20/&gt;" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         Sometimes Im a Bombaloo
        </a>
        and
        <a href='http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0870293710/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0870293710&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=thepipe-20"&gt;When%20Bad%20Things%20Happen:%20A%20Guide%20to%20Help%20Kids%20Cope%20(Elf-Help%20Books%20for%20Kids)&lt;/a&gt;&lt;img%20src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=thepipe-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=0870293710"%20width="1"%20height="1"%20border="0"%20alt=""%20style="border:none%20!important;%20margin:0px%20!important;"%20/&gt;' rel="nofollow" target="_blank">
         When Bad Things Happen: A Guide to Help Kids Cope
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Embrace role playing and play therapy.
        </strong>
        As cheesy as it sounds, characters, skits, and mini plays can help kids to process. My mom (an elementary school counselor at the time) used to role play with me to help me to practice things like saying no to strangers and turning down drugs. This can also be used to help kids process their feelings after bad things happen. For example, you could have a puppet ask your child questions in silly voices to make the conversation less threatening and serious.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Seek out a professional.
        </strong>
        My mom is a counselor, our daughters are very young, and we have no physical scars or reminders of the attack. Therefore, we chose not to seek family counseling. However, if our girls were older, this would have been an option we pursued.
       </p>
       <p>
        Instead, after the attack, I processed by
        <a href="http://nateandrachael.com/knife-attack/" target="_blank">
         sharing our story
        </a>
        and asking a few self-defensive experts what we could have done differently. A police officer friend (thank you, David G.) wrote this:
       </p>
       <p>
        In a situation like this where you have not only you, but your family to worry about, do whatever it takes to make the criminals comfortable and give them what they want. Money and belongings are replaceable. Dont fight or scream if you can determine that all they want is what you have. Give it to them and let them leave. This is not the response to have if the attacker is trying to harm or kill you and/or your family. In a situation like that you do whatever you have to, to win.
       </p>
       <p>
        But in this situation, the biggest key is to try to remain calm and not give any reason for the attacker(s) to consider you a threat. The more comfortable and safe they feel, the less likely they are to hurt someone over money and valuables. Their mindset is not to hurt or kill, but to steal and run. Dont risk your safety to protect replaceable things.
       </p>
       <p>
        Your dad and Nate did exactly what they should have: nothing. Attempting to disarm or engage your assailants is an incredible risk that should only be taken as a last resort. While remaining calm and giving them what they want, try to observe everything you can about them so you can describe them later. Size, shape, hair, clothing, tattoos, scars, etc.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Dont expect to move on right away.
        </strong>
        When we were in Puerto Rico, I started sweating and breathing fast the first night we went out for dinner after the attack. We had to park in a parking garage and take the elevator with a group of guys.
       </p>
       <p>
        I now know I am vulnerable.
       </p>
       <p>
        Back home, Im no longer having daily flashbacks, but Im much more guarded. I was angry when carpet cleaning salesman made eye contact with me through my front door the other day, and I felt obligated to open it.
       </p>
       <p>
        I now suspect strangers, especially men.
       </p>
       <p>
        When Im loading and unloading the girls downtown by the museum, I rehearse what I would do if one of the people walking past us tried to grab one of the girls.
       </p>
       <p>
        I no longer assume everyone is benevolent and kind.
       </p>
       <p>
        On the other hand,
        <strong>
         dont be paralyzed by fear
        </strong>
        . After the attack, we didnt pack up and fly home right away. We stayed for three or four more days like we originally planned. Was it hard to play on the beach in the EXACT spot where we were attacked? Yes, of course.
       </p>
       <p>
        And a few people have asked us if this will change our travel plans. Yes. And no. Well travel smarter, but well keep traveling.
       </p>
       <p>
        We hope you will too.
       </p>

        <a href="https://www.facebook.com/NothingifNotIntentional" target="_blank">
         Nothing if Not Intentional facebook pag
        </a>
        e.
       </address>
