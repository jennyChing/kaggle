        <h2 class="subheading">
         #3. A Human-Catching Net-Launcher
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/6/2/45762.jpg?v=1" height="203" width="610"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         A while ago, the Air Force announced it was interested in
         <a href="http://www.wired.com/dangerroom/2009/01/air-force-seeks/" target="c">
          developing a "Spider-Man cannon" to trap would-be assailants
         </a>
         coming down on parachutes or hand gliders (because it finally realized something needs to be done about all those hand-gliding terrorists). Actually, that sort of gadget already exists: Police officers in China have been using compact web-shooters to catch subjects up to 49 feet away as part of what is obviously an organized global effort for law-enforcing agencies to "be more like Spider-Man."
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/3/45753.jpg?v=1" height="218" width="295"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          A goal we can get behind 100 percent.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         But why should such awesome, cartoonish tools remain in the hands of trained professionals?
         <a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/Build-A-Net-Gun/?ALLSTEPS" target="c">
          This tutorial
         </a>
         gives simple instructions on how to make one out of some PVC pipes and $75 worth of materials.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/2/45752.jpg?v=1" height="295" width="320"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         According to the creator, it can fire a 90-foot net 15 to 20 feet away using about 100 PSI of compressed air -- perfect for capturing Penelope Pitstop and dragging her to the nearest set of train tracks. Here's a demonstration of the homemade net launcher in action (from the point of view of the victim):
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <object height="390" width="480">
          <param name="movie" value="http://www.youtube.com/v/UrlSqDe-_-Q?fs=1&amp;hl=en_US">
          </param>
          <param name="allowFullScreen" value="true">
          </param>
          <param name="allowscriptaccess" value="always">
          </param>
          <embed allowfullscreen="true" allowscriptaccess="always" height="390" src="http://www.youtube.com/v/UrlSqDe-_-Q?fs=1&amp;hl=en_US" type="application/x-shockwave-flash" width="480">
          </embed>
         </object>
        </p>
        <p>
         Notice how time slows down as the net approaches you? Yeah, that's not the video, that's your brain telling you you're about to be captured and probably molested.
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #2. A Homemade EMP
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/6/1/45761.jpg?v=1" height="203" width="610"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         Remember the scene in
         <em>
          The Matrix
         </em>
         where they use an EMP (electro-magnetic pulse) to instantly disable those machines attacking the ship? Pretty awesome, right? But what if anyone could do that? What if that was your robot squid they shot down, causing you to be late for work? This scenario is closer to reality than you might think.
        </p>
        <p>
         Fun fact: EMPs aren't all big bombs that send out pulses in every direction, like you see in the movies. Apparently, anyone who knows a little about electronics can make his own EMP gun and fire cone-based waves of data-killing energy with frightening precision. Technically, when it's a weapon, it's called a HERF gun, or high-energy radio frequency gun, also known as a microwave gun.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/4/45754.jpg?v=1" height="320" width="320"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          Better than our microwave gun concept that fired convenience store burritos.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.savel.org/old/mad/microwavegun.html" target="c">
          This website
         </a>
         offers instructions on how to build a microwave gun out of an actual microwave oven. Basically, all you need to do is carefully take it apart and put it back together in the shape of a pistol. It works just like a regular EMP, only on a smaller scale. The writer says you can use it to "kill cockroaches and computers," which should come in pretty handy when you're in bed and can't be arsed to get up and turn off the PC. It's not portable, though ... unless you can find a power source capable of generating 1 kilowatt of electricity.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/5/45755.jpg?v=1" height="315" width="320"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          "GREAT SCOTT! That's -- actually, that's pretty doable."
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         So could someone, in theory, build a microwave gun big enough to, say, stop a car dead in its tracks? Yes, and they already have:
         <a href="http://gizmodo.com/#!5454295/this-emp-cannon-stops-cars-almost-instantly" target="c">
          These guys
         </a>
         built one capable of shutting down the electrical system of a car from 600 feet. away. That's right: Within a few seconds, this thing can disable
         <em>
          any type of vehicle that relies on electricity
         </em>
         -- that is, anything made within the last 35 or so years.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/6/45756.jpg?v=1" height="281" width="320"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          You know, it might be time to get the old Pontiac out of the garage.
         </font>
        </p>
        <h2 class="subheading">
         #1. A Giant Robotic Hand
        </h2>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/6/0/45760.jpg?v=1" height="203" width="610"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         You can tell why Christian Ristow, a former Hollywood animatronics expert, won
         <a href="http://www.popularmechanics.com/home/how-to-plans/woodworking/4329771" target="c">
          <em>
           Popular Mechanics'
          </em>
          2009 "Backyard Geniuses" contest for DiY projects
         </a>
         just by taking a quick look at his picture:
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/7/45757.jpg?v=1" height="220" width="320"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          It's because of his rugged good looks.
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Ristow, who used to build puppets for Jim Henson's company, moved on to bigger and better things, by which we mean sitting in his yard building
         <a href="http://blog.makezine.com/archive/2009/06/hand_of_man_maker_faire.html" target="c">
          a giant robot called "Hand of Man."
         </a>
         It's a 20-foot-tall gasoline-powered mechanical hand made primarily of steel girders and pneumatics. It's even controlled by a glove that mimics your real hand movements, like what kids in the 90s thought the
         <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_glove" target="c">
          Power Glove
         </a>
         would do.
        </p>
        <p align="center">
         <img data-img="http://i.crackedcdn.com/phpimages/article/7/5/8/45758.jpg?v=1" height="174" width="320"/>
         <br/>
         <font size="1">
          It can also crush your entire family in one movement!
         </font>
        </p>
        <p>
         Seriously, watch
