       <p>
        If youve been around my blog awhile, you know Im all for experimentation. But 
        <br/>
        Kevin is a meat and potatoes kind of guy. He has an immediate distrust of new foods. He fears bacteria (this is undoubtedly a reasonable fear). He fears
        <b>
         large clumpy cauliflower-esque SCOBYs
        </b>
        contained in what logic tells him should be soured milk. Now, if that sentence didnt scare you off, you may want to try this out!
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Milk Kefir
        </strong>
        is jam-packed with multitudes of
        <em>
         probiotic bacteria
        </em>
        . Its somewhat similar to yogurt; but the probiotic content is MUCH higher. Sounds like a great reason to try it! So when I first read about it a few years ago, that is exactly what I did.
       </p>
       <p>
        The only necessary ingredients are
        <a href="http://www.yemoosmarket.com/cultures/milk-kefir-grains/" target="_blank">
         milk kefir grains
        </a>
        , milk, and salt, preferably either
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B008EKF5M6/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B008EKF5M6&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=yoguhydr-20&amp;linkId=SDIDZJLXH4F2ZMKY" target="blank">
         cheese salt
        </a>
        <img alt="cheese salt" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=yoguhydr-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B008EKF5M6" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
        or
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000SWTKV0/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000SWTKV0&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=yoguhydr-20&amp;linkId=52PDK5TRSPEOCCUL" target="blank">
         sea salt
        </a>
        <img alt="sea salt" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=yoguhydr-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B000SWTKV0" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        I was immediately intent on experimenting with this new wonderful flavor! If you havent yet tried Milk Kefir, put it on your list!! The grains only take a few hours to convert regular milk into an intriguing milk product. The flavor and probiotic content develop over time,
        <em>
         ranging from a mildly tangy yogurt-y drink to a sour and thick milkshake-texture
        </em>
        . And your body will thank you! Gut health affects overall health, and extra probiotic intake
        <em>
         helps balance gut flora
        </em>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Straining milk kefir through a few layers of
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001B14ODG/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B001B14ODG&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=yoguhydr-20&amp;linkId=LIMLAH7MTSDQQ643" target="blank">
         cheesecloth
        </a>
        <img alt="cheesecloth" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=yoguhydr-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B001B14ODG" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
        separates the curd from the whey, leaving you with an
        <strong>
         amazing impersonation of cream cheese.
        </strong>
        <br/>
        <small>
         I did this in several batches, freezing each batch of curds until I had enough for a pressed cheese. You could also freeze the kefir and strain after thawing.
        </small>
       </p>

       <p>
        Once the Milk Kefir has been strained, it can be made into a pressed cheese; without using heat or rennet. They whey can be drank as is, or added to breads and soups.
       </p>
       <p>
        I added cheese salt, black pepper and red chili flakes to my kefir cream cheese, but there is tons of space for experimenting here!!
       </p>
       <p>
        In an ideal world Id use a proper
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0064OM1OE/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B0064OM1OE&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=yoguhydr-20&amp;linkId=XPBUN3TQRUJYWELE" target="blank">
         cheese mold, follower
        </a>
        <img alt="cheese mold" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=yoguhydr-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B0064OM1OE" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
        , and
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0081N5LT8/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B0081N5LT8&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=yoguhydr-20&amp;linkId=3WJKU7RXPD33FT7G" target="blank">
         cheese press
        </a>
        <img alt="cheese press" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=yoguhydr-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B0081N5LT8" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
        . But this is not an ideal world  I was forced to improvise!
        <br/>
        After a little trial and error, I used 2 small margarine containers to create a mold. In one container I cut slits and drilled holes on the sides and bottom respectively. With the second container, I removed the bottom  to be used as a sleeve to support the first container (I discovered the need for this
        <em>
         after
        </em>
        the picture was taken:)). The bottom of that container acted as a follower.
        <br/>
        For weights I used some big heavy books (approx. 15 pds.) and a 20 pd. bag of flour (approx. total 35 lbs.).
        <br/>
        Everything was placed on a wire rack over a baking pan to let the whey drain. I also used 2 lids along the sides to prevent anything from slipping under the weight.
        <br/>
        It worked great.:)
       </p>

       <p>
        The cream cheese was scooped into the cheese mold (lined with dampened and well-wrung cheesecloth). Then the cloth was folded to cover the top and a follower topped it off. The cheese was gently pressed using 15 pds. weight for 30 minutes. Then the curd was flipped and pressed another 30 minutes.
       </p>
       <p>
        After the initial hour, the cheesecloth was rinsed and wrung out before I placed it back in the mold.
        <br/>
        The cheese was flipped and pressed using 35 pds weight for 24 hours.
       </p>
       <p>
        (So hard to wait!!)
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         After 24 hours, I deconstructed the leaning tower of cheese squashery.
        </strong>
       </p>

       <p>
        I combined a small amount of
        <em>
         vinegar, water, and cheese salt
        </em>
        , and using a clean cheesecloth I rubbed the mixture over the entire surface of the cheese making sure to get any little divots. The cheese stayed in a cool room another 24 hours on parchment paper on top of a wire rack.
       </p>
       <p>
        I then buffed the entire surface with
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B000U0MTMW/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B000U0MTMW&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=yoguhydr-20&amp;linkId=XY5OHOGM52U6HA46" target="blank">
         clover honey
        </a>
        <img alt="clover honey" border="0" height="1" src="http://ir-na.amazon-adsystem.com/e/ir?t=yoguhydr-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=B000U0MTMW" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
        and let it sit 2  3 hours longer to absorb any stickiness from the honey.
       </p>
       <p>
        I debated vacuum sealing and aging it, but I dont think I could stand the waiting and I decided Id rather eat it!
        <em>
         As it turned out, Kevin was impressed enough to eat some too! I finally found a way to get him to try milk kefirand he likes it!
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Because it was never heated, it retains all those friendly bacteria (probiotics).
        </strong>
       </p>

         The pressed kefir cheese tastes great.
        </strong>

        </strong>

             posted by
             <a class="url" href="http://yogurthydro.com" rel="external nofollow">
              YogurtHydro
             </a>

             </span>
            </p>
            <div class="comment">
             <p>
              Whoa. One at a time LOL. The pressed cheese will be mild, tangy, or sour depending how long you do your second ferment before freezing it, just like when making the kefir. I love that idea because you can drastically change the finished cheese so easily. I did short second ferments, so mine was mildvery much like the cream cheese flavor.:) The chili flakes really gave it a kick though!
             </p>
             <p>
              Again, with the texture it was actually pretty smooth with this method, but could be altered to suit. When Ive made other cheeses, the general idea is the more you stir while the curds are separating, the more whey will be released. The result from more stirring is a drier, more crumbly cheese. Ill have to experiment with that to tell you an exact method, but you may get a crumbly cheese by a straining the milk kefir for a longer period and agitating it occasionally.
             </p>
             <p>
              I left it at room temperature throughout the process, but my kitchen is a bit on the cool end of room temp. Probably 68  70 degrees. Depending on the size of your batches of milk kefir, you could strain it anywhere from 12  24 hours.
             </p>
             <p>
              It is a very nice cheese, youll love it, its definitely worth doing! I think I got all your questions, lol. Let me know how it goes!:)
             </p>
