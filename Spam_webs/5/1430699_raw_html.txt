        <div class="heading-text">
         <h1>
          A designers guide to creating a logo design brief
         </h1>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="inner-page-wrap has-right-sidebar has-one-sidebar row clearfix">
       <!-- OPEN article -->
       <article class="clearfix span8 post-1664 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-logo-design" id="1664" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
        <div class="page-content clearfix">
         <figure class="media-wrap" itemscope="">
          <img alt="Logo-Design-Brief" height="500" itemprop="image" src="http://logogeek.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Logo-Design-Brief1-770x500.jpg" width="770"/>
         </figure>
         <section class="article-body-wrap">
          <div class="body-text clearfix" itemprop="articleBody">
           <p>
            Unless you plan to create your own projects and work for yourself, in almost all cases when designing a logo you will need to work with other people. This includes people who are new to business that are just starting out, to those who have run businesses for years.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some of these people may have worked with designers before and have a prepared design brief, however in most cases they will be unfamiliar with the
            <a href="http://logogeek.co.uk/logo-design/logo-design-process/" title="Logo Design Process">
             logo design process
            </a>
            , so your job will be to guide and educate along the way, which also includes assisting with, and
            <strong>
             preparing a logo design brief
            </strong>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            What is a design brief?
           </h2>
           <p>
            <span class="_Tgc">
             A design brief is a document containing a set of instructions which outline the desired results of a project.
            </span>
           </p>
           <p>
            Theres no set way of writing a design brief, but they will always share the same purpose to set out a list of aims and objectives.
            <span class="_Tgc">
             This is s
            </span>
            o that you
            <strong>
             understand what youre designing and why
            </strong>
            , and more importantly, so both you the designer and the client understand what the final outcome is intended to achieve.
           </p>
           <p>
            Before you can create a design brief, you need to gain a good understanding of the business, its competition and its audience. You do this by asking questions.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            Understanding the business
           </h2>
           <p>
            Personally I like to send a questionnaire to the client. Then if required, I will follow this up with a discussion to fill in any blanks.
           </p>
           <p>
            The reason I choose to send a questionnaire instead of conducting an interview is because sometimes questions are difficult to answer on the spot. A questionnaire however gives the client time to think, research and discuss topics with others if needed, to provide you with the information needed.
           </p>
           <p>
            When I first started out as a designer I expected company owners to know everything about their business vision, mission and brand. In reality however this is not always true. This is because a lot of business owners are focused on making products, making money and managing people, so the questions you ask will help them to focus on specific topics which may have never been considered or fully refined.
           </p>
           <p>
            The questionnaire I use has been designed in InDesign, exported as a PDF and then made interactive using Acrobat Professional. I prefer this over using Microsoft Word as it gives me control over the design, which looks much more professional. On occasion however PDFs can cause issues, so I do have a back-up version in word for these instances.
           </p>
           <p>
            You can see a sample page from my logo design questionnaire below:
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://logogeek.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Logo_Design_Questionnaire.jpg">
             <img alt="Logo Design Questionnaire" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1668" height="1655" src="http://logogeek.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Logo_Design_Questionnaire.jpg" width="1170"/>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             (Note: If youre keen to see the full document, just send me an email and I will send you a copy. If you find it useful, write a blog to return the favor.)
            </em>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Heres a few examples of the questions I ask in this document:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What is the name of your company?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Do you have a company tagline or slogan that could be used as part of the logo?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What product(s) or service(s) do your business provide?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             How many years has your company been in business?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             How many employees work for your company?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Who are your competitors?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What differentiates you from your competitors?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Is their a unique story behind your business?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Are their any inspiring visuals associated with your business?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Where do you see your business/service in 5 years time?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What does your audience care about?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What does your audience want?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             How does your audience learn about your product, organisation or service?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Why should your audience choose you over the competition?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             What words do you want your audience to associate with your company?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Generally, what logos or brands do you think will appeal to your audience and why?
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Your design preference likes/dislikes or expectations
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            Your ultimate aim is to
            <strong>
             fully understand the business or product, and its target audience
            </strong>
            , so you understand in detail what youre designing the logo for and why.
           </p>
           <p>
            On occasion, the answers provided may mean other questions need to be asked. A follow up discussion should resolve this.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some business owners may give very little information at this stage, due to time limitations or general uncertainty. In these cases a follow-up discussion
            <strong>
             is essential
            </strong>
            so you gain all the information you need. I will explain in more detail later in this article why this is so important.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            Creating logo design objectives
           </h2>
           <p>
            Based on the collated information, pull out the essential information to create a list of objectives which the logo design is to fulfill.
           </p>
           <p>
            A bullet point list makes it easy for your client to skim through, which also allows them to assess the project and make any changes needed before you begin. This also acts as a tick-list you can follow whilst designing, and a reference point to refer back to when presenting designs.
           </p>
           <p>
            Once you have created a project brief its (really) important that your client, and the decision maker if its a different person, checks and approves the brief before you proceed with any designs. Theirs nothing worse than working on designs and getting to a presentation where you and your client have different expectations.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            The importance of a design brief: Success or Failure
           </h2>
           <p>
            I cant stress the importance of a solid design brief. You might get lucky, but from experience if your design brief is poor, even if you think the designs are good, they are most likely to be rejected by the client, simply because
            <strong>
             you failed to understand what you were trying to achieve
            </strong>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            This is why its important to get the required information from your client otherwise youll have an angry customer on your case, and only yourself to blame.
           </p>
           <p>
            On the other hand, a good design brief means you understand what the client is trying to achieve, meaning you can thoroughly research the company and target audience, and then back up your design decisions when presenting. Your designs are much more likely to be approved, and more importantly, are more likely to succeed in the real world.
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            <img alt="Good Design Brief - Poor Design Brief" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-1676" height="498" src="http://logogeek.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/Good-Poor-Design-Brief.jpg" width="1000"/>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            An example logo design project brief
           </h2>
           <p>
            Below is an example project brief created for Minternet, a web design development service from freelance web designer Mike Munro. You will notice that this list details specific information about the business, its target audience as well as personal preferences.
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             To design a logo for Minternet Web Design
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Where required to include the tagline Fresh, Clean, Crisp Web Design
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             To represent a sole trader, who works with, and has a passion for web design and development.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             To represent a social individual who supports the design and development community.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Where possible promote a professional individual who is happy to discuss requirements face to face, and to listen to and respect clients input and ideas as part of a collaborative service.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             To promote a business with a transparent and honest service.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Where possible promote the unique personal history of being a part-qualified architect.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             To target individuals who are looking for a cost effective, comprehensive web design service.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             The design must work well both online, through social media and website, as well as offline through promotional materials.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             The design must be simple, elegant, clean and minimal to fit Minternet design ethos: Fresh, Clean, Crisp Web Design
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             The design must not be loud, bright, busy or cluttered.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Where possible the design should promote the following words: Professional, friendly, timely, competitive, ambitious, relevant, enthusiastic, accessible, creative, knowledgeable, informed and skilled/talented.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Focus on Attention to detail: A core focus of the business ongoing.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
           </p>
           <p>
            If interested, for the above I have detailed the
            <a href="http://learnlogodesign.com/logo-design-process/logo-design-process-for-minternet" target="_blank" title="Logo design process for Minternet">
             logo design process for this project here
            </a>
            , and you can see the
            <a href="http://logogeek.co.uk/portfolio/minternet-web-design/" title="Minternet Web Design Logo Design">
             final logo design here
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
           </p>
           <h2>
            Whats your process for creating a project brief?
           </h2>
           <p>
            This blog covers my personal process for creating a
            <strong>
             logo design project brief
            </strong>
            , however Im also keen to hear how others do this for a book I am working on. Share your ideas and suggestions in the comments box below, send me an email, or get in touch with me through twitter
            <a href="https://twitter.com/Logo_Geek" target="_blank" title="Logo Geek on Twitter">
             @Logo_Geek
            </a>
            . Id love to hear from you.
           </p>
