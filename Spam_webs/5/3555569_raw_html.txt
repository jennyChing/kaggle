       <h1 class="entry-title">
        Why I Feel Lucky With My Sons Hemophilia Condition
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        By
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/author/judy/" rel="author">
           judy
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
        
        <span class="post-comments">
         <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/feel-lucky-sons-hemophilia-condition-3/#respond">
          Leave a Comment
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <input class="forceUpdateSocialLinkz_id" type="hidden" value="1265"/>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_1257" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-1.jpg">
          <img alt="A beautiful day for a Walk!" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-1257" height="150" src="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-1-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          A beautiful day for a Walk!
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         Today I feel lucky. No, I didnt win the lottery and I didnt find out a long-lost relative left me a pile of money. Today, the family, including Sarah, the most awesome babysitter on the planet, participated in the
         <a href="http://www.hemophilia.org/walk/">
          New York Chapter of the Hemophilia Foundations annual walk
         </a>
         . So why would this make me feel lucky? Because although my son, Jack, is a hemophiliac, his condition is very mild in comparison to so many of the other children we saw at todays walk. Yes, he still has to be careful (as careful as an active, rambunctious 6-year old can be), but if he had to be born with this condition, he (and we) definitely won the lottery.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_1253" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo.jpg">
          <img alt="Sarah - the most awesome babysitter on the planet!" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-1253" height="150" src="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Sarah  the most awesome babysitter on the planet!
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         According to the CDC, its estimated that about 20,000 individuals in the U.S. have hemophilia. Could that number be any smaller? And how in the world did Jack get this? For a little history: because my father was a hemophiliac, as a female, I automatically carried the gene, but that didnt mean if I had a boy I would pass it on to him. Guess what? He got it. But like my father, his case is very mild. Now if he could only get his 64 height.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_1254" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-4.jpg">
          <img alt="Feeling lucky &amp; blessed!" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-1254" height="150" src="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-4-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Feeling lucky &amp; blessed!
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         So what does this even mean, and what is hemophilia? Its a bleeding disorder typically seen in males, whereby if there is an incident as minor as a dental procedure or nosebleed to a more major trauma like a bone break, head bonk or fingers caught in the door, for a hemophiliac it will take longer for their blood to clot. If not treated with special medication called Factor, there could be long-lasting, severe complications. And even though Jack is what is considered a mild case, there is still concern for him, especially in cases where you cant see his injury. Its not lost on me that I will be able to use this condition when explaining why he will never play football or any other major contact sport  again, I feel lucky!
        </p>
        <p>
         Once considered a royal disease, hemophilia can be traced as far back as
         <a href="http://www.hemophilia.org/NHFWeb/MainPgs/MainNHF.aspx?menuid=178&amp;contentid=6&amp;rptname=bleeding">
          Queen Victoria
         </a>
         . She apparently passed it on to several of her daughters and by way of one of her granddaughters, Alexandra, who married Nicholas, the Tsar of Russia; their son Alexi had a very severe case. It is thought that they were paying so much attention to his condition that they werent paying enough attention to what was happening in Russia at the time and this (among other reasons) is what led to the Russian Revolution in 1917. Talk about passing the blame!
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_1255" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-3.jpg">
          <img alt="Jack getting ready to walk with his pedometer!" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-1255" height="150" src="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-3-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Jack getting ready to walk with his pedometer!
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_1256" style="width: 160px">
         <a href="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-2.jpg">
          <img alt="Happy Family!" class="size-thumbnail wp-image-1256" height="150" src="http://judy-ismsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/photo-2-150x150.jpg" width="150"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          Happy Family!
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         As we were walking around the grounds this morning talking to the different pharmaceutical reps and nurses who were there peddling their meds and treatment options, a total calm came over me. While Im not happy and feel enormous guilt that Jack was born with this condition I essentially passed on to him, and I hate seeing him in pain each year as they test his blood, and during the times he has to have injections because of an accident (only 2 times so far; Im sure there is more to come since hes all boy!), its all okay. Ive fast come to learn that in life, if its not one thing  its another, so be grateful for the little things (like mild conditions of a disease) and always count your blessings, especially if one of your biggest blessings is a little boy named Jack.
        </p>
        <p>
         P.S.  Next year well be forming Team Jack for the walk so if you want to learn how you can help please let me know or visit the National Hemophilia Foundations
         <a href="http://www.hemophilia.org/NHFWeb/MainPgs/MainNHF.aspx?menuid=0&amp;contentid=1">
          website
         </a>
         for more information.
        </p>
