     <p>
      <b>
       Death
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional_character" title="Fictional character">
       fictional character
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">
       DC
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">
       comic book
      </a>
      series,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sandman_(Vertigo)" title="The Sandman (Vertigo)">
        The Sandman
       </a>
      </i>
      (19891996). The character first appeared in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sandman_(Vertigo)" title="The Sandman (Vertigo)">
        The Sandman
       </a>
      </i>
      vol. 2, #8 (August 1989), and was created by
      <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gaiman" title="Neil Gaiman">
       Neil Gaiman
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mike_Dringenberg" title="Mike Dringenberg">
       Mike Dringenberg
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vert-deat_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vert-deat-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the stories, Death is both the end of life and a
      <a href="/wiki/Psychopomp" title="Psychopomp">
       psychopomp
      </a>
      . Like most
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">
       anthropomorphic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)" title="Death (personification)">
       personifications of death
      </a>
      , Death meets with the recently deceased and guides them into their new existence. However, unlike most personifications of
      <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">
       death
      </a>
      , she also visits people as they are born, according to
      <a href="/wiki/Destruction_(DC_Comics)" title="Destruction (DC Comics)">
       Destruction
      </a>
      in the
      <i>
       Sandman Special: The Song of Orpheus
      </i>
      . Evidently, only she seems to remember these encounters. In the special issue, it is also revealed that Death was known in Ancient Greece as Teleute.
     </p>
     <p>
      Physically, Death is also opposite to the traditional western culture personification of death (see
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grim_Reaper" title="Grim Reaper">
       Grim Reaper
      </a>
      ). In
      <i>
       The Sandman
      </i>
      , Death instead appears as an attractive, pale young goth woman dressed in casual clothes - often a black top and jeans. She also wears a silver
      <a href="/wiki/Ankh" title="Ankh">
       ankh
      </a>
      on a chain around her neck, and has a marking similar to the
      <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" title="Eye of Horus">
       eye of Horus
      </a>
      around her right eye.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc-ency_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dc-ency-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She is pleasant, down-to-earth, perky, and has been a nurturing figure for both incarnations of
      <a href="/wiki/Dream_(comics)" title="Dream (comics)">
       Dream
      </a>
      . This irony has helped make Death one of the most popular characters from
      <i>
       Sandman
      </i>
      . Death was named the fifteenth greatest comic book character by
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empire_Magazine" title="Empire Magazine">
        Empire Magazine
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deathpcsmall.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="319" height="345" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/23/Deathpcsmall.jpg/220px-Deathpcsmall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/23/Deathpcsmall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/23/Deathpcsmall.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">

         </a>
        </div>

        </i>

       </div>

     <p>
      Other personifications of Death have appeared in the DC Universe. These altering physical appearances may be rationalized as being completely different characters with an unknown relation (or lack thereof) to the overall Death shown in the
      <i>
       Sandman
      </i>
      series, or may be explained by a sequence at the beginning of the
      <i>
       Sandman
      </i>
      series. In the sequence where Morpheus is searching for his objects of power, he appears at
      <a href="/wiki/Martian_Manhunter" title="Martian Manhunter">
       Martian Manhunter
      </a>
      's door, and for a frame we see him as Martian Manhunter sees him: it's nothing like humans see him. This could indicate that the physical appearance of the Endless (the appearance of Dream, at the very least) is subject to the mind of the person doing the observing. Many references are made throughout Death and the Sandman series which indicate that perception of the form of the Endless is subjective, not objective.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Captain_Atom" title="Captain Atom">
        Captain Atom
       </a>
      </i>
      #42 Death appears alongside
      <a href="/wiki/Black_Racer_(DC_Comics)" title="Black Racer (DC Comics)">
       Black Racer
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/New_Gods" title="New Gods">
       New Gods
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Nekron" title="Nekron">
       Nekron
      </a>
      (a being embodying the will of "The Black", the solitude and peace death represents from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Green_Lantern" title="Green Lantern">
        Green Lantern
       </a>
      </i>
      ). The story stated that all three were equal, representing different aspects of death. Gaiman has denied this, however, and his stories make it clear that Death of the Endless is the ultimate personification of Death. It might be assumed, however, Nekron, the Racer, and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Flash" title="Black Flash">
       Black Flash
      </a>
      are connected to her in some way. Alternatively, there may be multiple avatars or gods of Death in the DC universe, besides those claiming to be Death.
      <a href="/wiki/Blackest_Night" title="Blackest Night">
       Blackest Night
      </a>
      has resolved this apparent contradiction or ambiguity, with Nekron no longer being referred to as an aspect of death but instead as a construct formed of darkness in response to the emerging light of the emotional spectrum.
     </p>
     <p>
      A more traditional version of Death, a skeleton in a bluish or purplish cloak, appeared as host in such DC titles such as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Weird_Mystery_Tales" title="Weird Mystery Tales">
        Weird Mystery Tales
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Secrets_(DC_Comics)" title="House of Secrets (DC Comics)">
        House of Secrets
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ghosts_(comics)" title="Ghosts (comics)">
        Ghosts
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Weird_War_Tales" title="Weird War Tales">
        Weird War Tales
       </a>
      </i>
      (including being in the story in issue #94),
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics_Presents" title="DC Comics Presents">
        DC Comics Presents
       </a>
      </i>
      #29, etc.
      <i>
       Weird War Tales
      </i>
      typically featured Death as a skeleton in some sort of military uniform relevant to the era and locality of the war depicted. This character appeared as recently as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cassandra_Peterson" title="Cassandra Peterson">
        Elvira
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Mystery" title="House of Mystery">
        House of Mystery
       </a>
      </i>
      #2 (February 1986). How this
      <a href="/wiki/Crisis_on_Infinite_Earths" title="Crisis on Infinite Earths">
       pre-Crisis
      </a>
      Death relates to Gaiman's Death, if at all, is unclear, although her older brother
      <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(DC_Comics)" title="Destiny (DC Comics)">
       Destiny
      </a>
      appeared with the character,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and both Deaths have appeared with incarnations of
      <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">
       Superman
      </a>
      . Superman referred to the earlier Death with the term "Grim Reaper", so perhaps that could be regarded as a distinct character, however, in the pages of
      <i>
       Weird War Tales
      </i>
      , he called himself "Death."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He also appeared when
      <i>
       Weird War Tales
      </i>
      was published under the Vertigo imprint and in the 2010
      <i>
       Weird War Tales
      </i>
      <a href="/wiki/One-shot_(comics)" title="One-shot (comics)">
       one-shot
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Death first appeared as a woman in

     <p>
      The current incarnation of Death first appeared in the final chapter of
      <i>
       Sandman
      </i>
      s first story arc
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sandman:_Preludes_and_Nocturnes" title="The Sandman: Preludes and Nocturnes">
        Preludes and Nocturnes
       </a>
      </i>
      , "The Sound of Her Wings", (issue #8) where she gave Dream direction and a degree of understanding. Death instantly became very popular to readers, and she appears at least briefly in each of the nine subsequent story arcs. However, Gaiman attempted to entice and tease readers by rationing out the number of appearances from Dreams family, so Death did not appear as frequently as one might expect for such a popular character. At the end of the ninth Sandman story arc
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sandman:_The_Kindly_Ones" title="The Sandman: The Kindly Ones">
        The Kindly Ones
       </a>
      </i>
      , there is a lengthy and noteworthy appearance from Death, in which she finally brings her brother peace.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_character_biography">
       Fictional character biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_(DC_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fictional character biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Death is the second eldest of
      <a href="/wiki/Endless_(comics)" title="Endless (comics)">
       the Endless
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc-endl_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-dc-endl-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a family of anthropomorphic beings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc-ency_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-dc-ency-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Death is possibly the most powerful of the Endless (and may be the most powerful being in the universe) having been shown (in a flashback in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sandman:_Brief_Lives" title="The Sandman: Brief Lives">
        Brief Lives
       </a>
      </i>
      ) to be virtually omniscient and being able to intimidate the Furies, who show no fear of the other Endless, simply by raising her voice in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sandman:_The_Kindly_Ones" title="The Sandman: The Kindly Ones">
        The Kindly Ones
       </a>
      </i>
      . The witch Thessaly mentions that Death is the only one of the Endless who is bound by no rules, supported in Dream's portion of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Sandman:_Endless_Nights" title="The Sandman: Endless Nights">
        Endless Nights
       </a>
      </i>
      in which she briefly makes an appearance at a conference designed to set functions for entities and leaves before it begins. In addition, it is mentioned in
      <i>
       Brief Lives
      </i>
      that she is the only one of the Endless who may survive the end of this incarnation of the universe. Death's realm is not portrayed in detail in the series, except for a brief scene in her 'house' in the
      <i>
       Sandman Special
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Song of
       <a href="/wiki/Orpheus" title="Orpheus">
        Orpheus
       </a>
      </i>
      , and later in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Books_of_Magic" title="The Books of Magic">
        The Books of Magic
       </a>
      </i>
      series. This is where she keeps her floppy hat collection, her
      <a href="/wiki/Goldfish" title="Goldfish">
       goldfish
      </a>
      Slim and Wandsworth and possibly her gallery. A brief glimpse of her realm can also be seen in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Endless_Storybook" title="The Little Endless Storybook">
        The Little Endless Storybook
       </a>
      </i>
      , when Barnabas visits her, although this time in her 'apartment suite.'
     </p>
     <p>
      One day every century, Death lives (and dies) as a mortal, in order to understand the value of the life she takes. She does this by becoming a mortal fated to die that day. At the end of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Death:_The_High_Cost_of_Living" title="Death: The High Cost of Living">
        Death: The High Cost of Living
       </a>
      </i>
      her Endless self briefly converses with her mortal self.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quasi-canonical_appearances">
       Quasi-canonical appearances
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_(DC_Comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Quasi-canonical appearances">
        edit
       </a>

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">
       AIDS
      </a>
      -awareness eight-page comic
      <i>
       Death Talks About Life
      </i>
      by Gaiman and McKean (which was first included in various Vertigo titles, and later released as a stand-alone giveaway pamphlet), Death demonstrates safe sex by placing a
      <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">
       condom
      </a>
      on a
      <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">
       banana
      </a>
      held by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Constantine" title="John Constantine">
       John Constantine
      </a>
      . Lightening the impact of the underlying message, she informs the reader that when one is through with the demonstration, "you can eat the banana."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This was used in high school health classes and is also reprinted as an addendum to the
      <i>
       Death: The High Cost of Living
      </i>
      trade paperback.
     </p>
     <p>
      This version of Death also made a cameo appearance in the crossover special
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avengers/JLA" title="Avengers/JLA">
        Avengers/JLA
       </a>
      </i>
      #2. She is represented in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandmaster_(DC_Comics)" title="Grandmaster (DC Comics)">
       Grandmaster
      </a>
      's home base, alongside
      <a href="/wiki/Deadman" title="Deadman">
       Deadman
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hela_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Hela (Marvel Comics)">
       Hela
      </a>
      and the purple-robed version of
      <a href="/wiki/Death_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Death (Marvel Comics)">
       Death
      </a>
      native to the
      <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">
       Marvel Universe
      </a>
      , which, as the plots of other crossover comics have hinged upon, exists in the same continuum of
      <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">
       fictional universes
      </a>
      as DC's. (Marvel's version of Death appears alternatively as a coldly beautiful woman in a purple robe or a walking skeleton (sometimes male and sometimes female in form, depending upon the context).
     </p>
     <p>
      She made an appearance in the Marvel Universe, at the wedding of
      <a href="/wiki/Rick_Jones_(comics)" title="Rick Jones (comics)">
       Rick Jones
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Marlo_Chandler" title="Marlo Chandler">

      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Hulk_(comics)" title="Hulk (comics)">
        The Incredible Hulk
       </a>
      </i>
      #418 (handing Marlo a hair brush, a visual pun referring to Marlo's recent 'brush with death').

Yes, Her. Death. Of the Endless. The cute chick in black dress from Neil Gaiman's comics. She was there, standing still, looking right into my eyes. I moved forward, I touched Her face. Dust. I folded Her and took Her with me (along with those Rockets and some Rick Veitch's books."
