               <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMGXl9j2AI/AAAAAAAABy0/zaMMGJyCaOg/s1600/z.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">

              <div style="text-align: center;">
               <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                 Rafal Malczewski, Auto na tle pejzau zimowego
                </span>
               </span>
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                  <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                   , 1930
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </span>
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="text-align: center;">
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                  <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                  </span>
                 </span>
                 <br/>
                </span>
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <span class="" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 Rafal Malczewski (1892-1965) was born in
                </span>
               </span>
               <span class="tagLead">
                Krakw, Poland, as
               </span>
               <span class="" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 son of the famous painter
                 <a href="http://www.all-art.org/symbolism/malczeewski1.html">
                  Jacek Malczewski
                 </a>
                 and his wife Mary Gralewski. After
                </span>
               </span>
               <span class="" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 graduating from high school
                </span>
                <span title="">
                 in Krakw,
                </span>
               </span>
               Malczewski studied philosophy, architecture, and agronomy in Vienna between 1910 and 1915. His father,
               <span class="recordTag recordTag_os">
                then
               </span>
               a professor at Krakw's Academy of Fine Arts, introduced him to painting. Between 1917 and 1939 Malczewski lived in  Zakopane, where he was a member of the artistic and intellectual  elite concentrated around the painter
               <a href="http://weimarart.blogspot.com/2010/07/stanislaw-ignacy-witkiewicz.html">
                Stanislaw Ignacy Witkiewicz
               </a>
               and composer
               <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karol_Szymanowski">
                <span class="recordTag recordTag_os">
                 Karol Szymanowski
                </span>
               </a>
               .
               <span class="" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 He was a keen mountaineer and a great skier.
                </span>
                <span title="">
                 In   1917, while climbing in the Tatra mountains, his companion was killed, and Malczewski spent all   night tied to the hook waiting for rescue assistance.
                </span>
               </span>
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMFStupREI/AAAAAAAABys/z5efunYyv8I/s1600/z.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="400" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMFStupREI/AAAAAAAABys/z5efunYyv8I/s400/z.jpg" width="280"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                 Rafal Malczewski, Jesin, 1926
                </span>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <br/>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <span class="" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 Malczewski
                </span>
               </span>
               joined the
               <i>
                Podhale Arts Society
               </i>
               ,  which promoted the idea of seeking out foundations for Polish art in the  local folk culture. He designed productions for the Formist  Theatre which began operating in Zakopane in 1925, creating scenery for  the plays of Witkiewicz and Strindberg. Malczewski first exhibited his works in public in 1924 in Warsaw. Other exhibitions followed in many Polish cities.
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 During the interwar period Malczewski gained considerable fame as a painter, p
                </span>
                <span title="">
                 resenting   his work at the Biennale in Venice in 1932, in Berlin, Helsinki, Los   Angeles, Moscow, and New York.
                </span>
                <span title="">
                 He won a   gold medal for his painting
                 <i>
                  Spring in the Mountains
                 </i>
                 (below) at the World   Exhibition of Art and Technology in Paris in 1937.
                </span>
               </span>
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                </span>
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNL_AzIFhCI/AAAAAAAAByk/RN4T3dc_SbM/s1600/z.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                <img border="0" height="305" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNL_AzIFhCI/AAAAAAAAByk/RN4T3dc_SbM/s400/z.jpg" width="400"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: center;">
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                  Rafal Malczewski, Spring in the Mountains, 1937
                 </span>
                </span>
               </span>
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: center;">
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               Landscapes dominate Malczewski's oeuvre. His early works  were influenced by the Symbolist art of his father.   In the second half of the 1920s he was inspired by Cubism and  Futurism. His landscapes  primarily depicted the Tatra Mountains and the  surrounding hills of the  Podhale region. Frequent subjects included  sleepy towns and  empty landscapes, which often were supplemented with  the technical  achievements of contemporary civilization. Malczewski painted sparsely  populated train stations,  telegraph lines, and railroad tracks in  numerous variations. Clouds,  snow-covered peaks, fields, streams, and the crystal clarity of  the  atmosphere give his paintings a  metaphysical dimension.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMElkM3GrI/AAAAAAAAByo/Csre_oeSIdU/s1600/z.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="312" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMElkM3GrI/AAAAAAAAByo/Csre_oeSIdU/s400/z.jpg" width="400"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                 Rafal Malczewski, Kamieniolom, 1927
                </span>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <br/>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               Between 1927 and 1929 Malczewski created a series of paintings  depicting the landscapes of Yugoslavia and the French Riviera. In  1934 and 1935 he resided in Upper Silesia and recorded his  impressions of  this area in a series of dark, gloomy landscapes that  reflect the  industrial character of Black Silesia.   These contrast starkly with his sun-saturated, green-glowing   hilly landscapes of the Beskid Mountains. In his images of Silesia Malczewski depicted steel mills, mines and coal hills - nature   being destroyed by industry. The realistic observation and emotional   charge inherent in these works brings them very close to the German   "New Objectivity" movement.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>
              <div style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif; text-align: justify;">
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMFvq_xJfI/AAAAAAAAByw/fHarQeYxMQg/s1600/z.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="302" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMFvq_xJfI/AAAAAAAAByw/fHarQeYxMQg/s400/z.jpg" width="400"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                 Rafal Malczewski, Pejza grski, 1928
                </span>
                <br/>
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div style="text-align: justify;">
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en" style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                <span title="">
                 In   1939, after the outbreak of World War II and the German occupation of Poland, Malczewski fled to Paris.
                </span>
               </span>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
               </span>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                One year later, he went to Brazil, where he spent almost two years creating  watercolor views of Rio de Janeiro and Curitiba, and landscape paintings  of Parana. Malczewski then moved on to the United States, where he  lived in New York among other places. Finally, in 1942, he settled in  Montreal, traveling extensively in Canada and the United States in  search of subjects to paint.
               </span>
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMIjBG8uBI/AAAAAAAABy4/08XIiIeF9W0/s1600/z.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="282" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_Tyk2yoqzVkE/TNMIjBG8uBI/AAAAAAAABy4/08XIiIeF9W0/s400/z.jpg" width="400"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <span>
                 <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                  <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                   Rafal Malczewski, Pejza przedwiosenny (Snop wiatla), 1926
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </span>
               </div>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <span>
                 <span style="font-size: x-small;">
                  <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                  </span>
                 </span>
                </span>
               </div>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                The
               </span>
               <i style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                Canadian National Railways
               </i>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                and the
               </span>
               <i style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                Pacific National  Railways
               </i>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                commissioned
               </span>
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en" style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                <span title="">
                 Malczewski
                </span>
               </span>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                to create a series of watercolors advertising  the companies.
               </span>
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en" style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                <span title="">
                 In  December 1942, he had his first Canadian exhibition at the Montreal Museum  of Art.
                </span>
               </span>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                In 1944, he exhibited in Washington's prestigious Corcoran Gallery. Only in 1959
               </span>
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en" style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                <span title="">
                 Malczewski
                </span>
               </span>
               <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                visited  Poland again. A stroke suffered in 1957 resulted in partial paralysis, forcing  him to give up painting. Rafal Malczewski
               </span>
               <span class="long_text" id="result_box" lang="en">
                <span title="">
                 <span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">
                  died at a hospital in Montreal in 1965.
                 </span>
                </span>
               </span>
              </div>
