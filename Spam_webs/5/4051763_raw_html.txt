             <h1 class="post-title single">
              <a href="http://blogs.discovermagazine.com/inkfish/2014/12/30/time-hive-makes-bees-exhausted/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Time in the Hive Makes Bees Exhausted">
               Time in the Hive Makes Bees Exhausted
              </a>
             </h1>
             <div class="meta">
              <span class="meta-date">
               By
               <a href="http://discovermagazine.com/authors?name=Elizabeth+Preston" style="color:#555">
                Elizabeth Preston
               </a>
               | 
		December 30, 2014 10:33 am
              </span>
             </div>
             <div class="shareIcons" style="border-top:1px solid #999;border-bottom:1px solid #999;padding:7px 0 7px 0;margin-bottom:20px;">
              <div class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style addthis_32x32_style">
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_1">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_2">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_3">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_preferred_4">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_button_compact">
               </a>
               <a class="addthis_counter addthis_bubble_style">
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <p>
              <a href="http://blogs.discovermagazine.com/inkfish/files/2014/12/6953858614_0cf847f1ae_z.jpg">
               <img alt="6953858614_0cf847f1ae_z" class="aligncenter wp-image-2037" height="444" src="http://blogs.discovermagazine.com/inkfish/files/2014/12/6953858614_0cf847f1ae_z.jpg" width="444"/>
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              Facing a whole hive of bees at once can be overwhelmingeven for a bee. Young honeybees sleep more afterspending time in the hive than after being by themselves. They need the extra nap time, it seems, to build and maintain theirlearning brains.
             </p>
             <p>
              The first surprising thing about thismightbe that insects sleep at all. Since around the 1980s there is good evidence that insects showcharacteristics of sleep, says Guy Bloch, who studies bee behavior at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. Yes, their brains are tiny and organized differently from ours. But they rest in a similar way. And just assleeping helps ussort through the new things weve learned eachday, theres evidence that sleep in bees and fruit flies isalsotied up withmemory and learning, Bloch says.
             </p>
             <p>
              When a young worker honeybee emerges into the hive, shes thrust into a social life with countless rules to learn. She finds herself surrounded by waving antennae,fuzzybodies squeezing past each other, and a flurryofmessage-bearing chemicals.
              <span id="more-2043">
              </span>
              <span id="more-2036">
              </span>
              To find out how the bustle of hive life affects sleep, Bloch and his coauthor Ada Eban-Rothschild took some bees out of their colonies altogether.
             </p>
             <p>
              The researchers looked atbees that had just emerged as adults. They let some of these beesspend their first one or two days in the colony; others were isolated in the lab. Thenthe scientists moved all the bees into individual cages for monitoring.
             </p>
             <p>
              <a href="http://jeb.biologists.org/content/early/2014/12/09/jeb.110619.abstract">
               Bees that had beenin ahive spent more time sleeping
              </a>
              . And it wasnt just an extra nap here or there.Immediately after leavingthe hive, bees slept about five more hours per day. (In all, the socialized bees spent more than 60 percent of the day dozing.)
             </p>
             <p>
              Something aboutcolony life clearly conks out bees. To try to pindown that factor, the scientists put young bees back into theirhivesbut this time, they were inmesh cages that kept them separated from their sister bees. The young workers couldnt touch antennae or rub bodies with the other hive members. This meant they were partially cut off from the colonys communication. Surprisingly, though, these bees slept just as much afterward as bees that moved about freely.
             </p>
             <p>
              Beehives are dark and densely populated, Bloch says, so bees probably dont learn much by looking around. Its not visual inputthats wearing them out. But pheromones or other smells may be part of the flood of information they experience. They may also learn from vibrations and sounds in the colony, or possibly even electrical signals, Bloch says.
             </p>
             <p>
              Whatever informationcomes rushing at a bee during her first days in the hive, her brainclearly needs a lot of down time to process it. Next, Bloch wants to try and find out exactly what factors make these beessleep more. Honeybees need tolearn and remember a lot of thingsflight paths to new food sources, dances to communicate their findings to each otherbut Bloch wonders whether the social learning that happens inside hives might influence sleep even more than these tasks. It may be that no matter what species you are, navigating a buzzing social life is exhausting.
             </p>
