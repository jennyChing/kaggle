      <h3>
       Horsepower
      </h3>
      <p>
       Horsepower is the imperial (British) unit of power. A horsepower is the ability to do work at the rate of
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <strong>
          33,000 ft.lb per min
         </strong>
        </i>
        or
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <strong>
          550 ft.lb per second
         </strong>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that Power is "Work per unit time" and work is "Force through distance". In gravity systems Force is Weight - mass multiplied with gravity.
      </p>
      <p>
       The total horsepower developed by water falling from a given height is the product of the mass flow rate in pounds per minute times the falling height in feet divided by
       <i>
        33,000
       </i>
       . It can be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp
         </sub>
         = m
         <sub>
          min
         </sub>
         h g / 33000 (1)
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         where
        </em>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp
         </sub>
         = power (
         <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/major-loss-ducts-tubes-d_459.html" title="horsepower">
          horsepower, hp
         </a>
         )
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         m
         <sub>
          min
         </sub>
         = mass flow rate per minute (lb
         <sub>
          m
         </sub>
         /min)
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         h =
         <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/static-pressure-head-d_610.html" title="head pressure">
          head or height
         </a>
         (ft)
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         g = acceleration of gravity (32 ft/s
         <sup>
          2
         </sup>
         )
        </em>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       (1) can alternatively be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp
         </sub>
         = m
         <sub>
          sec
         </sub>
         h g / 550 (1b)
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         where
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         m
         <sub>
          sec
         </sub>
         = mass flow rate per second (lb
         <sub>
          m
         </sub>
         /s)
        </em>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       (1) can also be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp
         </sub>
         =  Q h / 33000
        </em>
        <i>
         (1c)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <i>
         where
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Q
        </em>
        <i>
         = volume flow rate (ft
         <sup>
          3
         </sup>
         /min)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         
        </em>
        <i>
        </i>
        <em>
         =
        </em>
        <i>
         <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/density-specific-weight-gravity-d_290.html" title="specific weight">
          specific weight
         </a>
         (lb
         <sub>
          f
         </sub>
         /ft
         <sup>
          3
         </sup>
         ) (weight is force)
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h4>
       Water Horsepower for Flow in gal/min
      </h4>
      <p>
       Water horsepower for flow in gal/min can be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          whp
         </sub>
         = SG Q
         <sub>
          gal
         </sub>
         h / 3960
        </em>
        <i>
         (1d)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <i>
         where
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Q
        </em>
        <i>
         = volume flow rate (gpm)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         SG
        </em>
        <i>
        </i>
        <em>
         =
        </em>
        <i>
         <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/density-specific-weight-gravity-d_290.html" title="specific gravity">
          specific gravity
         </a>
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         h
        </em>
        <i>
         =
         <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/static-pressure-head-d_610.html" title="head">
          head
         </a>
         (ft)
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       <em>
        SG
       </em>
       for water is
       <i>
        1.001
       </i>
       at
       <i>
        32
        <sup>
         o
        </sup>
        F
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        0.948
       </i>
       at
       <i>
        240
        <sup>
         o
        </sup>
        F
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/specific-gravity-liquid-fluids-d_294.html" title="specific gravity">
         Specific Gravity for some common Fluids and Liquids
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       Power to Pump Water
      </h3>
      <p>
       Required power (hp) to pump
       <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
        <em>
         1 cubic foot of water per minute (ft
         <sup>
          3
         </sup>
         /min)
        </em>
       </span>
       - efficiency
       <em>
        85%
       </em>
       - is indicated in the diagram below:
      </p>
      <p style="text-align: center;">
       <img alt="horsepower due head water" height="356" src="http://docs.engineeringtoolbox.com/documents/472/horsepower_due_head_water.png" title="horsepower due head water" width="566"/>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         1 hp (English horse power) = 745.7 W
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         1 ft (foot) = 0.3048 m
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       Example - Required Power to lift
       <em>
        10 ft
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        /min
       </em>
       of Water
       <em>
        600 ft
       </em>
      </h4>
      <p>
       According the diagram above
       <em>
        1 hp
       </em>
       is required to lift
       <em>
        1 ft
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        /min
       </em>
       of water
       <em>
        600 ft
       </em>
       . Required power to pump
       <em>
        10 ft
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        /min
       </em>
       can be calculated as
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       <em>
        (10 ft
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        /min) (1 hp) / (1 ft
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        /min) =
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         10
        </span>
        hp
       </em>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Shaft or Brake Horsepower
      </h3>
      <p>
       The brake horsepower is the amount of real horsepower going to the pump, not the horsepower used by the motor. In the metric system kilowatts (kW) is used.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to
       <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/pump-fan-efficiency-d_633.html" title="hydraulic pressure loss">
        hydraulic, mechanical and volumetric losses
       </a>
       in a pump or turbine the actual horsepower available for work on or from the fluid is less than the total horsepower supplied.
      </p>
      <h4>
       Shaft or Brake Horsepower for a Pump or Fan
      </h4>
      <p>
       The brake horse power -
       <i>
        bhp
       </i>
       - for a pump or fan can be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          bhp
         </sub>
         = (  Q h / 33000 ) / 
        </em>
        <i>
         (2)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <i>
         where
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          bhp
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         = brake horse power (horsepower, hp)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Q
        </em>
        <i>
         = volume flow rate (ft
         <sup>
          3
         </sup>
         /min, cfm)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         
        </em>
        <i>
         =
         <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/pump-fan-efficiency-d_633.html" title="efficiency">
          overall efficiency
         </a>
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h4>
       Shaft or Brake Horsepower for a Turbine
      </h4>
      <p>
       The brake horse power -
       <i>
        bhp
       </i>
       - for a turbine can be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          bhp
         </sub>
         =  (  Q h / 33000 )
        </em>
        <i>
         (2b)
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       Input Horsepower to the Electrical Motor
      </h3>
      <p>
       The input horsepower to the electrical motor for a pump or fan can be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp_el
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         =
        </i>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          bhp
         </sub>
         /
        </em>
        <i>
        </i>
        <em>
         
         <sub>
          e
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         (3)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <i>
         or
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp_el
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         = (
        </i>
        <em>
         m h g / 33000) /
        </em>
        <i>
         (
        </i>
        <em>
         
        </em>
        <i>
        </i>
        <em>
         
         <sub>
          e
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         ) (3b)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <i>
         where
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          hp_el
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         = input power to the electrical motor
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         
         <sub>
          e
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         = mechanical efficiency of the electrical motor
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       Horsepower in Kilowatts and other Units
      </h3>
      <p>
       Horsepower can be
       <a href="http://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/unit-converter-d_185.html" title="unit converter">
        converted to other common units
       </a>
       as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         1 hp (English horse power) = 745.7 W = 0.746 kW = 550 ft.lb/s = 2545 Btu/h = 33000 ft.lb/m = 1.0139 metric horse power
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Transforming horsepower to kiloWatt can be expressed as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          kW
         </sub>
         = 0.746 P
         <sub>
          hp
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         (4)
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <i>
         where
        </i>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          kW
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         = power (kW)
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Together with the equations above it's possible to express (4) in many common combinations as:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <em>
         P
         <sub>
          kW
         </sub>
         = 0.746 (m h / 33000) /  
         <sub>
          e
         </sub>
        </em>
        <i>
         (5)
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       Example - Pump Power
      </h3>
      <p>
       The horsepower required to pump
       <em>
        50 lb
        <sub>
         m
        </sub>
        /min
       </em>
       water a head of
       <em>
        10 ft
       </em>
       with a pump with overall efficiency
       <em>
        0.7
       </em>
       - can be calculated with eq.
       <em>
        (3)
       </em>
       as
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       <em>
        P
        <sub>
         hp
        </sub>
        = ((50 lb
        <sub>
         m
        </sub>
        /min)(10 ft) (32 ft/s
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ) / 33000) / 0.8
       </em>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       <em>
        =
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         0.6
        </span>
        hp
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       The power in kW can be calculated as
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       <em>
        P
        <sub>
         kW
        </sub>
        = 0.746 (0.6 hp)
       </em>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       <em>
        =
        <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
         0.45
        </span>
        kW
       </em>
      </p>
