             <b>
              FOUND POSTCARDS - #tbt Throwback Dispatches
             </b>
            </p>
            <p>
             Long before the days of
posting your vacation pictures on Facebook, tweeting about your weekend getaway
or Instagramming the view from your beach towel, there were postcards.
Essentially, dropping a postcard into the mailbox was the only inexpensive way
to provide a short status update, along with a picture of where you were
while out on the road.
            </p>
            <p>
             The American Guide feels
postcards were also in effect miniature dispatches, similar to what we do here on
Tumblr. They were brief guides to places much further away than they are today
 reachable then only through effort and sacrifice. They led insight into the
location where the sender purchased his or her postcard, a place they might never be
again and a spot the receiver would likely never see themselves.
            </p>
            <p>
             Today, stop into any
antique shop and youll typically find a dilapidated and dusty box somewhere in the
corner stuffed with old postcards. Some are blank but many arent.Who
buys these things?Do people ever read the messages on the back? Well, now
WE do!
            </p>
            <p>
             Starting today, as a
regular Thursday feature (or as regularly as possible), The American Guide
will post #tbt Throwback Dispatches in the form of found postcards. Some of
the messages may be funny, some may be charming and likely some will be
extremely boring, but we hope they will all take you on a quick journey back in
time to a place we can never go again
            </p>

             </b>
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              New Harbor was the home of Samoset, the Indian who, in March 1621, startled the Pilgrims of Plymouth by appearing among them with the words, Much welcome, Englishmen. He explained that he was a sachem and had learned the language from Englishmen engaged in fishing off Monhegan. On his next visit he brought with him Squando, who became a friend of the settlers. Chief Samoset was a magnificent figure, tall and straight, his body naked save for a loin cloth. The advice of these Indians enabled the Pilgrims to replenish their dwindling stores, a friendly act that was later repaid with treachery. -

            <p>
             I ride the ferry from New Harbor to Monhegan Island, tracing a well-worn journey over waters once so plentiful that early explorers kept knowledge of them shrouded in mystery. When no hope of protecting the secret of these fishing grounds was left, a lighthouse was built on Monhegan to show everyone their way. Today I land on the island and hike up to and past this lighthouse, marching through rugged woods to the tallest cliffs on the coast of Maine. The wind here whips up a wildness and a searching in anyone who stands looking looking for the secret that was lost with Samoset.

             <b>
              SUISUN CITY, CALIFORNIA
             </b>
            </p>
            <p>
             Suisun City is home to Suisun Marsh, a critical component of the San Francisco Bay-Delta estuary ecosystem. Established during the California gold rush, Suisun City grew to be a major transportation and commercial hub due to its proximity to shipping links, the transcontinental railroad, and agricultural opportunities created by the diking of Suisun Bay shorty after the founding of the city. Increasing salinity and land subsidence caused farms and ranches to fail, while the construction of Interstate 80 and Travis Air Force Base in neighboring Fairfield diverted commerce. In the 1980s a survey of San Francisco bay area communities listed Suisun City as the least attractive place in the region. Crime and gang activity were prevalent. Today Suisun City is attempting various redevelopment projects. The land and waterways that make up Suisun Marsh are managed collectively by federal and state wildlife agencies, land trusts and over 150 privately owned duck hunting clubs.

             <b>
              VISUAL CONTRADICTIONS - Saugerties, New York
             </b>
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              Set in the hill slopes of the headland formed by the confluence of EsopusCreek and the Hudson River, SAUGERTIES (Dutch, sawyers town),45 m. (100 alt.,3,918 pop.), is an attractive village of Colonial, Greek Revival, and white frame homes with deep lawns, fences, and flagstone walks.Factories turning out paper, leather, and canvas do not mar the pleasingpicture. -
              <a href="http://babel.hathitrust.org/cgi/pt?id=mdp.39015008915889;view=2up;seq=6" target="_blank">
               New York: A Guide to The Empire State
              </a>
              (WPA, 1940)
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             ***
            </p>
            <p>
             The village of Saugerties is still filled with picturesque homes, many of them stone and brick historical homes built in the 18th and 19th century, but like so many smaller American towns there are very few factories and industries left. The village itself sits on the Hudson River and is relatively quaint and suburban in appearance, but the Town of Saugerties is more spread out and has a decidedly rural character, with several smaller farms, vacation homes and remnants of a declining industrial past. I lived on and off in the more rural areas of the town for several years, and continue to travel through the area on a weekly basis. I am always captivated by the visual contradictions I encounter that reflect a range of failed human endeavors surrounded by the natural beauty and idyllic light of the Catskills.
            </p>

           <div class="caption">
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              From handsome stock-breeding farms to forlorn holdings in cutover timber, or dust-ridden farms in the western portion, Minnesota is very much an agricultural state, concerned with the preservation of its resources. Attempts are being made to curb soil erosion by strip and terrace farming and to control the grasshopper and other insect pests. The farmers of Minnesota are constantly trying to raise the standard of rural life -
              <a href="https://archive.org/details/minnesotastategu00writrich" target="_blank">
               Minnesota: A State Guide
              </a>
              (WPA, 1938)
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             <b>
              ***
             </b>
            </p>
            <p>
             <b>
              THE LATCH ON THE SCREEN - Western Minnesota
             </b>
            </p>
            <p>
             This project is about daily life in the farming communities of western Minnesota today. The appearance of main street, once vibrant town centers, the land and its homes speak to a weathered history and slow decline. Yet local residents remain proud of their way of life and, in spite of economic challenges, many young families continue to return from urban areas, opting for a rural small town living.
            </p>
            <p>
             The idea for this project grew out of frequent two hundred mile commutes from my home in St. Paul to a rural hospital in western Minnesota where I have moonlighted for the past few years. The roads are often empty and I seldom see an individual in the towns I drive through. The topography of western Minnesota is flat and provides expansive views that leave one enveloped by a sense of solitude, one that is furthered during long and cold winters. We have two young children and live in a busy, urban neighborhood surrounded by perpetual development. The perceived disparity between my life and those along my commute triggered questions about the experience of a life lived in rural Minnesota townships.
            </p>
            <p>
             Over time I have come to appreciate the profound intimacy between people and land, earth and tractor, crop and farmer. Harvest yields and prices received for corn, sugar beets and soy beans fluctuate due to factors outside farmers control-climate, global food supply, market prices and demands. This is how it has been for decades, with the local knowledge, wisdom and practice for dealing with it passed from one generation to the next. Faith in God and a Christian tradition continue. Restaurants and churches show their age, the dcor is often fading and yet their continued presence remain vital as pillars of communal support and togethering. Though declining student populations have forced many smaller schools to close, the regional district school and its sports teams continue to serve as a point of pride for residents.
            </p>
            <p>
             While people I met of all ages were candid about the fading prosperity of their communities, they remain sanguine regarding the future of life in western Minnesota.
            </p>
            <p>
             The title of the project comes from the following Wright Morris quote:
            </p>
            <p>
             <i>
              Theres little to see, but things leave an impression. Its a matter of time and repetition. As something old wears thin or out, something new wears in. The handle on the pump, the crank on the churn, the dipper floating in the bucket, the latch on the screen, the door on the privy, the fender on the stove, the knees of the pants and the seat of the chair, the handle of the brush and the lid to the pot exist in time but outside taste; they wear in more than they wear out. It cant be helped. Its neither good nor bad. Its the nature of life.
             </i>
            </p>
            <p>
             While the project is still ongoing and evolving, I think at its core this series is about the common emotional experience I feel with those l meet in rural western Minnesota. It is that connection that I also feel with my grandparents and their parents. Both of my grandparents lived in houses with latches on the screen door. My childhood home has a latch on the screen door. Our current house built in 1923 has a latch on the screen door. My parents and grandparents both started families, aged and died. I will too. I hope that as I continue to age I will wear in, more than out.
            </p>
            <p>
             This collection of photographs is an attempt to convey an honest and unassuming portrait of the land, its people and their traditions. I want the work to shed light on the resilience, intelligence, understated confidence and integrity of those I met.
            </p>

            <p>
             <b>
              THE CITY VIEW - Trenton, New Jersey
             </b>
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              One-fifth of the State, a long strip barely 20 miles wide, the city belt of New Jersey, lies within the Triassic Lowland division, which extends from Delaware River to Hudson River, and north from US I (the straight-line highway between Newark and Trenton) to the base of the Ramapo Mountains. Manufacturing and commerce have centered in this area, with the result that it includes Paterson, Passaic, Jersey City, Newark, Elizabeth, New Brunswick and Trenton every large urban center in the State with the exceptions of Camden and Atlantic City.
              <br/>
              
              <a href="https://archive.org/details/newjerseyaguide00fedemiss" target="_blank">
               New Jersey, A Guide To Its Present and Past
              </a>
              (WPA, 1939)
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             ***
            </p>
            <p>
             Trenton Makes, the World Takes was adopted by the city of Trenton, New Jersey as a slogan in 1917 to acknowledge the citys history as a major manufacturing center in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, as well as its leading role at the time as a leader in manufacturing of rubber, wire rope, ceramics and cigars. The city installed the slogan as a sign made from 2,400 electric light bulbs onto the bridge connecting Trenton with Morrisville, Pennsylvania.
            </p>
            <p>
             Trenton is an example of the cities that grew up along the Atlantic Seabord Fall Line. A fall line is a zone in which the harder crystalline rocks of an upland region transition to the softer sedimentary rock of a costal plain. Rivers that cross the area erode the soil and expose the harder rocks. Rapids and waterfalls form as the land slopes downward to the lower elevations on the rivers course to the ocean. Cities such as Trenton, Philadelphia, Baltimore and Richmond grew up along these fall lines because the falls and rapids provided the water power needed to run mills, hence the growth of industries in these areas. Fall lines mark the head of navigation which is the farthest point above the mouth of a river that can be navigated by ships. Like many US cities, Trentons manufacturing and industrial sectors declined as job were moved oversees, and today the citys larget employer is the state of New Jersey.
            </p>
            <p>
             The Quakers established the first settlement in the area in 1679, and in 1719 the town was named Trent-towne after William Trent, a prominent trader and merchant who was a leading landholder in the area. General George Washingtons army crossed the icy Delaware River on December 26, 1776 in a surprise attack on the Hession troops in Trenton. The ensuing Battle of Trenton was George Washingtons first military victory and a significant turning point in the American Revolutionary War. Trenton also served briefly as the national capital in November and December 1784. Trenton officially became the New Jersey state capital in 1790, making it the only state capital to be located on the border with another state, a distinction that it held until 1864 when Nevada entered the union with Carson City as its capital.
            </p>
