       <h1 itemprop="name">
        <a class="c " href="/columns/ask_hank/phyllo_and_puff_pastry" title="Phyllo and puff pastry, compared: Similar but different">
         Phyllo and puff pastry, compared
        </a>
       </h1>
       <h2>
        Similar but different
       </h2>
       <h5 class="c_meta">
        By
        <span itemprop="author">
         <a href="/user/hsawtelle" itemprop="url">
          Hank Sawtelle
         </a>
        </span>
       </h5>
       <h5 class="c_meta">
        July 13, 2010
       </h5>
       <div class="c_rightBox">
        <div class="n_utilityBox">
         <ul class="n_utilityLinks">
          <li>
           <a class="n_email" href="/forms/mailto?cid=289277" rel="noindex">
            <span>
            </span>
            E-Mail to a friend
           </a>
          </li>
          <li>
           <a class="n_print" href="/columns/ask_hank/phyllo_and_puff_pastry/print" rel="noindex" target="_blank">
            <span>
            </span>
            Print Friendly
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
        </div>
        <div class="clear">
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Whats the difference between puff-pastry sheets and phyllo dough? Can they be used interchangeably?
        </strong>
        <br clear="none"/>
         Jamey G., Portland, Oregon
       </p>
       <p>
        I was sure I knew the answer to this question as soon as I read it; it just seems like something I should know. But when I went into my brain to access the answer, all I found were dirty jokes and Seinfeld quotes. OK, time to hit the books.
       </p>
       <p>
        Phyllo (pronounced FEE-low) and puff pastry are not identical, but they do have a lot in common. Both are made of layered, thin sheets of low-fat wheat dough. These layers are the leaves referred to in the words phyllo (Greek for leaf) and
        <em>
         pte feuillete
        </em>
        (the French name for puff pastry, which means leaf dough  at least sort of). When cooked (usually baked), phyllo and puff pastry both rise, as the layers separate somewhat and turn golden brown and crisp. So its easy to imagine these products sharing a common, proto-European, layered-dough ancestry. (See also
        <a href="/articles/features/strudel_secrets" shape="rect">
         strudel
        </a>
        .)
       </p>
       <table>
        <tr>
         <td colspan="1" rowspan="1">
          <div class="c_photo" style="width:300px;">
           <img alt="" height="225" src="/hunk/116256" title="Photo courtesy Hank Sawtelle (#289297)" width="300"/>
           <div class="c_photoCaption">
            A sidecut of phyllo.
           </div>
          </div>
         </td>
         <td colspan="1" rowspan="1">
          <div class="c_photo" style="width:300px;">
           <img alt="" height="225" src="/hunk/116257" title="Photo courtesy Hank Sawtelle (#289296)" width="300"/>
           <div class="c_photoCaption">
            A sidecut of puff.
           </div>
          </div>
         </td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
         <td colspan="1" rowspan="1">
          <div class="c_photo" style="width:300px;">
           <img alt="" height="225" src="/hunk/116258" title="Photo courtesy Hank Sawtelle (#289295)" width="300"/>
           <div class="c_photoCaption">
            A phyllo triangle.
           </div>
          </div>
         </td>
         <td colspan="1" rowspan="1">
          <div class="c_photo" style="width:300px;">
           <img alt="" height="225" src="/hunk/116259" title="Photo courtesy Hank Sawtelle (#289298)" width="300"/>
           <div class="c_photoCaption">
            A puff triangle.
           </div>
          </div>
         </td>
        </tr>
       </table>
       <p>
        But there are significant differences between the two. Phyllo sheets are relatively dry, and stored (refrigerated or frozen) stacked on top of each other. The layers can be separated, and cooks can select the particular number of layers theyd like to use.
        <a href="/recipes/collections/Contributors/Ivy+Manning/Beet+Greens+and+Feta+Phyllo+Pockets+with+Yogurt+Dill+Dip" shape="rect">
         Recipes
        </a>
        often call for brushing butter or oil between the layers before reassembling them.
       </p>
       <p>
        Puff pastry, on the other hand, is a more integrated product, with thin layers of butter trapped between the sheets of wheat dough. The layers cant be separated before cooking, so what you see is what you get. Puff pastry usually contains more layers, and generally rises higher from the steam and expanding air that forms between the dough sheets. Cooked phyllo has a crisp crackle, while puff pastry has a buttery richness (think aerated pie crust).
       </p>
       <p>
        They cant always be used interchangeably, because their properties are so different. But with the understanding that you are venturing into test-pilot territory, I cant think of any reason not to experiment. In fact, it should be fun.
       </p>
       <p>
        My mom (hi, Mom!) used to make
        <a href="http://find.myrecipes.com/recipes/recipefinder.dyn?action=displayRecipe&amp;recipe_id=257599" shape="rect" target="_blank">
         phyllo cheese puff
        </a>
        hors doeuvres  crunchy triangles of salty, creamy goodness. I dont see any reason why these wouldnt work with puff-pastry dough. Theyd be puffier and richer and flakier, probably more of an appetizer than a party snack. But youd save yourself the step of brushing butter between the phyllo layers.
       </p>
       <p>
        On the other hand, crunchy and gooey
        <a href="/books/collections/all_books/The+Language+of+Baklava/Poetic+Baklava" shape="rect">
         baklava
        </a>
        , a dessert made from phyllo, walnuts, and honey, might not work so well with puff-pastry dough. First of all, you cant separate the layers to get walnuts between them. And the gravity of rich, buttery puff pastry smothered in rich, sticky honey syrup would be overwhelming. (Or would it? Hmmm.)
       </p>
       <div class="l_adWell l_ad-mediumrectangle l_adpos-textright">
        <div class="l_adHeader">
         Advertisement
        </div>
        <a href="/app/htce">
         <img alt="How to Cook Everything for iPad ad" height="250" src="http://ads.culinate.com/htce/HTCE-iPad-Med-Rect.png" width="300"/>
        </a>
       </div>
       <p>
        From the puff-pastry angle, a
        <a href="http://www.ciachef.edu/enthusiasts/recipes/goat_cheese_tartlets.asp" shape="rect" target="_blank">
         tartlet
        </a>
        or other savory-topped thing should work, in its own way, with phyllo. Youd probably want to brush some butter or oil between the layers to avoid extreme dryness, but the result would still be lighter and crisper than the puff-pastry version. (Phyllo also needs to be protected with a damp towel when youre not actively working with it, or it will dry out and crumble.)
       </p>
       <p>
        Some applications, however, pretty much demand puff pastry. Can you imagine a buttery, tender croissant (essentially a puff-pastry triangle rolled and shaped into a crescent) executed in crunchy, dry phyllo dough? I cannot. Although
        <a href="/articles/features/how_to_make_palmiers" shape="rect">
         palmiers
        </a>
        , the flat, heart-shaped, rolled puff-pastry cookies, might fall into the just-crazy-enough-to-work-with-phyllo category. Jamey, I nominate you to try it and let us know.
       </p>
       <p>
        There are countless yards of frozen phyllo and puff pastry available in most supermarkets and at some
        <a href="http://www.grandcentralbakery.com/u-bake/U-Bake-Menu" shape="rect" target="_blank">
         bakeries
        </a>
        , but what if youre masochistic enough to make it yourself? (One reason you might decide to is that
        <a href="http://www.pepperidgefarm.com/ProductDetail.aspx?catID=767&amp;prdID=120690" shape="rect" target="_blank">
         Pepperidge Farm
        </a>
        , the most common grocery-store brand of puff pastry, is made with palm and soybean oils instead of butter.)
       </p>
       <p>
        Puff pastry is made by hand at hardcore bakeries. Im not going to lie to you: it is a pain to produce. The method involves encasing a brick of butter in a jacket of dough and then repeatedly rolling it out and folding it over onto itself until you have a bazillion layers. Sounds easy enough, but temperature control and rolling technique are crucial; if done incorrectly, the layers will fuse and the result is basically a very tough pie dough. Nonetheless, I have done it, mainly just to prove I could. (In everyday life, I just buy it at the store.) The best description of the process I have found is in the book
        <em>
         <a href="/books/collections/all_books/puff" shape="rect">
          Puff
         </a>
         ,
        </em>
        by Martha Holmberg.
       </p>
       <p>
        Homemade phyllo recipes are more elusive. The paper-thin sheets of store-bought phyllo cant be realistically produced without specialized industrial equipment (hello, Amazon wish list!). But determined cooks have turned to their pasta machines, cranking out sheets of dough as thin as possible and stacking them to create
        <a href="http://greekfood.about.com/od/greekbreadspitas/r/phyllo.htm" shape="rect" target="_blank">
         homemade phyllo
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        So go mix it up and have fun. Just
        <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jyaLZHiJJnE" shape="rect" target="_blank">
         don't cross the streams
        </a>
        . It would be bad.
       </p>
       <p class="bio">
        Based in Portland, Oregon,
        <strong>
         Hank Sawtelle
        </strong>
        has engineering, legal, and culinary degrees.
       </p>
