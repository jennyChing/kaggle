         The Houston Rockets got blown out by the Oklahoma City Thunder in Game 1 in what many call a "traditional" 1-8 seed matchup. This was the matchup the Thunder wanted, an offensive minded Rockets team that didn't try to play defense despite how young, quick, and long they are. The Rockets aren't the tallest team to contest and that will come back to bite them this series, but Houston's defense game plan played right into the hands of the Thunder.
         <p>
          Kevin Durant and Russell Westbrook both shot 7-15. Durant finished with 24 points while Westbrook finished with 19 points. The Rockets opted to try Carlos Delfino try his hand at defending Kevin Durant, a feat that proved to be disastrous. Delfino is one of the league's better versatile defenders, but Durant's quickness coupled with his height was too much for him to handle. Houston seemed to want to play a straight man defense, with some minimal help (that wasn't there the majority of the night). They tried to outscore the Thunder, something Oklahoma City tries to do also. The Thunder are usually more successful at that end because of Durant, a 6'10" small forward who can shoot the 3-point ball and take the ball off the dribble from beyond the arc to the basket with ease.
         </p>
         <p>
          If the Rockets want to win the series, they have to force the ball out of Durant's hands. Houston can definitely come up with a game plan on some switches on Durant at the top of the key and switching someone smaller on Kendrick Perkins, who's not a player close to being an offensive threat anywhere on the court. The point is clear: the Rockets need to force someone else to beat them. In losses, Durant's statistics are still pretty good with 30.3ppg in 41.8mpg. His field goal attempts are 9.5-19.6 (48%). Those are in losses, so you can imagine how well he does in wins. Since we're focusing in losses, the ball has to get out of his hands and passing it to another player. This is where Russell Westbrook enters in. His statistics in losses for a member of their Big 3 are pretty disappointing compared to Durant's, but that's a huge comparison anyway. In losses, Westbrook has averaged 22.9ppg in 37.5mpg. He's field goal attempts are 7.9-20.3 (39%). He's not only taking more shots than Durant and missing more in the process, but he's averaging 8 points less than his teammate. Westbrook is the key for the Rockets' chances of winning a game or two in the next upcoming games, especially games in the Thunder's homecourt.
         </p>
         <p>
          When Westbrook is forced to carry the team, his confidence skyrockets and his shot selection becomes very questionable. Unlike Durant, Westbrook doesn't tend to get his teammates involved when he has the hot hand or is forced to shoulder the offense, which is exactly why the Rockets have to force him to carry the team. The more shots he takes, the less shots Durant takes. The more Westbrook holds on to the ball, the colder Durant gets on the court. The Rockets have enough personnel to force Durant to give up the ball and enough coaching smarts to force Westbrook to create his own shot consistently in this series. If the Rockets want to give themselves any chance to win this series, the answer lies with Russell Westbrook.
         </p>
