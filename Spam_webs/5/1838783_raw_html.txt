        <div class="author">

         <a class="cat-converse" href="http://www.deathandtaxesmag.com/converse/">

        <div class="article-content clearfix">
         <p>
          Once upon time, hesher-beach metal ruled the world. Seriously. Turning on the radio nowadays youd have no idea, but when Van Halen were still in the garage in the 70s they were drawing 1500 kids to their shows with no airplay at all. Same for Black Flag.Nowadays the biggest guitar band in the world is probably Coldplay. Think about that.
         </p>
         <p>
          The Shrine bring us back to a time when rock shredded and grabbed you and shook the piss out of you in a way it mostly doesnt above ground anymore. That stuff is underground, where The Shrine are deserving kings.
         </p>
         <p>
          The Shrine founder and great dude Josh Landau caught up with us to explain the bands non-stop touring, the good life in Venice Beach and the big problem with your sisters boyfriends band.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Who had The Shrines back in the early years, or helped put you on?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           Josh Landau
          </b>
          :
          <a href="http://www.deathandtaxesmag.com/214507/black-flag-the-great-punk-soap-opera/" target="_blank" title="Black Flag">
           Chuck Dukowski.
          </a>
          We played a lot of shows with him early on and he helped us make and release some of our first recordings. We still go to him when we need help or advice on things and hes as badass as ever. We always try and catch a little bit of his sweat in a jar and experiment with harnessing the power that fuels him! Our road dogs Tortilla, Babygurl, Shredwards and Klaus-Hammer who slept on kitchen floors with us and drove all night to the next show(s). Sam Mancino and Kris Kirk for cranking out epic art. Olivia Jaffe for trespassing into peoples backyards to shoot photos of us in their empty pools. Buddy Nichols and Rick Charnoski for shooting. Jordan Joseffer at Thrasher Magazine.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Is there anyone that you hope to help now that The Shrine has earned more notoriety?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL:
          </b>
          Probably just the people mentioned above and our road dogs and artist friends who have helped us. Its the sickest thing to be able to give our friends some fundage because we sold a shit-load of T-shirts with their art on it, and now some skate rat in Russia is wearing it.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           From the outside Burger Records seems to be very much like a family. Do you guys feel that camaraderie?
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           JL:
          </strong>
          <a href="http://www.deathandtaxesmag.com/215812/cons-project-l-a-dishes-diy-culture-for-the-kids/" target="_blank" title="Burger Records">
           Burger
          </a>
          is rad, but theyre down in Fullerton and were in Venice. We dont see them that often. Weve played the store a few times and they put out our Primitive Blast tape but they have 10,000 bands on the label so theyre mega-busy. Our tape for Bless Off is coming out on Tankcrimes Records out of Oakland.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           How does your working relationship with Burger differ from Tee Pee?
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <strong>
           JL:
          </strong>
          We talk with Tee Pee every day. They call ourlimousines when we need to go do laundry or skate a pool. Burger sends us tapes before we go on tour and weve had some parking lot skate sessions with those dudes.
          <b>
          </b>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Why are you guys so hell-bent on destroying rock and roll?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : Because your sisters boyfriends band is being hailed as TheSaviorsof Rock and Roll and thats stupid.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           What are your plans for supporting Bless Off? Can we expect you guys at SXSW?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : Definitely, were heading back to Europe for another month with Red Fang. Were flying to Austin for the Thrasher Death Match at SXSW with Converse. Were flying to Austin for one day and then its straight to Europe. After that were playing the Tankcrimes Brainsqueeze festival in Oakland and doing a West Coast Tour with Kadavar including Psycho De Mayo Festival w/ Pentagram and Saint Vitus. Holy shit, thats gonna fucking rule.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           You guys are kind of leading the pack on a classic, nihilist, hesher rock. Do you think you can get it back on the charts, where it used to be?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : It doesnt matter, it can sit on the toilet for eternity. Were touring the world and as long as there are 5 kids there who are stoked well do it till the wheels fall off.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Where do you see punk and metal going in the next five years? Are there any artists that you feel really deserve the limelight, or artists that you believe have the potential to revolutionize the landscape?
           <br/>
           JL:
          </strong>
          Dirty Fences.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Whats the story behind the lyrics by Chuck Dukowski? How did you get a hold of them and the permission to use?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : He just told em to me one day and they blew me away. Then later, I asked if I could put them to these riffs we had and he said sure. Went over to his house and played the riffs with him for a few hours. Then six months later we recorded it.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Who are a few artists relevant to the last three years that inspires The Shrine?And the last ten years?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : Dirty Fences, Hot Lunch, Lecherous Gaze, Annihilation Time, Boris, Earthless, Chuck Dukowski Sextet, Eyehategod, Zig Zags. And skaters like Raney Beres, Don Nguyen, Spanky,
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Doesyour relationship with street culture, punk and metal inspire your fashion style or aesthetic as individuals? As a group?
           <br/>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : It just comes from the neighborhood. From growing up in Venice and Santa Monica and going to Rip City Skateshop at 10 years old and seeing all the old 70s boards and records nailed to the walls. It comes from Motorhead and Thin Lizzy and Black Flag and the Z-Boys.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           You are constantly around other cultures and scenes because of your show schedule. How has your travel and touring experience informed your style?
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
          </strong>
          <b>
           JL
          </b>
          : Its definitely made us even more proud and stoked of where were from. It wasnt until we left home a lot, and I had people say Holy shit youre from the promised land while were standing in a foot of snow in Slovenia and they can only skate for a month or whatever a year, that I really realized how sick it is here. It blew my mind to come home and skate down to the beach  which Ive done forever  but I never appreciated it like I do now. But at the same time, theyve got this epic fantasy about Venice and Los Angeles thats not really true. Things are pretty mellow most of the time here in Mar Vista. But its still sick.
         </p>

          featuring the Shrine
