  <meta content="Ever since I wrote this post about World War I and the knitting associated 
with it, I have seen several other articles pop up around the internet 
about this topic and I thought I'd share.

This one from Australia is interesting in that it discusses the wool 
issues, covers the First World War through the Korean War, and has some 
great photos.

This one from Washington State is long but includes some great details 
about what sorts of things were knit during the war, like &quot;wool helmets and 
vests, chest covers and fingerless mitts to allow trigger access.&quot; Socks 
(of course), sweaters and &quot;mufflers&quot; were also in demand. Everyone was 
knitting, expected to knit and discouraged from knitting for personal use. 
This article really gives you a sense of the pervasiveness of the wartime 
knitting and the urgency felt by knitters around the world who were asked 
to knit for the boys in the trenches.

Popular at the time was the notion of not wasting precious time that could 
be spent knitting. This &quot;bag of the hour&quot; was both useful and &quot;smart.&quot; 
Also, note its air distingue [sic]. Trs adorable!



I also discovered that there is a National WWI Museum here in the U.S. As 
of this time they don't seem to have a knitting program like the National 
WWII Museum has, it still looks as though it has many interesting programs 
and exhibits. The next time you're in Kansas City, MO, check it out--and of 
course I'm going to want to hear all about it!

Some folks in England are filming a movie about WWI, and some knitters 
have banded together to knit costumes based on old patterns and recreations 
from old photos. Be careful following these links if you love history and 
knitting--I am still wiping the drool off my keyboard.

I will leave you with a Monday Medley of Patterns--all free. Holly Shaltz 
has a great website with patterns from the era, reinterpreted for modern 
knitters. There's even a pattern for socks.

You probably figured out that I'm fascinated by all this, so if you see 
anything else related to WWI out there that you think I might like, 
especially in commemoration of the 100th anniversary, let me know!" property="og:description"/>

    <section data-content-field="main-content" id="page" role="main">
     <div class="article-wrapper">
      <article class="hentry tag-history tag-knitting tag-patterns author-tamara-goff post-type-text" data-item-id="53eb57a4e4b03183e5ef1ed1" id="article-53eb57a4e4b03183e5ef1ed1">

       <div class="blog-entry-content">
        <header>
         <h1 class="entry-title">
          <a href="/blog/2014/9/8/moreaboutwwone">
           A bit more about WWI
          </a>
         </h1>
        </header>
        <!--SPECIAL CONTENT-->
        <div class="body entry-content">
         <div class="sqs-layout sqs-grid-12 columns-12" data-layout-label="Post Body" data-type="item" data-updated-on="1407936241659" id="item-53eb57a4e4b03183e5ef1ed1">
          <div class="row sqs-row">
           <div class="col sqs-col-12 span-12">
            <div class="sqs-block html-block sqs-block-html" data-block-type="2" id="block-53120cd192ea7b4db275">
             <div class="sqs-block-content">
              <p>
               Ever since I wrote this post about
               <a href="http://www.knitoasis.com/blog/2014/7/29/remembering-wwi-and-its-knitters">
                World War I and the knitting
               </a>
               associated with it, I have seen several other articles pop up around the internet about this topic and I thought I'd share.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://theconversation.com/one-million-pairs-of-socks-knitting-for-victory-in-the-first-world-war-30149">
                This one from Australia
               </a>
               is interesting in that it discusses the wool issues, covers the First World War through the Korean War, and has some great photos.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.historylink.org/index.cfm?DisplayPage=output.cfm&amp;File_Id=5721">
                This one from Washington State
               </a>
               is long but includes some great details about what sorts of things were knit during the war, like "wool helmets and vests, chest covers and fingerless mitts to allow trigger access." Socks (of course), sweaters and "mufflers" were also in demand. Everyone was knitting, expected to knit and discouraged from knitting for personal use. This article really gives you a sense of the pervasiveness of the wartime knitting and the urgency felt by knitters around the world who were asked to knit for the boys in the trenches.
              </p>
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="sqs-block image-block sqs-block-image" data-block-type="5" id="block-yui_3_17_2_1_1409999919421_67853">
             <div class="sqs-block-content">
              <div class="image-block-outer-wrapper layout-caption-below ">
               <div class="intrinsic" style="max-width:605.0px;">
                <div class="image-block-wrapper has-aspect-ratio" data-description='&lt;p&gt;Popular at the time was the notion of not wasting precious time that could be spent knitting.&nbsp; This "bag of the hour" was both useful and "smart."&nbsp; Also, note its air distingue [sic].&nbsp; Trs adorable!&lt;/p&gt;' style="padding-bottom:150.74380493164062%;">
                 <noscript>
                  <img alt='Popular at the time was the notion of not wasting precious time that could be spent knitting. This "bag of the hour" was both useful and "smart." Also, note its air distingue [sic]. Trs adorable!' src="http://static1.squarespace.com/static/53989e26e4b04c5c0979151f/t/540b5a77e4b098a72eaaacc2/1410204309443/WorldWarIKnits.JPG"/>
                 </noscript>
                 <img alt='Popular at the time was the notion of not wasting precious time that could be spent knitting. This "bag of the hour" was both useful and "smart." Also, note its air distingue [sic]. Trs adorable!' class="thumb-image" data-image="http://static1.squarespace.com/static/53989e26e4b04c5c0979151f/t/540b5a77e4b098a72eaaacc2/1410204309443/WorldWarIKnits.JPG" data-image-dimensions="605x912" data-image-focal-point="0.5,0.5" data-image-id="540b5a77e4b098a72eaaacc2" data-load="false" data-src="http://static1.squarespace.com/static/53989e26e4b04c5c0979151f/t/540b5a77e4b098a72eaaacc2/1410204309443/WorldWarIKnits.JPG" data-type="image"/>
                </div>
                <div class="image-caption-wrapper">
                 <div class="image-caption">
                  <p>
                   Popular at the time was the notion of not wasting precious time that could be spent knitting. This "bag of the hour" was both useful and "smart." Also, note its air distingue [sic]. Trs adorable!
                  </p>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="sqs-block html-block sqs-block-html" data-block-type="2" id="block-yui_3_17_2_1_1409999919421_68068">
             <div class="sqs-block-content">
              <p>
              </p>
              <p>
               I also discovered that there is a
               <a href="https://theworldwar.org/">
                National WWI Museum
               </a>
               here in the U.S. As of this time they don't seem to have a
               <a href="http://www.nationalww2museum.org/learn/knit-your-bit/index.html">
                knitting program
               </a>
               like the National WWII Museum has, it still looks as though it has many interesting programs and exhibits. The next time you're in Kansas City, MO, check it out--and of course I'm going to want to hear all about it!
              </p>
              <p>
               Some folks in England are
               <a href="http://wagscreen.wordpress.com/tell-them-of-us/">
                filming a movie
               </a>
               about WWI, and
               <a href="http://orkneytoomaha.wordpress.com/">
                some knitters
               </a>
               have banded together to knit costumes based on old patterns and recreations from old photos. Be careful following these links if you love history and knitting--I am still wiping the drool off my keyboard.
              </p>
              <p>
               I will leave you with a Monday Medley of Patterns--all free. Holly Shaltz has a great
               <a href="http://www.hjsstudio.com/redscarf.html">
                website
               </a>
               with patterns from the era, reinterpreted for modern knitters. There's even a pattern for
               <a href="http://www.hjsstudio.com/redsocks.html">
                socks
               </a>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               You probably figured out that I'm fascinated by all this, so if you see anything else related to WWI out there that you think I might like, especially in commemoration of the 100th anniversary, let me know!
              </p>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
