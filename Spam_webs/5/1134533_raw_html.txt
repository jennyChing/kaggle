     <p>
      The
      <b>
       oxygenhemoglobin dissociation curve
      </b>
      plots the proportion of
      <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">
       hemoglobin
      </a>
      in its saturated form on the vertical axis against the prevailing
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_gas_tension" title="Blood gas tension">
       tension
      </a>
      on the horizontal axis. The oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve is an important tool for understanding how our blood carries and releases oxygen. Specifically, the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve relates oxygen saturation (sO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) and partial pressure of oxygen in the blood (pO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ), and is determined by what is called "Hemoglobin affinity for oxygen"; that is, how readily hemoglobin acquires and releases oxygen molecules into the fluid that surrounds it.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
       Background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">
       Hemoglobin
      </a>
      (Hb), a
      <a href="/wiki/Globular_protein" title="Globular protein">
       globular protein
      </a>
      , is the primary vehicle for transporting
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       oxygen
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">
       blood
      </a>
      . Oxygen is also carried dissolved in the blood's
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">
       plasma
      </a>
      , but to a much lesser degree. Hemoglobin is contained in erythrocytes, more commonly referred to as
      <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">
       red blood cells
      </a>
      . Under certain conditions, oxygen bound to the hemoglobin is released into the blood's plasma and absorbed into the
      <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">
       tissues
      </a>
      . Each hemoglobin molecule has the capacity to carry four oxygen molecules. How much of that capacity is filled by oxygen at any time is called the oxygen saturation. Expressed as a percentage, the oxygen saturation is the ratio of the amount of oxygen bound to the hemoglobin, to the oxygen-carrying capacity of the hemoglobin. The oxygen-carrying capacity of hemoglobin is determined by the type of hemoglobin present in the blood. The amount of oxygen bound to the hemoglobin at any time is related, in large part, to the partial pressure of oxygen to which the hemoglobin is exposed. In the
      <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">
       lungs
      </a>
      , at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar%E2%80%93capillary_interface" title="Alveolarcapillary interface">
       alveolarcapillary interface
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">
       partial pressure
      </a>
      of oxygen is typically high, and therefore the oxygen binds readily to hemoglobin that is present. As the blood circulates to other body tissue in which the partial pressure of oxygen is less, the hemoglobin releases the oxygen into the tissue because the hemoglobin cannot maintain its full bound capacity of oxygen in the presence of lower oxygen partial pressures.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sigmoidal_shape">
       Sigmoidal shape
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sigmoidal shape">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oxyhaemoglobin_dissociation_curve.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="439" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Oxyhaemoglobin_dissociation_curve.png/220px-Oxyhaemoglobin_dissociation_curve.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Oxyhaemoglobin_dissociation_curve.png/330px-Oxyhaemoglobin_dissociation_curve.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/Oxyhaemoglobin_dissociation_curve.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

        <div class="magnify">

     <p>
      The curve is usually best described by a
      <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_function" title="Sigmoid function">
       sigmoid
      </a>
      plot, using by a formula of the kind:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <img alt="S(t) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-t}}." class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/f/9/df9200fdfbae7a1195e1ca1ce3f5e372.png"/>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      A hemoglobin molecule can bind up to four oxygen molecules in a reversible way.
     </p>
     <p>
      The shape of the curve results from the interaction of bound oxygen molecules with incoming molecules. The binding of the first molecule is difficult. However, this facilitates the binding of the second, third and fourth, this is due to the induced conformational change in the structure of the haemoglobin molecule induced by the binding of an oxygen molecule.
     </p>
     <p>
      In its most simple form, the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve describes the relation between the partial pressure of oxygen (x axis) and the oxygen saturation (y axis). Hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen increases as successive molecules of oxygen bind. More molecules bind as the oxygen partial pressure increases until the maximum amount that can be bound is reached. As this limit is approached, very little additional binding occurs and the curve levels out as the hemoglobin becomes saturated with oxygen. Hence the curve has a sigmoidal or S-shape. At pressures above about 60mmHg, the standard dissociation curve is relatively flat, which means that the oxygen content of the blood does not change significantly even with large increases in the oxygen partial pressure. To get more oxygen to the tissue would require blood transfusions to increase the hemoglobin count (and hence the oxygen-carrying capacity), or supplemental oxygen that would increase the oxygen dissolved in plasma. Although binding of oxygen to hemoglobin continues to some extent for pressures about 50mmHg, as oxygen partial pressures decrease in this steep area of the curve, the oxygen is unloaded to peripheral tissue readily as the hemoglobin's affinity diminishes. The partial pressure of oxygen in the blood at which the hemoglobin is 50% saturated, typically about 26.6mmHg (3.5kPa) for a healthy person, is known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P50_(biochemistry)" title="P50 (biochemistry)">
       P
       <sub>
        50
       </sub>
      </a>
      . The P
      <sub>
       50
      </sub>
      is a conventional measure of hemoglobin affinity for oxygen. In the presence of disease or other conditions that change the hemoglobin's oxygen affinity and, consequently, shift the curve to the right or left, the P
      <sub>
       50
      </sub>
      changes accordingly. An increased P
      <sub>
       50
      </sub>
      indicates a rightward shift of the standard curve, which means that a larger partial pressure is necessary to maintain a 50% oxygen saturation. This indicates a decreased affinity. Conversely, a lower P
      <sub>
       50
      </sub>
      indicates a leftward shift and a higher affinity.
     </p>
     <p>
      The 'plateau' portion of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve is the range that exists at the pulmonary capillaries (minimal reduction of oxygen transported until the p(O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) falls 50mmHg).
     </p>
     <p>
      The 'steep' portion of the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve is the range that exists at the systemic capillaries (a small drop in systemic capillary p(O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) can result in the release of large amounts of oxygen for the metabolically active cells).
     </p>
     <p>
      To see the relative affinities of each successive oxygen as you remove/add oxygen from/to the hemoglobin from the curve compare the relative increase/decrease in p(O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ) needed for the corresponding increase/decrease in s(O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Factors that affect the standard dissociation curve The strength with which oxygen binds to hemoglobin is affected by several factors. These factors shift or reshape the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve. A rightward shift indicates that the hemoglobin under study has a decreased affinity for oxygen. This makes it more difficult for hemoglobin to bind to oxygen (requiring a higher partial pressure of oxygen to achieve the same oxygen saturation), but it makes it easier for the hemoglobin to release oxygen bound to it. The effect of this rightward shift of the curve increases the partial pressure of oxygen in the tissues when it is most needed, such as during exercise, or hemorrhagic shock. In contrast, the curve is shifted to the left by the opposite of these conditions. This leftward shift indicates that the hemoglobin under study has an increased affinity for oxygen so that hemoglobin binds oxygen more easily, but unloads it more reluctantly. Left shift of the curve is a sign of hemoglobin's increased affinity for oxygen (e.g. at the lungs). Similarly, right shift shows decreased affinity, as would appear with an increase in body temperature, hydrogen ion,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,3-diphosphoglycerate" title="2,3-diphosphoglycerate">
       2,3-diphosphoglycerate
      </a>
      (also known as bisphosphoglycerate) or carbon dioxide concentration
     </p>

      </sup>
      Factors that move the oxygen dissociation curve to the right are those physiological states where tissues need more oxygen. For example during exercise, muscles have a higher metabolic rate, and consequently need more oxygen, produce more carbon dioxide and lactic acid, and their temperature rises.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="pH">
       pH
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: pH">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A decrease in pH (increase in
      <span class="Unicode">
      </span>
      <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">
       H
       <sup>
        +
       </sup>
      </a>
      ion concentration) shifts the standard curve to the right, while an increase shifts it to the left. This occurs because at greater
      <span class="Unicode">
      </span>
      <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">
       H
       <sup>
        +
       </sup>
      </a>
      ion concentration, various amino acid residues, such as Histidine 164 exist predominantly in their protonated form allowing them to form ion pairs that stabilize deoxyhemoglobin in the T state.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The T state has a lower affinity for oxygen than the R state, so with increased acidity, the hemoglobin binds less O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      for a given P
      <sub>
       O2
      </sub>
      (and more H
      <sup>
       +
      </sup>
      ). This is known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Bohr_effect" title="Bohr effect">
       Bohr effect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacquez_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jacquez-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A reduction in the total binding capacity of hemoglobin to oxygen (i.e. shifting the curve down, not just to the right) due to reduced pH is called the
      <a href="/wiki/Root_effect" title="Root effect">
       root effect
      </a>
      . This is seen in bony fish.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_Dioxide">
       Carbon Dioxide
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Carbon Dioxide">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
       Carbon dioxide
      </a>
      affects the curve in two ways: first, CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      accumulation causes
      <a href="/wiki/Carbamino" title="Carbamino">
       carbamino
      </a>
      compounds to be generated through chemical interactions, which bind to hemoglobin forming
      <a href="/wiki/Carbaminohemoglobin" title="Carbaminohemoglobin">
       carbaminohemoglobin
      </a>
      and second, it influences
      <a href="/wiki/Intracellular_pH" title="Intracellular pH">
       intracellular pH
      </a>
      due to formation of bicarbonate ion. Formation of
      <a href="/wiki/Carbaminohemoglobin" title="Carbaminohemoglobin">
       carbaminohemoglobin
      </a>
      stabilizes T state hemoglobin by formation of ion pairs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Only about 510% of the total CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      content of blood is transported as carbamino compounds, whereas (8090%) is transported as bicarbonate ions and a small amount is dissolved in the plasma.The formation of a bicarbonate ion will release a proton into the plasma, increasing acidity which also shifts the curve to the right as discussed above.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="2.2C3-DPG">
       2,3-DPG
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2,3-DPG">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      2,3-Bisphosphoglycerate or 2,3-DPG (also 2,3-Diphosphoglycerate) is an organophosphate, which is created in erythrocytes during glycolysis. The production of 2,3-DPG is likely an important adaptive mechanism, because the production increases for several conditions in the presence of diminished peripheral tissue O
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      availability, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypoxaemia" title="Hypoxaemia">
       hypoxaemia
      </a>
      , chronic lung disease, anaemia, and congestive heart failure, among others. High levels of 2,3-DPG shift the curve to the right (as in childhood), while low levels of 2,3-DPG cause a leftward shift, seen in states such as
      <a href="/wiki/Septic_shock" title="Septic shock">
       septic shock
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypophosphataemia" title="Hypophosphataemia">
       hypophosphataemia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacquez_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jacquez-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the absence of 2,3 DPG, hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen increases. 2,3 DPG as a
      <a href="/wiki/Allosteric_regulation" title="Allosteric regulation">
       heteroallosteric effector
      </a>
      of hemoglobin, lowers hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen by binding preferentially to deoxyhemoglobin. An increased concentration of DPG in red blood cells favours formation of the T, low-affinity state of hemoglobin and so the oxygen-binding curve will shift to the right.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">
       Temperature
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Temperature">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Temperature does not have such a dramatic effect compared to the previous factors, but
      <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">
       hyperthermia
      </a>
      causes a rightward shift, while
      <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">
       hypothermia
      </a>
      causes a leftward shift.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Increasing temperature will weaken and denature the bond between an oxygen and a hemoglobin which in turn decreases the concentration of the oxyhemoglobin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_monoxide">
       Carbon monoxide
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carbon monoxide">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Hemoglobin binds with
      <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
       carbon monoxide
      </a>
      200250 times more readily than with oxygen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacquez_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Jacquez-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The reaction: HbO2 + CO  HbCO + O2, completely dissociates the oxygen molecules for the more favorable carbon monoxide, yielding Carboxyhemoglobin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Moreover, because of hemoglobin's affinity for carbon monoxide over oxygen, carbon monoxide is a highly successful competitor. Even at minuscule partial pressures, carbon monoxide can displace oxygen and results in a shift of the curve to the left.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marieb_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Marieb-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With an increased level of carbon monoxide, a person can suffer from severe tissue
      <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">
       hypoxia
      </a>
      while maintaining a normal pO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_methemoglobinaemia">
       Effects of methemoglobinaemia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects of methemoglobinaemia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobinemia" title="Methemoglobinemia">
       Methemoglobinaemia
      </a>
      is a form of abnormal hemoglobin where ferrous (Fe
      <sup>
       2+
      </sup>
      ), which is normally found in hemoglobin, is converted to the ferric (Fe
      <sup>
       3+
      </sup>
      ) state. This causes a leftward shift in the curve as
      <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobin" title="Methemoglobin">
       methemoglobin
      </a>
      does not unload O2 from Hb. However, methemoglobin has increased rbc affinity for
      <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">
       cyanide
      </a>
      , and is therefore useful in the treatment of
      <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_poisoning" title="Cyanide poisoning">
       cyanide poisoning
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">
       Nitrites
      </a>
      can be used to oxidize hemoglobin to methemoglobin which can then bind cyanide. This restores
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytochrome_oxidase" title="Cytochrome oxidase">
       cytochrome oxidase
      </a>
      's ability to function. Furthermore,
      <a href="/wiki/Thiosulfate" title="Thiosulfate">
       thiosulfate
      </a>
      can be given to bind cyanide, forming
      <a href="/wiki/Thiocyanate" title="Thiocyanate">
       thiocyanate
      </a>
      , which can be excreted by the kidneys.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fetal_hemoglobin">
       Fetal hemoglobin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fetal hemoglobin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="533" data-file-width="553" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg/250px-Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg/375px-Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg/500px-Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fetal_hemoglobin_chart.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Fetal hemoglobin saturation curve
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) is structurally different from normal adult hemoglobin (HbA), giving HbF a higher affinity for oxygen than HbA. HbF is composed of two alpha and two gamma chains whereas HbA is composed of two alpha and two beta chains. The fetal dissociation curve is shifted to the left relative to the curve for the normal adult because of these structural differences.
     </p>
     <p>
      Typically, fetal arterial oxygen pressures are lower than adult arterial oxygen pressures. Hence higher affinity to bind oxygen is required at lower levels of partial pressure in the fetus to allow diffusion of oxygen across the
      <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">
       placenta
      </a>
      . At the placenta, there is a higher concentration of 2,3-DPG formed, and 2,3-DPG binds readily to beta chains rather than to alpha chains. As a result, 2,3-DPG binds more strongly to adult hemoglobin, causing HbA to release more oxygen for uptake by the fetus, whose HbF is unaffected by the 2,3-DPG.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lippincotts_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Lippincotts-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      HbF then delivers that bound oxygen to tissues that have even lower partial pressures where it can be released.
     </p>
     <p>
      BINS are also an effect of the oxygen saturation(%) flexing its muscles.
     </p>
