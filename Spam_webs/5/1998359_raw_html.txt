      <p>
       You might expect to see a colour change after combining two different hues. But a demo involving a banana shows that it's not always the case.
      </p>
      <p>
       In this video, courtesy of
       <a href="http://www.eyewonderbooks.com/227/al-seckel/">
        Al Seckel
       </a>
       from Eye Wonder Books, a banana image turns green when covered with a bluish filter that matches its shape. However when a larger filter of the same colour, which encompasses the whole piece of paper, is placed over the top, no colour change is perceived.
      </p>
      <p>
       Do you know what causes the illusion? Let us know in the comments below and the first correct answer will win a
       <i>

      <p>
       It can sometimes be hard to get an image out of your mind. After staring at a coloured pattern,
       <a href="https://www.newscientist.com/blogs/nstv/2011/06/friday-illusion-colour-an-image-with-your-eyes.html">
        you may see it linger in its complementary colours when you look at a white background
       </a>
       , due to the overstimulation of photoreceptors in your eyes.
       <br/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Now
       <a href="http://psych.cf.ac.uk/contactsandpeople/pgs/powell.html" target="ns">
        Georgie Powell
       </a>
       from Cardiff University in the UK and colleagues have come up with a twist on this classic illusion - one that causes phantom images to shrink or grow. While fixing your eyes on a dot in the centre, an arrangement of coloured circles is presented (see video above). Then, when the image is replaced with an outline of the pattern, it appears to be filled in with the complementary colours of the original image even when the circles change size.
      </p>
      <p>
       The illusion shows that
       <a href="http://www.cell.com/current-biology/abstract/S0960-9822%2809%2900811-2" target="ns">
        contour edges influence ghost images
       </a>
       ,
an
       <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10191878" target="ns">
        effect that can also occur with "real" colours
       </a>
       when surrounded by edges that they don't fill in completely. 
"However, we have shown that after-image signals might be even more constrained and enhanced by edges than signals arising from real objects," says Powell.
She suspects it's because the brain somehow "knows" that the after-image is an illusion: by providing an edge it gives the colour a shape, which makes it feel more real.
      </p>
      <p>
       Powell came across the illusion while investigating different factors that can either cause or suppress an after-image, such as the brightness of edges and other aspects of a scene.
      </p>
      <p>
       The brain trick was recently presented at the
       <a href="http://ecvp2012.uniss.it/" target="ns">
        European Conference on Visual Perception
       </a>
       in Alghero, Italy.
      </p>
