              </span>
              <h1>
               New Developments in Crohn's Disease Care
              </h1>
             </header>
             <aside class="disclaimer">
             </aside>
             <h2>
              Recent advances in Crohn's disease research may improve how this digestive condition is managed and treated.
             </h2>
             <div class="bottom">
              <div class="byline">
               By
               <a href="/contributing-writers-and-editors.aspx">
                Wyatt Myers
               </a>
               ; Updated by
               <a href="/contributing-writers-and-editors.aspx">
                Beth W. Orenstein
               </a>
              </div>
              <div class="reviewedby">
               Medically reviewed by
               <a href="/medical-reviewers.aspx">
                Pat F. Bass III, MD, MPH
               </a>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="clear">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div id="video-player-top">
            </div>
            <aside class="aside">
            </aside>
            <p>
             If
youre one of the more than half a million Americans living with
             <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease-treatment-management.aspx">
              Crohns
disease
             </a>
             , you know that the condition is anything but predictable. Living with
on-again, off-again digestive symptoms related to Crohns disease can be
frustrating, and because there's been limited research into how to better
manage the condition, there are many holes left in how well it is understood.
            </p>
            <p>
             However,
current studies are providing new developments that may help increase knowledge
of the disease, with potential
             <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease/joy-bauer-lifestyle-tips-for-crohns.aspx">
              benefits for Crohns
             </a>
             disease management and
treatment. Heres a look at some of the latest advances.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              A Better Understanding of the
Triggers of Crohns Disease
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             A
breakthrough in Crohns disease research has been in identifying the most
             <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease/avoiding-common-crohns-triggers.aspx">
              potent triggers
             </a>
             that can set off symptoms of Crohns disease in people with an
underlying susceptibility. Antibiotics and smoking are two of the strongest links.
            </p>
            <p>
             A
Swedish study of more than 1,000 people with Crohns disease determined that
children who were given antibiotics for pneumonia before age 5 were more likely
to develop Crohns disease later in life. Another study, published in the
journal
             <em>
              Inflammatory Bowel Diseases
             </em>
             , looked at more than
800 people with Crohns disease and showed how detrimental both active smoking
and secondhand smoke can be, both as a risk factor for developing Crohns
disease and for aggravating its symptoms.
            </p>
            <p>
             Although
there's been some research
             <a href="/health-report/crohns-disease/joy-bauer-healthy-recipes-for-crohns.aspx">
              related to diet
             </a>
             , no specific foods have been identified
as definite triggers for symptoms of Crohns disease.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Advances in the
Treatment of Crohns Disease
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             In the
late 1990s and early 2000s, the treatment of Crohns disease changed
drastically with the approval of a number of new drug therapies. Delivered by
injection, these medications specifically target the inflammatory response in
the body. They are now the mainstay of Crohns disease treatment.
            </p>
            <p>
             New ways
of administering and combining drugs is also yielding further success. A study
in the
             <em>
              New England Journal of Medicine
             </em>
             of more than 500
people with Crohns disease found that a combination of two therapies can be
more effective at putting Crohns disease into remission than using either of
the drugs on its own. However, treatment for Crohns disease is individualized,
and the best treatment plan for you should be determined by your doctor.
            </p>
            <p>
             Perhaps
the most exciting news, says Richard Desi, MD, a gastroenterologist with the
Melissa L. Posner Institute for Digestive Health and Liver Disease
at Mercy Medical Center in Baltimore, is that new medications for treating Crohns
disease should be on the market soon.
            </p>
            <p>
             One such medication is a new biologic drug, which
are medications made using living cells, says Brian Bosworth, MD, a gastroenterologist, associate
professor of medicine, and associate attending physician at New York-Presbyterian
Hospital/Weill Cornell Medical Center in New York City. Results of a trial of the biologic drug vedolizumab, published in the
             <em>
              New
England Journal of Medicine
             </em>
             in August 2013, showed encouraging
results for people with moderate to severe Crohns disease whose previous
treatments had failed.
            </p>
            <p>
             Vedolizumab
blocks the antibodies that attack the digestive tract and cause damage in the
small intestine and colon. This new drug could be a huge benefit to treating
Crohns disease, Dr. Bosworth says, because its specifically targeted at the
digestive tract.
            </p>
            <p>
             Because
biologics suppress the immune system, people treated with these drugs face an
increased risk for infections and other diseases. But theres a promising sign
that researchers may be close to developing a safe and effective treatment for
hard-to-treat cases of Crohns disease: None of the participants in the vedolizumab
trial developed progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML), a rare but
serious brain infection associated with this class of medication, Bosworth
says. PML is a concern with the immunosuppressant drug natalizumab (Tysabri), an
earlier drug approved for people with Crohns disease thats moderate to severe
and isnt responding to other treatments.
            </p>
            <p>
             In terms
of complementary and alternative medicine approaches to treating Crohns
disease, a study published in mid-2013 in the journal
             <em>
              Alimentary
Pharmacology &amp; Therapeutics
             </em>
             looked at herbal therapies for
inflammatory bowel diseases like Crohns disease and showed some promising
results, Dr. Desi says. The researchers analyzed 21 randomized controlled
trials that included nearly 1,500 participants. They found that people with
Crohns disease who took certain herbs went into remission and stayed there
longer than those who didnt take them. However, the researchers noted that larger
studies are still needed before this can become a standard part of Crohns
treatment.
            </p>
