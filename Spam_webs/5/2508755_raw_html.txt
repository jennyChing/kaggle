            The Safest Convertible Car Seats: Ultimate Guide to Finding the Best Car Seat for Your Baby with Reviews, Safety Ratings and Expert Tips

           <p>
            So heres the thing. In the parenting world, car seat discussions can get
            <strong>
             heated
            </strong>
            . And by heated, we mean hot, hot, hot  to the point of boiling over.
           </p>
           <p>
            But heres the other thing: as parents, we all agree that car seats
            <em>
             should
            </em>
            be a hot topic because theyre one of the most important aspects to keeping our kids safe. The average family spends a lot of time in the car: grocery runs and after-school sports and weekend jaunts and, well, all those interminable minutes and hours you spend going to and fro, hither and thither.
           </p>
           <p>
            Since youre reading this guide, we already know you want to keep your kiddos safe in the car. Well skip the high-horse speeches  you probably hear enough of that in playgroup, anyway  and jump right to the cold, hard facts. Here are three that stand out:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             Road-related injuries are the
             <strong>
              leading cause of preventable injury and death to children
             </strong>
             in the U.S.
            </li>
            <li>
             The good news: Car seats, correctly installed, can
             <strong>
              reduce the risk of death by an incredible 71%.
             </strong>
            </li>
            <li>
             The bad news: An estimated
             <strong>
              73% of car seats are not installed correctly
             </strong>
             .
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            Those are the statistics. Here are a few steps you can take on the way to keeping your children safe:
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             All car seats sold in the U.S. are required to meet or exceed
             <a href="http://www.nhtsa.gov/cars/rules/rulings/ChildRestrSyst/part1-4.html" target="_blank">
              Federal
             </a>
             and
             <a href="http://www.astm.org/SNEWS/MARCH_2003/waller_mar03.html" target="_blank">
              ASTM Safety Standards
             </a>
             .
            </li>
            <li>
             Car seats should also be
             <a href="http://www.nhtsa.gov/Research/NCAP+Child+Safety+Seat+Research" target="_blank">
              NCAP crash tested
             </a>
             , the industry benchmark for evaluating seat performance in the event of a severe accident.
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="http://www.safercar.gov/cpsApp/crs/listing.htm" target="_blank">
              Parent Central
             </a>
             at SaferCar.gov provides excellent reviews on all U.S. car seats. The site also rates each car seat based on its ease of use  an important consideration, given the above statistic that 73% of car seats are used incorrectly.
            </li>
            <li>
             For all-in-one seats and convertibles that transition to boosters, you can also
             <a href="http://www.iihs.org/iihs/ratings/child-boosters" target="_blank">
              check booster seat ratings
             </a>
             at the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety (IIHS).
            </li>
            <li>
             Third-party review sites, like
             <a href="http://consumerreports.org/cro/car-seats.htm" target="_blank">
              Consumer Reports
             </a>
             , also offer unbiased comfort and safety reviews.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            All that said, well add one more layer to the equation  four additional considerations before purchasing a car seat:
           </p>
           <ol>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Extended Rear-Facing
             </strong>
             : You may have heard a lot about rear-facing versus front-facing car seats. Lets cut to the chase: without a doubt,
             <strong>
              <a href="http://www.car-safety.org/rearface.html" target="_blank">
               rear-facing is the safest
              </a>
             </strong>
             way for your children to ride. Consider purchasing a seat that rear-faces to 40+ pounds. Remember, just because your child hits the minimum required to front-face (1+ year and 20+ pounds), doesnt mean she should. Rear-facing saves lives.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Extended Front-Facing
             </strong>
             : As with the transition from rear- to front-facing, your child will likely hit the minimum height and weight required to transition to a booster before hes actually ready, physically and developmentally. Choose a seat with a high front-facing weight limit (up to 70 pounds) and only transition to a booster when your child outgrows her car seat.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Check the expiration date
             </strong>
             : Some car seats have a 5-year life, which means theyll likely expire before your child has the chance to outgrow them. If youre buying an extended-use or all-in-one car seat, choose one with an 8-, 10-, or 12-year lifespan.
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Never buy used
             </strong>
             : Its tempting to spring for a deal on a used seat, but you should know that age, wear, and accidents (even just a teeny, tiny fender bender) can compromise a car seats safety features. Unless you are 110% sure of a seats history  never been in an accident, never left in a spot where the sun shines brightly, etc.  dont risk your childs safety for a discount.
            </li>
           </ol>
