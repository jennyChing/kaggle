          <span class="arttle">
           <h1>
            Dengue mosquitoes now immune to repellants
           </h1>
          </span>
         </div>
         <span class="byline" style="padding:10px 0px;display:inline-block;">

          </span>

         <div id="bxml">
          <div class="section1">
           <div class="Normal">
            <p>
             LONDON: Dengue-transmitting mosquitoes have become immune to the world's most commonly used insect repellent. British scientists have found that mosquitoes are able to ignore the smell of the insect repellent  DEET for up to three hours after being exposed to it.
            </p>
            <p>
             Although most insects are strongly repelled by the smell of DEET, studies led by Dr James Logan from the  London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine have shown that some flies and mosquitoes carry a genetic change in their odour receptors that makes them insensitive to the smell. DEET was developed by the US military during jungle warfare in World War II.
            </p>
            <p>
             The scientists tested changes in responses to DEET in Aedes aegypti mosquitoes, which are notorious for biting during the day and are capable of transmitting
             <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Dengue-Fever">
              dengue fever
             </a>
             and
             <a href="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/topic/Yellow-Fever">
              yellow fever
             </a>
             viruses.
            </p>
            <div id="gads">
             <iframe align="center" allowtransparency="true" frameborder="0" height="190" hspace="0" id="adshowbtm" marginheight="0" marginwidth="0" name="adshowbtm" scrolling="no" src="http://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/configspace/ads/PM_article_middle.html" title="Advertisement" vspace="0" width="660">
             </iframe>
            </div>
            <p>
             They found that a brief exposure to DEET was sufficient to make some mosquitoes less sensitive to the repellent.
            </p>
            <p>
             Three hours after the exposure, these mosquitoes were not deterred from seeking attractants like heat and human skin despite the presence of DEET.
            </p>
