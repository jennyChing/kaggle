   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Noise barrier
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <p>
      A
      <b>
       noise barrier
      </b>
      (also called a
      <b>
       soundwall
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       sound berm
      </b>
      ,
      <b>
       sound barrier
      </b>
      , or
      <b>
       acoustical barrier
      </b>
      ) is an exterior structure designed to protect inhabitants of sensitive
      <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">
       land use
      </a>
      areas from
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">
       noise pollution
      </a>
      . Noise barriers are the most effective method of mitigating
      <a href="/wiki/Roadway_noise" title="Roadway noise">
       roadway
      </a>
      , railway, and industrial noise sources  other than cessation of the source activity or use of source controls.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the case of surface transportation noise,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noise_mitigation#Roadways" title="Noise mitigation">
       other methods
      </a>
      of reducing the source noise intensity include encouraging the use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicles" title="Hybrid vehicles">
       hybrid
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">
       electric vehicles
      </a>
      , improving
      <a href="/wiki/Automotive_aerodynamics" title="Automotive aerodynamics">
       automobile aerodynamics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">
       tire
      </a>
      design, and choosing low-noise
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pavement_(material)" title="Pavement (material)">
       paving material
      </a>
      . Extensive use of noise barriers began in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      after
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_regulation" title="Noise regulation">
       noise regulations
      </a>
      were introduced in the early 1970s.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noise_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Noise barriers have been built in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      since the mid-twentieth century, when vehicular traffic burgeoned. In the late 1960s, analytic
      <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">
       acoustical
      </a>
      technology emerged to mathematically evaluate the efficacy of a noise barrier design adjacent to a specific
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roadway" title="Roadway">
       roadway
      </a>
      . By the 1990s, noise barriers that included use of transparent materials were being designed in
      <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
       Denmark
      </a>
      and other western European countries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Below, a researcher collects data to calibrate a
      <a href="/wiki/Roadway_noise" title="Roadway noise">
       roadway noise
      </a>
      model for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_Route_G5_(California)" title="County Route G5 (California)">
       Foothill Expressway
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noisebarrierm.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="932" data-file-width="1856" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Noisebarrierm.jpg/290px-Noisebarrierm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Noisebarrierm.jpg/435px-Noisebarrierm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Noisebarrierm.jpg/580px-Noisebarrierm.jpg 2x" width="290"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noisebarrierm.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Acoustical
        <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">
         scientist
        </a>
        measures sound in noise barrier design study,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_County" title="Santa Clara County">
         Santa Clara County
        </a>
        , California.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The best of these early computer models considered the effects of roadway
      <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">
       geometry
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">
       topography
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">
       vehicle
      </a>
      volumes, vehicle speeds, truck mix,
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roadway_surface&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roadway surface (page does not exist)">
       roadway surface
      </a>
      type, and micro-
      <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">
       meteorology
      </a>
      . Several U.S. research groups developed variations of the computer modeling techniques:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltrans" title="Caltrans">
       Caltrans
      </a>
      Headquarters in
      <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">
       Sacramento, California
      </a>
      ; the ESL Inc. group in
      <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California" title="Palo Alto, California">
       Palo Alto, California
      </a>
      ; the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolt,_Beranek_and_Newman" title="Bolt, Beranek and Newman">
       Bolt, Beranek and Newman
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      group in
      <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">
       Cambridge, Massachusetts
      </a>
      , and a research team at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">
       University of Florida
      </a>
      . Possibly the earliest published work that scientifically designed a specific noise barrier was the study for the Foothill Expressway in
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Altos,_California" title="Los Altos, California">
       Los Altos, California
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Numerous case studies across the U.S. soon addressed dozens of different existing and planned highways. Most were commissioned by state highway departments and conducted by one of the four research groups mentioned above. The U.S.
      <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">
       National Environmental Policy Act
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      effectively mandated the quantitative analysis of
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">
       noise pollution
      </a>
      from every
      <a href="/wiki/Federal-Aid_Highway_Act" title="Federal-Aid Highway Act">
       Federal-Aid Highway Act
      </a>
      Project in the country, propelling noise barrier model development and application. With passage of the
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_Control_Act" title="Noise Control Act">
       Noise Control Act of 1972
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      demand for noise barrier design soared from a host of
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_regulation" title="Noise regulation">
       noise regulation
      </a>
      spinoff.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the late 1970s, more than a dozen research groups in the U.S. were applying similar
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_model" title="Computer model">
       computer modeling
      </a>
      technology and addressing at least 200 different locations for noise barriers each year. As of 2006, this technology is considered a standard in the evaluation of
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">
       noise pollution
      </a>
      from highways. The nature and accuracy of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_model" title="Computer model">
       computer models
      </a>
      used is nearly identical to the original 1970s versions of the technology.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_of_design">
       Theory of design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noise_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theory of design">

       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">

     <p>
      The acoustical science of noise barrier design is based upon treating an airway or railway as a
      <a href="/wiki/Line_source" title="Line source">
       line source
      </a>
      . The theory is based upon blockage of sound ray travel toward a particular
      <a href="/wiki/Sensory_receptor" title="Sensory receptor">
       receptor
      </a>
      ; however,
      <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
       diffraction
      </a>
      of sound must be addressed.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_waves" title="Sound waves">
       Sound waves
      </a>
      bend (downward) when they pass an edge, such as the apex of a noise barrier. Further complicating matters is the phenomenon of
      <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
       refraction
      </a>
      , the bending of sound rays in the presence of an
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/heterogeneous" title="wiktionary:heterogeneous">
       inhomogeneous
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">
       Wind shear
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">
       thermocline
      </a>
      produce such inhomogeneities.
     </p>
     <p>
      The sound sources modeled must include
      <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">
       engine
      </a>
      noise,
      <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">
       tire
      </a>
      noise, and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic">
       aerodynamic
      </a>
      noise, all of which vary by vehicle type and speed. The resulting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_model" title="Computer model">
       computer model
      </a>
      is based upon dozens of physics
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equations" title="Equations">
       equations
      </a>
      translated into thousands of lines of computer code. Software applications are available which are able to model these situations and assist in the design of such noise barriers.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noisercrazorback.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="888" data-file-width="1840" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Noisercrazorback.jpg/310px-Noisercrazorback.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Noisercrazorback.jpg/465px-Noisercrazorback.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Noisercrazorback.jpg/620px-Noisercrazorback.jpg 2x" width="310"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noisercrazorback.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Noise barrier earth berm along
        <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_12" title="California State Route 12">
         California State Route 12
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Sonoma_County,_California" title="Sonoma County, California">
         Sonoma County
        </a>
        , California
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Some noise barriers consist of a masonry wall or earthwork, or a combination thereof (such as a wall atop an earth
      <a href="/wiki/Berm" title="Berm">
       berm
      </a>
      ). Sound abatement walls are commonly constructed using steel, concrete, masonry, wood, plastics, insulating wool, or composites. In the most extreme cases, the entire roadway is surrounded by a noise abatement structure, or dug into a tunnel using the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cut-and-cover" title="Cut-and-cover">
       cut-and-cover
      </a>
      method. The noise barrier may be constructed on private land, on a public
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-of-way_(railroad)" title="Right-of-way (railroad)">
       right-of-way
      </a>
      , or on other public land. Because sound levels are measured using a
      <a href="/wiki/Logarithmic_scale" title="Logarithmic scale">
       logarithmic scale
      </a>
      , a reduction of nine
      <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">
       decibels
      </a>
      is equivalent to elimination of approximately 80 percent of the unwanted sound.
     </p>
     <p>
      Noise barrier walls made of absorptive material mitigate sound differently than those that are a hard surface.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A sound reducing barrier wall with a porous surface material and sound-dampening content material is said to be absorptive. This means little or no noise is reflected back towards the source or elsewhere. Hard surfaces such as masonry or concrete are considered to be reflective.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This means most of the noise is reflected back towards the noise source and beyond.
     </p>
     <p>
      Noise barriers can be extremely effective tools for
      <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">
       noise pollution
      </a>
      abatement, but theory calculates that certain locations and topographies are not suitable for use of any reasonable noise barrier. Cost and
      <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">
       aesthetics
      </a>
      play a role in the final choice of any noise barrier.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tradeoffs">

      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">

       </div>

     <p>
      Disadvantages of noise barriers include:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Aesthetic impacts for motorists and neighbors, particularly if scenic vistas are blocked
      </li>
      <li>
       Costs of design, construction, and maintenance
      </li>
      <li>
       Necessity to design custom drainage that the barrier may interrupt
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Normally, the benefits of noise reduction far outweigh aesthetic impacts for residents protected from unwanted sound. These benefits include lessened
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sleep_disturbance" title="Sleep disturbance">
       sleep disturbance
      </a>
      , improved ability to enjoy outdoor life, reduced
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Speech_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Speech interference (page does not exist)">
       speech interference
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)">
       stress
      </a>
      reduction, reduced risk of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_impairment" title="Hearing impairment">
       hearing impairment
      </a>
      , and a reduction in the elevated
      <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">
       blood pressure
      </a>
      created by noise (that improves
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiovascular" title="Cardiovascular">
       cardiovascular
      </a>
      health).
     </p>
     <p>
      Sound barrier walls vary in cost depending on the type and quality. Concrete is popular due to lower cost. Since they are reflective, they could potentially create noise for those across from the barrier. Absorptive barriers absorb, and thus abate the noise more effectively, but cost more as they are often custom built and are composed of a variety of materials that allow for
      <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(acoustics)" title="Absorption (acoustics)">
       absorption
      </a>
      . With regard to
      <a href="/wiki/Berm" title="Berm">
       bermconstruction
      </a>
      costs, a major factor is the availability of excess soil in the immediate area which could be used for
      <a href="/wiki/Berm" title="Berm">
       berm
      </a>
      construction. If the soil is present, it is often cheaper to construct an earth berm noise barrier than to haul away the excess dirt, provided there is sufficient land area available for berm construction. Generally a four-to-one ratio of berm cross sectional width to height is required. Thus, for example, to build a 6-foot-high (1.8m) berm, one needs an available width of 24 feet (7.3m).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_downwind_pollution_concentrations">
       Effect on downwind pollution concentrations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noise_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effect on downwind pollution concentrations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Roadside noise barrier have shown to reduce the near road air pollution
      <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">
       concentration
      </a>
      levels. Within 1550 m from the roadside, air pollution concentration levels at the
      <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">
       lee side
      </a>
      of the noise barriers can be to reduce up to about 50% compared to open road values
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Noise barriers force the pollution plumes coming from the road to move up and over the barrier creating the effect of an elevated source and enhancing vertical
      <a href="/wiki/Dispersive_mass_transfer" title="Dispersive mass transfer">
       dispersion
      </a>
      of the plume. The deceleration and the deflection of the initial flow by the noise barrier, force the plume to disperse horizontally. A highly
      <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
       turbulent
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">
       shear
      </a>
      zone characterized by slow velocities and a re-circulation cavity is created in the lee side of the barrier and further enhances the dispersion; all of which result in a well-mixed zone with lower pollutant concentrations downwind behind the barrier.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
