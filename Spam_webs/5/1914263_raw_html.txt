        <div class="post-title left">
         Are You Qualifying Your Leads? Heres How to Do It.
        </div>
        <!--/post-title-->
        <div class="post-date left">
         <span>
          November 13, 2014
          <br/>
          Categories:
          <a href="http://wwex.com/blog/category/career-advice" rel="category tag">
           Career Advice
          </a>
         </span>
         <span>
          0 COMMENTS
         </span>
         <span>
         </span>
        </div>
        <!--/post-date-->
        <div class="post-text left">
         <div class="shortcodes left">
          <p>
           Its natural for a
           <a href="http://wwex.com/opportunities">
            salesperson
           </a>
           to have the urge to pounce on a potentially interested client. But in the initial stages of discussion, the question of qualification comes into play  as in just because someone is interested in buying doesnt mean that they can, or should, or have the authority to do so.
          </p>
          <p>
           <a href="http://www.coachingyou.org/qualifying-business-ensure-sales-effectiveness-a-54.html">
            A story on coachingyou.org
           </a>
           puts this concept in an easy-to-understand way (especially for those with a taste for cars): Most men would love to have a Ferrari.We would probably even take time out of our busy schedules to go test drive one just to see what it was like. In the same way, this story argues, salespeople are interested in prospects, but how many are taking the time they should to qualify these opportunities?The answer: Most arent. These salespeople dont know if this lead has the authority to purchase, the ability to purchase or even the need to purchase.
          </p>
          <p>
           Heres a look at five elements of the qualifying process.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Guide and pace the meeting:
           </strong>
           Qualification begins with the initial sales discussion. It is often less about an instant showcase for your product or service than it is about engaging with the potential client and establishing a connection. Tim Donnelly explores this in
           <a href="http://www.inc.com/guides/201108/how-to-qualify-a-sales-lead.html">
            a story for inc.com
           </a>
           , and he quotes Paul Cherry, founder and managing partner of Performance Based Results. Its really being able to tap into somebodys ego, their emotions, frustrations and concerns, Cherry says. I think most customers and prospects do want to talk if you ask them the right questions. When starting to talk about features and benefits, the prospect just tunes out.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Identify the decision makers:
           </strong>
           When qualifying a prospect, its important to know youre speaking with the right person  someone in an authority position who can make the transaction happen. Otherwise you may be barking up the wrong tree. As
           <a href="http://davidmasover.com/blog/2012/04/qualification-the-third-step-of-the-sales-process/">
            sales consultant David Masover writes on his website
           </a>
           , You may have to dig to find out what financial or technical or administrative people and processes need to occur within the prospects company before the decision to buy your product or service is made. But getting this information now will save you from the too-often-heard I need to talk to someone else before making the decision. When that comes after a long needs analysis and proposal, you spent a lot of time talking to only part of the decision-making team, and facing an objection that is almost impossible to overcome.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Determine if a sale is possible:
           </strong>
           Once youre talking with the right person, youll need to frame your questions in a way to figure out if a sale is realistic. In using the aforementioned Ferrari example, just because someone has the authority to make such a purchase doesnt mean they have the ability to actually do it. To determine ability to purchase, you want to understand how the purchase will be funded and approved,
           <a href="http://www.coachingyou.org/qualifying-business-ensure-sales-effectiveness-a-54.html">
            the coachingyou.org story says
           </a>
           . Not only identify if there is the money available, but you will want to identify what other projects are going to compete for those funds and what process will the prospect have to go through to get funds approved and allocated.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Determine the need:
           </strong>
           In the process of exploring the prospects situation, it may become clear that there is not a major need to buy from you. But that doesnt mean you should close the door, as sales consultant
           <a href="http://sales.about.com/od/leadgeneration/a/5-Steps-To-Identify-Qualified-Leads.htm">
            Wendy Connick writes for about.com
           </a>
           . In that case, dont try to fast-talk or pressure them into buying from you, she writes. Its much better to confess, Mr. Prospect, I think that your current setup is just fine for you right now. The prospect will appreciate your honesty and youll have a great chance of making the sale later on, when their situation changes (e.g. their product breaks down or their current provider hikes the fees way up).
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Be prepared to be qualified as well:
           </strong>
           As you offer targeted questions, you may get as much as you give. Prospects may fire back at you in an attempt to qualify you as a seller. Paul McCord writes about this in
           <a href="http://www.eyesonsales.com/content/article/maybe_theyre_not_objections/">
            a piece for eyesonsales.com
           </a>
           , saying, That means were neither dealing with an enemy to be overcome nor are we dealing with someone asking us to close them.Instead were dealing with a human being who wants to know whether or not were trustworthy, whether or not our offering is appropriate for them, whether or not were wasting their time.  If we recognize that the sales process involves both parties qualifying one another and that the qualifying process involves the investigation and questioning of each party, we can relax and begin to address the prospects questions for what they really are  a legitimate desire to find out who we are and whether or not we are someone they want to work with.
          </p>
