   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Ideomotor phenomenon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from

      )
     </span>
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Ideomotor phenomenon
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological" title="Psychological">
       psychological
      </a>
      phenomenon wherein a subject makes motions
      <a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">
       unconsciously
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2438" data-file-width="3050" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg/220px-Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg/330px-Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg/440px-Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tables_tournantes_1853.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An example of
        <a href="/wiki/Table-turning" title="Table-turning">
         table-turning
        </a>
        in 19th century France. A circle of participants press their hands against a table, and the ideomotor effect causes the table to tilt in such a way as to produce a written message, in a manner similar to a ouija board.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       ideomotor response
      </b>
      (or "ideomotor reflex"), often abbreviated to IMR, is a concept in
      <a href="/wiki/Hypnosis" title="Hypnosis">
       hypnosis
      </a>
      and psychological research.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is derived from the terms "
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ideo" title="wikt:ideo">
       ideo
      </a>
      " (idea, or
      <a href="/wiki/Mental_representation" title="Mental representation">
       mental representation
      </a>
      ) and "
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/motor" title="wikt:motor">
       motor
      </a>
      " (muscular action). The phrase is most commonly used in reference to the process whereby a thought or mental image brings about a seemingly "reflexive" or automatic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscular" title="Muscular">
       muscular
      </a>
      reaction, often of minuscule degree, and potentially outside of the awareness of the subject. As in reflexive responses to pain, the body sometimes reacts reflexively with an
      <b>
       ideomotor effect
      </b>
      to ideas alone without the person consciously deciding to take action. The effects of
      <a href="/wiki/Automatic_writing" title="Automatic writing">
       automatic writing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Dowsing" title="Dowsing">
       dowsing
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Facilitated_communication" title="Facilitated communication">
       facilitated communication
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Ouija" title="Ouija">
       Ouija boards
      </a>
      have been attributed to the phenomenon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mystics have often attributed these effects to
      <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">
       paranormal
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">
       supernatural
      </a>
      force. Many subjects are unconvinced that their actions are originating solely from within themselves.
     </p>
     <p>
      The associated term "ideo-
      <i>
       dynamic
      </i>
      response" (or "reflex") applies to a wider domain, and extends to the description of all bodily reactions (including, but not limited to ideo-motor and ideo-sensory responses) caused in a similar manner by certain ideas, e.g., the salivation often caused by imagining sucking a lemon, which is a secretory response. The notion of an ideo-dynamic response contributed to James Braid's first neuro-psychological explanation of the principle through which
      <a href="/wiki/Suggestion" title="Suggestion">
       suggestion
      </a>
      operated in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypnotism" title="Hypnotism">
       hypnotism
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_scientific_investigation">
       History of scientific investigation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideomotor_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of scientific investigation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      With the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Spiritualism" title="Spiritualism">
       Spiritualism
      </a>
      in 1840s,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediums" title="Mediums">
       mediums
      </a>
      devised and refined a variety of techniques for communicating with the spirit world including
      <a href="/wiki/Table-turning" title="Table-turning">
       table-turning
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Planchette" title="Planchette">
       planchette
      </a>
      writing boards (the precursor to later
      <a href="/wiki/Ouija" title="Ouija">
       Ouija
      </a>
      boards). These phenomena and devices quickly became the subject of scientific investigation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term Ideomotor was first used in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_paper" title="Scientific paper">
       scientific paper
      </a>
      discussing the means through which these spiritualistic phenomena produced effect, by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Benjamin_Carpenter" title="William Benjamin Carpenter">
       William Benjamin Carpenter
      </a>
      in 1852,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      hence the alternative term
      <b>
       Carpenter effect
      </b>
      . (Carpenter derived the word
      <i>
       ideomotor
      </i>
      from the components
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ideo" title="wikt:ideo">
        ideo
       </a>
      </i>
      , meaning "idea" or "mental representation", and
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/motor" title="wikt:motor">
        motor
       </a>
      </i>
      , meaning "muscular action"). The terms "ideomotor effect" and "ideomotor response" were both introduced by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Benjamin_Carpenter" title="William Benjamin Carpenter">
       William Benjamin Carpenter
      </a>
      . In the paper, Carpenter explained his theory that
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscular" title="Muscular">
       muscular
      </a>
      movement can be independent of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conscious" title="Conscious">
       conscious
      </a>
      desires or emotions.
     </p>
     <p>
      Carpenter was a friend and collaborator of
      <a href="/wiki/James_Braid_(surgeon)" title="James Braid (surgeon)">
       James Braid
      </a>
      , the founder of modern hypnotism. Braid soon adopted Carpenter's ideo-motor terminology, to facilitate the transmission of his most fundamental views, based upon those of his teacher, the philosopher
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Brown_(philosopher)" title="Thomas Brown (philosopher)">
       Thomas Brown
      </a>
      , that the efficacy of hypnotic suggestion was contingent upon the subject's concentration upon a single (thus, "dominant") idea. In 1855, Braid explained his decision to abandon his earlier term "mono-ideo-motor", based on Carpenter's (1852) "ideo-motor principle", and adopt the more appropriate and more descriptive term "mono-ideo-dynamic". His decision was based upon suggestions made to Carpenter (in 1854), by their friend in common, Daniel Noble, that the activity that Carpenter was describing would be more accurately understood in its wider applications (viz., wider than pendulums and ouija boards) if it were to denominated the "ideo-dynamic principle":
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote class="toccolours" style="float:none; display:table;">
      <div style="padding: 10px 15px 10px 15px;">
       In order that I may do full justice to two esteemed friends, I beg to state, in connection with this term
       <i>
        <b>
         monoideo-dynamics
        </b>
       </i>
       , that, several years ago, Dr.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Benjamin_Carpenter" title="William Benjamin Carpenter">
        W. B. Carpenter
       </a>
       introduced the term
       <i>
        <b>
         ideo-motor
        </b>
       </i>
       to characterise the reflex or automatic muscular motions which arise merely from ideas associated with motion existing in the mind, without any conscious effort of volition. In 1853, in referring to this term,
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Noble_(physician)" title="Daniel Noble (physician)">
        Dr. Noble
       </a>
       said, 
       <i>
        <b>
         Ideo-dynamic
        </b>
       </i>
       would probably constitute a phraseology more appropriate, as applicable to a wider range of phenomena. In this opinion I quite concurred, because I was well aware that an idea could
       <i>
        <b>
         arrest
        </b>
       </i>
       as well as
       <i>
        <b>
         excite
        </b>
       </i>
       motion automatically, not only in the muscles of voluntary motion, but also as regards the condition of
       <i>
        <b>
         every other function of the body
        </b>
       </i>
       . I have, therefore, adopted the term
       <i>
        <b>
         monoideo-dynamics
        </b>
       </i>
       , as still more comprehensive and characteristic as regards the true mental relations which subsist during all dynamic changes which take place, in every other function of the body, as well as in the muscles of voluntary motion."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         7
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Scientific tests by the English scientist
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">
       Michael Faraday
      </a>
      , Manchester surgeon
      <a href="/wiki/James_Braid_(surgeon)" title="James Braid (surgeon)">
       James Braid
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the French chemist
      <a href="/wiki/Michel_Eug%C3%A8ne_Chevreul" title="Michel Eugne Chevreul">
       Michel Eugne Chevreul
      </a>
      , and the American psychologists
      <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">
       William James
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ray_Hyman" title="Ray Hyman">
       Ray Hyman
      </a>
      have demonstrated that many phenomena attributed to spiritual or paranormal forces, or to mysterious "energies," are actually due to ideomotor action. Furthermore, these tests demonstrate that "honest, intelligent people can unconsciously engage in muscular activity that is consistent with their expectations".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hyman_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-hyman-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also show that suggestions that can guide behavior can be given by subtle clues (Hyman 1977).
     </p>
     <p>
      Some operators use ideomotor responses to communicate with a subject's "
      <a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">
       unconscious mind
      </a>
      " using a system of physical signals (such as finger movements) for the unconscious mind to indicate "yes", "no","I don't know", or "I'm not ready to know that consciously".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A simple experiment to demonstrate the ideomotor effect is to allow a hand-held pendulum to hover over a sheet of paper. The paper has keywords such as YES, NO and MAYBE printed on it. Small movements in the hand, in response to questions, can cause the pendulum to move towards key words on the paper. This technique has been used for experiments in
      <a href="/wiki/Extrasensory_perception" title="Extrasensory perception">
       extrasensory perception
      </a>
      , lie detection, and ouija boards. The validity of these experiments has not been proven. This type of experiment was used by
      <a href="/wiki/Kreskin" title="Kreskin">
       Kreskin
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and has also been used by illusionists such as
      <a href="/wiki/Derren_Brown" title="Derren Brown">
       Derren Brown
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">
       Uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideomotor_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Responding_to_questions">
       Responding to questions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideomotor_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Responding to questions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It is strongly associated with the practice of
      <a href="/wiki/Hypnotherapy" title="Hypnotherapy">
       analytical hypnotherapy
      </a>
      based on "uncovering techniques" such as
      <a href="/wiki/John_G._Watkins" title="John G. Watkins">
       Watkins'
      </a>
      "Affect Bridge",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">

      whereby a subject's "yes", "no", "I don't know", or "I don't want to answer" responses to an operator's questions are indicated by physical movements rather than verbal signals; and are produced
      <i>

      </i>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="An_example">
       An example
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideomotor_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: An example">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      If you were to be asked to
      <i>
       imagine
      </i>
      doing up your shoelaces as vividly as possible, your brain would consciously fixate on the task and work through it as vividly and as logically as possible. The theory of IMR would imply that your muscular memory associated with your hands would then attempt the task physically. However, it would abort the process unless it was truly necessary, and curtail the events that would unfold if you were
      <i>
       actually
      </i>
      willing to send the complete information along your nervous system to your hands
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      . This may therefore involve an involuntary "twitch" or movement, as the associated digits are placed into preparation of function, processed against reality, and then given the signal not to actually act
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In hypnosis, this may be circumvented by dissociating the particular
      <i>
       thought-process-response-abort
      </i>
      of a digit or entire limb, and therefore give control to the
      <a href="/wiki/Unconscious_mind" title="Unconscious mind">
       unconscious mind
      </a>
      to enable (by suggestion) the route of
      <i>
       conscious thought-unconscious process-conscious process (of the fact)-conscious response (do not act)- unconscious response (dissociate from conscious)- ignore abortive conscious attempt- unconscious unabort
      </i>
      , which would then display a reaction or response in a physical manner or behavioral context
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Body language may be considered the most commonly visible aspect of IMR, but may also include such unconscious activities as doodling or art as the conscious thought is sublimated into a different type of activity in unconscious expression
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      .
     </p>
