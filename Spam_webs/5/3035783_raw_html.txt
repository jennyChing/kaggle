   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Ted Strehlow
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      <b>
       Theodor George Henry Strehlow
      </b>
      (6 June 19083 October 1978) was an
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropologist
      </a>
      who studied the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrernte_people" title="Arrernte people">
       Arrernte
      </a>
      (Aranda, Arunta)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigine" title="Australian Aborigine">
       Australian Aborigines
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Australia" title="Central Australia">
       Central Australia
      </a>
      . He was considered a member of the Arrernte people, by dint of his ritual adoption by the tribe. He married twice, to Bertha James, in
      <a href="/wiki/Prospect,_South_Australia" title="Prospect, South Australia">
       Prospect, South Australia
      </a>
      , on 21 December 1935, with whom he had three children, Theo, Shirley and John, and to Kathleen Stuart in 1972, with whom he had a son, Carl.
     </p>

     </p>

     <p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Life">
       Life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ted_Strehlow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Strehlow's father was
      <a href="/wiki/Carl_Strehlow" title="Carl Strehlow">
       Carl Strehlow
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">
       Lutheran
      </a>
      pastor and
      <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_(ecclesiastical)" title="Superintendent (ecclesiastical)">
       Superintendent
      </a>
      , since 1896, of the
      <a href="/wiki/Hermannsburg,_Northern_Territory" title="Hermannsburg, Northern Territory">
       Hermannsburg Mission
      </a>
      , southwest of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_Springs,_Northern_Territory" title="Alice Springs, Northern Territory">
       Alice Springs
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Finke_River" title="Finke River">
       Finke River
      </a>
      . Strehlow was born, a month premature, at
      <a href="/wiki/Hermannsburg,_Northern_Territory" title="Hermannsburg, Northern Territory">
       Hermannsburg
      </a>
      , the native place name being
      <a href="/wiki/Ntaria_Ladies_Choir" title="Ntaria Ladies Choir">
       Ntaria
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was raised
      <a href="/wiki/Multilingualism" title="Multilingualism">
       trilingually
      </a>
      , speaking, in addition to English, also
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrernte_language" title="Arrernte language">
       Arrernte
      </a>
      with the
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">
       aboriginal
      </a>
      maids and native children, and
      <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
       German
      </a>
      with his immediate family.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      After a family visit to
      <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
       Germany
      </a>
      when he was three years old (1911), he returned with his parents, and grew up parted from his four elder brothers and a sister,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      who were raised in Germany. He studied both
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      as part of his
      <a href="/wiki/Homeschooling" title="Homeschooling">
       home school
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">
       curriculum
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      When Strehlow was 14 years of age his domineering and charismatic father contracted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dropsy" title="Dropsy">
       dropsy
      </a>
      and the story of the transport of his dying father to a station where medical help was available was recalled in Strehlow's novel
      <i>
       Journey to Horseshoe Bend
      </i>
      . The tragic death of his father marked Strehlow for life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He left Hermannsburg for
      <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">
       secondary schooling
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_College_(Australia)" title="Immanuel College (Australia)">
       Immanuel College
      </a>
      , a boarding school for country boys of German stock, in
      <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">
       Adelaide
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was top of the State in Latin, Greek and German in his final year
      <a href="/wiki/Leaving_Certificate" title="Leaving Certificate">
       Leaving Certificate
      </a>
      examinations in 1926, and thus won a government
      <a href="/wiki/Bursary" title="Bursary">
       bursary
      </a>
      to study at the
      <a href="/wiki/University_of_Adelaide" title="University of Adelaide">
       University of Adelaide
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      At University Strehlow eventually enrolled in a joint honours course in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_languages" title="Classical languages">
       Classics
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Literature" title="English Literature">
       English
      </a>
      , graduating in 1931 with
      <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">
       Honours
      </a>
      in both.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With support from his tutor, and from both
      <a href="/wiki/A._P._Elkin" title="A. P. Elkin">
       A. P. Elkin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Norman_Tindale" title="Norman Tindale">
       Norman Tindale
      </a>
      , Strehlow received a
      <a href="/wiki/Grant_(money)" title="Grant (money)">
       research grant
      </a>
      from the
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Research_Council" title="Australian Research Council">
       Australian National Research Council
      </a>
      to study Arrernte culture, and to that purpose returned to his home in
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Australia" title="Central Australia">
       Central Australia
      </a>
      which was stricken by four years of
      <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">
       drought
      </a>
      and disease that had carried off many people, and emptied the land of
      <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">
       wildlife
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The tribes of Central Australia had already become the object of worldwide interest through the
      <a href="/wiki/Horn_Expedition" title="Horn Expedition">
       joint work of exploration
      </a>
      and ethnographic enquiry undertaken by
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Baldwin_Spencer" title="Walter Baldwin Spencer">
       Baldwin Spencer
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Francis_James_Gillen" title="Francis James Gillen">
       Frank Gillen
      </a>
      , whose researches exercised a notable impact on both
      <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
       sociological
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
       anthropological
      </a>
      theory, in the works of
      <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim" title="mile Durkheim">
       mile Durkheim
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_G._Frazer" title="James G. Frazer">
       James G. Frazer
      </a>
      , and on
      <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">
       psychoanalysis
      </a>
      , in the thesis proposed by
      <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">
       Sigmund Freud
      </a>
      in his
      <a href="/wiki/Totem_and_Taboo" title="Totem and Taboo">
       Totem and Taboo
      </a>
      . One of Freud's disciples,
      <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_R%C3%B3heim" title="Gza Rheim">
       Gza Rheim
      </a>
      , had actually conducted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fieldwork" title="Fieldwork">
       fieldwork
      </a>
      while based in Hermannsburg among the Arrernte in 1929.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His first major informants, old and fully initiated men, were Gurra, from the northern Arrernte, and Njitia and Makarinja from Horseshoe Bend,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      later to be joined by Rauwiraka, Makarinja, Kolbarinja,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Utnadata and Namatjira, the father of
      <a href="/wiki/Albert_Namatjira" title="Albert Namatjira">
       the famous painter of that name
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mickey Gurra (Tjentermana),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      his earliest informant and last of the
      <i>
       ingkata
      </i>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceremonial" title="Ceremonial">
       ceremonial
      </a>
      chiefs
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Bandicoot" title="Bandicoot">
       bandicoot
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Totem" title="Totem">
       totem
      </a>
      centre known as Ilbalintja, confided in Strehlow in May 1933 that neither he nor any of the other old men had sons or grandsons responsible enough to be trusted with the secrets of their sacred objects (
      <a href="/wiki/Tjurunga" title="Tjurunga">
       tjurunga
      </a>
      ) (many of which were being sold for food and tobacco as the native culture broke down),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      together with the accompanying chants and ceremonies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were worried that all their secrets would die with them. Several, such as Rauwiraka, confided to Strehlow their secret knowledge, and even their names, trusting him to conserve the details of all their sacred lore and rites.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the following two years, covering more than 7,000 grueling miles of desert to witness and record aboriginal ways, Strehlow witnessed and recorded some 166 sacred ceremonies dealing with totemic acts,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      most of which are no longer practiced. His academic stature firmed with the publication of
      <i>
       Aranda Traditions
      </i>
      (1947). This work had been assembled in 1934 but Strehlow delayed publication until all his informants were dead.
     </p>
     <p>
      Soon after, in 1949, he received an
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">
       ANU
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">
       fellowship
      </a>
      , which, though he soon found out carried with it no prospect for an academic career in
      <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">
       Canberra
      </a>
      , enabled him to complete further studies in the field, and travel to England for research. His sojourn left him disappointed, both with England, and with many of its leading anthropologists, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Firth" title="Raymond Firth">
       Raymond Firth
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Rupert_Firth" title="John Rupert Firth">
       J. R. Firth
      </a>
      , who in his view failed to extend to him the support and interest his research required, since they were critical of his lack of formal anthropological credentials. He toured the continent and lectured, with considerable success, in France and Germany, and met up with his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siblings" title="Siblings">
       siblings
      </a>
      and mother in
      <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">
       Bavaria
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      He gained recognition for the linguistic work which his father had begun. After the war, in 1946, he was appointed lecturer in English and Linguistics, and then
      <a href="/wiki/Reader_(academic_rank)" title="Reader (academic rank)">
       Reader
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       Linguistics
      </a>
      at Adelaide University in 1954,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and became a full professor when awarded a personal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chair_(academic)" title="Chair (academic)">
       chair
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      in 1970.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 1971, after many years of difficulty due also to the special
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fonts" title="Fonts">
       fonts
      </a>
      required to reproduce his text, he published
      <i>
       Songs of Central Australia
      </i>
      , a monumental study of the ceremonial poetry of the Arrernte tribes. Although reviewed with condescending hostility in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Times_Literary_Supplement" title="Times Literary Supplement">
       TLS
      </a>
      , it was acclaimed by Australian experts like
      <a href="/wiki/A._P._Elkin" title="A. P. Elkin">
       A. P. Elkin
      </a>
      as one of the three most significant books ever published on Australian anthropology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The last three decades of his life were intermittently troubled by the question of the ownership and custodianship on the objects, and records on the aboriginals which he had accumulated during his fieldwork over a long career. The Government and two universities, who had subsidized his labours, and, towards the end, a younger generation of Aranda people on the Land Rights Council,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      believed they were the proper bodies for taking over the care and housing of this extensive material. Strehlow felt a personal responsibility for this material, as the man exclusively entrusted by a generation of elders with myths and songs, their secret knowledge and ceremonial artefacts, and held a grievance for what he considered to be the shabby treatment he had received during his life by the establishment. He set difficult and exacting conditions through many negotiations, and when the issue came to a head, determined to will his private collection to his new family, who would house and conserve it in their own home.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Strehlow justified his retention of these objects by the personal expense he had laid out, and by the fact, he insisted, that they had been formally handed into his care by 'surrender ceremonies'.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In an apparent paradox, once the Lutheran mission at Hermannsburg had sufficient confidence in the Christianized native community to accord them autonomy, and yield church leases on the area to their aboriginal congregation, many local natives moved out, claimed their
      <i>
       tjurunga
      </i>
      rights to the land, and began to re-celebrate the older ceremonies. In his final return to the area, he was surprised to discover that his 'twin', Gustav Malbunka, who had once saved his life, and who had not only renounced his culture but become an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelical" title="Evangelical">
       evangelical
      </a>
      preacher, was capable of singing
      <i>
       tjilpa
      </i>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Quoll" title="Western Quoll">
       totemic quoll
      </a>
      ) verses that once formed a key part of rituals that Strehlow thought were extinct. The culture, even among Christian converts, had been secretly passed on.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Strehlow died of a heart attack in 1978, just before the opening of an exhibition of his collection of artefacts, while conversing with
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kirby_(judge)" title="Michael Kirby (judge)">
       Justice Kirby
      </a>
      and his friend and colleague
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Berndt" title="Ronald Berndt">
       Ronald Berndt
      </a>
      on the extinction of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilby" title="Bilby">
       bilby
      </a>
      (the key animal in the bandicoot ritual) by introduced rabbits, a metaphor for what was happening to the aborigines and their culture with the spread of white civilisation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was cremated. His career and his role as the custodian of Aboriginal secrets have been dogged by controversy which has followed him beyond the grave.
     </p>
     <p>
      A decade later, negotiations between his widow and the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">
       Northern Territory
      </a>
      government led to the purchase of most, though not all, of the collection on 27 February 1987.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Strehlow_Research_Centre" title="Strehlow Research Centre">
        Strehlow Research Centre
       </a>
      </i>
      at Alice Springs was established for the preservation and public display of these works.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Involvement_in_the_Rupert_Stuart_child_rape_case">
       Involvement in the Rupert Stuart child rape case
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ted_Strehlow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Involvement in the Rupert Stuart child rape case">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1958 a nine-year-old girl, Mary Hattam, was found raped and murdered on the beach at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceduna" title="Ceduna">
       Ceduna
      </a>
      . The police subsequently arrested an aboriginal,
      <a href="/wiki/Max_Stuart" title="Max Stuart">
       Rupert Max Stuart
      </a>
      , for the crime. Stuart was convicted and condemned to death in late April 1959. The case quickly assumed the character of a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cause_c%C3%A9l%C3%A8bre" title="Cause clbre">
        cause clbre
       </a>
      </i>
      as
      <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">
       civil rights groups
      </a>
      questioned the evidence based solely on a confession made to the police which the prosecution and officers affirmed had been taken down word for word. The verdict was
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appealed" title="Appealed">
       appealed
      </a>
      , went to the
      <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">
       High Court
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" title="Her Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council">
       Privy Council
      </a>
      in London, and concluded with a review by a
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission" title="Royal Commission">
       Royal Commission
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Strehlow's involvement came after a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_priest" title="Catholic priest">
       Catholic priest
      </a>
      who was convinced of Stuart's innocence asked him for an informed judgement on the language of the evidence by which the aborigine had been convicted.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Strehlow, it turned out, had known during his days as a
      <a href="/wiki/Patrol" title="Patrol">
       Patrol Officer
      </a>
      at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay_Creek,_Northern_Territory" title="Jay Creek, Northern Territory">
       Jay Creek
      </a>
      both Stuart's grandfather, Tom Ljonga, and Stuart himself. Ljonga had been his trusted companion through many long journeys through the Central Australian deserts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Four days before the appointed
      <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">
       hanging
      </a>
      , Strehlow, with the Catholic chaplain, interviewed Stuart at
      <a href="/wiki/Yatala_Labour_Prison" title="Yatala Labour Prison">
       Yatala prison
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the subsequent review process, Strehlow testified several times on what he saw as the incompatibility between the English of the confession and the
      <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
       dialect
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
       vernacular
      </a>
      Stuart used. Familiar with white men in the Centre who had raped aboriginal girls of that age,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Strehlow did not think this crime fitted with Aboriginal behaviour.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Stuart's conviction was upheld but on release from gaol, he went on to become a major figure on an
      <a href="/wiki/Central_Land_Council" title="Central Land Council">
       Aboriginal Land Rights Council
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
