                <div class="date-outer">
                 <h2 class="date-header">
                  <span>
                   Monday, May 18, 2015
                  </span>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="date-posts">
                  <div class="post-outer">
                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-CsH6ttv85tc/VVobMwSX2fI/AAAAAAAAErY/PdAT59AE-nQ/s320/E%2BMajor%2BTriad.jpg" itemprop="image_url"/>
                    <meta content="24196751460318331" itemprop="blogId"/>
                    <meta content="8873646520402823273" itemprop="postId"/>

                    </a>
                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2015/05/vultures-john-mayers-shape-usage.html">
                      "Vultures": John Mayer's Shape Usage
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8873646520402823273" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <b>
                      Everyone's heard
                     </b>
                     of John Mayer's classic album Continuum and the hit songs it's given us. But one of its most musically-intriguing and impressive songs is not a pop radio hit nor a song the album is really known for at face-value by the public; it's a part of a category of songs to which, when he adds a new one, he astonishes and inspires the guitar playing community.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     His song "Vultures,"defines Continuum's categorical participation in this realm of John's guitar-centric songwriting. When you first hear it, you haven't heard anything like it, and its catchy, repetitive sound never leaves your head. It's a trademark of John's innovation and skill in combining electric guitar and pop music into a song that's a hit by its own definition.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     More so, there's a lot to be learned from analyzing a song like this because, believe it or not, it is a rather simple composition but comes off as inspiring because of its perceived complexity. As long as you know what notes constitute a major triad, the major scale itself, and how the same notes can be played on different geographical locations on the guitar, you can learn this song
                     <i>
                      and
                     </i>
                     apply its technique to your own style of playing. Some knowledge of scale modes is beneficial, too.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     So, let's give it a shot and see what we come up with.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      "Vultures," is in the key of E Major, in common time, and is played, throughout the entire song, past the twelfth fret on the guitar. The actual fingering of the one lick that composes the whole song is unique and, like almost all of Mayer's guitar riffage, a bit difficult to play at first. But the most significant point to make here when talking about technique is that it's all based around the tonic chord's major triad shape.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      John starts off quarter-bending a fifth harmony for a full quarter note on the fourteenth fret that composes of the perfect fifth and root notes of the E Major triad. In fret-talk, it's the A and D strings on the fourteenth fret, which play the notes B and E, respectively. If you can't quite play that high up on the neck with efficiency yet, you can drop it an octave and play the same notes on the same strings on the second fret. From there, he plucks an eighth note on the A string, sixteenth fret (or A string, fourth fret) for a C# note and then jumps down to a third harmony composed of E and G#. He fiddles around with the song's time signature, but that riff is basically the entire song and, if you haven't already noticed, it's all based around the Jimi Hendrix-rendition of the major triad chord, geographically-speaking.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      Let me explain. Below is a chord chart depicting the Jimi Hendrix-rendition of the major triad chord; in this case, the E Major triad.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-CsH6ttv85tc/VVobMwSX2fI/AAAAAAAAErY/PdAT59AE-nQ/s1600/E%2BMajor%2BTriad.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="290" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-CsH6ttv85tc/VVobMwSX2fI/AAAAAAAAErY/PdAT59AE-nQ/s320/E%2BMajor%2BTriad.jpg" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      Now, let me show you what parts of the chord he plays.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-4YDXqb2Rb1g/VVoiODFuQnI/AAAAAAAAEro/TgNps0da940/s1600/E%2BMajor%2BTriad.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="290" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-4YDXqb2Rb1g/VVoiODFuQnI/AAAAAAAAEro/TgNps0da940/s320/E%2BMajor%2BTriad.jpg" width="320"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      As illustrated, the harmony circled in red is what John starts the riff with, and the harmony circled in blue is what he ends it with. The single, eighth note he plucks is not depicted.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <span style="text-align: left;">
                       In regards to the shape itself, both John and Jimi use their thumbs to finger the bass E note on the low E string, but for the sake of proper form, I've added that bar thing that says, "Use your index finger p*ssy," but good luck doing that. Remember, you can also play this exact shape on the second and first frets in the E Major open chord format, but this is how John plays it in the song.
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <span style="text-align: left;">
                       <br/>
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      Anyway, as I explained before, the
                      <i>
                       entire song
                      </i>
                      branches off this one, single chord shape. John solos around this and plays with it but always returns to it in the end, illustrating the dimensional beauty that can be had in musical simplicity. Like I said, a hit song by its own definition.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      Mayer is dominantly a Blues player and, therefore, knows his shapes. "Vultures," itself is a Blues idea with flares of Pop, both lyrically and musically; in fact, the entire album of Continuum consists of compositions under the umbrella of one, sonic idea. John's playing is representative of this, most especially in his live shows where most of the songs allow him to be musically-open and free and do not dictate what he has to do each night. John gives us great teaching tools in Continuum that can help us all improve as writers and players.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      If you want to start playing and writing differently
                      <i>
                       tonight,
                      </i>
                      start increasing your awareness of what shapes your using and how they can be manipulated to make your songs more interesting.
                     </div>
                     <div style="clear: both;">
                     </div>
                    </div>

                 <h2 class="date-header">
                  <span>
                   Sunday, April 26, 2015
                  </span>
                 </h2>
                 <div class="date-posts">
                  <div class="post-outer">
                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-L1_FWv6wraw/VT7sahtGEWI/AAAAAAAAEoo/q7IflIkfIvw/s1600/Colbie.PNG" itemprop="image_url"/>
                    <meta content="24196751460318331" itemprop="blogId"/>
                    <meta content="4936054046269430194" itemprop="postId"/>

                    </a>
                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2015/04/colbie-caillat-body-is-wonderland.html">
                      Colbie Caillat's Body Is a Wonderland
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4936054046269430194" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <b>
                      I think
                     </b>
                     I've realized something that has, until now, always just skimmed past me. It's a little fluorescent for lack of a better term, but it does illustrate how you can create creative complexity in one simple line.
                     <br/>
                     <div>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-L1_FWv6wraw/VT7sahtGEWI/AAAAAAAAEoo/q7IflIkfIvw/s1600/Colbie.PNG" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="320" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-L1_FWv6wraw/VT7sahtGEWI/AAAAAAAAEoo/q7IflIkfIvw/s1600/Colbie.PNG" width="180"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <div style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       Earlier today, I was listening to Colbie Caillat's song "Bubbly" which I quite like. It's one of those songs that has a heard-around-the-world chorus
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        that
                        <i>
                         everybody
                        </i>
                        knows. Even hearing the phrase, "Starts with my toes, crinkles my nose," will undoubtedly trigger a mental reference of some sort to this song. But, it wasn't this popular part of the chorus that caught my attention.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        Nope, it was the last two lines.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        So, let me just give you the entire chorus:
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         It starts with my toes,
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         Makes me crinkle my nose,
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         Wherever it goes, I'll always know
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         That you make me smile
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         Please stay for a while now,
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         Just take your time
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         Wherever you go
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <i>
                         <br/>
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        You may have noticed, or I may just be telling you now, that the last four lines seem to contradict each other in a strange way. She's asking this guy to "stay for a while," but, at the same time, asking him to "just take [his] time / wherever [he] goes."
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        ...to take his time, wherever he goes.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        Yea.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        What a steamy realization. I had never thought Colbie Caillat could be so subtly risqu in such a cute way.
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Helvetica Neue Light, HelveticaNeue-Light, helvetica, arial, sans-serif;">
                        But who knows for sure, this is just me. What do you think?
                       </span>
                      </div>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2015/04/musica-especial.html">
                      Msica Especial: "Balderrama"
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4543254982491558278" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <b>
                      Una de
                     </b>
                     mis pelculas favoritas est dirigida y escrita por Steven Soderbergh. Se llama
                     <i>
                      Che,
                     </i>
                     y detalla la vida y las luchas del revolutionario Cubano Ernesto "Che" Guevara durante La Revolucin Cubana. El actor que interpreta a Che Guevara se llama Benicio Del Toro, uno de mis actores favoritos latinos tambin.
                     <br/>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      Aparte de su dilogo realsitico y personajes interesantes que respaldan a Benicio, su msica representa una atmsfera honesta que se flota encima de lo que sientas. Una cancin que logra esto fenomenalmente es
                      <i>
                       Balderrama
                      </i>
                      por Mercedes Sosa, guitarrista y cantante latina. La escena en la cual se reproduce esta cancin es la que ilustra la muerte de Che. Mientras los soldados llevan su cuerpo por camilla, se oyen las primeras notas bellsimas de la guitarra, seguido por la voz tranquila de Mercedes Sosa.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      Escchela, y se sentir el mensaje puro de la cancin.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>

                   <div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
                    <meta content="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-Ec9s6QlQnLQ/VSclw6bds_I/AAAAAAAAEZo/qGsMGYoe6ro/s1600/Blog%2BPic.PNG" itemprop="image_url"/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2015/04/the-complexity-of-human-relationships.html">
                      The Complexity of Human Relationships in Music
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2337250735767006603" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <b>
                       It takes
                      </b>
                      a mind of creative control and comprehension, lyrically and musically, to be able to portray ideas in songs that people not only relate to, but feel as if they were their own. Complex emotional ideas that aren't really unheard of, but can be constantly sent back to the factory to be produced again in a different way, are the centerpieces for the most resounding of songs that sock you directly in the gut because of their top-level accessibility that can expand into deeper, larger ideas.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-Ec9s6QlQnLQ/VSclw6bds_I/AAAAAAAAEZo/qGsMGYoe6ro/s1600/Blog%2BPic.PNG" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                       <img border="0" height="200" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-Ec9s6QlQnLQ/VSclw6bds_I/AAAAAAAAEZo/qGsMGYoe6ro/s1600/Blog%2BPic.PNG" width="142"/>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Human relationships, something we all experience, is one of these ideas that has been sung about through the ages. But as we near the peak of the second decade in this our 21st Century, artists are finding new ways to define how they drape their interpretations of this extremely emotional and eccentric idea over their listeners. They're allowing our Pop Culture to evolve by providing fresh slots into which it can move, or redefining what it gives them to work with.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      A modern artist that has exemplified mastery of this art well is Drake. I especially enjoy his album
                      <i>
                       Take Care,
                      </i>
                      and some of the sad truths conveyed in its message.Two specific songs that can be used as examples of portrayals of the complexity of both broken and invaluable human relationships, romantic and filial, are "Doing It Wrong," and "Look What You've Done," respectively.
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     <div style="text-align: left;">
                      Have a listen to "Doing It Wrong" to try and get a feel for what I mean.
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="YOUTUBE-iframe-video" data-thumbnail-src="https://i.ytimg.com/vi/RfiSW3jfJhA/0.jpg" frameborder="0" height="266" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/RfiSW3jfJhA?feature=player_embedded" width="320">
                      </iframe>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      The opening verse is a gut check:
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       When a good thing goes bad
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       It's not the end of the world
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       It's just the end of a world
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       That you had with one girl
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      That sense of singularity, how he points a finger at everything that defined a relationship that is now in the past, can be hurtful to hear, and can stir up vaulted emotions of your own. But at the same time, what could be more true? When a relationship dies, it's not the end of the world, it's just the ending of a phase; another check mark on your timeline. Although it may certainly
                      <i>
                       feel
                      </i>
                      like everything around you is burning down, it's important to keep what is important in perspective. Drake says this all in four, straightforward lines that, even if you're not reeling from a recently routed relationship, still hit home pretty hard.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      Another verse in the song that's truthfully painful comes after the first repetition of the chorus:
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       We live in a generation of
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Not being in love
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       And not being together
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      Plasticity is becoming an increasingly relevant and accurate term that can be applied to how we look at love in this age. People want to go out and have fun without commitment, without having to worry about the messy emotions and feelings that come with relationships. But at the same time, some people don't know how to truly identify when they are in love, or when, if they have identified it, it's worth pursuing and when it's not. Even with couples who are in committed relationships, the word "love" is often used incorrectly, temporarily labeling feelings of satisfaction, lust, or the simple conclusion that it's what should be said. Although not expounding with great detail, Drake puts this verse in that general direction, giving us yet another thought-provoking, challenging, and heart-hitting message.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      The second example is "Look What You've Done":
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div style="text-align: center;">
                      <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="YOUTUBE-iframe-video" data-thumbnail-src="https://i.ytimg.com/vi/z1VlFPSK6p8/0.jpg" frameborder="0" height="266" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/z1VlFPSK6p8?feature=player_embedded" width="320">
                      </iframe>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      After the story the first verse tells, the depth of this song hits in the chorus:
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Is this s*** real
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Should I pinch you
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       After all the things we've been through
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       I got you
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Look what you've done
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Look what you've done
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Look what you've done for me now
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       You knew that I was gonna be something
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       We stressed out, and you need some
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       I got you
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Look what you've done
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       Look what you've done
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <i>
                       <br/>
                      </i>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      The overall message of this song is Drake reaffirming to his grandmother and uncle, two people enormously responsible for his success, what they've done for him, given him, and provided for him when things could not have gotten harder. The filial connection that's felt is heart-wrenching; it almost makes you want to call up your own mother just to tell her you love her, and if it weren't for how well Drake feeds you that emotion through these lyrics and his voice, you would probably just shrug your shoulders and move on with the rest of the song. This is a
                      <i>
                       phenomenal
                      </i>
                      ability to have if you're a songwriter, and it becomes even more phenomenal when you learn to recognize and control it.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      When I say that artists like Drake are redefining what Pop Culture gives them to evolve, this is only a minute sampler of what I mean.The man is gifted, and it's a responsibility of his to take the baton and keep running until he can pass it off to a worthy successor.
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                      What other artists do you like that convey such strong emotions for you?
                     </div>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2015/04/songwriting-planting-your-mood.html">
                      Songwriting: Planting Your Mood
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-46766541893515139" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <strong>
                      Listen to
                     </strong>
                     just the first ten seconds of this song, and describe to yourself what you're feeling.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="YOUTUBE-iframe-video" data-thumbnail-src="https://i.ytimg.com/vi/TXk6iE3rvvQ/0.jpg" frameborder="0" height="266" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/TXk6iE3rvvQ?feature=player_embedded" width="320">
                      </iframe>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     From the moment he trills that first chord, the atmosphere of the song catapults itself into your brain. Your own mood sulks, your adrenaline lightens, your heart rate decreases, and those emotional twangs of sappiness begin popping all over your body. Urges of warmth and security surface and you begin to, hypothetically, look around for your lover, or the one you
                     <i>
                      want
                     </i>
                     to call your lover.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     It's emotions and feelings like these that keep listeners engaged and sell songs.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     You almost already know what to expect lyrically by just listening to the instrumental intro. Once the melody hits, that mood that's barely ten seconds old is enforced and stacked upon until you feel this psychological weight on your shoulders that's almost too cumbersome to bear. At this point, the song has reached the very depths of your gut, and you have no choice but to sit through it and revel in its murderous beauty.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I cannot stress this component of songwriting enough. If your lyrics and chords don't work together well enough to mutually create a mood that grabs your listeners like this, it'll pass through them nonchalantly and leave them with a shoulder-shrugging reaction, instead of a non-satiable thirst for more.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The public is smarter than you are, so learning to create solid songs that drive home that full message will leave them no choice but to succumb to the sound to learn how it works within them. Once that happens, it's already in their iTunes Library.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I cover in more detail the importance of mood in my series
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2014/08/easy-songwriting-from-scratch-lyrics.html">
                      Easy Songwriting from Scratch
                     </a>
                     , so if you're interested, head on over and check it out.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     How do you maximize the moods of your songs?

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2014/08/easy-songwriting-from-scratch-lyrics.html">
                      Easy Songwriting from Scratch: The Lyrics, Part 1
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2192358681612713475" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     Hello again, and welcome back to "Easy Songwriting from Scratch." This installment begins part one of the lyric writing section, detailing for music laymen how lyrics are chosen, written and placed over the music in a song. If you haven't read
                     <a href="http://ldubsmusic.blogspot.com/2014/08/easy-songwriting-from-scratch-music.html">
                      part one
                     </a>
                     or
                     <a href="http://ldubsmusic.blogspot.com/2014/08/easy-songwriting-from-scratch-music_18.html">
                      part two
                     </a>
                     of the
                     <b>
                      music-writing
                     </b>
                     section, I highly recommend doing so in order to follow along with the knowledge we learned in those two sections. Let's cut to the chase and begin.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <u>
                       Part #1: Melody+ Words = Lyrics
                      </u>
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      <u>
                       <br/>
                      </u>
                     </b>
                     <b>
                      <u>
                       <br/>
                      </u>
                     </b>
                     We left off in part two of the music section talking about
                     <b>
                      melody
                     </b>
                     and
                     <b>
                      lyrics
                     </b>
                     , both of which I loosely touched on; however, it's important for us to go a bit more in-depth now and discuss exactly what makes up melody and lyrics. The
                     <b>
                      melody
                     </b>
                     of a song is the cadence of musical notes over which the singer is
                     <b>
                      singing
                     </b>
                     , whereas the
                     <b>
                      lyrics
                     </b>
                     of a song are the
                     <b>
                      words
                     </b>
                     themselves that the singer is
                     <b>
                      saying
                     </b>
                     . There's a very important difference there, which is why I've decided to entitle this section "Melody+ Words = Lyrics" because that's exactly the equation songwriters use to write the
                     <b>
                      lyrics
                     </b>
                     of a song,
                     <b>
                      not
                     </b>
                     the
                     <b>
                      melody
                     </b>
                     . A
                     <b>
                      melody
                     </b>
                     must first be established before the words themselves can be written, and therefore, that's what we're going to focus on first.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     There are many ways to write a melody: you could sit down and literally plot out the notes over which you wish to sing, but that requires extensive ear-training and an inclined musical ear; you could use the same instrument you used to record the underlying chord progression of your song and try to play out a melody using just one line of notes, but again, that's probably too musical for a music layman; or, you could just hum and sing whatever pops into your head while playing the underlying chord progression of your song. The last method, believe it or not, is actually very effective, and since I'm addressing the mainly musically impaired in this series of lessons, that's the method I'm going to want you to use.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div>
                      When I'm mining for melodies,I'm usuallyjust mumbling, humming and singing random babble as well as
                      <b>
                       whatever pops into my head
                      </b>
                      over the chord progression I'm playing, but I am also informing myself what type of
                      <b>
                       sound,
                      </b>
                      or
                      <b>
                       tone,
                      </b>
                      I want my melody to have. Since I'm going for a sad and gloomy
                      <b>
                       tone
                      </b>
                      of melody for this specific example song, I'm singing in exactly that manner: sad and gloomy. I'm not singing upbeat and bright notes simply because I don't want to; you can most certainly choose to do so if you're aiming for a happy and cheerful
                      <b>
                       tone
                      </b>
                      of melody, but it's important to discern what you want your
                      <b>
                       tone
                      </b>
                      of melody to be before you start hunting. Decide before hand, begin playing your progression and see what comes out; you'll be surprised as to what you find. Also, I highly recommend recording each melody-hunting session you embark on in order to
                      <b>
                       capture
                      </b>
                      the best melody-discovering moments that will most likely come out of nowhere and soon be forgotten. That way, you can play it back and know exactly what notes you have to sing in order get the best
                      <b>
                       tone
                      </b>
                      of melody that you're aiming for. Just bust out the old iPhone Voice Memo app or any recording device you have, nothing fancy. You'll be glad you did when you stumble upon that one cadence of notes that you know this girl for whom you're writing this song will hear and absolutely love, whether sweetly or bitter-sweetly. That's about 90% of the songwriting process: stumbling.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Once you've found a basic and general idea of a melody, it's time to work on those words. Remember that lyrics are composed of a melody
                      <b>
                       and
                      </b>
                      words, not just words, so make sure you've got that general idea of a melody down before you begin writing. You can fine-tune and fiddle with your melody while writing the words, but it's imperative that you at least know the
                      <b>
                       tone
                      </b>
                      of your melody before you begin.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      So, what do we need to know about writing the
                      <b>
                       words
                      </b>
                      for our
                      <b>
                       lyrics?
                      </b>
                      Many things, but I'm going to keep it simple here. I'm not going to micromanage either, I'm just going to pitch you the fundamentals that you can use in your own creative way. First things first, you need to begin to gather and group ideas, or brainstorm. Take out a notebook or piece of paper and pencil and begin to write down
                      <b>
                       anything
                      </b>
                      that comes to your mind regarding the general topic you want to write about; words, visual imagery, pairs of rhymes, anything. Circle, underline, group, just get it all flowing. By doing this, you can pull from this exercised cloud of ideas when creating a title, writing lines, rhymes etc., and access to important concepts will be much easier than starting right from scratch and trying to think it all through. Once you've written down a nice group of ideas and have decided on a topic, your next step is to write your title. Although not required, writing your title first is a
                      <b>
                       very
                      </b>
                      efficient way of writing your words because, by doing so, it's easier to relate the messages of each piece of the song to each other so that they all ultimately fall under the umbrella of your title nice and neatly. Your title is entirely up to you, so brainstorm away! Finally, once you've got all your ideas written down and your title decided on, it's time to move onto writing the bulk of your song: the finalized words themselves.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      There are two basic
                      <b>
                       tools
                      </b>
                      we use when writing the words for our lyrics:
                      <b>
                       types of rhymes
                      </b>
                      and
                      <b>
                       rhyme schemes.
                      </b>
                      We can define
                      <b>
                       types of rhymes
                      </b>
                      as what words rhyme when
                      <b>
                       sung,
                      </b>
                      not
                      <b>
                       said,
                      </b>
                      and
                      <b>
                       rhyme schemes
                      </b>
                      can be defined as what rhymes we put where in our song. So think of it like this: when writing the words for your lyrics, it's important to keep in mind
                      <b>
                       what
                      </b>
                      words rhyme,
                      <b>
                       where
                      </b>
                      they rhyme and
                      <b>
                       when
                      </b>
                      those rhyming words rhyme in the song, like a
                      <b>
                       Three W's
                      </b>
                      method of word-writing. Let's cover the
                      <b>
                       what
                      </b>
                      first.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      An efficient way of finding rhymes is to, believe it or not, use a rhyming dictionary. Doing so will help greatly; even the professionals use rhyming dictionaries, so it certainly wouldn't hurt to have one, but it's okay if you don't have one as well. What you can do is write down the ten most relatable words, mainly nouns, to your topic and number them 1 to 10 horizontally, like so:
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      1.
                      <b>
                       Love
                      </b>
                      2.
                      <b>
                       Grief
                      </b>
                      3.
                      <b>
                       Pain
                      </b>
                      4.
                      <b>
                       Sorrow
                      </b>
                      5.
                      <b>
                       Heart
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <b>
                       <br/>
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      6.
                      <b>
                       Call
                      </b>
                      7.
                      <b>
                       Dream
                      </b>
                      8.
                      <b>
                       Key
                      </b>
                      9.
                      <b>
                       Lock
                      </b>
                      10.
                      <b>
                       Face
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <b>
                       <br/>
                      </b>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      Then, using a rhyming dictionary or your mind (rhyming dictionary is
                      <i>
                       highly recommended
                      </i>
                      though as it can stimulate brand new ideas and concepts you would not have thought of originally; rhymezone.com is a great online one) begin to list words that rhyme with each of the ten words you wrote down underneath them in column forum, like so:
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br/>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto; background-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0);">

                        </b>
                       </span>

                       </span>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto; background-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0);">
                       </span>
                       <b style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                        Grief
                       </b>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                        3.
                        <b>
                         Pain
                        </b>
                       </span>
                       <b style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       </b>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <b style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       </b>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                        <i>
                         Enough     Thief        Gain
                        </i>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>

                       </b>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto; background-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0);">
                        5.
                       </span>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto; background-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0);">
                       </span>

                        Heart
                       </b>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">

                        <b>

                        </b>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <b style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto; background-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0);">
                       </b>
                       <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto; background-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0);">

                      </div>

                     </div>

                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       <i>
                        Trace
                       </i>
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       <i>
                        Create
                       </i>
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       <i>
                        Take
                       </i>
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">

                        Wake
                       </i>

                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">

                       </i>
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       Notice how the rhymes get less
                       <b>
                        perfect
                       </b>
                       as each list descends. It's good to write down words that half-rhyme and barely
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       rhyme as to stimulate unique ideas, phrases and other cool sounding lines, which should already be flowing just by writing these words down. Remember, you're trying to find words that rhyme when
                       <b>
                        sung,
                       </b>
                       not
                       <b>
                        said.
                       </b>
                       Keep an eye on specific vowels and consonants that may rhyme between two words when
                       <b>
                        sung
                       </b>
                       according to your melody,and write them down. This also covers the
                       <b>
                        where
                       </b>
                       of rhyming words: the parts of two different words that either perfeclty rhyme, half-rhyme or barely rhyme. That's the basic, fundamental way of looking at it, and it's a great method to start off with.
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <span style="-webkit-text-size-adjust: auto;">
                       <br/>
                      </span>
                     </div>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://www.musiclessonswithluke.com/2015/03/tristan-prettymans-say-anything.html">
                      Tristan Prettyman's "Say Anything": Listener's Aftermath
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4791297453651258004" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <b>
                      Driving to
                     </b>
                     one of my favorite mind-clearing spots one cold and snowy night with my sister, we couldn't help but notice the doleful atmosphere of our silence. But we also recognized a certain quality of solace it had and didn't want to demolish anything prematurely. We searched for something that could give us some middle-space, and she recommended putting on Tristan Prettyman's song "Say Anything."
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                      <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="YOUTUBE-iframe-video" data-thumbnail-src="https://ytimg.googleusercontent.com/vi/BfRpWHkhFSg/0.jpg" frameborder="0" height="266" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/BfRpWHkhFSg?feature=player_embedded" width="320">
                      </iframe>
                     </div>
                     <br/>
                     Listening to this song while driving was a harrowing experience. We can go into an entire sermon about how songs relate to emotional experiences and churn the gears in our hearts, but once the song was over the silence that followed could not have been louder.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     I didn't want to put on anything else afterwards because I was afraid that, no matter what song we put on, it just wouldn't be able to live up to the expectation Say Anything left behind. I was utterly flattened and we finished our drive in silence.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     That was the first time a song had ever left me in such shock. Have you ever had any experiences like this?
                     <div style="clear: both;">
                     </div>
