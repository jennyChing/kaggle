          <h1 class="single-post-title entry-title">
           4 Simple Steps to Raising All the Money Your Nonprofit Needs
          </h1>
          <!-- .single-post-title -->
          <ul class="meta clr meta-with-title">
           <li class="meta-date">
            <span class="fa fa-clock-o">
            </span>
            <span class="updated">
             August 12, 2014
            </span>
           </li>
           <li class="meta-author">
            <span class="fa fa-user">
            </span>
            <span class="vcard author">
             <a href="http://www.501c3.org/author/srees/" rel="author" title="Posts by Sandy Rees, CFRE">
              Sandy Rees, CFRE
             </a>
            </span>
           </li>
           <li class="meta-category">
            <span class="fa fa-folder-o">
            </span>
            <a href="http://www.501c3.org/category/education/" rel="category tag">
             Education
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="http://www.501c3.org/category/funding/" rel="category tag">
             Funding
            </a>

            <a href="http://www.501c3.org/category/managing-a-nonprofit/" rel="category tag">
             Managing a Nonprofit
            </a>
           </li>
          </ul>
          <!-- .meta -->
         </header>
         <!-- .single-blog-header -->
         <div class="entry clr">
          <p>
           <strong>
            Guest Post by Fundraising Expert, Sandy Rees, CFRE
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p>
           <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-5495 alignleft" height="250" src="http://www.501c3.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/fundraising.jpg" style="margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 10px;" title="fundraising" width="202"/>
           In the world of nonprofits, its all about fundraising. You cant do much in the way of service delivery or mission fulfillment without money.
          </p>
          <p>
           For fundraising staff, it IS all about raising money and sometimes it can be a challenge. Ive spent years raising money for all kinds of nonprofit organizations and I know what works and what doesnt. One thing I know for sure is that we must aim high.
          </p>
          <p>
           I learned early on in my career to shoot for the stars. This came from my unwillingness to settle. I saw so many people waiting to be helped at the rescue mission and at the food bank, and I knew that if I raised more money, we could help more of them. So, I started working toward fully funding my organization. I wanted to do everything in my power to make sure that people had a warm bed and a hot meal.
          </p>
          <p>
           I call it Getting Fully Funded. It means that your nonprofits staff have everything they need to deliver service. It means that all the bills are paid and you have a rainy day fund established. You have lots of happy and engaged donors. You have diversified revenue streams and fundraising is fun. Its a wonderful place to be! (Ive been there myself and with my clients many times!)
          </p>
          <p>
           Before you can Get Fully Funded, there are a few things you need to have in place. You must have:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <strong>
             The right mindset.
            </strong>
            Your attitude must be positive and you must believe that the support you are looking for is out there.
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Passion for the cause.
            </strong>
            You must be passionate for your nonprofits cause. If you dont care deeply, how do you expect others to care and to donate?
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Strong leadership.
            </strong>
            Your nonprofit must have a strong Director and a strong Board who embrace their roles. Without strong leaders, fundraising will be tough if not very impossible.
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Compelling mission.
            </strong>
            I believe that most nonprofit missions are compelling. More importantly, YOU must believe your nonprofits mission is worthwhile and deserves attention, and you must be able to communicate it to others.
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Donor-based fundraising.
            </strong>
            Your fundraising must be focused on your donors, and your activities must seek to build relationships with these partners in your work.
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           Once you get these five criteria established, youre ready to start raising money.
          </p>
          <p>
           To make it very simple, Ive boiled it down to 4 steps. If you follow and fully implement these 4 simple steps, you can raise all the money your nonprofit needs to fulfill its mission.
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Tell your story.
            </strong>
            Since childhood, weve been conditioned to listen to stories. Telling your story engages your listener and educates them at the same time. Start with a powerful elevator speech  a 30 second version of who your organization is and what you do. Focus on how youre changing lives. Leave out the jargon. And be prepared to share a story about a specific person your nonprofit has helped. Remember that the best stories are short and interesting so dont firehose information at people.
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Ask for a gift.
            </strong>
            No matter how wonderful your nonprofit is, you must ask for a gift. I suggest you ask several times during the year, and use a variety of strategies (like events, letters, and face-to-face asks). Tie the ask to something tangible if possible (your gift of $1.81 will provide a homeless person with a hot meal).
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Thank the donor.
            </strong>
            If you dont get anything else right, get this piece right! Send out a thank-you letter to each donor within 2 days if possible. Add a hand-written note a few days later or a phone call and you deepen the impact of the recognition, plus you subconsciously let the donor know youre on the ball.
            <br/>
            A timely, warm thank-you letter serves many purposes. First, it lets the donor know you got their check. Second, it builds trust and relationship, which are two keys to fundraising. You can include a short paragraph about how you will use the donors gift to further engage the donor.
           </li>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Build relationships.
            </strong>
            You must build relationships with donors if you want to Get Fully Funded. Our donors are not ATM machines. We cant just show up whenever we need to withdraw money. We must engage our donors as partners in our work and treat them with respect. One key to building relationship is to maintain regular communication. Have you ever had a friend that the only time you heard from them was when they wanted something? Do you want to show up like that to your donors? Create a plan for how and when you will communicate with your donors.
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           These four steps are simple and youve probably heard them before. The key is to go deep into each step to make sure you are doing the best job you can possibly do. And when you do that, youre on your way to raising the money of your dreams.
          </p>

                  It all depends on the actions of the charity itself.  Designated gifts ARE restricted, even without solicitation, unless the charity informs the donor otherwise.  For example, if a charity receives a designated gift, but tells the donor it may be used elsewhere, the charity is free to do sothat is, assuming the donor agrees to it.  Its all about mutual understanding between the donor and the charity.  If the donor doesnt agree, then the charity should either accept the gift and consider it restricted, or refuse the gift.  If the organization accepts the unsolicited, designated gift and says nothing, the money is restricted.
                 </p>

                 <p>
                  Lets try to tackle these in order
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  1.  Having met your registered agent really isnt a material issue.  The bigger issue is that you need to have 501(c)(3) status in order for people to donate tax deductibly.  The other issue is that DC requires charities to register with the Division of Charities prior to soliciting.  Usually registration requires 501(c)(3) status to be in place.
                  <br/>
                  2.  You can certainly create a website.  You cannot say or imply that your organization has charity status, however, without 501(c)(3) approval.
                  <br/>
                  3.  The IRS considers any corporation to be for-profit and taxable unless is has been granted 501(c) status, regardless of what your Articles say.
                  <br/>
                  4.  You will not likely experience good success in requesting funding until the issues above are dealt with.  In particular, you cannot fundraise with registering (see Q 1 above).
                  <br/>
                  5.  That question is probably better answered by those in your particular arena who have been there, done that!  Ill stick to what I know (regulatory issues).
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Good luck with it all.  If you need assistance with getting 501(c)(3) status, give us a call.

                <div class="comment-content">
                 <p>
                  Its usually not difficult, actually.  The limit in question is the public support test, which says public charities must have no less than 33% of donations coming from a broad base of smaller donors (2%) and/or other public charities.  Also, this is calculated on a 5 year running average, not any individual year.  The actual equation using round numbers makes it easier to understand:
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  If an organization brings in $100,000 over 5 years, at least $33,000 of it must be from donors giving less than $2,000 (2% of $100,000) each over the same 5 years.  Not so bad when you look at it that way.  Also, donations of any amount from other public charities go toward the 33%, helping offset any larger individual donations that may be causing an organization to cut it close.  For example, if in the same 5 years, XYZ Charity donated $33,000 or more to the organization, the public support test is considered met and it no longer matters how much anyone else gave during that time period.
                 </p>

                <div class="comment-content">
                 <p>
                  Hi, JonathanIm glad you are enjoying the site.  Lets tackle your questions:
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  1.  Dont confuse board director with Executive Director.  A member of the board of directors is part of the governing body and is one of however many equals from a voting perspective.  Executive Director is usually a title given to the manager of the day-to-day operations.  Other similar titles may include headmaster for a school, lead pastor for a church, etc..  There are no prohibitions really on dual managers, regardless of the title used.  My only caution is that equal managerial authority in difficult to pull off in practice.  As someone who used to have an equal business partner, I speak from experience
                  <img alt=":-)" class="wp-smiley" src="http://www.501c3.org/wp-includes/images/smilies/simple-smile.png" style="height: 1em; max-height: 1em;"/>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  2.  Be careful with this one.  If you hire staff and require them to be certified in certain things, paying for the certification can be considered an ordinary business expense since the certification is a condition of employment.  The difficulty comes in applying that to yourself.  You didnt say so, but I presume you will also be part of the governing board.  Because of the conflict of interest as founder, board member, and potentially paid staff, it isnt sufficiently arms-length to pay for your certifications out of organizational funds.  Remember that these certifications have potential benefit to you outside the proposed organization.  Just food for thought.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I hope that helps.  Good luck with it!
                 </p>
