   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Problem of evil
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       problem of evil
      </b>
      is the question whether
      <a href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">
       evil
      </a>
      exists, and if so does it disprove the existence of
      <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
       God
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stanford_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Stanford-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Certain
      <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">
       religions
      </a>
      say there is a
      <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">
       god
      </a>
      who is all-loving, all-knowing and all-powerful. In these religions, the question to answer is how an all-loving and all-powerful god could allow evil to exist.
     </p>
     <p>
      Responses include the argument that if there is
      <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">
       free will
      </a>
      , evil must necessarily exist; that humans cannot understand God; that evil is simply being without god; or that there is evil because the world is corrupted and fallen (from
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Grace" title="Grace">
       grace
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Logical_problem_of_evil">
       Logical problem of evil
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" href="/w/index.php?title=Problem_of_evil&amp;veaction=edit&amp;vesection=1" title="Change section: Logical problem of evil">
        change
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-divider">
        |
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Problem_of_evil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: Logical problem of evil">
        change source
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A version of the problem of evil, perhaps by
      <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">
       Epicurus
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      goes as follows:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       If a perfectly good god exists, then evil does not.
      </li>
      <li>
       There is evil in the world.
      </li>
      <li>
       Therefore, a perfectly good god does not exist.
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Another argument goes:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       God exists.
      </li>
      <li>
       God is all-powerful, all-knowing, and perfectly good.
      </li>
      <li>
       A perfectly good being would want to prevent all evils.
      </li>
      <li>
       An all-knowing being knows every way in which evils can happen.
      </li>
      <li>
       An all-powerful being, who knows every way in which an evil can come into existence, has the power to prevent that evil from happening.
      </li>
      <li>
       A being who knows every way in which an evil can happen, who is able to prevent that evil from happening, and who wants to do so, would prevent it.
      </li>
      <li>
       If there exists an all-powerful, all-knowing, and perfectly good being, then no evil exists.
      </li>
      <li>
       Evil exists (logical contradiction).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IepEvidential_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IepEvidential-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>
     <p>
      Arguments such as these are about the
      <i>
       logical
      </i>
      problem of evil. They attempt to show that the assumed
      <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">
       propositions
      </a>
      lead to a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical" title="Logical">
       logical
      </a>
      <a class="extiw" href="//simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/contradiction" title="wikt:contradiction">
       contradiction
      </a>
      and cannot therefore all be correct.
     </p>
     <p>
      A common response is that God can exist with and allow evil in order to achieve a greater good. Some philosophers accept that arguments such as "God allows evil in order to achieve the greater good of
      <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">
       free will
      </a>
      " are logically possible and thus solve the logical problem of evil.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since the aim is only to defeat the assertion that God and evil are
      <i>
       logically incompatible
      </i>
      , even a highly implausible instance of God's coexistence with evil is sufficient for the purpose.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IepLogical_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-IepLogical-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Philosophies of science have approached the problem from the angle of
      <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">
       empiricism
      </a>
      . For
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logical_positivism" title="Logical positivism">
       logical positivism
      </a>
      the issue with God is the lack of any independent method of
      <a class="extiw" href="//simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/verify" title="wikt:verify">
       verification
      </a>
      . In their view, this makes the proposition "God exists", not true or false, but meaningless.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A similar position points to the lack of any way the proposition might be falsified.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
