     <div class="articleBody" itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="cT-imageLandscape">
       <img alt="Super powers: As well as being a good source of fibre, seaweed also includes  protein, vitamins, minerals such as iron and zinc, antioxidants and omega-3 fats." src="/content/dam/images/3/k/d/0/y/image.related.articleLeadwide.620x349.11mjjw.png/1416142800000.jpg" title=""/>
       <p>
        Super powers: As well as being a good source of fibre, seaweed also includes  protein, vitamins, minerals such as iron and zinc, antioxidants and omega-3 fats.
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- class:cT-imageLandscape/cT-imagePortrait -->
      <p>
       Think of seaweed's health benefits and it's likely that topping up levels of iodine springs to mind. But there are more reasons for eating a nori roll than just iodine, a trace mineral that's important for a healthy thyroid gland but which can also be scarce in our food supply. Besides being a good source of fibre, seaweed is such a useful package of other nutrients  including protein, vitamins, minerals such as iron and zinc, antioxidants and omega-3 fats  that we should include more of it in our diet, says marine scientist Dr Alecia Bellgrove from Deakin University's School of Life and Environmental Sciences.
      </p>
      <p>
       What makes sea vegetables different to terrestrial vegetables is that they grow in an environment that's richer in trace minerals like iodine and zinc  Australian soils, on the other hand, tend to be low in both these nutrients, says Bellgrove who's researching Australia's potential as a future source of sustainable sea vegetables.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Most of the seaweed we eat in Australia comes from Japan, Korea and China and although Australian grown wakame is available from wild harvested seaweed introduced to Tasmania, we have no seaweed farms like those in Asia. But we do have seaweeds that are unique to Australia  some of which would have been part of the traditional diet of Aboriginal people in coastal areas," says Bellgrove whose research has trialled local seaweed from Victorian beaches and found that they passed the taste test when used in miso soup and seaweed salad.
      </p>
      <p>
       Still, it could be a while before Australian native seaweed turns up on the dinner plate.
      </p>
      <div class="ad adAlignRight" id="adspot-300x250-pos3" style="display:none;">
       <small>
        Advertisement
       </small>
       <script>
        titan.requestAd.push("adspot-300x250-pos3");
       </script>
      </div>
      <p>
       "First we need to understand more about the environmental impact of farming seaweed commercially or collecting it from the wild in a way that's sustainable," she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Seaweed may also have advantages beyond nutrition  there are compounds in sea vegetables that have health benefits such as anti-inflammatory and anti-viral properties, Bellgrove adds. One is fucoidan, a carbohydrate found in brown seaweed such as wakame, that's showing promise with viral infections like influenza and herpes as well as inflammatory conditions like ulcerative colitis, says Dr Helen Fitton, Chief Scientist with Marinova, a Tasmanian biotechnology company that makes fucoidan ingredients for nutritional supplements and pharmaceutical products from marine plants.
      </p>
      <p>
       "The best evidence so far is from human studies that have found a benefit for improving symptoms of osteoarthritis and for improving immune function," says Fitton who's speaking about seaweed's role in health and nutrition at the Nutrition Society of Australia's Annual Scientific Meeting later this month.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Fucoidan has also been found to have anti-cancer effects against breast and colon cancer  but this is very early research in lab studies and still has a long way to go. It's been suggested that consumption of fucoidan in seaweed may be one reason for the lower incidence of breast cancer in Japanese women, but we need more research," she says.
      </p>
      <p>
       Can you get too much of a good thing and overdose on seaweed's high iodine content?
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Food Standards Australia New Zealand, a 2010 survey of iodine levels in seaweed showed that most seaweed and seaweed products have safe levels of iodine, although some seaweed, especially kombu, was very high in iodine which can be unsafe for some people including pregnant women and small children.
      </p>
      <p>
       But it's hard to overdose with seaweeds like nori and wakame which tend to be lower in iodine, says Fitton. And while it's possible to get too much iodine from some seaweed, this is unlikely with the amount that most people would eat, adds Bellgrove.
      </p>
      <p>
       "It's a case of everything in moderation, but there's good evidence that Australians should be eating seaweeds more regularly for the health benefits they offer. People with thyroid conditions should consume seaweeds cautiously and under the guidance of a doctor, as they should with any other iodine-rich products. "
      </p>
      <p>
       If you're keen to cook with seaweed at home, she suggests adding soaked wakame to stir fries and soups or making this simple salad:
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Vinegared wakame and cucumber salad
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ingredients:
      </p>
      <p>
       1 continental cucumber (the long, skinny variety of cucumber)
      </p>
      <p>
       Pinch of salt
      </p>
      <p>
       5g dried wakame
      </p>
      <p>
       50ml rice vinegar
      </p>
      <p>
       1 tbsp sugar
      </p>
      <p>
        tbsp light shoyu (Japanese soya sauce)
      </p>
      <p>
        tsp salt
      </p>
      <p>
       Slice cucumber thinly; add a pinch of salt to extract some of the moisture.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rehydrate wakame in water until slightly firm. Drain in a colander; pour boiling water over the wakame while it's still draining.
      </p>
      <p>
       Once fully drained, cut wakame into 3cm lengths.
      </p>
      <p>
       Add vinegar, sugar, shoyu and remaining salt to a small saucepan. Mix over low heat until the sugar and salt dissolve. Cool.
      </p>
      <p>
       Drain salted cucumber by squeezing excess moisture from it by hand.
      </p>
      <p>
       Just before serving mix the cucumber, wakame and vinegar dressing.
      </p>
