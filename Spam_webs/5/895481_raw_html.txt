      <div itemprop="reviewBody">
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <em>
         <strong>
          Between Shades of Gray
         </strong>
        </em>
        <strong>
         by Ruta Sepetys: A Useless Read
         <br/>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Once again, it looks like Ill be giving a well-loved book with generously high ratings one star. I did not like this book. At all. From the moment I reached the end of page 5wherein the last sentence said, We were about to become cigarettes,I knew the story was going to go downhill from there.
       </p>
       <p>
        I was initially overcome with excitement when I first read the synopsis of
        <em>
         Between Shades of Gray
        </em>
        . There are countless works of historical fiction that touch on the subject of Germany and the Holocaust during World War II. The prospect of reading a story about Soviet oppressors in a country that has not had its story told in depth was fascinating, especially considering the fact that the author, Ruta Sepetys, is the daughter of a Lithuanian refugee. Not to mention that my AP European History class had just read about how thousands of eastern Europeans were forced to work in Siberian labor camps. Consequently, I pushed this book up to the top of my to-read shelf.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Plot
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        In 1941, during the Baltic regions annexation to the Soviet Union, a 15-year-old Lithuanian girl named Lina, along with her mother and younger brother, is being deported from their native country and sent to a Siberian labor camp for the alleged crimes her father, who is sentenced to death, has committed. In a struggle to survive, Lina takes dangerous risks by documenting their experiences in hopes of letting her father know that they are alive.
       </p>
       <p>
        Sounds pretty gripping, right? WRONG. This was quite possibly the worst historical fiction book Ive ever read, and Im not just saying that because its written with teen audiences in mind. One thing that really frustrated me was the structure of the actual story. There are 341 pages total, and
        <em>
         85 chapters
        </em>
        . Sepetys spent the first four chapters describing the event in which the Vilkases were thrown out of their home, which couldve easily been condensed into one chapter. Then the author spends the next 18 chapters talking about the train ride. By the time they actually got to Siberia, I was ready to throw in the towel.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: center;">
        <span style="color: #444444;">
         <script async="" src="//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js">
         </script>
         <!-- shakespeare336x280 -->
         <ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-5597071798599191" data-ad-slot="7507859050" style="display:inline-block;width:336px;height:280px">
         </ins>
         <script>
          (adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});
         </script>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        The story itself was one giant apathy fest. It wasnt powerful, deep, or touching. It was shallow, tedious, and incredibly sophomoric. I was simply baffled when I scrolled through some of the raving reviews readers gave this book. Many said that they were grief-stricken and cried their eyes out. When it comes to booksor anything for that matterI cannot properly experience even a flit of emotion if neither the psychology of human behavior nor historical statistics are not alluded with a certain degree of accuracy. In other words:
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <img alt="" class="escapedImg" src="https://p.gr-assets.com/540x540/fit/hostedimages/1401838617/9856350.jpg" style="border: 0px; max-width: 430px; color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Let me give just a few examples of what Im talking about. *WARNING: SPOILER ALERT*
       </p>
       <p>
        Linas brother is very ill with scurvy, and it is generally known that the cure for scurvy is Vitamin-C. Does Lina inform an adult? Does she feed the boy the remnants of what little food they have in an attempt to get some vitamins into his system? Nope! She gives him a pep talk: I grabbed my brother by the shoulders. Jonas, listen to me. We are going to live. Do you hear me? Were going home. Were not going to die. Were going home to our house, and were going to sleep in our beds with the goose-down comforters. We will. All right?'
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <img alt="" class="escapedImg" src="https://p.gr-assets.com/540x540/fit/hostedimages/1401850232/9858229.jpg" style="border: 0px; max-width: 430px; color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        But dont worry! Have no fear, for their fairy godmother is here! Actually, a random doctor arrives just in time to cure the ginuwine scurvified kids because one Soviet officer had a change of heart.
       </p>
       <p>
        Which brings me to my second point. The Soviets werent as cruel as I expected them to be, and Sepetys made them seem incapable of having an actual vocabulary, as the only thing they consistently said throughout the entire novel was 
        <em>
         Davai
        </em>
        !hurry. Okay, maybe they felt a girl up once, or shot a mother whose baby died on the journey, but I honestly think I couldve fulfilled the duty of a Soviet officer more accurately than the lot of them. When you consider the fact that anywhere from 20 million to 60 million people died under Stalins regime, a mere two to three dead at the hands of the NKVD throughout the remainder of this book is just pitiful. For example, the NKVD force the prisoners from their huts during the night, threatening to kill them if they dont adhere to the contract regarding their service on the collective farm. The Lithuanians let their pride get the best of them, and a majority of them staunchly refuse to sign the contract. Instead of enacting a demonstration to incite fear and compliance from the prisoners (i.e. following through with their plans to kill or torture the Lithuanians into submission), the NKVD simply tell them to get back to work, only to repeat the same routine the next day.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <br style="color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
        <img alt="" class="escapedImg" src="https://p.gr-assets.com/540x540/fit/hostedimages/1380222758/541602.gif" style="border: 0px; max-width: 430px; color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        Not to mention that these prisoners are able to repeatedly steal supplies from the Soviet camp, and no one blinks or bats an eye. With as many supplies as they got away with, its a wonder they werent able to build Buckingham Palace.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Characters
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        LINA: If I had to describe Lina in one word, that word would be just what she is: flat. Its understood that when you get to the point where you can no longer split an atom, you have actually formed a black hole. Thats how I would best describe Lina. She was so flat that the whole story collapsed and no rays of light could escape from her giant hole of nothingness. Perhaps she had one flicker of hope that kept her from completely disappearing off the face of the bibliophilic universeshe was in denial and self-absorbed.
       </p>
       <p>
        One aspect of literature that has become quite cliched is this sort of stream-of-consciousness authors use to elicit a passionately emotional response from readers. After being up to my ears in YA fiction for most of my life, Ive decided that it gets old pretty fast, especially when its repetitive: I thought about running, running until I couldnt run anymore. Id run to the university to look for Papa. Id run to our house. Id just run. Yeah, good luck with that. Ill just be sitting by, watching as the rest of your family gets blown to bits because you didnt think about them enough to be compliant for the time being.
       </p>
       <p>
        Which brings me to my final complaint about our self-absorbed protagonist. Now, I know that there are clever people like Katniss and brilliant people like Beetee who use their skills to shake the Capitols authority, but Lina, who doesnt have the sense God gave a billy-goat, seems to think that doodling on handkerchiefs and sketching portraits of people will bring her one step closer to home. Sorry, Lina, but youre only getting colder. (See what I did there?) I blame Linas father. He and Lina miraculously find each other before being separated again, and Papa tells her, Dont cry, Lina. Courage You can help me find you. So Lina gets the bright idea to draw on handkerchiefs and send them in the mail, which is closely monitored by the Soviets, by the way. Need I go on?
       </p>
       <p>
        ANDRIUS: Obligatory love interest. Theres nothing much more to say about him except that all he does is nod. Thats it.
       </p>
       <p>
        MR. STALAS (a.k.a the bald man): If I had to pick a favorite character of this monstrous tale, it would be him, because he was the only character who had a lick of common sense, such as the part when they were all on the train and a womans baby had just died, and the bald man said, What are you crying for? You knew it was going to happen. What was the baby going to eat, lice? Youre all imbeciles. The thing is better off. When I die, if youre smart youll eat me if you all want to survive so badly.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Writing
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        This was my biggest complaint about the book. This book is written for a young adult audience, but the prose has the intellectual capacity of a six-year-old child. The sentences are short and choppy, and the descriptions are vague. When Im reading about Siberia, I want to feel like Im in Siberia. I read most of this book outside in 85-degree weather, and only felt myself roasting even more when Lina attempted to describe Siberia. Yes, I understand that Siberias highest temperatures average to about 63 degrees Fahrenheit in July, but Lina cannot even capture what it feels like during winter. Dont tell me that the storm raged on. DESCRIBE IT.
       </p>
       <p>
        I hate the way the dialogue is set up. The author somehow felt the necessity to include a speaker tag after each persons speech, even if there were only two people speaking, which leads me to my next complaint. Using explicit verbs all the time in your speaker tags is lazy and sloppy. It may be acceptable for childrens books, but unacceptable for young adult fiction. When you write books for older audiences, you have to depict the anguish your characters are going through. You have to show that someone scrunched up his or her face and cried; dont tell me about it. Needless to say, this book has a lot more telling than showing. The writing alone is what made me ultimately not care a bit about what happened to the characters.
       </p>
       <p>
        Example:
       </p>
       <p>
        Hold still! I complained.
        <br/>
        I had an itch, said my father, grinning.
        <br/>
        You did not, youre just trying to make this difficult, I teased.
        <br/>
        Im testing you. Real artists must be able to capture the moment, he said.
        <br/>
        But if you dont hold still, your eyes will be crooked, I said, shading in the side of his face with my pencil.
        <br/>
        Theyre crooked anyway, he said, crossing his eyes. I laughed.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="escapedImg" src="https://p.gr-assets.com/540x540/fit/hostedimages/1396710412/9175790.gif" style="border: 0px; max-width: 430px; color: #181818; font-family: Georgia, serif; font-size: 14px; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: 19.31999969482422px; orphans: auto; text-align: left; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: auto; word-spacing: 0px; -webkit-text-stroke-width: 0px; background-color: #ffffff;"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Conclusion
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        This book was ultimately mind-numbing. It just goes to show that authors having personal connections with the subject on which theyre writing can, in fact, be inversely proportional to the credibility of their story. There was evidently little research done; just some vague, elementary facts about the Soviets, but no sign of knowledgeable life was to be found at all.
       </p>
       <p>
        If you decide that you want to heed my warning in exchange for more meaningful World War II fiction (especially for young adults), I would strongly recommend
        <em>
         The Hiding Place
        </em>
        by Corrie ten Boom,
        <em>
         Anne Frank: The Diary of a Young Girl
        </em>
        , and
        <em>
         Number the Stars
        </em>
        by Lois Lowry.
       </p>

       <p>
        Salvete, omnes!

       <p>
        Thank you for checking out my blog! Herein exists posts and reviews that comprise my bibliophilic nature. Some of my favorite genres include, but are not limited to, speculative fiction, historical fiction, high fantasy, adult dystopian/utopian, Amish fiction, the classics, sci-fi, and yes, Shakespearean fiction, needless to say, procures its own category.
       </p>

        I am also a characteristically spontaneous writer. (You are likely to find neither plot diagrams nor outlines in any of my portfolios.) My most recent project is entitled Red Sky in the Morning.
       </p>

        When I'm not pursuing literary means (which is almost never), you may find me singing, playing the piano, watching PGA tournaments, biking, spending too much time on Tumblr, channeling my inner Vulcanian self, and deliberately avoiding people like the INTJ I've been diagnosed to be.

String.prototype.trim = function() {
  return this.replace(/^\s+|\s+$/g,"");
}

function createCookie(name,value,days) {
  if (days) {
    var date = new Date();
    date.setTime(date.getTime()+(days*24*60*60*1000));
    var expires = "; expires="+date.toGMTString();
  } else {
    var expires = "";
  }
  document.cookie = name+"="+value+expires+"; path=/";
}

function readCookie(name) {
  var nameEQ = name + "=";
  var ca = document.cookie.split(';');
  for(var i=0;i &lt; ca.length;i++) {
    var c = ca[i];
    while (c.charAt(0)==' ') {
      c = c.substring(1,c.length);
    }
    if (c.indexOf(nameEQ) == 0) {
      return c.substring(nameEQ.length,c.length);
    }
  }
  return null;
}

function eraseCookie(name) {
  createCookie(name,"",-1);
}

function addExpandCollapse(id, expandSym, collapseSym, accordion) {
  jQuery('#' + id + ' .expand').live('click', function() {
    if (accordion==1) {
      var theDiv = jQuery(this).parent().parent().find('span.collapse').parent().find('div');
      jQuery(theDiv).hide('normal');
      jQuery(this).parent().parent().find('span.collapse').removeClass('collapse').addClass('expand');
        createCookie(theDiv.attr('id'), 0, 7);
    }
    jQuery('#' + id + ' .expand .sym').html(expandSym);
    expandCat(this, expandSym, collapseSym);
    return false;
  });
  jQuery('#' + id + ' .collapse').live('click', function() {
    collapseCat(this, expandSym, collapseSym);
    return false;
  });
}
