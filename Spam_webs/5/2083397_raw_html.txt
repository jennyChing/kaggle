       <h1>
        Alcohol researchers forecast drinking rates in WA to fall as mining boom ends
       </h1>
       <div class="byline">
        By
        <a href="/news/erin-parke/4421154" target="_self" title="">
         Erin Parke
        </a>
       </div>
       <p class="published">
        Posted
        <span class="timestamp">
         January 25, 2015 14:27:12
        </span>
       </p>
       <div class="attached-content">
        <div class="inline-content photo left">
         <a href="/news/2015-01-25/man-drinking-beer/6045086">
          <img alt="Man drinking beer" height="227" src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/6044540-3x2-340x227.jpg" title="Man drinking beer" width="340"/>
         </a>
         <a class="inline-caption" href="/news/2015-01-25/man-drinking-beer/6045086">
          <strong>
           Photo:
          </strong>
          Broome hotel managers have seen a change in drinking attitudes in recent years.
          <span class="source">
           (ABC News)
          </span>
         </a>
        </div>
        <div class="inline-content map left">
         <div class="story-map">
         </div>
         <a class="inline-caption" href="http://maps.google.com/?q=-31.9234,115.8834(Perth%206000)&amp;z=5">
          <strong>
           Map:
          </strong>
          Perth 6000
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        Alcohol researchers are waiting to see if an economic downturn will force West Australians to sober up.
       </p>
       <p>
        The average Australian drinks around 10 litres of pure alcohol each year, less than they were a decade ago.
       </p>
       <p>
        The decline started in 2008, when the global financial crisis caused people to reduce their spending at bars and bottle shops.
       </p>
       <p>
        But data collected by the National Drug Research Institute shows WA was the exception.
       </p>
       <p>
        West Australians increased their annual consumption from 11.2 litres of alcohol in 2005, to 12.4 litres in 2010.
       </p>
       <p>
        Professor Tanya Chikritzhs said it seemed to be linked to the higher level of disposable income in the west.
       </p>
       <p>
        "WA has been booming with the help of the mining industry, with lots of disposable income around, and we saw no evidence of decline in consumption," she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The economic situation in WA only recently started to decline, and if I were to do some crystal-ball gazing, I'd expect that consumption may start to decline too.
       </p>
       <p>
        "We know there is such a strong relationship between the price of alcoholic beverages and disposable income, and how much people are drinking, so it will be good to keep an eye on that and see if WA does follow the same pattern that the rest of Australia and the world does."
       </p>
       <p>
        The answers will be contained in the next report of the Commonwealth-funded National Alcohol Sales Data Project, due to be released this year.
       </p>
       <h2>
        Drinking 'style' varies from north to south
       </h2>
       <p>
        Monitoring drinking rates in Australia is difficult, because the Australian Bureau of Statistics does not break the statistics down on a state by state basis.
       </p>
       <p>
        Only four states and territories collect detailed alcohol sales figures for the Sales Data Project, so it is hard to pin down where the biggest drinkers are.
       </p>
       <p>
        But the stereotype of the hard-drinking north appears to be correct, with Northern Territory averaging 14 to 15 litres of pure alcohol a year, up 50 per cent on the national average.
       </p>
       <p>
        Queenslanders drink a relatively modest 11 litres per year.
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content quote right">
        <blockquote class="pullquote">
         <div class="quote">
          <p>
           You could draw a line across the upper third, or the upper half of Australia, and you'd find that the upper half drinks more than the southern half.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="cite">
          Professor Tanya Chikritzhs
         </div>
        </blockquote>
       </div>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Professor Chikritzhs said the heat and lifestyle did not appear to drive top-end residents to drink harder and faster.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It's an interesting phenomenon, but that north-south divide is actually quite real," she said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "You could draw a line across the upper third, or the upper half of Australia, and you'd find that the upper half drinks more than the southern half.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Climate could have a fair bit to do with that, and we do notice that beer consumption is higher in the hotter areas.
       </p>
       <p>
        "But I think it's also to do with ingrained drinking styles in those areas.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There are differences in population demographics - certain types of people are attracted to living in those hot, difficult to handle regions of Australia - so it certainly is an interesting phenomenon."
       </p>
       <h2>
        Is the drinking culture of the north changing?
       </h2>
       <p>
        Broome shire president Graeme Campbell said while drinking was part of the relaxed and outdoorsy lifestyle, attitudes were beginning to shift.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Certainly in the north there's a significant amount of partaking of alcohol, because it's warm, it's an outdoor activity, and sporting is associated with drinking, as are a lot of other activities," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "But things are changing. A lot of men you now see choosing to drink mid or light-strength beer, and you don't see as much of the drunk and disorderly behaviour around the licensed venues. More people are choosing to drink at home privately instead."
       </p>
       <p>
        Hotel managers in the Kimberley agreed there had been a shift in attitude in recent years.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mick Wescombe, who runs the Divers Tavern near Broome's Cable Beach, said people no longer took pride in getting themselves rip-roaring drunk.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Since I've been up here I've noticed it's changed a fair bit, especially amongst the Broome locals," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There's a lot of mid-strength drinkers up here now, especially with the tradies, because they seem to be a lot more educated now about drink-driving and so on.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I think the younger generation probably behave a lot better than my generation, keeping in mind I'm only about to turn 30, because they're more educated about the way they can act in a licensed venue."
       </p>
