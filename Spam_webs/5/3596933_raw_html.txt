        <p>
         There I was, quietly reading last weeks
         <a href="http://www.economist.com/">
          Economist
         </a>
         , and I came across
         <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/17680973">
          this article
         </a>
         on UK telephone calling habits, drawing from
         <a href="http://senseable.mit.edu/network/network&amp;society2.html">
          MITs Senseable City Lab research
         </a>
         on the subject.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/confusedofcalcutta.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/20101211_brm968.gif">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2366" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/confusedofcalcutta.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/20101211_brm968.gif?resize=290%2C354" title="20101211_brm968"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Its core finding?
         <em>
          Calling habits tend to reflect cultural/political boundaries.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         While Id been aware of the study, I hadnt seen this particular representation of the data. And it reminded me of something else Id read about, and written about, a few years ago. There was a paper titled
         <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/0806.1256">
          Understanding Individual Human Mobility Patterns
         </a>
         , by Gonzalez, Hidalgo and Barabasi (yes, he of Linked and Bursts fame).
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://i1.wp.com/confusedofcalcutta.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/2010-12-14_1942.png">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2367" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i1.wp.com/confusedofcalcutta.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/2010-12-14_1942.png?resize=446%2C422" title="2010-12-14_1942"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Its core finding?
         <em>
          People were creatures of habit when it came to making mobile phone calls
         </em>
         . Often from the same place, at the same time, to the same people, for the same duration.
        </p>
        <p>
         A little while later, a bbc.co.uk headline caught my eye.
         <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-11989723">
          Facebook connections map the world
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         <a href="http://i0.wp.com/confusedofcalcutta.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/50421293_facebook_conn_image_976x462.gif">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2368" data-recalc-dims="1" src="http://i0.wp.com/confusedofcalcutta.com/wp-content/uploads/2010/12/50421293_facebook_conn_image_976x462.gif?resize=410%2C194" title="_50421293_facebook_conn_image_976x462"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         Apparently an intern had been working at Facebook, trying to map friendship pairs relative to cities of residence. The core finding?
         <em>
          The friend graph network looked a lot like the political map of the world
         </em>
         , with a few glaring exceptions.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         When it comes to communications, were as predictable as can be. Were creatures of habit. We tend to speak to the same people. Many of these people have similar cultural, geographical or political backgrounds to us.
        </p>
        <p style="text-align: left;">
         Thats enough, I hear you say, mumbling scatological questions about ursines in forested areas. Which is all fine, but given recent events to do with secrets and leaks, I felt it was worth making a few points here:
        </p>
        <ul>
         <li>
          1. If you really want to keep something completely secret, dont tell anyone.
         </li>
         <li>
          2. If you do want to (or sometimes even
          <em>
           have
          </em>
          to) tell someone, then only tell people you trust.
         </li>
         <li>
          3. Which means that if you do want to keep a secret online, you must have a way of making sure that all the people youre telling are people you trust.
         </li>
         <li>
          4. Which in turn means that you have to have a way of figuring out who those people are and whether you trust them or not.
         </li>
         <li>
          5. The more people you tell, the harder it will be to make sure of these things.
         </li>
        </ul>
        <p>
         Or, as
         <a href="http://www.schneier.com/about.html">
          Bruce Schneier
         </a>
         so elegantly put it,
         <strong>
          access control is hard
         </strong>
         . [If you havent done so already, read his article on
         <a href="http://www.schneier.com/blog/archives/2009/09/real-world_acce.html">
          Real-World Access Control
         </a>
         from just over a year ago.]
        </p>
        <p>
         Trust is a key component of secrets, and for that matter a critical component of privacy as well. When my daughter friends me on Facebook, she trusts me not to delve into her wall and posts and conversations and photographs. She trusts me to respect her privacy. Even though she gives me the right to invade it.
        </p>
        <p>
         This is nothing new. There are drawers in my bedroom I dont open, theyre my wifes things. There are drawers in our childrens rooms that we dont open, theyre their things. And theyre private. If a guest should sleep in one of our bedrooms, we dont expect that guest to be looking into cupboards and drawers.
        </p>
        <p>
         Respecting privacy is common courtesy amongst friends. We trust each other.
        </p>
        <p>
         When you trust someone with a secret, you give that person the right to betray you. Trust, like faith, grows only when exercised.
        </p>
        <p>
         The very concept of bankruptcy comes from a breach of trust, and the consequences of breaching trust. The word bank itself comes from the word banco, meaning bench. Lombardy merchants, the early bankers, gathered together and transacted business while seated on benches. Your word was your bond. You were trusted. And if you breached that trust, they sent the heavy mob after you. Who dumped you unceremoniously off the bench, then, just to make sure you understood what had happened, they broke the bench in half, for good measure. So the banco was rutto. Bankrupt.
        </p>
        <p>
         Privacy and confidentiality and secrecy have always been about trust.
        </p>
        <p>
         And yet trust is about one other thing.
        </p>
        <p>
         Transparency.
        </p>
        <p>
         Bilateral transparency. Multilateral transparency. And sometimes universal transparency.
        </p>
        <p>
         We have to be careful. Some of the events that have happened, they happened because of poor design (too many people trusted) poor implementation (too many things to be kept secret) and poor behaviour (too many indiscreet actions).
        </p>
        <p>
         The events did not happen because trust broke down; the events did not happen because transparency is wrong. Trust models will continue to emerge, will continue to evolve, and we will continue to learn about how to scale them.
        </p>
        <p>
         We have to be careful. Because there will be a backlash against trust-based models, there will be a backlash against transparency. If we dont do anything, there will be a reversion to the evils of lobbying and briefing, behaviours designed to break trust down even further. You know the types, they exist in every government, every firm, every department of any scale. Whispering behind the scenes. Wielding the power of corruption. The corruption of power.
        </p>
        <p>
         And this world will be poorer as a result.
        </p>

           <p>
            Pedants Corner: Our word bank probably comes from banca, meaning bank. Its reasonable to assume this has a common origin with banco, bench. Its not clear whether the specialised meaning of banca to mean a merchants counter happened while the Lombard merchants were still speaking a pidgin of Latin &amp; Old High German or after Italian became a fairly homogeneous language.
           </p>
           <p>
            My somewhat tedious point being this: bank ultimately comes from the Germanic word bank (meaning bank as in a river bank) which was then used by them to mean something they *made* that had the same affordance (i.e. you could sit on it with legs downwards). The Lombards adopted this same word for both the sitting arrangement (banco, bench) and the counter (banca) which has the other affordance in common with a bank: you can put things on it at a convenient height.
           </p>
           <p>
            Phew. And sorry.

          <div class="comment-content">
           <p>
            On a more substantive point: you characterise trust as being transparent with more than a specific confidence (giving access to private drawers and trusting that that access will not be abused). Clearly this is different to trust as defined by a Non-Disclosure Agreement, for instance, since that covers a specific set of information.
           </p>
           <p>
            Are you saying this broad transparency is a necessary part of trust to be trust, or simply that trust relationships are unmanageable if defined too specifically?
           </p>
