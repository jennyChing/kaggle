   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    High-voltage cable
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_voltage_cable&amp;redirect=no" title="High voltage cable">
       High voltage cable
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       high-voltage cable
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       HV cable
      </b>
      ) is a cable used for
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
       electric power transmission
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/High_voltage" title="High voltage">
       high voltage
      </a>
      . A cable includes a conductor and insulation, and is suitable for being run underground or underwater. This is in contrast to a
      <i>
       conductor
      </i>
      , which does not have insulation. High-voltage cables of differing types have a variety of applications in instruments, ignition systems, and AC and
      <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
       DC
      </a>
      power transmission. In all applications, the insulation of the cable must not deteriorate due to the high-voltage stress, ozone produced by electric discharges in air, or tracking. The cable system must prevent contact of the high-voltage conductor with other objects or persons, and must contain and control leakage current. Cable joints and terminals must be designed to control the high-voltage stress to prevent breakdown of the insulation. Often a high-voltage cable will have a metallic shield layer over the insulation, connected to the ground and designed to equalize the dielectric stress on the insulation layer.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="926" data-file-width="1300" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG/220px-Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG/330px-Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG/440px-Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hochspannungskabel_110kV_400kV.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Segments of high-voltage cables
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      High-voltage cables may be any length, with relatively short cables used in apparatus, longer cables run within buildings or as buried cables in an industrial plant or for power distribution, and the longest cables often run as
      <a href="/wiki/Submarine_power_cable" title="Submarine power cable">
       submarine cables
      </a>
      under the ocean for power transmission.
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">
       Construction
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1166" data-file-width="1260" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG/220px-Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG/330px-Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG/440px-Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hochspannungskabel_400kV_Querschnitt.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A cross-section through a 400kV cable, showing the stranded segmented copper conductor in the center, semiconducting and insulating layers, copper shield conductors, aluminum sheath and plastic outer jacket
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Like other
      <a href="/wiki/Power_cable" title="Power cable">
       power cables
      </a>
      , high-voltage cables have the structural elements of one or more conductors, insulation, and a protective jacket. High-voltage cables differ from lower-voltage cables in that they have additional internal layers in the insulation jacket to control the electric field around the conductor.
     </p>
     <p>
      For circuits operating at or above 2,000 volts between conductors, a conductive shield may surround each insulated conductor. This equalizes electrical stress on the cable insulation. This technique was patented by Martin Hochstadter in 1916;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-underground_systems_reference_book_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-underground_systems_reference_book-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the shield is sometimes called a Hochstadter shield. The individual conductor shields of a cable are connected to the ground at the ends of the shield, and at splices. Stress relief cones are applied at the shield ends.
     </p>
     <p>
      Cables for power distribution of 10kV or higher may be insulated with oil and paper, and are run in a rigid steel pipe, semi-rigid aluminum or lead sheath. For higher voltages the oil may be kept under pressure to prevent formation of voids that would allow
      <a href="/wiki/Partial_discharge" title="Partial discharge">
       partial discharges
      </a>
      within the cable insulation.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Ziani_de_Ferranti" title="Sebastian Ziani de Ferranti">
       Sebastian Ziani de Ferranti
      </a>
      was the first to demonstrate in 1887 that carefully dried and prepared paper could form satisfactory cable insulation at 11,000 volts. Previously paper-insulated cable had only been applied for low-voltage telegraph and telephone circuits. An extruded lead sheath over the paper cable was required to ensure that the paper remained absolutely dry.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulcanized_rubber" title="Vulcanized rubber">
       Vulcanized rubber
      </a>
      was patented by
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Goodyear" title="Charles Goodyear">
       Charles Goodyear
      </a>
      in 1844, but it was not applied to cable insulation until the 1880s, when it was used for lighting circuits.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-underground_systems_reference_book_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-underground_systems_reference_book-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Rubber-insulated cable was used for 11,000 volt circuits in 1897 installed for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls_Power_Generation" title="Niagara Falls Power Generation">
       Niagara Falls Power Generation
      </a>
      project.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mass-impregnated paper-insulated medium voltage cables were commercially practical by 1895. During
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      several varieties of synthetic rubber and
      <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">
       polyethylene
      </a>
      insulation were applied to cables.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-black-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Modern high-voltage cables use polymers or polyethylene, including (XLPE) for insulation.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="AC_power_cable">
       AC power cable
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: AC power cable">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/High_voltage" title="High voltage">
       High voltage
      </a>
      is defined as any voltage over 1000 volts. Cables for 3000 and 6000 volts exist, but the majority of cables are used from 10kV and upward.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Those of 10 to 33kV are usually called
      <i>
       medium voltage
      </i>
      cables, those over 50kV
      <i>
       high voltage
      </i>
      cables.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HV_Cable.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="491" data-file-width="1890" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/HV_Cable.jpg/300px-HV_Cable.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/HV_Cable.jpg/450px-HV_Cable.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/HV_Cable.jpg/600px-HV_Cable.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HV_Cable.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 1, cross section of a high-voltage cable, (1) conductor (3) insulation (2) and (4) semiconducting layers (5) outer conductor and outer coat.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Modern HV cables have a simple design consisting of few parts. A conductor of
      <a href="/wiki/Copper_wire_and_cable" title="Copper wire and cable">
       copper
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">
       aluminum
      </a>
      wires transports the current, see (1) in figure 1. (
      <i>
       For a detailed discussion on
       <a href="/wiki/Copper_wire_and_cable" title="Copper wire and cable">
        copper cables
       </a>
       , see main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Copper_wire_and_cable" title="Copper wire and cable">
        Copper wire and cable
       </a>
       .
      </i>
      )
     </p>
     <p>
      Conductor sections up to 2000mm
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      may transport currents up to 2000 amperes. The individual strands are often preshaped to provide a smoother overall circumference. The
      <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">
       insulation
      </a>
      (3) may consist of
      <a href="/wiki/Cross-linked_polyethylene" title="Cross-linked polyethylene">
       cross-linked polyethylene
      </a>
      , also called XLPE. It is reasonably flexible and tolerates operating temperatures up to 120C. EPDM is also an insulation.
     </p>
     <p>
      At the inner (2) and outer (4) sides of this insulation, semi-conducting layers are fused to the insulation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The function of these layers is to prevent air-filled cavities between the metal conductors and the dielectric so that little
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_discharge" title="Electric discharge">
       electric discharges
      </a>
      cannot arise and endanger the insulation material.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <br/>
      The outer conductor or sheath (5) serves as an earthed layer and will conduct leakage currents if needed.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most high-voltage cables for power transmission that are currently sold on the market are insulated by a sheath of cross-linked
      <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">
       polyethylene
      </a>
      (XLPE). Some cables may have a lead or aluminium jacket in conjunction with XLPE insulation to allow for fiber optics. Before 1960, underground power cables were insulated with oil and paper and ran in a rigid steel pipe, or a semi-rigid aluminium or lead jacket or sheath. The oil was kept under pressure to prevent formation of voids that would allow
      <a href="/wiki/Partial_discharge" title="Partial discharge">
       partial discharges
      </a>
      within the cable insulation. There are still many of these oil-and-paper insulated cables in use worldwide. Between 1960 and 1990, polymers became more widely used at distribution voltages, mostly
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPDM" title="EPDM">
       EPDM
      </a>
      (ethylene propylene diene M-class); however, their relative unreliability, particularly early XLPE, resulted in a slow uptake at transmission voltages. While cables of 330kV are commonly constructed using XLPE, this has occurred only in recent decades.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quality">
       Quality
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quality">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the development of HV insulation, which has taken about half a century, two characteristics proved to be paramount. First, the introduction of the semiconducting layers. These layers must be absolutely smooth, without even protrusions as small as a few
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="m">
       m
      </a>
      . Further the fusion between the insulation and these layers must be absolute;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      any fission, air-pocket or other defect - of the same micro-dimensions as above - is detrimental for the breakdown characteristics of the cable.
     </p>
     <p>
      Secondly, the insulation must be free of inclusions, cavities or other defects of the same sort of size. Any defect of these types shortens the voltage life of the cable which is supposed to be in the order of 30 years or more.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cooperation between cable-makers and manufacturers of materials has resulted in grades of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XLPE" title="XLPE">
       XLPE
      </a>
      with tight specifications. Most producers of XLPE-compound specify an "extra clean" grade where the number and size of foreign particles are guaranteed. Packing the raw material and unloading it within a
      <a href="/wiki/Cleanroom" title="Cleanroom">
       cleanroom
      </a>
      environment in the cable-making machines is required. The development of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extruder" title="Extruder">
       extruders
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Plastics_extrusion" title="Plastics extrusion">
       plastics extrusion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Cross-link" title="Cross-link">
       cross-linking
      </a>
      has resulted in cable-making installations for making defect-free and pure insulations. The final quality control test is an elevated voltage 50 or 60Hz partial discharge test with very high sensitivity (in the range of 5 to 10 picoCoulombs) This test is performed on every reel of cable before it is shipped.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Extruder_section.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="572" data-file-width="1024" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Extruder_section.jpg/220px-Extruder_section.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Extruder_section.jpg/330px-Extruder_section.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Extruder_section.jpg/440px-Extruder_section.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Extruder_section.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An extruder machine for making insulated cable
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="HVDC_cable">
       HVDC cable
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: HVDC cable">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      A high-voltage cable for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HVDC" title="HVDC">
       HVDC
      </a>
      transmission has the same construction as the AC cable shown in figure 1. The physics and the test-requirements are different.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this case the smoothness of the semiconducting layers (2) and (4) is of utmost importance. Cleanliness of the insulation remains imperative.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many HVDC cables are used for DC
      <a href="/wiki/Underwater" title="Underwater">
       submarine
      </a>
      connections, because at distances over 30km AC can no longer be used. The longest submarine cable today is the
      <a href="/wiki/NorNed" title="NorNed">
       NorNed
      </a>
      cable between Norway and Holland that is almost 600km long and transports 700 megawatts, a capacity equal to a large power station.
      <br/>
      Most of these long deep-sea cables are made in an older construction, using oil-impregnated paper as an insulator.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cable_terminals">
       Cable terminals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cable terminals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Field_concentration.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="602" data-file-width="380" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Field_concentration.jpg/120px-Field_concentration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Field_concentration.jpg/180px-Field_concentration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Field_concentration.jpg/240px-Field_concentration.jpg 2x" width="120"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Field_concentration.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 2, the earth shield of a cable (0%) is cut off, the equipotential lines (from 20% to 80%) concentrate at the edge of the earth electrode, causing danger of breakdown.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Terminals of high-voltage cables must manage the
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">
       electric fields
      </a>
      at the ends.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Without such a construction the electric field will concentrate at the end of the earth-conductor as shown in figure 2.
      <br/>
      <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
       Equipotential lines
      </a>
      are shown here which can be compared with the
      <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
       contour lines
      </a>
      on a map of a mountainous region: the nearer these lines are to each other, the steeper the slope and the greater the danger, in this case the danger of an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_breakdown" title="Electric breakdown">
       electric breakdown
      </a>
      . The equipotential lines can also be compared with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">
       isobars
      </a>
      on a weather map: the denser the lines, the more wind and the greater the danger of damage.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HV_Terminal.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1182" data-file-width="945" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/HV_Terminal.jpg/220px-HV_Terminal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/HV_Terminal.jpg/330px-HV_Terminal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/HV_Terminal.jpg/440px-HV_Terminal.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HV_Terminal.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Figure 3, a rubber or elastomer body
        <b>
         R
        </b>
        is pushed over the insulation (blue) of the cable. The equipotential lines between
        <b>
         HV
        </b>
        (high voltage) and
        <b>
         earth
        </b>
        are evenly spread out by the shape of the earth electrode. Field concentrations are prevented in this way.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In order to control the equipotential lines (that is to control the electric field) a device is used that is called a
      <b>
       stress-cone
      </b>
      , see figure 3.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The crux of stress relief is to flare the shield end along a logarithmic curve. Before 1960, the stress cones were handmade using tapeafter the cable was installed. These were protected by
      <a href="/wiki/Pothead" title="Pothead">
       potheads
      </a>
      , so named because a potting compound/ dielectric was poured around the tape inside a metal/ porcelain body insulators. About 1960, preformed terminations were developed consisting of a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">
       rubber
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">
       elastomer
      </a>
      body that is stretched over the cable end.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On this rubber-like body
      <b>
       R
      </b>
      an shield electrode is applied that spreads the equipotential lines to guarantee a low electric field.
     </p>
     <p>
      The crux of this device, invented by NKF in
      <a href="/wiki/Delft" title="Delft">
       Delft
      </a>
      in 1964,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is that the
      <a href="/wiki/Boring_(manufacturing)" title="Boring (manufacturing)">
       bore
      </a>
      of the elastic body is narrower than the diameter of the cable. In this way the (blue) interface between cable and stress-cone is brought under mechanical pressure so that no cavities or air-pockets can be formed between cable and cone. Electric breakdown in this region is prevented in this way.
     </p>
     <p>
      This construction can further be surrounded by a
      <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">
       porcelain
      </a>
      or silicone insulator for outdoor use,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or by contraptions to enter the cable into a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_transformer" title="Power transformer">
       power transformer
      </a>
      under oil, or
      <a href="/wiki/Switchgear" title="Switchgear">
       switchgear
      </a>
      under gas-pressure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cable_joints">
       Cable joints
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cable joints">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Connecting two high-voltage cables with one another poses two main problems. First, the outer conducting layers in both cables shall be terminated without causing a field concentration,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      similar as with the making of a cable terminal. Secondly, a field free space shall be created where the cut-down cable insulation and the connector of the two conductors safely can be accommodated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These problems have been solved by NKF in
      <a href="/wiki/Delft" title="Delft">
       Delft
      </a>
      in 1965
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      by introducing a device called
      <b>
       bi-manchet
      </b>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HV_Joint.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="678" data-file-width="2132" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/HV_Joint.jpg/300px-HV_Joint.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/HV_Joint.jpg/450px-HV_Joint.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/HV_Joint.jpg/600px-HV_Joint.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HV_Joint.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Photograph of a section of a high-voltage joint,
        <b>
         bi-manchet
        </b>
        , with a high-voltage cable mounted at the right hand side of the device.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Figure 4 shows a photograph of the cross-section of such a device. At one side of this photograph the contours of a high-voltage cable are drawn. Here
      <b>
       red
      </b>
      represents the conductor of that cable and
      <b>
       blue
      </b>
      the insulation of the cable. The black parts in this picture are semi-conducting rubber parts. The outer one is at earth potential and spreads the electric field in a similar way as in a cable terminal. The inner one is at high-voltage and shields the connector of the conductors from the electric field.
      <br/>
      The field itself is diverted as shown in figure 5, where the equipotential lines are smoothly directed from the inside of the cable to the outer part of the bi-manchet (and vice versa at the other side of the device).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Field_in_joint.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1173" data-file-width="1753" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Field_in_joint.jpg/220px-Field_in_joint.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Field_in_joint.jpg/330px-Field_in_joint.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Field_in_joint.jpg/440px-Field_in_joint.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Field_in_joint.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Field distribution in a
        <b>
         bi-manchet
        </b>
        or HV joint.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The crux of the matter is here, like in the cable terminal, that the inner bore of this bi-manchet is chosen smaller than the diameter over the cable-insulation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In this way a permanent pressure is created between the bi-manchet and the cable surface and cavities or electrical weak points are avoided.
     </p>
     <p>
      Installing a terminal or bi-manchet is skilled work. Removing the outer semiconducting layer at the end of the cables, placing the field-controlling bodies, connecting the conductors, etc., require skill, cleanness and precision.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="X-ray_cable">
       X-ray cable
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: X-ray cable">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      X-ray cables
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      are used in lengths of several meters to connect the HV source with an
      <a href="/wiki/X-ray_tube" title="X-ray tube">
       X-ray tube
      </a>
      or any other HV device in scientific equipment. They transmit small currents, in the order of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliampere" title="Milliampere">
       milliamperes
      </a>
      at
      <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">
       DC
      </a>
      voltages of 30 to 200kV, or sometimes higher. The cables are flexible, with rubber or other
      <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">
       elastomer
      </a>
      insulation, stranded conductors, and an outer sheath of braided copper-wire. The construction has the same elements as other HV power cables.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_of_high-voltage_cables">
       Testing of high-voltage cables
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-voltage_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Testing of high-voltage cables">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are different causes for faulty cable insulations. Hence, there are various test and measurement methods to prove fully functional cables or to detect faulty ones. One needs to distinguish between
      <b>
       cable testing
      </b>
      and
      <b>
       cable diagnosis
      </b>
      . While cable testing methods result in a go/no go statement cable diagnosis methods allow judgement of the cables current condition. In some cases it is even possible to locate the position of the fault in the insulation. One of the favorite testing methods is
      <a href="/wiki/VLF_cable_testing" title="VLF cable testing">
       VLF cable testing
      </a>
      . Using a very low frequency voltage with frequencies in the range of 0.1 to 0.01Hz protects the device under test from deteriorating due to the test itself, as it used to be with DC testing methods in the older days. Depending on the sort of treeing in the insulation two cable diagnostics methods are common.
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_treeing#Water_tree" title="Electrical treeing">
       Water trees
      </a>
      can be detected by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tan_delta_measurement" title="Tan delta measurement">
       tan delta measurement
      </a>
      . Interpretation of measurement results yield the possibility to distinguish between new, strongly aged and faulty cables and appropriate maintenance and repair measures may be planned. Damages to the insulation and
      <a href="/wiki/Electrical_treeing" title="Electrical treeing">
       electrical treeing
      </a>
      may be detected and located by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_discharge_measurement" title="Partial discharge measurement">
       partial discharge measurement
      </a>
      . Data collected during the measurement procedure is compared to measurement values of the same cable gathered during the acceptance-test. This allows simple and quick classification of the dielectric condition of the tested cable.
     </p>
