         <div class="entry-content">
          <p>
           PHOENIX  Science has always brought exciting things to Dr. Mae Carol Jemison.
          </p>
          <p>
           Both a chemical engineer and an M.D., Jemison was the first African-American woman to travel in space. She served on the Space Shuttle Endeavour crew that left Earth back on Sept. 12, 1992, and remained in orbit for almost eight days.
          </p>
          <p>
           For Jemison, who was 35, the space mission encompassed all that she loved  knowledge, space and science.
          </p>
          <p>
           It was a love that shed had since she was a young child and had pondered the theories and concepts of infinity.
          </p>
          <p>
           I also remember the Civil Rights movement and the National Guard marching outside my home following the riots of 1968, after Martin Luther King was assassinated, she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           I followed the moon landing. I just loved it and I danced. I looked at space exploration, and all around me, I saw this explosion of ideas and possibilities.
          </p>
          <p>
           Born in Decatur, Ala., Jemison moved to Chicago with her parents when she was 3. Her mother was an English and math teacher, and her father was a maintenance supervisor.
          </p>
          <p>
           I was raised by parents who recognized the importance of imagination and moving things forward. I had the pleasure and the honor and the privilege to be with school teachers who would let me challenge and push the boundaries, she said.
          </p>
          <p>
           Graduating from high school and entering Stanford University at the age of 16, she received her Bachelors of Science degree in chemical engineering. She also completed African and Afro-American studies.
          </p>
          <p>
           She earned her Doctor of Medicine degree in 1981 from Cornell Medical College.
          </p>
          <p>
           This capable person first worked as a general practitioner, and then entered the Peace Corps, working in Liberia and Sierra Leone from 1983-1985.
          </p>
          <p>
           In 1986, she was accepted into NASA.
          </p>
          <p>
           Going into space was something that she always knew she could accomplish.
          </p>
          <p>
           Basically, children will live up or down to our expectations, she said. We have to expect children to achieve.
          </p>
          <p>
           Her mission in 1992, along with six other astronauts, focused on dozens of microgravity experiments that required tending at all times.
          </p>
          <p>
           Their work produced information about biotechnology, fluid dynamics and materials like glass, ceramics and metals.
          </p>
          <p>
           They also brought along test subjects for research  themselves, as well as Japanese koi, animal and plant cells, chicken embryos, insects, fungi and plant seeds.
          </p>
          <p>
           Jemison returned from space determined to making science accessible and exciting for young people. She resigned from NASA in 1993 and started a company, the Jemison Group, to study how social sciences interact with technology.
          </p>
          <p>
           She also started a foundation that sponsored a program called The Earth We Share. At this four-week international science camp, teenagers use science to work on practical issues  such as designing homes or developing protein supplies for the world.
          </p>
          <p>
           She encourages young people to use their powers for good, not evil  to use peer pressure to entice their friends to study harder, learn more and get involved in helping others.
          </p>
          <p>
           As part of her goal of inspiring young people to study science, Jemison is also the current principal of the 100 Year Starship program. This program seeks to ensure that all capabilities required for human interstellar travel are realities within 100 years. Its also designed to spark the imagination of students and others for continued research and the study of technology and exploration of space.
          </p>
          <p>
           Since 1995, she has also served as the national spokesperson for the Bayer CropScience, Making Science Make Sense program. This initiative advances science literacy throughout the United States through hands-on, inquiry-based science learning.
          </p>
          <p>
           Through this program, dedicated Bayer CropScience employees volunteer to boost student knowledge in their communities through activity-based experiences.
          </p>
          <p>
           There is the whole idea of experiencebecause so frequently, when we think about how people learn things, we forget that humans basically learn through experience, she said. The best way to learn is through hands-on experiments.
          </p>
          <p>
           Speaking at the 10th annual Bayer CropScience Ag Issues Forum, Jemison pointed out all of the connections between agriculture and the space program. Global positioning systems, weather satellites and remote sensors are used today in agriculture and were developed through the space program.
          </p>
          <p>
           If we are looking back at tornadoes, weather, those are things we are very interested in finding out about, she said. We can look at it and understand more about weather emergence when you look at it from the vantage point of space.
          </p>
          <p>
           In all, NASAs space shuttles flew 135 missions. Each flight increased the scientific knowledge of Earth and space.
          </p>
          <p>
           Today, astronauts fly to the International Space Station in Russian Soyuz spacecraft.
          </p>
          <p>
           Tomorrow, the Space Launch System could take astronauts to an asteroid or to Mars. Then its on to the bright star Alpha Centauri that is 4 light years away.
          </p>
          <p>
           Jemison told of an African proverb, If you wait for tomorrow, tomorrow comes. If you dont wait for tomorrow, tomorrow comes. This means that preparing, studying, learning science, experiencing science and setting goals are important activities as they take you to another place tomorrow.
          </p>
          <p>
           We are actually where we are creating the future, and we have to think about it, she said. The more comfortable we all feel with science, the more likely we are to share it, to have high expectations.
          </p>
