  <meta content="As I have mentioned in previous blog posts, I have been blessed with an 
abundant milk supply. There are many factors that affect a mothers milk 
supply. My personal opinion/observations (not medical by any means) on how 
I maintain this incredible milk supply are as follows:

1) I pump often. As I have learned, life is unpredictable. As clich 
as this sounds, tomorrow is not guaranteed; therefore, I began stock piling 
breast milk in the freezer for our little one immediately. I find the 
morning and evening the easiest to do this, as I am always home. My 
routine goes as follows: I feed on one breast, pump the other; the pumped 
milk either makes a bottle for the days outing or goes in a freezer bag. 
On an average day, after feeding Savannah I am able to put about 10 oz. of 
milk in the freezer. This way, in case I am not able to breastfeed for as 
long as my boyfriend and I desire, Savannah will have breast milk to drink.

2) I drink tons of water. I find when I am dehydrated, my milk 
production slows down. It gets hard to make sure you are drinking enough 
water with a newborn around that requires all your attention. The trick I 
found to scheduling water intake is to drink as much water as I can each 
time I feed Savannah; this insures I am drinking multiple times a day.

3) It Works! Its Vital Core Nutrition  after I finished my prenatal

currently going through the application to start donating/selling my breast 
milk to a milk bank. I am still researching and learning all the details, 
but stay tuned for a future blog post on this process! 

Storage Capacity Information  storage capacity is the amount of milk a 
breast can store between feedings. A womans storage capacity is not 
determined by her breast size (although breast size can limit the amount of 
milk that can be stored). Storage capacity ranges widely from mom to mom, 
even between breasts for a mom. A mom with a larger milk storage capacity 
can go longer in-between feedings; a mom with limited storage capacity must 
nurse her baby more frequently to maintain her milk supply (a full breast = 
slower milk production, an empty breast = faster milk production).

          <h1 class="post-title" data-content-field="title">
           <a href="/teenyzebrablog/2014/8/8/breast-milk-production">
            Breast Milk Production
           </a>
          </h1>
         </div>
         <div class="body">
          <div class="sqs-layout sqs-grid-12 columns-12" data-layout-label="Post Body" data-type="item" data-updated-on="1407552021022" id="item-53e589fae4b09a55555b84bb">
           <div class="row sqs-row">
            <div class="col sqs-col-12 span-12">
             <div class="sqs-block html-block sqs-block-html" data-block-type="2" id="block-e80e30de2f485001d6ec">
              <div class="sqs-block-content">
               <p>
                As I have mentioned in previous blog posts, I have been blessed with an abundant milk supply. There are many factors that affect a mothers milk supply. My personal opinion/observations (not medical by any means) on how I maintain this incredible milk supply are as follows:
               </p>
               <p>
                1)
                <strong>
                 I pump often
                </strong>
                . As I have learned, life is unpredictable. As clich as this sounds, tomorrow is not guaranteed; therefore, I began stock piling breast milk in the freezer for our little one immediately. I find the morning and evening the easiest to do this, as I am always home. My routine goes as follows: I feed on one breast, pump the other; the pumped milk either makes a bottle for the days outing or goes in a freezer bag. On an average day, after feeding Savannah I am able to put about 10 oz. of milk in the freezer. This way, in case I am not able to breastfeed for as long as my boyfriend and I desire, Savannah will have breast milk to drink.
               </p>
               <p>
                2)
                <strong>
                 I drink tons of water
                </strong>
                . I find when I am dehydrated, my milk production slows down. It gets hard to make sure you are drinking enough water with a newborn around that requires all your attention. The trick I found to scheduling water intake is to drink as much water as I can each time I feed Savannah; this insures I am drinking multiple times a day.
               </p>
               <p>
                3)
                <a href="http://savannahswraps.myitworks.com/shop/product/305/">
                 It Works! Its Vital Core Nutrition
                </a>
                 after I finished my prenatal vitamins, around three weeks after giving birth, I switched my vitamins to the
                <a href="http://savannahswraps.myitworks.com/shop/product/305/">
                 It Works! Its Vital Core Nutrition
                </a>
                . This plant-based, whole-food multivitamin supplies the essential antioxidants, phytonutrients, and vitamins you need throughout the day with controlled-release technology, giving you sustained nourishment for up to 12 hours. Its core nutrition for your best health! Just as vitamins change the color of your urine, this vitamin slightly tints the color of my breast milk as well!
               </p>
               <p>
                In only one month, I have about 100 oz.of frozen breast milk. I am currently going through the application to start donating/selling my breast milk to a milk bank. I am still researching and learning all the details, but stay tuned for a future blog post on this process!
               </p>
               <p>
                <strong>
                 Storage Capacity Information
                </strong>
                 storage capacity is the amount of milk a breast can store between feedings. A womans storage capacity is not determined by her breast size (although breast size can limit the amount of milk that can be stored). Storage capacity ranges widely from mom to mom, even between breasts for a mom. A mom with a larger milk storage capacity can go longer in-between feedings; a mom with limited storage capacity must nurse her baby more frequently to maintain her milk supply (a full breast = slower milk production, an empty breast = faster milk production).
               </p>
