   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="hatnote">
      This article is about the term.  For other uses, see
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Yankee_(disambiguation)" title="Yankee (disambiguation)">
       Yankee (disambiguation)
      </a>
      .
     </div>
     <p>
      The term "
      <b>
       Yankee
      </b>
      " and its contracted form "
      <b>
       Yank
      </b>
      " have several interrelated meanings, all referring to people from the United States. Its various senses depend on the scope of context. Most broadly:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Outside the United States, "Yank" is used informally to refer to any American, including Southerners.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         1
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Within the United States, it usually refers to people from the north, largely those from the
       <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">
        northeast
       </a>
       , but especially those with
       <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
        New England
       </a>
       cultural ties, such as descendants from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonial_New_England" title="Colonial New England">
        colonial New England
       </a>
       settlers, wherever they live.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruth_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ruth-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its sense is more cultural than literally geographic. The
       <a href="/wiki/New_England_English" title="New England English">
        speech dialect of New England
       </a>
       is called "Yankee" or "Yankee dialect."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         3
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Within
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">
        Southern American English
       </a>
       , "Yankee" refers to
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_United_States" title="Northern United States">
        Northerners
       </a>
       , or those from the regions of the Union side of the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
        American Civil War
       </a>
       .
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The informal
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=British_and_Irish_English&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="British and Irish English (page does not exist)">
       British and Irish English
      </a>
      "Yank" is especially popular among Britons and Australians and sometimes carries pejorative overtones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Southern American English "Yank" is typically uncontracted and at least mildly pejorative, although less vehemently so as time passes from the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_history_of_the_word">
       Origins and history of the word
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and history of the word">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boston1775.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="484" data-file-width="592" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Boston1775.jpg/350px-Boston1775.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Boston1775.jpg/525px-Boston1775.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/85/Boston1775.jpg 2x" width="350"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boston1775.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Loyalist newspaper cartoon from Boston ridicules "Yankie Doodles" militia who have encircled the British forces inside the city
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_usage">
       Early usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The origin of the term is uncertain. In 1758, British General
      <a href="/wiki/James_Wolfe" title="James Wolfe">
       James Wolfe
      </a>
      made the earliest recorded use of the word Yankee to refer to people from what was to become the United States, referring to the New England soldiers under his command as Yankees: "I can afford you two companies of Yankees, and the more because they are better for ranging and scouting than either work or vigilance".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mathews_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mathews-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Later British use of the word often was derogatory, as in a cartoon of 1775 ridiculing "Yankee" soldiers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mathews_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mathews-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      New Englanders themselves employed the word in a neutral sense: the "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennamite-Yankee_War" title="Pennamite-Yankee War">
       Pennamite-Yankee War
      </a>
      ", for example, was the name given to a series of clashes in 1769 over land titles in Pennsylvania, in which the "Yankees" were the claimants from Connecticut.
     </p>
     <p>
      The meaning of
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      has varied over time. In the 18th century, it referred to residents of New England descended from the original English settlers of the region.
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">
       Mark Twain
      </a>
      , in the following century, used the word in this sense in his novel
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Connecticut_Yankee_in_King_Arthur%27s_Court" title="A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court">
        A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court
       </a>
      </i>
      , published in 1889. As early as the 1770s, British people applied the term to any person from what became the United States. In the 19th century, Americans in the
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">
       southern United States
      </a>
      employed the word in reference to Americans from the
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_United_States" title="Northern United States">
       northern United States
      </a>
      (though not to recent emigrants from Europe; thus a visitor to
      <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">
       Richmond, Virginia
      </a>
      , in 1818 commented, "The enterprising people are mostly strangers; Scots, Irish, and especially New England men, or Yankees, as they are called").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rejected_theories_of_a_Native_American_origin">
       Rejected theories of a Native American origin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rejected theories of a Native American origin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Many faulty etymologies have been devised for the word, including one by a British officer in 1789 who said it derived from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">
       Cherokee
      </a>
      word
      <i>
       eankke
      </i>
      ("coward"), but no such word exists in the
      <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_language" title="Cherokee language">
       Cherokee
      </a>
      language.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MW_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-MW-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Etymologies purporting an origin in languages of the
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       aboriginal inhabitants of the United States
      </a>
      are not well received by linguists. One such surmises that the word is borrowed from the
      <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">
       Wyandot
      </a>
      (called Huron by the French) pronunciation of the French
      <i>
       l'anglais
      </i>
      (meaning "the Englishman" or "the English language"), sounded as
      <i>
       Y'an-gee
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MW_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-MW-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Linguists, however, do not support any Native American origins.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MW_7-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-MW-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      James Fenimore Cooper, the writer of the classic tale "The Deerslayer" made a
      <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">
       non-fiction
      </a>
      footnote that claimed, "There can be little doubt that the sobriquet of "Yankees" is derived from "Yengees," the manner in which the tribes nearest to New England pronounced the word "English." The change from "Eng-lish" to "Yengees" is very trifling." Despite his being a prominent writer from the early 1800's, and comparatively close to when the word originated, his version of the word origin is rejected by professional linguists writing for the Merriam-Webster book of word histories.
     </p>

       </span>

     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">

     <p>
      Most linguists look to Dutch sources, noting the extensive interaction between the colonial Dutch in
      <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">
       New Netherland
      </a>
      (now largely
      <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">
       New York
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">
       New Jersey
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">
       Delaware
      </a>
      , and western
      <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">
       Connecticut
      </a>
      ) and the colonial English in
      <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
       New England
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">
       Massachusetts
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">
       Rhode Island
      </a>
      , and eastern
      <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">
       Connecticut
      </a>
      ). The
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
       Dutch
      </a>
      given names
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_(given_name)" title="Jan (given name)">
        Jan
       </a>
      </i>
      ("John") and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Kees_(given_name)" title="Kees (given name)">
        Kees
       </a>
      </i>
      ("Cornelius") were and still are common and the two sometimes are combined in a single name, e.g.,
      <a href="/wiki/Jan_Kees_de_Jager" title="Jan Kees de Jager">
       Jan Kees de Jager
      </a>
      . The word
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      is a variation that could have referred to the Dutch Americans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MW_7-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-MW-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, as Americans of Dutch descent rejected the term as being derogatory, Americans in New England embraced it and adopted it for themselves.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Quinion" title="Michael Quinion">
       Michael Quinion
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Hanks" title="Patrick Hanks">
       Patrick Hanks
      </a>
      argue that the term refers to the Dutch girls name
      <i>
       Janneke
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-posh_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-posh-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      or
      <i>
       Janke
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yanky_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-yanky-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which  owing to the
      <a href="/wiki/Dutch_orthography" title="Dutch orthography">
       Dutch pronunciation
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/J" title="J">
        J
       </a>
      </i>
      as the
      <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">
       English
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Y" title="Y">
       Y
      </a>
       would be
      <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">
       Anglicized
      </a>
      as "Yankee". Quinion and Hanks posit it was "used as a nickname for a Dutch-speaking American in colonial times" and could have grown to include non-Dutch colonists as well.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-posh_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-posh-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/H._L._Mencken" title="H. L. Mencken">
       H. L. Mencken
      </a>
      derived it from the slur "John Cheese", applied by the English colonists to the Dutch  "Here comes a John Cheese"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
       owing to the importance of their dairy cultivation, which introduced the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holstein_cattle#United_States" title="Holstein cattle">
       black-and-white dairy cow
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">
       Friesland
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/North_Holland" title="North Holland">
       North Holland
      </a>
      to America in the mid-1600s. The modern Dutch for John Cheese is
      <i>
       Jan Kaas
      </i>
      but this would be spoken
      <i>
       Jan Kees
      </i>
      in some dialects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yanky_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-yanky-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There is also the Dutch "jonkheer," a term applied to the younger sons of the nobility, who themselves bear no title. It may be translated as "young gentleman" or "esquire".
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_uses">
       Historic uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historic uses">

        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_usage">
       Canadian usage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canadian usage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      An early use of the term outside the United States was in the creation of
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Slick" title="Sam Slick">
       Sam Slick
      </a>
      , the "Yankee Clockmaker", in a column in a newspaper in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" title="City of Halifax">
       Halifax
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">
       Nova Scotia
      </a>
      , Canada, in 1835. The character was a plain-talking American who served to poke fun at Nova Scotian customs of that era, while, initially, trying to urge the old-fashioned Canadians to be as clever and hard-working as Yankees. The character, developed by
      <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Chandler_Haliburton" title="Thomas Chandler Haliburton">
       Thomas Chandler Haliburton
      </a>
      , evolved over the years between 1836 and 1844 in a series of publications.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCB_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCB-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Damn_Yankee">
       Damn Yankee
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Damn Yankee">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       damned Yankee
      </i>
      usage dates from 1812.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mathews_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Mathews-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      During and after the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      (186165) Confederates popularized it as a derogatory term for their Northern enemies. In an old joke, a Southerner alleges, "I was twenty-one years old before I learned that 'damn' and 'Yankee' were separate words". In fact, the spelling "damnyankee" is not uncommon.
     </p>
     <p>
      It became a catch phrase, often used humorously for Yankees visiting the South, as in the mystery novel,
      <i>
       Death of a Damn Yankee: A Laura Fleming Mystery
      </i>
      (2001) by
      <a href="/wiki/Toni_Kelner" title="Toni Kelner">
       Toni Kelner
      </a>
      . Another popular although facetious saying is that "a Yankee is someone from the North who visits the South. A damn Yankee is one who moves here."
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Yankee_Doodle">
       Yankee Doodle
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Yankee Doodle">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Perhaps the most pervasive influence on the use of the term throughout the years has been the song "
      <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Doodle" title="Yankee Doodle">
       Yankee Doodle
      </a>
      ", which was popular during the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">
       American Revolutionary War
      </a>
      (177583) as, following the
      <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">
       battles of Lexington and Concord
      </a>
      , it was broadly adopted by American rebels. Today, "Yankee Doodle" is the official
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._state_songs" title="List of U.S. state songs">
       state song
      </a>
      of Connecticut.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Yankee_cultural_history">
       Yankee cultural history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Yankee cultural history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      now may mean any resident of New England or of any of the
      <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">
       Northeastern United States
      </a>
      . The original Yankees diffused widely across the northern United States, leaving their imprints in New York, the
      <a href="/wiki/Upper_Midwest" title="Upper Midwest">
       Upper Midwest
      </a>
      , and places as far away as
      <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">
       Seattle
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
       San Francisco
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">
       Honolulu
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Yankees typically lived in villages consisting of clusters of separate farms. Often they were merchants, bankers, teachers or professionals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Village life fostered local democracy, best exemplified by the open
      <a href="/wiki/Town_meeting" title="Town meeting">
       town meeting
      </a>
      form of government which still exists today in rural New England. Village life also stimulated mutual oversight of moral behavior, and emphasized civic virtue. From the New England seaports of
      <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">
       Boston
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Massachusetts" title="Salem, Massachusetts">
       Salem
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">
       Providence
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/New_London,_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">
       New London
      </a>
      , among others, the Yankees built international trade routes, stretching to China by 1800. Much of the profit from trading was reinvested in the textile and machine tools industries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Stereotypes">
       Stereotypes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stereotypes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Yankee_ingenuity" title="Yankee ingenuity">
       Yankee ingenuity
      </a>
      was a worldwide stereotype of inventiveness, technical solutions to practical problems, "know-how," self-reliance and individual enterprise.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The stereotype first appeared in the 19th century. As Mitchell Wilson notes, "Yankee ingenuity and Yankee git-up-and-go did not exist in colonial days."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The peculiar Yankee became a stock character in standardized comedic venues, especially the widely popular humor magazine,
      <i>
       Yankee Notions,
      </i>
      published in New York City in the years leading up to the
      <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
       American Civil War
      </a>
      . As developed in the humor literature of the English-speaking world, the visceral stereotype of the greedy, witch-burning Yankee was epitomized in the character of
      <a href="/wiki/Brother_Jonathan" title="Brother Jonathan">
       Brother Jonathan
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">
       Burlesque
      </a>
      s, or comedic performances by Yankee impersonators, dominated popular theater in the 1800s. The Yankee, as an irksome, meddling, and purer-than-thou peddler was a theme appearing in
      <a href="/wiki/American_literature" title="American literature">
       American literature
      </a>
      written by
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">
       Washington Irving
      </a>
      (critical of his character
      <a href="/wiki/Ichabod_Crane" title="Ichabod Crane">
       Ichabod Crane
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/James_Fenimore_Cooper" title="James Fenimore Cooper">
       James Fenimore Cooper
      </a>
      (particularly in his
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Chainbearer" title="The Chainbearer">
        The Chainbearer; Or, The Littlepage Manuscript
       </a>
      </i>
      series), and
      <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hawthorne" title="Nathaniel Hawthorne">
       Nathaniel Hawthorne
      </a>
      (author of the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scarlet_Letter" title="Scarlet Letter">
        Scarlet Letter
       </a>
      </i>
      ), a
      <a href="/wiki/Copperhead_(politics)" title="Copperhead (politics)">
       copperhead
      </a>
      who sought more Christian compassion for sinners and violators of civil laws. In defense, the New Englander embraced the insult, "Yankee". Although the great majority of Yankees gravitated toward the burgeoning cities of the American Northeast, wealthy New Englanders also sent ambassadors to frontier communities, where they became influential bankers and newspaper printers. Using their influence in positive ways, they introduced the term, "Universal Yankee Nation" to represent and proselytize their hopes for national and global influence.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
       Religion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Religion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In religion, New England Yankees originally followed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">
       Puritan
      </a>
      tradition, as expressed in
      <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">
       Congregational churches
      </a>
      ; beginning in the late colonial period, however, many became Presbyterians,
      <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">
       Episcopalians
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">
       Methodists
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/American_Baptist_Churches_USA" title="American Baptist Churches USA">
       Baptists
      </a>
      , or, later,
      <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">
       Unitarians
      </a>
      . Strait-laced 17th-century moralism as described by novelist
      <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hawthorne" title="Nathaniel Hawthorne">
       Nathaniel Hawthorne
      </a>
      faded in the 18th century. The
      <a href="/wiki/First_Great_Awakening" title="First Great Awakening">
       First Great Awakening
      </a>
      (under
      <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Edwards_(theologian)" title="Jonathan Edwards (theologian)">
       Jonathan Edwards
      </a>
      and others) in the mid-18th century and the
      <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Awakening" title="Second Great Awakening">
       Second Great Awakening
      </a>
      in the early 19th century (under
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grandison_Finney" title="Charles Grandison Finney">
       Charles Grandison Finney
      </a>
      , among others) emphasized personal piety, revivals, and devotion to civic duty. Theologically,
      <a href="/wiki/Arminianism" title="Arminianism">
       Arminianism
      </a>
      replaced the original
      <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">
       Calvinism
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Horace_Bushnell" title="Horace Bushnell">
       Horace Bushnell
      </a>
      introduced the idea of Christian nurture, through which children would be brought to religion without revivals.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_reform">
       Politics and reform
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Politics and reform">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      After 1800, Yankees spearheaded most reform movements, including those for abolition of slavery, temperance in use of alcohol, increase in women's political rights, and improvement in women's education.
      <a href="/wiki/Emma_Willard" title="Emma Willard">
       Emma Willard
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lyon" title="Mary Lyon">
       Mary Lyon
      </a>
      pioneered in the higher education of women, while Yankees comprised most of the reformers who went South during
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era_of_the_United_States" title="Reconstruction era of the United States">
       Reconstruction
      </a>
      in the late 1860s to educate the
      <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">
       Freedmen
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Historian John Buenker has examined the worldview of the Yankee settlers in the Midwest:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Because they arrived first and had a strong sense of community and mission, Yankees were able to transplant New England institutions, values, and mores, altered only by the conditions of frontier life. They established a public culture that emphasized the work ethic, the sanctity of private property, individual responsibility, faith in residential and social mobility, practicality, piety, public order and decorum, reverence for public education, activists, honest, and frugal government, town meeting democracy, and he believed that there was a public interest that transcends particular and stick ambitions. Regarding themselves as the elect and just in a world rife with sin, air, and corruption, they felt a strong moral obligation to define and enforce standards of community and personal behavior. This pietistic worldview was substantially shared by British, Scandinavian, Swiss, English-Canadian and Dutch Reformed immigrants, as well as by German Protestants and many of the Forty-Eighters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         21
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Politically, Yankees, who dominated New England, much of
      <a href="/wiki/Upstate_New_York" title="Upstate New York">
       upstate New York
      </a>
      , and much of the upper Midwest, were the strongest supporters of the new Republican party in the 1860s. This was especially true for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregationalists" title="Congregationalists">
       Congregationalists
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">
       Presbyterians
      </a>
      among them and (after 1860), the Methodists. A study of 65 predominantly Yankee counties showed that they voted only 40% for the
      <a href="/wiki/Whig_Party_(United_States)" title="Whig Party (United States)">
       Whigs
      </a>
      in 1848 and 1852, but became 6165%
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="History of the Republican Party (United States)">
       Republican
      </a>
      in presidential elections of 1856 through 1864.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">
       Ivy League
      </a>
      universities, particularly
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_Harvard_University" title="History of Harvard University">
       Harvard
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">
       Yale
      </a>
      , as well as "
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Ivies" title="Little Ivies">
       Little Ivy
      </a>
      "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">
       liberal arts
      </a>
      colleges, remained bastions of old Yankee culture until well after
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_Yankees">
       Presidential Yankees
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Presidential Yankees">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      President
      <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">
       Calvin Coolidge
      </a>
      was a striking example of the Yankee stereotype. Coolidge moved from rural
      <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">
       Vermont
      </a>
      to urban
      <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">
       Massachusetts
      </a>
      and was educated at elite
      <a href="/wiki/Amherst_College" title="Amherst College">
       Amherst College
      </a>
      . Yet his flint-faced, unprepossessing ways and terse rural speech proved politically attractive: "That Yankee twang will be worth a hundred thousand votes", explained one Republican leader.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Coolidge's laconic ways and dry humor were characteristic of stereotypical rural "Yankee humor" at the turn of the 20th century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The fictional
      <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
       Harvard
      </a>
      graduate
      <a href="/wiki/Thurston_Howell,_III" title="Thurston Howell, III">
       Thurston Howell III
      </a>
      of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Gilligan%27s_Island" title="Gilligan's Island">
        Gilligan's Island
       </a>
      </i>
      typifies the old Yankee elite in a comical way.
     </p>
     <p>
      By the beginning of the 21st century, systematic Yankee ways had permeated the entire society through education. Although many observers from the 1880s onward predicted that Yankee politicians would be no match for new generations of ethnic politicians, the presence of Yankees at the top tier of modern American politics was typified by presidents
      <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
       George H. W. Bush
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">
       George W. Bush
      </a>
      , and former
      <a href="/wiki/Democratic_National_Committee" title="Democratic National Committee">
       Democratic National Committee
      </a>
      chairman
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Dean" title="Howard Dean">
       Howard Dean
      </a>
      (as well as the losing 2004 Democratic presidential nominee
      <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">
       John Forbes Kerry
      </a>
      , descendant through his mother, of the Scottish
      <a href="/wiki/Forbes_family" title="Forbes family">
       Forbes family
      </a>
      , which emigrated to Massachusetts the 1750s). President
      <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">
       Barack Obama
      </a>
      is of Yankee descent
      <a href="/wiki/Ann_Dunham" title="Ann Dunham">
       on his mother's side
      </a>
      ; his high school was
      <a href="/wiki/Punahou_School" title="Punahou School">
       Punahou School
      </a>
      , founded to serve the children of Yankee missionaries to
      <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
       Hawaii
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_uses">
       Contemporary uses
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Contemporary uses">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_United_States">
       In the United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In the United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Within the United States, the term
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      can have many different contextually and geographically dependent meanings.
     </p>
     <p>
      Traditionally,
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      was most often used to refer to a New Englander descended from the original settlers of the region (thus often suggesting Puritanism and thrifty values).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the mid-20th century, some speakers applied the word to any American born north of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mason%E2%80%93Dixon_Line" title="MasonDixon Line">
       MasonDixon Line
      </a>
      , though usually with a specific focus still on New England.
      <i>
       New England Yankee
      </i>
      might be used to differentiate.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, within New England itself, the term still refers more specifically to old-stock New Englanders of English descent. The term "
      <a href="/wiki/White_Anglo-Saxon_Protestant" title="White Anglo-Saxon Protestant">
       WASP
      </a>
      ", in use since the 1960s, refers to all Protestants of English ancestry, including both Yankees and Southerners, though its meaning is often extended to refer to any Protestant
      <a href="/wiki/European_American" title="European American">
       white American
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">
       Major League Baseball
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">
       New York Yankees
      </a>
      acquired the name from journalists after it moved from Baltimore in 1903 (though they were officially known as the Highlanders until 1913). The regional
      <a href="/wiki/Yankees%E2%80%93Red_Sox_rivalry" title="YankeesRed Sox rivalry">
       sports rivalry
      </a>
      between the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">
       New York Yankees
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">
       Boston Red Sox
      </a>
      can make the utterance of the "Yankee" term unwelcome to some fans in
      <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
       New England
      </a>
      , especially to
      <a href="/wiki/Red_Sox_Nation" title="Red Sox Nation">
       the most dedicated Red Sox fans
      </a>
      living in the northeastern United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Swamp_Yankee" title="Swamp Yankee">
        Swamp Yankee
       </a>
      </i>
      is sometimes used in rural
      <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">
       Rhode Island
      </a>
      , Connecticut, and southeastern Massachusetts to refer to Protestant farmers of moderate means and their descendants (in contrast to richer or urban Yankees); "swamp Yankee" is often regarded as a derogatory term.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruth_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ruth-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scholars note that the famous Yankee "twang" survives mainly in the hill towns of interior New England, though it is disappearing even there.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The most characteristic Yankee food was pie; Yankee author
      <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">
       Harriet Beecher Stowe
      </a>
      in her novel
      <i>
       Oldtown Folks
      </i>
      celebrated the social traditions surrounding the Yankee pie.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the southern United States, the term is sometimes used in derisive reference to any Northerner, especially one who has migrated to the South; a more polite term is
      <i>
       Northerner
      </i>
      . Senator
      <a href="/wiki/J._William_Fulbright" title="J. William Fulbright">
       J. William Fulbright
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">
       Arkansas
      </a>
      pointed out as late as 1966, "The very word 'Yankee' still wakens in Southern minds historical memories of defeat and humiliation, of the
      <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Campaign" title="Atlanta Campaign">
       burning of Atlanta
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Sherman%27s_March_to_the_Sea" title="Sherman's March to the Sea">
       Sherman's march to the sea
      </a>
      , or of an ancestral farmhouse burned by
      <a href="/wiki/Quantrill%27s_Raiders" title="Quantrill's Raiders">
       Cantrill's raiders
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Bierce" title="Ambrose Bierce">
       Ambrose Bierce
      </a>
      defines the term in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Devil%27s_Dictionary" title="The Devil's Dictionary">
        The Devil's Dictionary
       </a>
      </i>
      as:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       "n. In Europe, an American. In the Northern States of our Union, a New Englander. In the Southern States the word is unknown. (See DAMNYANK.)"
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      A humorous
      <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">
       aphorism
      </a>
      attributed to
      <a href="/wiki/E._B._White" title="E. B. White">
       E. B. White
      </a>
      summarizes the following distinctions:
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="says who? (January 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <i>
          To foreigners, a Yankee is an
          <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">
           American
          </a>
          .
         </i>
        </dd>
        <dd>
         <i>
          To Americans, a Yankee is a
          <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">
           Northerner
          </a>
          .
         </i>
        </dd>
        <dd>
         <i>
          To Northerners, a Yankee is an
          <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">
           Easterner
          </a>
          .
         </i>
        </dd>
        <dd>
         <i>
          To Easterners, a Yankee is a
          <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
           New Englander
          </a>
          .
         </i>
        </dd>
        <dd>
         <i>
          To New Englanders, a Yankee is a
          <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">
           Vermonter
          </a>
          .
         </i>
        </dd>
        <dd>
         <i>
          And in Vermont, a Yankee is somebody who eats pie for breakfast.
         </i>
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Another variant of the aphorism replaces the last line with: "To a Vermonter, a Yankee is somebody who still uses an
      <a href="/wiki/Outhouse" title="Outhouse">
       outhouse
      </a>
      ". There are several other
      <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">
       folk
      </a>
      and humorous
      <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
       etymologies
      </a>
      for the term.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of Mark Twain's novels,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Connecticut_Yankee_in_King_Arthur%27s_Court" title="A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court">
        A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court
       </a>
      </i>
      , popularized the word as a nickname for residents of Connecticut. Appropriately, the State of Connecticut's Air National Guard unit,
      <a href="/wiki/103d_Airlift_Wing" title="103d Airlift Wing">
       103d Airlift Wing
      </a>
      , is nicknamed "The Flying Yankees"
     </p>
     <p>
      A 1950 film about
      <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes,_Jr." title="Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr.">
       Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr.
      </a>
      , is called
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Magnificent_Yankee_(1950_film)" title="The Magnificent Yankee (1950 film)">
        The Magnificent Yankee
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The title of the 1955 musical
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Damn_Yankees" title="Damn Yankees">
        Damn Yankees
       </a>
      </i>
      refers specifically to the
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">
       New York Yankees
      </a>
      baseball team but also echoes the older cultural term. Similarly, a book about the ball club echoes the title of the Holmes film:
      <i>
       The Magnificent Yankees
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_English-speaking_countries">
       In other English-speaking countries
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In other English-speaking countries">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In English-speaking countries outside the United States, especially in
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       Britain
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">
       Ireland
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
       New Zealand
      </a>
      , Yankee, almost universally shortened to
      <i>
       Yank
      </i>
      , is used as a derogatory, playful, or
      <a href="/wiki/Colloquialism" title="Colloquialism">
       colloquial
      </a>
      term for Americans. "Look at Me, I'm Wonderful" by the British Bonzo Dog Band refers to a boastful celeb as driving a "Yankee car".
     </p>
     <p>
      In the aforesaid Western
      <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
       Commonwealth countries
      </a>
      , especially Britain, Canada, Australia and New Zealand,
      <i>
       Yank
      </i>
      has been in common use since at least World War II, when hundreds of thousands of Americans were stationed in Britain, Australia and New Zealand. Depending on the country,
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      may be considered mildly derogatory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Cockney
      <a href="/wiki/Rhyming_slang" title="Rhyming slang">
       rhyming slang
      </a>
      a Yank is a Septic or Seppo (as in "Septic Tank").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_parts_of_the_world">
       In other parts of the world
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yankee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In other parts of the world">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In some parts of the world, particularly in
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
       Latin American
      </a>
      countries and in
      <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">
       East Asia
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       yankee
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       yanqui
      </i>
      (the same word spelled according to Spanish
      <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
       orthography
      </a>
      ) is sometimes associated with
      <a href="/wiki/Anti-Americanism" title="Anti-Americanism">
       anti-Americanism
      </a>
      and used in expressions such as "Yankee go home" or "we struggle against the yanqui, enemy of mankind" (words from the
      <a href="/wiki/Sandinista_National_Liberation_Front" title="Sandinista National Liberation Front">
       Sandinista
      </a>
      anthem). In
      <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
       Spain
      </a>
      , however, just as in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
       United Kingdom
      </a>
      or other English-speaking countries, the term (
      <i>
       yanqui
      </i>
      in Spanish spelling) is simply used to refer to someone from the United States, whether colloquially, playfully or derogatively, with no particular emphasis on the latter use. This can also be the case in many countries of
      <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
       Latin America
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
       Venezuelan Spanish
      </a>
      there is the word
      <i>
       pitiyanqui
      </i>
      , derived c.1940 around the oil industry from
      <i>
       petty yankee
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       petit yanqui
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a derogatory term for those who profess an exaggerated and often ridiculous admiration for anything from the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the late 19th century, the
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japanese
      </a>
      were called "the Yankees of the East" in praise of their industriousness and drive to modernization.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      since the late 1970s, the term
      <i>
       Yank
      </i>
      has been used to refer to a type of delinquent youth.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine-American_War" title="Philippine-American War">
       Philippine-American War
      </a>
      , Filipinos referred to Americans as "yanquis".
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
       Finland
      </a>
      , the word
      <i>
       jenkki
      </i>
      (yank) is sometimes used to refer to any U.S. citizen, and with the same group of people
      <i>
       Jenkkil
      </i>
      (Yankeeland) refers to the United States itself. It is not considered offensive or anti-U.S., but rather a
      <a href="/wiki/Spoken_language" title="Spoken language">
       colloquial
      </a>
      expression.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, more commonly a U.S. citizen is called
      <i>
       amerikkalainen
      </i>
      ("American") or
      <i>
       yhdysvaltalainen
      </i>
      ("United Statesian") and the country itself
      <i>
       Amerikka
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       Yhdysvallat
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The variant
      <i>
       Yankee Air Pirate
      </i>
      was used during the
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">
       North Vietnamese
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">
       propaganda
      </a>
      to refer to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">
       United States Air Force
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
       Iceland
      </a>
      , the word
      <i>
       kani
      </i>
      is used for Yankee or Yank in the mildly derogatory sense. When referring to residents of the United States,
      <i>
       norurrkjamaur
      </i>
      , or more commonly
      <i>
       bandarkjamaur
      </i>
      , is used.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
       Polish
      </a>
      , the word
      <i>
       jankes
      </i>
      can refer to any U.S. citizen, has little
      <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">
       pejorative
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Connotation" title="Connotation">
       connotation
      </a>
      if at all, and its use is somewhat obscure (it is mainly used to translate the English word
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      in a less formal context, like in
      <a href="/wiki/Category:American_Civil_War_films" title="Category:American Civil War films">
       Civil War movies
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
       Sweden
      </a>
      the word is translated to
      <i>
       jnkare
      </i>
      . The word is not itself a negative expression, though it can of course be used as such depending on context. When a Swedish person uses the word
      <i>
       jnkare
      </i>
      , it usually refers to cars from the United States, but could also be used as a slang term for any U.S. citizen.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
       Hungarian
      </a>
      the word "jenki" (pronounced like "Yenkie") is a slang term to Americans in general (or white American individuals) and carries no pejorative connotation.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      , an ice cream chain with the name
      <i>
       Yankee Doodle
      </i>
      was popular during the 1980s.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Indonesia, the term
      <a href="/wiki/Jengki_style" title="Jengki style">
       Jengki style
      </a>
      (local spelling of "Yankee") is a term for post-war Modernist houses popular in Indonesia. It is so called because of its heavy influence from the American
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-century_modern" title="Mid-century modern">
       Mid-century modern
      </a>
      architecture.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Slocum" title="Joshua Slocum">
       Joshua Slocum
      </a>
      , in his 1899 book
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Sailing_Alone_Around_the_World" title="Sailing Alone Around the World">
        Sailing Alone Around the World
       </a>
      </i>
      refers to
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_from_Nova_Scotia" title="List of people from Nova Scotia">
       Nova Scotians
      </a>
      as being the only or true Yankees. It thus may be implied, as he himself was a Nova Scotian, that he had pride in his ancestry.
      <i>
       Yankee
      </i>
      in this instance, instead of connoting a form of derision, is therefore a form of praise; perhaps relevant to the hardy seagoing people of the East Coast at that time.
     </p>
     <p>
      Yankee is the code word for the letter "Y" in the
      <a href="/wiki/NATO_phonetic_alphabet" title="NATO phonetic alphabet">
       NATO phonetic alphabet
      </a>
      .
     </p>
