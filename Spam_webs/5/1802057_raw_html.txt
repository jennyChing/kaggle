         September 15, 2008

        <div class="entry" id="entry-55641512">
         <h3 class="entry-header">
          Thirteen
         </h3>
         <div class="entry-content">
          <div class="entry-body">
           <p>
            <a href="http://svmomblog.typepad.com/photos/uncategorized/2008/09/15/1.jpg">
             <img alt="1" border="0" height="149" src="http://svmomblog.typepad.com/nyc_moms/images/2008/09/15/1.jpg" style="margin: 0px 5px 5px 0px; float: left;" title="1" width="149"/>
            </a>
            I have seen what Thirteen looks like...and it ain't good.
           </p>
           <p>
            Notice I didn't say "it ain't pretty." Because it is pretty. And stylish, and made up, and poised and texting and TERRIFYING. See, I went to my niece's Bat Mitzvah yesterday, and I saw the future.
           </p>
           <p>
            When I was thirteen, lo, those many years ago, thirteen was awkward and gawky with a mouth full of braces. Now, kids get braces when they're eight. (I should know, with twins, I'm paying for BOTH of my eight year olds at once, right now. Ouch.) They're done with the metal mouth thing by nine or ten. What with Hannah Montana as a roll model, they're into fashion by the time they're eleven, wearing make-up by twelve. And by thirteen, well, they've already developed the poise, style, and skill with a make-up brush that would make
            <a href="http://tlc.discovery.com/fansites/whatnottowear/whatnottowear.html">
             Stacey and Clinton
            </a>
            proud.
           </p>
           <p>
            The thing is - -it all kind of creeps me out.
           </p>
          </div>
          <a id="more">
          </a>
          <div class="entry-more">
           <p>
            I know kids are maturing faster. I know they're more exposed. But something about seeing a thirteen year old, suburban, seventh grade girl perfectly at ease in a skin-tight ruched dress, lipstick, and high heels just doesn't seem right. (My own niece, I'd like to note, was dressed completely appropriately in a floral full-skirted dress with a royal blue bolero sweater, no make-up, and flats.)
           </p>
           <p>
            I'm wondering what else these girls are more advanced at. Is this just a sophistication-facade and underneath they're still innocent? I hope so, but I somehow doubt it. There have been rumors here in NY, that at a certain suburban junior high, the kids needed chaperon's on the buses that took the kids from the synagogues where the Bar Mitzvah Services took place, to the restaurants where the parties took place, because of a rash of certain
            <a href="http://www.urbandictionary.com/define.php?term=rainbow%20party&amp;defid=1003777">
             girl-on-boy oral activity
            </a>
            going on in the back seats. And I'm not talking about kissing. (I'm also not saying this doesn't happen in the city - just that the rumors where about a school in the 'burbs.) Yikes. When I was that age, I hadn't even had a "real" kiss.
           </p>
           <p>
            Even if these grown-up styled thirteen year olds aren't overly sexually active (because, call me crazy, I think oral sex at thirteen is a BIT premature.) their outfits and looks put them at risk. Men who see them posing as sophisticated mini-adults might treat them as such. And they're just not ready. They couldn't possibly be. I know, I know, in the Middle Ages, girls got married at twelve. They also died by the time they were forty.
           </p>
           <p>
            Maybe all of this is bothering me so much, because I see changes in my own daughter. She's eight, and she still plays "baby" with her dolls, puts them in strollers, and
pushes them around the house. She still happily wears pigtails. She
still wants to sit in my lap. But she's starting to show signs of serious 'tude. Flicking her head
like a
            <a href="http://tv.disney.go.com/disneychannel/originalmovies/cheetahgirls/franchise/index.html">
             Cheetah Girl
            </a>
            , rolling her eyes like a teenager,
getting exasperated in a very grown-up "how can you NOT understand
this" way, when I don't understand what she's talking about. She's turning into a "tween" right
before my eyes. It's freaking me out.
           </p>
           <p>
            I know I can't stave off her growing up and moving on. But does it have to start at eight? I want her to hold on to the wonder of childhood a bit longer. The joy of it that certainly disappears once Junior High begins. If what I saw this weekend is what's coming...well, it
            <em>
             ain't
            </em>
            pretty.
           </p>
           <p>
            All this worrying makes me feel so disconnected. Like I'm some 1950's parent bemoaning the advent of Rock n Roll. I'm only a heart-beat away from saying things like "kids today!" while I pull on my reading glasses and have a cup of tea in a cardigan with my cat in my lap.
           </p>
           <p>
            But still. It seems to me I had two babies only a minute ago. Do they they really have to be grown up so soon?
           </p>
           <p>
            <em>
             This is an original post to NYC Moms blog. Nancy also write and rants about life and motherhood after forty and her twenty year quest to lose the same ten pounds at
             <a href="http://agelessbodytimelessmom.wordpress.com">
              agelessbodytimelessmom.com
             </a>
             .
            </em>
           </p>
