       <h2 class="node-title">
        <a href="/article/touring-brazils-favelas">
         Touring Brazil's Favelas
        </a>
       </h2>
       <span class="rdf-meta element-hidden" content="Touring Brazil's Favelas" property="dc:title">
       </span>
       <span class="rdf-meta element-hidden" content="0" datatype="xsd:integer" property="sioc:num_replies">
       </span>

      <span class="print-link">
      </span>

       <div class="field-items">
        <div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded">
         <p>
          Miniscule, pigmented little rectangles and squares everywhere. A random collage or even a puzzle may be  whatever your mind perceives it to be.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          But if you zoom in and magnify those tiny shapes before your eyes, youll see that theres more to them  theyre homes, with people! Dotting the landscape of Brazilian cities like Rio de Janeiro and So Paulo, favelas (as they are known locally) arent a new concept.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Derived from the Portuguese word 'favela', it refers to a shanty or small town in urban areas of Brazil with high population densities. Originally built to accommodate the slaves who had moved into the city and didnt own land, the numbers of favelas have increased almost exponentially due to the mass migration of people from rural to urban areas, in search of better education, jobs and higher standards of living. Located on the outskirts of large metropolitan cities, these makeshift settlements are made of the most basic and cheap materials like plastic, wood, corrugated iron and more recently, concrete and bricks have also been used.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="" class="media-image" height="210" src="http://www.vidadelatinos.com/sites/default/files/favela-painting-project.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="628"/>
          <br/>
          <span style="color: #888888;">
           The Favela Painting Project, initiated by Dutch artists Hass and Hahn. Image Source:
           <a href="http://www.magicalurbanism.com/archives/809" target="_blank" title="http://www.magicalurbanism.com/archives/809">
            <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
             http://www.magicalurbanism.com/archives/809
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          These ever-burgeoning, self-constructed houses, that best portray the economic and social inequalities in the nation, face everyday problems such as the lack of basic amenities and public utilities which are difficult to resolve because of the haphazard manner in which they have been constructed.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          These suburban areas may be home to millions of people, but are also known for its notorious links with gangsters, drug cartels and traffickers and criminals, portraying these favelas in negative light. This has resulted in many government interventions in the past few years, as officials have made numerous attempts at apprehending evading lawbreakers.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          First observed in the early 1920s, these slums are now home to over 6 percent of the Brazilian population and are dramatically growing in size every day. These hand-built suburbs are typically found on hillsides, with the first few settlers of every favela located at the less steep regions of the hillside, while newer residents have the option of building their homes only at higher altitudes.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          When the city was unable to deal with the sudden influx of people, it gave rise to crowded favelas, something that officials were unsuccessful at eradicating in its early stages. Although a number of favela removal programs were advocated in the past, with the aim of improving the quality of life of its residents, few turned out to be successful.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Strong disapproval from the residents and lack of alternative settlements made the program even more unappealing. Although, one of the programs that received quite an amount of attention was the Growth Acceleration Program (PAC) which upgraded the facilities of several favelas in Rio in two phases. As a result of this program, the infrastructure, lighting facilities and public housing were upgraded, among many others.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Faveladors (name given to residents in favelas) have a community and social life of their own, separate from the urban manner of life. Generally employed in the homes of wealthy people or in industries nearby and forming an integral part of the workforce in surrounding areas, faveladors who live in the lower parts of the hillside tend to obtain better employment opportunities due to their proximity to neighbourhoods in urban areas than those who reside at less levelled areas of the slope.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <img alt="" class="media-image" height="447" src="http://www.vidadelatinos.com/sites/default/files/favela_01.jpg" typeof="foaf:Image" width="628"/>
          <br/>
          <span style="color: #888888;">
           Photo by Insight Guides
          </span>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Favelas may not seem all that resourceful to an outsider, but recent protests in these areas have resulted in new developments - a wireless revolution that has increased the connectivity of people who live here, making them even more receptive to the outside world. Despite belonging to the lower economic strata with a huge prevalence of poverty, people here are no longer cut-off from the global population.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          As Brazil gears up to host the 2014 FIFA World Cup and the Olympics in 2016, some residents of these favelas also hope to gain some much need revenue in terms of favela tours and probably even rent out unused spaces in their homes to football fanatics who might camp in the cities as part of the football fever. More recently, a new trend has risen in developing nations, which has stirred quite a controversy  Slum tourism, where tourists visit these shanty towns and settlements as a means of gauging the exact situation of the poverty-stricken.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          While many people associate favelas with a certain amount of negativity, these favelas themselves have opened up to visitors from outside who'd like to get a tour of the the way of life of the residents of these shanty towns. Although it hasn't changed significantly, it has become more safer for tourists. While the favelas may be open to visitors, it is advisable to travel in groups with a local guide or if you know the people who live there. It is strongly recommended that you don't try venturing out all by yourself as it can get unpredictable. Also do avoid bringing valuables with you while on your visit.
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Shanty towns, crowded slums or even centres of violence; be it what you may call it, for after all they are homes. Homes, that people and their families live in and carry on with what life has got to offer them with, no matter how hard it is and they only thing we could do is help. Not by shedding tears over stories that you read or pictures that you may see, but by helping people in and around you. Just imagine, if everyone just cared for the people that live around them, how much the world could have changed.
