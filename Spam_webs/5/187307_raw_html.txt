             <p>

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p>
              Germs can quickly build-up in the kitchen. The only way you can prevent nasty microorganisms from spreading is to properly disinfect food preparation surfaces and tools. This includes counters, cookware, cutting boards, faucets, mixing bowls and utensils.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              The most important thing you need to know:
              <em>
               You must always
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/Products/ss/Dirty-Things-in-Your-Home-to-Clean-Now-Baseboard-Radiators.htm">
                CLEAN
               </a>
               before sanitizing.
              </em>
             </p>
             <p>
              Here's the best way to hand wash all food preparation surfaces and tools:
             </p>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Remove all debris using a mild dish soap and warm water.
              </li>
              <li>
               Rinse washed items with fresh warm water.
              </li>
              <li>
               Air dry. Why? Hand drying with a clean towel will pick up and spread germs from one item you wipe to the next.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Once the cleaned surfaces and tools are dry, you are ready to sanitize with either distilled white vinegar or hydrogen perioxide. But you'll need to heat the cleaner of your choice to 150 degrees Fahrenheit. We explain why and how on the following pages.
             </p>
             <p>
              <strong>
               Tip:
              </strong>
              Got a dishwasher? If it has a sanitizing option, it can clean and disinfect dishwasher safe kitchen tools. FYI, water needs to be heated to at least 140 degrees Fahrenheit in order to zap harmful
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/Products/ss/Need-More-Storage-Space-in-Your-Kitchen-Go-Vertical.htm">
               kitchen
              </a>
              bacteria.

                 <img alt="natural_cleaning_products_smallspaces.about.com.jpg - " class=" addPinit" data-description="You've Been Cleaning With Vinegar and Hydrogen Peroxide The Wrong Way: Room Temperature Vinegar or Hydrogen Peroxide Will Not Kill Many Germs" itemprop="image" src="http://f.tqn.com/y/smallspaces/1/S/P/8/-/-/natural_cleaning_products_smallspaces.about.com.jpg"/>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
            </figure>
           </div>
           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p>
              When used correctly, two ordinary and natural household products, distilled white vinegar and hydrogen peroxide, can zap
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/Products/tp/Why-Your-Clean-Humidifier-is-Making-You-Sick.htm">
               illness-causing germs
              </a>
              in your
              <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/Apartments/ss/9-Space-Making-Kitchen-Storage-Ideas.htm">
               kitchen
              </a>
              such as:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Listeria monocytogenes: It's the third leading cause of food poisoning deaths according to the CDC.
              </li>
              <li>
               E. coli - While most types are harmless, vinegar and hydrogen peroxide can zap the strains that cause digestive distress and even life threatening illnesses.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <ul class="cb-split">
              <li>
               Salmonella -It's responsible for more than one million cases of food poisoning in the U.S. each year.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              To unleash distilled white vinegar's or hydrogen peroxide's germ killing powers, you will need to heat either liquid to 150 degrees Fahrenheit. This is based on research compiled by the U.S. Department of Agriculture.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Here is why:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Room temperature undiluted distilled white vinegar (5%) will only kill salmonella after ten minutes of contact time. But it won't zap listeria monocytogenes and e. coli.
              </li>
              <li>
               Room temperature undiluted Hydrogen Peroxide (3%) will kill salmonella and e. coli after ten minutes of contact time. But it won't zap listeria monocytogenes.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              <em>
               To learn how to safely heat distilled white vinegar and hydrogen peroxide keep reading.
              </em>
             </p>

              <h2 class="heading ">
               The Right Way to Disinfect Your Kitchen With Vinegar or Hydrogen Peroxide
              </h2>

           <article class="sbs content widget expert-content">
            <div class="expert-content-text">
             <p>
              Here is how to boost the disinfecting effectiveness of distilled white vinegar and hydrogen peroxide:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Heat four ounces of either undiluted distilled white vinegar (5%) or hydrogen peroxide (3%) to 150 degrees Fahrenheit using a saucepan on your stove top.
              </li>
              <li>
               To gauge the liquid's temperature use a cooking thermometer.
              </li>
              <li>
               Pour the heated liquid into a spray bottle. Use a funnel to avoid spilling.
              </li>
              <li>
               Immediately spray on clean
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="1" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/Apartments/ss/House-Tour-A-500-Square-Foot-Bachelorette-Pad_2.htm#step-heading">
                kitchen
               </a>
               surfaces and items.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <div adsense-displaylabel="true" adsense-numlinks="1" class="adsense-slot adsense ads-half" id="adsense1">
             </div>
             <ul class="cb-split">
              <li>
               Let the sprayed liquid sit for one minute and then wipe with a clean and dry cloth or paper towel.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              Here is more you need to know:
             </p>
             <ul>
              <li>
               Both distilled white vinegar and hydrogen peroxide will produce a non-harming
               <a data-component="link" data-ordinal="2" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" href="/od/Products/ss/How-to-Allergy-Proof-Your-Small-Home.htm">
                odor
               </a>
               when heated.
              </li>
              <li>
               Heated hydrogen peroxide can fade some surfaces. To avoid, test on a small unseen area.
              </li>
             </ul>
             <p>
              <strong>
               Note:
              </strong>
              You should never mix distilled white vinegar and hydrogen peroxide together in a saucepan or spray bottle. The combination can create a corrosive acid.
             </p>
