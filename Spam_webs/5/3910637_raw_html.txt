         <p>
          Newly mobile babies are pretty cute as they scoot, but theres a serious side to your munchkins new skill  he could get himself into trouble by venturing into potentially dangerous territory. So once your wee one starts to wander (even if hes still on all fours) youll need to step up your
          <a href="/nursery-decorating/childproofing-basics.aspx">
           childproofing
          </a>
          efforts and close off some areas of your home. Heres what to know about buying indoor safety gates.
         </p>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           Why You Need Indoor Safety Gates
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          A childs safety gate is designed specifically to keep pint-sized explorers (defined in product info as tots up to two years old, 34 inches tall, and 30 pounds) from getting themselves into unsafe situations. Smart spots to stick a gate: at the entrance to the kitchen (way too many hot, sharp things and tippy chairs) and any room with a fireplace, and at the top and bottom of staircases. (For tots working on mastering stairs, install a child safety gate two or three steps up from the bottom stair to give your tyke a small, safe space to practice.)
         </p>
         <h2>
          <strong>
           What to Look for in a Childs Safety Gate
          </strong>
         </h2>
         <p>
          There are several different indoor
          <a href="/first-year/baby-gear/safety-gate-styles.aspx">
           safety-gate styles
          </a>
          to choose from, so first figure out which will work best in your home. Then check for the following features before you buy:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           <strong>
            The JPMA seal.
           </strong>
           This means the gate meets the Juvenile Products Manufacturers Associations rigorous safety standards.
          </li>
          <li>
           <strong>
            Safe measurements.
           </strong>
           The gate should be 22 inches or higher, so your tot cant step over it, with vertical bars (if any) less than three inches apart, so he cant wedge his little head between them, and spaces (if any) smaller than your tots foot, so he cant climb up. Another measurement to consider: the width of the space where the gate will go. Size it up before you shop to avoid wasting your time and money.
          </li>
          <li>
           <strong>
            Safe materials.
           </strong>
           Whether its made of wood or plastic, all gates should have a smooth, nontoxic finish with no sharp pieces, parts that can catch tiny fingers, or small components that can break off and end up in your childs mouth.
          </li>
          <li>
           <strong>
            A safe design.
           </strong>
           Skip the old-school accordion-style gates, characterized by a series of half diamonds that create a V-shaped zigzag pattern across the top (where a child might decide to stick his head!). And remember, while indoor safety gates are an important part of your
           <a href="/first-year/baby-gear.aspx">
            baby gear
           </a>
           , theyre not foolproof. A watchful eye (yours or your caregivers) is your first and best line of defense when it comes to keeping your little one safe and sound. For more childproofing tips, watch this
           <a href="/family-home-safety.aspx">
            family home safety video.
           </a>
          </li>
         </ul>
