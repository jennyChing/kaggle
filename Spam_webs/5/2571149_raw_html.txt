     <h1 class="entry-title">
      Sayaka Ganz's Animal Sculptures Made of Salvaged Plastic
     </h1>
     <div class="article">
      <span class="posted-in author vcard">
       published in:
       <span>
        By
        <span class="fn">
         <a href="/author/Polina-Liarostathi" target="_self">
          Polina Liarostathi
         </a>
        </span>
        , 18 October 2012
       </span>
      </span>
      <p class="posted-in-community">
       <a class="twitter-share-button" data-count="horizontal" data-via="Yatzer" href="https://twitter.com/share">
        Tweet

      <div class="entry-content">
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <img alt="" height="714" src="/assets/Article/2999/images/sayaka-ganz-yatzer-5.jpg" width="714"/>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <span style="font-size: x-small;">
         Sayaka Ganz, photo  Act4.co
        </span>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <span style="font-size: x-large;">
         M
        </span>
        any artists seem to enjoy experimenting with the idea of downcycling by reusing waste materials in order to create inspired artworks. However, when this passion is supported by a genuine belief in the value of resources, the result cannot be anything less than breathtaking.
        <strong>
         <a href="http://www.sayakaganz.com/" target="_blank">
          Sayaka Ganz
         </a>
        </strong>
        was born in Japan but grew up in numerous cities all over the world.
        <strong>
         She was raised under the Japanese philosophy of
         <span style="color: #f60e8b;">
          Shinto
         </span>
         where all objects and organisms are believed to have a spirit and a soul
        </strong>
        . Since an early age, when she was stillin kindergarten, she was taught that '
        <em>
         <strong>
          objects that are discarded before their time, weep at night inside the trash bin
         </strong>
        </em>
        '. As she explains, this belief became her inspiration and guidance throughout her art journey, satisfying her constant need to adjust in various environments by creating her own harmony.
        <strong>
         <a href="http://www.sayakaganz.com/" target="_blank">
          Ganz
         </a>
        </strong>
        collects previously used and discarded objects and with her talent and enthusiasm she transfigures them into animal forms by simply combining plastic residues.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <img alt="" height="713" src="/assets/Article/2999/images/sayaka-ganz-yatzer-7.jpg" width="714"/>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <span style="font-size: x-small;">
         photo  Act4.co
        </span>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <img alt="" height="446" src="/assets/Article/2999/images/sayaka-ganz-yatzer-8.jpg" width="714"/>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <span style="font-size: x-small;">
         photo  Act4.co
        </span>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <strong>
         In her basement,
         <a href="http://www.sayakaganz.com/" target="_blank">
          Sayaka Ganz
         </a>
         contains 30  plastic bins in which she stores all kinds of coloured sorted plastic  objects.
        </strong>
        When the objects from one colour are enough to get her started  she then decides the subject of her new sculpture. All of her artworks  are defined by a fascinating sense of motion. A sense that captivates  viewers with an overall goal to remind them that even small forgotten  pieces can coexist and form a beautiful piece of art. At first, she  wires the outlines of her new sculpture and after studying every  different angle of it, she starts enjoying the creation process by  putting the objects together. Although the whole procedure is long and  demanding, she claims that she feels increasingly enthusiastic and eager  to have fun with the start of each new project.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <img alt="" height="476" src="/assets/Article/2999/images/sayaka-ganz-yatzer-1.jpg" width="714"/>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <span style="font-size: x-small;">
         photo 
        </span>
        <span style="font-size: x-small;">
         Sayaka Ganz
        </span>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <img alt="" height="402" src="/assets/Article/2999/images/sayaka-ganz-yatzer-2.jpg" width="714"/>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <span style="font-size: x-small;">
         photo  Act4.co
        </span>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        &gt;
        <em>
         <span style="font-size: large;">
          This  is like a puzzle to me. I make sure that all the objects are properly  aligned to maximize the effect of motion, then add, step back, add  something else, step back again and then maybe remove a piece. I keep on  going until the piece looks completely formed but not overly  dense...When my sculptures are observed from a distance, they represent  the great beauty and harmony in our community. Through my sculptures I  believe that I transmit a message of hope.
         </span>
        </em>
        &lt;
        <br/>
        <strong>
         <a href="http://www.sayakaganz.com/" target="_blank">
          Sayaka Ganz
         </a>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p style="text-align: justify;">
        <strong>
         <a href="http://www.sayakaganz.com/" target="_blank">
          Sayaka Ganz
         </a>
        </strong>
        s  work has been exhibited in many places around the world, among them  London, Tokyo, Takaoka, the Isle of Man, New York, San Francisco to name  a few. Her attention to detail is what makes her work differentiate and  stand out and her ability to imagine all these everyday objects as a  unique part of a bigger vision is what makes her art so special.
       </p>
