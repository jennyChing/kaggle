     <p>
      <b>
       Susan George
      </b>
      (born June 29, 1934) is an American and French
      <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">
       political
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">
       social scientist
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activist" title="Activist">
       activist
      </a>
      and writer on global
      <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">
       social justice
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">
       Third World
      </a>
      poverty,
      <a href="/wiki/Underdevelopment" title="Underdevelopment">
       underdevelopment
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">
       debt
      </a>
      . She is a fellow and president of the board of the
      <a href="/wiki/Transnational_Institute" title="Transnational Institute">
       Transnational Institute
      </a>
      in Amsterdam. She is a fierce critic of the present policies of the
      <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">
       International Monetary Fund
      </a>
      (IMF),
      <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">
       World Bank
      </a>
      (IBRD) and what she calls their '
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maldevelopment_model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maldevelopment model (page does not exist)">
       maldevelopment model
      </a>
      '. She similarly criticizes the structural reform policies of the
      <a href="/wiki/Washington_Consensus" title="Washington Consensus">
       Washington Consensus
      </a>
      on Third World development. She is of U.S. birth but now resides in France, and has
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_nationality" title="Dual nationality">
       dual citizenship
      </a>
      since 1994.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_George_(political_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Born Susan Vance Akers on June 29, 1934 in
      <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">
       Akron, Ohio
      </a>
      . She was the only child of Edith and Walter Akers,
      <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">
       Episcopalians
      </a>
      whose families had been in America for many generations; her ancestors arrived in Massachusetts in 1632. Her father was an insurance broker, and her mother was a homemaker and a member of the
      <a href="/wiki/Junior_League" title="Junior League">
       Junior League
      </a>
      . Born during the
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
       Great Depression
      </a>
      , she was raised in a privileged environment; she had a
      <a href="/wiki/Nursemaid" title="Nursemaid">
       nursemaid
      </a>
      and took dance classes, music lessons, and, at a
      <a href="/wiki/YMCA" title="YMCA">
       YMCA
      </a>
      , swimming lessons.
     </p>
     <p>
      After attending a public, co-educational primary school, she went on to enroll at all-girls private
      <a href="/wiki/University-preparatory_school" title="University-preparatory school">
       preparatory academy
      </a>
      . She stated that
      <a href="/wiki/Single-sex_education" title="Single-sex education">
       single-sex schooling
      </a>
      "made me not a feminist. It was normal that women do whatever anybody did. Women were the sports experts. Women were the brains. You weren't in competition with men. You weren't expected to shut up--on the contrary! Even in my era, I never felt that I was particularly put down as a woman ever."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curbio_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-curbio-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      George's father encouraged all her interests, including those outside the realm of traditional femininity, such as science and baseball. When Walter Akers went to serve in World War II, his daughter assisted in planting a
      <a href="/wiki/Victory_garden" title="Victory garden">
       victory garden
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As a young student, George was a voracious reader and always ranked first in her class. Around the age of 12, she began to develop a strong passion for the culture, language, and people of France. As a teenager she chose to attend
      <a href="/wiki/Smith_College" title="Smith College">
       Smith College
      </a>
      , in Northampton, Massachusetts, specifically in order to participate in the junior-year- abroad program in France. In Paris during the 1954-55 academic year, she took courses at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institut_d%27%C3%89tudes_Politiques_de_Paris" title="Institut d'tudes Politiques de Paris">
       Sciences Po
      </a>
      , a school specializing in social sciences. During that time, at the age of 20, she met a successful French lawyer, Charles-Henry George, 12 years her senior. In 1956 she married George and made France her permanent residence, but she did not obtain French citizenship until 1994. Quoted about her early years in France she said she felt homesick "for my women friends, probably, but not for America, per se. I'd made my choice."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curbio_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-curbio-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The couple soon started a family. Once her three children were in school full-time, George attended the
      <a href="/wiki/Sorbonne" title="Sorbonne">
       Sorbonne
      </a>
      , obtaining the French equivalent of a bachelor's degree in philosophy in 1967.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2002 Charles-Henry George died at their country home in France. Susan has three childrenValerie, Michel, and Stephanieand is a grandmother. In an interview she states: "Either we achieve together a new level of human emancipation, and do so in a way that preserves the earth, or we shall leave behind us the worst future for our children that capitalism and nature can deal them. No one knows in which direction the balance will tip nor does anyone know which actions, which writings, which alliances may achieve the critical mass that leads us one way or another, backwards or forwards. I am acutely conscious of the precariousness of our moment and my four much-loved grandchildren give me added resolve to address it."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curbio_1-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-curbio-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Career">
       Career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_George_(political_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Throughout her career, George has been an antiwar activist as well as criticising what she saw as acts of corporate greed. At a time when women were not often allowed places of power in any organizational hierarchy, George established herself as a leader in the antihunger movement.
     </p>
     <p>
      She became a political activist in response to
      <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">
       France's war in Algeria
      </a>
      and U.S. involvement in Vietnam. The
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
       Vietnam War
      </a>
      "was this sort of gateway to understanding what America could be, which is to say something quite negative, which I had not understood at all when I lived there. I had accepted the usual propaganda."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curbio_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-curbio-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1967 she joined the Paris-American Committee to Stop War (PACS). In 1973 PACS was forcibly dismantled by the French government. She then collaborated with the directors of the
      <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Policy_Studies" title="Institute for Policy Studies">
       Institute for Policy Studies
      </a>
      in Washington, D.C., to form a new
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGO" title="NGO">
       NGO
      </a>
      devoted to social justice. The
      <a href="/wiki/Transnational_Institute" title="Transnational Institute">
       Transnational Institute
      </a>
      (TNI) opened its doors in Amsterdam, the Netherlands that same year; George remains a fellow at TNI and also serves as its board chair.
     </p>
     <p>
      She became assistant to the director of an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGO" title="NGO">
       NGO
      </a>
      , the American Centre for Students and Artists, in 1969. This sparked the interest of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">
       CIA
      </a>
      who had already been looking into P.A.C.S.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1971 she began working with the Front Solidarite Indochine, a group that organized antiwar lectures and protests in France. During this time she started public speaking.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1974 she attended the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Food_Conference" title="World Food Conference">
       World Food Conference
      </a>
      in Rome, Italy where she felt that corporate agribusiness representatives dominated the proceedings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curbio_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-curbio-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1976 her first book was published:
      <i>
       How the Other Half Dies: The Real Reasons for World Hunger
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1984 she had an active role in organizing the World Food Assembly, a meeting held in Rome, Italy.
     </p>
     <p>
      From 1990 to 1995 she served on the board of conservation group
      <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">
       Greenpeace
      </a>
      International, as well as that of Greenpeace France.
     </p>
     <p>
      She opposed the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">
       OECD
      </a>
      's proposed
      <a href="/wiki/Multilateral_Agreement_on_Investment" title="Multilateral Agreement on Investment">
       Multilateral Agreement on Investment
      </a>
      (MAI) in the 1990s, and the ill-fated "Millennium Round" objectives of the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">
       World Trade Organization
      </a>
      at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization_Ministerial_Conference_of_1999_protest_activity" title="World Trade Organization Ministerial Conference of 1999 protest activity">
       Seattle
      </a>
      in 1999.
     </p>
     <p>
      From 1999 to 2006 she was vice-president of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_pour_la_Taxation_des_Transactions_pour_l%27Aide_aux_Citoyens" title="Association pour la Taxation des Transactions pour l'Aide aux Citoyens">
       ATTAC France
      </a>
      (Association for Taxation of (financial) Transactions to Aid Citizens) and remains a member of the scientific council.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She was awarded the title of honorary president in 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-france.attac.org_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-france.attac.org-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1999 she also participated in the Helsinki Process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2004 she supported
      <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">
       John Kerry
      </a>
      for president. She canvassed for Kerry in Pennsylvania, but wrote for OpenDemocracy.org (November 3, 2004), "we all thought [Kerry] had a very good chance, even though everyone admitted it was hard to get really enthusiastic about him.... The man isn't the most charismatic ever to walk the earth. But at least he's not a proto-fascist or a go-it-aloner, and that's what we seem--apart from a last-minute miracle--to be stuck with now. With four years clear ahead of him and no re-election to worry about, I fear Bush and the ghastly neo- con/neo-liberals around him will now go on the rampage. They can continue with impunity their attacks on the Constitution and on hard-won freedoms..."
     </p>
     <p>
      2006: attends Table of Free Voices conference, held in Berlin, Germany, in September
     </p>
     <p>
      2008: appeared in the documentary film,
      <a href="/wiki/The_End_of_Poverty%3F" title="The End of Poverty?">
       The End of Poverty?
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      Susan George is president of the Board of the
      <a href="/wiki/Transnational_Institute" title="Transnational Institute">
       Transnational Institute
      </a>
      in Amsterdam.
     </p>
     <p>
      Susan George is also a Patron of the international development charity,
      <a href="/wiki/Tools_for_Self_Reliance" title="Tools for Self Reliance">
       Tools for Self Reliance
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She is on the advisory board of CEO,
      <a href="/wiki/Corporate_Europe_Observatory" title="Corporate Europe Observatory">
       the Corporate Europe Observatory
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      She has acted as a consultant to various United Nations specialised agencies and is a frequent public speaker, particularly for ATTAC groups, trade unions, and environment/development non-governmental organisations in many countries.
     </p>
