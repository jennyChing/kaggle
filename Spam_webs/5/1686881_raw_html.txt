           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="featured-image">
          <img alt="" class="attachment-post-thumbnail" data-description="Voodoo faithful pray to goddess Erzulie Dantor in the Petionville suburb of Port-au-Prince during a ceremony to mark the Christmas season. (Photograph by Thony Belizaire, AFP/Getty Images)" height="426" src="http://intelligenttravel.nationalgeographic.com/files/2014/10/voodoo-faithful-haiti-590.jpg" title="Picture of voodoo faithful praying - Haiti" width="590"/>
          <div class="caption" style="width:590px;">
           Voodoo faithful pray to goddess Erzulie Dantor in the Petionville suburb of Port-au-Prince during a ceremony to mark the Christmas season. (Photograph by Thony Belizaire, AFP/Getty Images)
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="main">
          <div class="content entry-content">
           <p class="p1">
            Vengeful gods, terrifying sorcerers, and death-dealing demons populate the legends and beliefs of the Caribbean, deriving from a potent blend of voodoo, Catholicism, and folklore.
           </p>
           <p class="p2">
            <span class="s1">
             <b>
              1.
             </b>
             <a href="http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/470825/Port-au-Prince" target="_blank" title="Britannica site - Port-Au-Prince">
              <span class="s2">
               <b>
                Port-au-Prince, Haiti
               </b>
              </span>
             </a>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            In the home of voodoo, the god of death,
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gu%C3%A9d%C3%A9" target="_blank" title="Wikipedia site - Guede (Gede)">
             <span class="s2">
              Gede
             </span>
            </a>
            , is said to stand at the crossroads to the afterworld. Represented as an undertaker, his clothes are black and he wears dark glasses, while his followers are disguised as corpses. Some voodoo sorcerers own a magic stick called a
            <em>
             coco
            </em>
            <i>
             macaque
            </i>
            that walks by itself and is sent out to perform vengeful deeds.
           </p>
           <p class="p2">
            <span class="s1">
             <b>
              2.
             </b>
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vieux_Fort_Quarter" title="Wikipedia site - Vieux Fort Quarter, St. Lucia">
              <span class="s2">
               <b>
                Vieux Fort, St. Lucia
               </b>
              </span>
             </a>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            Practitioners of
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obeah" target="_blank" title="Wikipedia site - Obeah">
             <span class="s3">
              <i>
               obeah
              </i>
             </span>
            </a>
            , a form of voodoo found on
            <a href="http://stlucianow.com/" target="_blank" title="St. Lucia Now site">
             <span class="s2">
              St. Lucia
             </span>
            </a>
            , can bestow authority and riches as well as pain and death. But their power can backfire. One legend tells of a sorcerer who, in the guise of a spirit, used magic dust to drug a man and make love to his wife. One night the dust failed to work, and the husband plunged a knife into the spirit, killing the sorcerer.
           </p>
           <p class="p2">
            <span class="s1">
             <b>
              3.
             </b>
             <a href="http://www.barbados.org/btown.htm#.VEFBD4vF_DU" target="_blank" title="Barbados tourism site - Bridgetown">
              <span class="s2">
               <b>
                Bridgetown, Barbados
               </b>
              </span>
             </a>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            In obeah and voodoo beliefs, a person has two soulsa good soul and an earthly one. In death, the good soul goes to heaven, while the earthly one stays in the coffin for three days. If the earthly soul escapes, it becomes a harmful entity known as a
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duppy" target="_blank" title="Wikipedia site - Duppy">
             <span class="s2">
              <i>
               duppy
              </i>
             </span>
            </a>
            . In
            <a href="http://www.barbados.org/" target="_blank" title="Barbados tourism site">
             <span class="s2">
              Barbados
             </span>
            </a>
            , duppies are repelled by walking backward, or by hanging herbs or funeral clothes in a window.
           </p>
           <p class="p2">
            <span class="s1">
             <b>
              4.
             </b>
             <a href="http://www.lonelyplanet.com/cuba/havana" target="_blank" title="Lonely Planet site - Havana">
              <span class="s2">
               <b>
                Havana, Cuba
               </b>
              </span>
             </a>
            </span>
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            When a man is seriously ill in Cuba, the god
            <a href="http://cuban-exile.com/doc_226-250/doc0234-35.html" target="_blank" title="Cuban Exile site - Cuban Religious Beliefs of African Origins">
             <span class="s2">
              Ic
             </span>
            </a>
            appears to demand his body and soul. To fool the god, the obeah witch makes a life-size puppet of the dying man, which the mans wife dresses in her husbands clothes and then takes to the cemetery at midnight. Seeing the woman crying over the grave, Ic is convinced that the man is dead and already in the underworld.
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            <b>
             5.
             <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laudat" target="_blank" title="Wikipedia site - Laudat">
              Laudat, Dominica
             </a>
            </b>
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            A devil woman known as La Diablesse lives alone in this village, plotting revenge on cheating husbands. She hides behind trees on lonely roads, waiting to catch them. Although she is beautiful, there is something that betrays herher cloven hooves. If these are spotted, she must fly toward the safety of
            <a href="http://www.lonelyplanet.com/dominica/morne-trois-pitons-national-park/activities/water-sports/titou-gorge" target="_blank" title="Lonely Planet site - Titou Gorge">
             Titou Gorge
            </a>
            before she turns into a hideous old hag.
           </p>
           <p class="p1">
            <i>
             This list originally appeared in the
            </i>
            <em>
             National Geographic
            </em>
            <i>
             book
            </i>
            <a href="http://shop.nationalgeographic.com/ngs/product/books/exploration/national-geographic-guide-to-the-world-s-supernatural-places">
             <span class="s2">
              Guide to the Worlds Supernatural Places
             </span>
            </a>
            <i>
             , by Sarah Bartlett.
            </i>
           </p>
          </div>
