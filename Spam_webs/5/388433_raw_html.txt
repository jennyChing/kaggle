     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Hit Man: A Technical Manual for Independent Contractors
       </b>
      </i>
      is a book written under the
      <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">
       pseudonym
      </a>
      Rex Feral and published by
      <a href="/wiki/Paladin_Press" title="Paladin Press">
       Paladin Press
      </a>
      in 1983. Paladin Press owner Peder Lund claimed, in an interview with
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">
        60 Minutes
       </a>
      </i>
      , that the book started life as a detailed crime novel written by a Florida housewife, and that the format was later changed to appeal to Paladin's reader base. The book portrays itself as a
      <a href="/wiki/How-to" title="How-to">
       how-to
      </a>
      manual on starting a career as a
      <a href="/wiki/Contract_killing" title="Contract killing">
       hit man
      </a>
      , fulfilling contracts.
     </p>
     <p>
      Existing copies at Paladin Press were destroyed after the book was found to be used as a guide during a triple murder in 1993 (see below), but it can still be found online or can be purchased used from independent sellers. It is believed that 13,000 copies were sold, although
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Reason_(magazine)" title="Reason (magazine)">
        Reason Magazine
       </a>
      </i>
      estimates there are 20,000 copies of the book in existence.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">
       Controversy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hit_Man:_A_Technical_Manual_for_Independent_Contractors&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Controversy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1993, a triple murder was committed in
      <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Maryland" title="Montgomery County, Maryland">
       Montgomery County, Maryland
      </a>
      , by a man who claimed to have used the book as his guide.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      James Perry, who had been imprisoned for a violent crime, was caught, convicted, and sentenced to death. He had been hired by
      <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Horn" title="Lawrence Horn">
       Lawrence T. Horn
      </a>
      , who sought to receive the proceeds of a trust fund that resulted from his ex-wife's suing a hospital over injuries to their son. The families of Mildred Horn, her son Trevor, and her nurse Janice Saunders sued Paladin Press, the publisher of the book, claiming Paladin Press "aided and abetted" the murder. The suit,
      <i>
       Rice v Paladin Enterprises
      </i>
      , claimed that Paladin Press had a share of responsibility in the murders by virtue of their publication of a book that, by Paladin's own admission, could be used by criminals and would-be criminals in the solicitation, planning, and commission of murder for hire.
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 1997, a U.S. appeals court ruled 30 that
      <i>
       Hit Man
      </i>
      was not protected by the free speech/free press clause of the
      <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">
       First Amendment
      </a>
      and thus Paladin Enterprises could be held liable for a triple murder committed by one of its readers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On May 21, 1999, Paladin Press' insurance company agreed to settle the case out-of-court, against the wishes of Paladin Press themselves, who were confident that they would prevail in court; however, Paladins insurance company balked at going to court again, figuring expenses for a lengthy trial in federal court, plus the posting of a bond in case they lost and appealed, would have cost much more than the settlement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Murder_of_the_First_Amendment_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Murder_of_the_First_Amendment-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Under this settlement, Paladin's insurance policy paid several million dollars to the families of those killed by the murderer, while also agreeing to destroy the remaining 700 copies of the book in their possession and surrendering any rights they had to publish and reproduce the work. Jon Ford, Paladins editorial director, called the settlement "economic censorship."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Murder_of_the_First_Amendment_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-The_Murder_of_the_First_Amendment-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 2000 TV movie,
      <i>
       Deliberate Intent
      </i>
      , starring Timothy Hutton, is based on this case.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The book was also cited as a source of information in a similar crime committed by Robert Vaughn Jones in 1999.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
