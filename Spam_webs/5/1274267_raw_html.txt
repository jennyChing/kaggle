         <p>
          The U.K. general election is tomorrow. Woo! Four nations--England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland--will decide who should govern the United Kingdom.
         </p>
         <p>
          Didn't even know this was happening? Not sure who's running? Unclear on why this even matters? Read on.
         </p>
         <h2>
          Parties, parties, parties!
         </h2>
         <p>
          In Britain, an election is really about choosing who should be members of Parliament (MPs). So it's a chance for voters to shape Parliament and potentially put their party in the majority.
         </p>
         <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="500">

         <p>
          Not sure what Parliament is? It's where taxes, laws, and government oversight
          <a href="http://www.parliament.uk/about/how/role/" target="_blank">
           come from
          </a>
          . So, you know, a pretty big deal.
         </p>
         <h2>
          New Prime Minister?
         </h2>
         <p>
          If a party wins the majority in Parliament, then its leader becomes Prime Minister (PM). In this case, we have two parties with the most seats in Parliament--the Conservatives (or Tories, colloquially), led by current PM David Cameron, and the Labour Party, led by his opposition, Ed Miliband.
         </p>
         <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="500">
          <p dir="ltr" lang="en">

          <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">

         </p>
         <h2>
          This one is gonna be close.
         </h2>
         <p>
          This one is
          <a href="http://www.theguardian.com/politics/2014/dec/27/2015-general-election-unpredictable-green-party-ukip" target="_blank">
           <em>
            tight
           </em>
          </a>
          . Right now in Parliament, there are
          <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/general-election-2015/11475255/definition.html" target="_blank">
           303 seats
          </a>
          for the Conservatives and
          <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/general-election-2015/11475255/definition.html" target="_blank">
           256
          </a>
          for the Labour Party.
         </p>
         <p>
          The race is so close, in fact, that it might lead to a
          <a href="http://rt.com/uk/255825-hung-parliament-labour-tories/" target="_blank">
           "hung parliament,"
          </a>
          wherein two parties have to form a government together. That could be messy...
         </p>
         <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="500">

         </p>
         <h2>
          Back up--tell me about the different parties.
         </h2>
         <p>
          The
          <a href="https://www.conservatives.com/Together" target="_blank">
           Conservative Party
          </a>
          is Prime Minister David Cameron's party. They also go by the cute lil' name Tories because the group
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)#Policies" target="_blank">
           evolved
          </a>
          from the Tory Party back in 1834. Both Winston Churchill and Margaret Thatcher were prominent Conservative leaders.
         </p>
         <p>
          Conservatives can get down with economic liberalism and British unionism, but they aren't super into
          <a href="http://www.economist.com/news/britain/21569710-europe-prime-minister-must-placate-five-types-conservative-tory-taxonomy" target="_blank">
           the whole EU thing
          </a>
          , meaning they would prefer to have independence as a nation state rather than be integrated into the EU.
         </p>
         <p>
          The
          <a href="http://www.labour.org.uk/" target="_blank">
           Labour Party
          </a>
          , on the other hand, has its roots in socialism. Over the course of the last 100 years or so, however, it has become
          <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" target="_blank">
           more inclusive
          </a>
          . Its "broad church" attitude means it hosts a huge diversity of ideological positions and trends, from the more overtly socialist to more moderate points of view. Former MP Tony Blair is one notable member of the Labour Party.
         </p>
         <h2>
          What about the smaller parties?
         </h2>
         <p>
          There are plenty of less powerful, but still equally interesting, parties that are vying for seats in Parliament in this election.
         </p>
         <p>
          The one with the most seats as of now is the more centrist
          <a href="http://www.libdems.org.uk/" target="_blank">
           Liberal Democrats
          </a>
          , led by Nick Clegg. They won't win this election--British government has been ruled by the Conservative or Labour parties since 1922--but they still have a strong voice.
         </p>

         <p>
          Among the other parties is the
          <a href="https://www.greenparty.org.uk/" target="_blank">
           Green Party
          </a>
          , which is, as its name suggests, pretty into environmentalism and eco-conscious political strategies.
         </p>
         <p>
          There are a bunch of other strange but fascinating
          <a href="http://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/generalelection/general-election-2015-a-guide-to-the-smaller-parties-from-the-the-national-health-action-party-to-the-church-of-the-militant-elvis-party-10227365.html" target="_blank">
           parties
          </a>
          in the running, too, several of which have garnered increased support and interest. While none have the power or sway of the Conservative or Labour parties, the sheer diversity of parties proves there is a growing desire for change within the U.K.
         </p>

         <h2>
          But seriously, why does this election matter?
         </h2>
         <p>
          Nearly
          <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/general-election-2015/11475255/definition.html" target="_blank">
           48 million voters
          </a>
          are eligible to participate in this election, yet a
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/election-2015-32587879" target="_blank">
           third of them
          </a>
          are unlikely to even show up at the polls. So who cares? And why does this matter to anyone living outside the U.K.?
         </p>
         <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="500">
          <p dir="ltr" lang="en">
           With just hours until polling day, make sure you have a say in
           <a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/GE2015?src=hash">
            #GE2015
           </a>
           <a href="http://t.co/tHQv0DBa3L">
            http://t.co/tHQv0DBa3L
           </a>
           <a href="http://t.co/pmz75tOscl">
            pic.twitter.com/pmz75tOscl
           </a>
          </p>
          <p>
            BBC Politics (@BBCPolitics)
           <a href="https://twitter.com/BBCPolitics/status/595978118793437184">
            May 6, 2015
           </a>
          </p>
         </blockquote>
         <p>
          <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
          </script>
         </p>
         <p>
          Well, here are a few important reasons:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           Britain is powerful--it has the
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures" target="_blank">
            fifth largest
           </a>
           defense budget in the world. So pretty much everyone in the world is affected by who manages that money.
          </li>
          <li>
           Immigration is integral to Britain's identity, and whoever wins this election will
           <a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/general-election-2015/11451936/General-Election-2015-Immigration-policy.html" target="_blank">
            shape immigration policy
           </a>
           . And the U.K. has a lot of immigrants.
           <br/>
           <blockquote class="twitter-tweet" width="500">
            <p dir="ltr" lang="en">
             UK
             <a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/immigration?src=hash">
              #immigration
             </a>
             under
             <a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/TonyBlair?src=hash">
              #TonyBlair
             </a>
             and
             <a href="https://twitter.com/hashtag/DavidCameron?src=hash">
              #DavidCameron
             </a>
             premierships.
             <a href="http://t.co/97bcwCdbTU">
              pic.twitter.com/97bcwCdbTU
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
              Mark Constantine (@vexmark)
             <a href="https://twitter.com/vexmark/status/593533322782900225">
              April 29, 2015
             </a>
            </p>
           </blockquote>
           <p>
            <script async="" charset="utf-8" src="//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js">
            </script>
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           Scotland said
           <a href="http://gokicker.com/2014/09/19/scotland-votes-independence-55-3/" target="_blank">
            "No" to independence
           </a>
           last fall--and the elected PM will certainly have a voice in the future of Scotland.
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          Only 50 years ago, the two party system earned a
          <a href="http://www.bbc.com/news/election-2015-32587879" target="_blank">
           90% shared vote
          </a>
          , but now these parties will struggle to garner just two-thirds of all votes cast. Britain is undergoing an intensely transitional time, and this election will play a major role in how the country's various parties and practices evolve.
         </p>
         <p>
          So you may come for the funny bacon-sandwich-eating-faces, but please, stay for the chance to see Britain's complex history unfold before your eyes.
         </p>
