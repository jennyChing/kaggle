        <div id="mainBodyArea" itemprop="articleBody">
         <div class="firstPar">
          <p>
           One could be forgiven wondering why anyone would want to run Silverstone, the struggling home of the British Grand Prix. Facing a consistent stream of criticism from Bernie Ecclestone  not to mention the enormous 15 million annual fee paid to Formula Ones ringmaster  the privilege of hosting the famous old race can often seem like a poisoned chalice.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p>
           Despite the daunting challenge before them, the new men installed to turn the home of British motor racing around dont see it that way. After several years of almost no profit and failed attempts by previous executives at a management buyout, the owners, the British Racing Drivers Club, lost patience with the old guard, most of whom have now gone.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p>
           It seems significant that Patrick Allen, the new chief executive, and John Grant, the BRDC chairman, met
           <i>
            Telegraph Sport
           </i>
           for their first newspaper interview since taking the reins not far from the multi-million wing development at the clubs historic farm, the private living quarters-cum archives of the BRDC. The message is clear: after years of allowing Silverstone to drift, the BRDC are back in control.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p>
           A poisoned chalice would be the wrong word, Grant says. The thing about the grand prix is its what contributes so much to the Silverstone brand. Allen, a former head of marketing at The Co-operative, adds: You cant see it as a discrete event. If you want to do a driving experience, do you want to do it at an airfield somewhere or on the corners Lewis Hamilton drives?
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p>
           But there are strong words for the governing body of Formula One, with traditional circuits in almost mortal danger, particularly those like Silverstone which do not enjoy government backing.
          </p>
         </div>

         <div class="body">
          <p>
           What sort of a world championship would it be without the British Grand Prix? an exasperated Grant says. We dont have the French anymore, we might not have the German this year, so it becomes increasingly important that the historic events remain on the calendar. I think it worries us, and I think it should worry the FIA a lot more. They are responsible for the world championship and for ensuring its a world championship of the name.
          </p>
          <p>
           Action has been swift since the new regime began its work last October. Richard Phillips, the managing director of 10 years, left his post along with a tranche of other senior managers, signing non-disclosure agreements on departure. A major hospitality and catering contract was axed. Even with the threat of a 3 million legal action brewing, Allen is particularly bullish on this subject.
          </p>
          <p>
           What we cant do is rip people off, he says. We cant ramp up the price of food and everything for a one-off event, and leave a bad taste in peoples mouths for a captured audience. He claims the price of a standard meal has fallen from 15 to 5. Security and PR deals have also been terminated.
          </p>
          <p>
           The pair have cut away some of the froth from what they saw as a bloated organisation. We found more than we expected that not just could be improved but needed to be, Grant reveals. The organisation wasnt very cohesive and the costs had grown a lot, a lot more than they needed to.
          </p>
          <p>
           When we got into it and took a good look we concluded the changes we needed were so dramatic that it couldnt be done with the existing management team, because they were too associated with the last eight years.
          </p>
          <p>
           The flipside to this constraint is an attempt to squeeze more out of Silverstone than ever before. Concerts, Christmas shows and even fireworks displays will come in while Allen has set the target of lowering the cheapest ticket for the British Grand Prix to 99; a laudable aim, but surely at some point it will come into conflict with the bottom line? The race day crowd in recent years has not been far off capacity  126,000 - after all.
          </p>
          <p>
           Ive got handwritten letters from people thanking me, because this was their annual holiday which had been unaffordable, but now they can come again, Allen says. Weve got to have a reality check. These families who have supported it for years are dying away because they simply cannot afford to come. I think we have a sort of duty to them to make it affordable again.
          </p>
          <p>
           With Lewis Hamilton reigning champion, and Jenson Button retained  Allen speaks of a huge spike in sales the day Button resigned for McLaren  the cards are in Silverstones favour. Ticket sales are up 29 per cent on this time last year, despite Formula Ones more muted soundtrack.
          </p>
          <p>
           My personal feeling is it is a bad thing the cars being quieter, Allen admits. We need to get that adrenaline back into the sport. At least he will have one point of resounding agreement with Ecclestone when he goes in for his first meeting with the sports supremo early next month.
          </p>
          <p>
           However, critics  of which there are many - say it is when we are without a British world champion, that Allen and Grants stripped out Silverstone will falter. They have the race Prix until 2026, by which time Hamilton will be gone. The new management at least have to try and make the business sustainable should the unthinkable happen.
          </p>
          <p>
           Grant goes on: We really want to keep hold of the Grand Prix, absolutely, and make sure it earns a bit of money. But we also need to be sure that if for some reason it was to go away, we need to be able to survive without it as well.
          </p>
          <p>
           In the meantime, their job is simply to restore some tranquillity after a tumultuous six months. According to Allen, staff engagement and morale is through the roof diametrically opposed to before the takeover. But the real test will come on July 5, with the first British Grand Prix of his tenure.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Five greatest Silverstone moments:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <i>
             Lewis Hamilton the rainmaster (2008)
            </i>
           </strong>
            In his championship-winning year, Hamilton memorably decimated the entire field in the pouring rain. He won by 67 seconds.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <i>
             Ferrari bending the rules (1998)
            </i>
           </strong>
            Confusion reigned as Michael Schumacher came into the pits on the last lap crossing the line first before taking his punishment. His victory stood.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <i>
             Mansellmania (1987)
            </i>
           </strong>
            Half a minute behind his team-mate with 30 laps to go, Nigel Mansell drove out of his skin, overhauling Nelson Piquet with a spectacular dummy pass three laps from the end.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <i>
             Mayhem at the start (1973)
            </i>
           </strong>
            Jody Scheckter, driving for McLaren, lost control on the first lap, taking out nine cars. He finished fourth once the race had restarted.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            <i>
             Formula One is born (1950)
            </i>
           </strong>
            On 13 May 1950, in the presence of the Royal Family, 200,000 spectators attended the first ever Formula One grand prix.
          </p>

                        '&lt;/div&gt;';

            var init = function(currentArticleId, articleList, displayThumbnail) {

                settings.articleList = articleList;
                settings.currentArticleId = currentArticleId;
                settings.displayThumbnail = displayThumbnail;

                settings.articleListToSkip = unescape($.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName)).split(',');

                if ($.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName) != null) {
                    chooseNextArticle(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    updateCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                } else {
                    chooseNextArticle(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    createCookieBasedOnArticleList(settings.currentArticleId, settings.articleList, settings.articleListToSkip);

                    $("#nextArticleWrapper a, #readNextOpenInner a").click(function () {

                        getNavPos($(this).attr("href"), "wayfarerReadNext");
                    });

                    bindUIActions();

                    var displayed = false;

                    setTimeout(function () {
                        if (!displayed) {
                            displayed == true;
                            readNextWidget.display();

                        if (isScrolledIntoView("#tmg-related-links")) {

                $(settings.openButton).mouseenter(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();

                    openArticleContainer();

                $(settings.widgetContainer).mouseleave(function (event) {
                    event.preventDefault();

                    updateCookie(settings.articleId, settings.articleListToSkip);
                    closeArticleContainer();
                    settings.autoOpen = false;

                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                    settings.autoOpen = false; //set auto open to false once user has viewed this once
                }
                return false;

                if (settings.panelOpen) {
                    $(settings.articleContainer).toggle('slide', {direction: 'left'}, function () {
                        //animation complete
                    });
                    settings.panelOpen = !settings.panelOpen;
                }
                return false;

            var isScrolledIntoView = function(elem) {
                if($(elem).length) { //check if element exists
                    var docViewTop = $(window).scrollTop();
                    var docViewBottom = docViewTop + $(window).height();

                    var elemTop = $(elem).offset().top;
                    var elemBottom = elemTop + $(elem).height();

                    return ((elemBottom &lt;= docViewBottom) &amp;&amp; (elemTop &gt;= docViewTop));
                } else {
                    return false;
                }
            };

            var unique = function(array) {
                return array.filter(function (el, index, arr) {
                    return index == arr.indexOf(el);
                });
            }

            var createCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        articleListToSkip[0] = currentArticleId;
                        $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookieBasedOnArticleList = function(currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if (article.id == currentArticleId) {
                        updateCookie(currentArticleId, articleListToSkip);
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            var updateCookie = function(articleIdToSkip, articleListToSkip) {
                articleListToSkip.push(articleIdToSkip);
                articleListToSkip = unique(articleListToSkip);
                $.cookie(settings.readNextCookieName, escape(articleListToSkip.join(',')), {path: '/', expires: 1, domain: 'telegraph.co.uk'});
            }

            var chooseNextArticle = function (currentArticleId, articleList, articleListToSkip) {
                articleList.some(function (article) {
                    if ((article.id != currentArticleId) &amp;&amp; ($.inArray(article.id, articleListToSkip) === -1)) {

                        //set article
                        settings.articleTitle = article.title;
                        settings.articleUrl = article.url;
                        settings.articleId = article.id;
						settings.articleImage = article.imgUrl;
                        return true;
                    }
                });
            }

            //public API
            return {
                settings: settings,
                init: init,
                display: display,
                openArticleContainer: openArticleContainer,
                closeArticleContainer: closeArticleContainer
            }
