     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Ecotopia: The Notebooks and Reports of William Weston
       </b>
      </i>
      is a
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/seminal" title="wikt:seminal">
       seminal
      </a>
      utopian novel by
      <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Callenbach" title="Ernest Callenbach">
       Ernest Callenbach
      </a>
      , published in 1975. The society described in the book is one of the first
      <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">
       ecological
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">
       utopias
      </a>
      and was influential on the
      <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">
       counterculture
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_movement" title="Green movement">
       green movement
      </a>
      in the 1970s and thereafter. The author himself claimed that the society he depicted in the book is not a true utopia (in the sense of a perfect society), but, while guided by societal intentions and values, was
      <i>
       im
      </i>
      perfect and in-process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callenbach.2C_Ernest_and_Heddle.2C_James_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Callenbach.2C_Ernest_and_Heddle.2C_James-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_book.27s_context_and_background">
       The book's context and background
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The book's context and background">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Callenbach wove his story using the fiber of technologies,
      <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">
       lifestyles
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folkways_(sociology)" title="Folkways (sociology)">
       folkways
      </a>
      , and attitudes that were common in Northern California and the Pacific Northwest. The "leading edges" (his main ideas for Ecotopian values and practices) were patterns in actual social experimentation taking place in the
      <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">
       American West
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      To draw an example, Callenbach's fictional Crick School was based upon Pinel School, an
      <a href="/wiki/Alternative_education" title="Alternative education">
       alternative school
      </a>
      located outside
      <a href="/wiki/Martinez,_California" title="Martinez, California">
       Martinez, California
      </a>
      , and attended for a time by his son.
     </p>
     <p>
      Besides the important social dimensions of the story, Callenbach talked publicly about being influenced, during work on the novel, by numerous streams of thought: The scientific discoveries in the fields of ecology and conservation biology. The urban-ecology movement, concerned with a new approach to urban planning. The soft-energy movement, championed by
      <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">
       Amory Lovins
      </a>
      and others. Much of the environmentally benign energy, homebuilding, and transportation technology described by the author was based on his reading of research findings published in such journals as
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">
        Scientific American
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">
        Science
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callenbach.2C_Ernest_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Callenbach.2C_Ernest-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Callenbachs concept does not reject
      <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">
       high technology
      </a>
      (or
      <i>
       any
      </i>
      technology) as long as it does not interfere with the Ecotopian social order and serves the overall objectives. Members of his fictional society prefer to demonstrate a
      <i>
       conscious selectivity
      </i>
      toward technology, so that not only human health and sanity might be preserved, but also social and ecological wellbeing. Hence, as an example, Callenbachs story anticipated the development and liberal usage of
      <a href="/wiki/Videoconferencing" title="Videoconferencing">
       videoconferencing
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      During the 1970s when
      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>
      was written and published many prominent counterculture and new left thinkers decried the consumption and overabundance that they perceived as characteristic of post-World War Two America.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The citizens of Ecotopia share a common aim: they seek a balance between themselves and nature. They were literally sick of bad air, chemicalized food, and lunatic advertising. They turned to politics because it was finally the only route to self-preservation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the mid-20th century as firms grew in size and complexity citizens needed to know the market would still serve the interests of those for whom it claimed to exist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hilton.2C_Matthew_2007_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hilton.2C_Matthew_2007-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Callenbachs
      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>
      targets the fact that many people did not feel that the market or the government were serving them in the way they wanted them to. This book could be interpreted as a protest against consumerism and materialism, among other aspects of American life."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hilton.2C_Matthew_2007_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hilton.2C_Matthew_2007-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The term "
      <a href="/wiki/Utopian_and_dystopian_fiction#Ecotopian_fiction" title="Utopian and dystopian fiction">
       ecotopian fiction
      </a>
      ", as a subgenre of
      <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
       science fiction
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utopian_fiction" title="Utopian fiction">
       utopian fiction
      </a>
      , makes implicit reference to this book.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">
       Plot summary
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot summary">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The book is set in 1999 (25 years in the future from 1974) and consists of diary entries and reports of journalist William Weston, who is the first American
      <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_media" title="Mainstream media">
       mainstream media
      </a>
      reporter to investigate Ecotopia, a small country that
      <a href="/wiki/Secession" title="Secession">
       broke away
      </a>
      from the USA in 1980. Prior to Weston's reporting, most Americans had been barred from entering the new country, which is depicted as being on continual guard against
      <a href="/wiki/Revanchism" title="Revanchism">
       revanchism
      </a>
      . The new nation of Ecotopia consists of
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">
       Northern California
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">
       Oregon
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">
       Washington
      </a>
      ; it is hinted that
      <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">
       Southern California
      </a>
      is a lost cause. The novel takes its form as a narrative from Weston's diary in combination with dispatches that he transmits to his publication, the fictional
      <i>
       Times-Post
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Together with Weston (who at the beginning is curious about, but not particularly sympathetic to the Ecotopians), the reader learns about the Ecotopian transportation system and the preferred lifestyle that includes a wide range of gender roles, sexual freedom, and acceptance of non-monogamous relationships. Liberal
      <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">
       cannabis
      </a>
      use is evident. Televised mass-spectacle sports are displaced due to a preference for local arts, participatory sports, and general fitness. Some Ecotopians participate in a peculiar ritual of (voluntary) mock warfare, fought with actual weapons and often resulting in injuries. Ecotopia has tolerated the voluntary separatism of many people of African descent who have, in fact, chosen to live in a mini-nation in the San Francisco East Bay-area.
     </p>
     <p>
      Ecotopian society has favored decentralized and renewable energy production and green building construction. The citizens are technologically creative, while remaining involved with and sensitive to nature. Thorough-going education reform is described, along with a highly localized system of universal medical care. (The narrator discovers that Ecotopian healing practices may include sexual stimulation.) The national defense strategy has focused on developing a highly advanced arms industry, while also allegedly maintaining hidden
      <a href="/wiki/Weapon_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapon of mass destruction">
       WMD
      </a>
      within major US population centers to discourage conquest and annexation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Through Weston's diary we learn of observations he does not include in his columns, such as his personally transformative love affair with an Ecotopian woman. The book's parallel narrative structures allow the reader to see how Weston's internal reflections, as recorded in his diary, are diffracted in his external pronouncements to his readers. Despite Weston's initial reservations, throughout the novel Ecotopian citizens are characterized as clever, technologically resourceful, emotionally expressive, and even occasionally violent  but also socially responsible, patriotic. They often live in extended families, and tend to live by choice in ethnically separated localities. Their economic enterprises are generally employee-owned and -controlled. The current governmental administration that of a woman-led (but not exclusively female) party, and government structures are highly decentralized.
     </p>
     <p>
      The novel concludes with Weston's finding himself enchanted by Ecotopian life and deciding to stay in Ecotopia as its interpreter to the wider world.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Values_exemplified_in_the_novel">
       Values exemplified in the novel
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Values exemplified in the novel">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The values embodied by those Ecotopians depicted in the novel reflect the values espoused by its author. Callenbach said that his Ecotopians attach fundamental importance to environmental and social stability within which variety can flourish. They value creativity. They ensure equality for women. They implement the protection and restoration of natural systems. They promote food production in their cities.
     </p>
     <p>
      As well, they treasure personal quality-of-life values, such as health and friendliness, and both meaningful discussion and play.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callenbach.2C_Ernest_and_Heddle.2C_James_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Callenbach.2C_Ernest_and_Heddle.2C_James-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Callenbach began writing the novel by depicting the
      <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
       recycling
      </a>
      of valuable materials and substances by the society; he saw a much-expanded role for recycling of all sorts, and this is key to many concepts underpinning Ecotopia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callenbach.2C_Ernest_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Callenbach.2C_Ernest-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Anticipation_of_emerging_realities">
       Anticipation of emerging realities
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anticipation of emerging realities">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Worth mentioning is Callenbach's speculation on the roles of TV in his envisioned society. In some ways anticipating
      <a href="/wiki/C-SPAN" title="C-SPAN">
       C-SPAN
      </a>
      , which would first be broadcast in 1979, the story mentions that the daily life of the legislature and some of that of the judicial courts is televised in Ecotopia. Even highly technical debates are televised, addressing the needs and desires of Ecotopian viewers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another interesting detail in the story is "
      <a href="/wiki/Print_on_demand" title="Print on demand">
       print on demand
      </a>
      " (POD) publishing. Ecotopian customers could choose selected print media from a
      <a href="/wiki/Jukebox" title="Jukebox">
       jukebox
      </a>
      -like device that would then print and bind the book. In the 21st century, POD services that print, bind and ship books for customers who order on-line, have become commonplace.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">
       Impact
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotopia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impact">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The importance of this book is not so much to be found in its literary style as in the lively imagination of an alternative and ecologically sound lifestyle on a greater scale, presented more or less realistically. It expressed on paper the dream of an alternative future held by many in the movements of the 1970s and later. Even the names of the two characters most reflective of their respective viewpoints  "Will West(on)", the representative for materialist American culture and "Vera Allwen" (= "All women + all men"), the President and spokeswoman for Ecotopia  suggest the degree to which the author intended the book to be a reflection of what he saw as American ecological and cultural deficiencies.
     </p>
     <p>
      However, in contrast to much of the Green movement in contemporary America, with its preference for regulation, Callenbach's Ecotopia has relatively laissez-faire economic tendencies, guided by intense moral pressure toward sustainable practices both in private life and in business.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1981, Callenbach published
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ecotopia_Emerging" title="Ecotopia Emerging">
        Ecotopia Emerging
       </a>
      </i>
      , a multi-strand "prequel" suggesting how the sustainable nation of Ecotopia could have come into existence.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1990, Audio Renaissance released a partial dramatization of
      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>
      on audiocassettes in the form of recordings of a radio network broadcast (the
      <i>
       Allied News Network
      </i>
      replacing the
      <i>
       Times-Post
      </i>
      ). The tape-recorded diaries of William Weston were read by the book's author, Ernest Callenbach. Weston's reports were read by veteran news reporter
      <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Newman" title="Edwin Newman">
       Edwin Newman
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In the online
      <i>
       Earth Island Journal
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>
      was reviewed by Brian Smith, identifying himself as a child not of the 1960s but the 1980s. He read the novel 30 years after it was first published, and said of it: "I felt great affinity for the details of the world Callenbach predicted. Even better, I was impressed by how many of his ideas came to pass."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Child_of_Dystopia_Reads_Ecotopia_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-A_Child_of_Dystopia_Reads_Ecotopia-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Ecotopia is now required reading in a number of colleges (see NY Times article
      <i>
       The Novel That Predicted Portland
      </i>
      referenced below).
     </p>
     <h2>

      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>

      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>
      is a shoddy amalgam of Swedish social democracy, Swiss neutrality, and Yugoslav workers' co-ops cobbled together with the authoritarianism of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/A_Blueprint_for_Survival" title="A Blueprint for Survival">
        A Blueprint for Survival
       </a>
      </i>
      ...
      <i>
       Ecotopia
      </i>
      is a flawed vision of a flawed future."

         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      In marked contrast,
      <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">
       Ralph Nader
      </a>
      praised the book, noting that "None of the happy conditions in Ecotopia are beyond the technical or resource reach of our society."
