     <p>
      <b>
       Robert Cochrane
      </b>
      (26 January 1931  3 July 1966), who was born as
      <b>
       Roy Bowers
      </b>
      , was an English occultist who founded the tradition of Pagan Witchcraft known as
      <a href="/wiki/Cochrane%27s_Craft" title="Cochrane's Craft">
       Cochrane's Craft
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Born to a working-class family in West London, he became interested in occultism after attending a
      <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Psychical_Research" title="Society for Psychical Research">
       Society for Psychical Research
      </a>
      lecture, taking a particular interest in witchcraft. He founded one coven, but it soon collapsed.
     </p>
     <p>
      He began to claim to have been born to a hereditary family of witches whose practices stretched back to at least the 17th century; these statements have later been dismissed. He subsequently went on to found a
      <a href="/wiki/Coven" title="Coven">
       coven
      </a>
      known as the Clan of Tubal Cain, through which he propagated his Craft. In 1966, he committed suicide.
     </p>
     <p>
      Cochrane continues to be seen as a key inspirational figure in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_Witchcraft" title="Traditional Witchcraft">
       Traditional Witchcraft
      </a>
      movement. Ever since his death, a number of Neopagan and magical groups have continued to adhere to his teachings.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As noted by Michael Howard, "factual details about Cochrane's early life are scant".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck5_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck5-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He was born in an area between
      <a href="/wiki/Hammersmith" title="Hammersmith">
       Hammersmith
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shepherds_Bush" title="Shepherds Bush">
       Shepherds Bush
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
       West London
      </a>
      into a family of eight children.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_41_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_41-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He later described it as a "slum", though this has been refuted by family members, who considered it a "respectable
      <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">
       working class
      </a>
      area".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_41_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_41-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There, he lived through the
      <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">
       Blitz
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_41_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_41-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some of his family emigrated to
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      , while he went to art school, living a bohemian lifestyle.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_41_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_41-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      His aunt would later claim that he first took an interest in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occultism" title="Occultism">
       occultism
      </a>
      after attending a talk of the
      <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Psychical_Research" title="Society for Psychical Research">
       Society for Psychical Research
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">
       Kensington
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:246px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #ACE1AF;">
      <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
       <div>
        <p>
         "I come from the country of the oak, ash and thorn... I describe myself as a '
         <a href="/wiki/Cunning_folk" title="Cunning folk">
          pellar
         </a>
         '. The People are formed in clans or families and describe themselves by the local name of the Deity. I am a member of the People of Goda - the Clan of Tubal Cain. We were known locally as 'witches', 'the Good People', Green gowns (females only), 'Horsemen' and finally Wizards."
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="text-align: right;">
        Cochrane.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck8_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck8-4">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         4
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      During the early 1950s, he joined the army as a part of his
      <a href="/wiki/National_service" title="National service">
       national service
      </a>
      , but went absent without leave; as punishment, he was sentenced to 90 days imprisonment in a military prison in
      <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">
       Colchester
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_41_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_41-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He admitted to having a violent temper in his youth, but calmed after meeting Jane, whom he would later marry.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_41_2-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_41-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For a time he worked for
      <a href="/wiki/London_Transport_(brand)" title="London Transport (brand)">
       London Transport
      </a>
      as a
      <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">
       blacksmith
      </a>
      in a foundry; one potential reason why he adopted the mythical blacksmith
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clan_of_Tubal_Cain" title="Clan of Tubal Cain">
       Tubal Cain
      </a>
      as a part of the mythos for his tradition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_42_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_42-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He and Jane later worked as bargees transporting coal around the English Midlands, taking an interest in the folklore of the Bargee community, later believing that it contained traces of the "Old Faith".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_42_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_42-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      By the start of the 1960s, he was living with Jane and their son on a
      <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">
       London County Council
      </a>
      -run
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_estate" title="Council estate">
       council estate
      </a>
      near to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slough,_Berkshire" title="Slough, Berkshire">
       Slough, Berkshire
      </a>
      ; he did not like the neighbours, considering them "the biggest load of monkeys there have been trained since the
      <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">
       Ark
      </a>
      ."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He worked as a typographical draughtsman in an office, but disliked his job.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He founded a witches' coven, but it soon broke up as one member died and he fell out with another.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Later, in the 1960s, he claimed that members of his family had been practitioners of
      <a href="/wiki/Witch-cult_hypothesis" title="Witch-cult hypothesis">
       an ancient pagan Witch-cult
      </a>
      since at least the 17th century, and that two of them had been executed for it.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck7_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck7-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Claiming that his great-grandfather had been "the last Grand Master of the Staffordshire witches",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck7_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck7-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      he said that his grandparents had abandoned the Craft and converted to Methodism, for which his great-grandfather had cursed them. He said that his father had practiced witchcraft, but that he kept it a secret, and made his wife promise to not tell his son, Robert. Despite her oath, according to Cochrane, after his father's death, her mother did in fact tell him, at which he embraced his heritage.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck7_6-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck7-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He asserted that his Aunt Lucy actually taught him all about the faith.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck7_6-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck7-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, these claims would later be denounced by members of his own family. His nephew, Martin Lloyd, has refuted that the family were ever Witches, insisting that they were Methodists,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoward201146_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoward201146-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while his wife Jane also later asserted that Cochrane's claims to have come from a hereditary Witch-Cult were bogus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011208_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011208-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_the_Clan_of_Tubal_Cain">
       Founding the Clan of Tubal Cain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding the Clan of Tubal Cain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cochrane formed his second coven, which provided the basis for the Clan of Tubal Cain, in the early 1960s.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Searching for members, he placed an advert in the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchester_Guardian" title="Manchester Guardian">
        Manchester Guardian
       </a>
      </i>
      requesting that anyone interested in
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">
       Graves
      </a>
      '
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_White_Goddess" title="The White Goddess">
        The White Goddess
       </a>
      </i>
      contact him; he received a response from the schoolteacher Ronald Milland White, known to his friends as "Chalky".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      White then introduced him to George Arthur Stannard (also known as George Winter), who ran a betting shop near
      <a href="/wiki/Kings_Cross,_London" title="Kings Cross, London">
       Kings Cross
      </a>
      in Central London.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      White and Stannard joined this nascent coven, the latter taking up the position of Summoner.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_43_3-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_43-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Describing his creation of his Witchcraft tradition, later Maid of the Clan Shani Oates remarked that "Like any true craftsman, he was able to mold raw material into a magical synthesis, creating a marvelous working system, at once instinctively true and intrinsically beautiful."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The group performed their rituals either at Cochrane's house, or, more often, at
      <a href="/wiki/Burnham_Beeches" title="Burnham Beeches">
       Burnham Beeches
      </a>
      , though they also performed rituals at the
      <a href="/wiki/South_Downs" title="South Downs">
       South Downs
      </a>
      , after which they would stay the night at Doreen Valiente's flat in
      <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">
       Brighton
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cochrane.27s_Craft">
       Cochrane's Craft
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cochrane's Craft">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        Main article see
        <a href="/wiki/Cochrane%27s_Craft" title="Cochrane's Craft">
         Cochrane's Craft
        </a>
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The Clan of Tubal Cain revere a
      <a href="/wiki/Horned_God" title="Horned God">
       Horned God
      </a>
      and Fate, expressed as the Pale Faced Goddess, named Hekate. The Goddess was viewed as "
      <a href="/wiki/The_White_Goddess" title="The White Goddess">
       the White Goddess
      </a>
      ", a term taken from
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">
       Robert Graves
      </a>
      ' book of the same name. The God was associated with fire, the underworld and time, and was described as "the goat-god of fire, craft, lower magics, fertility and death". The God was known by several names, most notable
      <a href="/wiki/Tubal-cain" title="Tubal-cain">
       Tubal Cain
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Bran" title="Bran">
       Bran
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wayland_Smith" title="Wayland Smith">
       Wayland
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Herne_the_Hunter" title="Herne the Hunter">
       Herne
      </a>
      . Cochrane's tradition held that these two deities had a son, the Horn Child, who was a young sun god.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, differences between the two also existed, for instance Gardnerians always worked
      <a href="/wiki/Skyclad_(Neopaganism)" title="Skyclad (Neopaganism)">
       skyclad
      </a>
      , or naked, whereas Cochrane's followers wore black hooded robes. Similarly, Cochrane's coven did not practice
      <a href="/wiki/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">
       scourging
      </a>
      , as Gardner's did. Cochrane himself disliked Gardner and the Gardnerians and often ridiculed them, even coining the term "Gardnerian" himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Whilst they used ritual tools, they differed somewhat from those used by Gardner's coven. The main five tools in Cochrane's Craft were a ritual knife, a staff known as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stang_(witchcraft)" title="Stang (witchcraft)">
       stang
      </a>
      (according to
      <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Hutton" title="Ronald Hutton">
       Ronald Hutton
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       Triumph of the Moon
      </i>
      , Bowers is responsible for the introduction of this into Wicca), a cup, a stone (used as a whetstone to sharpen the knife), and a ritual cord worn by the coven members.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cochrane never made use of a
      <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Shadows" title="Book of Shadows">
       Book of Shadows
      </a>
      or similar such books, but worked from a "traditional way of doing things", which was both "spontaneous and
      <a href="/wiki/Shamanism" title="Shamanism">
       shamanistic
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ValienteCochrane_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ValienteCochrane-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Valiente notes that this spontaneity was partly because the Cochrane coven did not use a
      <i>
       Book of Shadows
      </i>
      in which structured rituals were pre-recorded, leading to more creativity.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cochrane.27s_later_years">
       Cochrane's later years
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cochrane's later years">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:246px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #ACE1AF;">
      <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
       <div>
        <p>
         "I am a witch descended from a family of witches. Genuine witchcraft is not
         <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">
          paganism
         </a>
         , though it retains the memory of ancient faiths... [Witchcraft is] the last real
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mystery_cult" title="Mystery cult">
          mystery cult
         </a>
         to survive, with a very complex and evolved philosophy that has strong affinities with many Christian beliefs. The concept of a sacrificial god was not new to the ancient world; it is not new to a witch... I come from an old witch family. My mother told me of things that had been told to her grandmother by her grandmother. I have two ancestors who died by hanging for the practice of witchcraft."
        </p>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="text-align: right;">
        Cochrane, "Genuine Witchcraft is Defended", 1963.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Cochrane arose to public prominence in November 1963, when he published an article titled "Genuine Witchcraft is Defended" in
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psychic_News" title="Psychic News">
        Psychic News
       </a>
      </i>
      , a weekly
      <a href="/wiki/Spiritualism" title="Spiritualism">
       Spiritualist
      </a>
      publication. In it, he outlined his beliefs regarding Witchcraft, and first publicly made the claim that he came from a hereditary line of Witches.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_44-45_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_44-45-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1964, further individuals joined the Clan. Among these was Evan John Jones, who would later become an author upon the subject of pagan witchcraft. Jones had met Cochrane through his wife Jane, as they both worked at the same company.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck_10-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Witchcraft_Research_Association_and_Gardnerianism">
       Witchcraft Research Association and Gardnerianism
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Witchcraft Research Association and Gardnerianism">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      His friend and correspondent, the Qabbalist and ceremonial magician
      <a href="/wiki/William_G._Gray" title="William G. Gray">
       William G. Gray
      </a>
      introduced him to John Math, a practicing Witch and the son of the
      <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Gainsborough" title="Earl of Gainsborough">
       Earl of Gainsborough
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_48_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_48-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Math joined the Clan, and invited Cochrane to publish some of his articles in
      <i>
       Pentagram
      </i>
      , the newsletter of the
      <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft_Research_Association" title="Witchcraft Research Association">
       Witchcraft Research Association
      </a>
      (WRA), which Math had recently co-founded along with
      <a href="/wiki/Sybil_Leek" title="Sybil Leek">
       Sybil Leek
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_58_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_58-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cochrane took a particularly hostile attitude toward the Gardnerian tradition of Wicca, deeming its founder,
      <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Gardner_(Wiccan)" title="Gerald Gardner (Wiccan)">
       Gerald Gardner
      </a>
      , to be a con man and sexual deviant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011211_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011211-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He referred to the tradition as "Gardnerism" and its adherents as "Gardnerians", the latter of which would become the standard term for such practitioners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEValiente1989122Doyle_White2011211_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEValiente1989122Doyle_White2011211-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Upon examining Cochrane's writings, Pagan studies scholar Ethan Doyle White has identified four possible reasons for this animosity. First, Cochrane disliked the publicity seeking that a variety of prominent Gardnerians (among them Gardner,
      <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Crowther_(Wiccan)" title="Patricia Crowther (Wiccan)">
       Patricia Crowther
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Bone" title="Eleanor Bone">
       Eleanor Bone
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Monique_Wilson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Monique Wilson (page does not exist)">
       Monique Wilson
      </a>
      ), had embarked on; they appeared on television and in tabloid newspapers to present their tradition as the face of Wicca in Britain, which angered Cochrane, whose own tradition differed from Gardnerianism in focus.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011209_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011209-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Second, Cochrane disliked Gardnerianism's focus on ritual liturgy and magic, instead emphasising a mystical search for gnosis, while third, Cochrane appeared jealous of the success that Gardnerianism had achieved, which was far in advance of that achieved by his own tradition.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011210_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011210-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The fourth point purported by Doyle White was that Cochrane might have been hostile to Gardnerianism as a result of a poor experience with it in the past.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011211_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011211-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cochrane was nevertheless intrigued by Gardnerianism. He was a friend of Gardnerian High Priestess Cynthia Swettenham, who introduced him and his wife to the Gardnerian
      <a href="/wiki/Bricket_Wood_coven" title="Bricket Wood coven">
       Bricket Wood coven
      </a>
      on one occasion, probably in late 1965 or early 1966.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011212_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011212-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was here that he met the coven's High Priest
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_Bracelin" title="Jack Bracelin">
       Jack Bracelin
      </a>
      , who liked Jane Bowers but disliked Cochrane, later describing him as a "weirdie" and his subsequent letters as "a load of drivel".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011212_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011212-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is known that Cochrane was also in contact with two other prominent Gardnerian initiates around this time; Eleanor Bone and
      <a href="/wiki/Lois_Bourne" title="Lois Bourne">
       Lois Bourne
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011213_23-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011213-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is also evidence to suggest that Cochrane was initiated into the Gardnerian tradition un until the second degree by Cynthia Swettenham and her partner Dick, who ran a coven in West London.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011216.E2.80.93218_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011216.E2.80.93218-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Doreen_Valiente_and_the_Clan.27s_breakup">
       Doreen Valiente and the Clan's breakup
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Doreen Valiente and the Clan's breakup">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1964 Cochrane met
      <a href="/wiki/Doreen_Valiente" title="Doreen Valiente">
       Doreen Valiente
      </a>
      , who had formerly been a High Priestess of the Gardnerian Bricket Wood coven, through mutual friends which he had met at a gathering at
      <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Tor" title="Glastonbury Tor">
       Glastonbury Tor
      </a>
      held by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brotherhood_of_the_Essenes" title="Brotherhood of the Essenes">
       Brotherhood of the Essenes
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The two became friends, and Valiente joined the Clan of Tubal Cain. She later remarked that there were certain things in this coven that were better than those in Gardner's, for instance she thought that "[Cochrane] believed in getting close to nature as few Gardnerian witches at that time seemed to do".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She also commented on how Cochrane did not seem to want lots of publicity, as Gardner had done, something which she admired. She began to become dissatisfied with Cochrane however, over some of his practices.
     </p>
     <p>
      Cochrane often insulted and mocked Gardnerian witches, which annoyed Valiente. This reached such an extreme that at one point in 1966 he called for "a
      <a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Long_Knives" title="Night of the Long Knives">
       Night of the Long Knives
      </a>
      of the Gardnerians", at which point Doreen, in her own words, "rose up and challenged him in the presence of the rest of the coven. I told him that I was fed up with listening to all this senseless malice, and that, if a 'Night of the Long Knives' was what his sick little soul craved, he could get on with it, but he could get on with it alone, because I had better things to do".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valiente_129_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Valiente_129-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      She left the coven, and never came back.
     </p>
     <p>
      After Doreen's departure, Cochrane committed
      <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">
       adultery
      </a>
      with a new woman who had joined the coven, and, according to other coven members, did not care that his wife Jane knew.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valiente_129_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Valiente_129-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In May 1966, Jane left Cochrane, initiating divorce proceedings and considering performing a death rite against her husband involving the sacrifice of a black cockerel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_70_28-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_70-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Without her, the coven collapsed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_71_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Howard_71-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Cochrane was also aware of
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cardell" title="Charles Cardell">
       Charles Cardell
      </a>
      , who ran his own coven in Suffolk, but disliked him.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoyleWhite192_30-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DoyleWhite192-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Joe_Wilson_and_the_1734_Tradition.2C_ca._1973">
       Joe Wilson and the 1734 Tradition,
       <i>
        ca.
       </i>
       1973
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Joe Wilson and the 1734 Tradition, ca. 1973">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In December 1965 to April 1966,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Cochrane corresponded with an American witch named
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bearwalker_Wilson" title="Joseph Bearwalker Wilson">
       Joe Wilson
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Mr. Wilson formed a new tradition, known as the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1734_tradition" title="1734 tradition">
       1734 tradition
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w1734_33-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-w1734-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      based upon teachings of Ruth Wynn Owen, a tradition taught by a man he refers to as Sean, and Robert Cochrane's teaching.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trad1734_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trad1734-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The numerological number '1724' (a possible misprint in the book), was explained by Doreen Valiente in her 1989 book
      <i>
       The Rebirth of Witchcraft
      </i>
      . Valiente claimed that Cochrane had given the American witch Justine Glass a photograph of a copper platter with '1724' printed on it for her 1965 book
      <i>
       Witchcraft, the Sixth Sense - and Us
      </i>
      . He had told Glass that it depicted a witch's ritual bowl that had been in his family for many centuries. Valiente revealed that this was a lie by Cochrane - she had herself, in fact, bought that very item for him only the year before in a
      <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">
       Brighton
      </a>
      antiques shop to be used in a ritual.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w1734_33-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-w1734-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death.2C_1966">
       Death, 1966
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death, 1966">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Cochrane ingested
      <a href="/wiki/Atropa_belladonna" title="Atropa belladonna">
       belladonna
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chlordiazepoxide" title="Chlordiazepoxide">
       Librium
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">
       Midsummer
      </a>
      eve 1966, and died nine days later in hospital without recovering consciousness. He left a suicide note expressing his intent to kill himself "while of sound mind".
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
       Personal life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Valiente described Cochrane as "a remarkable man",
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEValiente19907_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEValiente19907-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      asserting that he "had something" which could be termed "magical power, charisma or what you will. He may have been devious; but he was no charlatan."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEValuente19908_37-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEValuente19908-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      According to Jonathan Tapsell, Cochrane was "an unsung giant of modern Wicca" due to the fact that he "gave inspiration to those who came later to escape the narrow confines of Gardner's philosophy".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETapsell201369_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETapsell201369-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Michael Howard considered him to be "one of the most fascinating, enigmatic and controversial figures of the modern Craft revival."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoward201141_39-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoward201141-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      John of Monmouth claimed that Cochrane was "the man behind, what is now called, 'Traditional Witchcraft'."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohn_of_Monmouth201119_40-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohn_of_Monmouth201119-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Historian Ethan Doyle White asserted that Cochrane left behind "an ever-expanding legacy", noting that by the 21st century, he had become an "almost tutelary figure" within the Traditional Witchcraft movement, and warrants the title of "Father of Traditional Witchcraft" more than any other occultist.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White201377.E2.80.9378_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White201377.E2.80.9378-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Elsewhere, Doyle White asserted that Cochrane had been "without doubt the most influential" of Gardner's rivals in the mid-20th century Wiccan movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011205_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White2011205-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following Cochrane's death, the Mantle of Magister of the Clan of Tubal Cain as given to Evan John Jones.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White201377_43-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White201377-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Another of Cochrane's initiates, Evan John Jones wrote a book,
      <i>
       Witchcraft: A Tradition Renewed
      </i>
      (a collaboration with
      <a href="/wiki/Doreen_Valiente" title="Doreen Valiente">
       Doreen Valiente
      </a>
      )
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      outlining his version of the Cochrane tradition. Whilst there was no objective way to validate Cochrane's claim to be a hereditary witch, the experience of being in his coven was that of being one of "Diana's darling crew" (Jones, cited in Clifton, 2006). A group called The Regency was formed by Ronald "Chalky" White and his friend, George Winter, to preserve and continue Cochrane's tradition; it eventually disbanded in 1978 but recently a website has been set up to preserve The Regency memory.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following correspondence with Cochrane in the mid 1960s, an American named
      <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bearwalker_Wilson" title="Joseph Bearwalker Wilson">
       Joseph Wilson
      </a>
      founded a tradition called the
      <a href="/wiki/1734_Tradition" title="1734 Tradition">
       1734 Tradition
      </a>
      , based on his teachings circa 1974.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White201377_43-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoyle_White201377-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trad1734_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Trad1734-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A similarly Cochrane-inspired tradition was the Roebuck,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      whose lore is also used by the "Ancient Keltic Church".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are currently two groups operating under the title of
      <i>
       Clan of Tubal Cain
      </i>
      ; each of them has their own interpretation and expression of the legacy of Robert Cochrane, although they may not necessarily completely agree with each other.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Published_writings">
       Published writings
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Cochrane_(witch)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Published writings">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Cochrane did not write any books in his lifetime,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roebuck5_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Roebuck5-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though some of his collected writings and letters have been assembled since his death:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <i>
        The Roebuck in the Thicket: An Anthology of the Robert Cochrane Witchcraft Tradition
       </i>
       , Capall Bann Publishing, 2001
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        The Robert Cochrane Letters: An Insight into Modern Traditional Witchcraft
       </i>
       , Capall Bann Publishing, 2002
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Other works have been published about Cochrane based upon his teachings, and on his Craft, or based upon his ideas
     </p>

      <li>
       <i>
        Sacred Mask, Sacred Dance
       </i>
       by Evan John Jones with
       <a href="/wiki/Chas_S._Clifton" title="Chas S. Clifton">
        Chas S. Clifton
       </a>
       , Llewellyn, 1997
      </li>
      <li>
       <i>
        Witchcraft, A Tradition Renewed
       </i>
       , by Evan John Jones with
       <a href="/wiki/Doreen_Valiente" title="Doreen Valiente">
        Doreen Valiente
       </a>
       , Hale, 1989
      </li>
      <li>
       " The Star Crossed Serpent Vol One by Evan John Jones, Edited by Shani Oates, Mandrake of Oxford 2011
      </li>
      <li>
       " The Star crossed Serpent Vol Two by Shani Oates, Mandrake of Oxford, 2012
      </li>
      <li>
       " The People of Goda by Shani Oates, Create Space, 2012
      </li>
      <li>
       " Tubelo's Green Fire by Shani Oates, Mandrake of Oxford, 2010
      </li>
      <li>
       " The Arcane Veil by Shani Oates, Mandrake of Oxford, 2011
      </li>
