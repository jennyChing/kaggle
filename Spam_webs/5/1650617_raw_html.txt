         <div class="toptitle" id="entrytitle">
          <h1>
           Guy Vanderhaeghe on bad scenery and imaginary people
          </h1>
         </div>
         <div class="entrymeta" id="entrymeta">
          <!--entrymeta-->
          <span class="byline">
           On
           <abbr class="published" title="2014-05-30T06:45:41-05:00">
            May 30, 2014  6:45 AM
           </abbr>
           <div id="headlines" style="display:inline">
            <span class="viafoura" style="display: inline;">
             <span class="vf-counter vf-widget" data-path="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2014/05/guy-vanderhaeghe-on-bad-scenery-and-imaginary-people.html" data-widget="counter">
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!--ENDentrymeta-->
        </div>
        <!--ENDentryheader-->
        <div class="entrycontent" id="entrycontent">
         <!--mecontent-->
         <!-- removed the platform media player -->
         <div class="entrybody clearfix" id="entrybody">
          <!--entrybody-->
          <div>
           <i>
            The legendary author and 2014 Margaret Laurence Lecturer answers questions from the Canadian literati and reveals possibly the strangest literary insult we've ever heard.
           </i>
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
         </div>
         <!--ENDentrybody-->
         <div class="entrymore" id="entrymore">
          <img alt="" class="mt-image-left" height="333" src="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/images/Vanderhaeghe,%20Guy.JPG" style="float: left; margin: 0px 20px 20px 0px;" width="250"/>
          <div>
           <b>
            1.
            <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2014/08/gail-bowen-on-creating-strong-characters-in-crime-fiction.html" target="_blank">
             <font style="font-size: 1em;">
              Gail Bowen
             </font>
            </a>
            asks: Claims of suffering writer's block are just excuses for laziness. Agree or disagree?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           Maybe not an excuse for laziness so much as a fear of failure. If you dont try to do a thing, you cant fail at it.
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            2.
           </b>
           <b style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px; color: rgb(0, 0, 0); font-size: 12px; line-height: 18px;">
            <font style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-size: 1em;">
             <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2012/11/sharon-butala-the-brink.html" style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px; outline: none 0px; color: rgb(159, 1, 95);">
              Sharon Butala
             </a>
            </font>
           </b>
           <b>
            asks: Do you ever feeltrapped by your writing life and wish you could escape?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           I seem to be rewriting all the questions. I dont feel so much trapped by the writing life as I feel trapped inside my own head when Im writing. For me, writing a novel is often like running on a treadmill, a lot of effort and the bad scenery never changes.
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            3.
            <font style="font-size: 1em;">
             <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2014/06/kate-pullinger-how-i-wrote-landing-gear.html" target="_blank">
              Kate Pullinger
             </a>
            </font>
            asks: Do you pay attention to the opinions of your familyparents, spouse, siblings, children, etc.when it comes to your writing, both in terms of what you write about, but also how you write?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           Never. Somehow Ive never been beset by that problem.
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            4.
           </b>
           <b style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px; color: rgb(0, 0, 0); font-size: 12px; line-height: 18px;">
            <font style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-size: 1em;">
             <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2011/10/battle-1-to-plot-or-not.html#andrew" style="margin: 0px; padding: 0px; outline: none 0px; color: rgb(159, 1, 95);" target="_blank">
              Andrew Pyper
             </a>
            </font>
            <font face="Arial, sans-serif">
             asks
            </font>
           </b>
           <b>
            : Have you ever veered away from something in your workexplicit sex, say, or bloody violence, or a character uttering offensive thoughtsbecause it might soil the book for certain sensitive readers? If so, have you regretted it?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           Not that Im aware of. But then self-censorship is likely to be largely unconscious.
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            5.
            <font style="font-size: 1.25em;">
             <font style="font-size: 0.8em;">
              <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2011/09/timothy-taylor-why-novels-still-matter.html" target="_blank">
               Timothy Taylor
              </a>
             </font>
            </font>
            asks: What book were you reading when you were first inspired to write? How old were you? Why do you think that book affected you the way it did at that moment in your life?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           Setting aside comic books, it was probably
           <i>
            The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn.
           </i>
           I read that when I was about nine. What appealed to me about that book at that time was Hucks blissful embrace of freedom. He had escaped civilization to float down the Mississippi on a raft with Jim. I was starting to hate school and I wanted to run away from it the way Jim had.
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            6.
            <font style="font-size: 1em;">
             <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2013/02/donna-morrisey-takes-the-magic-8.html" target="_blank">
              Donna Morrissey
             </a>
            </font>
            asks: What are the biggest hurdles to overcome in your personal life while you're creating a book?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           The biggest hurdle is giving the real people around me the attention they deserve. The imaginary people seem to suck up a lot of psychic energy.
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            7.
            <font style="font-size: 1em;">
             <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2012/11/pasha-malla-takes-the-magic-8.html" target="_blank">
              Pasha Malla
             </a>
            </font>
            asks: Please quote one egregiously stupid criticismeither specific or generalof your writing, and address, refute or mock it.
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           I was once called a voice from the shithouse. Does that mean I read a lot?
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <b>
            8.
            <font style="font-size: 1em;">
             <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2013/11/bloodlines-shadows-by-jj-lee.html" target="_blank">
              JJ Lee
             </a>
            </font>
            asks: If you had to write a country song right now, what would the chorus be?
           </b>
          </div>
          <div>
           Why are questions so easy and answers so hard?
          </div>
          <div>
           <br/>
          </div>
          <div>
           <i>
            <br/>
            Guy Vanderhaeghe has authored five novels, three short story collections, two plays, and has won among other honours the Governor Generals Literary Award (twice), CBCs Canada Reads, the Writers' Trust Timothy Findley Award, the Canadian Booksellers Association Libris Prize for Fiction Book of the Year, and the Lieutenant Governors Arts Award for Lifetime Achievement in the Arts. He is an Officer of the Order of Canada, a Member of the Saskatchewan Order of Merit, and a Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada. He lives in Saskatoon.
           </i>
          </div>
          <div>
           <i>
            <br/>
           </i>
          </div>
          <div>
           <div>
           </div>
           <div>

             <i>

               Margaret Laurence Lecture
              </a>
              in St. John's, Newfoundland.
              <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/books/canadawrites/2014/06/guy-vanderhaeghe-on-the-illicit-reading-that-made-him-a-writer.html">

              </a>
              .
             </i>
            </font>
