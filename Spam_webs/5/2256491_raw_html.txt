   //set primary image container to same width as primary image
	function primaryImageSizer() {
		var primaryImageContainer = $("div.primaryImage");
		var primaryImage = $("div.primaryImage img");
		var imageWidth = primaryImage.width();

		if (primaryImage.length &gt; 0) {

   //set top padding of the workspace to the height of the ribbon
function setTopPadding() {
	   
	var wrkElem = document.getElementById('s4-workspace');
	var ribHeight = document.getElementById('topfixedcontentarea').offsetHeight;

	if (window.location.search.match("[?&amp;]IsDlg=1")) {
		//margin works better for dialogs b/c of scrollbars

	    wrkElem.style.paddingTop = '0px';

	   else {
	     //padding works better for the main window
	     wrkElem.style.paddingTop = ribHeight + 'px';
	   }
	}
	 
	// bind top padding reset to ribbon resize event so that the page always lays out correctly.
	ExecuteOrDelayUntilScriptLoaded(function () { SP.UI.Workspace.add_resized(setTopPadding); }, "init.js");

 });

              </div>
              <div aria-labelledby="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ctl02_label" class="ms-rtestate-field" id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ctl02__ControlWrapper_RichHtmlField" style="display:inline">
               <p>

             <div class="primaryImage">
              <div class="primaryImagephotoCredit prettify">

             <div class="prettify">
              <div id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ArticleBody2010Display_label" style="display:none">
               Article Body 2010
              </div>
              <div aria-labelledby="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ArticleBody2010Display_label" class="ms-rtestate-field" id="ctl00_PlaceHolderTwoColumnBodyContent_EditModePane2_ArticleBody2010Display__ControlWrapper_RichHtmlField" style="display:inline">
               <p>
               </p>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivcenter" style="width:475px;margin-left:auto;margin-right:auto">
                <div style="width:475px;height:300px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/infrared_secondary.jpg" style="width:475px"/>
                </div>
                <div class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Sara Dickherber
                </div>
                <div class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 The eye can detect light at wavelengths in the visual spectrum. Other wavelengths, such as infrared and ultraviolet, are supposed to be invisible to the human eye, but Washington University scientists have found that under certain conditions, it's possible for us to see otherwise invisible infrared light.
                </div>
               </div>
               <br/>
               <p>
               </p>
               <p>
                Any science textbook will tell you we cant see infrared light. Like X-rays and radio waves, infrared light waves are outside the visual spectrum.
               </p>
               <p>
                But an international team of researchers co-led by scientists at
                <a href="http://medicine.wustl.edu/" target="_blank">
                 Washington University School of Medicine
                </a>
                in St. Louis has found that under certain conditions, the retina can sense infrared light after all.
               </p>
               <p>
                Using cells from the retinas of mice and people, and powerful lasers that emit pulses of infrared light, the researchers found that when laser light pulses rapidly, light-sensing cells in the retina sometimes get a double hit of infrared energy. When that happens, the eye is able to detect light that falls outside the visible spectrum.
               </p>
               <p>
                Were using what we learned in these experiments to try to develop a new tool that would allow physicians to not only examine the eye but also to stimulate specific parts of the retina to determine whether its functioning properly, said senior investigator Vladimir J. Kefalov, PhD, associate professor of ophthalmology and visual sciences at Washington University. We hope that ultimately this discovery will have some very practical applications.
               </p>
               <p>
                The findings are published Dec. 1 in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) Online Early Edition. Collaborators include
                <span>
                 scientists in Cleveland, Poland, Switzerland and Norway,
                 <span style="display:inline-block">
                 </span>
                </span>
               </p>
               <p>
                The research was initiated after scientists on the research team reported seeing occasional flashes of green light while working with an infrared laser. Unlike the laser pointers used in lecture halls or as toys, the powerful infrared laser the scientists worked with emits light waves thought to be invisible to the human eye.
                <br/>
               </p>
               <p>
                They were able to see the laser light, which was outside of the normal visible range, and we really wanted to figure out how they were able to sense light that was supposed to be invisible, said Frans Vinberg, PhD, one of the studys lead authors and a postdoctoral research associate in the Department of Ophthalmology and Visual Sciences at Washington University.
               </p>
               <p>
                Vinberg, Kefalov and their colleagues examined the scientific literature and revisited reports of people seeing infrared light. They repeated previous experiments in which infrared light had been seen, and they analyzed such light from several lasers to see what they could learn about how and why it sometimes is visible.
               </p>
               <p>
                We experimented with laser pulses of different durations that delivered the same total number of photons, and we found that the shorter the pulse, the more likely it was a person could see it, Vinberg explained. Although the length of time between pulses was so short that it couldnt be noticed by the naked eye, the existence of those pulses was very important in allowing people to see this invisible light.
               </p>
               <p>
                Normally, a particle of light, called a photon, is absorbed by the retina, which then creates a molecule called a photopigment, which begins the process of converting light into vision. In standard vision, each of a large number of photopigments absorbs a single photon.
               </p>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivright" style="width:300px">
                <div style="width:300px;height:300px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/Kefalov_secondary.jpg" style="width:300px"/>
                </div>
                <div class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Robert Boston
                </div>
                <div class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Kefalov's team developed this adapter that allowed scientists to analyze retinal cells and photopigment molecules as they were exposed to infrared light. The device already is commercially available and in use at several vision research centers around the world.
                </div>
               </div>
               But packing a lot of photons in a short pulse of the rapidly pulsing laser light makes it possible for two photons to be absorbed at one time by a single photopigment, and the combined energy of the two light particles is enough to activate the pigment and allow the eye to see what normally is invisible.
               <p>
               </p>
               <p>
                The visible spectrum includes waves of light that are 400-720 nanometers long, explained Kefalov, an associate professor of ophthalmology and visual sciences. But if a pigment molecule in the retina is hit in rapid succession by a pair of photons that are 1,000 nanometers long, those light particles will deliver the same amount of energy as a single hit from a 500-nanometer photon, which is well within the visible spectrum. Thats how we are able to see it.
               </p>
               <p>
                Although the researchers are the first to report that the eye can sense light through this mechanism, the idea of using less powerful laser light to make things visible isnt new. The two-photon microscope, for example, uses lasers to detect fluorescent molecules deep in tissues. And the researchers said they already are working on ways to use the two-photon approach in a new type of ophthalmoscope, which is a tool that allows physicians to examine the inside of the eye.
               </p>
               <p>
                The idea is that by shining a pulsing, infrared laser into the eye, doctors might be able to stimulate parts of the retina to learn more about its structure and function in healthy eyes and in people with retinal diseases such as macular degeneration.
               </p>
               <p>
                The research was made possible, in part, by the Kefalov teams development of a tool that allowed the scientists to obtain light responses from retinal cells and photopigment molecules. That device already is commercially available and being used at several vision research centers around the world.
               </p>
               <p>
               </p>
               <div class="ms-rteElement-photodivright" style="width:475px">
                <div style="width:475px;height:284px">
                 <img alt="" class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit" src="/news/PublishingImages/Kefalov_summary.jpg" style="width:475px"/>
                </div>
                <div class="ms-rteStyle-photoCredit">
                 Robert Boston
                </div>
                <div class="ms-rteStyle-photocaption">
                 Frans Vinberg, PhD (left), and Vladimir J. Kefalov, PhD, sit in front of a tool they developed that allows them to detect light responses from retinal cells and photopigment molecules.
                </div>
               </div>
               <br/>
               <p>
               </p>
