      <h1 itemprop="headline">
       Ukraine's Economy Is At The Breaking Point
      </h1>
      <!-- Make individual group author appear -->
      <div class="mninst">
      </div>
      <div class="comment_links">
       <a class="scroll_to post_your_comment" href="#comment_reply">

      <div class="body">
       <div class="body_inner">
        <p>
         Given the actual condition of Ukraineseconomy, a surprisingly large amount of recent media coverage was modestly upbeat. Rather than focusing on the rapidly depleting foreign currency reserves or the fast-shrinking GDP, numerous articles instead focused on the plethora of Western-educated reformers in the new government and their ambitious plans for change. Both t
         <a href="http://www.rferl.org/content/ukraine-new-ministers-foreign-born-jaresko-abromavicius-kvitashvili/26723564.html">
          he finance and economy ministries
         </a>
         are now under the guidanceof highly educated foreigners, and a lot of people, including one of the
         <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/02/04/us-templeton-funds-ukraine-hasenstab-idUSKBN0L81HI20150204">
          worlds most successful bond managers
         </a>
         , expected fairly rapid progress in the implementation ofstructural reforms.
        </p>
        <p>
         Over the past day, however, Ukraine has finally reached a breaking point. After the central bank announced that it would
         <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2015/02/05/ukraine-crisis-cenbank-hryvnia-idUSR4N0T100O20150205">
          no longer directly intervene in the currency market
         </a>
         , while also, rather incongruously, claiming that this was
         <em>
          not
         </em>
         a decision to let the currency float freely, the hryvnia has just totally collapsed in value.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now it is true that the hrynvia hasbeen on a long slow slide against the dollar for most of the past year
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="hryvnia" class=" size-full wp-image-3418" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/markadomanis/files/2015/02/hryvnia.png"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         However the magnitude of the losses this morning was nothing less than stunning, with the currency losingmore than 30% of its value in
         <em>
          just a few hours
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="hryvnia252015" class=" size-full wp-image-3419" src="http://blogs-images.forbes.com/markadomanis/files/2015/02/hryvnia252015.png"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         This isnt a simple continuation of previous trends, its a sharp downward adjustment to an already harrowing downward trajectory. However bad Ukraine was doing before today its now doing much, much worse.
        </p>
        <p>
         It would be easy to blame the central bank, but its hard to see what else it could haverealistically done: its practical ability to intervene in the currency market has been essentially exhausted. The countrystotal foreign currency reserves are now less than $8 billion, and if they go any lower there could be a catastrophe. So the bank did what it could: it is letting the currency float and is hiking interest rates in a (likely futile) attempt to convince people to hold on to the hryvnia. Its a similar, but far more acute, version of the problems that have recently faced Russias central bank.
        </p>
