                  <p style="margin-top:5px;width:100%; text-align:justify;font-size: 13px;font-style: normal;color: #494949;">
                   <span id="ctl00_ContentPlaceHolder1_RepeaterBlogArticls_ctl00_pbody" style="text-align:justify;">
                    The
                    <i>
                     Advanced Technology Solar Telescope
                    </i>
                    (ATST) is a next generation ground-based large doomed solar telescope that is proposed by the National Solar Observatory to allow the telescope to follow the movement of the sun across the sky. The project is being funded by The National Science Foundation (NSF) and is being operated by the Association of Universities for Research in Astronomy (AURA). The whole project represents a collaboration of 22 institutions and reflects a broad segment of the solar physics community. The project is set to begin operations in 18 months within the University of Hawaii, Institute of Astronomy (UH IfA) Haleakal High Altitude Observatory site at the summit of Haleakal on the Hawaiian island of Maui.
                    <br/>
                    <br/>
                    <img align="none" alt="Solar Telescope Hawaii" height="500" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/-eOwB-E-DVTA/UkVigH9lBlI/AAAAAAAAAYo/9HNBk7w0mfE/s512/earth%2520slideworld.jpg" title="Advance Technology Solar Telescope" width="576"/>
                    <br/>
                    <br/>
                    The site was selected by the decision makers as it offers a clear daytime atmospheric seeing condition, which will enable study of the solar corona. The ATST enclosure will house a 26 meters (85 ft.) diameter of solar telescope that will be capable of observing objects on the Sun that are 30 km across. The recent reports say that the dome, 24 meters high and weighs 600 tons has been designed by the two Spanish companies which after undergoing some trials will be disassembled for transport to Hawaii by sea.
