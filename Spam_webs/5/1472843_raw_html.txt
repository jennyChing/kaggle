         <div class="blog-body" id="202019920214337650-blog" style="float: left;">

            <div class="blog-header">
             <h2 class="blog-title">
              <a class="blog-title-link blog-link" href="http://www.mouthofthebeast.com/the-beasts-blog/working-for-it">
               Working For It.
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p class="blog-date">
              <span class="date-text">
               4/12/2015
              </span>
             </p>
             <p class="blog-comments">
              <a class="blog-link" href="http://www.mouthofthebeast.com/the-beasts-blog/working-for-it#comments">
               0 Comments
              </a>
             </p>
            </div>
            <div class="blog-separator">
            </div>
            <div class="blog-content">
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/5/9/7/4/597486/780350947.jpg" style="width:100%;max-width:1100px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              <span style="">
               Nearly a year ago, I got a job as an undergraduate researcher in an evolutionary biology lab. Other undergrads, and even some grad students, seemed very surprised and impressed that I was able to obtain this position. I was always confused by this, since really, all it took was a well written resume, a good attitude, and a couple emails to professors to get where I am today. Over the next few weeks I'd like to share with you all the wisdom of what I have learned through obtaining my job, watching others, and working in the lab.
              </span>
             </div>
             <div>
              <!--BLOG_SUMMARY_END-->
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              This post will be focused solely on how to pick a professor to work with, my posts in the coming days will cover more on how to get a position, what to expect, advice, etc.
              <br/>
              <span style="line-height: 1.5; background-color: initial;">
               <br/>
              </span>
              <br/>
              <span style="line-height: 1.5; background-color: initial;">
               Before we go any further, I'd like to say that if you want to do lab work just for a resume booster or for better chances of getting into grad/professional school, you are already doing this for the wrong reasons. Sure, a research position looks great on your resume, but if you don't have a legitimate interest in the research you're doing, chances are you will just have a miserable time. Furthermore, professors tend to have a sixth sense for people just in it for a sentence on a resume or a recommendation letter down the road, and try to avoid these people like the plague. They want people who are going to grow up to replace them as researchers, not someone who just wants a leg up into a completely different field.
              </span>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Also, please do NOT be afraid to apply for a position in your freshman and sophomore year. A lot of undergrads I've met seem afraid to get into the lab until they are a junior, but most professors see this as a drawback, not an advantage. Professors want you young, even if you don't have all of the education you would as a junior. They'd rather have 4 years of you working for them than 2 years, especially given they'll have to spend about 6 months teaching you how to do your job.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              In addition, going into the lab for the first time as an upperclassman means that you probably won't be able to obtain your own research project, as someone who joined as a freshman or sophomore might. This means no poster, no paper, no national and state conferences, no wonderful learning experience and resume booster for you. Plus that's two less years to meet and greet with professors, grad students, and maybe even your future advisor (if you plan to go to grad school). So don't wait, get out there and do science!
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I will warn you that lab work is probably a lot different than you think, there's a lot of repetitiveness, you will encounter issues and setbacks that will cost you dear time and money and that will make you swear your hate for science. You will probably have to do a lot of dirty work for grad students who will be as strung out as you are after 4 hours of long, tedious work. But if you can get past that, and get to the real science, chances are you will find it much more satisfying mentally and intellectually, but you can't get to that point until you pay your dues, so hold your breath and get through it!
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Let's move on to how to narrow down a professor to work with. Obviously you want to look within your department, but don't forget that there may be people working in your field elsewhere as well, for example, some biologists are actually classified as archaeologists due to their focus on ancient peoples, and will be located in those departments, and some environmental scientists will actually be found in biology departments. Long story short, if you don't find what you're looking for in your department, think about what other areas your field might work in.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              When choosing a professor it is vital to have some idea of what you want to work in, if you have no idea what you like and just want to work in the lab, I'd recommend holding off. Even within fields there are an infinite number of subjects to focus on and study, your task is the pick the one you'll love the most, and that's pretty hard and a bit of a crapshoot if you were still as young as I was when I applied. If you aren't sure what you like, chances are you'll find a position in something just to find out later that you hate it, or that you enjoy something else better. Pay attention in your classes, look for something that piques your interest, and learn as much about it as you can, that's how I became interested in evolution, and I love my job in the lab.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Once you've decided on the perfect area of study, it's time to narrow down on what professors you want to work with. Make a list of all the professors whose interests line up with yours and who work in the same field. You won't apply to all of these professors, but it's good to get a list to narrow it down. Once you've done that, the real work begins. Automatically eliminate any professors who have a reputation of being tempermental, rude, or any other negative characteristic; I've heard horror stories about working for bad professors, you don't want to be part of those stories.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              If at all possible try to get in contact with people who work in their lab as an undergrad and see what their experience is like, you don't want to be cleaning dishes your first 4 months after all, you want to get right to the real lab work. Don't worry if your professor is a bad teacher however, that doesn't matter as much as you'd think. Don't be afraid to talk to your GAs or TAs about professors, they know everyone and might even be able to point you to some professors looking for undergraduate researchers, or give you recommendations for which to avoid.
              <br/>
              <span style="line-height: 1.5; background-color: initial;">
               <br/>
              </span>
              <br/>
              <span style="line-height: 1.5; background-color: initial;">
               Now that you've eliminated these bad professors from your list, it's time to really narrow it down, rank your professors from your favorite to least favorite, and glance at their research papers to make sure they work in something you think you'll enjoy. You will apply at your favorite professor first, and then keep going down the list. Keep your ears open for professors that have positions open, but also know that just because they aren't advertising doesn't mean they aren't looking. In my experience only a small proportion of the professors who are looking for people actually advertise it, they expect YOU to come to THEM!
              </span>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              So, to Recap..
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <ul>
               <li>
                Be in research for the right reasons. Only want a resume booster? You're going to be miserable.
               </li>
               <li>
                <span style="line-height: 1.5; background-color: initial;">
                 Don't be afraid to apply early on in your undergrad career. Professors LOVE freshmen.
                </span>
                <br/>
               </li>
               <li>
                Lab work is different than you think. Remember that things do get better when you move up.
               </li>
               <li>
                Know what you want to work in before you get a position in it. It will save you time and tears.
               </li>
               <li>
                Make a list of professors who share you're interest, weed out the ones with a bad rep.
               </li>
               <li>
                Rank your list professors from your favorite to least favorite. Apply at the best one first.
               </li>
               <br/>
              </ul>
              <br/>
              In my next post I'll be going over how to apply for a research position, as well as advice for before, during, and after the interview. Stay tuned!
