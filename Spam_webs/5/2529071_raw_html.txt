      <h1 class="single_title">
       Memories of Friendship and Blueberry Pie
      </h1>
      <div class="date">
       <div class="dateleft">
        <p>
         <span class="time">
          October 27, 2009
         </span>
         by
         <a href="http://womensvoicesforchange.org/author/pallen" rel="author" title="Posts by Patricia Yarberry Allen, M.D.">
          Patricia Yarberry Allen, M.D.
         </a>
        </p>

      <div class="post-content">
       <p>
        <a href="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/Patlunch1cropped.jpg">
         <img alt="Patlunch1cropped" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-8356" height="200" src="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/03/Patlunch1cropped.jpg" title="Patlunch1cropped" width="165"/>
        </a>
        My friend Adam Dolle wrote me last week to let me know that his mother, Viola Dolle, had died after only a brief illness. Mrs. Dolle was a devout Catholic who traveled to Rome when she was 90 for an outdoor Christmas Eve Mass celebrated by Pope John Paul II. She lived a long life with attention and affection given to all those around her. She had a life that I am honored to remember.
       </p>
       <p>
        The St.Petersburg Times
        <a href="http://www.tampabay.com/news/obituaries/tampa-resident-viola-mary-dolle-dies-at-99/1043926">
         chose Violas life to celebrate
        </a>
        in their weekly edition, describing her excitement when she visited Rome and her passion for baseball after her dear husband died:
        <em>
         She had never cared about it until then, but became a devoted fan of the Rays. She watched every game, and even stayed up until 2:30 in the morning watching playoff games that went into extra innings.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        But I have my own memory of Violet, one that never made it into that article: It was about my mothers blueberry pie  which became Mrs. Dolles.
       </p>
       <p>
        Until now I have hidden the secret to that pie, which was a sure success every summer. The secret is that I had stolen the recipe from the wife of the local general surgeon in Columbia, Ky., Mrs. Aaron. I then told everyone that I had just created this pie myself. I know it was a lie but it is a Southern cooking lie, and those dont count toward Hell.
       </p>
       <p>
        But when I told my friend Adam this year that I couldnt find my recipe, he told me that my mother had sent the recipe to his mother over 20 years ago. Our mothers had met at my home in New York City, and the pie had been served for dessert. Viola Dolle had asked my mother for the recipe.
       </p>
       <p>
        A few days after I talked to Adam, I received a beautifully hand written note from Mrs. Dolle. May 16, 2009:
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Dear Pat,
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Im so glad to be able to send you the original recipe for the blueberry pie that your dear Mom sent to me years ago. Ive made the pie so many times and its delicious. Enjoy.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Love,
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Vi Dolle
        </em>
       </p>
       <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_9351" style="width: 173px">
        <a href="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/10/Viewithpie.jpg">
         <img alt="Vi with a strawberry pie, when she was 98." class="size-large wp-image-9351" height="200" src="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/10/Viewithpie-163x200.jpg" title="Viewithpie!!" width="163"/>
        </a>
        <p class="wp-caption-text">
         Vi with a strawberry pie, when she was 98.
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        Mrs. Dolle was 99 then. She sent along the recipe written by my mother on one of those 80s note pads that I used to get as gifts: At the top of the hot pink paper in big type:
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         THIS NOTE COMES
         <br/>
         FROM A BEAUTIFUL,
         <br/>
         RICH, FAMOUS, THIN,
         <br/>
         CHIC PERSON
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Mommie had used a hot pink thin magic marker pen to write the recipe onto this shameless card.
       </p>
       <p>
        F
        <em>
         OR ADAMS MOTHER
         <br/>
         FROM EDNA
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         BLUEBERRY PIE
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         1. One home baked pie crust
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Now, Southern cooks know that a recipe given to another woman, even a nice woman who didnt live in your town, would always be filled with deceit and sneaky one-ups-womanship. As if one home-baked pie crust was just a simple thing to produce. Mommie was a master at pie crustsso thin that I could never imagine what force she used to press the rolled out rich dough into the pie pans, so flaky that the filling was just an excuse to get to the pie crust. And Edna could make pie crusts while talking on the phone, helping with homework, singing to a child, basting a roastit was always a miracle.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         2. Filling
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         1 quart blueberries
         <br/>
          C sugar
         <br/>
         1 C water
         <br/>
         2 tablespoons of corn starch
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Boil water and 1 C blueberries with sugar for 2 minutes. Strain through sieve. Add corn starch and cook the blueberry sauce until it thickens just a bit.
        </em>
        You notice that she never said how long to cook that sauce or how thick was thickjust a bit.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Chill blueberry sauce.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Before placing the fresh blueberries into the cooked pie shell, place a thin layer of softened cream cheese on the bottom of the pie shell. Fill the pie shell with uncooked blueberries.
        </em>
        Notice that thin layer of cream cheese can be interpreted in a number of ways. Mommie always put too much cream cheese. I wanted only a waft of cream cheese over that delicious crust. This is not a cheese cake, I would argue.
       </p>
       <p>
        T
        <em>
         hen dribble the chilled blueberry sauce over the blueberries.
        </em>
        Mommie and I always fought over that sauce. She made it too sweet. That recipe should say, 1/2 C sugar, not  C. I am appalled that Mrs. Dolle served a pie that was too sweet all those years.
       </p>
       <p>
        I have this memory of those days in the kitchen with Mommie because Vi Dolle saved this hand-written recipe from my mother for all those years. Then she sent it to me to keep.
       </p>
       <p>
        I am the recipient of her care now, as I have decoded that recipe written by my mother for Vi to keep all those years. I weep for Vis kindness. I weep for Adams loss.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/10/blueberrypie2.jpg">
         <img alt="blueberrypie2" class="alignleft size-large wp-image-9352" height="200" src="http://3sqwx948hrd81g4i2f3jiqzdn8z.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/10/blueberrypie2-266x200.jpg" title="blueberrypie2" width="266"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        And, now all of you have the famous blueberry pie recipe saved by Vi, fought over by Mommie and me, and stolen from Mrs. Aaron.
       </p>
       <p>
        Remember, just start with one home-made pie crust.
       </p>
