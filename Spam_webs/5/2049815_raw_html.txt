       <h1>
        Reef project takes Google Street View underwater
       </h1>
       <div class="byline">
        By Jemima Garrett for Radio Australia
       </div>
       <p class="published">
        Updated
        <span class="timestamp">
         March 01, 2012 11:46:18
        </span>
       </p>
       <div class="inline-content photo full">
        <a href="/news/2012-02-29/underwater-street-view/3861054">
         <img alt="Underwater Street View" height="467" src="http://www.abc.net.au/news/image/3861046-3x2-700x467.jpg" title="Underwater Street View" width="700"/>
        </a>
        <a class="inline-caption" href="/news/2012-02-29/underwater-street-view/3861054">
         <strong>
          Photo:
         </strong>
         A diver captures images for the reef Street View.
         <span class="source">
          (Caitlin Seaview Survey)
         </span>
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="attached-content">
        <div class="inline-content map left">
         <div class="story-map">
         </div>
         <a class="inline-caption" href="http://maps.google.com/?q=-28.07607,153.414073(QLD)&amp;z=5">
          <strong>
           Map:
          </strong>
          QLD
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
       <p>
        A new marine research project is aiming to create an underwater version of Google's street view, offering 360 degree views of parts of Australia's Great Barrier Reef.
       </p>
       <p>
        The University of Queensland is teaming up with Google and not-for-profit group Underwater Earth to photograph sites along the length of the reef to a depth of 100 metres.
       </p>
       <p>
        Marine photographer Richard Vevers has spent 12 months refining the camera technology.
       </p>
       <p>
        "It is just an amazing experience to be using one of these cameras and going round the corner and not knowing what is on the other side," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "We are looking at doing surveys of around four kilometres at a time and these are areas that really don't get any visitation at all, so you just do not know what is round the next corner."
       </p>
       <p>
        He says the survey will be conducted at representative sites along 2,300 kilometres of the reef.
       </p>
       <p>
        "We've actually got three parts to the survey," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There is the shallow reef survey, where divers are using underwater scooters with a camera attached and that is across about 20 sites along the Great Barrier Reef.
       </p>
       <p>
        "There is also a deep reef survey but that will be driven really using robots.
       </p>
       <p>
        "And then, there is also the megafauna part of the survey, where again divers will be used, and that is really involved with the tagging of animals."
       </p>
       <p>
        Early images from the pilot project are available on the Catlin Seaview Survey website, and the project has teamed up with search-giant Google to make them available in a street-view style format.
       </p>
       <p>
        Professor Ove Hoegh-Guldberg from the Global Change Institute at the University of Queensland says there could not be a more powerful way to bridge the gap between science and the general public.
       </p>
       <p>
        "If you don't know the reef, you don't know what is at risk from things like climate change," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        "So, taking these 360 degree shots, they are like little image bubbles, and then incorporating them into panorama on Google, we'll be allowing people to literally select locations on the reef and go for a dive, and more or less experience what a diver would experience.
       </p>
       <p>
        "So you could be 81 and in your armchair and diving the Great Barrier Reef. Very exciting from that perspective."
       </p>
