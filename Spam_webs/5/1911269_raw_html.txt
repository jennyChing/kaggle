    Darin Gap

     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Darin Gap
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
       Spanish
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Regin del Darin
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Tapn del Darin
       </i>
      </span>
      ) is a break in the
      <a href="/wiki/Pan-American_Highway" title="Pan-American Highway">
       Pan-American Highway
      </a>
      consisting of a large swath of undeveloped swampland and forest within
      <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
       Panama
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Dari%C3%A9n_Province" title="Darin Province">
       Darin Province
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
       Central America
      </a>
      and the northern portion of
      <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
       Colombia
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department" title="Choc Department">
       Choc Department
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
       South America
      </a>
      . It measures just over 160km (99mi) long and about 50km (31mi) wide. Roadbuilding through this area is expensive, and the environmental cost is high. Political consensus in favor of road construction has not emerged. Consequently there is no road connection through the Darin Gap connecting North America with South America and it is the missing link of the Pan-American Highway.
     </p>
     <p>
      The geography of the Darin Gap on the Colombian side is dominated primarily by the
      <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">
       river delta
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Atrato_River" title="Atrato River">
       Atrato River
      </a>
      , which creates a flat
      <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">
       marshland
      </a>
      at least 80km (50mi) wide, half of this being
      <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">
       swampland
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Serran%C3%ADa_del_Baud%C3%B3" title="Serrana del Baud">
       Serrana del Baud
      </a>
      occupy Colombia's Pacific coast and extend into Panama. The Panamanian side, in sharp contrast, is a mountainous
      <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">
       rainforest
      </a>
      , with terrain reaching from 60m (200ft) in the valley floors to 1,845m (6,053ft) at the tallest peak (Cerro Tacarcuna).
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-American_Highway">
       Pan-American Highway
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dari%C3%A9n_Gap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pan-American Highway">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Pan-American_Highway" title="Pan-American Highway">
       Pan-American Highway
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Pan-American Highway is a system of roads measuring about 48,000km (30,000mi) long that crosses through the entirety of North, Central, and South America, with the sole exception of the Darin Gap. On the South American side, the highway terminates at
      <a href="/wiki/Turbo,_Colombia" title="Turbo, Colombia">
       Turbo, Colombia
      </a>
      near
      <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
       <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Dari%C3%A9n_Gap&amp;params=8_6_N_76_40_W_">
        <span class="geo-default">
         <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
          <span class="latitude">
           86N
          </span>
          <span class="longitude">
           7640W
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          / 
        </span>
        <span class="geo-nondefault">
         <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
          8.100N 76.667W
         </span>
         <span style="display:none">
           /
          <span class="geo">
           8.100; -76.667
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
       </a>
      </span>
      . On the Panamanian side, the road terminus is the town of
      <a href="/wiki/Yaviza" title="Yaviza">
       Yaviza
      </a>
      at
      <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
       <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Dari%C3%A9n_Gap&amp;params=8_9_N_77_41_W_type:landmark">
        <span class="geo-default">
         <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
          <span class="latitude">
           89N
          </span>
          <span class="longitude">
           7741W
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          / 
        </span>
        <span class="geo-nondefault">
         <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
          8.150N 77.683W
         </span>
         <span style="display:none">
           /
          <span class="geo">
           8.150; -77.683
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
       </a>
      </span>
      . This marks a straight-line separation of about 100km (60mi). In between is marshland and forest.
     </p>
     <p>
      Efforts have been made for decades to remedy this missing link in the Pan-American highway. Planning began in 1971 with the help of United States funding, but this was halted in 1974 after concerns raised by environmentalists. Another effort to build the road began in 1992, but by 1994 a
      <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
       United Nations
      </a>
      agency reported that the road, and the subsequent development, would cause extensive environmental damage. Cited reasons include evidence that the Darin Gap has prevented the spread of diseased cattle into Central and North America, which have not seen
      <a href="/wiki/Foot-and-mouth_disease" title="Foot-and-mouth disease">
       foot-and-mouth disease
      </a>
      since 1954, and since at least the 1970s this has been a substantial factor in preventing a road link through the Darin Gap.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Embera-Wounaan" title="Embera-Wounaan">
       Embera-Wounaan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kuna_people" title="Kuna people">
       Kuna
      </a>
      have also expressed concern that the road would bring about the potential erosion of their cultures.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many people, groups, indigenous populations, and governments are opposed to completing the Darin portion of the highway. Reasons for opposition include protecting the rain forest, containing the spread of tropical diseases, protecting the livelihood of indigenous peoples in the area, preventing drug trafficking
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and its associated violence, and preventing
      <a href="/wiki/Foot-and-mouth_disease" title="Foot-and-mouth disease">
       foot-and-mouth disease
      </a>
      from entering North America. The extension of the highway as far as
      <a href="/wiki/Yaviza" title="Yaviza">
       Yaviza
      </a>
      resulted in severe deforestation alongside the highway route within a decade.
     </p>
     <p>
      One option proposed, in a study by Bio-Pacifico, is a short ferry link from Colombia to a new ferry port in Panama, with an extension of the existing Panama highway that would complete the highway without violating these environmental concerns. Another idea is to use a combination
      <a href="/wiki/Fixed_link" title="Fixed link">
       bridges and tunnels
      </a>
      to avoid the environmentally sensitive regions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Tamiami_Trail" title="Tamiami Trail">
       Everglades Skyway
      </a>
      is an example of an environmentally conscious bridge being developed over the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Everglades" title="Florida Everglades">
       Florida Everglades
      </a>
      in order to avoid the environmental impact of a conventional roadway.
     </p>
     <p>
      The gap has been crossed by adventurers on bicycle, motorbike, all-terrain vehicle, and foot, dealing with jungle, swamp, insects, and other hazards.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="People">
       People
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dari%C3%A9n_Gap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: People">

     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Panama_Embera0607.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3504" data-file-width="2336" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Panama_Embera0607.jpg/170px-Panama_Embera0607.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Panama_Embera0607.jpg/255px-Panama_Embera0607.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Panama_Embera0607.jpg/340px-Panama_Embera0607.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Panama_Embera0607.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Embera-Wounaan" title="Embera-Wounaan">
         Embera
        </a>
        girl,
        <a href="/wiki/Dari%C3%A9n_Province" title="Darin Province">
         Darin Province
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Darin Gap is home to the
      <a href="/wiki/Embera-Wounaan" title="Embera-Wounaan">
       Embera-Wounaan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Kuna_people" title="Kuna people">
       Kuna
      </a>
      Indians (and the former home of the
      <a href="/wiki/Cueva_people" title="Cueva people">
       Cueva people
      </a>
      before their extermination in the 16th century). Travel is often by dugout canoe (
      <a href="/wiki/Pirogue" title="Pirogue">
       piragua
      </a>
      ). On the Panamanian side,
      <a href="/wiki/La_Palma,_Dari%C3%A9n" title="La Palma, Darin">
       La Palma
      </a>
      is the capital of the province and the main cultural centre. Other
      <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">
       mestizo
      </a>
      population centers include
      <a href="/wiki/Yaviza" title="Yaviza">
       Yaviza
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/El_Real" title="El Real">
       El Real
      </a>
      . The Darin Gap had a reported population of 1,700 in 1980.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">
       Corn
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandioca" title="Mandioca">
       mandioca
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantain_(cooking)" title="Plantain (cooking)">
       plantains
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">
       bananas
      </a>
      are staple crops wherever land is developed.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_resources">
       Natural resources
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dari%C3%A9n_Gap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural resources">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Two major national parks exist in the Darin Gap:
      <a href="/wiki/Dari%C3%A9n_National_Park" title="Darin National Park">
       Darin National Park
      </a>
      (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Parque Nacional Darin
       </i>
      </span>
      ) in Panama and
      <a href="/wiki/Los_Kat%C3%ADos_National_Park" title="Los Katos National Park">
       Los Katos National Park
      </a>
      (Spanish:
      <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
       <i>
        Parque Nacional de Los Katos
       </i>
      </span>
      ) in Colombia. The Darin Gap forests had extensive
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Cedrela" title="Cedrela">
        cedrela
       </a>
      </i>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">
       mahogany
      </a>
      cover at one time, but many of these trees were removed by loggers.
     </p>
     <p>
      Darin National Park covers around 5,790 square kilometres of land and was established in 1980. It is the largest national park in Central America.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing_the_Dari.C3.A9n_Gap">
       Crossing the Darin Gap
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dari%C3%A9n_Gap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crossing the Darin Gap">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Gap can be transited by off-road vehicles attempting intercontinental journeys. The first post-colonial expedition to the Darin was the Marsh Darien Expedition
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      in 192425, supported by several major sponsors, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">
       Smithsonian Institution
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PanAmericanHwy.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1828" data-file-width="1325" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PanAmericanHwy.png/220px-PanAmericanHwy.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PanAmericanHwy.png/330px-PanAmericanHwy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PanAmericanHwy.png/440px-PanAmericanHwy.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PanAmericanHwy.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The Pan-American Highway with the Darin Gap between Panama and Colombia
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first vehicular crossing of the Gap was by the
      <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">
       Land Rover
      </a>
      <i>
       La Cucaracha Cariosa
      </i>
      (The Affectionate Cockroach) and a
      <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">
       Jeep
      </a>
      of the Trans-Darin Expedition of 195960, crewed by Amado Araz (Panama), his wife Reina Torres de Araz, former
      <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">
       Special Air Service
      </a>
      man Richard E. Bevir (UK), and engineer Terence John Whitfield (Australia).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They left
      <a href="/wiki/Chepo,_Panam%C3%A1_Province" title="Chepo, Panam Province">
       Chepo, Panama
      </a>
      , on 2 February 1960 and reached
      <a href="/wiki/Quibd%C3%B3" title="Quibd">
       Quibd
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
       Colombia
      </a>
      , on 17 June 1960, averaging 201 m (220 yd) per hour over 136 days. They traveled a great deal of the distance up the vast Atrato River.
     </p>
     <p>
      In December 1960, on a motorcycle trip from Alaska to
      <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
       Argentina
      </a>
      , adventurer Danny Liska
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      attempted to transit the Darin Gap from Panama to Colombia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Liska was forced to abandon his motorcycle and proceed across the Gap by boat and foot. In 1961, a team of three 1961
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Corvair" title="Chevrolet Corvair">
       Chevrolet Corvairs
      </a>
      and several support vehicles departed from Panama. The group was sponsored by Dick Doane Chevrolet, a Chicago Chevrolet dealer and the Chevrolet division of General Motors. After 109 days they reached the Colombia Border with two Corvairs, the third having been abandoned in the jungle. This was the first crossing by a standard two wheel drive passenger car. It has been documented by a Jam Handy Productions film along with an article in
      <i>
       Automobile Quarterly
      </i>
      magazine (Volume 1 number 3, from the fall of 1962).
     </p>
     <p>
      A pair of
      <a href="/wiki/Range_Rover" title="Range Rover">
       Range Rovers
      </a>
      was used on the British Trans-Americas Expedition in 1972 led by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Blashford-Snell" title="John Blashford-Snell">
       John Blashford-Snell
      </a>
      , which is claimed to be the first vehicle-based expedition to traverse both American continents north to south through the Darin Gap. The Expedition crossed the Atrato Swamp in Colombia with the cars on special inflatable rafts that were carried in the backs of the vehicles. However, they received substantial support from the
      <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">
       British Army
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/John_Blashford-Snell" title="John Blashford-Snell">
       John Blashford-Snell
      </a>
      's book,
      <i>
       Something Lost Behind the Ranges
      </i>
      (Harper Collins) has several chapters on the Darin expedition.
      <i>
       The Hundred Days of Darien
      </i>
      , a book written by
      <a href="/wiki/Russell_Braddon" title="Russell Braddon">
       Russell Braddon
      </a>
      in 1974, also chronicles this expedition.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first fully overland wheeled crossing (others used boats for some sections) of the Gap was that of British cyclist
      <a href="/wiki/Ian_Hibell" title="Ian Hibell">
       Ian Hibell
      </a>
      , who rode from
      <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">
       Cape Horn
      </a>
      to Alaska between 1971 and 1973. Hibell took the "direct" overland south-to-north route, including an overland crossing of the Atrato Swamp in Colombia. Hibell completed his crossing of the Gap accompanied by two New Zealand cycling companions who had ridden with him from Cape Horn, but neither of these continued with Hibell on to Alaska. Hibell's "Cape Horn to Alaska" expedition forms part of his 1984 book
      <i>
       Into the Remote Places
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1780" data-file-width="2700" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg/220px-Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg/330px-Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg/440px-Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Culberson%27s%22Amigo%22.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Eric Culberson's "Amigo" (a BMW R80G/S motorcycle) was the first vehicle to fully navigate the Pan-American Highway by land.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first motorcycle crossing was by Robert L. Webb in March 1975. Another four-wheel drive crossing was in 19781979 by Mark A. Smith and his team. Smith and his team drove the 400km (250mi) stretch of the gap in 30 days using five stock Jeep CJ-7s. They travelled many miles up the Atrato River on barges. Smith has released his book,
      <i>
       Driven by a Dream
      </i>
      , which documents the crossing.
     </p>
     <p>
      The first all-land auto crossing was in 198587 by Loren Upton and Patty Mercier in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CJ-5_Jeep" title="CJ-5 Jeep">
       CJ-5 Jeep
      </a>
      , taking 741 days to travel 125 miles (201km). This crossing is documented in the 1992
      <i>
       Guinness Book of Records
      </i>
      . Ed Culberson was the first one to follow the entire Pan-American highway including the Darin Gap proposed route on a motorcycle, a
      <a href="/wiki/BMW_R80G/S" title="BMW R80G/S">
       BMW R80G/S
      </a>
      . From Yaviza he first followed the Loren Upton team but would go solo just before Pucuru, hiring his own guides.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the 1990s, the gap was briefly joined by ferry service, provided by
      <a href="/wiki/Crucero_Express" title="Crucero Express">
       Crucero Express
      </a>
      , but this company ceased operations in 1997.
     </p>
     <p>
      There have been several notable crossings by foot.
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Snow" title="Sebastian Snow">
       Sebastian Snow
      </a>
      crossed the Gap with
      <a href="/wiki/Wade_Davis" title="Wade Davis">
       Wade Davis
      </a>
      in 1975 as part of his unbroken walk from
      <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">
       Tierra del Fuego
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
       Costa Rica
      </a>
      . The trip is documented in his 1976 book
      <i>
       The Rucksack Man
      </i>
      . In 1981,
      <a href="/wiki/George_Meegan" title="George Meegan">
       George Meegan
      </a>
      crossed the gap on a similar journey. He too started in Tierra del Fuego and eventually ended in Alaska. His 1988 biography,
      <i>
       The Longest Walk
      </i>
      , describes the trip and includes a 25-page chapter on his foray through the Gap. In 2001, as a part of his
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goliath_Expedition" title="Goliath Expedition">
       Goliath Expedition
      </a>
      a trek to forge an unbroken footpath from the tip of South America to the
      <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">
       Bering Strait
      </a>
      and back to his home in England
      <a href="/wiki/Karl_Bushby" title="Karl Bushby">
       Karl Bushby
      </a>
      (UK) crossed the gap on foot, using no transport or boats, from Colombia to Panama.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1979 evangelist
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Blessitt" title="Arthur Blessitt">
       Arthur Blessitt
      </a>
      traversed the gap while carrying a 12-foot wooden cross, a trek confirmed by Guinness World Records as part of "the longest round the world pilgrimage" for Christ. Traveling alone with a machete plus one backpack crammed with water bottles, a hammock, Bible, notepad, lemon drops, and Blessitt's signature Jesus stickers saying "Smile! God Loves you", Blessitt describes his experience in a book,
      <i>
       The Cross
      </i>
      , and in a full-length movie of the same name.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg/220px-Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg/330px-Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg/440px-Jungle_path_in_the_Dari%C3%A9n_Gap.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>

     <p>
      Most crossings of the Darin Gap region have been from Panama to Colombia. In July 1961, three college students crossed from the
      <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_San_Miguel" title="Bay of San Miguel">
       Bay of San Miguel
      </a>
      to Puerto Obaldia on the
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Parita" title="Gulf of Parita">
       Gulf of Parita
      </a>
      (near Colombia) and ultimately to Mulatupu in what was then known as
      <a href="/wiki/San_Blas_Islands" title="San Blas Islands">
       San Blas
      </a>
      and now identified as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuna_Yala" title="Kuna Yala">
       Kuna Yala
      </a>
      . The trip across the Darin was by banana boat, piraqua, and foot via the
      <a href="/wiki/Turia_(river)" title="Turia (river)">
       Turia
      </a>
      river (
      <a href="/wiki/La_Palma,_Dari%C3%A9n" title="La Palma, Darin">
       La Palma
      </a>
      and El Real de Santa Maria), Ro Chucunaque (
      <a href="/wiki/Yaviza" title="Yaviza">
       Yaviza
      </a>
      ), Rio Tuquesa (Chaua's (General Choco Chief) Trading Post
      <a href="/wiki/Embera-Wounaan" title="Embera-Wounaan">
       Choco
      </a>
      Indian village) and Serrana del Darin.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1985, Project Raleigh, which evolved from Project Drake in 1984 and in 1989 became
      <a href="/wiki/Raleigh_International" title="Raleigh International">
       Raleigh International
      </a>
      , sponsored an expedition which also crossed the Darin coast to coast.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Their path was similar to the 1961 route above, but in reverse. The expedition started in
      <a href="/wiki/Darien_scheme" title="Darien scheme">
       The Bay of Caledonia
      </a>
      at the Serrana del Darin and followed the Ro Membrillo ultimately to the Ro Chucunaque and Yaviza, roughly following the route taken by
      <a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Nez de Balboa">
       Balboa
      </a>
      in 1513.
     </p>
     <p>
      Between the early 1980s and mid-1990s a British adventure travel company, Encounter Overland, organized 23 week trekking trips through the Darin Gap from Panama to Colombia or vice versa. These trips used a combination of whatever transport was available: jeeps, bus, boats, and of course plenty of walking, with travellers carrying their own supplies. These groups were made up of male and female participants from any number of nationalities and age groups, and were led by experienced trek leaders. One leader went on to do nine Darin Gap trips and later acted as a logistics guide and coordinator for the BBC Natural History Unit during the production of a documentary called
      <i>
       A Tramp in the Darien
      </i>
      , which screened on BBC in 199091.
     </p>
     <p>
      A complete overland crossing of the Darin rainforest by foot and riverboat (i.e., from the last road in Panama to the first road in Colombia) became more dangerous in the 1990s because of the
      <a href="/wiki/Colombian_conflict_(1964%E2%80%93present)" title="Colombian conflict (1964present)">
       Colombian conflict
      </a>
      . The Colombian portion of the Darin rainforest in the Katios Park region eventually fell under control of armed groups. Furthermore, combatants from Colombia even entered Panama, occupied some Panamanian jungle villages and kidnapped or killed inhabitants and travelers. Just as hostilities were starting to worsen, 18-year-old Andrew Egan traversed the Darien Rainforest, detailing the excursion in the book
      <i>
       Crossing the Darien Gap
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As of 2013, the coastal route on the east side of the Darin Isthmus had become relatively safe. This is by motorboat across the Gulf of Uraba from Turbo to Capurgana, and then hopping the coast to Sapzurro and hike from there to La Miel (Panama). Any inland routes through the Darien remain highly dangerous.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      From May 2013, Colombian neo-paramilitary forces were reported to be very active in the Darien around Los Katios National Park and the Cuenca Cacarica
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The Darin Gap is subject to the presence and activities of the Marxist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Armed_Forces_of_Colombia" title="Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia">
       Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia
      </a>
      (FARC), which has committed assassinations, kidnappings, and human rights violations during its decades-long insurgency against the Colombian government.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      FARC rebels are present on both the Colombian and Panamanian sides of the border.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2000, two British travellers,
      <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hart_Dyke" title="Tom Hart Dyke">
       Tom Hart Dyke
      </a>
      and Paul Winder, were kidnapped by suspected FARC guerillas in the Darin Gap while
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orchid_hunters" title="Orchid hunters">
       hunting for rare orchids
      </a>
      , a plant for which Dyke has a particular passion. The two were held captive for nine months and threatened with death before eventually being released unharmed and without a ransom being paid. Dyke and Winder later documented their experience in the book
      <i>
       The Cloud Garden
      </i>
      and an episode of
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locked_Up_Abroad" title="Locked Up Abroad">
        Locked Up Abroad
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Other political victims of the Darin Gap include three
      <a href="/wiki/New_Tribes_Mission" title="New Tribes Mission">
       New Tribes missionaries
      </a>
      , who disappeared from Pucuro on the Panamanian side in 1993.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2003,
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Young_Pelton" title="Robert Young Pelton">
       Robert Young Pelton
      </a>
      , on assignment for
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Adventure" title="National Geographic Adventure">
        National Geographic Adventure
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine, and two traveling companions, Mark Wedeven and Megan Smaker, were detained for one week by the
      <a href="/wiki/United_Self-Defense_Forces_of_Colombia" title="United Self-Defense Forces of Colombia">
       United Self-Defense Forces of Colombia
      </a>
      , a pro-government paramilitary organization, in a highly publicized incident.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>

        </span>
        23
        <span>

        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">
       In popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      The Darin gap is referenced directly in the 2014 film
      <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_(film)" title="Indigenous (film)">
       Indigenous
