     <p>
      <b>
       Douglas Gordon
      </b>
      (born 20 September 1966) is a Scottish artist; he won the
      <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">
       Turner Prize
      </a>
      in 1996 and the following year he represented Britain at the
      <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">
       Venice Biennale
      </a>
      . He lives and works in
      <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
       Berlin
      </a>
      , Germany.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
       Biography
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_Gordon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Gordon was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">
       Glasgow
      </a>
      and grew up in
      <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">
       Dumbarton
      </a>
      . He was a pupil at
      <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton_Academy" title="Dumbarton Academy">
       Dumbarton Academy
      </a>
      between 1978 and 1983. He then studied art (at the
      <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_School_of_Art" title="Glasgow School of Art">
       Glasgow School of Art
      </a>
      ) from 1984-1988 and later at the
      <a href="/wiki/Slade_School_of_Fine_Art" title="Slade School of Fine Art">
       Slade School of Fine Art
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">
       University College London
      </a>
      , from 1988-1990.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Work">
       Work
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Douglas_Gordon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Much of Gordon's work is seen as being about memory and uses repetition in various forms. He uses material from the public realm and also creates performance-based videos. His work often overturns traditional uses of video by playing with time elements and employing multiple monitors.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Douglas_Gordon_(Scottish,_born_1966),_%27Monster%27,_1996-7,_color_photograph,_HAA.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="877" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Douglas_Gordon_%28Scottish%2C_born_1966%29%2C_%27Monster%27%2C_1996-7%2C_color_photograph%2C_HAA.jpg/220px-Douglas_Gordon_%28Scottish%2C_born_1966%29%2C_%27Monster%27%2C_1996-7%2C_color_photograph%2C_HAA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Douglas_Gordon_%28Scottish%2C_born_1966%29%2C_%27Monster%27%2C_1996-7%2C_color_photograph%2C_HAA.jpg/330px-Douglas_Gordon_%28Scottish%2C_born_1966%29%2C_%27Monster%27%2C_1996-7%2C_color_photograph%2C_HAA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1e/Douglas_Gordon_%28Scottish%2C_born_1966%29%2C_%27Monster%27%2C_1996-7%2C_color_photograph%2C_HAA.jpg/440px-Douglas_Gordon_%28Scottish%2C_born_1966%29%2C_%27Monster%27%2C_1996-7%2C_color_photograph%2C_HAA.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Douglas_Gordon_(Scottish,_born_1966),_%27Monster%27,_1996-7,_color_photograph,_HAA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Monster
        </i>
        , 1996-7, color photograph by Douglas Gordon,
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Private_ownership-_Michael_Hue_Williams&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Private ownership- Michael Hue Williams (page does not exist)">

     <p>
      In one early work,
      <i>
       Meaning and Location
      </i>
      (1990), a passage from the
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">
       Gospel of Luke
      </a>
      is given with a comma in different places, thus subtly changing the meaning of the sentence.
      <i>
       List of Names
      </i>
      (1990present) is a list of every person Gordon has ever met and can remember. One version of this is applied onto the wall of a stairwell in the
      <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Gallery_of_Modern_Art" title="Scottish National Gallery of Modern Art">
       Scottish National Gallery of Modern Art
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Gordon has often reused older film footage in his photographs and videos.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guggenheim_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-guggenheim-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      One of his best-known art works is
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/24_Hour_Psycho" title="24 Hour Psycho">
        24 Hour Psycho
       </a>
      </i>
      (1993) which slows down
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">
       Alfred Hitchcock
      </a>
      's film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Psycho_(1960_film)" title="Psycho (1960 film)">
        Psycho
       </a>
      </i>
      so that it lasts twenty four hours.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <i>
       Between Darkness and Light (After William Blake)
      </i>
      (1997),
      <a href="/wiki/William_Friedkin" title="William Friedkin">
       William Friedkin
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Exorcist_(film)" title="The Exorcist (film)">
        Exorcist
       </a>
      </i>
      (1973) and
      <a href="/wiki/Henry_King_(director)" title="Henry King (director)">
       Henry King
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Bernadette_(film)" title="The Song of Bernadette (film)">
        The Song of Bernadette
       </a>
      </i>
      (1943) - two films about adolescent girls driven by external forces
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      - are projected on either side of a single free-standing semi-transparent screen so they can be seen simultaneously.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The video installation
      <i>
       left is right and right is wrong and left is wrong and right is right
      </i>
      (1999) presents two projections of
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Preminger" title="Otto Preminger">
       Otto Preminger
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Whirlpool_(1949_film)" title="Whirlpool (1949 film)">
        Whirlpool
       </a>
      </i>
      (1949) side by side, with the one on the right reversed so that the two sides mirror each other; by digital means, Gordon separated individual frames of the original film so that odd-numbered ones on one side alternate with even-numbered ones on the other.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Feature Film
      </i>
      (1999) is a projection of Gordon's own film of
      <a href="/wiki/James_Conlon" title="James Conlon">
       James Conlon
      </a>
      conducting
      <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Herrmann" title="Bernard Herrmann">
       Bernard Herrmann
      </a>
      's score to
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertigo_(movie)" title="Vertigo (movie)">
        Vertigo
       </a>
      </i>
      , thus drawing attention to the
      <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">
       film score
      </a>
      and the emotional responses it creates in the viewer. In one installation, this was placed at the top of a tall building, referencing one of the film's main plot points. In
      <i>
       Through a looking glass
      </i>
      (1999), Gordon created a double-projection work around the climactic 71-second scene in
      <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">
       Martin Scorsese
      </a>
      s film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Taxi_Driver" title="Taxi Driver">
        Taxi Driver
       </a>
      </i>
      (1976), in which the main character addresses the camera; the screens are arranged so that the character seems to be addressing himself.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guggenheim_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-guggenheim-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      At first, the 71-second loops are in sync, but they get progressively out and then progressively back with each repetition of the whole, hourlong program.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Originally conceived as a site-specific video projection for
      <a href="/wiki/Gagosian_Gallery" title="Gagosian Gallery">
       Gagosian Gallery
      </a>
      in Chelsea,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Play Dead; Real Time
      </i>
      (2003) consists of two videos projected on two large screens showing a circus elephant named Minnie ponderously performing for an off-screen trainer in the empty, spacious, white-walled gallery room. In each projection the camera circles as the elephant walks around, lies down to play dead and gets up.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_5-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The footage showing Minnies sequences of tricks is simultaneously presented in a front and a rear life-sized projection and on a monitor, with each one depicting the same event from a range of perspectives, including close-ups of the animal's eyes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gordon also made a film about
      <a href="/wiki/Zinedine_Zidane" title="Zinedine Zidane">
       Zinedine Zidane
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zidane,_un_portrait_du_21e_si%C3%A8cle" title="Zidane, un portrait du 21e sicle">
        Zidane, un portrait du 21e sicle
       </a>
      </i>
      (2006), an idea first seen in a film by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hellmuth_Costard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hellmuth Costard (page does not exist)">
       Hellmuth Costard
      </a>
      , who, in 1970, made a film about
      <a href="/wiki/George_Best" title="George Best">
       George Best
      </a>
      titled
      <i>
       Football as Never Before.
      </i>
      The feature-length film, which he co-directed with fellow artist
      <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Parreno" title="Philippe Parreno">
       Philippe Parreno
      </a>
      and assembled from footage shot by seventeen synchronized cameras placed around the stadium in real time over the course of a single match,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      premiered outside the competition of the 2006
      <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">
       Cannes Film Festival
      </a>
      before screenings at numerous international venues.
      <i>
       k.364
      </i>
      premiered at the
      <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">
       Venice Film Festival
      </a>
      in September 2010.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gordon has also made photographs, often in series with relatively minor variations between each individual piece. His
      <i>
       Blind Stars
      </i>
      (2002) featured publicity photographs of mid-century movie stars in which the sitters' eyes were replaced by expressionless black, white or mirrored surfaces.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, Gordon was a member of the Official Competition Jury at the
      <a href="/wiki/65th_Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="65th Venice International Film Festival">
       65th Venice International Film Festival
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2010, Gordon collaborated with
      <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Wainwright" title="Rufus Wainwright">
       Rufus Wainwright
      </a>
      , creating the visuals for his tour which accompany Rufus'
      <i>
       All Days Are Nights: Songs for Lulu
      </i>
      album. In
      <i>
       Phantom
      </i>
      (2011), another collaboration with Wainwright, Gordon employs slow-motion film produced with a high-speed
      <a href="/wiki/Vision_Research_Phantom" title="Vision Research Phantom">
       Phantom camera
      </a>
      focusing on Wainright's eye  blackened with make-up, weeping, and glaring back at the viewer, echoing melodramatic performances by stars of the silent screen.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Contemporary_Art,_Los_Angeles" title="Museum of Contemporary Art, Los Angeles">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">
