      <!-- Begin Title -->
      <h1>
       Prime Formulas
      </h1>
      <!-- End Title -->
      <!-- Begin Total Content -->
      <!-- Begin Content -->
      <p>
       There exist a variety of formulas for either producing the
       <img alt="n" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline1.gif" width="7"/>
       th prime as a function
 of
       <img alt="n" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline2.gif" width="7"/>
       or taking on only prime values. However, all such
 formulas require either extremely accurate knowledge of some unknown constant, or
 effectively require knowledge of the primes ahead of time in order to use the formula
 (Dudley 1969; Ribenboim 1996, p.186). There also exist simple
       <a href="/Prime-GeneratingPolynomial.html">
        prime-generating
 polynomials
       </a>
       that generate only primes for the first (possibly large) number of
 integer values.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are also many beautiful formulas involving
       <a href="/PrimeSums.html">
        prime sums
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/PrimeProducts.html">
        prime products
       </a>
       that can be done in
 closed form.
      </p>
      <p>
       Considering examples of formulas that produce only prime numbers (although not necessarily the complete set of prime numbers
       <img alt="P" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline3.gif" width="9"/>
       ), there exists
 a
       <a href="/Constant.html">
        constant
       </a>
       <img alt="theta=1.3063..." border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline4.gif" width="79"/>
       (OEIS
       <a href="http://oeis.org/A051021">
        A051021
       </a>
       ) known as
       <a href="/MillsConstant.html">
        Mills'
 constant
       </a>
       such that
      </p>
      <div>
       <table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" style="padding-left: 50px" summary="" width="100%">
        <tr>
         <td align="left">
          <img alt=" f(n)=|_theta^(3^n)_|, " border="0" class="numberedequation" height="22" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/NumberedEquation1.gif" width="75"/>

          </div>

      </div>
      <p>
       where
       <img alt="|_x_|" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline5.gif" width="17"/>
       is the
       <a href="/FloorFunction.html">
        floor
 function
       </a>
       , is prime for all
       <img alt="n&gt;=1" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline6.gif" width="29"/>
       (Ribenboim
 1996, p.186). The first few values of
       <img alt="f(n)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline7.gif" width="26"/>
       are 2, 11,
 1361, 2521008887, ... (OEIS
       <a href="http://oeis.org/A051254">
        A051254
       </a>
       ). It is
 not known if
       <img alt="theta" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline8.gif" width="6"/>
       is
       <a href="/IrrationalNumber.html">
        irrational
       </a>
       .
 There also exists a
       <a href="/Constant.html">
        constant
       </a>
       <img alt="omega approx 1.9287800" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline9.gif" width="85"/>
       (OEIS
       <a href="http://oeis.org/A086238">
        A086238
       </a>
       ) such that
      </p>
      <div>
       <table align="center" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" style="padding-left: 50px" summary="" width="100%">
        <tr>
         <td align="left">
          <img alt=" g(n)=|_2omegan_| " border="0" class="numberedequation" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/NumberedEquation2.gif" width="82"/>
         </td>
         <td align="right" width="3">
          <div class="eqnum" id="eqn2">
           (2)

         </td>
        </tr>
       </table>
      </div>
      <p>
       (Wright 1951; Ribenboim 1996, p.186) is prime for every
       <img alt="n&gt;=1" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline10.gif" width="29"/>
       . The first
 few values of
       <img alt="g(n)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline11.gif" width="25"/>
       are 3, 13, 16381, ... (OEIS
       <a href="http://oeis.org/A016104">
        A016104
       </a>
       ).
 In the case of both
       <img alt="f(n)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline12.gif" width="26"/>
       and
       <img alt="g(n)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline13.gif" width="25"/>
       , the numbers
 at
       <img alt="n=4" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline14.gif" width="31"/>
       grow so rapidly that an extremely
 precise value of
       <img alt="theta" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline15.gif" width="6"/>
       or
       <img alt="omega" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline16.gif" width="10"/>
       is needed
 in order to obtain the correct value, and values for
       <img alt="n&gt;=5" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline17.gif" width="29"/>
       are effectively
 incomputable.
      </p>
      <p>
       Explicit
       <a href="/Formula.html">
        formulas
       </a>
       exist for the
       <img alt="n" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline18.gif" width="7"/>
       th prime both as
 a function of
       <img alt="n" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline19.gif" width="7"/>
       and in terms of the primes 2, ...,
       <img alt="p_(n-1)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline20.gif" width="25"/>
       (Hardy and Wright 1979, pp.5-6, 344-345,
 and 414; Guy 1994, pp.36-41), a number of which are given below. However, it
 should again be emphasized that these formulas are extremely inefficient, and in
 many (if not all) cases, simply performing an efficient sieving would yield the primes
 much more quickly and efficiently.
      </p>

       Let

      <p>
       for
       <img alt="j&gt;1" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline27.gif" width="28"/>

       <img alt="|_x_|" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline28.gif" width="17"/>

       <img alt="F(j)=1" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline30.gif" width="50"/>
       , i.e., the

       <img alt="F(j)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline31.gif" width="26"/>
       are 1, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 0,
 1, ... (OEIS
       <a href="http://oeis.org/A080339">
        A080339
       </a>
       ). Then
      </p>

       Gandhi gave the formula in which
       <img alt="p_(n+1)" border="0" class="inlineformula" height="14" src="/images/equations/PrimeFormulas/Inline39.gif" width="25"/>

      </p>
