     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Queen's Gambit
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Chess_opening" title="Chess opening">
       chess opening
      </a>
      that starts with the moves:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       1.
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Chess_Opening_Theory/1._d4" title="b:Chess Opening Theory/1. d4">
        d4
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Chess_Opening_Theory/1._d4/1...d5" title="b:Chess Opening Theory/1. d4/1...d5">
        d5
       </a>
      </dd>
      <dd>
       2.
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Chess_Opening_Theory/1._d4/1...d5/2._c4" title="b:Chess Opening Theory/1. d4/1...d5/2. c4">
        c4
       </a>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The Queen's Gambit is one of the oldest known chess openings. It was mentioned in the
      <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen_manuscript" title="Gttingen manuscript">
       Gttingen manuscript
      </a>
      of 1490 and was later analysed by masters such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gioachino_Greco" title="Gioachino Greco">
       Gioachino Greco
      </a>
      in the seventeenth century. In the eighteenth century it was recommended by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phillip_Stamma" title="Phillip Stamma">
       Phillip Stamma
      </a>
      , and is sometimes known as the
      <b>
       Aleppo Gambit
      </b>
      in his honour. During the early period of modern chess, queen pawn openings were not in fashion, and the Queen's Gambit did not become common until the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1873_tournament_in_Vienna" title="1873 tournament in Vienna">
       1873 tournament in Vienna
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      As
      <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Steinitz" title="Wilhelm Steinitz">
       Steinitz
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Siegbert_Tarrasch" title="Siegbert Tarrasch">
       Tarrasch
      </a>
      developed chess theory and increased the appreciation of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_terminology#P" title="Chess terminology">
       positional play
      </a>
      , the Queen's Gambit grew more popular, reaching its zenith in the 1920s and 1930s, and was played in all but two of 34 games in the
      <a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship_1927" title="World Chess Championship 1927">
       1927 world championship
      </a>
      match between
      <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ra%C3%BAl_Capablanca" title="Jos Ral Capablanca">
       Jos Ral Capablanca
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Alekhine" title="Alexander Alekhine">
       Alexander Alekhine
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      After
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      , it was less frequently seen, as many Black players moved away from symmetrical openings, tending to use the
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_Defence" title="Indian Defence">
       Indian Defences
      </a>
      to combat queen pawn openings.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Queen's Gambit is still frequently played and it remains an important part of many
      <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_(chess)" title="Grandmaster (chess)">
       grandmasters'
      </a>
      opening repertoires.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
       Overview
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen%27s_Gambit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      With 2.c4, White threatens to
      <a href="/wiki/Exchange_(chess)" title="Exchange (chess)">
       exchange
      </a>
      a wing pawn (the c-pawn) for a center pawn (Black's d-pawn) and dominate the center with e2e4. This is not a true gambit, as Black cannot hold the pawn, for example: 1.d4 d5 2.c4 dxc4 3.e3 b5? (Black tries to guard his pawn but should pursue development with 3...e5!) 4.a4 c6? 5.axb5 cxb5?? 6.Qf3! winning a piece.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Queen's Gambit is divided into two major categories based on Black's response: The
      <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gambit_Accepted" title="Queen's Gambit Accepted">
       Queen's Gambit Accepted
      </a>
      (QGA) and the
      <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gambit_Declined" title="Queen's Gambit Declined">
       Queen's Gambit Declined
      </a>
      (QGD). In the QGA, Black plays 2...dxc4, temporarily giving up the center to obtain freer development. In the QGD, Black usually plays to hold d5. Frequently Black will be cramped, but Black aims to exchange pieces and use the pawn breaks at c5 and e5 to free his game.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Main_variations">
       Main variations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Queen%27s_Gambit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Main variations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      After 1.d4 d5 2.c4:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       2...dxc4 (
       <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gambit_Accepted" title="Queen's Gambit Accepted">
        Queen's Gambit Accepted
       </a>
       ) (QGA)
      </li>
      <li>
       2...Nc6 (
       <a href="/wiki/Chigorin_Defense" title="Chigorin Defense">
        Chigorin Defense
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       2...c5 (
       <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_Defense" title="Symmetrical Defense">
        Symmetrical Defense
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       2...c6 (
       <a href="/wiki/Slav_Defense" title="Slav Defense">
        Slav Defense
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       2...e5 (
       <a href="/wiki/Albin_Countergambit" title="Albin Countergambit">
        Albin Countergambit
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       2...e6 (
       <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gambit_Declined" title="Queen's Gambit Declined">
        Queen's Gambit Declined
       </a>
       ) (QGD)
      </li>
      <li>
       2...Bf5!? (
       <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Defense" title="Baltic Defense">
        Baltic Defense
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       2...Nf6?! (
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Defense" title="Marshall Defense">
        Marshall Defense
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       2...g6 (Alekhine's Variation)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Technically, any Black response other than 2...dxc4 (or another line with an early ...dxc4 that transposes into the QGA) is a Queen's Gambit Declined, but the Slav, Chigorin Defense, and Albin Counter Gambit are generally treated separately. There are so many QGD lines after 2...e6, that many of them are distinctive enough to warrant separate treatment. The Orthodox Defense and the
      <a href="/wiki/Tarrasch_Defense" title="Tarrasch Defense">
       Tarrasch Defense
      </a>
      are two important examples. See
      <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gambit_Declined" title="Queen's Gambit Declined">
       Queen's Gambit Declined
      </a>
      for more.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are many other possible responses:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Slav_Defense" title="Slav Defense">
        Slav Defense
       </a>
       is a solid response, although many variations are very tactical. If Black plays both ...c6 and ...e6 (in either order), the opening takes characteristics of both the Slav and the Orthodox Defense and is classified as a
       <a href="/wiki/Semi-Slav_Defense" title="Semi-Slav Defense">
        Semi-Slav Defense
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Chigorin_Defense" title="Chigorin Defense">
        Chigorin Defense
       </a>
       takes the game away from the normal positional channels of the QGD, and has been favoured by
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Morozevich" title="Alexander Morozevich">
        Alexander Morozevich
       </a>
       at top level; it appears to be playable for Black.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Albin_Countergambit" title="Albin Countergambit">
        Albin Countergambit
       </a>
       is a sharp attempt for Black to gain the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chess_terminology#Initiative" title="Chess terminology">
        initiative
       </a>
       . It is not common in top-level chess, but can be a dangerous weapon in club play.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_Defense" title="Symmetrical Defense">
        Symmetrical Defense
       </a>
       is very rarely played. Although it has not been definitely refuted, play seems to favor White.
      </li>
      <li>
       If White chooses to
       <a href="/wiki/Fianchetto" title="Fianchetto">
        fianchetto
       </a>
       his king's bishop, the game transposes into the
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_Opening" title="Catalan Opening">
        Catalan Opening
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Defense" title="Baltic Defense">
        Baltic Defense
       </a>
       is offbeat but playable.
      </li>
      <li>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Defense" title="Marshall Defense">
        Marshall Defense
       </a>
       is the weakest of the Black replies listed. Named after
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Marshall_(chess_player)" title="Frank Marshall (chess player)">
        Frank Marshall
       </a>
       , who was the first to devise the move, he briefly played it in the 1920s before abandoning it.
      </li>
     </ul>
