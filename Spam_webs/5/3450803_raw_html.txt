       <header>
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Predicting the Unexpected for the US Share Market
        </h1>
       </header>
       <div class="row row-meta">
        <span class="col-md-6 author vcard">
         <i class="el-icon-user icon">
         </i>
         <a class="url fn n" href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/author/vern-gowdie/" rel="author" title="View all posts by Vern Gowdie">
          Vern Gowdie
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="col-md-6 date">

           January 5, 2015
          </time>
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <img alt="Stock Market Analyze" class="featured-image wp-post-image" height="440" src="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/stock_analysis_660.jpg" width="661"/>
        <div class="addtoany_share_save_container addtoany_content_top">

          <a class="a2a_button_google_plus_share addtoany_special_service" data-annotation="none" data-href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/predicting-us-share-market/2015/01/05/">
          </a>
          <a class="a2a_button_reddit" href="http://www.addtoany.com/add_to/reddit?linkurl=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.dailyreckoning.com.au%2Fpredicting-us-share-market%2F2015%2F01%2F05%2F&amp;linkname=Predicting%20the%20Unexpected%20for%20the%20US%20Share%20Market" rel="nofollow" target="_blank" title="Reddit">

        <blockquote>
         <p align="center">
          
          <em>
           I  cannot imagine any condition which would cause a ship to founder. I cannot  conceive of any vital disaster happening to this vessel. Modern shipbuilding  has gone beyond that.
          </em>
          
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p style="text-align: right">
          Edward Smith, Captain, RMS Titanic
        </p>
        <p>
         Making predictions is a dangerous business.
        </p>
        <p>
         The lacklustre performance of the
         <a href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/category/australian-share-market-1/" title="more on the Australian share market ">
          Australian share market
         </a>
         in 2014 has  most likely influenced fund managers rather downbeat outlook for 2015.
        </p>
        <p>
         Whereas across the sea, Wall Street analysts are unanimously predicting  the
         <strong>
          US share market
         </strong>
         to continue its six-year bull run. Confidence begets even greater  confidence.
        </p>
        <p>
         According to
         <em>
          Business Insider
         </em>
         , the 2015 consensus from Wall  Streets top 13 analysts is: 
         <em>
          The average  year-end target on the S&amp;P 500 is 2,225 on earnings of $US125.35.
         </em>
         
        </p>
        <p>
         Currently the S&amp;P 500 index is 2,058 and operating earnings are US$116.
        </p>
        <p>
         On average, the analysts are predicting an 8% increase in the index and  earnings.
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         When youre in the business of selling positive performance, its easy  to understand why analysts pick a relatively high level of return (compared to  zero bound interest rates) for the coming year.
        </p>
        <p>
         The fact analysts are notoriously overly-optimistic in their forecasting  abilities seems to matter little to mainstream financial mediaespecially  when
         <strong>
          US markets
         </strong>
         have been so strong for so long.
        </p>
        <p>
         In April 2010, McKinsey &amp; Co produced a study titled: Equity analysts: Still too bullish.
        </p>
        <p>
         The following is an extract from the McKinsey report:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           Moreover, analysts have been persistently overoptimistic for the past 25  years, with estimates ranging from 10 to 12 percent a year, compared with  actual earnings growth of 6 percent. Over this time frame, actual  earnings growth surpassed forecasts in only two instances, both during the  earnings recovery following a recession. On average, analysts forecasts have  been almost 100 percent too high.
          </em>
          
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         The only times analysts have under-estimated earnings growth is the year  immediately following recessions. This is entirely consistent with the  psychological pattern of extrapolating the past into the futureforecasting  based on the rear view mirror.
        </p>
        <p>
         After six years of positive gains, its a no-brainer US analysts believe  2015 is destined to be a continuation of the past trend.
        </p>
        <p>
         Based on the McKinsey study, analysts have a proven track record of  over-estimating earnings by
         <em>
          almost 100  percent.
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Over the 25-year period earnings grew on average at an annual rate of 6%  (compared to forecasts ranging from 1012%). The 6% earnings growth rate  mirrored the growth rate of the US economy over the same period. Put simply,  the reason for this is earnings cannot grow faster than the economy. Its not possible.
        </p>
        <p>
         Based on this premise, a consensus of 8% earnings growth in 2015  suggests the US economy is also likely to produce a growth number of this  magnitude. Short of some global economic miracle, the US economy is going to  fall well short of 8% growth somewhere around 3% to 4% is at the upper end  of economic growth projections.
        </p>
        <p>
         The other headwind to optimistic US earnings forecasts is the slowdown  in share buyback schemes. Rising corporate bond interest rates have curtailed  the ability of companies to access cheap financing to artificially boost  earnings per share.
        </p>
        <p>
         According to data compiled by Bloomberg and the S&amp;P Dow Jones  Indices it 
         <em>
          shows
         </em>
         <em>
          S&amp;P 500 companies have been ploughing 95  percent of their profits into stock buybacks  and that stocks with the most  repurchases gained more than 300 percent since March 2009.
         </em>
         
        </p>
        <p>
         Minus the tailwind of creative accounting, US companies (on average) are  going to be assessed on their real performance. This is exactly what happened  to IBM in October 2014, prompting this headline from CBC News:
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          <em>
           IBM stock falls 7% after it downgrades earnings forecast
          </em>
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         IBM had been a serial abuser of the stock buyback trick and had failed  to reinvest sufficient capital into the business for future growth. This little  bit of account skulduggery rewarded IBM shareholders with a165% gain from late  2008 to early 2013. Since then, IBMs share price has fallen 25%. The moral of  the IBM story is that eventually the truth wins out. This moral will be learnt  by investors in the broader market in due course.
        </p>
        <p>
         My prediction for 2015 is that Wall Street analysts are going to  continue the not-so-proud tradition of over-egging the earnings numbers.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, in spite of my projected sluggish US earnings growth, the  analysts prediction of the S&amp;P 500 index hitting 2,225 points is a very  real possibility. Momentum buying based on nothing but optimism and hubris is  not uncommon in the late stages of an ageing bull market.
        </p>
        <p>
         Those (like myself) waiting for a significant market correction (a  decline of 50+%) may just have to wait a little longer.
        </p>
        <p>
         In his latest quarterly newsletter, Jeremy Grantham of Boston-based GMO  (and one of the smartest investment brains in the business) said:
        </p>
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          
          <em>
           Nevertheless, despite my nervousness [in current market values] I am  still a believer that the Fed will engineer a fully-fledged bubble (S&amp;P 500  over 2250) before a very serious decline.
          </em>
          
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         The difference between Grantham and the Wall Street analysts (besides  the fact he has an impressive 40+ year track record) is that he sees the  S&amp;P 500 at 2250 points as a
         <em>
          fully-fledged  bubble
         </em>
         , whereas the analysts see it as marker on an ever northward heading  chart.
        </p>
        <p>
         Grantham and his firm, GMO have an impressive track-record in  identifying bubbles. However, in a market boom the most under-appreciated quality  is experience. No one wants to listen to the words of caution from those who  have been there and done that. People want the hot-tip. They dont want to be  told to take a cold shower and be patient.
        </p>
        <p>
         Therefore Granthams prescient warning will go largely unnoticed.
        </p>
        <p>
         Since the GFC,
         <a href="http://www.dailyreckoning.com.au/category/banks/us-federal-reserve/" title="more on the US Federal Reserve">
          the Federal Reserve
         </a>
         has done everything within its power to levitate  the US share market. In their single-minded determination to create the  pre-determined wealth-effect objective, the fact that market values have been  grossly distorted has counted for nought.
        </p>
        <p>
         Based on past actions, Granthams belief that 
         <em>
          the
         </em>
         <em>
          Fed will engineer a  fully-fledged bubble
         </em>
         is an accurate call. If the US share  market begins to seriously wobble in 2015, its a fair bet the Fed will delay  increasing interest rates and/or begin another round of QEsetting up the  last hurrah phase for markets.
        </p>
        <p>
         Against this backdrop the Australian share market is likely to play  follow the leader and could well rise another 5% to 10% in 2015.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, when the
         <em>
          last hurrah
         </em>
         is over and the
         <em>
          serious decline
         </em>
         phase  hits the US share market, theres no doubt well be caught in the downdraft  emanating from Wall Street.
        </p>
        <p>
         Prudent investors who value the wisdom borne from experience, need to  ask themselves whether a possible 10% gain is worth a probable 50+% decline.
        </p>
        <p>
         The US share market has been subjected to 38 severe bear markets since  1870an average of one in four years.
        </p>
        <p>
         Over one-third of the 38 bear markets have inflicted losses in excess of  40%.
        </p>
        <p>
         The last severe bear market was from 11 Oct 2007 to 6 Mar 2009a loss  of 57.7% over a 17 month period. NOTE  this 17 month bear market wiped out the  entire gains of the preceding five-year period. Thats how swift and vicious  bear markets can be.
        </p>
        <p>
         The law of averages (and common sense) tells us the next severe bear  market is much closer than the Wall Street analysts would have you believe.
        </p>
        <p>
         The US share market on every long term valuation and sentiment metric is  overvalued, overbought and over bullish. The Fed (as Grantham suspects) may  well continue to play to the cheering crowd, and 2015 could be another positive  one.
        </p>
        <p>
         However, in my opinion, average investors are playing with fire if they  try to milk the last drop out of this market. The last 10% to 20% of a bull  market run disappears very quickly when the bear awakens from its hibernation.
        </p>
        <p>
         While on the topic of unpredictable predictions, who would have thought  in January 2007 (when the All Ords index was around 5700 points) that eight  years later the market would be trading below that level.
        </p>
        <p>
         Experience has taught me to expect the unexpected with markets. There is  no pre-ordained rule that says markets must go up. Markets can and have been  manipulated to achieve short term objectives. However, as in the case of IBM,  the truth eventually wins out.
        </p>
        <p>
         The longer the US market continues to move away from its historical  valuation averages, the harder the fall that awaits us.
        </p>
        <p>
         When it does, severe bear market number 39 is assured  a place near the very top of the table.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Regards,
         </strong>
        </p>
