          <div class="article-body__content">
           <p>
            May 15 (Bloomberg) -- The Pentagon risks exposing itself to
hackers by opening its communications networks to Apple Inc. and
Samsung Electronics Co. smartphones and tablets, according to
cybersecurity officials.
           </p>
           <p>
            The two companies want to crack a market long dominated by
Waterloo, Ontario-based BlackBerry, which provides about 470,000
of the U.S. militarys more than 600,000 mobile devices. While
those numbers are a fraction of BlackBerrys 76 million
subscribers worldwide, the company has promoted the Defense
Departments security endorsement to commercial and government
customers.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Pentagons plan would eventually give employees the
flexibility of connecting devices such as Samsung Galaxy S4
smartphones and Apple iPads to unclassified networks. It also
would create more vulnerabilities to cybersecurity breaches,
said Pat McGarry, principal systems engineer at Ixia, a network
security company based in Calabasas, California.
           </p>

           <p>
            It is a debacle, a disaster waiting to happen, McGarry
said in a phone interview. There is no technology that would
make Apple and Samsung smartphones and tablets immune to new
malicious software known as malware, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            The military on May 2 approved use of Suwon, South Korea-based Samsungs devices running a secure version of Google
Inc.s Android operating system. At the same time, it cleared
Pentagon offices to buy BlackBerry PlayBook tablets and new
BlackBerry 10 smartphones.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Apple Approval
           </h2>
           <p>
            Apple, based in Cupertino, California, may win approval as
early as this week to sell its iPhones and iPads to the
Pentagon.
           </p>
           <p>
            Samsung, Apple and the newly approved BlackBerry devices
pose an acceptable risk for unclassified communications when
used with a mobile-device management system, said Mark Orndorff,
a program executive officer at the Defense Information Systems
Agency. Testing will continue to ensure the products are secure,
he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Defense Department wont allow new smartphones and
tablets to connect to its networks until a secure device-management system is in place, Orndorff said in an e-mail. It
plans to award a contract to build and operate the system by the
end of June, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            Samsungs Android and Apples iOS operating systems have
vulnerabilities that will remain even with a device-management
system, McGarry said. They have different architectures than
BlackBerry and offer more attack vectors for hackers, including
the use of 3G, 4G, Wi-Fi and Bluetooth networks, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            BlackBerry was architected from day one to allow for end-to-end secure communications, McGarry said.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Google Android
           </h2>
           <p>
            Samsung is aggressively pursuing the necessary features
and security enhancements to meet the stringent requirements of
doing business with the U.S. military, Ashley Wimberly, a
company spokeswoman, said in an e-mail.
           </p>
           <p>
            The electronics company worked with the U.S. National
Security Agency to create Knox, a secure version of Googles
Android operating system with multiple layers of software and
hardware protection.
           </p>
           <p>
            The system lets employers keep corporate and military
applications and data in a secure place on a smartphone or
tablet, and remotely erase them if necessary, according to
Samsung.
           </p>
           <h2>
            BlackBerry Security
           </h2>
           <p>
            BlackBerry has more than 1 million U.S. government
customers and the market has remained remarkably stable,
Scott Totzke, senior vice president for BlackBerry security,
said in an interview.
           </p>
           <p>
            The company has long emphasized the security of its
products.
           </p>
           <p>
            Privacy is built into everything we do, and weve been
doing it longer and better than anyone in the industry, Chief
Operating Officer Kristian Tear said in a speech yesterday at a
conference in Orlando, Florida. Security has been baked into
BlackBerry from the ground up.
           </p>
           <p>
            Apple declined to comment, deferring questions to the
defense agency, said Trudy Miller, a company spokeswoman.
           </p>
           <p>
            Giri Sreenivas, a vice president at Rapid7 LLC, a Boston-based cybersecurity company, said the process of updating Apple
and Samsung mobile systems with security fixes may pose issues
for the military.
           </p>
           <p>
            Samsung and Apple push out security patches and depend on
users to install the fixes, Sreenivas said in a phone interview.
BlackBerry controls its own network to deliver security patches
to devices, which are automatically installed, he said.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Security Patches
           </h2>
           <p>
            That difference is significant because it may be more
difficult for the military to ensure that all its Samsung Knox
and Apple devices are protected with the latest security fixes,
Sreenivas said. Samsung and Apple users in the military who
havent properly updated their devices would probably be blocked
from accessing networks, he said.
           </p>
           <p>
            There isnt a good way to tell if the two companies
devices have been compromised with malicious code, especially
through downloaded applications, said Richard Bejtlich, chief
security officer for Mandiant Corp., a computer security firm
based in Alexandria, Virginia.
           </p>
           <p>
            There are certain countries you go to, you bring your
phone up on the national network and they will push rogue code
onto your system, Bejtlich said in an interview. The second
problem you run into is how easy is it for a rogue party to
introduce a malicious app onto your system.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Lost Tablets
           </h2>
           <p>
            The Pentagon sought bids in November from vendors capable
of securing as many as 300,000 mobile devices in the next
several years.
           </p>
           <p>
            The winner will enforce security policies on all mobile
devices, ensuring only authorized users can tap into military
networks. When smartphones or tablet computers are lost or
compromised, the vendor must be capable of remotely wiping
devices of data, according to the agency.
           </p>
           <p>
            Companies such as Sunnyvale, California-based Good
Technology and Fort Lauderdale, Florida-based Citrix Systems
Inc. are on teams that have submitted bids.
           </p>
           <p>
            Good Technology already has performed similar work with
other agencies such as the Department of Homeland Security, said
Jeff Ait, public-sector director for the company.
           </p>
           <h2>
            Pentagon Endorsement
           </h2>
           <p>
            While the Pentagons security endorsements are considered
key milestones, they wont lead to immediate boosts for Apple
and Samsung devices.
           </p>
           <p>
            I expect its going to take at least another year or two
before there is a true broad embrace of these kinds of platforms
for the type of usage youve seen historically with BlackBerry
devices, said Sreenivas, the Rapid7 vice president.
           </p>
           <p>
            The Defense Departments security clearance is valuable
because it may encourage other industries, such as health care
and financial services, to switch to Apple or Samsung devices,
said Carolina Milanesi, research vice president at Stamford,
Connecticut-based Gartner Inc.
           </p>
           <p>
            Other organizations that might have been reluctant to
open up their doors to these providers might change their views
going forward, she said in an e-mail.
           </p>

Samsung and Apple users in the military who\nhaven&#x2019;t properly updated their devices would probably be blocked\nfrom accessing networks, he said. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;There isn&#x2019;t a good way to tell if the two companies&#x2019;\ndevices have been compromised with malicious code, especially\nthrough downloaded applications, said Richard Bejtlich, chief\nsecurity officer for Mandiant Corp., a computer security firm\nbased in Alexandria, Virginia. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;&#x201C;There are certain countries you go to, you bring your\nphone up on the national network and they will push rogue code\nonto your system,&#x201D; Bejtlich said in an interview. &#x201C;The second\nproblem you run into is how easy is it for a rogue party to\nintroduce a malicious app onto your system.&#x201D; &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Lost Tablets &lt;/h2&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The Pentagon sought bids in November from vendors capable\nof securing as many as 300,000 mobile devices in the next\nseveral years. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;The winner will enforce security policies on all mobile\ndevices, ensuring only authorized users can tap into military\nnetworks. Sunnyvale, California-based Good\nTechnology and Fort Lauderdale, Florida-based Citrix Systems\nInc. are on teams that have submitted bids. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;p&gt;Good Technology already has performed similar work with\nother agencies such as the Department of Homeland Security, said\nJeff Ait, public-sector director for the company. &lt;/p&gt;\n&lt;h2&gt;Pentagon Endorsement
