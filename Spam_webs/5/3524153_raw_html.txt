          <p>
           It shouldnt matter much to Californians whether Antonio Villaraigosa gets to be a U.S. senator or governor someday. But it should matter whether Antonio Villaraigosa gets to be Antonio Villaraigosa.
          </p>
          <p>
           Villaraigosa didnt get that opportunity over the past six weeks, as he pondered, and ultimately decided against, a campaign for the U.S. Senate. Instead, in the media coverage and public discussion of the political drama, he was portrayed as one thing above all: the Latino candidate.
          </p>
          <p>
           Villaraigosas supporters expressed hopes that he would be a historic Latino candidate. Opponents pointedly said that Latinos dont vote at high rates, as they urged him to skip the race and support Attorney General Kamala Harris. And then critics of those opponents claimed that pushing the Latino candidate out of the race was an insult to Latinos.
           <br/>
          </p>
          <p>
           The media gleefully played up the possibility of conflict between Latinos and African-Americans backing Harris, who is African-American and Indian-American. Ethnic battles brewing in 2016 California U.S. Senate race? asked a San Francisco Chronicle headline. And when Villaraigosa announced last week that he would not run  hinting at a preference to run for governor in 2018  the absence of his Latino-ness from the race was mourned. Villaraigosa was leaving California Latinos without an immediate standard-bearer for the office, the Sacramento Bee reported.
          </p>
          <p>
           In the face of this relentless ethnic reductionism, the best response may be: Ay Ay Ay Ay. If one of this eras most complicated, accomplished and frustrating Californians can be defined so narrowly, then what hope do the other 38 million of us have to be considered as full and complex humans, products not only of our heritage but also of our deeds and dreams?
          </p>
          <p>
           While media and political figures may think all the Latino talk is a celebration of a rising demographic, such conversation can sound like an attempt to cage an incredibly diverse array of 15 million Californians in a single minority box. And that in turn perpetuates the societal assumption, still maddeningly prevalent in 2015 California, that Anglos define the mainstream.
          </p>
          <p>
           The recent Villaraigosa coverage demonstrates the durability and absurdity of this mindset. Put simply, its hard to think of a more mainstream California politician than a former Assembly speaker and two-term mayor of Los Angeles. Or a more interesting one. But it was hard to find details of the real, kaleidoscopic Villaraigosa story in the recent Villaraigosa political stories.
          </p>
          <p>
           There was only a tiny bit about his terms in Sacramento in the late 1990s, and momentous legislative decisions on energy, budgets and pensions. And there was precious little assessment of his eight years as mayor  which saw dramatic victories in policing and public transit and dramatic defeats in education and the local economy.
          </p>
          <p>
           The central questions that should be asked about a politician went unanswered: How does he make decisions? Did his tenure leave his city better or worse off? What would be his policy priorities as a senator? Instead, the stories were about Villaraigosa as potential Latino candidate. The irony of this relentless focus on Villaraigosas Latino identity is that the mayor spent so much of his time in office reaching out to people across Los Angeles (he attended more bar mitzvahs than many rabbis) that he was criticized for neglecting the details of governing.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pride in who we are is vital, and conversation about our diverse backgrounds is great. But not when it comes at the expense of substantive thinking about our problems. When I attended the UC Berkeley law school commencement two years ago, the speakers, including U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder, talked for more than two hours about their ethnic backgrounds without offering a single idea or scholarly argument. I left angry  but thats probably because Im Scots-Irish.
           <br/>
          </p>
          <p>
           One downside to Californias ethnic obsession is the degree to which it prevents us from embracing our shared origins as Californians. The majority of Californians, for the first time in modern history, have been born and raised in the state. So while its worth noting that Villaraigosas parents and grandparents came from a different place than mine (or those of rival politicians), that fact should take a back seat to discussions of his record and qualifications to improve living standards across the state.
           <br/>
          </p>
          <p>
           Because at the end of the day, our next governor and senator need to be standard bearers for the one demographic that often gets neglected in our obsessing over identity: Californians.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Mathews is California and Innovation editor at Zcalo Public Square (www.zocalopublicsquare.org
           </em>
          </p>
