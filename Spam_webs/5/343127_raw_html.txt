           Top Ten Films of 2011
          </h1>
          <div class="byline">
           by
           <a href="/user/ur0765133/" onclick="(new Image()).src='/rg/list/byline/images/b.gif?link=%2Fuser%2Fur0765133%2F';">

          <div class="description" id="view-list-desc">
           Managing Editor Keith Simanton's list of the top ten films of 2011. "For all the claims that Hollywood has run out of ideas and that movies are only about packaging I'd counter with the top ten films of this year," Simanton says. "Just outside of my top ten are
           <a href="/title/tt0983193/">
            The Adventures of Tintin
           </a>
           (#11),
           <a href="/title/tt1615147/">
            Margin Call
           </a>
           (#12),
           <a href="/title/tt1605783/">
            Midnight in Paris
           </a>
           (#13) and
           <a href="/title/tt0970179/">
            Hugo
           </a>
           (#14). PS: There are a handful of films I haven't seen yet, like
           <a href="/title/tt1568346/">
            The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo
           </a>
           .

               #9 -
               <a href="/title/tt1723811/">
                Shame
               </a>
               : Director
               <a href="/name/nm2588606/">
                Steve McQueen
               </a>
               follows up his first, assured film
               <a href="/title/tt0986233/">
                Hunger
               </a>
               with a film even more depressing and enervating than the Bobby Sands hunger-strike. Shame is also as deliberate and as sculpted.
               <a href="/name/nm1055413/">
                Michael Fassbender
               </a>
               and
               <a href="/name/nm1659547/">
                Carey Mulligan
               </a>
               are fearless, playing a brother and sister who "aren't bad people" but "come from a bad place." That place included a lot of weird sex which has warped both siblings to the point where normal human relations aren't very tenable. It's a stunning statement about the way that humans can be twisted by anything, food, power, money, into something approaching demons.

               </a>

               #1 -
               <a href="/title/tt0478304/">
                Tree of Life
               </a>
               : Director
               <a href="/name/nm0000517/">
                Terrence Malick
               </a>
               's greatest film to date is an audacious, spare, deeply moving, and gorgeous work, that dares to approach the very nature of the universe and how it is present in the lamp in a front yard of patchy grass or in a baby's delicate foot.
               <a href="/name/nm0000093/">
                Brad Pitt
               </a>
               gives a memorable performance as a Texas father whose shattered dreams mold and deform his own children while
               <a href="/name/nm1567113/">
                Jessica Chastain
               </a>
               makes her Earth-mother role one of quiet grace. The music includes classical works such as Smetana's Moldau and a near-gothic score by
               <a href="/name/nm0523881/">
                Alexandre Desplat
               </a>
               , that seems to come from the deep.
               <a href="/name/nm0523881/">
                Emmanuel Lubezki
               </a>
               's cinematography allows the light to be as much a character as any individual in the film. It's a divisive movie, however. After its early morning screening at Cannes boos erupted from one quarter while applause came from the other. It was as if we were all wrestling for the soul of the film and was one of the most electric moments I've ever experienced with cinema.
