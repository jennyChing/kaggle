     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Indian_religions" title="Indian religions">
       South Asian
      </a>
      traditions, the state of being trapped in the illusory belief that one is the
      <b>
       false ego
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Ahamkara" title="Ahamkara">
       ahamkara
      </a>
      : "I am a doer") is known as
      <a href="/wiki/Maya_(illusion)" title="Maya (illusion)">
       maya
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Sa%E1%B9%83s%C4%81ra" title="Sasra">
       samsara
      </a>
      . In
      <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
       psychology
      </a>
      , 'Ego' is presumed as one of the three parts of
      <a href="/wiki/Psychic_apparatus" title="Psychic apparatus">
       psychic apparatus
      </a>
      defined by
      <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">
       Sigmund Freud
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_model" title="Structural model">
       structural model
      </a>
      . It is also defined by various religions across the world where slight differences also occur in their conceptions.
      <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
       Hinduism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      have least differences in defining various psychological terms such as self, ego, soul, etc.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_.28Advaita_and_Dvaita_philosophy.3B_and_Dvaitadvaita.29">
       Definition (Advaita and Dvaita philosophy; and Dvaitadvaita)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ego_(religion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition (Advaita and Dvaita philosophy; and Dvaitadvaita)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "It is the spontaneous identity of an individual to represent (itself)." According to religious scriptures, it is the characteristic of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_form" title="Life form">
       living beings
      </a>
      with a higher level of consciousness that includes humans. Reflection of ego with 'name' is commonly observed in all living beings; that is how they
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recognize" title="Recognize">
       recognize
      </a>
      and represent themselves. Ego is also considered as 'identity over identity', e.g., name to a person where name is the secondary identity used by, or for, the person (primary identity or '
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_(spirituality)" title="Self (spirituality)">
       self
      </a>
      ') to represent. It (ego) exists in the state of consciousness.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_.28A_Course_In_Miracles.29">
       Definition (A Course In Miracles)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ego_(religion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition (A Course In Miracles)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Course_In_Miracles" title="A Course In Miracles">
       A Course In Miracles
      </a>
      the word ego is used to describe the belief in your dream character, the person who seems to be the doer and be affected by everything within the world of perception. This is contrasted with the Self of the collective Son of God, which is formless, unchanging, invulnerable, and eternal Oneness. In contrast with the beliefs in separation, attack, and condemnation, which drive the ego, the Self of the Sonship's true nature is characterized by unconditional love and seamless Oneness. The closest thing to a definition of ego in A Course in Miracles is below The ego is nothing more than a part of your belief about yourself."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ego_.28Ahamkara.29_and_Self_.28Jivatma.29">
       Ego (
       <a href="/wiki/Ahamkara" title="Ahamkara">
        Ahamkara
       </a>
       ) and Self (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jivatma" title="Jivatma">
        Jivatma
       </a>
       )
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ego_(religion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ego (Ahamkara) and Self (Jivatma)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Although ego is generally accepted as a synonym of
      <a href="/wiki/Self" title="Self">
       self
      </a>
      , religious doctrine says otherwise.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jivatma" title="Jivatma">
       Jivatma
      </a>
      is the
      <i>
       <b>
        spontaneous identity
       </b>
      </i>
      part of some self, inherent of all living beings.
      <a href="/wiki/Ahamkara" title="Ahamkara">
       Ahamkara
      </a>
      is the human ego. Jivatma's truest representation cannot
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Exist" title="Exist">
       exist
      </a>
      of itself in some nowadays, because since it is eternal, it is presumably incorruptible, and perfect; whereas ahamkara is more temporal"any created thing" here or "to do" now. The spontaneity of Jivatma exists in consciousness with ahamkara, but also subsists with sense organs and feeling in an unconscious responsiveness.
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Bhagavad_Gita" title="Bhagavad Gita">
       Bhagavad Gita
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shri_Krishna" title="Shri Krishna">
       Shri Krishna
      </a>
      says to Arjun that ahamkara must be removed.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veda_Vyasa" title="Veda Vyasa">
       Veda Vyasa
      </a>
      and their
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_scripture" title="Religious scripture">
       religious scriptures
      </a>
      also enforce the merging of anyone's jeevatma existence to a supreme soul (paramatman), and anyone's ahamkara existence to its creator.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ego_and_consciousness">
       Ego and consciousness
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ego_(religion)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ego and consciousness">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Higher levels of consciousness
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      are seen in humans and (exceptionally) to a certain extent in some animals, e.g., elephants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Humans are the only entities where ego is observed apparently and up to variable extent in the state of consciousness dividing them further into egoistic, egotistic, egomaniac, etc.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      'Spontaneous identity to represent' disappears in all living beings when ego merges with self in the state of unconsciousness and is not recovered until consciousness;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though in cases of humans it may occasionally appear in
      <a href="/wiki/Dream" title="Dream">
       dreams
      </a>
      but that is spontaneous and not a part of will (or determination).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
