       <!-- CONTENT OF THE POST -->
       Bunnie Huang
       <a href="http://www.bunniestudios.com/blog/?p=826">
        quotes a bit from an All Things Considered airing
       </a>
       of some of Alain De Bottons
       <a href="http://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=122256847">
        The Enlightening Bridge Between Art and Work
       </a>
       : Two centuries ago, our forebears would have known the precise history and source of almost every one of the limited number of things they ate and owned. They would have been familiar with the pig, the carpenter, the weaver, the loom and the dairymaid. The range of items available for purchase may have grown exponentially since then, but our understanding of their genesis has grown ever more obscure. We are now as imaginatively disconnected from the production and distribution of our goods as we are practically in reach of them, a process of alienation which has stripped us of opportunities for wonder, gratitude and guilt. Thats some good Maker stuff right there.

               January 13, 2010 at 11:10 am
              </a>
             </div>
             <blockquote>
              <p>
               
               <i>
                Two centuries ago, our forebears would have known the precise history and source of almost every one of the limited number of things they ate and owned.
               </i>
               
              </p>
              <p>
               No, most of them would have still held the mediaeval notion that mystery was a good thing in itself.
              </p>
             </blockquote>
             <p>
              Aside: lots and lots of people still hold that notion.
             </p>
             <p>
              Point: it isnt about choosing to know these things, or prefering them to stay as the magical-dust which makes the world work. The point is that in their daily lives (and the knowledge and traditions of centuries up-to-that-point) people probably knew more about the origins of their stuff and its processes, because it was as it always had been before: localized, small-scale production, whose manufacture and manufacturers were more a part of peoples daily/social life.
             </p>
             <p>
              As opposed to now, where most of our things (orders-of-magnitude more variety of things) now come from who-knows-where, using who-knows-what materials, also sourced from who-knows-where by who-knows-who.
             </p>
            </div>
