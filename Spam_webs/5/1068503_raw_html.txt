      <h1>
       Classics as Comics: Marvel Illustrateds Moby-Dick
      </h1>
      <br/>
      <!-- templatecell : articleRow -->
      <p>
       <span class="summary_breadcrumb">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       By
       <a href="/cgi-bin/artman2/search.cgi?action=search&amp;keywordSearchFields=author&amp;keyword=Andy Frisk&amp;template=searchEngine/articles_by_author_results.html" rel="noindex">
        <span itemprop="reviewer">
         Andy Frisk
        </span>
       </a>
       <br/>
       <span class="date" itemprop="dtreviewed">
        Apr 13, 2009 - 11:20
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        MOBY-DICK is considered by many to be the Great American Novel.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        It is a thrilling action/adventure tale to say the least and a ponderous tome of incredible depth and meaning to say the most.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Any sequential art adaptation worth its sea-salt should splash down some where in between these two views of the novel, satisfying the adventure aspects as well as at least touching on the novels depth.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Marvel Illustrateds version, adapted by the very capable Roy Thomas, accomplishes this task quite adequately.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        It is though, absolutely, as Roy Thomas himself states in his introduction, a bridge, an inspiration to seek out the original.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        For those of us who have read the original, perhaps more than once even, it serves also as a bridge leading back to a rediscovery of the original novel.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
         <table align="center" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="2" width="450">
          <tr>
           <td>
            <img alt="mobydickcharacters.jpg" border="1" class="pic" height="683" hspace="5" src="http://www.comicbookbin.com/artman2/uploads/5/mobydickcharacters.jpg" vspace="2" width="450"/>
           </td>
          </tr>
         </table>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        A look at the physical depictions of the novels most famous characters is a good place to start when discussing this adaptation.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ahab, among the most memorable characters in American Literature has been represented on film, in other sequential art adaptations and, perhaps most importantly, envisioned in the minds of literally millions of the novels readers.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Pascal Alixes version of Ahab is pretty traditional yet has it own interesting details.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Alixes Ahab is strong and stoutly built with lightly colored, or gray, hair and beard.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Its an interesting, slightly ambiguous color that doesnt directly reflect or peg his age down.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Perhaps his hair is meant to be more grayish in color since his eyebrows are jet black in sharp contrast.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        He wears a standard sea jacket with a red sash denoting his rank as captain but it is the scar on his face and the depiction of his sperm whale-jaw bone leg that are most striking about his appearance.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        His peg-leg is an unfinished bone with an uneven profile and a very rough look.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        It is not the polished and shapely reproduction of a human leg but rather a hastily and perhaps purposefully left rough hewn bone that reveals something, visually, about Ahabs character.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ahab is a bent and gnarled man inside due to his encounter with Moby Dick and is twisted with rage and all consuming hate.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        His gnarled, unpolished, and uneven whale bone leg helps give us a clue to the twisted spirit of this man.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        His anguish is made physically manifest though his legs appearance.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        The scar which runs down his face is also pretty accurately portrayed as in the novel, with one major exception.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        In the graphic novel it appears to terminate near his upper right lip whereas in the novel, Ahabs scar runs down his neck and disappears underneath his collar causing Ishmael to wonder whether it runs the whole length of his body like a lightening strike would run the course of an entire tree.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        To me this change is pretty important although small as it serves as a link to one of Melvilles inspirations for Ahab,
        <st1:city w:st="on">
         <st1:place w:st="on">
          Milton
         </st1:place>
        </st1:city>
        s Satan.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Satan in portrayed in PARADISE LOST as struck with deep lines in his face which are thunder entrenched, as a sign of his proud nature being struck down by Gods lightening.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ahabs scar, likened to a lightening strike, is metaphoric of his being struck down for his pride but like Satan, continuing to rebel against God, in Ahabs case the White Whale, as a symbol of an unknowable God.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        All of Alixe and Thomas renderings of the novels other characters reveal their natures accurately and realistically.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Queequeg is the heavily tattooed, small of stature South Pacific noble savage.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Starbuck is conservative in appearance much like his character calls for with short neat hair, no facial hair and a newer looking sea jacket than the one Ahab sports, perhaps suggesting his lesser amount of years at sea but more likely his closer attention to personal and professional upkeep of physical presentation in wardrobe and appearance, a hallmark of a mind less distracted by overwhelming emotion.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ishmael is the young, relatively and sufficiently stylishly un-kept moody youth that he is at the time of the novels action.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        The whale himself, Moby Dick, is accurately portrayed as the battle scarred, harpoon embedded, and lance bearing master of the seas.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <table align="center" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="2" width="404">
         <tr>
          <td>
           <img alt="mobydickcoverlarge_1.jpg" border="1" class="pic" height="524" hspace="5" src="http://www.comicbookbin.com/artman2/uploads/5/mobydickcoverlarge_1.jpg" vspace="2" width="404"/>
          </td>
         </tr>
        </table>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        Alixes renderings of the facial expressions and movements of the characters also represent the nature of each quite well.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ahab often has an angry madness about his countenance that borders on insanity, accurately depicting his inner madness and obsession with killing Moby Dick.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Queequag, for all his facial and body tattooing giving him a savage look, nearly always maintains a serene and regal facial expression and his movements are graceful and powerful like the son of a kings should be.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ishmael, our narrator and musing philosopher is sufficiently depicted with a wandering look upon his face as if hes lost in thought, particularly as he is shown either ascending or descending from the mast-head of the ship, which serves as the highest point on the ship and therefore the lookout.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        Alixes panel layouts and actions sequences capture quite well the storys momentum towards the final showdown between Ahab and Moby Dick.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        The panels separating the different scenes become more irregular and uneven as the work progresses with splashes bleeding through them at some points near the end.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        This helps convey the breakdown of the ship, and its crew, physically and mentally as they both begin to fall victim to Moby Dick.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        One particular scene conveyed especially well is the one where Moby Dick bites off Ahabs leg.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        It is not gory or bloody by any means and the actual moment is experienced through a harrowing panel where only Ahabs anguished face is visible surrounded by red and black.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        It is a visual that defies description in words yet strikingly portrays both Ahabs physical and psychological pain at the moment as only a graphic novels graphic language can.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        Speaking of images that are difficult to put into words, Chapter 55 of the novel MOBY-DICK, which is not recreated in the graphic novel but bears mentioning as it is a relevant observation which helps one understand the difficulty of translating a picture into words and vice versa, Ishmael suggests that drawings that existed at his time did not accurately portray what a whale really looked like and the only way to really get a feel for the image of a whale is to see one for oneself in real life.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Metaphorically, this observation can be applied to this graphic novel as it is an interpretation of a work that is nearly bottomless in its depth of meaning and serves only as a superficial rendering of the novel.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        One cannot relate the complexities of MOBY-DICK in graphic novel form.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        As mentioned though, Roy Thomas was fully aware of this fact.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        As he states, again in his introduction to the collected graphic novel: In the end, I decided it was best to concentrate pretty much solely on the storyline itself-heavens knows theres plenty of that!-but that didnt stop me, while re-reading the novel, from reveling in those chapter long asides and gleaning an occasional tidbit from them that could be sandwiched into the story proper.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        These chapter long asides are where most of the novels depth occurs and Thomas does do a pretty good job of bringing some of this depth in.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
         <table align="center" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="2" width="450">
          <tr>
           <td>
            <img alt="MOBY003_int-3.jpg" border="1" class="pic" height="683" hspace="5" src="http://www.comicbookbin.com/artman2/uploads/5/MOBY003_int-3.jpg" vspace="2" width="450"/>
           </td>
          </tr>
         </table>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        For example: Chapter 35 of MOBY-DICK titled The Mast-Head is a masterfully written commentary by Melville on his literary time periods largest movement, Transcendentalism.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Melville puts Ishmael up on the mast head of the ship where he experiences the Emersonian transcendent, sublime spiritual merging with the natural world, but while doing so realizes that he can nearly topple from this post, remarking that too much romantic daydreaming can be detrimental.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Thomas gives us one page of Ishmael either ascending or descending the ships rigging to or from the mast head where he remarks like he does in Chapter 35 that he kept poor watch due to his romantic, melancholy and absent-minded nature.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Ishmael, in this near full page spread is looking squarely out and up off of the page at the reader, much like as in the novel, he addresses the reader in the chapter.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Thomas also gives us a snippet of Chapter 99, The Doubloon where Ahab, Starbuck, Flask, Stubb, Queequag, and Pip (the cabin boy who goes insane after nearly getting left behind in the ocean after jumping ship) all relate their individual understanding of what the doubloon (a period valuable gold coin) nailed to the main mast by Ahab which is to be claimed by the first to spot Moby Dick, means and represents.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Each characters projection of their own personal meaning and understanding onto the doubloon is very telling of each of their natures and outlooks on the voyage and life in general.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Melville uses this chapter to further develop each of these mens character and Thomas could have left it out as the graphic novels readers by this point have probably developed a sense for these mens character already but Thomas manages to work a representation of this important chapter into his adaptation.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        Unfortunately Thomas either chose or was unable to work in aspects of two of MOBY-DICKs most powerful and philosophically important chapters, The Whiteness of The Whale (Chapter 42) which helps explain the sublime horror that Moby Dick, being a whale of an irregular all white color, can inspire in its visage and The Try-Works (Chapter 96) which reveal more of the intricacies of whaling as a well as more of Ishmaels musings on the nature of their quest to kill Moby Dick and how it is an inversion of what should be their true purpose-the hunting of whales for profit, not the hunting of one specific whale to profit their captains ego.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        Thomas also, perhaps due to the need to include a glossary of terms that the reader might be unfamiliar with, leaves out Ishmaels interesting but rather long collection of extracts on whales taken from several works of literature and various books on cetology the science of whales which opens the novel and helps set the stage for the complex and deep themes the reader would be encountering in the novel. This is a work based upon the action/adventure aspects of the novel though and the exclusion of these chapters and aspects do not detract from the purpose of this graphic novel.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        A brief inclusion of them would have rounded out the adaptation though.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        Overall, for those of who love MOBY-DICK (as you probably can guess, if youre still with me, as I do) Marvel Illustrateds Roy Thomas adapted graphic novel interpretation is a worthy one and is a pleasure to read and absorb.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        If youve never read MOBY-DICK before you will still enjoy this easy to read yet completely non-juvenile adaptation.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        I truly feel that Thomas accomplished his goal of creating an adaptation of a grand and fascinating novel that truly will inspire readers to want to either re-read or pick up for the first time Melvilles original work.
        <span style="mso-spacerun: yes">
        </span>
        It certainly has done so for me.
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p class="MsoNormal" style="MARGIN: 0in 0in 0pt">
       <span style="FONT-FAMILY: Arial; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">
        <o:p>
        </o:p>
       </span>
      </p>
