         <h1 class="title" id="page-title-small">
          Classroom Management: 10 Ways to Deal with Difficult Students
         </h1>
         <div class="region region-content">
          <div class="block block-system" id="block-system-main">
           <div class="content">
            <div class="node node-article node-promoted node-full clearfix" id="node-3224">

               <div class="field-items">

                 <p>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <img alt="10 Ways to Deal with Difficult Students" class="alignleft" src="http://cdn.teachhub.com/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/boxing%20gloves.jpg" style="width: 275px; height: 225px; margin-left: 10px; margin-right: 10px; float: left; "/>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  We all have those students who are habitually in trouble or are making trouble. This
                  <a href="http://www.teachhub.com/top-12-classroom-management-dos-and-donts">
                   <strong>
                    classroom management
                   </strong>
                  </a>
                  post is for all the teachers who struggle to reach those kids who snarl when you look at them, recoil when you are near them, and refuse to do what they are asked.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Here are ten
                  <a href="http://www.teachhub.com/essential-classroom-management-checklist">
                   <strong>
                    classroom management
                   </strong>
                  </a>
                  suggestions on how to deal with these difficult students:
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Stay in contact with
                   <a href="http://www.teachhub.com/5-common-fears-teachers-have-about-communicating-parents">
                    parents
                   </a>
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Make sure they know what is going on; how often he is in trouble, and what trouble it was. Create this bond and you wont regret it.
                 </p>

                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Use proximity to limit negative actions
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  When at all possible, place the student nearest you (hard when you are immersed in PBL) or stay within close proximity to him.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Have defined student
                   <a href="http://www.teachhub.com/two-simple-secrets-successful-school-year">
                    expectations
                   </a>
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Use the same steps to get the student on task and behaving EVERY TIMEi.e., this behavior ALWAYS equals this consequence.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Choose the best time to discipline
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  You cant win when you try to call out a student in front of his or her friends. Back off until you talk in private.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Try to empathize with the student
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  I know its hard, because the kid is RUDE, but try to find out what is really going on. Can you get someone to talk to the student? The student might need to take a safe seat to re-group.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Build on common ground
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Reflect on your relationship to see if there is any way you can relate to the student. Does the student play sports? In the Band? Have a sibling? Build on that and see what happens.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Utilize your teaching colleagues
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Talk to your colleagues; has anyone been able to reach this student? If so, set up a meeting with the student and the teacher.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Make class work a non-issue
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  This is HARD, but take the class work out of the equation for a short time and work on the relationshipwork the work back in later.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: rgb(51, 102, 153); ">
                   Try the peer tutor technique
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  Ask yourself- Is the difficult student good in one subject? Can he tutor a fellow student? Can he help someone else succeed? It works BOTH ways.
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  <strong style="color: #336699;">
                   Never give up
                  </strong>
                 </p>
                 <p>
                  No matter the behavior, dont give up on trying to reach that studentthe negativity may be a defense for something deeper.
                  <em>
                   <strong>
                    Keep up the good fight!
