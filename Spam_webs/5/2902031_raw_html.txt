          <img align="Right" class="imagetableright" height="372" hspace="8" src="http://w3.chabad.org/media/images/432/mCdX4321918.jpg" vspace="5" width="250"/>
          <co:body xmlns:co="www1.chabadonline.com/alpha1">
           <div class="co_body">
            <p>
             It is proper to place some money in a charity box before lighting the
             <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="14476" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
              Shabbat
             </span>
             candles.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('1a110394');" name="footnoteRef1a110394">
              1
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             The woman (or man) lights the candle(s). The match should be held to each candle until the flame has taken hold of the majority of the wick which is protruding from the candle.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('2a110394');" name="footnoteRef2a110394">
              2
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             As soon as all the candles are lit, Shabbat has begun for the woman who has kindled them.
            </p>
            <p>
             Therefore:
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              a) She may not extinguish the match. Instead, she should drop it somewhere safe and allow it to go out itself. Some drop the match on to the metal tray upon which the candles are standing, while others set out a small ceramic bowl for this purpose.
              <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('3a110394');" name="footnoteRef3a110394">
               3
              </a>
             </p>
             <p>
              b) She may not move the match box and charity box from the table after she lights the candles. She may ask her husband, or anyone else who has not yet accepted the Shabbat, to remove the box, or she should ensure before she lights that the box is somewhere where it can remain for the entire Shabbat.
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             After the candles are lit, the woman stretches her hands out towards the candles, and moves them inwards in a circular motion
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('4a110394');" name="footnoteRef4a110394">
              4
             </a>
              ushering in a special guest, the Shabbat Queen  three times. After the third time, the woman covers her eyes
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('5a110394');" name="footnoteRef5a110394">
              5
             </a>
             with her hands and recites the following blessing
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('6a110394');" name="footnoteRef6a110394">
              6
             </a>
             :
            </p>
            <blockquote>
             <center>
              <p>
                 - -           
              </p>
              <p>
               Transliteration:
              </p>
              <p>
               Baruch a-ta A-do-nay Elo-hei-nu me-lech ha-o-lam a-sher ki-dee-sha-nu bi-mitz-vo-tav vi-tzi-va-noo li-had-leek
               <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="17659" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
                ner
               </span>
               shel Sha-bat Ko-desh.
              </p>
              <p>
               Translation:
              </p>
              <p>
               Blessed are you, Lrd our
               <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="17588" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
                Gd
               </span>
               , King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments, and commanded us to kindle the light of the Holy Shabbat.
              </p>
             </center>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             The moments immediately after reciting the blessing, while the woman's eyes are still covered, are an extremely auspicious time for her to offer a private prayer for anything her heart desires. It is customary to use this special time to appeal to Gd for children who will glow with the radiance of the
             <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="16295" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
              Torah
             </span>
             , and that He light up the world with the light of the Redemption.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('7a110394');" name="footnoteRef7a110394">
              7
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             After reciting the blessing and uttering her silent prayer, the woman uncovers her eyes and traditionally says "Good
             <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="14477" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
              Shabbos
             </span>
             ," or "Shabbat Shalom," to all who are present.
            </p>
            <p>
             The
             <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="16530" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
              Zohar
             </span>
             emphasizes the importance of lighting the candles with hearty joy and positive feelings.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('8a110394');" name="footnoteRef8a110394">
              8
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             (The
             <a href="/Rebbe">
              Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson
             </a>
             , of righteous memory, explained that this also applies to the spiritual element of candle lighting. We must execute our task of illuminating the world around us with the light of Torah and
             <span class="glossary_item" glossary_item="14536" onclick="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.CompleteShow(this);" onmouseout="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Hide(this);" onmouseover="Co.Tools.Content.Glossary.Show(this);">
              mitzvot
             </span>
             with joy, recognizing that being Gd's "ambassador of light" to this world is a tremendous privilege.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('9a110394');" name="footnoteRef9a110394">
              9
             </a>
             )
            </p>
            <p>
             If one forgot to say the blessing, one may still say the blessing as long as the candles are still lit.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('10a110394');" name="footnoteRef10a110394">
              10
             </a>
            </p>
            <p>
             The one who lit the candles should not eat or drink after candle lighting until the Shabbat meal.
            </p>
            <p>
             The candles, candlesticks, the tray upon which they sit, and the charity box used before the lighting may not be moved or handled until after Shabbatnightfall of Saturday night.
            </p>
            <p>
             Great honor is accorded to the candles, and one is expected to behave respectfully and modestly in their presence. For this reason, it would be improper to, for example, change a diaper in the room where the candles are.
             <a class="footnote_ref" href="javascript:doFootnote('11a110394');" name="footnoteRef11a110394">
              11
             </a>
            </p>
           </div>

            <tr class="footnote" id="footnoteTR6a110394" valign="top">
             <td>
              <a class="footnote_link" href="#footnoteRef6a110394" name="footnote6a110394">
               6.
              </a>
             </td>
             <td>
              <p>
               Generally, blessings are recited before performing a mitzvah. You say the blessing on the matzah before eating it, etc. In the case of lighting the candles, however, the blessing is said after the candles are lit, because once the blessing is said, the woman has begun the mitzvah of lighting the candles and thereby ushers in the Shabbat, and it would now be inappropriate for her to light a candlean act that desecrates the Shabbat.
               <br/>
               So she lights the candles before saying the blessing, while it's still weekday. But, she still wants to fulfill the concept of saying the blessing before the act. How does she manage that? She does so by not completing the mitzvah entirely until after saying the blessing:
               <br/>
               After lighting the candles, she immediately covers her eyes. She then says the blessing and only afterwards uncovers her eyes and enjoys the candlelight. This way, she has fulfilled the concept of saying the blessing before the act, since the mitzvah is not complete until she actually enjoys the light.
               <br/>
               That is the technical reason. Practically, covering the eyes helps one to concentrate better on the blessing and the silent prayers that are said at that time.
              </p>
             </td>
