     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Dells of the Wisconsin River
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Winnebago_language" title="Winnebago language">
       Hoock
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <b>
        Nhakisc
       </b>
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      )  also called the
      <b>
       Wisconsin Dells
      </b>
      (from French
      <i>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dalles" title="wikt:dalles">
        dalles
       </a>
      </i>
      , or narrows)  is
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a 5-mile (8-km)
      <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">
       gorge
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_River" title="Wisconsin River">
       Wisconsin River
      </a>
      in south-central
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">
       Wisconsin
      </a>
      , USA. It is noted for its scenic beauty, in particular for its unique
      <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">
       Cambrian
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">
       sandstone
      </a>
      rock formations and
      <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">
       tributary
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">
       canyons
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The cliffs, some over 100 feet (30m) high, and side canyons are closed to the public to protect sensitive ecological features. The viewing of the rock formations by water is a popular tourist attraction in the area. The nearby city of
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Dells,_Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin Dells, Wisconsin">
       Wisconsin Dells
      </a>
      is the center of summer tourist activity, much of it in the form of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">
       theme parks
      </a>
      unrelated to the river features.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_formation">
       Description and formation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dells_of_the_Wisconsin_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and formation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WisconsinDells02.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="486" data-file-width="648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/WisconsinDells02.jpg/220px-WisconsinDells02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/WisconsinDells02.jpg/330px-WisconsinDells02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/WisconsinDells02.jpg/440px-WisconsinDells02.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WisconsinDells02.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Rock formation on the Wisconsin Dells
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The Dells was formed during the last
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
       ice age
      </a>
      approximately 15,000 years ago, although the rock itself is much older, dating from the
      <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">
       Cambrian
      </a>
      approximately 510-520 million years ago when the area of Wisconsin was at the bottom of a shallow sea.
     </p>
     <p>
      Approximately 19,000 years ago, the Dells was at the extreme eastern margin of the continental
      <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
       glacier
      </a>
      . However, the Dells itself was never covered by glacial ice sheets - it was part of the large
      <a href="/wiki/Driftless_Area" title="Driftless Area">
       Driftless Area
      </a>
      that was bypassed by the ice. The melting of the glacier formed
      <a href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Wisconsin" title="Glacial Lake Wisconsin">
       Glacial Lake Wisconsin
      </a>
      , a lake about the size of
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">
       Great Salt Lake
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">
       Utah
      </a>
      and as deep as 150 feet (45m). The lake was held back by an ice dam of the remaining glacier. The eventual bursting of the ice dam unleashed a catastrophic
      <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">
       flood
      </a>
      , dropping the lake's depth to 50 feet (15m) and cutting deep, narrow gorges and unusual rock formations into the sandstone seen today.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Unique_ecosystem">
       Unique ecosystem
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dells_of_the_Wisconsin_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unique ecosystem">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The area of the Dells provides a mixture of plant communities, including northern and southern
      <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">
       oak
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">
       pine
      </a>
      forests, as well as
      <a href="/wiki/Oak_savanna" title="Oak savanna">
       oak savanna
      </a>
      , moist cliffs, and dry cliffs.
     </p>
     <p>
      The cliffs provide unique niches for plants, some of which are very rare in Wisconsin, including:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Cliff cudweed (
       <i>
        Gnaphalium obtusifolium var saxicola
       </i>
       ), which is known in only two places on Earth - in the Dells and in the Kickapoo Valley, grows on protected rock ledges.
      </li>
      <li>
       Lapland azalea (
       <i>
        Rhododendron lapponicum
       </i>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       Round-stemmed false foxglove (
       <i>
        Agalinis gattingeri
       </i>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maidenhair_spleenwort" title="Maidenhair spleenwort">
        Maidenhair spleenwort
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Asplenium trichomanes
       </i>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       Fragrant fern (
       <i>
        Dryopteris fragrans
       </i>
       ).
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Among the rare animals in the dell are six
      <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">
       dragonfly
      </a>
      species, including the
      <a href="/wiki/Macromia_taeniolata" title="Macromia taeniolata">
       Royal river cruiser
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Macromia taeniolata
      </i>
      ), six rare
      <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">
       mussels
      </a>
      and numerous species of birds.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_history">
       Cultural history
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dells_of_the_Wisconsin_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultural history">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The cultural history of the area stretches back several thousand years, from early
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleo-Indian" title="Paleo-Indian">
       Paleo-Indian
      </a>
      people to the more recent
      <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">
       Native American
      </a>
      peoples, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ho-Chunk" title="Ho-Chunk">
       Ho-Chunk
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sac_(people)" title="Sac (people)">
       Sac
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Menominee" title="Menominee">
       Menominee
      </a>
      , who left behind effigy and burial mounds, camps and village sites, garden beds, and rock art.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Dells were made famous in 1886 by the
      <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">
       photographer
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/H._H._Bennett" title="H. H. Bennett">
       H. H. Bennett
      </a>
      , who took the first stop-action photo of his son jumping onto Stand Rock.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_status">
       Protected status
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dells_of_the_Wisconsin_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protected status">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The area is now owned by the
      <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Department_of_Natural_Resources" title="Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources">
       Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources
      </a>
      and was designated a State Natural Area in 1994.
     </p>
