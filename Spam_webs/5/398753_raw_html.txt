        <p>
         A recent afternoon in the woods teaching backwoods skills to Cubs reminded me of the importance of my cuts kit!
        </p>
        <p>
         I thought it would be a good idea to share the concept with other outdoors people and leaders.
        </p>
        <p>
         It might also serve as a reminder to those who may have heard about the concept before.
        </p>
        <p>
         The concept of a cuts kit is simple, something small and simple that you keep on your person to deal with the common minor injuries that we and others incur in the woods. Teaching people outdoor living skills and, in particular, to use cutting tools will, from time-to-time, result in injuries.  So its wise to have something close at hand to deal with this.
        </p>
        <p>
         A cuts kit is not a replacement for our main first aid kit, its complimentary to it. The important thing is to keep the cuts kit small enough so it can slip into your pocket and not be a hassle to carry. Mine goes into a thigh pocket on my outdoor trousers and I forget its there until I need it.
        </p>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_3909" style="width: 665px">
         <a href="http://cdn.frontierbushcraft.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/Cuts-kit-contents.jpg">
          <img alt="The contents of the cuts kit" class="size-full wp-image-3909" height="491" src="http://cdn.frontierbushcraft.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/Cuts-kit-contents.jpg" title="Cuts kit contents" width="655"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          The main components of the authors cuts kit. Photo: Barry Smith.
         </p>
        </div>
        <h4>
         So whats in my cuts kit?
        </h4>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Nitrile gloves  2 pairs;
         </li>
         <li>
          Aide memoire of emergency medical actions;
         </li>
         <li>
          An Aloksak (tough waterproof bag);
         </li>
         <li>
          Finger dressings;
         </li>
         <li>
          Plasters in assorted sizes;
         </li>
         <li>
          Antiseptic wipes;
         </li>
         <li>
          Steri-strips;
         </li>
         <li>
          Gauze swabs;
         </li>
         <li>
          Matches;
         </li>
         <li>
          Celox (Blood clotting agent);
         </li>
         <li>
          Resuscitation face shield;
         </li>
        </ul>
        <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_3910" style="width: 665px">
         <a href="http://cdn.frontierbushcraft.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/Aloksak-contents.jpg">
          <img alt="Contents of the cuts kit that are waterproofed with an Aloksak" class="size-full wp-image-3910" height="526" src="http://cdn.frontierbushcraft.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/11/Aloksak-contents.jpg" title="Aloksak contents" width="655"/>
         </a>
         <p class="wp-caption-text">
          The Aloksak keeps these items waterproofed.  Photo: Barry Smith
         </p>
        </div>
        <p>
         The Celox is for dealing with more serious cuts and as I havent been trained to use it on others I reserve it for use on me only.
        </p>
        <p>
         The last item is clearly not for cuts but I like to keep the face shield in my cuts kit so its always close to hand. Hopefully Ill never need it but I know where it is if I do.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Do you carry a cuts kit? If so, leave a comment below to let us and other readers know what you include in yours
         </strong>
        </p>

            <p>
             Hi Olly,
            </p>
            <p>
             Its good to hear from you.  Thanks for your comment.
            </p>
            <p>
             Spray-on plasters can be useful. I dont think they are any more or less applicable for someone practising bushcraft skills than any other outdoor skill.  I guess it just comes down to whether you like aerosols full stop.
            </p>
            <p>
             As you say they can stay on for a number of days and  when you have suffered one cheap plaster coming off after another while outdoors  then you can appreciate the attraction of this feature.
            </p>
            <p>
             I have two caveats though:
             <br/>
             1/ sealing cuts (i.e. not allowing them to breathe) is not always a good thing. There is also the possibility of sealing-in contaminants;
             <br/>
             2/ putting antiseptics directly onto a cut can hinder the healing process as some antiseptics damage your cells;
