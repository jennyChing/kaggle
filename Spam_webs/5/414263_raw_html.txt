     <p>
      An
      <b>
       abugida
      </b>
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
          <span title="// secondary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'father'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
           b
          </span>
          <span title="/u/ long 'oo' in 'food'">
           u
          </span>
          <span title="// primary stress follows">
           
          </span>
          <span title="'g' in 'guy'">
           
          </span>
          <span title="/i/ long 'e' in 'bead'">
           i
          </span>
          <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
           d
          </span>
          <span title="// 'a' in 'about'">
           
          </span>
         </span>
         /
        </a>
       </span>
      </span>
      (from
      <a href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_language" title="Ge'ez language">
       Ge'ez
      </a>
      
      <i>
       bugida
      </i>
      ), also called an
      <b>
       alphasyllabary
      </b>
      , is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writing_systems#Segmental_writing_system" title="Writing systems">
       segmental writing system
      </a>
      in which consonantvowel sequences are written as a unit: each unit is based on a
      <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
       consonant
      </a>
      letter, and
      <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
       vowel
      </a>
      notation is secondary. This contrasts with a full
      <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
       alphabet
      </a>
      , in which vowels have status equal to consonants, and with an
      <a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">
       abjad
      </a>
      , in which vowel marking is absent or optional. (In less formal contexts, all three types of script may be termed alphabets.) Abugidas include the extensive
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmic_family_of_scripts" title="Brahmic family of scripts">
       Brahmic family of scripts
      </a>
      of South and Southeast Asia.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Abugida
      </i>
      as a term in linguistics was proposed by
      <a href="/wiki/Peter_T._Daniels" title="Peter T. Daniels">
       Peter T. Daniels
      </a>
      in his 1990
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_typology" title="Linguistic typology">
       typology
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">
       writing systems
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       Abugida
      </i>
      is an Ethiopian name for the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_alphabet" title="Ge'ez alphabet">
       Geez script
      </a>
      , taken from four letters of that script,
      <i>
       ' bu gi da
      </i>
      , in much the same way that
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abecedary" title="Abecedary">
        abecedary
       </a>
      </i>
      is derived from Latin
      <i>
       a be ce de
      </i>
      , and
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabet
       </a>
      </i>
      is derived from the names of the two first letters in the
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
       Greek alphabet
      </a>
      ,
      <i>
       alpha
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       beta
      </i>
      . As Daniels used the word, an abugida is in contrast with a
      <a href="/wiki/Syllabary" title="Syllabary">
       syllabary
      </a>
      , where letters with shared consonants or vowels show no particular resemblance to one another, and also with an
      <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
       alphabet
      </a>
      proper, where independent letters are used to denote both consonants and vowels. The term
      <i>
       alphasyllabary
      </i>
      was suggested for the Indic scripts in 1997 by
      <a href="/wiki/William_Bright" title="William Bright">
       William Bright
      </a>
      , following South Asian linguistic usage, to convey the idea that "they share features of both alphabet and syllabary."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bright-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Abugidas were long considered to be syllabaries, or intermediate between syllabaries and alphabets, and the term
      <i>
       syllabics
      </i>
      is retained in the name of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Aboriginal_Syllabics" title="Canadian Aboriginal Syllabics">
       Canadian Aboriginal Syllabics
      </a>
      . Other terms that have been used include
      <i>
       neosyllabary
      </i>
      (
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James-Germain_F%C3%A9vrier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James-Germain Fvrier (page does not exist)">
       Fvrier
      </a>
      1959),
      <i>
       pseudo-alphabet
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Fred_Householder" title="Fred Householder">
       Householder
      </a>
      1959),
      <i>
       semisyllabary
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/David_Diringer" title="David Diringer">
       Diringer
      </a>
      1968; a word that has
      <a href="/wiki/Semi-syllabary" title="Semi-syllabary">
       other uses
      </a>
      ) and
      <i>
       syllabic alphabet
      </i>
      (
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Florian_Coulmas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Florian Coulmas (page does not exist)">
       Coulmas
      </a>
      1996; this term is also a synonym for
      <a href="/wiki/Syllabary" title="Syllabary">
       syllabary
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Bright-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
       Description
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In general, a letter of an abugida transcribes a consonant. Letters are written as a linear sequence, in most cases left to right. Vowels are written through modification of these consonantal letters, either by means of
      <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
       diacritics
      </a>
      (which may or may not follow the direction of writing the letters), or by changes in the form of the letter itself.
     </p>
     <p>
      Vowels not preceded by a consonant may be represented with a
      <a href="/wiki/Zero_consonant" title="Zero consonant">
       zero consonant
      </a>
      letter, modified to indicate the vowel, or separate letters for each vowel, that are distinct from the corresponding dependent vowel signs. Consonants not followed by a vowel may be represented with:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       a modification that explicitly indicates the lack of a vowel (
       <a href="/wiki/Virama" title="Virama">
        virama
       </a>
       ),
      </li>
      <li>
       a lack of vowel marking (often with ambiguity between no vowel and a default
       <a href="/wiki/Inherent_vowel" title="Inherent vowel">
        inherent vowel
       </a>
       ),
      </li>
      <li>
       vowel marking for a short or neutral vowel such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">
         schwa
        </a>
       </i>
       (with ambiguity between no vowel and that short or neutral vowel),
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">
        conjunct consonant
       </a>
       letters, where two or more letters are graphically joined in a
       <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">
        ligature
       </a>
       , or
      </li>
      <li>
       dependent consonant signs, which may be smaller or differently placed versions of the full consonant letters, or may be distinct signs altogether. When a dependent consonant is written beneath the preceding consonant, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Burmese_alphabet" title="Burmese alphabet">
        Burmese
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Khmer_alphabet" title="Khmer alphabet">
        Khmer
       </a>
       , the consonants are said to be "stacked".
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      There are three principal families of abugidas, depending on whether vowels are indicated by modifying consonants by
      <i>
       diacritics, distortion,
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       orientation.
      </i>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The oldest and largest is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmic_family" title="Brahmic family">
        Brahmic family
       </a>
       of India and Southeast Asia, in which vowels are marked with
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        diacritics
       </a>
       and syllable-final consonants, when they occur, are indicated with
       <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">
        ligatures
       </a>
       , diacritics, or with a special
       <a href="/wiki/Virama" title="Virama">
        vowel-canceling mark
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_alphabet" title="Ge'ez alphabet">
        Ethiopic family
       </a>
       , vowels are marked by modifying the shapes of the consonants, and one of the vowel-forms serves additionally to indicate final consonants.
      </li>
      <li>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Aboriginal_syllabics" title="Canadian Aboriginal syllabics">
        Cree family
       </a>
       , vowels are marked by rotating or flipping the consonants, and final consonants are indicated with either special diacritics or superscript forms of the main initial consonants.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C4%81na" title="Tna">
       Tna
      </a>
      of the
      <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">
       Maldives
      </a>
      has dependent vowels and a zero vowel sign, but no inherent vowel.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Indic_.28Brahmic.29">
       Indic (Brahmic)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Indic (Brahmic)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmic_family_of_scripts" title="Brahmic family of scripts">
       Brahmic family of scripts
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Indic scripts originated in
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      and spread to
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      . All surviving Indic scripts are descendants of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmi_alphabet" title="Brahmi alphabet">
       Brahmi alphabet
      </a>
      . Today they are used in most languages of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
       South Asia
      </a>
      (although replaced by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perso-Arabic" title="Perso-Arabic">
       Perso-Arabic
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">
       Urdu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Kashmiri_language" title="Kashmiri language">
       Kashmiri
      </a>
      and some other languages of
      <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
       Pakistan
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
       India
      </a>
      ) and mainland
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">
       Burma
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">
       Thailand
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">
       Laos
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">
       Cambodia
      </a>
      ; but not
      <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
       Malaysia
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">
       Vietnam
      </a>
      ). The primary division is into North Indic scripts used in Northern India, Nepal, Tibet and Bhutan, and Southern Indic scripts used in
      <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">
       South India
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">
       Sri Lanka
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      . South Indic letter forms are very rounded; North Indic less so, though
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriya_script" title="Oriya script">
       Oriya
      </a>
      , Golmol and Litumol of Nepal script are rounded.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most North Indic scripts' full letters incorporate a horizontal line at the top, with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gujarati_script" title="Gujarati script">
       Gujarati
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriya_script" title="Oriya script">
       Oriya script
      </a>
      as exceptions; South Indic scripts do not.
     </p>
     <p>
      Indic scripts indicate vowels through dependent vowel signs (diacritics) around the consonants, often including a sign that explicitly indicates the lack of a vowel. If a consonant has no vowel sign, this indicates a default vowel. Vowel diacritics may appear above, below, to the left, to the right, or around the consonant.
     </p>
     <p>
      The most widely used Indic script is
      <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">
       Devanagari
      </a>
      , shared by
      <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
       Hindi
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhojpuri" title="Bhojpuri">
       Bhojpuri
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">
       Marathi
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Konkani_language" title="Konkani language">
       Konkani
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nepali_language" title="Nepali language">
       Nepali
      </a>
      , and often
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      . A basic letter such as
      <a href="/wiki/Devanagari_ka" title="Devanagari ka">
       
      </a>
      in Hindi represents a syllable with the default vowel, in this case
      <i>
       ka
      </i>
      (
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [k]
      </span>
      ). In some languages, including Hindi, it becomes a final closing consonant at the end of a word, in this case
      <i>
       k
      </i>
      . The inherent vowel may be changed by adding vowel mark (
      <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
       diacritics
      </a>
      ), producing syllables such as 
      <i>
       ki,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       ku,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       ke,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       ko.
      </i>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">
       mora
      </a>
      a consonant letter represents, either with or without a marked vowel, is called an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Shiksha" title="Shiksha">
        akshara
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      In many of the Brahmic scripts, a syllable beginning with a cluster is treated as a single character for purposes of vowel marking, so a vowel marker like
      <span class="Unicode">
       
      </span>
      <i>
       -i,
      </i>
      falling before the character it modifies, may appear several positions before the place where it is pronounced. For example, the game
      <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">
       cricket
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
       Hindi
      </a>
      is 
      <i>
       krike;
      </i>
      the diacritic for
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /i/
      </span>
      appears before the
      <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">
       consonant cluster
      </a>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /kr/
      </span>
      , not before the
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /r/
      </span>
      . A more unusual example is seen in the
      <a href="/wiki/Batak_alphabet" title="Batak alphabet">
       Batak alphabet
      </a>
      : Here the syllable
      <i>
       bim
      </i>
      is written
      <i>
       ba-ma-i-(virama)
      </i>
      . That is, the vowel diacritic and virama are both written after the consonants for the whole syllable.
     </p>
     <p>
      In many abugidas, there is also a diacritic to suppress the inherent vowel, yielding the bare consonant. In
      <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">
       Devanagari
      </a>
      ,  is
      <i>
       k,
      </i>
      and  is
      <i>
       l
      </i>
      . This is called the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vir%C4%81ma" title="Virma">
        virma
       </a>
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       halantam
      </i>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
       Sanskrit
      </a>
      . It may be used to form
      <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">
       consonant clusters
      </a>
      , or to indicate that a consonant occurs at the end of a word. Thus in Sanskrit, a default vowel consonant such as  does not take on a final consonant sound. Instead, it keeps its vowel. For writing two consonants without a vowel in between, instead of using diacritics on the first consonant to remove its vowel, another popular method of special
      <a href="/wiki/Conjunct" title="Conjunct">
       conjunct
      </a>
      forms is used in which two or more consonant characters are merged to express a cluster, such as Devanagari: 
      <i>
       kla.
      </i>
      (Note that some fonts display this as  followed by , rather than forming a conjunct. This expedient is used by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISCII" title="ISCII">
       ISCII
      </a>
      and South Asian scripts of
      <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
       Unicode
      </a>
      .) Thus a closed syllable such as
      <i>
       kal
      </i>
      requires two
      <i>
       aksharas
      </i>
      to write.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%B3ng" title="Rng">
       Rng
      </a>
      script used for the
      <a href="/wiki/Lepcha_language" title="Lepcha language">
       Lepcha language
      </a>
      goes further than other Indic abugidas, in that a single
      <i>
       akshara
      </i>
      can represent a closed syllable: Not only the vowel, but any final consonant is indicated by a diacritic. For example, the syllable [sok] would be written as something like
      <span class="Unicode">
       s
      </span>
      , here with an underring representing
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /o/
      </span>
      and an overcross representing the diacritic for final
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /k/
      </span>
      . Most other Indic abugidas can only indicate a very limited set of final consonants with diacritics, such as
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       //
      </span>
      or
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /r/
      </span>
      , if they can indicate any at all.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ethiopic">
       Ethiopic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ethiopic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ethiopic_genesis.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="519" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Ethiopic_genesis.jpg/220px-Ethiopic_genesis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Ethiopic_genesis.jpg/330px-Ethiopic_genesis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Ethiopic_genesis.jpg/440px-Ethiopic_genesis.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ethiopic_genesis.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_alphabet" title="Ge'ez alphabet">
         Ge'ez script
        </a>
        , an abugida of Eritrea and Ethiopia
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopic" title="Ethiopic">
       Ethiopic
      </a>
      , where the term
      <i>
       abugida
      </i>
      originates, the diacritics have been fused to the consonants to the point that they must be considered modifications of the form of the letters. Children learn each modification separately, as in a syllabary; nonetheless, the graphic similarities between syllables with the same consonant is readily apparent, unlike the case in a true syllabary.
     </p>
     <p>
      Though now an abugida, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ge%27ez_alphabet" title="Ge'ez alphabet">
       Ge'ez alphabet
      </a>
      , until the advent of Christianity (
      <i>
       ca.
      </i>
      AD 350), had originally been what would now be termed an
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">
        abjad
       </a>
      </i>
      . In the Ge'ez abugida (or
      <i>
       fidel
      </i>
      ), the base form of the letter (also known as
      <i>
       fidel
      </i>
      ) may be altered. For example, 
      <i>
       h
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [h]
      </span>
      (base form), 
      <i>
       hu
      </i>
      (with a right-side diacritic that doesn't alter the letter), 
      <i>
       hi
      </i>
      (with a subdiacritic that compresses the consonant, so it is the same height), 
      <i>
       h
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [h]
      </span>
      or
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       [h]
      </span>
      (where the letter is modified with a kink in the left arm).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_Aboriginal_syllabics">
       Canadian Aboriginal syllabics
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canadian Aboriginal syllabics">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the family known as
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Aboriginal_syllabics" title="Canadian Aboriginal syllabics">
       Canadian Aboriginal syllabics
      </a>
      , which was inspired by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devanagari_script" title="Devanagari script">
       Devanagari script
      </a>
      of India, vowels are indicated by changing the orientation of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllabogram" title="Syllabogram">
       syllabogram
      </a>
      . Each vowel has a consistent orientation; for example,
      <a href="/wiki/Inuktitut" title="Inuktitut">
       Inuktitut
      </a>
      
      <i>
       pi,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       pu,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       pa;
      </i>
      
      <i>
       ti,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       tu,
      </i>
      
      <i>
       ta
      </i>
      . Although there is a vowel inherent in each, all rotations have equal status and none can be identified as basic. Bare consonants are indicated either by separate diacritics, or by superscript versions of the
      <i>
       aksharas
      </i>
      ; there is no vowel-killer mark.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Borderline_cases">
       Borderline cases
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Borderline cases">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowelled_abjads">
       Vowelled abjads
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vowelled abjads">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Consonantal scripts ("
      <a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">
       abjads
      </a>
      ") are normally written without indication of many vowels. However, in some contexts like teaching materials or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scripture" title="Scripture">
       scriptures
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
       Arabic
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
       Hebrew
      </a>
      are written with full indication of vowels via diacritic marks (
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harakat" title="Harakat">
        harakat
       </a>
      </i>
      ,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Niqqud" title="Niqqud">
        niqqud
       </a>
      </i>
      ) making them effectively abugidas. The Brahmic and Ethiopic families are thought to have originated from the Semitic abjads by the addition of vowel marks.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Arabic scripts used for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sorani_Kurdish" title="Sorani Kurdish">
       Kurdish
      </a>
      in Iraq and for
      <a href="/wiki/Uyghur_language" title="Uyghur language">
       Uyghur
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">
       Xinjiang
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
       China
      </a>
      , as well as the Hebrew script of
      <a href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">
       Yiddish
      </a>
      , are fully vowelled, but because the vowels are written with full letters rather than diacritics and there are no inherent vowels, these are considered alphabets, not abugidas.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Phagspa">
       Phagspa
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Phagspa">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The imperial Mongol script called
      <a href="/wiki/%27Phags-pa_script" title="'Phags-pa script">
       Phagspa
      </a>
      was derived from the Tibetan abugida, but all vowels are written in-line rather than as diacritics. However, it retains the features of having an inherent vowel /a/ and having distinct initial vowel letters.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pahawh">
       Pahawh
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pahawh">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Pahawh_Hmong" title="Pahawh Hmong">
       Pahawh Hmong
      </a>
      is a non-segmental script that indicates
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable_onset" title="Syllable onset">
       syllable onsets
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable_rime" title="Syllable rime">
       rimes
      </a>
      , such as consonant clusters and vowels with final consonants. Thus it is not segmental and cannot be considered an abugida. However, it superficially resembles an abugida with the roles of consonant and vowel reversed. Most syllables are written with two letters in the order rimeonset (typically vowel-consonant), even though they are pronounced as onset-rime (consonant-vowel), rather like the position of the
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /i/
      </span>
      vowel in Devanagari, which is written before the consonant. Pahawh is also unusual in that, while an inherent rime
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /u/
      </span>
      (with mid tone) is unwritten, it also has an inherent onset
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /k/
      </span>
      . For the syllable
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /kau/
      </span>
      , which requires one or the other of the inherent sounds to be overt, it is
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       /au/
      </span>
      that is written. Thus it is the rime (vowel) that is basic to the system.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Meroitic">
       Meroitic
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Meroitic">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      It is difficult to draw a dividing line between abugidas and other
      <a href="/wiki/Segment_(linguistics)" title="Segment (linguistics)">
       segmental
      </a>
      scripts. For example, the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meroitic_script" title="Meroitic script">
       Meroitic script
      </a>
      of ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">
       Sudan
      </a>
      did not indicate an inherent
      <i>
       a
      </i>
      (one symbol stood for both
      <i>
       m
      </i>
      and
      <i>
       ma,
      </i>
      for example), and is thus similar to Brahmic family of abugidas. However, the other vowels were indicated with full letters, not diacritics or modification, so the system was essentially an alphabet that did not bother to write the most common vowel.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Shorthand">
       Shorthand
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Shorthand">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Several systems of
      <a href="/wiki/Shorthand" title="Shorthand">
       shorthand
      </a>
      use diacritics for vowels, but they do not have an inherent vowel, and are thus more similar to
      <a href="/wiki/Thaana" title="Thaana">
       Thaana
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurdish_alphabet" title="Kurdish alphabet">
       Kurdish script
      </a>
      than to the Brahmic scripts. The
      <a href="/wiki/Gabelsberger_shorthand" title="Gabelsberger shorthand">
       Gabelsberger shorthand
      </a>
      system and its derivatives modify the
      <i>
       following
      </i>
      consonant to represent vowels. The
      <a href="/wiki/Pollard_script" title="Pollard script">
       Pollard script
      </a>
      , which was based on shorthand, also uses diacritics for vowels; the placements of the vowel relative to the consonant indicates
      <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
       tone
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Pitman_shorthand" title="Pitman shorthand">
       Pitman shorthand
      </a>
      uses straight strokes and quarter-circle marks in different orientations as the principal "alphabet" of consonants; vowels are shown as light and heavy dots, dashes and other marks in one of 3 possible positions to indicate the various vowel-sounds. However, to increase writing speed, Pitman has rules for
      <a class="external text" href="http://pitmanshorthand.homestead.com" rel="nofollow">
       "vowel indication"
      </a>
      using the positioning or choice of consonant signs so that writing vowel-marks can be dispensed with.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
       Development
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abugida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Development">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As the term
      <i>
       alphasyllabary
      </i>
      suggests, abugidas have been considered an intermediate step between
      <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
       alphabets
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Syllabary" title="Syllabary">
       syllabaries
      </a>
      . Historically, abugidas appear to have evolved from
      <a href="/wiki/Abjad" title="Abjad">
       abjads
      </a>
      (vowelless alphabets). They contrast with syllabaries, where there is a distinct symbol for each syllable or consonant-vowel combination, and where these have no systematic similarity to each other, and typically develop directly from
      <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">
       logographic scripts
      </a>
      . Compare the Devanagari examples above to sets of syllables in the Japanese
      <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">
       hiragana
      </a>
      syllabary: 
      <i>
       ka
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ki
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ku
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ke
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ko
      </i>
      have nothing in common to indicate
      <i>
       k;
      </i>
      while 
      <i>
       ra
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ri
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ru
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       re
      </i>
      , 
      <i>
       ro
      </i>
      have neither anything in common for
      <i>
       r
      </i>
      , nor anything to indicate that they have the same vowels as the
      <i>
       k
      </i>
      set.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most Indian and Indochinese abugidas appear to have first been developed from abjads with the
      <span class="Unicode">
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kharo%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADh%C4%AB" title="Kharoh">
        Kharoh
       </a>
      </span>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hm%C4%AB_script" title="Brhm script">
       Brhm scripts
      </a>
      ; the abjad in question is usually considered to be the
      <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet" title="Aramaic alphabet">
       Aramaic
      </a>
      one, but while the link between Aramaic and Kharosthi is more or less undisputed, this is not the case with Brahmi. The Kharosthi family does not survive today, but Brahmi's descendants include most of the modern scripts of
      <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
       South
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      . Ge'ez derived from a different abjad, the
      <a href="/wiki/Sabaean_language#Script" title="Sabaean language">
       Sabean script
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">
       Yemen
      </a>
      ; the advent of vowels coincided with the introduction of Christianity about AD 350;.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       <i>
        "
        <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
         Oiy
        </span>
        is encumbered with the drawback of an excessively awkward and cumbrous written character.... At first glance, an
        <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">
         Oiy
        </span>
        book seems to be all curves, and it takes a second look to notice that there is something inside each."
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/George_Abraham_Grierson" title="George Abraham Grierson">
        G. A. Grierson
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Linguistic Survey of India
       </i>
       , 1903)
      </p>

      <li>
