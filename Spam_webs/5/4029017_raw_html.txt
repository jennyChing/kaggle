      <h1 class="entry-title">
       Appeals court chilly to feds' arguments for NSA surveillance program
      </h1>
      <div class="story-toolbar">
       <ul class="toolbar-main">
        <li class="social-sharing">

      <div class="byline">
       By
       <a href="http://www.politico.com/reporters/JoshGerstein.html" rel="nofollow">
        JOSH GERSTEIN
       </a>
       |
       <h5 class="blog-date">
        <abbr class="published" title="2014-09-02T07:52">
         9/2/14 7:52 PM EDT
        </abbr>
       </h5>
      </div>
      <div class="entry-content p-content">
       <p>
        The first federal appeals court to hear a challenge to the National Security Agency's broad collection of data on Americans' telephone calls since the program was publicly revealed last year gave a surprisingly chilly reception Tuesday to the government's arguments for the legality of the surveillance.
       </p>
       <p>
        During an oral argument session that stretched for nearly one hour and 45 minutes, none of the three judges assigned to the appeal brought by the American Civil Liberties Union seemed to be squarely behind the Justice Department's claim that a 1979 Supreme Court precedent gave the government the legal running room to collect vast databases of information on phone calls as part of a program aimed at tracking calls that might be related to terrorism.
       </p>
       <p>
        The issue seems all but certain to reach the Supreme Court again soon, but a ruling against the government by the 2nd Circuit  which seemed a live possibility based on Tuesday's arguments in New York City  would deal a blow to those defending the legality of the current metadata collection program. Those defenders are fond of noting that only a single district court judge has ruled against the program, finding it likely unconstitutional, while more than a dozen have approved it either by rejecting lawsuits challenging it or by granting government applications to continue the surveillance.
       </p>
       <p>
        A decision against the government from the 2nd Circuit would alter that tally in a way that could give significant momentum to those arguing the program is unlawful.
       </p>
       <p>
        A particular concern raised by the judges Tuesday was that the government's rationale for bulk collection of so-called metadata on phone calls could apply to a slew of other records businesses possess, like bank and credit card records, which can also contain information useful for detecting potential terrorist plotting.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The same third-party argument that you're making as a matter of constitutional law and the same relevance argument that you're making under the statute apply to banking records and credit card records, don't they?" 2nd Circuit U.S. Court of Appeals Judge Gerard Lynch asked Justice Department Civil Division Chief Stuart Delery. "Isn't it at least as relevant to you whether somebody that you have some reasonable suspicion is engaged in terrorist connections used his credit card last week to buy a ton of fertilizer as it is to find out whether he called his gym?"
       </p>
       <p>
        District Court Judge Vernon Broderick, sitting on the 2nd Circuit case through a program that eases the appellate court's workload, also appeared troubled by the breadth of the governent's argument that it can obtain in bulk through the PATRIOT Act's Section 215 all records that it could get for specific individuals in a more run-of-the-mill criminal investigation by using a grand jury subpoena.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Couldn't then the government aggregate everybody's bank records and apply the same sort of query methodology to get at the same sort of linkages?" asked Broderick, an Obama appointee.
       </p>
       <p>
        Delery didn't rule that out. (Indeed,
        <a href="http://online.wsj.com/news/articles/SB10001424052702303559504579198370113163530">
         it's been reported
        </a>
        that the government uses 215 to monitor money transfers.) However, he said that question was better saved for a case where the government was going after banking records and not the telephone records at issue.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Other types of data, even if very valuable for law enforcement and used routinely, may not have the same benefits from aggregation
        <em>
         ex ante
        </em>
        and querying based on standardized formats. The answer here would not necessarily be the same. It's a question," Delery insisted.
       </p>
       <p>
        Lynch, for one, wasn't persuaded. "I just don't understand an argument about what's so different about telephone records that makes them so valuable," he said.
       </p>
       <p>
        The judges hearing the appeal also seemed open to the ACLU's arguments that the bulk collection violates the letter of the law repeatedly used to obtain authorization for the program from another court, the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court. The jurists also didn't seem to think much of the government's arguments that Congress ratified the bulk collection program by twice renewing the statutory provision relied on to pursue the surveillance.
       </p>
       <p>
        "I wonder how valid that ratification argument is when you're dealing with ... what is essentially secret law," said 2nd Circuit Judge Robert Sack, a Clinton appointee. "I thought the ratification notion is you're dealing with something that's public and that by ratifying it again and again you're somehow reflecting the public will because they know about it."
       </p>
       <p>
        Lynch also speculated publicly about whether the appeals court should try to force Congress to clarify its stance on the issue. Differing legislation to alter the program has passed by the House and Senate, with its prospect for passage this session unclear.
       </p>
       <p>
        "This I suppose is not a consideration that's entirely appropriate for a court, but if this court said, 'We don't think this is authorized by Congress,' that would kind of put it to them to actually act on whether they think this is something that should be done or not." Then "they could take their vote," Lynch said, while acknowledging that might not settle the constitutional issue of whether the bulk surveillance violates the Fourth Amendment.
       </p>
       <p>
        Broderick also seemed trouble by the breadth of the ruling his Southern District of New York colleague Judge William Pauley issued last year upholding the bulk metadata program. Broderick noted that Pauley seemed to accept that present-day metadata collection was identical to that of a 1979 pen register, except for the scale.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Judge Pauley didn't go each piece of metadata by metadata and do the analysis that was done in Smith [v. Maryland]. ... He said it's third-party and it's like, similar type information but doesn't it require that actually he  or should it require that actually determination is made based upon if you're talking about a right to privacy. ... why shouldn't the court have to go through that exercise?" Broderick said.
       </p>
       <p>
        When Delery insisted "the same type of call data records" are at issue now, Sack chimed in with another skeptical comment.
       </p>
       <p>
        "The question is whether the technology hasn't changed so much that the analysis that it's just a pen register doesn't work any more," Sack said.
       </p>
       <p>
        President Barack Obama has called for the program to be changed so that phone companies store the data instead, with the government querying when needed. The ACLU's Alexander Abdo said that amounted to a concession that the current structure of the program was unreasonable because it amounted to an unnecessary intrusion on privacy.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Even the president has conceded that the government's interests can be accomplished in [more] narrow means," Abdo said.
       </p>
       <p>
        While the judges seemed very receptive to the ACLU's arguments, no decision was rendered Tuesday, and judges sometimes use oral arguments to test out devil's advocate arguments they don't agree with. And there were moments where some of the judges sounded more sympathetic to the government.
       </p>
       <p>
        Lynch, an Obama appointee, did suggest that individuals might have less privacy interest in the numbers they call if phone companies can use those numbers however they wish. It was not clearly established at the arguments Tuesday what legal limits apply to other uses than sharing the numbers with the government.
       </p>
       <p>
        "Verizon has all of this information and presumably has the computer capacity to probe them if it chose," Lynch noted.
       </p>
       <p>
        However, he seemed not totally satisfied with the government's promise that various safeguards were in place to restrict use of the metadata.
       </p>
       <p>
        "We don't know what happens when some inhabitant of the White House, this one or another one, has some plumbers' unit and decides to let them have access to the data. These are realistic concerns about having this massive body of data," Lynch said.
       </p>
       <p>
        Tuesday's argument session was webcast live by C-SPAN and can be viewed
        <a href="http://www.c-span.org/video/?321163-1/aclu-v-clapper-oral-argument-phone-record-surveillance">
         here
        </a>
        .
       </p>
