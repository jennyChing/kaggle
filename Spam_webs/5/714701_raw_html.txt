        <h1 class="entry-title" itemprop="headline">
         The Value of a Quarter
        </h1>
        <p class="entry-meta">
         <time class="entry-time" datetime="2015-02-20T00:29:51+00:00" itemprop="datePublished">
          February 20, 2015
         </time>
         By
         <span class="entry-author" itemprop="author" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
          <a class="entry-author-link" href="http://letmestartbysayingblog.com/author/calicohangham12/" itemprop="url" rel="author">
           <span class="entry-author-name" itemprop="name">
            Kim Bongiorno at LetMeStartBySaying
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
         <span class="entry-comments-link">
          <a href="http://letmestartbysayingblog.com/2015/02/20/lemonade-stand/#comments">
           4 Comments
          </a>
         </span>
        </p>
       </header>
       <div class="entry-content" itemprop="text">
        <p>
         Summer break was barely a coupleweeks along, but she had been begging for permissionto run a lemonade stand since the bell rang on the last day of school. I agreed toSaturday afternoon, if it was sunnynot stiflingout.
        </p>
        <p>
         The day came and I followed through on my promise, despite not really being up for the labor, the mess, the hours of sitting in a tiny blue chair. When I told her it was time, she clapped, squealed, and put on a fluttery pink party dress covered in butterflies. Stirredthe lemonade mix to perfection. Slipped yellow slices of
         <em>
          real
         </em>
         lemon into the pitcher for authenticity. Plunked ice cubes in
         <em>
          plip-plop-plip
         </em>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         The table set, she unrolled a sign stating, Lemonade 25 in the neatest 6-year-old handwriting I had ever seen, and we were ready to go.
        </p>
        <p>
         She whispered taglines into my ear that she wanted us to chant together once the cars started rolling by:
        </p>
        <p>
         Lemonade Stand! Lemonade Stand! Come-n-getchya lemonaaaaade here! We were to say these things with a smile and a wave, of course.
        </p>
        <p>
         She stacked the cups into four even piles, asked me to make sure the sign by the curbdidnt topple over again.
        </p>
        <p>
         She had to sit on her hands, she was so excited to begin her career as lemonade saleslady.
        </p>
        <p>
         Looking left.
        </p>
        <p>
         Looking right.
        </p>
        <p>
         Waiting for the distant rumble of an approaching car.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://letmestartbysayingblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/The-Value-of-a-Quarter-by-Kim-Bongiorno.png">
          <img alt="The Value of a Quarter by Kim Bongiorno" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-84345" height="314" src="http://letmestartbysayingblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/The-Value-of-a-Quarter-by-Kim-Bongiorno.png" width="600"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Each time one headed our way, she leapt up and waved, a grinbursting with enthusiasm. I caught splashes of juice in my lap more than once.
        </p>
        <p>
         Our first customer stopped by the curb: a friend I had texted to swing by. Sold! To the family in the minivan en route to softball!
        </p>
        <p>
         The next was a dad from the neighborhood who drove past us at first, then pulled a u-turn and saunteredover with a grin he tried to hide.
        </p>
        <p>
         What do you have here? he asked her, furrowing his brow in serious interest.
        </p>
        <p>
         Ice cold lemonade! She poured him a tall frosty cup and said, Twenty-five cents, please!
        </p>
        <p>
         In her hand he pressed seventy-five and said, Keep the change for a job well done.
        </p>
        <p>
         Tears surprised me, burning my eyes behind sunglasses. I choked out a Thank you as she stared at the cool, sparkling coins like unexpected treasures.
        </p>
        <p>
         Car after car pulled over, usually because she stood and waved with an adorably toothy smile. Sometimes because she stood precariously close to the road to get their attention.
        </p>
        <p>
         When they didnt stop, when they looked straight ahead, ignoring my determined daughters twinkling gaze and frenetic cup waving, I wondered,
         <em>
          Who the HELL do you think you are?
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         I wanted to jump onto their hoods and shout, Do you have ANY idea how much it means to her when someone stops for a cup of our jazzed-up Country Time? Just fork over a quarter, dammit. We wont take but a minute of your precious time!
        </p>
        <p>
         But I couldnt get mad.
        </p>
        <p>
         It was lemonade karma coming back at me. I could feel it sour in my veins, tarting up my knowing heart. This is how those other parents felt when I drove by their kids over the years. Sure, Ive stopped now and again. But not often enough.
        </p>
        <p>
         I always felt guilty rushing by, rationalizing with myself,
         <em>
          Im sure their friends from the neighborhood will give those kids some business
         </em>
         . But I knew better.
        </p>
        <p>
         It took seeing my own childs excitement blown back in her face bythe hot wake of passing car after passing car for me to truly understand the heartbreak I caused by not making a simple gesture. Its not that difficult
         <a href="http://letmestartbysayingblog.com/2015/02/04/two-words/" target="_blank">
          to be kind
         </a>
         . Heck, it can cost as little as twenty-five cents and take less than a minute. My eyes were opened wide that day.
        </p>
        <p>
         Now all summer long I keep a special stash of lemonade stand change in my car. Im preparedto switch on the hazards and partake in a refreshing cup of entrepreneurship from kids with sticky hands and jack olantern smiles on the side of the roads I travel. It may only be a quarter to me, but it means the world to them.
        </p>
        <p>
         And the look on their faces when I hand over far too many coins, telling them to keep the change?
        </p>
        <p>
         Worth every penny.
        </p>
