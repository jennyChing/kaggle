              <h1 class="atitle">
               Fit, Not Fat, but Definitely Fifty: What's in Claire's Bug-Out Bag?
              </h1>
              <h4 class="abyline">
               By Claire Wolfe
              </h4>
             </td>
             <td align="center">
              <img border="0" height="149" src="../columns/pix/hardyvillesign-r.jpg" width="144"/>
             </td>
            </tr>
            <tr>
             <td class="text3i">
              <div>
               <img border="0" height="10" src="../art/dot.gif" width="100%"/>
              </div>
              <!--Spacer-->
              <p>
               September 1, 2005
              </p>
             </td>
            </tr>
           </table>
           <p>
            We've talked so much about bug-out bags here in Hardyville (including
            <a href="../columns/wolfe050815.html" target="_blank">
             emergency scenarios for the not so hardy
            </a>
            ) that I think it's time to reveal what's in my personal bug-out bag. Before I get to the nitty gritty, some background.
           </p>
           <div class="floatright" style="width:200px;">
            <img alt="" border="0" height="214" hspace="0" src="../columns/pix/wolfe050901-1.jpg" vspace="0" width="200"/>
           </div>
           <p>
            The very idea of "bugging out" in an emergency is creepy. As one helpful reader recently reminded me, "[S]ome survival expert once said ... 'Never, ever, no matter what, become a refugee.' His point is that it is infinitely better to hunker down in your own secure, stocked, familiar surroundings than to join a huge throng of displaced persons trudging who knows where only to become a ward of whatever government is still functioning." (And these days make that
            <a href="http://www.lewrockwell.com/duggan/duggan10.html" target="_blank">
             an abused, disarmed, and illegally searched ward
            </a>
            of government.)"
           </p>
           <p>
            I live on high ground in a rural area and can imagine only a few scenarios that would force me out of my house during an emergency. Even in most of those, I'd simply move into
            <a href="../articles2/wolfe76.html" target="_blank">
             my yurt
            </a>
            , a shed, a tent, or a vehicle on my property. If a forest fire or other localized disaster drove me from my land, I'd bunk with friends.
           </p>
           <p>
            Bigger, more widespread emergencies? Well, my cabin and land are probably as safe a location as any I could find. And unlike in New Orleans when
            <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/apps/news?pid=10000082&amp;sid=aslnyq4NgkdA&amp;refer=canada" target="_blank">
             Hurricane Katrina was aiming her monster self at them
            </a>
            , no emergency-management authority is likely to order me out of here.
           </p>
           <p>
            So although I do have a bug-out bag which I could grab in a hurry, most of my preps are based on staying right here but toughing it out through a time of no power, light, or running water.
           </p>
           <p>
            The bag is simply so that I'll have everything handy and know where it is in an emergency without having to hunt and fret.
           </p>
           <p>
            My next-most-likely alternate scenario would be being caught somewhere while on a road trip -- my truck broken down in the middle of nowhere, for instance, and in a cellphone "dead zone" so that I couldn't call for help. For that and similar eventualities, I do have "outdoorsy" items in my kit. But I hope I never have to use them.
           </p>
           <div class="floatright" style="width:250px;">
            <img alt="" border="0" height="180" hspace="0" src="../columns/pix/wolfe050901-2.jpg" vspace="0" width="250"/>
           </div>
           <p>
            What you need in a bug-out bag is partly in the mind of the beholder.
            <a href="http://www.aussurvivalist.com/bugout.htm" target="_blank">
             Here's a person, for instance
            </a>
            , whose rather thought-provoking list includes a knife sharpener, camouflage paint, a radiation dosimeter, and "knife-proof gloves" but doesn't include a ground pad or any form shelter. (Perhaps he's prepared to improvise those.)
           </p>
           <p>
            And here's a
            <a href="http://www.survival-center.com/dl-list/dl1-toc.htm" target="_blank">
             whole set of lists
            </a>
            of bug-out bag contents which gives you yet more idea of how varied peoples' ideas are on this subject.
           </p>
           <p>
            I can't say what anybody else
            <i>
             should
            </i>
            have in their bags. I can definitely tell you that there are things I'd
            <i>
             like
            </i>
            to have in my bag that I don't have because of cost. Or space. Or because I can't see quite enough likely need to justify the purchase.
           </p>
           <p>
            Here's what I do have, for good or ill:
           </p>
           <hr align="center" noshade="" size="1" width="70%"/>
           <p>
            <b>
             WE INTERUPT THIS COLUMN FOR A CRASS COMMERCIAL ANNOUNCEMENT:
            </b>
            Many of the links below are to products on Amazon.com. If you buy using my links, I'll get a small commission on your purchase (but won't receive any personal information on you). It's a way of helping out a Starving Artist
            <sup>
             TM
            </sup>
            . If you aren't in the market, I hope you'll still find the Amazon links informative.
           </p>
           <hr align="center" noshade="" size="1" width="70%"/>
           <p>
            <b>
             The bag itself.
            </b>
            Mine is simply a duffel bag. That's probably
            <i>
             not
            </i>
            what you'd want if you expect a long trek on foot to safety. (The Bobaloo Crew used
            <a href="http://www.vtarmynavy.com/cfp90_field_packs.htm" target="_blank">
             military-style backpacks
            </a>
            when they went on their wintertime
            <a href="../columns/wolfe050215.html" target="_blank">
             Bug-Out Campout
            </a>
            .) But I got my duffel for a buck at a garage sale -- which enabled me to spend more on what's inside. A duffel is sufficient for "bugging-in" and for tossing into the back of a truck.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             A tube tent
            </b>
            . Most of my scenarios don't involve being stuck outdoors with zero shelter. A cheap, compact tube tent gives me that tiny extra bit of "just in case" without breaking either my budget or my back.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0007IS69M/sr=1-2/qid=1125083104/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              Columbia Frosty Trail sleeping bag
             </b>
            </a>
            . A compromise purchase. This was half-way between my previous cheap Wenger sleeping bag (which had great features, but whose zipper would never go back together after the first time I unzipped it) and something really nice and guaranteed for life like a
            <a href="http://www.wiggys.com" target="_blank">
             Wiggy's bag
            </a>
            . The Columbia has a hood, is rated to +5 degrees and has a cotton (rather than sweaty nylon or polyester) inner liner. When not needed for bugouts or for regular old campouts, guests can use it.
           </p>
           <div class="floatright" style="width:250px;">
            <img alt="" border="0" height="186" hspace="0" src="../columns/pix/wolfe050901-3.jpg" vspace="0" width="250"/>
           </div>
           <p>
            <b>
             Rope.
            </b>
            A 50-foot coil. I'll be adding
            <b>
             paracord
            </b>
            as the Bug-Out Campers
            <a href="../columns/wolfe050301.html" target="_blank">
             suggested in the aftermath
            </a>
            of their semi-successful winter camping expedition.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             A ground pad.
            </b>
            Don't go into the wilds without it, unless you know primitive methods for keeping your body warm on bare ground. That was the #1 Learning Experience from the Bug-Out Campout.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             A little money.
            </b>
            -- including a few pre-1964 silver coins, just for paranoia's sake.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Pre-paid cellphone.
            </b>
            Don't leave home without it. Don't let the battery die. And don't let the airtime go out of date.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             A handgun.
            </b>
            And 50-round box of ammo.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0001WB9UC/sr=1-1/qid=1125088645/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              A Spyderco Assist knife.
             </b>
            </a>
            These are made for rescue work like cutting seat belts or breaking glass. There are actually two models
            <a href="http://www.clairewolfe.com/wolfesblog/00001210.html" target="_blank">
             as I explain in this review
            </a>
            . And I'm lucky to have them both, thanks to publisher Rich Lucibella of
            <a href="http://www.swatmag.com" target="_blank">
             <i>
              S.W.A.T. magazine
             </i>
            </a>
            . One goes with me everywhere. One -- although I consider it part of my emergency kit -- actually lives in the truck.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Hand-sized AM-FM flashlight with siren and flashing beacon.
            </b>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0006BJ680/qid=1125084658/" target="_blank">
             It would be extremely cool to have a no-battery, hand-crank model like this one
            </a>
            . But I'm happy. Mine runs on very cheap AA batteries and I got it for $2.25 at a close-out sale. Can't beat that.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Clip-on belt-carry flashlight with flashing red lights.
            </b>
            Which I got for a buck at the same close-out sale.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Headlamp for hands-free work.
            </b>
            (Another bargain purchase.)
           </p>
           <p>
            Okay, okay. I'm a little nutty about flashlights. But in a garden-variety emergency, they always seem to be the
            <i>
             first
            </i>
            thing you need. And sometimes you need different types for different emergencies. And all that light-power for so little money ...
           </p>
           <p>
            And did I mention
            <b>
             extra batteries for all those flashlights
            </b>
            ?
           </p>
           <p>
            Next to the kit lives a
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0006IA0Q0/qid=1125081863/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              GE Steelbeam lantern
             </b>
            </a>
            There are lots of lanterns in the world. But this is the handiest emergency lantern I've ever had my hands on. Its fluorescent bulb is rated for 8,000 hours. It runs for 16 hours on 4 D batteries. It's tough, compact, weather resistant, and it can be either hung, set on a table, or carried by a pop-up handle.
           </p>
           <p>
            Six
            <b>
             full-meal MREs
            </b>
            for lunches and dinners, six
            <b>
             granola/energy bars
            </b>
            because I don't think I could face an MRE at breakfast.
           </p>
           <p>
            Twelve
            <b>
             tea bags
            </b>
            and a jar of
            <b>
             brown sugar
            </b>
            . Three packets of
            <b>
             hot chocolate
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0006SNCAQ/sr=1-2/qid=1125085389/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              A Camelbak hydropack
             </b>
            </a>
            always kept clean and filled with water. Mine is pretty light duty. But also light weight. There are
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B00076Y1WA/sr=1-1/qid=1125085389/" target="_blank">
             beaucoup high-capacity ones
            </a>
            and
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0000AML1T/sr=1-10/qid=1125085389/" target="_blank">
             ones with extra carrying pockets
            </a>
            . But mine does the job for me. Part of the reason it does the job is that the rest of my kit isn't designed to ride on my back, so I can easily carry my water supply there.
           </p>
           <p>
            If your bug-out kit is backpack-borne, then you may be better off with a
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B00099ZUQG/qid=1125085202/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              super-extra large canteen
             </b>
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=http://www.amazon.com/gp/search.html/ref=br_ss_hs?platform=gurupa%26url=node%3D3400371%26keywords=water+purification+tablets%26Go.x=7%26Go.y=6%26Go=Go" target="_blank">
             <b>
              Water purification tablets.
             </b>
            </a>
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B00079YTS8/sr=1-4/qid=1125086977/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              A very simple emergency stove
             </b>
            </a>
            . And
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0009RGBOS/sr=1-1/qid=1125087628/" target="_blank">
             another one
            </a>
            . I also have a
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0002YUOQ4/sr=1-4/qid=1125088120/r" target="_blank">
             Campingaz Turbo 270 butane stove
            </a>
            and spare cartridges. But that one lives full-time in my truck because it's so easy to use and handy enough to heat tea at highway rest stops.
           </p>
           <div class="floatright" style="width:250px;">
            <img alt="" border="0" height="188" hspace="0" src="../columns/pix/wolfe050901-4.jpg" vspace="0" width="250"/>
           </div>
           <p>
            Yeah, maybe I'm a little nutty on emergency cookstoves, too. But they're handy and two of my four (the fourth being a regular Coleman gas stove) were also uber-bargain store-closeout items. I also have two models of
            <a href="http://www.pyromid.net/info/index.html" target="_blank">
             the fabulous, unmatchable Pyromid
            </a>
            . But Pyromid is out of production, and despite several years of promises to be "back in production in two months," these compact, portable, durable wonder-stoves are very hard to get.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Waterproof matches and tinder
            </b>
            . Just in case
            <i>
             all
            </i>
            those stoves fail me or I have to build a fire for warmth. In my case, the tinder (following an idea from Hardyville's bug-out campers) is a double-baggied wad of dryer lint.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Small pan for heating water (and, if need be, food).
            </b>
            Mine is just an ordinary kitchen pot. I'd like to have a compact, lightweight mess kit. Problem is,
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B00028XQII/sr=1-13/qid=1125086488/" target="_blank">
             even the very nice ones
            </a>
            are usually aluminum, and I don't believe it's healthy to cook in aluminum. Okay, three days won't kill you. It's just the principle of the thing. This isn't in my kit, but here's
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0000AQO5U/sr=1-9/qid=1125086760/" target="_blank">
             copper-bottom stainless steel mess kit
            </a>
            I wouldn't mind acquiring someday.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Cup and utensils.
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             TP
            </b>
            . Definitely don't leave home without that.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Toothbrush
            </b>
            and
            <b>
             toothpaste.
            </b>
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Dog food.
            </b>
            Also an
            <b>
             Outward Hound Port-A-Bowl
            </b>
            for water and food
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0009X5GMK/sr=1-1/qid=1125092587/" target="_blank">
             (sort of, but not exactly, like this one)
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Medical kit.
            </b>
            Since I'm disgustingly healthy, I probably don't put as much emphasis on emergency medical care as I should. My kit contains
            <b>
             aspirin
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             ibuprofen
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             antacid
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             Cephalexin
            </b>
            (doggie antibiotic -- which can also be used for humans),
            <b>
             Pepto Bismol
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             Immodium-AD
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             gauze bandages
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             tape
            </b>
            ,
            <b>
             Neosporin
            </b>
            , and a small bottle of
            <b>
             hydrogen peroxide
            </b>
            .
            <b>
             Sunscreen
            </b>
            and
            <b>
             insect repellant
            </b>
            , too. I admit that I am not prepared to deal with major medical emergencies on my own, just minor discomforts and small-scale infection prevention. Oh, I keep my tetanus shot current, too.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             One complete change of clothes.
            </b>
            And
            <i>
             think layers
            </i>
            .
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Wool sox -- three pairs.
            </b>
            Nothing will save your toesies like wool, which keeps you warm even when wet.
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0000AWX3V/sr=1-4/qid=1125083873/" target="_blank">
             These are some of the least expensive I've seen
            </a>
            and they're just fine.
           </p>
           <div class="floatright" style="width:200px;">
            <img alt="" border="0" height="190" hspace="0" src="../columns/pix/wolfe050901-5.jpg" vspace="0" width="200"/>
           </div>
           <p>
            <b>
             A balaklava.
            </b>
            Yeah, yeah. I can hear the ninja jokes now. But
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0000AWHAA/ref=pd_sim_a_1?%5Fencoding=UTF8%26v=glance" target="_blank">
             this lightweight silk face cover
            </a>
            adds warmth and protection against wind without making you feel strangled, and who cares how it looks when the heat and lights are out?
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B00020O9DC/ref=pd_sim_sg_1?%5Fencoding=UTF8%26v=glance" target="_blank">
             <b>
              A waterproof poncho
             </b>
            </a>
            , which doubles as an
            <b>
             emergency blanket
            </b>
            . I've always kept a "space blanket" around, too. But it's too easy to assume that little bit of mylar-coated plastic is a miracle device. In fact, it takes
            <a href="http://www.traditionalmountaineering.org/FAQ_SpaceBlankets.htm" target="_blank">
             knowhow to use one
            </a>
            and they won't perform wonders. This is another reason I hope to "bug in," not out. I like blanket blankets. Nice, fuzzy ones with silkie edges. To heck with roughing it. To heck with modern miracle items.
           </p>
           <p>
            <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/redirect?link_code=ur2&amp;camp=1789&amp;tag=clairewolfeco-20&amp;creative=9325&amp;path=tg/detail/-/B0001ZLBFW/qid=1125084402/" target="_blank">
             <b>
              Thermal long-johns
             </b>
            </a>
            . I don't bother with the tops. It's so easy to layer your top half. Much harder to keep skinny legs warm.
           </p>
           <p>
            <b>
             Baby wipes
            </b>
            -- for when there's no running water.
           </p>
           <p>
            Several
            <b>
             plastic bags
            </b>
            of various sizes for trash, wet clothes, etc.
           </p>
           <p>
            And oh yeah, the biggest, hardest item: Remembering to check all the other items periodically to ensure that batteries are still juicing, bulbs still glowing, food still edible, medicine still helpful, and that nothing has leaked, dried up, run out, or otherwise gone defunct.
           </p>
           <p>
            I may be forgetting some item that's lurking down in a dark pocket of the duffel bag. But basically, that's my kit. So what's in yours?
           </p>
