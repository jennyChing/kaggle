         <h4>
          Butter
         </h4>
         <p>
          Theres a whole lot of churnin going on these days, but that wasnt always the case. Until the mid- to-late-1800s, butter was made mainly on dairy farms.  It wasnt until around 1900 that technology, plus the expansion of commercial creameries, made butter widely available across the U.S.
         </p>

       <div class="slide slide-0" style="bor__der:1px solid red">

        <div class="subslide-content" style="display:none">
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Butter
          </h4>
          <p>
           Theres a whole lot of churnin going on these days, but that wasnt always the case. Until the mid- to-late-1800s, butter was made mainly on dairy farms.  It wasnt until around 1900 that technology, plus the expansion of commercial creameries, made butter widely available across the U.S.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Butter
          </h4>
          <p>
           Its time to split! Well, not us just the cream. You see, butter is made by churning or shaking pasteurized cream until the milkfat (butterfat) separates from the remaining fluid, called buttermilk.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="slide slide-1" style="bor__der:1px solid red">
        <img alt="Cottage Cheese" src="http://www.realcaliforniamilk.com/wp-content/uploads/dairy-cottege_cheese.png" style="width:100%;-webk__it-filter: blur(5px);"/>
        <div class="subslide-content" style="display:none">
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Cottage Cheese
          </h4>
          <p>
           Only one cheese gets to be first. And here in the U.S., thats cottage cheese! Early immigrants to America brought the tradition of fresh cheesemaking with them, and by the mid-1800s, the term cottage cheese had entered the American vocabulary.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Cottage Cheese
          </h4>
          <p>
           Oh my curd! The process of cottage cheese: By adding a bacterial culture that produces lactic acid or adding a food-grade acid, such as vinegar, to pasteurized milk, a separation occurs between the milk solids and the whey. After the curd is formed, it is gently cut into pieces that allow additional whey to drain from the curds.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="slide slide-2" style="bor__der:1px solid red">
        <img alt="Cream" src="http://www.realcaliforniamilk.com/wp-content/uploads/dairy-cream.png" style="width:100%;-webk__it-filter: blur(5px);"/>
        <div class="subslide-content" style="display:none">
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Cream
          </h4>
          <p>
           You knew that Caesar and the Roman senate were decadent, but did you know the Romans first used cream in the 9th Century A.D.? Hail, cream!
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Cream
          </h4>
          <p>
           No, really. The cream really does rise to the top! Cream naturally separates from milk because the milkfat in cream is less dense than the fluid milk.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <div class="slide slide-3" style="bor__der:1px solid red">
        <img alt="Crme Frache" src="http://www.realcaliforniamilk.com/wp-content/uploads/dairy-sour_cream.png" style="width:100%;-webk__it-filter: blur(5px);"/>
        <div class="subslide-content" style="display:none">
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Crme Frache
          </h4>
          <p>
           Next time youre feeling saucy,
           <br/>
           take a lesson in French! Crme frache  French for fresh cream
           <br/>
            is thought to have originated in Normandy. It possesses a slightly tangy, nutty flavor and velvety rich texture that makes it perfect for making sauces, drizzling over vegetables, and spooned
           <br/>
           on fresh fruit.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="subslide">
          <h4>
           Crme Frache
          </h4>
          <p>
           Yeah, yeah, you know yogurt. But did you know that crme frache is made much the same way? The cream is pasteurized and then seeded with a starter culture, similar to yogurt and cheese, but unlike those two, its left until slightly soured and thick.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
