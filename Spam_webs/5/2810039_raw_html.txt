     <p>
      <b>
       Felix the Cat
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/Funny_animal" title="Funny animal">
       funny animal
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">
       cartoon
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">
       character
      </a>
      created in the
      <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">
       silent film
      </a>
      era. The
      <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">
       anthropomorphic
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Black_cat" title="Black cat">
       black cat
      </a>
      with his black body, white eyes, and giant grin, coupled with the
      <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">
       surrealism
      </a>
      of the situations in which his cartoons place him, combine to make Felix one of the most recognized cartoon characters in film history. Felix was the first character from animation to attain a level of popularity sufficient to draw movie audiences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Felix's origins remain disputed. Australian
      <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">
       cartoonist
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Film_industry" title="Film industry">
       film entrepreneur
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Pat_Sullivan_(film_producer)" title="Pat Sullivan (film producer)">
       Pat Sullivan
      </a>
      , owner of the Felix character, claimed during his lifetime to be its creator. American
      <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">
       animator
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Messmer" title="Otto Messmer">
       Otto Messmer
      </a>
      , Sullivan's lead animator, has been credited as such.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      What is certain is that Felix emerged from Sullivan's studio, and cartoons featuring the character enjoyed success and popularity in the 1920s
      <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">
       popular culture
      </a>
      . Aside from the animated shorts, Felix starred in a
      <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">
       comic strip
      </a>
      (drawn by Sullivan, Messmer and later Joe Oriolo) beginning in 1923,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and his image soon adorned merchandise such as ceramics, toys and postcards. Several manufacturers made stuffed Felix toys.
      <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
       Jazz
      </a>
      bands such as
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Whiteman" title="Paul Whiteman">
       Paul Whiteman
      </a>
      's played songs about him (1923's "Felix Kept On Walking" and others).
     </p>
     <p>
      By the late 1920s, with the arrival of
      <a href="/wiki/Sound_film" title="Sound film">
       sound cartoons
      </a>
      , Felix's success was fading. The new
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">
       Disney
      </a>
      shorts of
      <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">
       Mickey Mouse
      </a>
      made the silent offerings of Sullivan and Messmer, who were then unwilling to move to sound production, seem outdated. In 1929, Sullivan decided to make the transition and began distributing Felix sound cartoons through Copley Pictures. The sound Felix shorts proved to be a failure and the operation ended in 1932. Felix saw a brief three cartoon resurrection in 1936 by the
      <a href="/wiki/Van_Beuren_Studios" title="Van Beuren Studios">
       Van Beuren Studios
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Felix cartoons began airing on American TV in 1953.
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Oriolo" title="Joe Oriolo">
       Joe Oriolo
      </a>
      introduced a redesigned, "long-legged" Felix, added new characters, and gave Felix a "Magic Bag of Tricks" that could assume an infinite variety of shapes at Felix's behest. The cat has since starred in other television programs and in two
      <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">
       feature films
      </a>
      . As of the 2010s, Felix is featured on a variety of merchandise from clothing to toys. Oriolo's son, Don Oriolo, later assumed creative control of Felix.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2002,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">
        TV Guide
       </a>
      </i>
      ranked Felix the Cat number 28 on its "50 Greatest Cartoon Characters of All Time" list.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2014, the rights to the character were acquired by
      <a href="/wiki/DreamWorks_Animation" title="DreamWorks Animation">
       DreamWorks Animation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      On 9 November 1919, Master Tom, a prototype of Felix, debuted in a Paramount Pictures short entitled "Feline Follies".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Produced by the New York City-based animation studio owned by Pat Sullivan, the cartoon was directed by
      <a href="/wiki/Cartoonist" title="Cartoonist">
       cartoonist
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Animator" title="Animator">
       animator
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Messmer" title="Otto Messmer">
       Otto Messmer
      </a>
      . It was a success, and the Sullivan studio quickly set to work on producing another film featuring Master Tom, the Felix the Cat prototype in "The Musical Mews" (released 16 November 1919). It too proved to be successful with audiences. Otto Messmer claimed that John King of Paramount Magazine suggested the name "Felix", after the
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      words
      <i>
       felis
      </i>
      (cat) and
      <i>
       felix
      </i>
      (lucky). The name was first used for the third film starring the character, "The Adventures of Felix" (released on December 14, 1919). Pat Sullivan claimed he named Felix after
      <a href="/wiki/Australia_Felix" title="Australia Felix">
       Australia Felix
      </a>
      from Australian history and literature. In 1924,
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nolan_(animator)" title="Bill Nolan (animator)">
       animator Bill Nolan
      </a>
      redesigned the character, making him both rounder and cuter. Felix's new looks, coupled with Messmer's
      <a href="/wiki/Character_animation" title="Character animation">
       character animation
      </a>
      , brought Felix to fame.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon34_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon34-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The question of who created Felix remains a matter of dispute. Sullivan stated in numerous newspaper interviews that he created Felix and did the key drawings for the character. On a visit to Australia in 1925, Sullivan told
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Argus_(Australia)" title="The Argus (Australia)">
        The Argus
       </a>
      </i>
      newspaper that "The idea was given to me by the sight of a cat which my wife brought to the studio one day."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On other occasions, he claimed that Felix had been inspired by
      <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">
       Rudyard Kipling
      </a>
      's "
      <a href="/wiki/Just_So_Stories" title="Just So Stories">
       The Cat that Walked by Himself
      </a>
      " or by his wife's love for strays.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon34_8-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon34-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Members of the Australian Cartoonist Association have claimed that lettering used in "Feline Follies" matches Sullivan's handwriting
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vixen_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-vixen-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and that Sullivan lettered within his drawings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vixen_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-vixen-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Sullivan's supporters also say the case is supported by his March 18, 1917 release of a cartoon short entitled "The Tail of Thomas Kat", more than two years prior to "Feline Follies". Both an Australian
      <a href="/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Australian Broadcasting Corporation">
       ABC-TV
      </a>
      documentary screened in 2004
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the curators of an exhibition at the
      <a href="/wiki/State_Library_of_New_South_Wales" title="State Library of New South Wales">
       State Library of New South Wales
      </a>
      , in 2005, suggested that Thomas Kat was a prototype or precursor of Felix. However, few details of Thomas have survived. His fur color has not been definitively established, and the surviving copyright synopsis
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      for the short suggests significant differences between Thomas and the later Felix. For example, whereas the later Felix magically transforms his tail into tools and other objects, Thomas is a non-
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphize" title="Anthropomorphize">
       anthropomorphized
      </a>
      cat who loses his tail in a fight with a rooster, never to recover it.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sullivan was the studio proprietor andas is the case with almost all film entrepreneurshe owned the copyright to any creative work by his employees. In common with many animators at the time, Messmer was not credited. After Sullivan's death in 1933, his estate in Australia took ownership of the character.
     </p>
     <p>
      It was not until after Sullivan's death that Sullivan staffers such as Hal Walker,
      <a href="/wiki/Al_Eugster" title="Al Eugster">
       Al Eugster
      </a>
      ,Gerry Geronimi, Rudy Zamora, George Cannata, and Sullivan's own lawyer, Harry Kopp, credited Messmer with Felix's creation. They claimed that Felix was based on an animated
      <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">
       Charlie Chaplin
      </a>
      that Messmer had animated for Sullivan's studio earlier on. The down-and-out personality and movements of the cat in "Feline Follies" reflect key attributes of Chaplin's, and, although blockier than the later Felix, the familiar black body is already there (Messmer found solid shapes easier to animate). Messmer himself recalled his version of the cat's creation in an interview with animation historian
      <a href="/wiki/John_Canemaker" title="John Canemaker">
       John Canemaker
      </a>
      :
     </p>
     <blockquote class="templatequote">
      <p>
       Sullivan's studio was very busy, and Paramount, they were falling behind their schedule and they needed one extra to fill in. And Sullivan, being very busy, said, "If you want to do it on the side, you can do any little thing to satisfy them." So I figured a cat would be about the simplest. Make him all black, you knowyou wouldn't need to worry about outlines. And one gag after the other, you know? Cute. And they all got laughs. So Paramount liked it so they ordered a series.
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Animation historians back Messmer's claims. Among them are
      <a href="/wiki/Michael_Barrier_(historian)" title="Michael Barrier (historian)">
       Michael Barrier
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Beck" title="Jerry Beck">
       Jerry Beck
      </a>
      , Colin and Timothy Cowles, Donald Crafton,
      <a href="/wiki/David_Gerstein" title="David Gerstein">
       David Gerstein
      </a>
      , Milt Gray, Mark Kausler,
      <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Maltin" title="Leonard Maltin">
       Leonard Maltin
      </a>
      , and Charles Solomon.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      No animation historians outside of Australia have argued on behalf of Sullivan.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sullivan marketed the cat relentlessly, while Messmer continued to produce a prodigious volume of Felix cartoons. Messmer did the animation directly on white paper with inkers tracing the drawings directly. The animators drew backgrounds onto pieces of
      <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">
       celluloid
      </a>
      , which were then laid atop the drawings to be photographed. Any perspective work had to be animated by hand, as the studio cameras were unable to perform pans or trucks. Pat Sullivan began a
      <a href="/wiki/Comic_strip" title="Comic strip">
       comic strip
      </a>
      in 1923, distributed by
      <a href="/wiki/King_Features_Syndicate" title="King Features Syndicate">
       King Features Syndicate
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon34_8-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon34-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Messmer took over drawing duties of the strip.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity_and_distribution">
       Popularity and distribution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_the_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Popularity and distribution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">
       Paramount Pictures
      </a>
      distributed the earliest films from 1919 to 1921.
      <a href="/wiki/Margaret_J._Winkler" title="Margaret J. Winkler">
       Margaret J. Winkler
      </a>
      distributed the shorts from 1922 to 1925, the year when
      <a href="/wiki/Educational_Pictures" title="Educational Pictures">
       Educational Pictures
      </a>
      took over the distribution of the shorts. Sullivan promised them one new
      <i>
       Felix
      </i>
      short every two weeks.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The combination of solid animation, skillful promotion, and widespread distribution brought Felix's popularity to new heights.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon34_8-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon34-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      References to alcoholism and
      <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">
       Prohibition
      </a>
      were also commonplace in many of the Felix shorts, particularly "Felix Finds Out" (1924), "
      <a href="/wiki/Whys_and_Other_Whys" title="Whys and Other Whys">
       Whys and Other Whys
      </a>
      " (1927), "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Felix_Woos_Whoopee" title="Felix Woos Whoopee">
       Felix Woos Whoopee
      </a>
      " (1930) to name a few. In "Felix Dopes It Out" (1924), Felix tries to help his hobo friend who is plagued with a red nose. By the end of the short, the cat finds the cure for the condition: "Keep drinking, and it'll turn blue."
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition, Felix was one of the first images ever broadcast by television when
      <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">
       RCA
      </a>
      chose a
      <a href="/wiki/Papier-m%C3%A2ch%C3%A9" title="Papier-mch">
       papier-mch
      </a>
      Felix doll for a 1928 experiment via New York in
      <a href="/wiki/Van_Cortlandt_Park" title="Van Cortlandt Park">
       Van Cortlandt Park
      </a>
      . The doll was chosen for its tonal contrast and its ability to withstand the intense lights needed. It was placed on a rotating
      <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">
       phonograph turntable
      </a>
      and photographed for approximately two hours each day. After a one-time payoff to Sullivan, the doll remained on the turntable for nearly a decade as RCA fine-tuned the picture's definition.
     </p>
     <p>
      Felix's great success also spawned a host of imitators. The appearances and personalities of other 1920s feline stars such as
      <a href="/wiki/Julius_the_Cat" title="Julius the Cat">
       Julius
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">
       Walt Disney
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Alice_Comedies" title="Alice Comedies">
        Alice Comedies
       </a>
      </i>
      , Waffles of
      <a href="/wiki/Terrytoons" title="Terrytoons">
       Paul Terry
      </a>
      's
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesop%27s_Film_Fables" title="Aesop's Film Fables">
        Aesop's Film Fables
       </a>
      </i>
      , and especially Bill Nolan's 1925 adaptation of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Krazy_Kat" title="Krazy Kat">
        Krazy Kat
       </a>
      </i>
      (distributed by the eschewed Winkler) all seem to have been directly patterned after Felix.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon37_14-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon37-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Felix's cartoons were also popular among critics. They have been cited as imaginative examples of
      <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">
       surrealism
      </a>
      in filmmaking. Felix has been said to represent a child's sense of wonder, creating the fantastic when it is not there, and taking it in stride when it is. His famous pacehands behind his back, head down, deep in thoughtbecame a trademark that has been analyzed by critics around the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Felix's expressive tail, which could be a shovel one moment, an exclamation mark or pencil the next, serves to emphasize that anything can happen in his world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">
       Aldous Huxley
      </a>
      wrote that the
      <i>
       Felix
      </i>
      shorts proved that "What the cinema can do better than literature or the spoken drama is to be fantastic."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      By 1923, the character was at the peak of his film career. "
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_in_Hollywood" title="Felix in Hollywood">
       Felix in Hollywood
      </a>
      ", a short released during this year, plays upon Felix's popularity, as he becomes acquainted with such fellow celebrities as
      <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Fairbanks" title="Douglas Fairbanks">
       Douglas Fairbanks
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cecil_B._DeMille" title="Cecil B. DeMille">
       Cecil B. DeMille
      </a>
      , Charlie Chaplin,
      <a href="/wiki/Ben_Turpin" title="Ben Turpin">
       Ben Turpin
      </a>
      , and even censor
      <a href="/wiki/Will_H._Hays" title="Will H. Hays">
       Will H. Hays
      </a>
      . His image could be seen on clocks (not to be confused with the
      <a href="/wiki/Kit-Cat_Klock" title="Kit-Cat Klock">
       Kit-Cat Klock
      </a>
      ), Christmas ornaments, and as the first giant balloon ever made for
      <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" title="Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade">
       Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade
      </a>
      (which flew away and popped after the parade). Felix also became the subject of several popular songs of the day, such as "Felix Kept Walking" by
      <a href="/wiki/Paul_Whiteman" title="Paul Whiteman">
       Paul Whiteman
      </a>
      . Sullivan made an estimated $100,000 a year from toy licensing alone.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon34_8-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon34-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With the character's success also emerged a handful of new costars. These included Felix's master Willie Jones, a mouse named Skiddoo, Felix's nephews Inky, Dinky, and Winky, and his girlfriend Kitty. Felix the Cat sheet music, with music by
      <a href="/wiki/Pete_Wendling" title="Pete Wendling">
       Pete Wendling
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Kortlander&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Max Kortlander (page does not exist)">
       Max Kortlander
      </a>
      , featuring lyrics by
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Bryan" title="Alfred Bryan">
       Alfred Bryan
      </a>
      , was published in 1928 by
      <a href="/wiki/Sam_Fox_Publishing_Company" title="Sam Fox Publishing Company">
       Sam Fox Publishing Company
      </a>
      . The cover art of Felix playing a banjo was done by Otto Messmer
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of the early Felix cartoons mirrored American attitudes of the "
      <a href="/wiki/Roaring_Twenties" title="Roaring Twenties">
       Roaring Twenties
      </a>
      ". Ethnic stereotypes appeared in such shorts as "Felix Goes Hungry" (1924). Recent events such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">
       Russian Civil War
      </a>
      were depicted in shorts like "Felix All Puzzled" (1924).
      <a href="/wiki/Flapper" title="Flapper">
       Flappers
      </a>
      were caricatured in "Felix Strikes It Rich" (1923). He also became involved in
      <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">
       union
      </a>
      organizing with "Felix Revolts" (also 1923). In some shorts, Felix even performed a rendition of the
      <a href="/wiki/Charleston_(dance)" title="Charleston (dance)">
       Charleston
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1928, Educational ceased releasing the Felix cartoons and several were reissued by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_National_Pictures" title="First National Pictures">
       First National Pictures
      </a>
      . Copley Pictures distributed them from 1929 to 1930. He saw a brief three-cartoon resurrection in 1936 by the
      <a href="/wiki/Van_Beuren_Studios" title="Van Beuren Studios">
       Van Beuren Studios
      </a>
      ("Felix the Cat and the Goose That Laid the Golden Egg", "Neptune Nonsense" and "Bold King Cole"). Sullivan did most of the
      <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">
       marketing
      </a>
      for the character in the 1920s. In these
      <a href="/wiki/Van_Beuren_Studios" title="Van Beuren Studios">
       Van Beuren Studios
      </a>
      shorts, Felix spoke and sang in a high pitched childlike voice provided by
      <a href="/wiki/Walter_Tetley" title="Walter Tetley">
       Walter Tetley
      </a>
      , a popular radio actor in the 1930s and 1940s ("Julius" on the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Phil_Harris-Alice_Faye_Show" title="The Phil Harris-Alice Faye Show">
        The Phil Harris-Alice Faye Show
       </a>
      </i>
      , and "Leroy" on
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Gildersleeve" title="The Great Gildersleeve">
        The Great Gildersleeve
       </a>
      </i>
      , however best known later in the 1960s as the voice of Sherman on the

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Felix_as_mascot_and_pop_culture_icon">

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      Given the character's unprecedented popularity and the fact that his name was partially derived from the Latin word for "lucky", some rather notable individuals and organizations adopted Felix as a mascot. The first of these was a Los Angeles
      <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">
       Chevrolet
      </a>
      dealer and friend of Pat Sullivan named Winslow B. Felix who first opened his showroom in 1921. The three-sided neon sign of Felix Chevrolet,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      with its giant, smiling images of the character, is today one of LA's best-known landmarks, standing watch over both
      <a href="/wiki/Figueroa_Street" title="Figueroa Street">
       Figueroa Street
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harbor_Freeway" title="Harbor Freeway">
       Harbor Freeway
      </a>
      . Others who adopted Felix included the 1922
      <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">
       New York Yankees
      </a>
      and aviator
      <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">
       Charles Lindbergh
      </a>

     </p>

     <p>
      This popularity persisted. In the late 1920s, the U.S. Navy's Bombing Squadron Two (VB-2B) adopted a unit insignia consisting of Felix happily carrying a bomb with a burning fuse. They retained the insignia through the 1930s when they became a fighter squadron under the designations VF-6B and, later, VF-3, whose members
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_O%27Hare" title="Edward O'Hare">
       Edward O'Hare
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/John_Thach" title="John Thach">
       John Thach
      </a>
      became famous naval aviators in
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . After the world war a U.S. Navy fighter squadron currently designated
      <a href="/wiki/VFA-31" title="VFA-31">
       VFA-31
      </a>
      replaced its winged meat-cleaver logo with the same insignia, after the original Felix squadron had been disbanded. The carrier-based night-fighter squadron, nicknamed the "Tomcatters," remained active under various designations continuing through the present day and Felix still appears on both the squadron's cloth jacket patches and aircraft, carrying his bomb with its fuse burning.
     </p>
     <p>
      Felix is also the oldest high school mascot in the state of
      <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">
       Indiana
      </a>
      , chosen in 1926 after a Logansport High School player brought his plush Felix to a basketball game. When the team came from behind and won that night, Felix became the mascot of all the Logansport High School sports teams.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="From_silent_to_sound">
       From silent to sound
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_the_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: From silent to sound">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:167px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:April-maze-copley.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="400" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/April-maze-copley.jpg/165px-April-maze-copley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/April-maze-copley.jpg/248px-April-maze-copley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/April-maze-copley.jpg/330px-April-maze-copley.jpg 2x" width="165"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:April-maze-copley.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Felix and Inky and Winky in "
        <a href="/wiki/April_Maze" title="April Maze">
         April Maze
        </a>
        " (1930)
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      With the advent of
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Jazz_Singer" title="The Jazz Singer">
        The Jazz Singer
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1927, Educational Pictures, who distributed the Felix shorts at the time, urged Pat Sullivan to make the leap to "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talkie" title="Talkie">
       talkie
      </a>
      " cartoons, but Sullivan refused. Further disputes led to a break between Educational and Sullivan. Only when Walt Disney's
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Steamboat_Willie" title="Steamboat Willie">
        Steamboat Willie
       </a>
      </i>
      made cinematic history as the first talking cartoon with a synchronized soundtrack (
      <a href="/wiki/My_Old_Kentucky_Home_(film)" title="My Old Kentucky Home (film)">
       My Old Kentucky Home
      </a>
      from Max and Dave Fleischer's Ko-Ko
      <a href="/wiki/Song_Car-Tunes" title="Song Car-Tunes">
       Song Car-Tunes
      </a>
      preceded Willie, but did not gain the same recognition) did Sullivan see the possibilities of sound. He managed to secure a contract with First National Pictures in 1928. However, for reasons unknown, this did not last, so Sullivan sought out Jacques Kopfstein and Copley Pictures to distribute his new sound Felix cartoons. On 16 October 1929, an advertisement appeared in
      <a href="/wiki/Film_Daily" title="Film Daily">
       Film Daily
      </a>
      with Felix announcing,
      <a href="/wiki/Al_Jolson" title="Al Jolson">
       Jolson
      </a>
      -like, "You ain't heard nothin' yet!"
     </p>
     <p>
      Unfortunately, Felix's transition to sound was not a smooth one. Sullivan did not carefully prepare for Felix's transition to sound, and added sound effects into the sound cartoons as a post-animation process.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The results were disastrous. More than ever, it seemed as though Disney's mouse was drawing audiences away from Sullivan's silent star. Not even entries such as the off-beat "Felix Woos Whoopee" (1931) or the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silly_Symphonies" title="Silly Symphonies">
       Silly Symphony
      </a>
      -esque "
      <a href="/wiki/April_Maze" title="April Maze">
       April Maze
      </a>
      " (both 1930) could regain the franchise's audience. Kopfstein finally canceled Sullivan's contract. Subsequently, he announced plans to start a new studio in California, but such ideas never materialized. Things went from bad to worse when Sullivan's wife, Marjorie, died in March 1932. After this, Sullivan completely fell apart. He slumped into an alcoholic depression, his health rapidly declined, and his memory began to fade. He could not even cash checks to Messmer because his signature was reduced to a mere scribble. He died in 1933. Messmer recalled, "He left everything a mess, no books, no nothing. So when he died the place had to close down, at the height of popularity, when everybody, RKO and all of them, for years they tried to get hold of Felix ... I didn't have that permission [to continue the character] 'cause I didn't have legal ownership of it."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1935, Amadee J. Van Beuren of the Van Beuren Studios called Messmer and asked him if he could return Felix to the screen. Van Beuren even stated that Messmer would be provided with a full staff and all of the necessary utilities. However, Messmer declined his offer and instead recommended
      <a href="/wiki/Burt_Gillett" title="Burt Gillett">
       Burt Gillett
      </a>
      , a former Sullivan staffer who was now heading the Van Beuren staff. So, in 1936, Van Beuren obtained approval from Sullivan's brother to license Felix to his studio with the intention of producing new shorts both in color and with sound. With Gillett at the helm, now with a heavy Disney influence, he did away with Felix's established personality and made him just another
      <a href="/wiki/Funny_animal" title="Funny animal">
       funny-animal
      </a>
      character of the type popular in the day. The new shorts were unsuccessful, and after only three outings Van Beuren discontinued the series.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon37_14-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon37-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">
       Revival
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_the_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Revival">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_the_Cat_(TV_series)" title="Felix the Cat (TV series)">
       Felix the Cat (TV series)
      </a>
     </div>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Felix_for_Judy.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="421" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Felix_for_Judy.png/220px-Felix_for_Judy.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Felix_for_Judy.png/330px-Felix_for_Judy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/Felix_for_Judy.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Felix_for_Judy.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An ink drawing of Felix by Messmer dating from around 1975.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1953,
      <a href="/wiki/Official_Films" title="Official Films">
       Official Films
      </a>
      purchased the SullivanMessmer shorts, added soundtracks to them, and distributed to the home movie and television markets. Messmer himself pursued the Sunday Felix comic strips until their discontinuance in 1943, when he began eleven years of writing and drawing Felix comic books for
      <a href="/wiki/Dell_Comics" title="Dell Comics">
       Dell Comics
      </a>
      that were released every other month. In 1954, Messmer retired from the Felix daily newspaper strips, and his assistant
      <a href="/wiki/Joe_Oriolo" title="Joe Oriolo">
       Joe Oriolo
      </a>
      (the creator of
      <a href="/wiki/Casper_the_Friendly_Ghost" title="Casper the Friendly Ghost">
       Casper the Friendly Ghost
      </a>
      ) took over. Oriolo struck a deal with Felix's new owner, Pat Sullivan's nephew, to begin a new series of Felix cartoons on television. Oriolo went on to star Felix in 260 television cartoons distributed by
      <a href="/wiki/Trans-Lux" title="Trans-Lux">
       Trans-Lux
      </a>
      beginning in 1958. Like the Van Beuren studio before, Oriolo gave Felix a more domesticated and pedestrian personality, geared more toward children, and introduced now-familiar elements such as Felix's Magic Bag of Tricks, a satchel that could assume the shape and characteristics of anything Felix wanted. The show did away with Felix's previous supporting cast and introduced many
      <a href="/wiki/Felix_the_Cat_(TV_series)#Characters" title="Felix the Cat (TV series)">
       new characters
      </a>
      , all of which were performed by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_actor" title="Voice actor">
       voice actor
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Mercer" title="Jack Mercer">
       Jack Mercer
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Oriolo's plots revolve around the unsuccessful attempts of the antagonists to steal Felix's Magic Bag, though in an unusual twist, these antagonists are occasionally depicted as Felix's friends as well. The cartoons proved popular, but critics have dismissed them as paling in comparison to the earlier SullivanMessmer works, especially since Oriolo aimed the cartoons at children.
      <a href="/wiki/Limited_animation" title="Limited animation">
       Limited animation
      </a>
      (required due to budgetary restraints) and simplistic story lines did nothing to diminish the series' popularity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon37_14-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-solomon37-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1970, Oriolo gained complete control of the Felix character, and continues to promote the character to this day.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the late 1980s, after his father's death, Don Oriolo teamed up with European animators to work on the character's first feature film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Felix_the_Cat:_The_Movie" title="Felix the Cat: The Movie">
        Felix the Cat: The Movie
       </a>
      </i>
      .

         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the film, Felix visits an alternate reality along with the Professor and Poindexter.
      <a href="/wiki/New_World_Pictures" title="New World Pictures">
       New World Pictures
      </a>
      planned a 1987 Thanksgiving release for U.S. theaters, which did not happen;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-superstars_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-superstars-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      the movie went
      <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-video" title="Direct-to-video">
       direct-to-video
      </a>
      in August 1991.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beacon_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-beacon-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1995, Felix appeared on television again, in the series
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Twisted_Tales_of_Felix_the_Cat" title="The Twisted Tales of Felix the Cat">
        The Twisted Tales of Felix the Cat
       </a>
      </i>
      .
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Baby_Felix" title="Baby Felix">
        Baby Felix
       </a>
      </i>
      followed in 2000 for the Japanese market, and also the direct-to-video
      <i>
       Felix the Cat Saves Christmas
      </i>
      . Felix co-starred with
      <a href="/wiki/Betty_Boop" title="Betty Boop">

     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_actors">

      </span>

     <p>
      Felix was silent until 1936, when the sudden popularity of
      <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">
       Mickey Mouse
      </a>
      prompted the animators to produce Felix cartoons with sound.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
       Legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Felix_the_Cat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       In 2004, Felix was voted among the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=100_Greatest_Cartoons_(UK_TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="100 Greatest Cartoons (UK TV series) (page does not exist)">
        100 Greatest Cartoons
       </a>
       in a poll conducted by the British television channel
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">
        Channel 4
       </a>
       , ranking at #89.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         28
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       In the same year, Felix was named #36 in
       <a href="/wiki/Animal_Planet" title="Animal Planet">
        Animal Planet
       </a>
       's 50 Greatest Movie Animals.
      </li>
      <li>
       In 2002, Felix was voted in
       <a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">
        TV Guide
       </a>
       's 50 greatest cartoon characters of all time, ranking #28.
      </li>
      <li>
       In 1929 when television was in the experimental stages, the very first image to ever be seen was an illustration of Felix the Cat. It remained on there for hours while engineers used it as a test pattern.
      </li>
     </ul>

       's helmet while he played for the
       <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">

       </a>
       featured Felix. There was a special trim-package called "Felix the Mira" offered at the time.
      </li>
      <li>
       In Italy, Felix was called
       <i>
        Mio Mao
       </i>
       under
       <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Corriere_dei_Piccoli" title="Corriere dei Piccoli">
        Corriere dei Piccoli
       </a>
       .
      </li>
      <li>
       The cartoon
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/My_Life_As_a_Teenage_Robot" title="My Life As a Teenage Robot">
         My Life As a Teenage Robot
        </a>
       </i>
       features a diner called "Mezmer's" (named after
       <a href="/wiki/Otto_Messmer" title="Otto Messmer">
        Otto Messmer
       </a>
       ), and the doorway to the restaurant is a giant Felix the Cat head.
      </li>
      <li>
       In an
       <a href="/wiki/Rosebud_(The_Simpsons)" title="Rosebud (The Simpsons)">
        episode
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">
         The Simpsons
        </a>
       </i>
       , Dean Scungio quotes from
       <i>
        The Encyclopaedia of Animated Cartoons
       </i>
       on the history of Felix: "A Felix doll became Charles Lindbergh's companion on his famed flight across the Atlantic."
       <a href="/wiki/The_Day_the_Violence_Died" title="The Day the Violence Died">
        Another episode
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">
         The Simpsons
        </a>
       </i>
       , in which the origins of the cartoon characters
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itchy_%26_Scratchy" title="Itchy &amp; Scratchy">
        Itchy &amp; Scratchy
       </a>

       <i>
        Manhattan Madness
       </i>
       , presented as the first
       <i>
        Itchy &amp; Scratchy
       </i>
       cartoon, supposedly from 1919, that is similar in style to "Felix in Hollywood" and other early Felix animations.
      </li>
      <li>
       Felix appeared in the 1927
       <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" title="Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade">
        Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade
       </a>
       , making him the first balloon to float in the parade. Unfortunately, when the balloons were released, he got tangled in some phone wires and caught fire. The fire was put out, but Felix was shelved.
      </li>
      <li>

        <a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">

        Europe

       Part of a Felix cartoon was played in the 2002 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/100_Women" title="100 Women">
         100 Women
        </a>
       </i>
       , by a projector that Sam was using to play the cartoon on Hope's building.

      <li>
       American
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ" title="DJ">
        DJ
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Felix_da_Housecat" title="Felix da Housecat">

       </a>

      <li>
       It is believed that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naoto_Oshima" title="Naoto Oshima">

       <a href="/wiki/Sonic_the_Hedgehog_(series)" title="Sonic the Hedgehog (series)">

       </a>
       while working at
       <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">
        Sega
       </a>
       .

       .

       </a>
       referenced Felix in the video for his song
       <a href="/wiki/Handy_(song)" title="Handy (song)">
        Handy
       </a>

      </li>
