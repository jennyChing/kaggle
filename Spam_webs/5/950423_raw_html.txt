     <p>
      <b>
       Vincent Eugene Craddock
      </b>
      (February 11, 1935  October 12, 1971), known as
      <b>
       Gene Vincent
      </b>
      , was an American musician who pioneered the styles of
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">
       rock and roll
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly" title="Rockabilly">
       rockabilly
      </a>
      . His 1956 top ten hit with his Blue Caps, "
      <a href="/wiki/Be-Bop-A-Lula" title="Be-Bop-A-Lula">
       Be-Bop-A-Lula
      </a>
      ", is considered a significant early example of rockabilly.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc8_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-pc8-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He is a member of both the
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">
       Rock and Roll Hall of Fame
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rockabilly Hall of Fame">
       Rockabilly Hall of Fame
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_Vincent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Vincent Eugene Craddock was born in
      <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">
       Norfolk
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_(U.S._state)" title="Virginia (U.S. state)">
       Virginia
      </a>
      . His musical influences included
      <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">
       country
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">
       rhythm and blues
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">
       gospel music
      </a>
      . He showed his first real interest in music while his family lived in Munden Point,
      <a href="/wiki/Princess_Anne_County,_Virginia" title="Princess Anne County, Virginia">
       Princess Anne County
      </a>
      (now
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach" title="Virginia Beach">
       Virginia Beach
      </a>
      ), Virginia, near the
      <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">
       North Carolina
      </a>
      line, where they ran a country store. He received his first guitar at the age of twelve as a gift from a friend.
     </p>
     <p>
      His father, Ezekiah Jackson Craddock, volunteered to serve in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">
       U.S. Coast Guard
      </a>
      and patrolled American coastal waters to protect Allied shipping against German
      <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">
       U-boats
      </a>
      during
      <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
       World War II
      </a>
      . His mother, Mary Louise Craddock, maintained a general store at Munden Point. Craddock's parents moved the family and opened a general store and sailors' tailoring shop in Norfolk.
     </p>
     <p>
      Having spent his youth in the Norfolk area, Craddock dropped out of school at seventeen and enlisted in the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">
       United States Navy
      </a>
      in 1952. Craddock's parents signed the forms allowing him to join the Navy. He completed boot camp and joined the fleet as a crewman aboard the fleet oiler
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Chukawan_(AO-100)" title="USS Chukawan (AO-100)">
       USS
       <i>
        Chukawan
       </i>
      </a>
      although he spent two weeks training period in the repair ship
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Amphion_(AR-13)" title="USS Amphion (AR-13)">
       USS
       <i>
        Amphion
       </i>
      </a>
      before returning to the
      <i>
       Chukawan
      </i>
      . Craddock never saw combat but completed a
      <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">
       Korean War
      </a>
      deployment. He sailed home from Korean waters aboard battleship
      <a href="/wiki/USS_Wisconsin_(BB-64)" title="USS Wisconsin (BB-64)">
       USS
       <i>
        Wisconsin
       </i>
      </a>
      , but was not part of the ship's company.
     </p>
     <p>
      Craddock planned a career in the Navy and, in 1955, used his $612 re-enlistment bonus to buy a new
      <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Engineering" title="Triumph Engineering">
       Triumph
      </a>
      motorbike. In July 1955, while in Norfolk, a motorcycle crash shattered his left leg. He refused to have it amputated. The leg was saved, but the crash left him with a limp and pain. He wore a steel sheath around the leg
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      for the rest of his life. Most accounts relate the accident as the fault of a drunk driver who struck him, although some claim Craddock had been riding drunk. Years later in some of his professional music bios, there is no mention of an accident, but it was claimed that he was wounded in combat in Korea.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_Rock_.27N.27_Roll_Years_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-NME_Rock_.27N.27_Roll_Years-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He spent time in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Naval_Hospital" title="Portsmouth Naval Hospital">
       Portsmouth Naval Hospital
      </a>
      and was medically discharged from the Navy shortly thereafter.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_music_career">
       Early music career
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_Vincent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early music career">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Craddock became involved in the local music scene in Norfolk. He changed his name to Gene Vincent, and formed a rockabilly band called the Blue Caps (a term used in reference to enlisted sailors in the U.S. Navy).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The band included
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ervin_Williams&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ervin Williams (page does not exist)">
       Willie Williams
      </a>
      on rhythm guitar, Jack Neal on upright bass, Dickie Harrell on drums, and lead guitarist,
      <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Gallup" title="Cliff Gallup">
       Cliff Gallup
      </a>
      . He and his band were named Gene Vincent and His Blue Caps. He also collaborated with another rising musician,
      <a href="/wiki/Jay_Chevalier" title="Jay Chevalier">
       Jay Chevalier
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Rapides_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Rapides Parish, Louisiana">
       Rapides Parish
      </a>
      , Louisiana.
     </p>
     <p>
      Gene Vincent and His Blue Caps soon gained a reputation playing in various
      <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">
       country
      </a>
      bars in his native Norfolk, Virginia. There, they won a talent contest organized by local radio DJ "Sheriff Tex" Davis, who became his manager.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Biggest_hits">
       Biggest hits
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_Vincent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biggest hits">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1956 he wrote "
      <a href="/wiki/Be-Bop-A-Lula" title="Be-Bop-A-Lula">
       Be-Bop-A-Lula
      </a>
      ", which drew comparisons to
      <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">
       Elvis Presley
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pc8_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-pc8-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and which
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">
        Rolling Stone
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine lists as No. 102 or 103 on
      <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone%27s_500_Greatest_Songs_of_All_Time" title="Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Songs of All Time">
       Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Songs of All Time
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rs-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Local radio DJ "Sheriff Tex" Davis arranged for this to be demo-ed and this secured him a contract with
      <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">
       Capitol Records
      </a>
      . He signed a publishing contract with
      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Lowery_(record_producer)" title="Bill Lowery (record producer)">
       Bill Lowery
      </a>
      of The Lowery Group of music publishers in Atlanta, Georgia. "Be-Bop-A-Lula" was not on Vincent's first album and was picked by Capitol producer Ken Nelson as the B side of his first single. Prior to the release of the single, Lowery pressed promotional copies of "Be-Bop-A-Lula" and sent them to radio stations throughout the country. By the time Capitol released the single, "Be-Bop-A-Lula" had already gained attention from the public and radio DJs. The song was picked up and played by other U.S. radio stations (obscuring the original "A-side" song), and became a hit and launched Vincent as a rock 'n' roll star.
     </p>
     <p>
      After "Be-Bop-A-Lula" became a hit (peaking at No. 5 and spending 20 weeks on the
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      Pop Chart, and No. 5 and 17 weeks in the
      <i>
       Cashbox
      </i>
      Chart), Gene Vincent and His Blue Caps were unable to follow it up with the same level of commercial success, but released critically acclaimed songs like "Race with the Devil" (No. 96 in
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      and No. 50 in
      <i>
       Cashbox
      </i>
      ) and "
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bluejean_Bop&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bluejean Bop (page does not exist)">
       Bluejean Bop
      </a>
      " (No. 49 in
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Cliff Gallup left the band in 1956 and Russell Williford joined as the new guitarist for the Blue Caps. Williford played and toured Canada with Vincent in late 1956 and left in early 1957. Gallup came back to do the next album, left again, then Russell came back and exited before Johnny Meeks entered the band.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The group had another hit with 1957's "
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lotta_Lovin%27&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lotta Lovin' (page does not exist)">
       Lotta Lovin'
      </a>
      " (highest position No. 13 and spending 19 weeks in
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      , and No. 17 and 17 weeks in
      <i>
       Cashbox
      </i>
      ). Vincent was awarded gold records for two million sales of "Be-Bop-A-Lula" and 1.5million sales of "Lotta Lovin'".
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The same year he toured the east coast of Australia with
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Richard" title="Little Richard">
       Little Richard
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cochran" title="Eddie Cochran">
       Eddie Cochran
      </a>
      , drawing audiences totaling 72,000 to their Sydney Stadium concerts. Vincent also made an appearance in the film,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Girl_Can%27t_Help_It" title="The Girl Can't Help It">
        The Girl Can't Help It
       </a>
      </i>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Jayne_Mansfield" title="Jayne Mansfield">
       Jayne Mansfield
      </a>
      , performing "Be-Bop-A-Lula" with The Blue Caps in a rehearsal room.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Dance to the Bop" was released by Capitol records on October 28, 1957.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rcs-discography1_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-rcs-discography1-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On November 17, 1957 Vincent and His Blue Caps performed the song on the nationally-broadcast
      <i>
       Ed Sullivan Show
      </i>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The song spent nine weeks on the
      <i>
       Billboard
      </i>
      charts and peaked at No. 23 on January 23, 1958 and No. 36 and 8 weeks in
      <i>
       Cashbox
      </i>
      , and would be Vincent's last American hit single.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The song was used in the movie
      <i>
       Hot Rod Gang
      </i>
      for a dance rehearsal scene featuring dancers doing
      <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Swing" title="West Coast Swing">
       West Coast Swing
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rcs-discography1_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-rcs-discography1-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Vincent and His Blue Caps also appeared several times on 'Town Hall Party', California's largest country music barn dance held at the Town Hall in Compton, California. 'Town Hall Party' drew in excess of 2,800 paid admissions each Friday and Saturday with room for 1,200 dancers. The show was also on from 8:30 to 9:30pm over the NBC Radio network. It was also shown on KTTV, channel 11 from 10pm to 1am on Saturday nights.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Appearances were on October 25, 1958, as well as July 25 and November 7, 1959. Songs performed were: "Be-Bop-A-Lula", "High Blood Pressure", "
      <a href="/wiki/Rip_It_Up_(Little_Richard_song)" title="Rip It Up (Little Richard song)">
       Rip It Up
      </a>
      ", "Dance to the Bop", "You Win Again", "For Your Precious Love", "Rocky Road Blues", "Pretty Pearly", "High School Confidential", "
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over_The_Rainbow" title="Over The Rainbow">
       Over The Rainbow
      </a>
      ", "
      <a href="/wiki/Roll_Over_Beethoven" title="Roll Over Beethoven">

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">
       Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_Vincent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A dispute with the
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">
       US tax authorities
      </a>
      and the American Musicians' Union over payments to his band and his having sold the band's equipment to pay a tax bill led him to leave the US and try his hand in Europe.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On December 15, 1959, Vincent appeared on
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Good_(producer)" title="Jack Good (producer)">
       Jack Good
      </a>
      's TV show
      <i>
       Boy Meets Girl
      </i>
      , his first appearance in England.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      He wore black leather, gloves, and a medallion, and stood in a hunched posture. Good is credited with the transformation of Vincent's image. After the TV appearance he toured France, the Netherlands, Germany, and the UK performing in his US stage clothes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      On April 16, 1960, while on tour in the UK, Vincent,
      <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cochran" title="Eddie Cochran">
       Eddie Cochran
      </a>
      , and songwriter
      <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Sheeley" title="Sharon Sheeley">
       Sharon Sheeley
      </a>
      were involved in a high-speed traffic accident in a private hire taxi in
      <a href="/wiki/Chippenham" title="Chippenham">
       Chippenham
      </a>
      , Wiltshire. Vincent broke his ribs and collarbone and further damaged his weakened leg. Sheeley suffered a broken pelvis. Cochran, who had been thrown from the vehicle, suffered serious brain injuries and died the next day. Vincent returned to the States after the accident.
     </p>
     <p>
      Promoter
      <a href="/wiki/Don_Arden" title="Don Arden">
       Don Arden
      </a>
      had Vincent return to the UK in 1961 to do an extensive tour in theatres and ballrooms with Chris Wayne and
      <a href="/wiki/The_Echoes" title="The Echoes">
       the Echoes
      </a>
      . Due to the overwhelming success of this tour, Vincent subsequently moved to England in 1963. The accompanying band,
      <a href="/wiki/Sounds_Incorporated" title="Sounds Incorporated">
       Sounds Incorporated
      </a>
      , a six-piece outfit which included three saxophones, guitar, bass and drums, later went on to play with
      <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">
       the Beatles
      </a>
      at their
      <a href="/wiki/Shea_Stadium" title="Shea Stadium">
       Shea Stadium
      </a>
      concert.
     </p>
     <p>
      He toured the UK again in 1963 with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Outlaws_(UK_band)" title="The Outlaws (UK band)">
       the Outlaws
      </a>
      , featuring future
      <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">
       Deep Purple
      </a>
      guitar player
      <a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Blackmore" title="Ritchie Blackmore">
       Ritchie Blackmore
      </a>
      , as a backing band. Vincent's alcohol problems marred the tour, resulting in problems both on stage and with the band and management.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      Vincent's attempts to re-establish his American career in
      <a href="/wiki/Folk_rock" title="Folk rock">
       folk rock
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Country_rock" title="Country rock">
       country rock
      </a>
      proved unsuccessful; he is remembered today for recordings of the 1950s and early 1960s that appeared on the
      <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">
       Capitol
      </a>
      label. In the early sixties, he also put out tracks on EMI's
      <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Graphophone_Company" title="Columbia Graphophone Company">
       Columbia
      </a>
      label (the British label, not the U.S. CBS/Columbia), including a cover of
      <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Alexander" title="Arthur Alexander">
       Arthur Alexander
      </a>
      's "Where Have You Been All My Life?". A backing band called the Shouts joined him.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1966 and 1967, in the States, he recorded for
      <a href="/wiki/Challenge_Records_(1950s)" title="Challenge Records (1950s)">
       Challenge Records
      </a>
      , backed by ex-members of
      <a href="/wiki/The_Champs" title="The Champs">
       the Champs
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Glen_Campbell" title="Glen Campbell">
       Glen Campbell
      </a>
      . Challenge released three singles in the US, and the UK
      <a href="/wiki/London_Records" title="London Records">
       London
      </a>
      label released two singles and collected recordings on to an LP,
      <i>
       Gene Vincent
      </i>
      , on the UK London label in 1967. Although well received, none sold well.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1968 in a hotel in Germany, Gene Vincent tried to shoot
      <a href="/wiki/Gary_Glitter" title="Gary Glitter">
       Gary Glitter
      </a>
      : he fired several shots and missed and a frightened Glitter left the country the next day.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1969, he recorded the album
      <i>
       I'm Back and I'm Proud
      </i>
      for long-time fan
      <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">
       John Peel
      </a>
      's
      <a href="/wiki/Dandelion_Records" title="Dandelion Records">
       Dandelion label
      </a>
      , produced by
      <a href="/wiki/Kim_Fowley" title="Kim Fowley">
       Kim Fowley
      </a>
      with arrangements by
      <a href="/wiki/The_Byrds" title="The Byrds">
       the Byrds
      </a>
      '
      <a href="/wiki/Skip_Battin" title="Skip Battin">
       Skip Battin
      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt" title="Linda Ronstadt">
       Linda Ronstadt
      </a>
      . He recorded two other albums for the
      <a href="/wiki/Kama_Sutra_Records" title="Kama Sutra Records">
       Kama Sutra
      </a>
      label, reissued on one CD by Rev-Ola in March 2008.
     </p>

      On his 1969 tour of the UK he was backed by the
      <a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Angels_(band)" title="The Wild Angels (band)">
       Wild Angels
      </a>
      , a British band who had worked at the
      <a href="/wiki/Royal_Albert_Hall" title="Royal Albert Hall">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haley_%26_His_Comets" title="Bill Haley &amp; His Comets">

      </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Duane_Eddy" title="Duane Eddy">
       Duane Eddy
      </a>
      . Because of pressure from his ex-wife, the

      <a href="/wiki/Don_Arden" title="Don Arden">
       Don Arden
      </a>
      , Vincent returned to the US.

       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      His final US recordings were four tracks for Rockin' Ronny Weiser's Rolling Rock label, a few weeks before his death. These were released on a compilation album of tribute songs, including "Say Mama" by his daughter, Melody Jean Vincent, accompanied by Johnny Meeks on guitar. He later recorded four tracks (released years later as
      <i>
       The Last Session
      </i>
      ) in Britain in October 1971 as part of his last tour. He was backed by Richard Cole (from the Bluecaps) and Kansas Hook. They recorded five tracks at the BBC studios in
      <a href="/wiki/Maida_Vale" title="Maida Vale">
       Maida Vale
      </a>
      , London, for
      <a href="/wiki/Johnnie_Walker_(DJ)" title="Johnnie Walker (DJ)">
       Johnnie Walker's
      </a>
      radio show. He managed one show at the Garrick Night Club in
      <a href="/wiki/Leigh,_Greater_Manchester" title="Leigh, Greater Manchester">
       Leigh
      </a>
      , Lancashire, and two shows at the Wookey Hollow Club in
      <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">
       Liverpool
      </a>
      on October 3 and 4 before his health gave out. Vincent returned to the US and died a few days later. Four of these tracks were later released on the BBC's own label pre-fix BEEB001 called
      <i>
       The Last Session
      </i>
      ; this includes a version of "Say Mama". The four tracks are now on Vincent's
      <i>
       White Lightning
      </i>
      album.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">
       Death and legacy
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gene_Vincent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Vincent died on October 12, 1971 from a ruptured
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomach_ulcer" title="Stomach ulcer">
       stomach ulcer
      </a>
      while visiting his father in California,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_Rock_.27N.27_Roll_Years_3-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-NME_Rock_.27N.27_Roll_Years-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is interred in the Eternal Valley Memorial Park,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newhall,_California" title="Newhall, California">
       Newhall, California
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      He was the first inductee into the
      <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rockabilly Hall of Fame">
       Rockabilly Hall of Fame
      </a>
      upon its formation in 1997.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The following year he was inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">
       Rock and Roll Hall of Fame
      </a>
      . Vincent has a star on the
      <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">
       Hollywood Walk of Fame
      </a>
      at 1749 N. Vine Street. In 2012, his band, the Blue Caps, were retroactively inducted into the
      <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">
       Rock and Roll Hall of Fame
      </a>
      by a special committee, alongside Vincent. On Tuesday, September 23, 2003 Vincent was honored with a Norfolk's Legends of Music Walk of Fame bronze star embedded in the Granby Street sidewalk.
     </p>
