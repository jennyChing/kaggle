                    <p>
                     At 34, my life was on a great trajectory. I was four years
into a wonderful marriage. I had an exciting career at a crazy, chaotic startup
in San Francisco. The year was 1999.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     An avid runner who had just run two half marathons, I
enjoyed traveling, cooking and watching movies. My husband and I were also
ready to start a family. We hoped to have a baby within the next year.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     In short, life was pretty great!
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Then, everything changed.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I still remember the morning when I first became aware that
something was not normal. I was going about my usual routine, when I was
distracted by a mild itch on my left breast.
As I scratched the area lightly, I felt a small, but palpable nodule to
the right of my nipple. Id never noticed anything like it before and ran my
fingers over the area some more, hoping I had just imagined it. But, there it
was.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     What it was I did not know, but I do remember feeling a
slight panic as my mind considered the possibilities.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I willed the lump to go away, and took a wait and see approach
for the rest of the day. I tried to keep my mind distracted, but inevitably, my
hand would wander to that area in question, and the lump remained. I decided to
ask my husband for his opinion, hoping he would reassure me and tell me that I
was worrying for nothing. But as he felt
the lump, I saw the change in his expression and heard the measured concern in
his voice as he said, You should probably just get it checked out, just in
case.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Still, I tried to will my mind from wandering to the worst-case
scenario. After all, I had no breast cancer in my family, I was healthy, I
didnt smoke, and I hardly drank.
                    </p>
                    <div class="post-title">
                     <div class="post_inner">
                      <span>
                       
                      </span>
                      My
heart raced, and the lump in my throat threatened to stop my breathing. I felt
scared, alone, and cold.
                      <span class="quote-position">
                       
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="share">
                      <a href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text=The lump in my throat threatened to stop my breathing. I felt scared, alone, and cold. @healthline http://bit.ly/1tdCHCA" target="_blank">
                       <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                        <img alt="Share" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/share_this_quote.png"/>

                    <p>
                     My OB/GYN tried to reassure me that it was most likely a
cyst given my young age, but she performed a fine needle aspiration to further
investigate. A week of anxious waiting, during which I tried to go back to
life as normal, ended with a call letting me know that the test results were
inconclusive. There were a few atypical cells in the fluid.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Atypical. That cant be good, I thought. A mammogram was
recommended, but the results didnt reveal anything.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The radiologist recommended we do an ultrasound for further
testing, simply because young womens breast tissue is dense and sometimes a
lump will not show up on the mammogram. I found myself in another room, in
another area of the hospital, lying on a table in the darkened room, while a
technician silently ran the ultrasound wand over the breast in question. I
tried to quiet my mind, tried to think of anything that would distract me from
my scary thoughts.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     And then:
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The technician said, Oh, I see the mass. It is rather
large. And with that, he left the room temporarily to supposedly consult with
someone.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     My heart raced, and the lump in my throat threatened to stop
my breathing. I felt scared, alone, and cold. Tears rolled down my face as I
lay there silently waiting.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Although they tried to tell me not to worry and that the
mass could be benign, I knew the other possibility existed. Because of the
results, I was now told to make an appointment with a breast surgeon. It felt
like I was being bounced from one professional to the next.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Fast forward to my first surgery, when I opted to have the
lump removed for testing. I received the
pathology report within a day, after my husbands cousin, whos a doctor,
called in a favor. My worst fears were manifested on that report with all kinds
of foreign, terrifying words. Invasive ductal carcinoma, 2.1 cm tumor. High
grade. Estrogen receptor  negative. Progesterone receptor  negative. HER2 
negative.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Translation: I had breast cancer.
                    </p>

                      Coping with a
New Reality

                    <p>
                     I felt like my world had come crashing down around me. The first
silent thought that I was afraid to utter aloud was: Am I going to die? As I
fought through a haze of tears, and made all the difficult calls to family to
share the news, I also felt a kernel of strength growing inside me. Even though
I had no choice in getting breast cancer, I now had a choice with respect to
what to do about it. And I was going to take back control in any way that I
could.
                    </p>
                    <p id="ad">
                     <a href="https://www.facebook.com/healthlinelivingwithbreastcancer" target="_blank">
                      <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                       <img src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/topic_centers/Cancer/news-clinical-trials-long-form-2/Breast-cancer-news-facebook-house-ad-longform_728x90.jpg"/>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     My life became inundated with appointments. I had meetings
with surgeons both general and reconstructive, anesthesiologists, a
nutritionist, a counselor, and a wig specialist. I had more tests: a bone
density, a CAT scan, and blood work. There was a lot of information to process.
But I had my own support team, which included my steadfast husband and his
cousin, the doctor, who accompanied me to the majority of my appointments and
helped ask questions my own frazzled brain couldnt process. In between all of
this, I tried to juggle the demands of work. This was challenging, but it also
gave me a reprieve from all the cancer stuff. Thankfully, my employer and my
coworkers were extremely supportive and gave me the flexibility to do what I
needed.
                    </p>
                    <div class="post-title">
                     <div class="post_inner">
                      <span>
                       
                      </span>
                      I never really thought about my attachment to my breast before. But now, faced with losing it, I worried that I would feel less like a woman.
                      <span class="quote-position">
                       
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="share">
                      <a href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text= But now, faced with losing [my breast], I worried that I would feel less like a woman.@healthline http://bit.ly/1tdCHCA" target="_blank">
                       <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                        <img alt="Share" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/share_this_quote.png"/>
                       </span>

                    </div>
                    <p>
                     Because I had a triple negative breast cancer, a
particularly aggressive form, I felt the right option for me was to be equally
aggressive. I opted for a mastectomy with reconstruction. I never really
thought about my attachment to my breast before. But now, faced with losing it,
I worried that I would feel less like a woman. But I also knew I had to do
whatever I felt was necessary to give me the best possible chance of survival.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I dont remember all that much about the surgery, but I do
recall feeling apprehensive about looking at my chest when I was first recovering
in my hospital room. I dont know what I was expecting, but because the
reconstructive surgeon had also put in a tissue expander following the
mastectomy, it actually looked like I still had a breast. It was bandaged, with
drains taped to the side, but it wasnt the gaping crater that my nervous mind
had conjured up.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Three more days in the hospital, followed by a week before
the drains were removed, and I was done with the first part of my
treatment.
                    </p>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="div3">
                   <div class="header">
                    <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                     <img alt="IV-photo" class="from_left" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/topic_centers/BreastCancer-1/1190x350-Img3-Living_with_Breast_Cancer.jpg" title="IV-photo"/>
                    </span>
                    <div class="header-tite3">
                     <div class="font2">
                      part 3
                     </div>
                     <h2>
                      The Biggest Fear
                     </h2>
                    </div>
                   </div>
                   <div class="block-body">
                    <p>
                     Next came the part I had probably feared the most: chemotherapy.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I would receive two drugs, Adriamycin and Cytoxan, in four
sessions over the course of 12 weeks.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     My husband accompanied me to my first appointment. The
infusion center was quite spacious and there were many nurses running about,
attending to patients who were there to receive treatment. There were lots of
big comfy chairs in separate stations with patients sitting in them, IVs attached
to their arms or ports, connected to IV bags containing various colored
solutions. As I settled into my chair and the nurses searched for a good
vein, I looked around me and noticed I was younger than most people in the
room.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     They dripped various drugs into my IV to prep me, and then
the nurse came to specially administer the first drug: Adriamycin. It came in a
very long, syringe-like tube and it was the color of cranberry juice. Because
of its toxic nature, a nurse had to sit there and slowly inject it into my IV
line, constantly checking to make sure the line was running well and that there
was no leakage into the surrounding tissue.
The Cytoxan was given via a slow drip, and I had another few hours to
read, chat with my husband, or watch something on TV. With no idea what to
expect from my first session, I recall feeling a bit off, but not bad. I
actually left the infusion center feeling more confident about the whole
experience, and even suggested to my husband that we grab a Jamba Juice and see
an afternoon matinee to get my mind off things.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     About halfway through the movie, I started feeling much
worse. Nauseous. We walked out of the theater and headed home.
                    </p>
                    <div class="post-title">
                     <div class="post_inner">
                      <span>
                       
                      </span>
                      There
were times when walking two blocks took a lot out of me.
                      <span class="quote-position">
                       
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="share">
                      <a href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text= There were times when walking two blocks took a lot out of me. @healthline http://bit.ly/1tdCHCA" target="_blank">
                       <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                        <img alt="Share" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/share_this_quote.png"/>
                       </span>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <p>
                     Those first 48 hours were extremely challenging, as the
nausea was overwhelming. At this point,
the anti-nausea medications werent even effective since I couldnt keep them
down. I remember telling my husband, who was desperately trying to find ways to
keep me comfortable, that I didnt know if I could handle any more chemo. The
vomiting thankfully stopped after those 48 hours, but I still felt queasy for
the remainder of that first week and spent most of my time in bed.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The following week, I felt better, but slightly tired. I got
up and tried to take walks outside to get fresh air when I could. By the third
week, I was feeling more like my old self, had a normal appetite, enjoyed
having visitors, and even got a lot of work done. But then it would be time to
head in for my next infusion and begin the cycle again.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <a href="http://www.healthline.com/health/breast-cancer/ceo-of-komen-on-power-of-awareness" target="_blank">
                      Related content: The CEO of Komen Defends Breast Cancer Awareness 
                     </a>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I learned a lot from that first treatment, and made sure I
took the maximum dosage of anti-nausea medications to help prep me on subsequent
treatments. Though I still dealt with nausea, it was nothing like that first
time, and for that, I was thankful.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The cumulative effects of the treatments left me tired, but I tried to be active
when I had the energy. I also volunteered to participate in a study that tested
the effects of exercise on reducing the side effects of chemotherapy.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The hardest part during this phase of treatment was not being able to be as active
as I was used to. There were times when walking two blocks took a lot out of
me. For someone accustomed to long distance running, this was frustrating and
sad. But I accepted that rest was the priority and that when this was all over,
I would get back to doing what I loved.
                    </p>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="div4">
                   <div class="header">
                    <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                     <img alt="hair_loss" class="from_left" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/topic_centers/BreastCancer-1/1190x350-Img4-Living_with_Breast_Cancer.jpg" title="hair_loss"/>
                    </span>
                    <div class="header-tite4">
                     <div class="font2">
                      part 4
                     </div>
                     <h2>
                      Tears, Hair Loss, and a New Me
                     </h2>
                    </div>
                   </div>
                   <div class="block-body">
                    <p>
                     One of the other things I knew Id be dealing with during
chemotherapy was hair loss. I had chosen to cut my long hair short prior to
starting treatment. My hairdresser had put me in touch with someone who made
wigs, so I had one made out of my own hair. I didnt know how quickly I would
lose my hair, but between the first and second treatments, I began noticing an
increasing number of strands coming off between my fingers whenever I would wash
my hair in the shower. By the time I completed my second treatment, it was more
noticeable and I decided that it might be best to simply shave the rest of it to
simplify things.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     My husband tried to lighten the mood by renting
                     <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YwApg-XlBlw" target="_blank">
                      <em>
                       G.I. Jane
                      </em>
                     </a>
                     for me, because it contained a
powerful scene in which Demi Moores character shaves her head. I channeled my inner
Demi as my mom and my husband went to work with the shaver. As I watched what
was left of my hair fall away, I was stunned. I stared at my bald reflection in
the mirror. I looked radically different. I looked like a cancer patient.
                    </p>
                    <div class="post-title">
                     <div class="post_inner">
                      <span>
                       
                      </span>
                      As I
watched what was left of my hair fall away, I was stunned I looked like a
cancer patient.
                      <span class="quote-position">
                       
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="share">
                      <a href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text=As I watched what was left of my hair fall away, I was stunned I looked like a cancer patient. @healthline http://bit.ly/1tdCHCA" target="_blank">
                       <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                        <img alt="Share" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/share_this_quote.png"/>
                       </span>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <p>
                     Even as my husband and my mom tried to make me feel better
by telling me I had a beautifully shaped head with no weird lumps or bumps, I
shed tears knowing another part of me had been lost.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Shedding those tears was cathartic, and even though I felt I
was being vain for worrying about how I looked, I also knew it was important
for me to allow myself to feel all the emotions associated with what I was
going through.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Some days I felt angry.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Why me?
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Some days I felt sad.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Will I make it through
this?
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Some days I felt isolated.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Will life ever be
normal again?
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Some days I felt appreciative. I have the best family,
friends, and a team of docs a girl could hope for!
                    </p>
                    <p id="ad">
                     <a href="https://www.facebook.com/healthlinelivingwithbreastcancer" target="_blank">
                      <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                       <img src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/topic_centers/Cancer/news-clinical-trials-long-form-2/Breast-cancer-news-facebook-house-ad-longform_728x90.jpg"/>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I even found moments to laugh. I remember one occasion when
I was out shopping with my mom. We came across a group of Buddhist monks in the
store, dressed in robes and sporting shaven heads. I turned to my mom and said,
Do you dare me to whip off my wig right now and go over to them and ask if I
could join them?
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I made it through the rest of my chemotherapy treatments and
although my body felt weaker, I also felt stronger mentally for having endured.
I was one bad, bald superhero, and I was determined that I was not going down
without a serious fight!
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     As my body began the healing process from the onslaught of
toxic warfare, I still had one more thing left to take care of. Considering
everything Id already been through, picking out new boobs sounded a lot more
fun than my mastectomy or chemo.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     A few weeks prior to the completion of chemo, and again a
few weeks afterward, I made weekly visits to my reconstructive surgeon, who
injected increasing amounts of saline into my tissue expander through a port
under the skin. This was done to help with skin growth to allow for placement
of the permanent saline implant. About three months after the end of chemo, I
went in to have my final surgery to replace the tissue expander with my implant
and have a small one put in on the other side for balance.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Just like that, I had new breasts.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <span class="imageAreaBordered rightAligned">
                      <img alt="Alisa Brozinsky breast cancer survivor" height="500" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/topic_centers/BreastCancer-1/400x500-Alisa%20Yuko%20Brozinsky_039_crop.png" style="float: right; margin: 10px 15px;" title="Alisa Brozinsky" width="400"/>
                     </span>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     By this time, my hair had also started to grow back and I
was sporting a chic, new look, and a new figure. To say that I was glad to be
done with all my procedures and treatments would be an understatement. I was
ecstatic! But I wasn't prepared for some other emotions I also began to
experience during this post-treatment phase. All of a sudden I went from regularly seeing my team of doctors and
nurses to being on my own and seeing them once every three months for those first
two years post-diagnosis. No one was checking on me, no one was administering
some life-saving drugs. While I was in treatment, I was actively fighting the
disease. Now I felt like I was in a passive, wait-and-see mode.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Was I really OK?
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Did the treatments
guarantee that the cancer was gone?
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     These feelings of uncertainty kept creeping into my mind
just as I was trying to get back to my normal life.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I soon realized that normal meant something different to me.
This was my new normal. Having cancer had changed me. On one hand, it made me
aware of my vulnerability and made me consider my own mortality. On the other,
it gave me the gift of appreciating my health, my life. I would never be the
same person I was before cancer came into my life, and it would take time to
adjust, but I knew that it was okay. That even though I would live with some
fear of recurrence, of having the other shoe drop, that I would also live my life
with a renewed appreciation for each new day, good and bad  that I would live
in the now.
                    </p>
                   </div>
                  </div>
                  <div class="div5">
                   <div class="header">
                    <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                     <img alt="marathon" class="from_left" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/topic_centers/BreastCancer-1/1190x350-Img5-Living_with_Breast_Cancer.jpg" title="marathon"/>
                    </span>
                    <div class="header-tite4">
                     <div class="font2">
                      part 5
                     </div>
                     <h2>
                      Running Away from Cancer
                     </h2>
                    </div>
                   </div>
                   <div class="block-body">
                    <p>
                     Today, 15 years removed from my diagnosis, it all seems a
bit surreal. Ive been active in my efforts to help those diagnosed with breast
cancer through my work as a peer support counselor with UCSFs Cancer Resource
Center, and Ive been an active advocate by fundraising through various breast
cancer walks. While I believe these walks help increase awareness and raise
much needed funds, I am also sensitive, especially as a survivor, to the fact
that there is still a lot of work to be done.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Awareness is but one small part, and much more needs to be
done on researching causes as well as treating metastatic disease. During
October, we are awash in a sea of pink in support of breast cancer. But breast
cancer is not pretty and pink. It is dark, serious, and scary. Too many
companies tout pink products, yet at the same time, market products that
contain cancer-causing chemicals or dont support a healthy lifestyle. Case in
point: pink KFC buckets.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     While I believe the intent to increase awareness is a good
thing, we should not be blinded to the reality that not all that is pink in
October makes a real difference when it comes to the disease. This is the main
reason Ive made different choices when it comes to my own personal advocacy
for the disease. This month, Ill walk 55 miles in the
                     <a href="http://www.immediateimpactwalk.org/">
                      Immediate Impact Breast Cancer Walk
                     </a>
                     ,
where at least 80 percent of the funds raised will go straight to three local (and
one national) breast cancer organizations, with a focus on research and patient
assistance.
                    </p>
                    <div class="post-title">
                     <div class="post_inner">
                      <span>
                       
                      </span>
                      But
breast cancer is not pretty and pink. It is dark, serious, and scary.
                      <span class="quote-position">
                       
                      </span>
                     </div>
                     <div class="share">
                      <a href="http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text=But breast cancer is not pretty and pink. It is dark, serious, and scary. @healthline http://bit.ly/1tdCHCA" target="_blank">
                       <span class="imageAreaBordered ">
                        <img alt="Share" src="http://www.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/share_this_quote.png"/>
                       </span>
                      </a>
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <p>
                     As time progresses, Ive been able to recoup some of that
pre-cancer, carefree feeling inside, and Im now back to being active, having even
fulfilled my dream of running my first full marathon.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     But Ill never forget what I went through.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The physical scars still exist to remind me. The memories
still fresh when I recount my story to others, the fear that the cancer may
come back. But I now have a 10-year-old daughter who serves as a reminder to me
of life moving forward, of new adventures and new opportunities Im lucky to
have. And I plan to make the most of it!
                    </p>
                   </div>
                  </div>
