     <p>
      This page lists English translations of notable
      <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
       Latin
      </a>
      phrases, such as
      <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
       <i>
        veni vidi vici
       </i>
      </span>
      and
      <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
       <i>
        et cetera
       </i>
      </span>
      . Some of the phrases are themselves translations of
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_Greek_phrases" title="List of Greek phrases">
       Greek phrases
      </a>
      , as
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
       Greek
      </a>
      rhetoric and literature reached its peak centuries before the rise of
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
       ancient Rome
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        This list covers the letter
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        . See
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_Latin_phrases" title="List of Latin phrases">
         List of Latin phrases
        </a>
        for the main list.
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>

        <b>
         id est

          (

           i.e.
          </i>
          )
         </small>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        that is
       </td>
       <td>
        "That is (to say)" in the sense of "that means" and "which means", or "in other words", or sometimes "in this case", depending on the context; may be followed by a comma, or not, depending on style (American English and British English respectively).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exempli_gratia" title="Exempli gratia">
         e.g.
        </a>
        ).
       </td>

       <td>
        that which generally happens
       </td>
       <td>

        <a href="/wiki/Ides_of_March" title="Ides of March">
         Ides of March
        </a>
       </td>
       <td>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">
         Roman calendar
        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
         Julius Caesar
        </a>
        was assassinated in 44 BC; the term has come to be used as a metaphor for impending doom.
       </td>
      </tr>

       <td>
        By that very fact
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="ira_deorum">
        </span>
        <b>
         ira deorum
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        wrath of the gods
       </td>
       <td>
        Like the vast majority of inhabitants of the ancient world, the
        <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
         ancient Romans
        </a>
        practiced
        <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">
         pagan
        </a>
        rituals, believing it important to achieve a state of
        <i>
         pax deorum
        </i>
        (peace of the gods) instead of
        <i>
         ira deorum
        </i>
        (wrath of the gods): earthquakes, floods, famine, etc.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="ira_furor_brevis_est">
        </span>
        <b>
         ira furor brevis est
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        Wrath (anger) is but a brief madness
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="ita_vero">
        </span>
        <b>
         ita vero
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        thus indeed
       </td>
       <td>
        A useful phrase, as the Romans had no word for "yes", preferring to respond to questions with the affirmative or negative of the question (e.g., "Are you hungry?" was answered by "I am hungry" or "I am not hungry", not "Yes" or "No).
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Ite,_missa_est" title="Ite, missa est">
          ite, missa est
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        Go, it is the dismissal
       </td>
       <td>
        Loosely: "You have been dismissed". Concluding words addressed to the people in the
        <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">
         Mass
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">
         Roman Rite
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          <span>
           [
          </span>
          4
          <span>
           ]
          </span>
         </a>
        </sup>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iter_legis">
        </span>
        <b>
         iter legis
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        The path of the law
       </td>
       <td>
        The path a law takes from its conception to its implementation.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iucunda_memoria_est_praeteritorum_malorum">
        </span>
        <b>
         iucunda memoria est praeteritorum malorum
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        Pleasant is the memory of past troubles
       </td>
       <td>
        <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
         Cicero
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/De_finibus_bonorum_et_malorum" title="De finibus bonorum et malorum">
          De finibus bonorum et malorum
         </a>
        </i>
        2, 32, 105.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iugulare_mortuos">
        </span>
        <b>
         iugulare mortuos
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        to cut the throat of corpses
       </td>
       <td>
        From Gerhard Gerhards' (14661536) [better known as Erasmus] collection of annotated Adagia (1508). It can mean attacking the work or personality of deceased person. Alternatively, it can be used to describe criticism of an individual already heavily criticised by others.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iuncta_iuvant">
        </span>
        <span id="juncta_juvant">
        </span>
        <b>
         iuncta iuvant
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        together they strive
       </td>
       <td>
        also spelled
        <i>
         juncta juvant
        </i>
        ; from the legal principle
        <i>
         quae non valeant singula, iuncta iuvant
        </i>
        ("What is without value on its own, helps when joined")
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Iura_novit_curia" title="Iura novit curia">
          iura novit curia
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        the court knows the law
       </td>
       <td>
        A legal principle in civil law countries of the Roman-German tradition that says that lawyers need not to argue the law, as that is the office of the court. Sometimes miswritten as
        <i>
         iura novat curia
        </i>
        (the court renews the laws).
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Jure_matris" title="Jure matris">
          iure matris
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        in right of his mother
       </td>
       <td>
        Indicates a right exercised by a son on behalf of his mother.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Jure_uxoris" title="Jure uxoris">
          iure uxoris
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        in right of his wife
       </td>
       <td>
        Indicates a right exercised by a husband on behalf of his wife.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iuris_ignorantia_est_cum_ius_nostrum_ignoramus">
        </span>
        <b>
         iuris ignorantia est cum ius nostrum ignoramus
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        it is ignorance of the law when we do not know our own rights
       </td>
       <td>
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="ius_accrescendi">
        </span>
        <b>
         ius accrescendi
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        right of accrual
       </td>
       <td>
        Commonly referred to as "right of survivorship": a rule in property law that surviving joint tenants have rights in equal shares to a decedent's property.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="ius_ad_bellum">
        </span>
        <b>
         ius ad bellum
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        law towards war
       </td>
       <td>
        Refers to the laws that regulate the reasons for going to war. Typically, this would address issues of self-defense or preemptive strikes.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Peremptory_norm" title="Peremptory norm">
          ius cogens
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        compelling law
       </td>
       <td>
        Refers to a fundamental principle of international law considered to have acceptance among the international community of states as a whole. Typically, this would address issues not listed or defined by any authoritative body, but arise out of case law and changing social and political attitudes. Generally included are prohibitions on waging aggressive war, crimes against humanity, war crimes, piracy, genocide, slavery, and torture.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="ius_in_bello">
        </span>
        <b>
         ius in bello
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        law in war
       </td>
       <td>
        Refers to the "laws" that regulate the conduct of combatants during a conflict. Typically, this would address issues of who or what is a valid target, how to treat prisoners, and what sorts of weapons can be used. The word
        <i>
         jus
        </i>
        is also commonly spelled
        <i>
         ius
        </i>
        .
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <b>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ius_primae_noctis" title="Ius primae noctis">
          ius primae noctis
         </a>
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        law of the first night
       </td>
       <td>
        The
        <i>
         droit de seigneur
        </i>
        .
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iustitia_fundamentum_regni">
        </span>
        <b>
         iustitia fundamentum regni
        </b>
       </td>
       <td>
        justice is the foundation of a reign
       </td>
       <td>
        Motto of the Supreme Public Prosecutor's Office of the Czech Republic.
       </td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
       <td>
        <span id="iustitia_omnibus">

        <b>
         iustitia omnibus

        justice for all
       </td>
       <td>
        The motto of
        <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
         Washington, D.C.
        </a>
       </td>
      </tr>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canberra_Girls%27_Grammar_School" title="Canberra Girls' Grammar School">
