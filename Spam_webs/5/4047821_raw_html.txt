       In an age when an artists manager can become a pseudo-celebrity, the crme of the manager crop is clearly doing something right. I'm talking about Aviciis
       <a href="http://www.askmen.com/music/1093066/article/edm">
        Ash Pournouri
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://www.askmen.com/celebs/men/entertainment/justin-bieber/">
        Justin Bieber
       </a>
       s Scooter Braun as well as
       <a href="http://www.askmen.com/celebs/women/singer/2_britney_spears.html">
        Britney Spears
       </a>
       and
       <a href="http://www.askmen.com/celebs/women/singer/miley-cyrus/">
        Miley Cyrus
       </a>
        Larry Rudolph.
       <p>
        But there's someone who's fast joining these ranks 
        <a href="http://www.askmen.com/entertainment/news/truth-or-dare-with-krewella.html">
         Krewella
        </a>
        s manager, Jake Udell.
       </p>
       <p>
        This former-rapper-turned-manager is truly one part entrepreneur and one part marketing genius, and at 25 years of age, is already a force to be reckoned with. Hes helped Krewella not only achieve mainstream radio success, but some of the highest DJ booking fees in the industry, all while building his own boutique management company Th3rd Brain.
       </p>
       <p>
        Under Udells guidance, emerging artists like ZHU are also garnering a global buzz and mainstream radio play. We caught up with this EDM power player and Chicago native during Lollapalooza, on the rooftop terrace of the citys hallowed Peninsula Hotel, to chat about the business, finding success and building an empire.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         David Morris (DM): At 13 years old you were already getting into the entrepreneurial spirit.
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Jake Udell (JU): Well, its actually funny that were at the Peninsula right now doing this interview because I was checking to see if there are any major celebrities (laughter). I used to camp outside with close friends because most of the major basketball teams stayed at this hotel. Without a doubt, The Pen has always been considered Chicagos premiere hotel and is the place to stay.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         DM: Youre like an early paparazzi?
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        JU: Sort of when I was 13, I got really into sports and was inspired by historic, one-of-a-kind moments like Willie Mays The Catch. When the holidays came around that year, I went to the local hobby store and got a few autographed pieces like this as gifts. A month later I wanted to go back to the store for more, but realized I could get the same merchandise cheaper if I bought it on eBay.
       </p>
       <p>
        One of the items I had purchased wasnt exactly what I wanted so I put it back on eBay; it was the first item I ever sold and it went for three times what I paid. That was the moment when my entrepreneurial spirit really kicked in. It made me want to develop my own relationships with these athletes. I remember going to downtown Chicago to begin to chase the signatures myself. I would always get the most unique merchandise signed and was innovating new product lines at the age of 15. I was willing to stand outside waiting for 36 hours to catch the right athlete if necessary. In fact, I did do that once for
        <a href="http://www.askmen.com/celebs/men/sports/53_lebron_james.html">
         LeBron James
        </a>
        during his rookie season until he finally signed my jersey. The jersey cost me $50 and I sold it for $1,750 the next day.
       </p>
       <p>
        <iframe allowfullscreen="" class="regularVideoEmbed" frameborder="0" height="390" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/0Fh-30Bni_U" width="520">
        </iframe>
       </p>
       <p>
        By the time I was 16, this venture was really becoming lucrative and I hired a team of people around the country to work with me in different cities so we could touch most of the major sporting events. At the time, my parents wouldnt let me travel a lot so I had to get better at the other side of the business  selling and marketing my products. I began to sell to charity auctions, wholesale to stores and eventually do deals directly with the players and agents themselves.
       </p>
