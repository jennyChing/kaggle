      <b>
       Anhedonia
      </b>
      (
      <span class="nowrap">
       <span class="IPA nopopups">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">
         /
         <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">

       </span>
      </span>
      <span class="Unicode" title="English pronunciation respelling">
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i>
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             AN
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -hee-
         <b>
          <span class="smallcaps">
           <span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;">
            <span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">
             DOH
            </span>
           </span>
          </span>
         </b>
         -nee-
        </i>
       </a>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
       Greek
      </a>
      : -
      <i>
       an
      </i>
      -, "without" and 
      <i>
       hdon
      </i>
      , "pleasure") is defined as the inability to experience pleasure from activities usually found enjoyable, e.g. exercise, hobbies, music, sexual activities or social interactions. While earlier definitions of anhedonia emphasized pleasurable experience, more recent models have highlighted the need to consider different aspects of enjoyable behavior, such as motivation or desire to engage in an activity ("motivational anhedonia"), as compared to the level of enjoyment of the activity itself ("consummatory anhedonia").
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to
      <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">
       William James
      </a>
      the term was coined by
      <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odule-Armand_Ribot" title="Thodule-Armand Ribot">
       Thodule-Armand Ribot
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       One can distinguish many kinds of pathological depression. Sometimes it is mere passive joylessness and dreariness, discouragement, dejection, lack of taste and zest and spring. Professor Ribot has proposed the name anhedonia to designate this condition. "The state of anhedonia, if I may coin a new word to pair off with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_analgesia" title="Congenital analgesia">
        analgesia
       </a>
       ," he writes, "has been very little studied, but it exists."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         2
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Anhedonia can be a characteristic of
      <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">
       mental disorders
      </a>
      including
      <a href="/wiki/Mood_disorder" title="Mood disorder">
       mood disorders
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Schizoaffective_disorder" title="Schizoaffective disorder">
       schizoaffective disorder
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Borderline_personality_disorder" title="Borderline personality disorder">
       borderline personality disorder
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Schizoid_personality_disorder" title="Schizoid personality disorder">
       schizoid personality disorder
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">
       schizophrenia
      </a>
      . For example, the 7th DSM-IV criterion for Borderline Personality Disorder: "chronic feelings of emptiness." Results indicate that emptiness is negligibly related to boredom, is closely related to feeling hopeless, pathologically lonely, and isolated, and is a robust predictor of depression and suicidal ideation (but not anxiety or suicide attempts). Findings are consistent with DSM-IV revisions regarding the 7th criterion for Borderline Personality Disorder. In addition, findings suggest that emptiness reflects pathologically low positive affect and significant psychiatric distress.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      People affected with schizophrenia often describe themselves as feeling emotionally empty.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Mood disturbances are commonly observed in many psychiatric disorders. Disturbing mood changes may occur resultant to stressful life events and they are not uncommon during times of physical illness.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While anhedonia can be a feature of such mood changes, they are not mutually inclusive.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
       Causes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Researchers theorize that anhedonia may result from the breakdown in the
      <a href="/wiki/Reward_system" title="Reward system">
       brain's reward system
      </a>
      , involving the
      <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">
       neurotransmitter
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">
       dopamine
      </a>
      . Studies by Paul Keedwell, MD, then of
      <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">
       King's College
      </a>
      , found that the brains of participants who were
      <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">
       clinically depressed
      </a>
      had to work harder to process rewarding experiences.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      While earlier research believed dopamine to be primarily involved in the subjective experience of pleasure, the last 20 years has seen a conceptual shift, such that dopamine is now believed to underlie various aspects of reward anticipation, learning, and motivation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Anhedonia is also a relatively common
      <a href="/wiki/Side_effect" title="Side effect">
       side effect
      </a>
      of antidopaminergic neuroleptics or
      <a href="/wiki/Antipsychotic" title="Antipsychotic">
       antipsychotic
      </a>
      drugs, as well a side effect of withdrawal from stimulants such as phenethylamines, or amphetamines.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_in_depression">
       Significance in depression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Significance in depression">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      As a clinical symptom in depression, anhedonia rates highly in making a diagnosis of this disorder. The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) describes a "lack of interest or pleasure", but these can be difficult to discern given that people tend to become less interested in things which do not give them pleasure. The DSM criterion of weight loss is probably related, and many individuals with this symptom describe a lack of enjoyment of food. People suffering from anhedonia in association with depression generally feel suicidal in the morning and better in the evenings as sleep seems the only escape resembling death and can portray any of the non-psychotic symptoms and signs of depression.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomb2007_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Tomb2007-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_anhedonia">
       Sexual anhedonia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sexual anhedonia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Sexual_anhedonia" title="Sexual anhedonia">
       Sexual anhedonia
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Sexual anhedonia in males is also known as 'ejaculatory anhedonia'. This condition means that the person will
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ejaculate" title="Ejaculate">
       ejaculate
      </a>
      with no accompanying sense of pleasure.
     </p>
     <p>
      The condition is most frequently found in males, but women can suffer from lack of pleasure when the body goes through the
      <a href="/wiki/Orgasm" title="Orgasm">
       orgasm
      </a>
      process as well.
     </p>
     <p>
      Sexual anhedonia may be caused by:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperprolactinaemia" title="Hyperprolactinaemia">
        Hyperprolactinaemia
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Hypoactive sexual desire disorder (HSDD), also called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhibited_sexual_desire" title="Inhibited sexual desire">
        inhibited sexual desire
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Low levels of the hormone
       <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">
        testosterone
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Spinal cord injury
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">
        Multiple sclerosis
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Use of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSRI" title="SSRI">
        SSRI
       </a>
       antidepressants
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         12
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Use (or previous use) of antidopaminergic neuroleptics (anti-psychotics)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         13
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         14
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Fatigue
      </li>
      <li>
       Physical illness
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      It is very uncommon that a neurological examination and blood tests can determine the cause of a specific case of sexual anhedonia.
     </p>
     <p>
      Patients may be prescribed sustained-release
      <a href="/wiki/Bupropion" title="Bupropion">
       bupropion
      </a>
      to aid in treatment, which has been shown to relieve sexual dysfunction even in patients without depression.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_anhedonia">
       Social anhedonia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social anhedonia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Asociality" title="Asociality">
       Asociality
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Emotional_detachment" title="Emotional detachment">
       Emotional detachment
      </a>
     </div>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
       Definition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Definition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <i>
       Social anhedonia
      </i>
      is defined as a trait-like disinterest in social contact and is characterized by social withdrawal and decreased pleasure in social situations. This characteristic typically manifests as an indifference to other people.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard.2C_J.J._2000_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blanchard.2C_J.J._2000-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In contrast to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extroversion_and_introversion" title="Extroversion and introversion">
       introversion
      </a>
      , a nonpathological dimension of human personality, social anhedonia represents a deficit in the ability to experience
      <a href="/wiki/Pleasure" title="Pleasure">
       pleasure
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Additionally, social anhedonia differs from
      <a href="/wiki/Social_anxiety" title="Social anxiety">
       social anxiety
      </a>
      in that social anhedonia is predominantly typified by diminished positive affect, while social anxiety is distinguished by both decreased
      <a href="/wiki/Affect_theory" title="Affect theory">
       positive affect
      </a>
      and exaggerated
      <a href="/wiki/Affect_theory" title="Affect theory">
       negative affect
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown.2C_L.H._2008_18-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brown.2C_L.H._2008-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This trait is currently seen as a central characteristic to, as well as a predictor of,
      <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">
       schizophrenia-spectrum disorders
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as it is seen as a potential evolution of most personality disorders, if the patient is above age 24, when
      <a href="/wiki/Prodrome#In_schizophrenia" title="Prodrome">
       prodromal schizophrenia
      </a>
      may be excluded.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Signs.2Fsymptoms">
       Signs/symptoms
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Signs/symptoms">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Decreased ability to experience interpersonal pleasure
      </li>
      <li>
       Social withdrawal/isolation
      </li>
      <li>
       Decreased need for social contact
      </li>
      <li>
       Lack of close friends and intimate relationships, and decreased quality of those relationships
      </li>
      <li>
       Poor social adjustment
      </li>
      <li>
       Decreased positive affect
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Blunted_affect" title="Blunted affect">
        Flat affect
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">
        Depressed mood
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       State-related anxiety
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         21
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwapil_T_R_1998_558.E2.80.93565_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kwapil_T_R_1998_558.E2.80.93565-22">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         22
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Social anhedonia is trait-related, meaning it remains stable throughout life, independent of diagnosis, treatment, or symptom remission.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_early_clinical_observation">
       Background and early clinical observation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Background and early clinical observation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The term
      <i>
       anhedonia
      </i>
      is derived from the Greek
      <i>
       an-
      </i>
      , "without" and
      <i>
       hdon
      </i>
      , "pleasure".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Interest in the nature of pleasure and its absence dates back to ancient Greek philosophers such as
      <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">
       Epicurus
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The symptoms of anhedonia were introduced to the realm of psychopathology in 1809 by
      <a href="/wiki/John_Haslam_(physician)" title="John Haslam (physician)">
       John Haslam
      </a>
      , who characterized a patient suffering from schizophrenia as indifferent to those objects and pursuits which formerly proved sources of delight and instruction..
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        26
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The concept was formally coined by
      <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odule-Armand_Ribot" title="Thodule-Armand Ribot">
       Thodule-Armand Ribot
      </a>
      and later used by psychiatrists
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Eugen_Bleuler" title="Paul Eugen Bleuler">
       Paul Eugen Bleuler
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Emil_Kraepelin" title="Emil Kraepelin">
       Emil Kraepelin
      </a>
      to describe a core symptom of schizophrenia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DerAvakian_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DerAvakian-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Theorists Sndor Rad and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Meehl" title="Paul Meehl">
       Paul Meehl
      </a>
      posited that anhedonia represents an underlying genetic vulnerability to schizophrenia-spectrum disorders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        28
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In particular, Rado postulated that schizotypes, or individuals with the schizophrenic phenotype, have two key genetic deficits, one related to the ability to feel pleasure (anhedonia) and one related to
      <a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">
       proprioception
      </a>
      . In 1962 Meehl furthered Rado's theory through the introduction of the concept of schizotaxia, a genetically-driven neural integrative defect thought to give rise to the personality type of
      <a href="/wiki/Schizotypy" title="Schizotypy">
       schizotypy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Loren and Jean Chapman further distinguished between two types of anhedonia: physical anhedonia, or a deficit in the ability to experience physical pleasure, and social, or a deficit in the ability to experience interpersonal pleasure.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        30
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Recent research suggests that social anhedonia may represent a
      <a href="/wiki/Prodrome" title="Prodrome">
       prodrome
      </a>
      of psychotic disorders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard.2C_J.J._2000_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blanchard.2C_J.J._2000-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        31
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      First-degree relatives of individuals with schizophrenia show elevated levels of social anhedonia,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      higher baseline scores of social anhedonia are associated with later development of schizophrenia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These findings provide support for the conjecture that it represents a genetic risk marker for schizophrenia-spectrum disorders.
     </p>
     <p>
      Additionally, elevated levels of social anhedonia in patients with schizophrenia have been linked to poorer social functioning.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Socially anhedonic individuals perform worse on a number of neuropsychological tests than non-anhedonic participants,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_A.S..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Saperstein_A.M..2C_Blanchard_J.J._2006_132.E2.80.93141_38-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cohen_A.S..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Saperstein_A.M..2C_Blanchard_J.J._2006_132.E2.80.93141-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and show similar physiological abnormalities seen in patients with schizophrenia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_A.S..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Saperstein_A.M..2C_Blanchard_J.J._2006_132.E2.80.93141_38-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Cohen_A.S..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Saperstein_A.M..2C_Blanchard_J.J._2006_132.E2.80.93141-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comorbidity">
       Comorbidity
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Comorbidity">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Anhedonia is present in several forms of psychopathology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, social anhedonia is not a necessary symptom criterion of any disorder. Social anhedonia manifests similarly in a variety of different mental illness, but for differing reasons. Most frequently, social anhedonia is associated with schizophrenia and schizophrenia spectrum disorders (including schizotypal personality disorder, paranoid personality disorder, and antisocial personality disorder). Social anhedonia has also been implicated in other psychological disorders:
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Depression">
       Depression
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Depression">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Social anhedonia is observed in both depression and schizophrenia. However, social anhedonia is state related to the depressive episode and the other is trait related to the personality construct associated with schizophrenia. These individuals both tend to score highly on self-report measures of social anhedonia. Blanchard, Horan, and Brown (2001) demonstrated that, although both the depression and the schizophrenia patient groups can look very similar in terms of social anhedonia cross sectionally, over time as individuals with depression experience symptom remission, they show fewer signs of social anhedonia, while individuals with schizophrenia do not.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Blanchard and colleagues (2011) found individuals with social anhedonia also had elevated rates of lifetime mood disorders including depression and dysthymia compared to controls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard_J.J..2C_Collins_L.M..2C_Aghevli_M..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Cohen_A.S._2011_587.E2.80.93602_41-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_J.J..2C_Collins_L.M..2C_Aghevli_M..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Cohen_A.S._2011_587.E2.80.93602-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_anxiety">
       Social anxiety
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Social anxiety">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      As mentioned above, social anxiety and social anhedonia differ in important ways.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown.2C_L.H._2008_18-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brown.2C_L.H._2008-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, social anhedonia and social anxiety are also often comorbid with each other. People with social anhedonia may display increased social anxiety and be at increased risk for social phobias and generalized anxiety disorder.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
       <a href="#cite_note-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It has yet to be determined what the exact relationship between social anhedonia and social anxiety is, and if one potentiates the other.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Individuals with social anhedonia may display increased stress reactivity, meaning that they feel more overwhelmed or helpless in response to a stressful event compared to control subjects who experience the same type of stressor. This dysfunctional stress reactivity may correlate with hedonic capacity, providing a potential explanation for the increased anxiety symptoms experienced in people with social anhedonia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In an attempt to separate out social anhedonia from social anxiety, the Revised Social Anhedonia Scale
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      didn't include items that potentially targeted social anxiety.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwapil_T_R_1998_558.E2.80.93565_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Kwapil_T_R_1998_558.E2.80.93565-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, more research must be conducted on the underlying mechanisms through which social anhedonia overlaps and interacts with social anxiety. The efforts of the social processes RDoC initiative will be crucial in differentiating between these components of social behavior that may underlie mental illnesses such as schizophrenia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_relevance_in_schizophrenia_and_schizophrenia_spectrum_disorders">
       Primary relevance in schizophrenia and schizophrenia spectrum disorders
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Primary relevance in schizophrenia and schizophrenia spectrum disorders">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Social anhedonia is a core characteristic of
      <a href="/wiki/Schizotypy" title="Schizotypy">
       schizotypy
      </a>
      , which is defined as a continuum of personality traits that can range from normal to disordered and contributes to risk for
      <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">
       psychosis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">
       schizophrenia
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Social anhedonia is a dimension of both negative and positive schizotypy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It involves social and interpersonal deficits, but is also associated with cognitive slippage and disorganized speech, both of which fall into the category of positive schizotypy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gooding.2C_D_C_2001_49-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gooding.2C_D_C_2001-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Not all people with schizophrenia display social anhedonia
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman.2C_L._J._1976_52-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chapman.2C_L._J._1976-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and likewise, people who have social anhedonia may never be diagnosed with a schizophrenia-spectrum disorder if they do not have the positive and cognitive symptoms that are most frequently associated with most schizophrenia-spectrum disorders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman.2C_L._J._1994_53-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chapman.2C_L._J._1994-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Social anhedonia may be a valid predictor of future schizophrenia-spectrum disorders;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman.2C_L._J._1994_53-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chapman.2C_L._J._1994-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
       <a href="#cite_note-54">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        54
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      young adults with social anhedonia perform in a similar direction to schizophrenia patients in tests of cognition and social behavior, showing potential predictive validity.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gooding.2C_D_C_2001_49-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gooding.2C_D_C_2001-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        55
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Social anhedonia usually manifests in adolescence, possibly because of a combination of the occurrence of critical neuronal development and synaptic pruning of brain regions important for social behavior and environmental changes, when adolescents are in the process of becoming individuals and gaining more independence.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">
       Treatment
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Treatment">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There is no validated treatment for social anhedonia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        56
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Future research should focus on genetic and environmental risk factors to home in on specific brain regions and neurotransmitters that may be implicated in social anhedonia etiology and could be targeted with specialized pharmacological or behavioral treatments. Social support may also play a valuable role in the treatment of social anhedonia. Blanchard et al. (2011)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blanchard_J.J..2C_Collins_L.M..2C_Aghevli_M..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Cohen_A.S._2011_587.E2.80.93602_41-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Blanchard_J.J..2C_Collins_L.M..2C_Aghevli_M..2C_Leung_W.W..2C_Cohen_A.S._2011_587.E2.80.93602-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      found that a greater number of social supports as well as a greater perceived social support network were related to fewer schizophrenia-spectrum symptoms and to better general functioning within the social anhedonia group. Therefore, future studies should also examine ways to increase social support among individuals with social anhedonia in order to alleviate some of the symptoms.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_differences">
       Gender differences
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gender differences">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the general population, males score higher than females on measures of social anhedonia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        57
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This sex difference is stable throughout time (from adolescence into adulthood) and is also seen in people with schizophrenia-spectrum disorders. These results may reflect a more broad pattern of interpersonal and social deficits seen in schizophrenia-spectrum disorders.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        58
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      On average, males with schizophrenia are diagnosed at a younger age, have more severe symptoms, worse treatment prognosis, and a decrease in overall quality of life compared to females with the disorder.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        59
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These results, coupled with the sex difference seen in social anhedonia, outline the necessity for research on genetic and hormonal characteristics that differ between males and females, and that may increase risk or resilience for mental illnesses such as schizophrenia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        60
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_components">
       Genetic components
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Genetic components">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      L.J. and J.P. Chapman
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapman.2C_L._J._1976_52-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chapman.2C_L._J._1976-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      were the first to discuss the possibility that social anhedonia may stem from a genetic vulnerability. The Disrupted in Schizophrenia 1 (DISC1) gene has been consistently associated with risk for, and etiology of, schizophrenia-spectrum disorders and other mental illnesses.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        65
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      More recently,
      <a href="/wiki/DISC1" title="DISC1">
       DISC1
      </a>
      has been associated with social anhedonia within the general population.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        66
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Tomppo (2009) identified a specific DISC1
      <a href="/wiki/Allele" title="Allele">
       allele
      </a>
      that is associated with an increase in characteristics of social anhedonia. They also identified a DISC1 allele associated with decreased characteristics of social anhedonia, that was found to be preferentially expressed in women. More research needs to be conducted, but social anhedonia may be an important intermediate phenotype (
      <a href="/wiki/Endophenotype" title="Endophenotype">
       endophenotype
      </a>
      ) between genes associated with risk for schizophrenia and phenotype of the disorder. Continued study of social anhedonia and its genetic components will help researchers and clinicians learn more about the etiology of schizophrenia-spectrum disorders.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Neurobiological_correlates">
       Neurobiological correlates
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Neurobiological correlates">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Researchers studying the neurobiology of social anhedonia posit that this trait may be linked to dysfunction of reward-related systems in the brain. This circuitry is critical for the sensation of pleasure, the computation of reward benefits and costs, determination of the effort required to obtain a pleasant stimulus, deciding to obtain that stimulus, and increasing motivation to obtain the stimulus. In particular, the ventral striatum and areas of the
      <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">
       prefrontal cortex
      </a>
      (PFC), including the
      <a href="/wiki/Orbitofrontal_cortex" title="Orbitofrontal cortex">
       orbitofrontal cortex
      </a>
      (OFC) and dorsolateral (dl) PFC, are critically involved in the experience of pleasure and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hedonic" title="Hedonic">
       hedonic
      </a>
      perception of rewards. With regards to neurotransmitter systems,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogenous_opioid" title="Endogenous opioid">
       opioid
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GABA" title="GABA">
       gamma-Aminobutyric acid
      </a>
      and endocannabinoid systems in the
      <a href="/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens" title="Nucleus accumbens">
       nucleus accumbens
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Ventral_pallidum" title="Ventral pallidum">
       ventral pallidum
      </a>
      , and OFC mediate the hedonic perception of rewards.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DerAvakian_27-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DerAvakian-27">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        27
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Activity in the PFC and ventral striatum have been found to be decreased in anhedonic individuals with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_Depressive_Disorder" title="Major Depressive Disorder">
       Major Depressive Disorder
      </a>
      (MDD) and
      <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">
       schizophrenia
      </a>
      . However, schizophrenia may be less associated with decreased hedonic capacity and more with deficient reward appraisal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        67
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        68
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Abnormal functioning of the anterior insula and the parietal cortex is also implicated in anhedonia. Dowd &amp; Barch conducted an
      <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">
       fMRI
      </a>
      study in which schizophrenia-spectrum disorder patients and control participants made
      <a href="/wiki/Valence_(psychology)" title="Valence (psychology)">
       valence
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Arousal" title="Arousal">
       arousal
      </a>
      ratings of their own responses to emotional stimuli. They found that higher levels of anhedonia were associated with diminished arousal, but not valence, ratings. Furthermore, they found that, in controls, greater levels of social anhedonia were related to decreased bilateral caudate activation in response to positive relative to negative stimuli. The authors posit that the striatum in anhedonic individuals might be dysfunctional such that it fails to tag the saliency of positive events. Consequently, these individuals may experience blunted emotion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        69
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Research further implicates that abnormalities in the circuitry underlying social cognition are also critically involved in the generation of anhedonic symptoms. Individuals high in social anhedonia show less activation in the anterior portion of the rostral medial prefrontal cortex (arMFC), right
      <a href="/wiki/Superior_temporal_gyrus" title="Superior temporal gyrus">
       superior temporal gyrus
      </a>
      , and left
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex" title="Somatosensory cortex">
       somatosensory cortex
      </a>
      during an emotion discrimination task; these regions are responsible for processing facial emotions. Moreover, the arMFC is highly relevant for social cognition, and the mPFC and somatosensory cortex are involved in theory of mind and mentalizing. Thus, social anhedonia appears to be related to dysfunction of neural systems involved in self/other representation and social perception.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70">
       <a href="#cite_note-70">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        70
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_musical_anhedonia">
       Specific musical anhedonia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anhedonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Specific musical anhedonia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Recent studies have found people who do not have any issue processing musical tones or beat, yet receive no pleasure from listening to music.
      <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S096098221400133X" rel="nofollow">
       [1]
      </a>
      Specific musical anhedonia is distinct from melophobia, the fear of music.
     </p>
