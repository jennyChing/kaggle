       <p>
        Just over a year ago, I finished up
        <a href="/2012/02/my-network-cupboard-the-dramatic-conclusion.html" title="MyNetworkCupboard: The Dramatic Conclusion">
         a project where I cabled a network across my entire house
        </a>
        and within the last month
        <a href="/2015/03/my-network-cupboard-extending-the-wifi.html" title="MyNetowrkCupboard: Extending the WiFi">
         I extended the networks WiFi reach with a PoE Access Point
        </a>
        . Early on in the
        <a href="/categories/mynetworkcupboard/">
         My Network Cupboard
        </a>
        project, we had a few little issues between getting the first few cable drops functional.  Nothing tremendously serious,  but it usually involved one of us having to repeat steps wed already taken like re-punching down the cable on the patch panel or one of the CAT5e keystones an additional time. At one point I wondered out loud if we needed to buy some sort of
        <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00GFH8B0I/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=B00GFH8B0I&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=brisblo0b-20&amp;linkId=PIRPFGVAY444K6PS" rel="nofollow" title="KINGLAKE 5E SC8108 Multifunction Network LAN Cable Tester">
         network cable tester
        </a>
        .  Because my network is relatively simple, it was an easy process of elimination to figure out exactly what wed done wrong and as we completed the dozen or so different drops, we got a bit more proficient.  But in the process, we couldve saved a few hours if we had a testing tool of some sort.
       </p>
       <p>
        Right around the time we completed the project,  I stumbled across a crowd-funded project on
        <a href="http://www.indiegogo.com" title="Indiegogo.com">
         Indiegogo
        </a>
        called
        <a href="https://www.indiegogo.com/projects/pockethernet-the-swiss-army-knife-for-network-administrators" title="Pockethernet on Indiegogo">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        . Pockethernet is a pocket-sized adapter which works over Bluetooth and an app on your phone (
        <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=de.pokethardware.pocketethernet" title="Pockethernet on the Google Play Store">
         Android
        </a>
        or
        <a href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/app/pockethernet/id966344406?mt=8&amp;ign-mpt=uo%3D4" title="Pockethernet on iTunes">
         iOS
        </a>
        ) while providing a litany of network testing functionality, most of which I didnt understand at the time and probably still dont!  The best part of all?  It was comparatively inexpensive to the other testers which provide the same functionality.
       </p>
       <p>
        In reading
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/#generalspecs" title="Pockethernet Specs">
         the specifications
        </a>
        ,  I knew that the following
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        features wouldve helped us with each of the issues we encountered during the
        <a href="/categories/mynetworkcupboard/">
         MyNetworkCupboard project
        </a>
        :
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         <u>
          Wiremap including shield
         </u>
         : Test if you punched the pins in the correct order on the RJ45 jacks. Pockethernet also tests the shield for continuity.
        </li>
        <li>
         <u>
          Bit error rate test (10/100/1000)
         </u>
         : Real-world cable quality measurement at gigabit speeds. Stop finger-pointing with measurement data.
        </li>
        <li>
         <u>
          Port blinker
         </u>
         : Let Pockethernet toggle the Link LED on switches and routers, so you know which cable you are looking for.
        </li>
        <li>
         <u>
          Link establishment test (10/100/1000)
         </u>
         : Check if a link can be established at different speeds. Make sure the wiring is up to date for gigabit.
        </li>
       </ol>
       <p>
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         <img align="right" src="/Images/2015/pockethernet/pockethernet_logo.jpg" style="margin:5px"/>
        </a>
        I decided to
        <a href="https://www.indiegogo.com/individuals/6490477/campaigns" title="Brian's Campaigns on Indiegogo">
         contribute to the Pockethernet campaign on Indiegogo
        </a>
        to receive a
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        as soon as they were manufactured. I figured this would be helpful for future cabling endeavors at my house and an excellent candidate to
        <a href="/2014/03/whats-in-brians-laptop-bag.html" title="What's in Brian's Laptop Bag?">
         carry around in my backpack
        </a>
        .  It paid dividends when we
        <a href="/2015/03/my-network-cupboard-extending-the-wifi.html" title="MyNetowrkCupboard: Extending the WiFi">
         added a new Access Point
        </a>
        to my network: we initially had issues when we punched down to the new drop on the patch panel.  On top of that, we used the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        to validate each point of failure as we cabled up the access points proprietary power-over-Ethernet injector.
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        even was able to detect and measure the voltage of that proprietary PoE injector.
       </p>
       <p>
        In total, it took about a year for the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        team to deliver their first batch of products. Im quickly learning through a number of crowd-funded endeavors that it can require a bit of patience before delivery. But waiting roughly a year for the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        was fine by me because it showed up exactly when I needed it again and it was everything that they described.
       </p>

        I especially liked that the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        is a Bluetooth device.  In the times that Ive used it,  its been very convenient to hook it up where it needs to go then grab your phone to look at the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        app (
        <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=de.pokethardware.pocketethernet" title="Pockethernet on the Google Play Store">

        <a href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/app/pockethernet/id966344406?mt=8&amp;ign-mpt=uo%3D4" title="Pockethernet on iTunes">
         iOS
        </a>
        ).  Especially if you have to rummage around behind something: its very nice to hook it up, retreat to somewhere more comfortable nearby, and then look at your phone/tablet.

       <h2>
        How To
       </h2>
       <p>
        About my only complaint about the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        is that it seems to be designed for people who already know how to use similar tools.  There was very little documentation that came with the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        and none of it really contained instructions on how to put it to use or described how to use all of the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/#generalspecs" title="Pockethernet Specs">
         Pockethernets features
        </a>
        .  I thought Id quickly write up how I used it in my project,  just in case it helped a fellow neophyte!
       </p>
       <h3>
        Wiremap &amp; Volts
       </h3>
       <p>
        I used this test to validate segments (and the totality) of cables that I punched down for
        <a href="/2015/03/my-network-cupboard-extending-the-wifi.html" title="MyNetowrkCupboard: Extending the WiFi">
         my new acesss point
        </a>
        .  Because of how we cabled it up,  there were many potential points of failure from the beginning to the end.  And I also  wanted to see if the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        would read the voltage of the proprietary power injector it used.
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         Connected the
         <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
          Pockethernet
         </a>
         to the beginning of the cable run on the patch panel.
        </li>
        <li>
         Connected the
         <strong>
          Wiremap
         </strong>
         adapter to the end of the cable run (wherever that was)
        </li>
        <li>
         Powered on the
         <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
          Pockethernet
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         Clicked Connect (or Refresh if youre already Connected) and selected the
         <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
          Pockethernet
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         From the
         <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=de.pokethardware.pocketethernet" title="Pockethernet on the Google Play Store">
          Pockethernet app
         </a>
         select
         <strong>
          Wiremap &amp; Volts
         </strong>
         (on the
         <em>
          Test
         </em>
         page in the app)
        </li>
       </ol>
       <p>
        The wiremap test allowed us to validate the potential physical errors of the installation.  It helped us determine that wed punched everything down and crimped the cables correctly.  It also allowed us to determine that there was power at the remote end of the cable for the access point.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align:center;">
        <img height="569" src="/Images/2015/pockethernet/small_12_pockethernet_app3.jpg" title="Wiremap &amp; Volts" width="320"/>
       </p>
       <h3>
        BER Test
       </h3>
       <p>
        We used the Bit Error Rate (BER) test to make sure the cable was good and capable of doing gigabit.  In the process of
        <a href="/2015/03/my-network-cupboard-extending-the-wifi.html" title="MyNetowrkCupboard: Extending the WiFi">
         wiring up the new Access Point
        </a>
        I also used the BER test to help determine if a problematic network cable was good or not.
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         Connected the
         <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
          Pockethernet
         </a>
         to the beginning of the cable run on the patch panel.
        </li>
        <li>
         Connected the
         <strong>
          Loopback
         </strong>
         adapter to the end of the cable run (wherever that was)
        </li>
        <li>
         Powered on the
         <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
          Pockethernet
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         Clicked Connect (or Refresh if youre already Connected) and selected the
         <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
          Pockethernet
         </a>
        </li>
        <li>
         From the
         <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=de.pokethardware.pocketethernet" title="Pockethernet on the Google Play Store">
          Pockethernet app
         </a>
         selected the
         <strong>
          Off-Line
         </strong>
         tab in the app
        </li>
        <li>
         In the BER section, set the speed to 1000Mbps
        </li>
        <li>
         Click Ok
        </li>
       </ol>
       <p>
        I ran a few different iterations of the BER tests tinkering with the Packet Size and Payload options to make sure that all combinations of the 1000Mbps test came back with a 0% error rate.
       </p>
       <p style="text-align:center;">
        <img height="569" src="/Images/2015/pockethernet/small_13_pockethernet_app4.jpg" title="BER Test" width="320"/>
       </p>
       <h3>
        Connected to Network
       </h3>
       <p>
        Theres a couple things that I also re-validated by plugging the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        into the network.  When plugged into the network, you can use the
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        to determine the link,  provide DHCP information, and ping a few devices on your network.
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         I validated the
         <strong>
          Link
         </strong>
         and
         <strong>
          DHCP
         </strong>
         information from the
         <em>
          Test
         </em>
         page.
        </li>
        <li>
         From the
         <strong>
          on-line
         </strong>
         page in the app, I punched in a server name and an IP address internal to my network and one IP address external to my network and ran a ping test.
        </li>
       </ol>
       <p style="text-align:center;">
        <img height="569" src="/Images/2015/pockethernet/small_10_pockethernet_app1.jpg" title="Link &amp; DHCP Info" width="320"/>
        <img height="569" src="/Images/2015/pockethernet/small_11_pockethernet_app2.jpg" title="On-line Ping Test" width="320"/>
       </p>
       <h2>
        Conclusion
       </h2>
       <p>
        For what I was needing,
        <a href="http://www.pockethernet.com/" title="Pockethernet Homepage">
         Pockethernet
        </a>
        was perfect. In fact,  its probably too useful for my simple needs.  But its a luxury to have such a nice piece of equipment for any kind of potential issues with my network at home.  It was handy enough when
        <a href="/2015/03/my-network-cupboard-extending-the-wifi.html" title="MyNetowrkCupboard: Extending the WiFi">
         wiring up my new access point
        </a>
        that I certainly wished that I had found it and that it was available before I began
        <a href="/2012/02/my-network-cupboard-the-dramatic-conclusion.html" title="MyNetworkCupboard: The Dramatic Conclusion">
         my network project
        </a>
        !  Its my understanding right now that you cant buy your own

         Pockethernet
        </a>
        yet,  but its in the works.  If youre interested in buying one, theres a
