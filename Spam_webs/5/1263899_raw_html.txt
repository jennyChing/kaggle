     <p>
      "
      <b>
       O Canada
      </b>
      " is the
      <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">
       national anthem
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
       Canada
      </a>
      . The song was originally commissioned by
      <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Quebec" title="Lieutenant Governor of Quebec">
       Lieutenant Governor of Quebec
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_Robitaille" title="Thodore Robitaille">
       Thodore Robitaille
      </a>
      for the 1880
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Holiday_(Quebec)" title="National Holiday (Quebec)">
       Saint-Jean-Baptiste Day
      </a>
      ceremony;
      <a href="/wiki/Calixa_Lavall%C3%A9e" title="Calixa Lavalle">
       Calixa Lavalle
      </a>
      composed the music, after which words were written by the poet and judge Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Adolphe-Basile_Routhier" title="Adolphe-Basile Routhier">
       Adolphe-Basile Routhier
      </a>
      . The lyrics were originally in French and an English version was created in 1906.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stanley_Weir" title="Robert Stanley Weir">
       Robert Stanley Weir
      </a>
      wrote in 1908 another English version, which is the official and most popular version, one that is not a literal translation of the French. Weir's lyrics have been revised twice, taking their present form in 1980, but the French lyrics remain unaltered. "O Canada" had served as a
      <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">
       de facto
      </a>
      national anthem since 1939, officially becoming Canada's national anthem in 1980 when the Act of Parliament making it so received
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">
       Royal Assent
      </a>
      and became effective on July 1 as part of that year's
      <a href="/wiki/Canada_Day" title="Canada Day">
       Dominion Day
      </a>
      celebrations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>

       <a href="#cite_note-2">

     <p>
      It has been noted that the opening theme of "O Canada" bears a strong resemblance to the "March of the Priests" from the opera
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/The_Magic_Flute" title="The Magic Flute">
        The Magic Flute
       </a>
      </i>
      , composed in 1791 by
      <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">
       Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colombo_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-colombo-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The line "The True North strong and free" is based on
      <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">
       the Lord Tennyson's
      </a>
      description of Canada as "that true North, whereof we lately heard / A strain to shame us". In the context of Tennyson's poem
      <i>
       To the Queen
      </i>
      , the word
      <i>
       true
      </i>
      means "loyal" or "faithful".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colombo_5-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-colombo-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The lyrics and melody of "O Canada" are both in the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      a status unaffected by the trademarking of the phrases "with glowing hearts" and "
      <span lang="fr">
       <i>
        des plus brillants exploits
       </i>
      </span>
      " for the
      <a href="/wiki/2010_Winter_Olympics" title="2010 Winter Olympics">
       2010 Winter Olympics
      </a>
      in Vancouver.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Two provinces have adopted
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
       Latin
      </a>
      translations of phrases from the English lyrics as their mottos:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_Manitoba" title="Coat of Arms of Manitoba">
       Manitoba
      </a>
      
      <span lang="la">
       <i>
        Gloriosus et Liber
       </i>
      </span>
      (Glorious and Free)
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_Alberta" title="Coat of Arms of Alberta">
       Alberta
      </a>
      
      <span lang="la">
       <i>
        Fortis et Liber
       </i>
      </span>
      (Strong and Free).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Similarly, the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">
       Canadian Army
      </a>
      's motto is
      <span lang="la">
       <i>
        Vigilamus pro te
       </i>
      </span>
      (we stand on guard for thee).
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Lyric_changes">
       Lyric changes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=O_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lyric changes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Weir's original lyrics from 1908 contained no religious references and used the phrase "thou dost in us command" before they were changed by Weir in 1914 to read "in all thy sons command".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1926, a fourth verse of a religious nature was added.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansard2002_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hansard2002-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In June 1990,
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto_City_Council" title="Toronto City Council">
       Toronto City Council
      </a>
      voted 12 to 7 in favour of recommending to the
      <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">
       Canadian government
      </a>
      that the phrase "our home and native land" be changed to "our home and cherished land" and that "in all thy sons command" be partly reverted to "in all of us command." Councillor
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Moscoe" title="Howard Moscoe">
       Howard Moscoe
      </a>
      said that the words
      <i>
       native land
      </i>
      were not appropriate for the many Canadians who were not native-born and that the word
      <i>
       sons
      </i>
      implied "that women can't feel true patriotism or love for Canada."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">
       Senator
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Vivienne_Poy" title="Vivienne Poy">
       Vivienne Poy
      </a>
      similarly criticized the English lyrics of the anthem as being
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-sexist_language" title="Non-sexist language">
       sexist
      </a>
      and she introduced a bill in 2002 proposing to change the phrase "in all thy sons command" to "in all of us command."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansard2002_12-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Hansard2002-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In the late 2000s, the anthem's religious references (to God in English and to the
      <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">
       Christian cross
      </a>
      in French) were criticized by secularists.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/Speech_from_the_throne" title="Speech from the throne">
       Throne Speech
      </a>
      delivered by
      <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">
       Governor General
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Micha%C3%ABlle_Jean" title="Michalle Jean">
       Michalle Jean
      </a>
      on March 3, 2010, a plan to have parliament review the "original gender-neutral wording of the national anthem" was announced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, three-quarters of Canadians polled after the speech objected to the proposal and,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      two days later, the
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Prime_Minister_(Canada)" title="Office of the Prime Minister (Canada)">
       prime minister's office
      </a>
      announced that the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Cabinet" title="Canadian Cabinet">
       Cabinet
      </a>
      had decided not to restore the original lyrics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=O_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="345" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png/220px-Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png/330px-Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b7/Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Adolphe-Basile_Routhier.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Adolphe-Basile_Routhier" title="Adolphe-Basile Routhier">
         Adolphe-Basile Routhier
        </a>
        c.1890
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The original French lyrics of "O Canada" were written by Sir
      <a href="/wiki/Adolphe-Basile_Routhier" title="Adolphe-Basile Routhier">
       Adolphe-Basile Routhier
      </a>
      , to music composed by
      <a href="/wiki/Calixa_Lavall%C3%A9e" title="Calixa Lavalle">
       Calixa Lavalle
      </a>
      , as a
      <a href="/wiki/French_Canadian" title="French Canadian">
       French Canadian
      </a>
      patriotic song for the
      <a href="/wiki/Saint-Jean-Baptiste_Society" title="Saint-Jean-Baptiste Society">
       Saint-Jean-Baptiste Society
      </a>
      and first performed on June 24, 1880, at a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Holiday_(Quebec)" title="National Holiday (Quebec)">
       Saint-Jean-Baptiste Day
      </a>
      banquet in
      <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">
       Quebec City
      </a>
      . At that time, the "Chant National", also by Routhier, was popular amongst Francophones as an anthem,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QHE_19-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-QHE-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      while "
      <a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen" title="God Save the Queen">
       God Save the Queen
      </a>
      " and "
      <a href="/wiki/The_Maple_Leaf_Forever" title="The Maple Leaf Forever">
       The Maple Leaf Forever
      </a>
      " had, since 1867, been competing as unofficial national anthems in
      <a href="/wiki/English_Canada" title="English Canada">
       English Canada
      </a>
      . "O Canada" joined that fray when a group of school children sang it for the 1901 tour of Canada by
      <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">
       the Duke and Duchess of Cornwall
      </a>
      (later
      <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">
       King George V
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_Teck" title="Mary of Teck">
       Queen Mary
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Five years later, the Whaley and Royce company in
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">
       Toronto
      </a>
      , Ontario, published the music with the French text and a first translation into English by Thomas Bedford Richardson and, in 1908,
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier's">
        Collier's Weekly
       </a>
      </i>
      magazine held a competition to write new English lyrics for "O Canada". The competition was won by Mercy E. Powell McCulloch, but her version never gained wide acceptance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QHE_19-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-QHE-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In fact, many made English translations of Routhier's words; however, the most popular version was created in 1908 by Robert Stanley Weir, a lawyer and Recorder of the City of
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      . A slightly modified version was officially published for the Diamond Jubilee of Confederation in 1927, and gradually it became the most widely accepted and performed version of this song.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:O_Canada_house_QC.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="2304" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/O_Canada_house_QC.jpg/220px-O_Canada_house_QC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/O_Canada_house_QC.jpg/330px-O_Canada_house_QC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/O_Canada_house_QC.jpg/440px-O_Canada_house_QC.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:O_Canada_house_QC.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        The house in
        <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">
         Quebec City
        </a>
        in which Lavalle lived on first moving to Quebec City in 1878
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The tune was thought to have become the de facto national anthem after King
      <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">
       George VI
      </a>
      remained at attention during its playing at the dedication of the
      <a href="/wiki/National_War_Memorial_(Canada)" title="National War Memorial (Canada)">
       National War Memorial
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">
       Ottawa
      </a>
      , Ontario, on May 21, 1939;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      though George was actually following a precedent set by his brother,
      <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII" title="Edward VIII">
       Edward
      </a>
      , the previous
      <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Canada" title="Monarchy of Canada">
       king of Canada
      </a>
      , when he dedicated the
      <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Vimy_Memorial" title="Canadian National Vimy Memorial">
       Canadian National Vimy Memorial
      </a>
      in France in 1936.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galbraith10_21-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Galbraith10-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Still, by-laws and practices governing the use of song during public events in municipalities varied; in
      <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">
       Toronto
      </a>
      , "God Save the Queen" was employed, while in
      <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">
       Montreal
      </a>
      it was "O Canada".
     </p>
     <p>
      Prime Minister
      <a href="/wiki/Lester_B._Pearson" title="Lester B. Pearson">
       Lester B. Pearson
      </a>
      in 1964 said one song would have to be chosen as the country's national anthem and the government resolved to form a joint committee to review the status of the two musical works. The next year, Pearson put to the House of Commons a motion that "the government be authorized to take such steps as may be necessary to provide that 'O Canada' shall be the National Anthem of Canada while 'God Save the Queen' shall be the Royal Anthem of Canada," of which parliament approved. In 1967, the Prime Minister advised
      <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">
       Governor General
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Georges_Vanier" title="Georges Vanier">
       Georges Vanier
      </a>
      to appoint the Special Joint Committee of the
      <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">
       Senate
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">
       House of Commons
      </a>
      on the National and Royal Anthems; the group first met in February and,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CE-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      within two months, on April 12, 1967, presented its conclusion that "O Canada" should be designated as the national anthem and "
      <a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen" title="God Save the Queen">
       God Save the Queen
      </a>
      " as the
      <a href="/wiki/Honors_music" title="Honors music">
       Royal Anthem
      </a>
      of Canada,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      one verse from each, in
      <a href="/wiki/Official_bilingualism_in_Canada" title="Official bilingualism in Canada">
       both official languages
      </a>
      , to be adopted by parliament. The group was then charged with establishing official lyrics for each song. For "O Canada", the Robert Stanley Weir version of 1908 was recommended, with a few minor changes, for the English words;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      two of the "stand on guard" phrases were replaced with "from far and wide" and "God keep our land."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Still, it was not until 1970 that the
      <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Canada" title="Monarchy of Canada">
       Queen in Right of Canada
      </a>
      purchased the right to the lyrics and music of "O Canada" and 1980 before the song finally became the official national anthem via the National Anthem Act.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galbraith10_21-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Galbraith10-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_22-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CE-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The act established a religious reference to the English lyrics and the phrase "From far and wide, O Canada" to replace one of the repetitions of the phrase "We stand on guard." This change was controversial with traditionalists and, for several years afterwards, it was not uncommon to hear people still singing the old lyrics at public events. In contrast, the French version has never been changed from its original.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Gordon V. Thompson Music, Toronto, held the publishing copyright to the English words of the song "O Canada". Thompson sold that copyright to the Government of Canada for $1, stating: "At a certain point, a song belongs to the people."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        25
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Laws_and_etiquette">
       Laws and etiquette
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=O_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Laws and etiquette">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The National Anthem Act specifies the lyrics and melody of "O Canada", placing both of them in the
      <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">
       public domain
      </a>
      , allowing the anthem to be freely reproduced or used as a base for derived works, including musical arrangements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      There are no regulations governing the performance of "O Canada", leaving citizens to exercise their best judgment.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      When it is performed at an event, traditional etiquette is to either start or end the ceremonies with the anthem, including situations when other anthems are played, and for the audience to stand during the performance.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Civilian men usually remove their hats, while women and children are not required to do so.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCHOCanada_1-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-DCHOCanada-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Military men and women in uniform traditionally keep their hats on and offer the military salute during the performance of the anthem, with the salute offered in the direction of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maple_Leaf_Flag" title="Maple Leaf Flag">
       Maple Leaf Flag
      </a>
      if one is present, and if not present it is offered standing at attention.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">
       Adaptations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=O_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adaptations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      "O Canada"'s melody, in the 1950s, was adapted to serve as the school anthem for the
      <a href="/wiki/Ateneo_de_Manila_University" title="Ateneo de Manila University">
       Ateneo de Manila University
      </a>
      in the Philippines, titled "
      <a href="/wiki/Culture_and_traditions_of_the_Ateneo_de_Manila" title="Culture and traditions of the Ateneo de Manila">
       A Song for Mary
      </a>
      " or simply "The Ateneo de Manila Graduation Hymn". The lyrics were written by Rf.
      <a href="/wiki/James_B._Reuter" title="James B. Reuter">
       James B. Reuter
      </a>
      , and the melody was adapted by Col. Jose Campaa.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
