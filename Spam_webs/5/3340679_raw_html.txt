     <p>
      A
      <b>
       nonprofit organization
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       NPO
      </b>
      , also known as a
      <b>
       non-business entity
      </b>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      ) is an
      <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">
       organization
      </a>
      that uses its
      <a href="/wiki/Economic_surplus" title="Economic surplus">
       surplus revenues
      </a>
      to further achieve its purpose or mission, rather than distributing its surplus income to the organization's directors (or equivalents) as profit or
      <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">
       dividends
      </a>
      . This is known as the distribution constraint.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The decision to adopt a nonprofit legal structure is one that will often have taxation implications, particularly where the nonprofit seeks income tax exemption, charitable status and so on.
     </p>
     <p>
      The nonprofit landscape is highly varied, although many people have come to associate NPOs with charitable organizations. Although charities do comprise an often high profile or visible aspect of the sector, there are many other types of nonprofits. Overall, they tend to be either member-serving or community-serving. Member-serving organizations include mutual societies, cooperatives, trade unions, credit unions, industry associations, sports clubs, retired serviceman's clubs and peak bodies  organizations that benefit a particular group of people  the members of the organization. Typically, community-serving organizations are focused on providing services to the community in general, either globally or locally: organizations delivering human services programs or projects, aid and development programs, medical research, education and health services, and so on. It could be argued many nonprofits sit across both camps, at least in terms of the impact they make.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, the grassroots support group that provides a lifeline to those with a particular condition or disease could be deemed to be serving both its members (by directly supporting them) and the broader community (through the provision of a helping service for fellow citizens).
     </p>
     <p>
      Many NPOs use the model of a double bottom line in that furthering their cause is more important than making a profit, though both are needed to ensure the organization's sustainability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Although NPOs are permitted to generate surplus revenues, they must be retained by the organization for its self-preservation, expansion, or plans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      NPOs have controlling members or a
      <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">
       board of directors
      </a>
      . Many have paid staff including management, whereas others employ unpaid
      <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">
       volunteers
      </a>
      and even executives who work with or without compensation (occasionally nominal).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In some countries, where there is a token fee, in general it is used to meet legal requirements for establishing a contract between the executive and the organization.
     </p>
     <p>
      Designation as a nonprofit does not mean that the organization does not intend to make a profit, but rather that the organization has no 'owners' and that the funds realized in the operation of the organization will not be used to benefit any owners. The extent to which an NPO can generate surplus revenues may be constrained or use of surplus revenues may be restricted.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives_and_goals">
       <span id="Objectives_and_Goals">
       </span>
       Objectives and goals
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives and goals">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Some NPOs may also be a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charitable_organizations" title="Charitable organizations">
       charity
      </a>
      or service organization; they may be organized as a nonprofit corporation or as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charitable_Trust" title="Charitable Trust">
       trust
      </a>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">
       cooperative
      </a>
      , or they exist informally. A very similar type of organization termed a
      <a href="/wiki/Supporting_organization_(charity)" title="Supporting organization (charity)">
       supporting organization
      </a>
      operates like a foundation, but they are more complicated to administer, hold more favorable tax status and are restricted in the public charities they support. Their goal is not to be successful in terms of wealth, but in terms of giving value to the groups of people they administer to.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">
       Functions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Functions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      NPOs have a wide diversity of structures and purposes. For legal classification, there are, nevertheless, some elements of importance:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Management provisions.
      </li>
      <li>
       Accountability and auditing provisions.
      </li>
      <li>
       Provisory for the amendment of the statutes or articles of incorporation.
      </li>
      <li>
       Provisions for the dissolution of the entity.
      </li>
      <li>
       Tax statuses of corporate and private donors.
      </li>
      <li>
       Tax status of the founders.
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Some of the above must be (in most jurisdictions in the USA at least), expressed in the organization's charter of establishment or constitution. Others may be provided by the supervising authority at each particular jurisdiction.
     </p>
     <p>
      While affiliations will not affect a legal status, they may be taken into consideration by legal proceedings as an indication of purpose.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most countries have laws which regulate the establishment and management of NPOs, and which require compliance with
      <a href="/wiki/Corporate_governance" title="Corporate governance">
       corporate governance
      </a>
      regimes. Most larger organizations are required to publish their financial reports detailing their income and expenditure publicly.
     </p>
     <p>
      In many aspects they are similar to corporate
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_entity" title="Business entity">
       business entities
      </a>
      though there are often significant differences. Both not-for-profit and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/For-profit" title="For-profit">
       for-profit
      </a>
      corporate entities must have board members, steering committee members, or trustees who owe the organization a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiduciary_duty" title="Fiduciary duty">
       fiduciary duty
      </a>
      of loyalty and trust. A notable exception to this involves
      <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">
       churches
      </a>
      , which are often not required to disclose finances to anyone, including church members.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_structure">
       Formation and structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation and structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , nonprofit organizations are formed by filing bylaws and/or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Articles_of_incorporation" title="Articles of incorporation">
       articles of incorporation
      </a>
      in the state in which they expect to operate. The act of incorporating creates a legal entity enabling the organization to be treated as a distinct body (corporation) by law and to enter into business dealings, form contracts, and own property as individuals or for-profit corporations can.
     </p>
     <p>
      Nonprofits can have members but many do not. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonprofit" title="Nonprofit">
       nonprofit
      </a>
      may also be a
      <a href="/wiki/Trust_law" title="Trust law">
       trust
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_association" title="Voluntary association">
       association
      </a>
      of members. The
      <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">
       organization
      </a>
      may be controlled by its members who elect the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Directors" title="Board of Directors">
       Board of Directors
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Governors" title="Board of Governors">
       Board of Governors
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Trustees" title="Board of Trustees">
       Board of Trustees
      </a>
      . A nonprofit may have a delegate structure to allow for the representation of groups or corporations as members. Alternatively, it may be a non-membership organization and the board of directors may elect its own
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Successors" title="Successors">
       successors
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The two major types of nonprofit organization are membership and
      <a href="/wiki/Board-only" title="Board-only">
       board-only
      </a>
      . A membership organization elects the board and has regular meetings and the power to amend the bylaws.
      <a href="/wiki/A" title="A">
       A
      </a>
      board-only organization typically has a self-selected board, and a membership whose powers are limited to those delegated to it by the board. A board-only organization's bylaws may even state that the organization does not have any membership, although the organization's literature may refer to its donors or service recipients as "members"; examples of such organizations are
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairvote" title="Fairvote">
       Fairvote
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_the_Reform_of_Marijuana_Laws" title="National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws">
       National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Model_Nonprofit_Corporation_Act" title="Model Nonprofit Corporation Act">
       Model Nonprofit Corporation Act
      </a>
      imposes many complexities and requirements on membership decision-making.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Accordingly, many organizations, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikimedia" title="Wikimedia">
       Wikimedia
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      have formed board-only structures. The
      <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Parliamentarians" title="National Association of Parliamentarians">
       National Association of Parliamentarians
      </a>
      has generated concerns about the implications of this trend for the future of openness, accountability, and understanding of public concerns in nonprofit organizations. Specifically, they note that nonprofit organizations, unlike business corporations, are not subject to
      <a href="/wiki/Market_discipline" title="Market discipline">
       market discipline
      </a>
      for products and shareholder discipline of their capital; therefore, without membership control of major decisions such as election of the board, there are few inherent safeguards against abuse.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A rebuttal to this might be that as nonprofit organizations grow and seek larger donations, the degree of scrutiny increases, including expectations of audited financial statements.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A further rebuttal might be that NPOs are constrained, by their choice of legal structure, from financial benefit as far as distribution of profit to its members/directors is concerned.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_exemption">
       Tax exemption
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tax exemption">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In many countries, nonprofits may apply for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_exempt_status" title="Tax exempt status">
       tax exempt status
      </a>
      , so that the organization itself may be exempt from income tax and other taxes. In the United States, to be exempt from federal income taxes the organization must meet the requirements set forth by the
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">
       Internal Revenue Service
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">
       Australia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
       Australia
      </a>
      , nonprofit organisations include trade unions, charitable entities, co-operatives, universities and hospitals, mutual societies, grass-root and support groups, political parties, religious groups, incorporated associations, not-for-profit companies,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trusts" title="Trusts">
       trusts
      </a>
      and more. Furthermore, they operate across a multitude of domains and industries, from health, employment, disability and other human services to local sporting clubs, credit unions and research institutes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A nonprofit organisation in Australia can choose from a number of legal forms depending on the needs and activities of the organisation: co-operative, company limited by guarantee, unincorporated association, incorporated association (by the Associations Incorporation Act 1985) or incorporated association or council (by the Commonwealth Aboriginal Councils and Associations Act 1976).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      From an academic perspective,
      <a href="/wiki/Social_enterprise" title="Social enterprise">
       social enterprise
      </a>
      is for the most part considered a sub-set of the nonprofit sector as typically they too are concerned with a purpose relating to a public good, however these are not bound to adhere to a nonprofit legal structure and many incorporate and operate as for-profit entities.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Australia, nonprofit organisations are primarily established in one of three ways: companies limited by guarantee, trusts and incorporated associations. However, the incorporated association form is typically used by organisations intending to operate only within one Australian state jurisdiction. Nonprofit organisations seeking to establish a presence across Australia typically consider incorporating as a company or as a trust.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">
       Canada
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Canada">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Canada allows nonprofits to be incorporated or unincorporated. Nonprofits may incorporate either federally, under Part II of the
      <a href="/wiki/Canada_Business_Corporations_Act" title="Canada Business Corporations Act">
       Canada Business Corporations Act
      </a>
      or under provincial legislation. Many of the governing Acts for Canadian nonprofits date to the early 1900s, meaning that nonprofit legislation has not kept pace with legislation that governs for-profit corporations; particularly with regards to
      <a href="/wiki/Corporate_governance" title="Corporate governance">
       corporate governance
      </a>
      . Federal, and in some provinces (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
       Ontario
      </a>
      ), incorporation is by way of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Letters_Patent" title="Letters Patent">
       Letters Patent
      </a>
      , and any change to the Letters Patent (even a simple name change) requires formal approval by the appropriate government, as do by-law changes. Other provinces (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">
       Alberta
      </a>
      ) permit incorporation
      <i>
       as of right
      </i>
      , by the filing of Articles of Incorporation or Articles of Association.
     </p>
     <p>
      During 2009, the federal government enacted new legislation repealing the Canada Corporations Act, Part II - the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_Not-for-Profit_Corporations_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canada Not-for-Profit Corporations Act (page does not exist)">
       Canada Not-for-Profit Corporations Act
      </a>
      . This Act was last amended on 10 October 2011 and the act was current till 4 March 2013.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It allows for incorporation
      <i>
       as of right
      </i>
      , by Articles of Incorporation; does away with the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">
        ultra vires
       </a>
      </i>
      doctrine for nonprofits; establishes them as legal persons; and substantially updates the governance provisions for nonprofits. Ontario also overhauled its legislation, adopting the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ontario_Not-for-Profit_Corporations_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ontario Not-for-Profit Corporations Act (page does not exist)">
       Ontario Not-for-Profit Corporations Act
      </a>
      during 2010; pending the outcome of an anticipated election during October 2011,
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
         <span title="The text near this tag is dated.">
          dated info
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      the new Act is expected to be in effect as of 1 July 2013.
     </p>
     <p>
      Canada also permits a variety of charities (including public and private foundations). Charitable status is granted by the
      <a href="/wiki/Canada_Revenue_Agency" title="Canada Revenue Agency">
       Canada Revenue Agency
      </a>
      (CRA) upon application by a nonprofit; charities are allowed to issue income tax receipts to donors, must spend a certain percentage of their assets (including cash, investments and fixed assets) and file annual reports in order to maintain their charitable status. In determining whether an organization can become a charity, CRA applies a
      <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">
       common law
      </a>
      test to its stated objects and activities. These must be:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The relief of poverty
      </li>
      <li>
       The advancement of education;
      </li>
      <li>
       The advancement of religion; or
      </li>
      <li>
       certain other purposes that benefit the community in a way the courts have said is charitable
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         22
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Charities are not permitted to engage in partisan political activity; doing so may result in the revocation of charitable status. However, a charity can carry out a small number of political activities that are non-partisan, help further the charities purposes, and subordinate to the charity's charitable purposes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="France">
       France
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: France">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
       France
      </a>
      , nonprofits are called
      <i>
       associations
      </i>
      . They are based on a law enacted 1 July 1901. As a consequence, the nonprofits are also called
      <i>
       association loi 1901
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      A nonprofit can be created by 2 people in order to accomplish a common goal. The
      <i>
       association
      </i>
      can have industrial and/or commercial activities but the members cannot get any profit from these activities. Thereby, worker's unions and political parties can be organized from this law.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, the national statistic institute (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INSEE" title="INSEE">
       INSEE
      </a>
      ) counted more than 1 million of these
      <i>
       associations
      </i>
      in the country, and about 16 million people older than 16 are members of a nonprofit in France (a third or the population). The nonprofits employ 1.6 million people and 8 million are volunteers for them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        24
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      This law is also relevant in a many
      <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">
       former French colonies
      </a>
      , particularly in
      <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
       Africa
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">
       Hong Kong
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     <p>
      In Japan, an NPO is any citizen's group that serves the public interest and does not produce a profit for its members. NPOs are given corporate status to assist them in conducting business transactions. As of February 2011, there were 41,600 NPOs in Japan. Two hundred of NPOs were given tax-deductible status by the government which meant that only contributions to those organization were tax deductible for the contributors.

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">
       Russia
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Russia">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Russian law contains many legal forms of NCOs (non-commercial organizations), resulting in a complex, often contradictory, and limiting regulatory framework.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        29
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The primary requirements are that NCOs, whatever their type, do not have the generation of profit as their primary objective and do not distribute any such profit among their participants (Article 50(1), Civil Code). Most commonly there are five forms of NCOs:
     </p>
     <ol>
      <li>
       Public associations - A public association is the form most comparable to an "association" as used in international parlance. A public association is a membership-based organization of individuals who associate on the basis of common interests and goals stipulated in the organization's charter.
      </li>
      <li>
       Foundations - Foundations are property-based, non-membership organizations created by individuals and/or legal persons to pursue social, charitable, cultural, educational, or other public benefit goals.
      </li>
      <li>
       Institutions - The institution (uchrezhdeniye) is a form that exists in Russia and several other countries of the Former Soviet Union. Like foundations, institutions do not have members. Unlike foundations, however, institutions do not acquire property rights in the property conveyed to them (Article 120, Civil Code, and Article 20, NCO Law). Moreover, the founders are liable for any obligations of the institution that it cannot meet on its own.
      </li>
      <li>
       Non-commercial partnerships - A non-commercial partnership (NP) (Article 8, NCO Law) is a membership organization pursuing activities for the mutual benefit of members. Therefore, assets which have been transferred to an NP as donations can be used for purposes other than those having public benefit.
      </li>
      <li>
       Autonomous non-commercial organizations - An autonomous non-commercial organization (ANO) (Article 10, NCO Law) is a non-membership organization undertaking services in the field of education, social policy, culture, etc., which in practice often generates income by providing its services for a fee.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         <span>
          [
         </span>
         30
         <span>
          ]
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ol>

     <p>
      In the UK, many nonprofit companies are incorporated as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company_limited_by_guarantee" title="Company limited by guarantee">
       company limited by guarantee
      </a>
      . This means that the company does not have shares or shareholders, but it has the benefits of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate" title="Corporate">
       corporate
      </a>
      status. This includes
      <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability" title="Limited liability">
       limited liability
      </a>
      for its members and being able to enter into contracts and purchase property in its own name. The profits of the company (also referred to as the trading surplus) must be invested in achieving these goals and not distributed to the company's members.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        32
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since the Companies Act 2006, nonprofit companies may be formed as a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_Interest_Company" title="Community Interest Company">
       Community Interest Company
      </a>
      (CIC). These are forms of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company_limited_by_guarantee" title="Company limited by guarantee">
       company limited by guarantee
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company_limited_by_shares" title="Company limited by shares">
       company limited by shares
      </a>
      but with special conditions and are intended specifically to ensure that the profits and assets of the company are used for the public good, even when managed for (limited) profit.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        33
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization#United_Kingdom" title="Charitable organization">
       charity
      </a>
      is a nonprofit organisation that meets stricter criteria regarding its purpose and the method in which it makes decisions and reports its finances.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        34
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      For example, a charity is generally not allowed to pay its Trustees. In
      <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">
       England and Wales
      </a>
      , charities may be registered with the
      <a href="/wiki/Charity_Commission_for_England_and_Wales" title="Charity Commission for England and Wales">
       Charity Commission
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        35
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Scottish_Charity_Regulator" title="Office of the Scottish Charity Regulator">
       Office of the Scottish Charity Regulator
      </a>
      serves the same function. Other organizations which are classified as nonprofit organizations elsewhere, such as trade unions, are subject to separate regulations, and are not regarded as "charities" in the technical sense.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
       United States
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: United States">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       <i>
        For a United States analysis of this issue, see
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)" title="501(c)">
         501(c)
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization#United_States" title="Charitable organization">
         Charitable organization (United States)
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      After a nonprofit organization has been formed at the state level, the organization may seek recognition of tax exempt status with respect to
      <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Income tax in the United States">
       U.S. federal income tax
      </a>
      . That is done typically by applying to the
      <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">
       Internal Revenue Service
      </a>
      (IRS), although statutory exemptions exist for limited types of nonprofit organizations. The IRS, after reviewing the application to ensure the organization meets the conditions to be recognized as a tax exempt organization (such as the purpose, limitations on spending, and internal safeguards for a charity), may issue an authorization letter to the nonprofit granting it tax exempt status for income tax payment, filing, and deductibility purposes. The exemption does not apply to other Federal taxes such as employment taxes. Additionally, a tax-exempt organization must pay federal tax on income that is unrelated to their exempt purpose.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        36
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Failure to maintain operations in conformity to the laws may result in an organization losing its tax exempt status.
     </p>
     <p>
      Individual states and localities offer nonprofits exemptions from other taxes such as
      <a href="/wiki/Sales_tax" title="Sales tax">
       sales tax
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/Property_tax" title="Property tax">
       property tax
      </a>
      . Federal tax-exempt status does not guarantee exemption from state and local taxes, and vice versa. These exemptions generally have separate applications and their requirements may differ from the IRS requirements. Furthermore, even a tax exempt organization may be required to file annual financial reports (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRS_Form_990" title="IRS Form 990">
       IRS Form 990
      </a>
      ) at the state and federal level. A tax exempt organization's 990 forms are required to be made available for public scrutiny. An example of nonprofit organization in the US is
      <a href="/wiki/Project_Vote_Smart" title="Project Vote Smart">
       Project Vote Smart
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_experienced_by_NPOs">
       Problems experienced by NPOs
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Problems experienced by NPOs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">
       Capacity building
      </a>
      is an ongoing problem experienced by NPOs for a number of reasons. Most rely on external funding (government funds, grants from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charitable_foundation" title="Charitable foundation">
       charitable foundations
      </a>
      , direct donations) to maintain their operations and changes in these sources of revenue may influence the reliability or predictability with which the organization can hire and retain staff, sustain facilities, create programs, or maintain tax-exempt status. For example, a university that sells research to for-profit companies may have tax exemption problems. In addition, unreliable funding, long hours and low pay can result in employee retention problems. During 2009, the US government acknowledged this critical need by the inclusion of the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.councilofnonprofits.org/public-policy/federal-issues/nonprofit-capacity-building" rel="nofollow">
       Nonprofit Capacity Building Program
      </a>
      in the Serve America Act. Further efforts to quantify the scope of the sector and propose policy solutions for community benefit were included in the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.councilofnonprofits.org/nscsact" rel="nofollow">
       Nonprofit Sector and Community Solutions Act
      </a>
      , proposed during 2010.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many nonprofit organizations find it difficult to create consistent messaging that resonates with their various stakeholders as marketing budgets are minimal or nonexistent. Marketing is in many cases a taboo word that NPOs or others don't like to associate with such community benefit organizations. There are strategic ways in which nonprofits can leverage their access to various community stakeholders to get their name and cause recognized by the public, but it is imperative to have an outreach strategy which includes a financial plan to execute that outreach/marketing strategy, particularly if the organization has plans to rebrand or expand their initiaives.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Founder%27s_syndrome" title="Founder's syndrome">
       Founder's syndrome
      </a>
      is an issue organizations face as they grow. Dynamic founders with a strong vision of how to operate the project try to retain control of the organization, even as new employees or volunteers want to expand the project's scope or change policy.
     </p>
     <p>
      Resource mismanagement is a particular problem with NPOs because the employees are not accountable to anybody with a direct stake in the organization. For example, an employee may start a new program without disclosing its complete liabilities. The employee may be rewarded for improving the NPO's reputation, making other employees happy, and attracting new donors. Liabilities promised on the full faith and credit of the organization but not recorded anywhere constitute
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accounting_fraud" title="Accounting fraud">
       accounting fraud
      </a>
      . But even indirect liabilities negatively affect the financial sustainability of the NPO, and the NPO will have financial problems unless strict controls are instated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        37
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Some commentators have also argued that receiving significant funding from large for-profit corporations can ultimately alter the NPO's functions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        38
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        39
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
       <a href="#cite_note-40">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        40
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Competition for employees with the public and private sector is another problem that Nonprofit organizations will inevitably face, particularly for management positions. There are reports of major talent shortages in the nonprofit sector today regarding newly graduated workers,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
       <a href="#cite_note-41">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        41
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and NPOs have for too long relegated hiring to a secondary priority,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_42-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-auto-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      which could be why they find themselves in the position many do. While many established NPO's are well-funded and comparative to their public sector competetitors, many more are independent and must be creative with which incentives they use to attract and maintain vibrant personalities. The initial interest for many is the wage and benefits package, though many who have been questioned after leaving an NPO have reported that it was stressful work environments and implacable work that drove them away.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
       <a href="#cite_note-43">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        43
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Public and private sector employment has, for the most part, been able to offer more for their employees than most nonprofit agencies throughout history. Either in the form of higher wages, more comprehensive benefit packages, or less tedious work, the public and private sector has enjoyed an advantage in attracting employees over NPOs. Traditionally, the NPO has attracted mission-driven individuals who want to assist their chosen cause. Compounding the issue is that some NPOs do not operate in a manner similar to most businesses, or only seasonally. This leads many young and driven employees to forego NPOs in favor of more stable employment. Today however, Nonprofit organizations are adopting methods used by their competitors and finding new means to retain their employees and attract the best of the newly minted workforce.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
       <a href="#cite_note-44">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        44
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been mentioned that most nonprofits will never be able to match the pay of the private sector
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
       <a href="#cite_note-45">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        45
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and therefore should focus their attention on benefits packages, incentives and implementing pleasurable work environments. Pleasurable work conditions are ranked as being more preferable than a high salary and implacable work.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_42-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-auto-42">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        42
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      NPOs are encouraged to pay as much as they are able, and offer a low stress work environment that the employee can associate him or herself positively with. Other incentives that should be implemented are generous vacation allowances or flexible work hours.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
       <a href="#cite_note-46">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        46
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      In the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
       United States
      </a>
      , two of the wealthiest nonprofit organizations are the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_and_Melinda_Gates_Foundation" title="Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation">
       Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation
      </a>
      , which has an endowment of US$38 billion,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
       <a href="#cite_note-47">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        47
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes_Medical_Institute" title="Howard Hughes Medical Institute">
       Howard Hughes Medical Institute
      </a>
      originally funded by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft" title="Hughes Aircraft">
       Hughes Aircraft
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
       <a href="#cite_note-48">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        48
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      prior to divestiture, which has an endowment of approximately $14.8 billion. Outside the United States, another large NPO is the British
      <a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">
       Wellcome Trust
      </a>
      , which is a "charity" by British usage. See:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_wealthiest_foundations" title="List of wealthiest foundations">
       List of wealthiest foundations
      </a>
      . Note that this assessment excludes
      <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
       universities
      </a>
      , at least a few of which have assets in the tens of billions of dollars. For example;
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._colleges_and_universities_by_endowment" title="List of U.S. colleges and universities by endowment">
       List of U.S. colleges and universities by endowment
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Measuring an NPO by its monetary size has obvious limitations, as the power and significance of NPOs are defined by more qualitative measurements such as effectiveness at performing charitable missions.
     </p>

     <p>
      However, there are also millions of smaller NPOs that provide
      <a href="/wiki/Social_services" title="Social services">
       social services
      </a>
      and relief efforts to people throughout the world. There are more than 1.6 million NPOs in the United States alone, and millions more informal, community-based entities, which Frumkin and others consider part of the nonprofit sector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
       <a href="#cite_note-49">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        49
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are also examples, for instance in Ireland of
      <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B3chas" title="Dchas">
       NGO umbrella organisations
      </a>
      bringing about a degree of self-regulation in the NGO sector.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_information">
       Other information
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other information">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many NPOs often use the
      <a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">
       .org
      </a>
      or
      <a href="/wiki/.us" title=".us">
       .us
      </a>
      (or the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCTLD" title="CCTLD">
       CCTLD
      </a>
      of their respective country) or
      <a href="/wiki/.edu" title=".edu">
       .edu
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">
       top-level domain
      </a>
      (TLD) when selecting a
      <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">
       domain name
      </a>
      to differentiate themselves from more commercial entities which typically use the
      <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">
       .com
      </a>
      space.
     </p>
     <p>
      In the traditional domain noted in
      <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc1591" rel="nofollow">
       RFC 1591
      </a>
      , .org is for "organizations that didn't fit anywhere else" in the naming system, which implies that it is the proper category for non-commercial organizations if they are not governmental, educational, or one of the other types with a specific TLD. It is not designated specifically for charitable organizations or any specific organizational or tax-law status, however; it encompasses anything that is not classifiable as another category. Currently, no restrictions are enforced on registration of .com or .org, so one can find organizations of all sorts in either of these domains, as well as other top-level domains including newer, more specific ones which may apply to particular sorts of organizations such as
      <a href="/wiki/.museum" title=".museum">
       .museum
      </a>
      for museums or
      <a href="/wiki/.coop" title=".coop">
       .coop
      </a>
      for
      <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">
       cooperatives
      </a>
      . Organizations might also register by the appropriate
      <a href="/wiki/Country_code_top-level_domain" title="Country code top-level domain">
       country code top-level domain
      </a>
      for their country.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusion">
       Conclusion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonprofit_organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Conclusion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Instead of being defined by "non" words, some organizations are suggesting new, positive-sounding terminology to describe the sector. The term "civil society organization" (CSO) has been used by a growing number of organizations, such as the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Center_for_the_Study_of_Global_Governance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Center for the Study of Global Governance (page does not exist)">
       Center for the Study of Global Governance
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
       <a href="#cite_note-50">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        50
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The term "citizen sector organization" (CSO) has also been advocated to describe the sector as one of citizens, for citizens by organizations such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashoka:_Innovators_for_the_Public" title="Ashoka: Innovators for the Public">
       Ashoka: Innovators for the Public
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
       <a href="#cite_note-51">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        51
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A more broadly applicable term, "Social Benefit Organization" (SBO) has been advocated for by organizations such as
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.minidonations.org" rel="nofollow">
       MiniDonations
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
       <a href="#cite_note-52">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        52
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Advocates argue that these terms describe the sector in its own terms, without relying on terminology used for the government or business sectors. However, use of terminology by a nonprofit of self-descriptive language that is not legally compliant risks confusing the public about nonprofit abilities, capabilities and limitations.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
       <a href="#cite_note-53">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        53
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In some Spanish-language jurisdictions, nonprofit organizations are called "civil associations".
     </p>
