      <p>
       It's the time of year for weddings,  but before you take those vows make sure you're on the same page when it comes to money.  In Smart Money,  Chris Harper from Hungerford  Nichols C-P-A's and advisors, and visiting instructor at Grand Valley State University shares financial advice for newlyweds.
      </p>
      <p>
       <u>
        #1  DEVELOP A BUDGET
       </u>
      </p>
      <p>
                A budget is critical because it allows you to control expenses so you can live on less than you earn.
      </p>
      <p>
                A budget is actually a stress reliever because it removes an element of uncertainty from your finances.
      </p>
      <p>
                Include each spouse's personal spending amounts as part of your budget.  The goal is to avoid surprises.
      </p>
      <p>
                Include saving as part of your budget.
      </p>
      <p>
       One goal is a cash emergency fund of three to six months of living expenses.
      </p>
      <p>
       Take advantage of 401(k) plans, 403(b) plans, IRAs and other qualified retirement accounts.
      </p>
      <p>
                Regularly review your budget and actual expenses together.
      </p>
      <p>
                The shared purpose of a budget can unify couples.  If not done together, it can be divisive.
      </p>
      <p>
       <u>
        #2  WHO WILL BE THE MONEY MANAGER?
       </u>
      </p>
      <p>
                Decide who will pay bills, balance bank accounts and handle the day-to-day tasks.
      </p>
      <p>
                The money manager should be sure to keep the other spouse in the loop regarding the big picture.
      </p>
      <p>
                Depending on the financial acumen of each spouse, you may consider consulting a financial planner.
      </p>
      <p>
                An emergency book is a useful tool for the spouse that isn't handling the day-to-day finances.  The emergency book can contain passwords, bank account information and other critical financial information that will allow either spouse to access information quickly if the other spouse is disabled or deceased.
      </p>
      <p>
                Consider taking a personal finance class as a couple.
      </p>
      <p>
       <u>
        #3  DEBT
       </u>
      </p>
      <p>
                Go into the marriage with a clear understanding of the amount of debt each spouse is bringing to the marriage.
      </p>
      <p>
                Be cautious with debt and use it wisely.  Debt can be helpful for prudent purchases (e.g. a house), but can magnify the impact of financial indiscretions (e.g. using credit cards to amplify overspending tendencies).
      </p>
      <p>
                Include debt reduction as a part of your household budget.
      </p>
      <p>
       <u>
        #4  INSURANCE
       </u>
      </p>
      <p>
                Life insurance is essential for meeting the financial needs of survivors who depend on a spouse's income.
      </p>
      <p>
                Disability insurance can provide a partial replacement of a disabled spouse's income.
      </p>
      <p>
                Ensure that you have sufficient coverage for your home, automobiles and other property.
      </p>
      <p>
                Consider the need for an umbrella policy to provide additional liability protection.
      </p>
      <p>
                If you rent your personal residence, don't forget to consider renter's insurance for your personal property.
      </p>
      <p>
       <u>
        #5  ESTATE PLANNING
       </u>
      </p>
      <p>
                At a minimum, have a will drafted.  Seriously consider hiring an attorney to draft the will, financial power of attorney, patient advocate designation and to consult with you regarding your unique situation.
      </p>
      <p>
                Ensure that the beneficiaries listed on life insurance policies and retirement plans are consistent with your estate planning objectives.
      </p>
      <p>
                Be sure to update your estate plan for major life events such as the birth of a child or a significant increase in wealth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Please remember these are general tips and advice.  If you have questions about your specific situation please contact a financial advisor.
      </p>
