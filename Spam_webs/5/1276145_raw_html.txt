    <h1>
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
    </h1>
    <p align="left">
     <b>
      Name:
     </b>
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
     (After an ancient version of a scalpel).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Phonetic:
     </b>
     Koe-pah-re-on.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Named By:
     </b>
     D. J. Chure - 1994.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Classification:
     </b>
     Chordata, Reptilia, Dinosauria,
Saurischia, Theropoda, Coelurosauria, Troodontidae.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Species:
     </b>
     <i>
      K. douglassi
     </i>
     (type).
     <br/>
     <b>
      Diet:
     </b>
     Carnivore.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Size:
     </b>
     Unavailable due to incomplete remains.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Known locations:
     </b>
     USA, Utah - Morrison
Formation.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Time period:
     </b>
     Kimmeridgian of the Jurassic.
     <br/>
     <b>
      Fossil representation:
     </b>
     Teeth.
    </p>
    <p align="left">
     The
name
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
     is based upon the comparison of the
serrated teeth
(currently the only known parts of this dinosaur) to ancient
versions of scalpels. Despite the lack of overall remains,
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
     is widely regarded as being a troodontid and if this is correct, it
would be one of the if not the oldest troodontid currently known.
There is counter speculation however that the
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
     teeth may be
those of a different kind of dinosaur.
     <br/>
     It
might seem unusual to name
an animal genus based only upon teeth, but this has happened many
times in the past.
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
     is also in good
company for such
precedence since the actual type genus of the Troodontidae
     <a href="http://www.prehistoric-wildlife.com/species/t/troodon.html">
      <i>
       Troodon
      </i>
     </a>
     was
initially described and known only from teeth only to eventually have
further and much more complete dinosaur fossils attributed to. This
was possible due to the unique form of the teeth in question, and
with
     <i>
      Koparion
     </i>
     having similar distinctive teeth,
it may one day be
possible to attribute further remains upon this basis.
    </p>
