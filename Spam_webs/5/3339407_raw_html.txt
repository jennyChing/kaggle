     <p>
      <b>
       Anita Berber
      </b>
      (10 June 1899  10 November 1928) was a German dancer, actress, and writer who was the subject of an
      <a href="/wiki/Otto_Dix" title="Otto Dix">
       Otto Dix
      </a>
      painting. She lived during the
      <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">
       Weimar
      </a>
      period.
     </p>

     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
       Early life
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anita_Berber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Born in
      <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">
       Leipzig
      </a>
      to musician father and an actress mother who later divorced, she was raised mainly by her grandmother in
      <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">
       Dresden
      </a>
      . By the age of 16, she had moved to
      <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
       Berlin
      </a>
      and made her debut as a
      <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">
       cabaret
      </a>
      dancer. By 1918 she was working in film, and she began dancing nude in 1919. Scandalously
      <a href="/wiki/Androgyny" title="Androgyny">
       androgynous
      </a>
      , she quickly made a name for herself. She wore heavy dancer's make-up, which on the black-and-white photos and films of the time came across as jet black lipstick painted across the heart-shaped part of her skinny lips, and charcoaled eyes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wound_magazine_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-wound_magazine-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Notoriety_in_Berlin">
       Notoriety in Berlin
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anita_Berber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notoriety in Berlin">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Her hair was cut fashionably into a short bob and was frequently bright red, as in 1925 when the German painter Otto Dix painted a portrait of her, titled "The Dancer Anita Berber". Her dancer friend and sometime lover
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Droste" title="Sebastian Droste">
       Sebastian Droste
      </a>
      , who performed in the film
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Algol_(film)" title="Algol (film)">
        Algol
       </a>
      </i>
      (1920), was skinny and had black hair with gelled up curls much like sideburns. Neither of them wore much more than lowslung
      <a href="/wiki/Loincloth" title="Loincloth">
       loincloths
      </a>
      and Anita occasionally a
      <a href="/wiki/Corsage_(bodice)" title="Corsage (bodice)">
       corsage
      </a>
      worn well below her small breasts.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wound_magazine_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-wound_magazine-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Her performances broke boundaries with their androgyny and total nudity, but it was her public appearances that really challenged taboos. Berber's overt drug addiction and
      <a href="/wiki/Bisexuality" title="Bisexuality">
       bisexuality
      </a>
      were matters of public chatter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In addition to her addiction to cocaine,
      <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">
       opium
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">
       morphine
      </a>
      , one of Berber's favourites was
      <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">
       chloroform
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">
       ether
      </a>
      mixed in a bowl.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon2006_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon2006-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      This would be stirred with a white rose, the petals of which she would then eat.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlin_2005_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berlin_2005-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Aside from her addiction to
      <a href="/wiki/Narcotic" title="Narcotic">
       narcotic
      </a>
      drugs, she was also a heavy
      <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">
       alcoholic
      </a>
      . In 1928, at the age of 29, she suddenly gave up alcohol completely, but died later the same year. She was said to be surrounded by empty morphine syringes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlin_2005_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Berlin_2005-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Mel Gordon, in
      <i>
       The Seven Addictions and Five Professions of Anita Berber: Weimar Berlin's Priestess of Debauchery
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_5-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gordon-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      she was diagnosed with severe
      <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">
       tuberculosis
      </a>
      while performing abroad. After collapsing in
      <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">
       Damascus
      </a>
      , she returned to Germany and died in a
      <a href="/wiki/Kreuzberg" title="Kreuzberg">
       Kreuzberg
      </a>
      hospital on 10 November 1928. She was buried in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauper" title="Pauper">
       pauper
      </a>
      's grave in St. Thomas Cemetery in
      <a href="/wiki/Neuk%C3%B6lln" title="Neuklln">
       Neuklln
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Marriages">
       Marriages
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anita_Berber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriages">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In 1919, she entered into a
      <a href="/wiki/Marriage_of_convenience" title="Marriage of convenience">
       marriage of convenience
      </a>
      with a man with the surname Nathusius. She later left him in order to pursue a relationship with a woman named Susi Wanowski, and became part of the Berlin lesbian scene.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gayhistory_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gayhistory-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Her second marriagein 1922was to
      <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Droste" title="Sebastian Droste">
       Sebastian Droste
      </a>
      . This lasted until 1923. In 1925, she married a gay American dancer named
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henri_Chatin-Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henri Chatin-Hofmann (page does not exist)">
       Henri Chatin-Hofmann
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gayhistory_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-gayhistory-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
