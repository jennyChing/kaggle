        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Vineyards of the Loire Valley, France
        </h1>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <div id="greet_block">

        <input data-frizzlyhovercontainer="" data-frizzlypostcontainer="" data-frizzlyposttitle="Vineyards of the Loire Valley, France" data-frizzlyposturl="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/2010/07/07/vineyards-of-the-loire-valley-france/" type="hidden" value=""/>
        <p>
         As I have said in previous posts, I love the Loire Valley  it really is one of the best places to visit in Europe. My previous trips there have been to explore the architectural heritage of the region, the many
         <a href="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/2010/06/16/castles-of-the-loire-valley-well-19-of-them/">
          Loire Valley castles
         </a>
         . But, as the area is so well known for many and various great wines, I decided my next trip would be to investigate the viticulture. And this I did last week; well I scratched the surface. I stayed at a charming holiday cottage and spent my days touring numerous vineyards.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/loire.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-13700 frizzly-group-13699" height="375" src="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/loire.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         With the right people, the vineyards themselves can be very interesting; they are not just row upon row of grape-producing vines. Look closely at the photograph above and you will see dotted about the vineyards small and somewhat quaint buildings. These buildings can be seen everywhere, in various states of repair, or disrepair. A close up of one can be seen in the photograph below. These are
         <em>
          maisons de vigne
         </em>
         , that once provided shelter for meals and rest to the farmers who worked the vineyards. Now, with more modern farming techniques and less of a reliance on the horse, workers tend to go back to their houses for lunch and to escape the midday heat.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/loire1.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-13701 frizzly-group-13699" height="375" src="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/loire1.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         And scattered amongst the more standard vineyards, using more usual techniques are the vineyards of those who experimented, those tried something different. One of those was the successful draper Antoine Cristal. This man, who knew some very influential people and was friends with the French President Georges Clmenceau, is credited with introducing Loire Valley red wines to wider market in the mid to late 1800s. The Loire Valley had until then been known only for its white wines. Cristal is also famous for his
         <em>
          Le Clos des Murs
         </em>
         , vineyard of walls, pictured below.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/closdumurs2.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-13704 frizzly-group-13699" height="296" src="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/closdumurs2.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         The quality of wine is said to be dependant on the growing conditions of the vine itself. Cristal believed the best wine would be produced from vines whose roots were in shade but where the grapes were in direct sun. So he produced his vineyard of walls, where he placed the root in the shade of the wall, and the vine was then passed through a whole in the wall and allowed to grow in direct sunlight.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/closdumurs.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-13705 frizzly-group-13699" height="667" src="http://www.europealacarte.co.uk/blog/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/closdumurs.jpg" width="500"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Visiting this fascinating and quirky vineyard, and learning of its history, was a real treat and a surprise. When he died Cristal bequeathed his
         <a href="http://closdesmurs-cristal.com/">
          Clos des Murs
         </a>
         to a local Hospital, and still today Cristals vineyards produce some very fines wines.
        </p>

          <p>
           Hello, I am Thomas Dowson - a freelance writer and archaeologist living in Normandy, France. My field of expertise is prehistoric art - such as the cave paintings in the Dordogne and South Africa. But I am becoming passionately interested in France more generally, and Normandy in particular, and what this country and one of its very well known regions has to offer people with all sorts of tastes and desires. In 2005 I exchanged a university archaeology lecture room for a Bed &amp; Breakfast in Normandy. More recently I started the Archaeology Travel website; sharing my expertise and love of archaeology and travel with others who also want to explore the many different pasts around the World.
