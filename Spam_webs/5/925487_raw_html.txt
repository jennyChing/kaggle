pathjs.getPageFormatIds = function(pageId){
	var myAdMeta = pathjs.AdConfig[pageId].AdSlotMeta;
	var formatIdArr = [];
	for (slot in myAdMeta){
		if (myAdMeta.hasOwnProperty(slot)){
			//if this is an array, add all members to formatIdArr
			if (pathjs.isArray(myAdMeta[slot])){
				formatIdArr.concat(myAdMeta[slot]);
			//otherwise just add single value
			} else {
				formatIdArr.push(myAdMeta[slot]);
			}
		}
	}
	//make the array a CSV string
	return formatIdArr.join();
};

       <h1 class="entry-title">
        The naked Christ in film: birth, death and resurrection
       </h1>
       <div class="post-info">
        <span class="date published time" title="2013-04-23T12:30:02+00:00">
         April 23, 2013
        </span>
        by
        <span class="author vcard">
         <span class="fn">
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/author/peterchattaway" rel="author" title="Peter T. Chattaway">
           Peter T. Chattaway
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
        <span class="post-comments">
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/2013/04/the-naked-christ-in-film-birth-death-and-resurrection.html#comments">
          <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="7221 http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/?p=7221">
           2 Comments
          </span>
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="entry-content">
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/passionofthechrist-resface-a.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-7227" height="172" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/passionofthechrist-resface-a-300x172.jpg" title="passionofthechrist-resface-a" width="300"/>
         </a>
         The Word became flesh, according to
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+1:14&amp;version=NIV" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          John 1:14
         </a>
         , but that flesh has been hidden, for the most part, in movie portrayals of Jesus. At certain key points in his life, history and even tradition would dictate that Jesus ought to be depicted nude  and there are good theological reasons for doing so. But films have tended to shy away from nudity in their own portrayals of those parts of the Jesus story.
        </p>
        <p>
         There are some obvious reasons for this reticence, of course, starting with the fact that film, for much of its history, has been forced to skirt around images of nudity in general, and images of male nudity in particular. Plus, when a film does show someones nudity, it does not merely show us the
         <i>
          characters
         </i>
         nudity; it shows us the
         <i>
          actors
         </i>
         nudity as well, and the knowledge that we are seeing an
         <i>
          actors
         </i>
         naked body can sometimes distract us from the
         <i>
          character
         </i>
         he is playing. This is especially true when the character is meant to be an embodiment of divinity like Jesus.
        </p>
        <p>
         There have been at least three significant exceptions, though  three films that each depict the nudity of Jesus at a different key point in his story.
        </p>
        <p>
         <span id="more-7221">
         </span>
         First,
         <i>
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/tag/nativity-story">
           The Nativity Story
          </a>
         </i>
         (2006) shows Joseph holding the naked baby Jesus up in the air immediately after the child is born:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/nativitystory-birth.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-7224" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/nativitystory-birth.jpg" title="nativitystory-birth" width="590"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         There is actually another shot before this one, which gives us an even clearer look at the babys naked backside, but Id rather not post it here. I will note, though, that the nudity here underscores the fact that God, in Jesus, has become both human and vulnerable  and that this film makes a point of showing not only the baby himself but a bit of the muck that you typically get on a baby when it has just been born. (Someone who saw an unfinished rough cut of the film once told me that there was originally less muck on the baby, so some of it might have been added digitally.)
        </p>
        <p>
         In this film, the baby is shown in all its messy nakedness for more or less the same reason the filmmakers made a point of casting actors
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/2006/11/ethnicity-in-jesus-films-does-it-matter.html">
          of a certain ethnicity
         </a>
         or showing the details of everyday life in a first-century village; its all part of a general effort to be realistic. But the realism of this scene only goes so far. While this film, like a few others, does show Mary experiencing
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/2005/05/pascha-ever-virginity-point-of-view-shots.html">
          labour pains
         </a>
         , it does
         <i>
          not
         </i>
         show Joseph or anyone else cutting an umbilical cord; the baby seems to have come out of the womb untethered. Even more interestingly, there is a point during the delivery of Jesus when the film suddenly fades to white. Details like these, intentionally or not, seem to hark back to a tradition that is at least as old as the 2nd-century
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://web.archive.org/web/20080611085726/http://www.gospels.net/translations/infancyjamestranslation.html" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Infancy Gospel of James
         </a>
         , which holds that a great light appeared in the cave so that their eyes could not bear it. And a little while later the same light withdrew until an infant appeared.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/hansbaldunggrien-holyfamily-detail.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-7256" height="280" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/hansbaldunggrien-holyfamily-detail-300x280.jpg" title="hansbaldunggrien-holyfamily-detail" width="300"/>
         </a>
         Be all that as it may, the main point here is that
         <i>
          The Nativity Story
         </i>
         does, in fact, show the baby Jesus naked, and it is the only film I can think of right now that does this. In other artforms, however, especially in the west, there is a long tradition of showing the infant naked  and of even drawing our attention to his genitalia  as a way of showing that God has become human. See, e.g., the detail here from Hans Baldung Griens
         <i>
          Holy Family
         </i>
         (1511). Leo Steinberg documented this pattern extensively in
         <i>
          <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0226771873/petertchatta" rel="external nofollow" title="">
           The Sexuality of Christ in Renaissance Art and in Modern Oblivion
          </a>
         </i>
         , where he noted that the sex of the newborn is a demonstrative sign which verifies the humanation of God.
        </p>
        <p>
         <i>
          The Nativity Story
         </i>
         doesnt go so far as to
         <i>
          draw our attention to
         </i>
         the genitalia of the Christ child  theyre actually obscured throughout the scene, which limits our vision to his back and side  but, believe it or not, I can think of one film that
         <i>
          does
         </i>
         draw our attention to them, though it does so without nudity. Specifically, I am thinking of Franco Zeffirellis
         <i>
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/tag/jesus-of-nazareth-1977">
           Jesus of Nazareth
          </a>
         </i>
         (1977), which is the only film I can think of that devotes a significant bit of screen time to the circumcision of Christ:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/vlcsnap-2013-04-20-01h26m23s129.png" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-7223" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/vlcsnap-2013-04-20-01h26m23s129.png" title="vlcsnap-2013-04-20-01h26m23s129" width="590"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         The circumcision of Jesus is mentioned briefly in
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=luke+2:21-35&amp;version=NIV" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Luke 2
         </a>
         , and it has
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feast_of_the_Circumcision_of_Christ" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          its own feast day
         </a>
         on the Christian liturgical calendar, but it is virtually ignored in Jesus films  even
         <i>
          The Nativity Story
         </i>
         omits it  and on the rare occasion that a film does include it, as in
         <i>
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/tag/jesus-of-nazareth-1977">
           Jesus
          </a>
         </i>
         (1979), it merits little more than a quick mention by the narrator.
        </p>
        <p>
         Zeffirellis film, on the other hand, pays significant attention to the Jewish customs within which Jesus was born and raised; the man who is about to perform the circumcision even declares that this is the seal
         <i>
          in flesh
         </i>
         of the covenant between the Lord and his people. So even if the film does not
         <i>
          show
         </i>
         any flesh during this scene, it certainly encourages us to
         <i>
          think
         </i>
         about the flesh, and what it means.
        </p>
        <p>
         As shown in this film, the moment of Jesus circumcision  the moment when the baby Jesus cries  is also what catches the attention of Simeon, the old man who was told by God that he would not die until he had seen the Messiah. In Lukes gospel, Simeon speaks to Joseph and Mary on a separate occasion, when they go to perform the purification rites required 33 days
         <i>
          after
         </i>
         the circumcision, as per
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=leviticus+12&amp;version=NIV" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Leviticus 12
         </a>
         . But Zeffirelli conflates the two episodes, so that Simeon hears the child cry and then approaches the table, where he directs our attention to the child once more:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/vlcsnap-2013-04-20-09h44m05s18.png" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-7266" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/vlcsnap-2013-04-20-09h44m05s18.png" title="vlcsnap-2013-04-20-09h44m05s18" width="590"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         The second episode from Jesus life that warrants depicting him in the nude is the Crucifixion, and the one film I can think of that actually does this  as I mentioned in my recent post on
         <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/2013/04/history-and-tradition-in-movie-depictions-of-the-cross.html">
          depictions of the Cross
         </a>
          is
         <i>
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/tag/last-temptation-of-christ">
           The Last Temptation of Christ
          </a>
         </i>
         (1988):
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/vlcsnap-2013-04-08-10h25m46s24.png" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-7228" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/vlcsnap-2013-04-08-10h25m46s24.png" title="vlcsnap-2013-04-08-10h25m46s24" width="590"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         This film features quite a bit of nudity, of course  some of it male, some of it female  so it is not surprising that it turns up here, too. Jesus appears nude in one earlier scene, as well, when the Roman soldiers beat him in prison. Scenes like these are remarkable because nearly every other depiction of Jesus in film lets him wear at least a loincloth at this point in his story. And yet, early Christian tradition tells us that Jesus was crucified naked.
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyril_of_Jerusalem" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          St. Cyril of Jerusalem
         </a>
          who may have witnessed crucifixion himself, since he was already in his 20s when the emperor Constantine
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crucifixion" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          abolished the practice
         </a>
          wrote in his
         <i>
          Catechetical Lectures
         </i>
         that early Christians, who were baptized in the nude, did so partly
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/npnf207.ii.iii.iv.html#ii.iii.iv-p19" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          to identify with Christ in his death
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/bar-crucifixion.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-7275" height="300" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/bar-crucifixion-266x300.jpg" title="bar-crucifixion" width="266"/>
         </a>
         Director Martin Scorsese claims in an opening title card that
         <i>
          The Last Temptation of Christ
         </i>
         is based on a work of fiction, with no direct ties to the Jesus of history or the gospels, but he strove for historical accuracy in this one area, at least, and he based his depiction of the crucifixion of Jesus (but not the crucifixion of the thieves) on an illustration that was first published in the
         <i>
          Israel Exploration Journal
         </i>
         in 1970. Two years earlier, archaeologists had discovered the bones of a crucified man named Yehohanan, and osteologists were able to reconstruct Yehohanans position on the cross by
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/biblical-topics/crucifixion/a-tomb-in-jerusalem-reveals-the-history-of-crucifixion-and-roman-crucifixion-methods/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          studying his wounds
         </a>
          and as you can see, the artist who drew the accompanying picture assumed that Yehohanan was naked when he was crucified, too.
        </p>
        <p>
         Finally, there is the Resurrection. Most of the films that
         <i>
          do
         </i>
         depict it in some way usually show Jesus wearing a robe, not unlike the robes he wore earlier in the film, or not unlike the glowing white robes that the angels might wear. But one striking exception to this is Mel Gibsons
         <i>
          <a href="http://www.patheos.com/blogs/filmchat/tag/passion-of-the-christ">
           The Passion of the Christ
          </a>
         </i>
         (2004).
        </p>
        <p>
         In this film, the camera moves across the tomb before settling on a tight close-up of Jesus, who has apparently been resurrected in a sort of crouching position. We can see right away that his shoulders are bare. Then his eyes open, and he stands up, and we get a quick but clear glimpse of his naked thigh
         <i>
          through
         </i>
         the hole in his hand:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/passionofthechrist-reshand1.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-7229" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/passionofthechrist-reshand1.jpg" title="passionofthechrist-reshand1" width="590"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         A split-second later, he begins to walk, and although our glimpse of the back of his thigh is blurred, we get the distinct, if brief, impression that Jesus might not be wearing
         <i>
          anything
         </i>
         at this point in the story  not even a loincloth:
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/passionofthechrist-reshand2.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-7230" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/passionofthechrist-reshand2.jpg" title="passionofthechrist-reshand2" width="590"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         Note: It would not matter for our purposes if the actor was actually wearing something on the set that day. What matters is the
         <i>
          image
         </i>
          and it seems that Gibson, after following all the artistic conventions that show Jesus wearing a loincloth on the cross, decided to suggest that Jesus might
         <i>
          not
         </i>
         be so adorned in his life
         <i>
          after
         </i>
         death.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/michelangelo-risenchrist.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-7293" height="300" src="http://wp.patheos.com.s3.amazonaws.com/blogs/filmchat/files/2013/04/michelangelo-risenchrist-223x300.jpg" title="michelangelo-risenchrist" width="223"/>
         </a>
         Again, theres a theological reason for this. As Steinberg notes, artists like
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cristo_della_Minerva" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          Michelangelo
         </a>
         sometimes depicted the resurrected Jesus fully nude  a fact that later curators have sometimes tried to cover up, as you can see from the picture to the right  and the reason for this nudity is that the resurrected Jesus has restored humanity to the innocence that Adam and Eve once enjoyed before the Fall: How then could he who restores human nature to sinlessness be shamed by the sexual factor in his humanity?
        </p>
        <p>
         This link between the innocence of Adam and the nudity of the resurrected Christ is echoed in the baptismal practices of the early Church. As noted above, converts were baptized in the nude back then, and St. Cyril of Jerusalem says they did this in imitation of Christs death; but he also says that they were baptized nude in imitation of the first-formed Adam, who was naked in the garden, and was not ashamed. Similarly, St. John Chrysostom, in his
         <i>
          Baptismal Instructions
         </i>
         , told the early Christians that they were baptized naked to remind you of your former nakedness, when you were in paradise
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://nearemmaus.com/2010/11/19/naked-baptism/" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          and you were not ashamed
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         All this talk of baptism raises an interesting point that I hadnt considered when I started writing this blog post. Early Christians were baptized in the nude, and you can find some
         <a class="ext-link" data-wpel-target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Baptistery.Arians06.jpg" rel="external nofollow" title="">
          very old depictions of the baptism of Jesus
         </a>
         in which he, too, is nude  but I cant think of any films that have depicted his baptism this way. Even in
         <i>
          The Last Temptation of Christ
         </i>
         , which imagines that John the Baptist was surrounded by naked and half-naked people chanting and swaying and cutting themselves, Jesus keeps his loincloth on when he kneels in the water and asks John to baptize him.
        </p>
        <p>
         In any case, it is intriguing to think that Christian baptism once incorporated the key paradox of nudity: It can sometimes be used to shame people, as the Roman soldiers tried to shame their victims, but it can also be used to express a kind of purity for which no one needs to be ashamed of anything. And just as genitalia reflect the animal side of human nature, and are thus typically thought to be lower than the things which make us spiritual, the fact that God became man and redeemed humanity is expressed most potently in the fact that, yes, he now has genitals too.
        </p>
        <p>
         The four films Ive cited here are too discreet to depict the actual genitalia  which is only fitting, as that would draw our attention to the
         <i>
          actors
         </i>
         as much as it would to the
         <i>
          character
         </i>
         that they are playing. But in showing Jesus nudity the way they do, they encourage us to think about this aspect of the mystery that is the Incarnation.
        </p>
       </div>
