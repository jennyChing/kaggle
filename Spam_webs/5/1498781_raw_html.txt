    Meretrix
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <div class="hatnote">

      <a href="/wiki/Meretrix_lusoria" title="Meretrix lusoria">

     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
       ancient Rome
      </a>
      , a
      <i>
       <b>
        meretrix
       </b>
      </i>
      (plural: meretrices) was a registered
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostitute" title="Prostitute">
       prostitute
      </a>
      , while an unregistered prostitute fell under the broad category
      <i>
       <b>
        prostibulae
       </b>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_conditions">
       Roman conditions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meretrix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Roman conditions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">
       Legal status
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meretrix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal status">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Roman law, the status of meretrices was specifically and closely regulated.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sokala_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Sokala-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were obliged to register with the
      <a href="/wiki/Aedile" title="Aedile">
       aediles
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and (from Caligula's day onwards) to pay imperial tax.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Because of the
      <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">
       social stigma
      </a>
      associated with the profession, they could not give evidence in court,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Roman freeborn were legally forbidden from marrying them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Clothing">
       Clothing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meretrix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clothing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Since the times of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Augustus" title="Emperor Augustus">
       Emperor Augustus
      </a>
      they were required to wear distinctive
      <a href="/wiki/Toga" title="Toga">
       togas
      </a>
      , typically dyed in yellow, to distinguish themselves from respected
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Matrona" title="Matrona">
       matrons
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson-32_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Gibson-32-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Bright colors - Colores meretricii - and jewelled anklets also marked them out from respectable women.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Brothels">
       Brothels
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meretrix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brothels">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Because intercourse with a meretrix was almost normative for the adolescent male of the period, and permitted for the married man as long as the prostitute was properly registered,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      brothels were commonly dispersed around Roman cities, often found between houses of respected families.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duncan-13_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Duncan-13-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      These included both large
      <a href="/wiki/Brothel" title="Brothel">
       brothels
      </a>
      and one-room
      <i>
       cellae meretriciae
      </i>
      , or "prostitute's cots".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGinn-2004-11_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-McGinn-2004-11-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, ancient authors often made distinctions between "good faith" meretrices who truly loved their clients, and "bad faith" prostitutes, who only lured them in for their money.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duncan-259_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Duncan-259-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duckworth-253_12-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Duckworth-253-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_condition">
       Medieval condition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meretrix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medieval condition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      In Medieval Europe, a meretrix was understood as any woman held in common, who turned no one away.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biffi-15_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Biffi-15-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      It was generally understood that money would be involved in this transaction, but it did not have to be: it was rather promiscuousness that defined the meretrix.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Medieval Christian authors often discouraged prostitution, but did not consider it a serious offence and under some circumstances even considered marrying a harlot to be an act of
      <a href="/wiki/Piety" title="Piety">
       piety
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brundage-308_15-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Brundage-308-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Every woman was considered to contain a latent meretrix, so that it was possible to both rise out of and fall into the category, as with tales of prostitutes repenting to become saints.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      It has been argued that a meretrix in the medieval mindset is closer to our modern understanding of a sexual identity or orientation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
