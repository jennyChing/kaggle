      <div class="entry-content">

            <p>
             I
             <strong>
              n 1985, Nintendo released
              <em>
               Super Mario Brothers
              </em>
             </strong>
             in North America.
             <strong>
              Its flagship franchise took the then fledging video gaming industry by storm.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             With 8-bit surreal landscapes populated with a brilliant blue sky, white clouds, verdant plants, cartoony adversaries, and the two plucky protagonists,
             <a data-cke-saved-href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Mario_Bros." href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Mario_Bros." target="_blank">
              Super Mario Brothers
             </a>
             became synonymous with family-friendly electronic entertainment.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>
              Fast forward to 2013, and the franchise has gotten an astonishing makeover.
             </strong>
            </p>
            <p>
             Written and directed by
             <span style="font-size:13px">
              <a data-cke-saved-href="http://www.imdb.com/name/nm2489193/" href="http://www.imdb.com/name/nm2489193/" target="_blank">
               Evan Daugherty
              </a>
              ,
             </span>
             <em>
              The Four Players
             </em>
             reframes the backstories of Mario, Luigi, Princess Peach, and her devoted courtier Toad in a series of four live-action, dark and gritty cinematic shorts.
            </p>
            <p>
             While the concept may sound very dubious on paper, the execution is a brilliant success.
            </p>
            <p>
             I attribute this accomplishment to:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              the exceptionally smart script
             </li>
             <li>
              gorgeous production designs by Holly Conrad and Jacob Thomsen
             </li>
             <li>
              sensational cinematography
             </li>
             <li>
              acknowledgement of iconic gameplay hallmarks in the form of clever easter eggs
             </li>
            </ul>
            <p>
             In my opinion, the standout among these exceptional shorts is
             <em>
              The Soldier,
             </em>
             which looks like a forgotten Guillermo del Toro movie!
            </p>
            <p>
             All four installments are available for viewing below.
            </p>
            <p>
             <strong>

               The Fixer
