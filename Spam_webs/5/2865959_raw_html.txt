     <h2>
      <strong>
       French V Tutorial: Basic Phrases, Vocabulary and Grammar
       <br/>
       <img align="absmiddle" height="20" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/logos/flags/30x20xfrance.gif.pagespeed.ic.0g1nySBX4-.png" width="30"/>
       <img align="absmiddle" height="20" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/photos/30x20xquebec.gif.pagespeed.ic.nhXQtlNAeK.png" width="30"/>

       <img align="absmiddle" height="20" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/photos/20x20xswitzlnd.gif.pagespeed.ic.b_STAwleUY.png" width="20"/>
      </strong>
     </h2>
     <hr/>
     <p>
     </p>

     <p>
      <a id="coll" name="coll">
       <!-- -->
      </a>
      <span class="d">
       81. Colloquial Expressions
      </span>
      <a href="mp3s/colloquial.mp3">
       <img align="absmiddle" alt="Listen to MP3" border="0" height="17" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/logos/75x17xmp3a.gif.pagespeed.ic.jwH50Awc7J.png" width="75"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Il faut + infinitive
      </b>
      (it is necessary, one must)
      <br/>
      Il faut tourner  gauche. It is necessary to turn left.
      <br/>
      Il faut faire les devoirs. One must do homework.
      <br/>
      Il lui faut aller  l'cole. He must go to school.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Il faut + noun
      </strong>
      (need)
      <br/>
      Il faut du fromage. You need some cheese.
      <br/>
      Il faut un jeton. You need a token.
      <br/>
      Il me faut un stylo. I need a pen.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Il reste
      </b>
      (there remains)
      <br/>
      Il reste une chambre. There is one room left.
      <br/>
      Il n'en reste plus. There are no more left.
      <br/>
      Il me reste trois jours. I have three days left.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Notice that il faut and il reste can both take an object pronoun to indicate
a person.
      </i>
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Il vaut mieux + infinitive
      </b>
      (it is better)
      <br/>
      Il vaut mieux prendre le bus. It is better to take the bus.
      <br/>
      Il vaut mieux apprendre les langues que la politique. It's better to
learn languages than politics.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Il s'agit de
      </b>
      (it's a question of, it's a matter of, it's about)
      <br/>
      De quoi s'agit-il ? What's is about?
      <br/>
      Il ne s'agit pas de a ! That's not the point!
      <br/>
      Il s'agit de ton avenir. It's a matter of your future.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Avoir beau
      </b>
      (although, despite the fact, however much)
      <br/>
      J'ai beau tudier cette langue, je ne la parle pas. Although I
study this language, I don't speak it.
      <br/>
      Il a beau faire froid, nous sortirons. Although is it cold, we will go
out.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Avoir l'air + adjective
      </strong>
      (to seem/look)
      <br/>
      a a l'air dlicieux. That looks delicious.
      <br/>
      Vous avez l'air fatigu. You seem tired.
      <br/>
      a n'a pas l'air d'aller. Things don't look so good.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       a vous dit ? / a vous dirait de... ?
      </strong>
      (Would
you like to? / How would you feel about...? / Does that interest you? / Does
that ring a bell?)
      <br/>
      a vous dirait de regarder un film ce soir ? Are you interested in watching
a movie tonight? / Would you like to watch a movie tonight?
      <br/>
      Non, a me dit rien. No, that doesn't interest me. / No, I don't want
to.
      <br/>
      a te dit quelque chose ? Does that ring a bell? / Does that remind you
of anything?
      <br/>
      Non, a me dit rien. No, that doesn't ring a bell. / That doesn't remind
me of anything.
      <br/>
      a ne me dit pas grande chose. That does nothing for me.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       a parle de quoi / qui ?
      </strong>
      (What / Who is it about?)
      <br/>
      a parle de quoi, le livre que tu cris ? What's the book about
that you're writing?
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       a y est !
      </strong>
      ( There! That's it ! It's done!)
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       a a t
      </strong>
      (How did it go? / It went well.)
      <br/>
      Ton examen, a a t ? How did your exam go?
      <br/>
      Oui, a a t. It went well.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       On dirait... [a ressemble ]
      </strong>
      (It seems / it
looks like / it tastes like / it smells like / it feels like / it sounds like)
      <br/>
      On dirait un chat. It looks like a cat.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       pas terrible
      </strong>
      (
      <em>
       terrible
      </em>
      actually means
      <em>
       terrific
      </em>
      in this phrase)
      <br/>
      C'est pas terrible, ce film. This movie is not very good.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       pas mal de
      </strong>
      (a lot of, lots of, tons of - synonym of
      <em>
       beaucoup
      </em>
      )
      <br/>
      J'ai pas mal de trucs  faire aujourd'hui. I have lots of things to do
today.
     </p>

     <p>
      The word
      <strong>
       soit
      </strong>
      has several meanings. It is also the third person
singular form of tre in the present subjunctive.
      <strong>
       Je veux qu'il
soit l
      </strong>
      . I want him to be here. It can also be used to mean
      <em>
       i.e.
      </em>
      or
      <em>

     <p>
      <a id="pass" name="pass">
       <!-- -->
      </a>
      <span class="d">
       86. Passive Voice
      </span>
      <a href="mp3s/passive.mp3">
       <img align="absmiddle" alt="Listen to MP3" border="0" height="17" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/logos/75x17xmp3a.gif.pagespeed.ic.jwH50Awc7J.png" width="75"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      As in English, the passive voice in French is composed of a tense
of the verb
      <em>
       to be
      </em>
      and a past participle. However, only a direct
object in French can become the subject of the passive form. The active
form,
      <b>
       le chat mange la souris
      </b>
      is made passive thus:
      <b>
       La souris
est mange par le chat.
      </b>
      <em>
       The cat eats the mouse
      </em>
      becomes
      <em>
       the
mouse is eaten by the cat.
      </em>
      The subject in the active sentence (le chat)
becomes the object of the passive. The direct object of the active sentence
(la souris) becomes the subject of the passive sentence preceded by "par." The
verb of the active sentence is changed into a past participle (mange becomes
mange, notice the agreement!) preceded by a form of
      <strong>
       tre
      </strong>
      .
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      Elle
      <b>
       est porte
      </b>
      par Jean. She is carried by
John.
      <br/>
      Elles
      <b>
       ont t inspires
      </b>
      par Van Gogh. They
were inspired by Van Gogh.
      <br/>
      Il
      <b>
       avait t tu
      </b>
      par les soldats franais.
He had been killed by French soldiers.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      <em>
       
      </em>
      Notice how pronominal verbs change from active to passive:
      <em>
      </em>
      <br/>
      <i>
       Active:
      </i>
      Je
      <b>
       me suis rveille
      </b>
      . I woke up.
      <br/>
      <i>
       Passive:
      </i>
      J'
      <b>
       ai t rveille
      </b>
      par quelque
chose. I was awakened by something.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      <strong>
       Passive is Not Possible
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p align="left">
       If a verb takes an indirect object, it cannot be transformed into
the passive voice in French. In this case,
      <b>
       on
      </b>
      is used in the active
construction, as long as the agent is not specified and the action is performed
by a human being (i.e. no natural forces, such as weather).
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      <b>
       On a donn
      </b>
      un cadeau  ma mre. My
mother was given a present. [Someone gave a present to my mother.]
      <br/>
      <b>
       On
      </b>
      lui
      <b>
       a dit
      </b>
      de retrouver le conservateur au muse. He
was told to meet the curator at the museum. [Someone told him to meet the curator
at the museum.]
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      <strong>
       Alternatives to the Passive
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p align="left">
       In addition to using
      <strong>
       on
      </strong>
      as the subject of an active
construction to replace the passive, you can also use the pronominal constructions:
      <strong>
       se voir
      </strong>
      [to see oneself]
      <strong>
       , s'entendre
      </strong>
      [to hear
oneself]
      <strong>
       , se faire
      </strong>
      [to get oneself] and
      <strong>
       se laisser
      </strong>
      [to let oneself] + infinitive. These verbs can be used when the agent is specified,
unlike the active construction with
      <strong>
       on
      </strong>
      .
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      Je
      <strong>
       me suis vu promettre
      </strong>
      une augmentation. I have
been promised a raise.
      <br/>
      Elle
      <strong>
       s'est entendu dire
      </strong>
      qu'il allait mourir. She was told that
he was going to die.
      <br/>
      Jean
      <strong>
       s'est fait arrter
      </strong>
      . John got arrested.
      <br/>
      Ils
      <strong>
       se sont laiss tomber
      </strong>
      malade. They let themselves
get sick.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
       You can also use a pronominal verb to translate the passive, as
long as the agent is not specified. However, this does not work for all verbs.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      a
      <strong>
       s'crit
      </strong>
      comment? How is that written?
      <br/>
      a ne
      <strong>
       se dit
      </strong>
      plus. That is not said anymore.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
       Use
      <strong>
       tre 
      </strong>
      + infinitive to translate
      <em>
       must
      </em>
      + English passive:
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      Ce monument
      <strong>
       est  voir
      </strong>
      ! This monument must
be seen!
      <br/>
      Ce rapport
      <strong>
       est  refaire
      </strong>
      . This report must be redone.
     </p>

      <a id="depuis" name="depuis">
       <!-- -->
      </a>
      <span class="d">
       87. Depuis, il y a, &amp; pendant in past contexts
      </span>
      <a href="mp3s/pastcontexts.mp3">
       <img align="absmiddle" alt="Listen to MP3" border="0" height="17" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/logos/75x17xmp3a.gif.pagespeed.ic.jwH50Awc7J.png" width="75"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      1. To express an action that has been going on, depuis (or il y a ... que)
is used with the
      <i>
       present tense.
      </i>
     </p>
     <div align="left">
      <table align="center" cellpadding="5" cellspacing="5" width="95%">
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Depuis quand avez-vous votre ordinateur ?
          <br/>
          Depuis combien de temps avez-vous votre ordinateur ?
         </b>
         <br/>
         <i>
          How long have you had your computer ?
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          Je l'ai depuis deux ans.
          <br/>
          a fait deux ans que je l'ai.
          <br/>
          Il y a deux ans que je l'ai.
         </b>
         <br/>
         <i>
          I've had it for two years.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Je conduis depuis longtemps.
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          I have been driving for a long time.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p align="left">
      2. But to express an action that had been going on for some time when something
else happened,
depuis is used with the
      <i>
       imperfect.
      </i>
     </p>
     <div align="left">
      <table align="center" cellpadding="5" cellspacing="5" width="95%">
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Nous conduisions depuis deux heures,
          <br/>
          quand j'ai propos de prendre le volant.
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          We had been driving for two hours
          <br/>
          when I volunteered to drive.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p align="left">
      3. To express an action that you have not done for some time, use depuis
with the
      <i>
       pass compos.
      </i>
     </p>
     <div align="left">
      <table align="center" cellpadding="5" cellspacing="5" width="95%">
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Je n'ai pas conduit depuis mon mariage.
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          I haven't driven since I've been married.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </div>
     <p align="left">
      4. To express an action that was done for a period of time,
      <b>
       pendant
      </b>
      is used, usually with the pass compos. But for an action
that was completed some time ago, use
      <b>
       il y a
      </b>
      , also with the pass
compos.
     </p>
     <center>
      <table align="center" cellpadding="5" cellspacing="5" width="95%">
       <tr>
        <td width="777">
         <b>
          J'ai lou une voiture pendant deux semaines.
         </b>
        </td>
        <td width="526">
         <i>
          I rented a car for two weeks.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          J'ai appris  conduire il y a deux ans.
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          I learned to drive two years ago.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </center>

     <p align="left">
      Faire + an infinitive is called the faire causative. It translates
to "have something done by someone or cause something to be done by someone,"

      </b>

      <b>

      </b>

      <br/>

      <br/>

      <b>

      </b>

     </p>

      <b>

      <span class="d">
       96. Ne Expletif
      </span>
      <a href="mp3s/neexpletif.mp3">
       <img align="absmiddle" alt="Listen to MP3" border="0" height="17" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/logos/75x17xmp3a.gif.pagespeed.ic.jwH50Awc7J.png" width="75"/>

      Sometimes ne must be inserted in a phrase even when it is not expressing the
negative. (However, do not confuse the use of
      <em>
       ne expltif
      </em>
      with
the verbs that can exist in the negative with only using
      <strong>
       ne
      </strong>
      and
      <em>
       not
      </em>
      <strong>
       pas
      </strong>
      in formal, written language: cesser, daigner,
oser, pouvoir, savoir).
     </p>
     <p>
      It is used 1) after certain conunctions:
      <strong>
       avant que,  moins
que
      </strong>
      ; 2) after expressions and verbs of fear:
      <strong>

de peur que, craindre que, avoir peur que, redouter que, trembler que, empcher

      ; 3) before a verb that follows a comparison
of inequality:
      <strong>

      </strong>

      <strong>

      <strong>

      </strong>
      I don't
doubt that you are making progress.
     </p>
     <hr/>
     <p>
      <a id="cond" name="cond">
       <!-- -->
      </a>
      <span class="d">
       97. Conditional Tenses: Present &amp; Past
      </span>
      <a href="mp3s/conditional.mp3">
       <img align="absmiddle" alt="Listen to MP3" border="0" height="17" src="http://www.ielanguages.com/logos/75x17xmp3a.gif.pagespeed.ic.jwH50Awc7J.png" width="75"/>
      </a>
     </p>
     <p>
      The present conditional tense corresponds to "would." It
is used after the imperfect in a conditional sentence. Most conditionals
sentences begin with
      <b>
       si
      </b>
      (if). However, do not confuse the
conditional
      <i>
       would
      </i>
      with the
      <i>
       would
      </i>
      that expresses a repeated action
in the past. If
      <i>
       would
      </i>
      means
      <i>
       used to
      </i>
      , then the imperfect
tense is used.  Another use of the conditional is in news reports to indicate
that the information is not confirmed.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Si j'
       <b>
        tais
       </b>
      </strong>
      <i>
       (imperfect)
      </i>
      <strong>
       dans
une autre famille, est-ce que je
       <b>
        serais
       </b>
      </strong>
      <i>
       (conditional)
      </i>
      <strong>
       plus heureuse ?
      </strong>
      If I were in another family, would I be happier?
      <strong>
       <br/>
       Quand nous
       <b>
        tions
       </b>
      </strong>
      <i>
       (imperfect)
      </i>
      <strong>
       en vacances,
nous
       <b>
        dormions
       </b>
      </strong>
      <i>
       (imperfect)
      </i>
      <strong>
       jusqu' midi.
      </strong>
      When we were on vacation, we would (used to) sleep until noon.
      <strong>
       <br/>
       Un otage tranger serait mort en route pour l'hpital.
      </strong>
      A foreign hostage (probably) died on the way to the hospital.
     </p>
     <p>
      To form the conditional, use the infinitive and add the imperfect
endings (but remember to drop the -e on -re verbs). You use the same irregular stems and exceptions for the conditional
that are used for the future tense.
      <br/>
     </p>
     <center>
      <table align="center" cellpadding="4" cellspacing="4" width="40%">
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          -ais
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          -ions
         </b>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          -ais
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          -iez
         </b>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          -ait
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          -aient
         </b>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
     </center>
     <p>
     </p>
     <p>
      The past conditional is formed by using the conditional of avoir or tre
and a past participle. It corresponds to "would have" and is used in
hypothetical sentences.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Il n'aurait jamais dit a !
      </b>
      He
      <i>
       would have
      </i>
      never
      <i>
       said
      </i>
      that!
     </p>
     <p>
      
      <strong>
       If... sentences:
      </strong>
      When si (if) is used in sentences of condition, the verb tenses change.
These pretty much correspond to English usage.
     </p>
     <p>
      1.
      <b>
       Si + present tense + present, imperative, or future
      </b>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Si je suis fatigue, je me repose.
      </strong>
      If I'm tired,
I rest.
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Repose-toi si tu es fatigu
      </strong>
      . Rest if you're tired.
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Si je suis fatigu demain, je me reposerai.
      </strong>
      If
I am tired tomorrow, I will rest.
     </p>
     <p>
      2.
      <b>
       Si + imperfect + present conditional
      </b>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Si j'tais riche, je pourrais acheter un chteau.
      </strong>
      If
I were rich, I would buy a castle.
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Il deviendrait roi s'il avait plus de courage.
      </strong>
      He would
become king if he had more courage.
     </p>
     <p>
      3.
      <b>
       Si + pluperfect + past conditional
      </b>
      <br/>
      <strong>
       Si j'avais su, j'aurais compris.
      </strong>
      If I had known, I would
have understood.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is possible to have past conditional with the imperfect, and
it is also possible to have present conditional with pluperfect. However,
you can never have the future or conditional tenses directly following si. They
must be in the other clause.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      <strong>
        Translating Would, Could, Should
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p align="left">
      In general, you use the conditional tense of a verb to express
      <em>
       would
+ infinitive,
      </em>
      such as
      <strong>
       je dirais
      </strong>
      - I
      <em>
       would say
      </em>
      .
Again, make sure to use the imperfect of the verb if you're referring to repeated
actions in the past (i.e. used to). You can also use the conditional of pouvoir
to mean
      <em>
       could
      </em>
      , as long as the meaning is something that is yet to happen.
      <strong>
       Il pourrait m'aider
      </strong>
      .
      <em>
       He could help me
      </em>
      . Otherwise,
you use the imperfect or pass compos to mean could if you're
referring to the past of can (i.e. was/were not able to).
      <strong>
       Elle ne pouvait
pas s'arrter de rire
      </strong>
      .
      <em>
       She couldn't stop laughing
      </em>
      . Should
is usually translated by using the conditional of devoir.
      <strong>
       Tu ne devrais
pas dire a
      </strong>
      .
      <em>
       You shouldn't say that.
      </em>
      For
      <em>
       would
have, could have
      </em>
      and
      <em>
       should have
      </em>
      , you use the past conditional
of the verb, past conditional of pouvoir + infinitive, and past conditional
of devoir + infinitive, respectively. Just remember that would and would have
are not followed by infinitives in French.
     </p>
     <p align="left">
     </p>
